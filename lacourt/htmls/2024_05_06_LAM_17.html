DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV02085    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

SEBASTIAN DONOVAN, et al

 

 

         vs.

 

QUANTGENE, INC., et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV02085

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 6, 2024

 

The Court finds that Defendants’ counsel has not waived privilege as to the allegedly privileged documents. However, Defendants are ordered to produce a privilege log within 15 days of entry of this order.

 

On 1/31/2023, Plaintiffs Sebastian Donovan and Arconex (collectively, Plaintiffs) filed suit against Quantgene, Inc. and Johannes Bhakdi (collectively, Defendants), alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) unjust enrichment; (4) intentional misrepresentation; (5) negligent misrepresentation; and (6) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200.

 

            On 3/3/2022, Cross-Complainants Quantgene, Inc. and Johannes Bhakdi filed a cross-complaint (XC) against Cross-Defendants Sebastian Donovan and Arconex, alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) false promise; (4) intentional misrepresentation; (5) concealment; (6) negligent misrepresentation; (7) conversion; (8) unjust enrichment; (9) intentional interference with contractual relations; and (10) unfair business practices.

 

            On 3/1/2024, Plaintiffs moved for a determination of privilege.

 

Factual Background

 

From late October 2023 to late January 2024, Defendants Quantgene, Inc. and Johannes Bhakdi (collectively, Defendants) made three document productions, containing a collective total of over 108,000 pages of documents.

 

Defendants have now admitted that these productions were a large-scale document dump that Defendants’ counsel did not reviewing before producing them. Rather, to save time, Defendants’ counsel relied on “search terms” to screen documents for privilege.

 

During Plaintiffs’ review of the documents, it became apparent that the production contained hundreds of documents (or thousands of individual communications) involving one of Quantgene’s in-house lawyers.

 

Discussion

 

When a party claims privilege over documents that have already been produced, the Code provides that the receiving party should “immediately sequester the information and either return the specified information and any copies that may exist or present the information to the court conditionally under seal for a determination of the claim.” (CCP § 2031.285(b).) The Code further provides that “[i]f the receiving party contests the legitimacy of a claim of privilege or protection, he or she may seek a determination of the claim from the court by making a motion within 30 days of receiving the claim and presenting the information to the court conditionally under seal.” Id. at § 2031.285(d)(1). “Until the legitimacy of the claim of privilege or protection is resolved, the receiving party shall preserve the information and keep it confidential and shall be precluded from using the information in any manner.” (Id. at § 2031.285(d)(2).)

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes its dismay that Defendants did not produce a claw-back list until 3/12/2024, approximately six weeks after Plaintiffs brought this issue to its attention. Even more concerning is that Defendants still not have not provided a privilege log, nor have they indicated when they will do so.

 

Given that Defendants have not actually asserted any privilege(s) as to any specific document, the Court cannot determine whether or not the unspecified documents are privileged. However, the Court can evaluate Plaintiffs’ contention that Defendants’ conduct amounts to a waiver of privilege.

 

After review, the Court determines that Defendants initial production did not result in waiver, nor did their subsequent conduct.

 

Under California law, when a party asserts that privileged materials were inadvertently produced, the holder of the privilege must demonstrate that it “has taken reasonable steps under the circumstances to prevent disclosure.” (Regents of Univ. of California v. Superior Ct. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 672, 683.)

 

Although it is common in modern discovery practice to use electronic discovery tools to aid in collection, review and production of documents that are in electronic form, the Code “clearly contemplates that . . . . parties will review ESI before producing it in order to protect information that is subject to a claim of privilege or of protection as attorney work product.” (Parkv. Law Offices of Tracey Buck-Walsh (2021) 73 Cal. Appl. 5th 179, 194 (citing Cal. Code. Civ. P. § 1985.8(j)). Thus, when a party makes the decision, whether to save time, money, or for some other reason, to use keyword searches alone to attempt to identify privileged documents, rather than actually reviewing the documents, that strategic decision has been found by many courts not to be “reasonable”, with the result that any claim of privileged is waived.

 

Here, Defendants’ counsel admitted that “this was a search term production” and that instead of actually reviewing the documents, “we used a variety of filters and rules to withhold certain privileged items.” (Carnevale Decl. ¶ 18, Ex. D.)

 

In its motion, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ counsel conduct was unreasonable, advancing arguments like:

 

[I]f Defendants’ counsel truly believed that it was not feasible or possible to review documents before producing them, there are reasonable steps that Defendant could have taken to prevent the inadvertent disclosure. In cases where a party believes a privilege review is not possible or practical for some reason, it is common to negotiate a “clawback agreement”, which “will permit the parties to conduct and respond to discovery in an expeditious manner, without the need for time-consuming and costly pre -production privilege reviews, and at the same time preserve the parties’ rights to assert the attorney-client privilege or work product immunity.”

 

            (Opp., 9: 7-13.)

 

            However, after review of Defendants’ opposition, the Court finds insufficient evidence to conclude that the inadvertent production was unreasonable.

 

In State Comp. Ins. Fund v. WPS, Inc. (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 644, 652 (1999), the Second Appellate District expressly that ‘waiver’ does not include accidental, inadvertent disclosure of privileged information by [the client's] attorney.” (Id. at p. 654.) In State Comp., outside counsel for plaintiff inadvertently sent defense counsel 273 pages of civil litigation claims forms as part of a reproduction of an earlier document production of 7,000 pages. The new set of documents bore clear markings as attorney-client communications. (See id. at 647-49.) The trial court found that the plaintiff had not waived the privilege by the inadvertent disclosure and production of these materials, and found defense counsel violated its ethical obligations to refrain from using and returning the documents to State Fund. (Id. at p. 651.) The Second Appellate District agreed. The court reasoned that there was no affirmative conduct on behalf plaintiff to consent to disclosure, and found that disclosure of the material was unintentional and had been made despite the procedures employed to prevent disclosure of privileged materials. (See id. at 653.)

 

Here, similarly, there is no indication that disclosure was intentional, but rather all evidence points to this being an inadvertent disclosure. While the Court agrees that Defendants’ counsel’s failure to closely review the document production is ill-advised, Plaintiff has not identified any controlling case law that requires a page-by-page, document-by-document review. Using keywords to capture potentially privileged items is common-place, and fails to support a finding waiver, and the Court is persuaded that Defendants’ counsel employed a method which it could have reasonably believed would not produce privileged materials.  The Court declines to consider the out of state authority submitted by Plaintiff.

 

Similarly, while the Court is troubled by Defendants’ counsel’s subsequent delay in specifically identifying the privileged documents, the Court finds inadequate authority to conclude that it rises to the level of waiver.

 

            When Plaintiff first notified Defendants that they may have produced documents subject to a potential claim of privilege, they made clear that the issue appeared to implicate many documents. (Carnevale Decl. ¶ 14, Ex. B.) Because Defendants were suspending their review and sequestering documents as required by CCP section 2031.285, Plaintiffs asked Defendants to identify the documents that they believed to be privileged. (Ibid.)

 

Although Defendants’ counsel sent emails making broad, blanket statements about having inadvertently produced documents, Defendants did not produce a claw-back list until 3/12/2024.

 

When evaluating “whether an inadvertent disclosure waived the attorney-client privilege” a relevant consideration is “the promptness with which the holder sought return of the inadvertently disclosed document.” (McDermott Will & Emery LLP v. Superior Ct. (2017)10 Cal. App. 5th 1083, 1102 (citing Ardon v. City of Los Angeles (2016) 62 Cal. 4th 1176. 1191); see also Luna Gaming San Diego, LLC v. Dorsey & Whitney, LLP(S.D. Cal. Jan. 13, 2010)  No. 06-cv-2804, 2010 WL 275083, at *6 (observing that “failing to take affirmative steps to retrieve the document . . . also waives the privilege”).)

 

            Here, Defendants did assert that the documents had been inadvertently produced, and have since produced a claw-back list identifying 222 privileged items. Plaintiff did not submit legal authority which could support a conclusion that six-weeks is sufficient delay to support a total waiver of privilege. However, the Court finds Defendants must produce a privilege log within 15 days of entry of this order, or Plaintiff may renew its motion.

 

            Finally, based on this conclusion, the Court directs the parties to meet and confer as to the issue regarding the specific communications that Plaintiffs seek to discover based on the crime-fraud exception, including the extent to which Plaintiffs’ counsel reviewed the materials and whether and how the Court may rule on such motion given the proscription against using the privileged material itself as a basis to prove the exception. 

           

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendants’ counsel has not waived privilege with respect to the inadvertently disclosed documents.   

 

It is so ordered.

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JIANGXI DACHENG ELECTROMECHANICAL EQUIPMENT IMPORT AND EXPORT CO. LTD

 

         vs.

 

CHW TRADING, INC., et al.

 

 Case No.:  24STCV03098

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 6, 2024

 

 

CHW Parties’ demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

            On 2/6/2024, Plaintiff Jiangxi Dacheng Electromechnical Equipment Import and Export Co. Ltd. filed suit against CHW Trading, Inc., Chundi Liu aka Chun Di Liu, Catwalk to Sidewalk Inc., and Kyong Won Kang, alleging: (1) fraud/misrepresentation; (2) breach of contract; and (3) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

            Now, Defendants CHW Trading, Inc. (CHW) and Chundi Liu aka Chun Di Liu (Liu) (collectively, Defendants or CHW Parties) demur to the first cause of action. Defendants also move to strike portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s fraud claim is barred by the statute of limitations and fails to allege sufficient facts to state a claim as to Defendants.

 

            As to the first contention, the statute of limitations for a fraud claim in California is three years from the date of injury. (CCP § 338, subd. (d).) The “discovery rule” may sometimes postpone accrual of a fraud cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action. (Fox v. Ethicon EndoSurgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 806.)

 

            Here, the events upon which Plaintiff’s first cause of action is based on events which are alleged to have begun in June 2019, when the first Sales Contract was executed. However, Plaintiff alleges that from 2020 to 2022, Plaintiff began urging the Defendants to pay the outstanding balance allegedly owed but that the parties “used various excuses and refused to make the payment.” (Complaint ¶ 29.) Accordingly, accepted as true at the pleading stage, Plaintiff has alleged facts which could show that it did not have reason to discover the fraud until 2022 when emails “between the parties and the phone conversation with Kang show[ed] that CHW Trading and Catwalk to Sidewalk had fraudulent intentions not to pay the outstanding balance and that they had no intention to pay for the goods when they entered into the agreements with Dacheng.” (Complaint ¶ 30.) While Defendants may dispute that this was the first opportunity for discovery, that is a factual determination not properly decided at this stage.

 

            As for the second contention, fraud’s “particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” Id. at 645. Fraud “must be pleaded in specific language descriptive of the acts which are relied upon to constitute fraud. It is not sufficient to allege it in general terms, or in terms which amount to mere conclusions.” (Fallbrook Pub. Util. Dist. v. Martin (1957) 151 Cal. App.2d 84, 94.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s alleges “Defendants made a false representation to Plaintiff in entering these three agreements because Defendants intended to first induce Plaintiff to ship the goods without paying Plaintiff in full at the time when a such promise was made and then induce Plaintiff in believing that Defendants would pay for the goods in full. As such, and induced by this false promise, Plaintiff shipped the goods to Defendants under the impression that Defendants would pay the goods in full pursuant to the three agreements.” (Complaint ¶ 35.)

 

In its demurrer, Defendants argue that these allegations are insufficient because Plaintiff alleges that it entered into agreements with Catwalk, not the CHW parties. However, Plaintiff clearly alleges that it negotiated to enter into the agreement with CHW, and “[a]s CHW Trading did not have sufficient credit line to sign a $500,000 worth of contract, it informed Dacheng that it asked Catwalk to Sidewalk to sign the contract on its behalf. Catwalk to Sidewalk had sufficient credit line to sign a $500,000 worth of contract.” (Complaint ¶ 23.)

 

While CHW may dispute this as true, the Court accepts well-pled allegations as true at the pleadings stage. Accepted as true, Plaintiff alleges that CHW knowingly induced Plaintiff to enter into an agreement with Catwalk acting on its behalf, CHW had no intention to perform under the agreement, accepted goods pursuant to the agreement, and then refused to pay.  This is sufficiently specific at the pleadings stage to state a claim for fraud. 

 

Based on the foregoing, CHW Parties’ demurrer is overruled.  Given this conclusion, Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to show fraud, malice, or oppression. (Civil Code § 3294.) Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to strike is denied.

 

It is so ordered.
