DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV43795    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Antonio Calderon, Ana Canchola, and Frank G. Martinez filed this action against Defendant, SCE for damages arising out of an electrical explosion and fire in an underground high-voltage vault.  Plaintiffs’ operative complaint is their Second Amended Complaint, which was filed on 5/08/23. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Additional IME

a.     Parties’ Positions

Hampton Teddy moves to compel Plaintiff to sit for a defense medical examination with a burn specialist.  It contends litigation has made clear that Plaintiff’s injuries are complex in nature, such that this additional examination is necessary.  It concedes it has conducted orthopedic and neuropsychological examinations of Plaintiff, but contends an examination with a plastic surgeon who specializes in burns is necessary to fully evaluate Plaintiff’s claimed damages. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  He argues Cross-Defendant’s orthopedic expert was retained to examine his burns, and has already opined concerning his burns.  He contends Cross-Defendant is dissatisfied with the orthopedic expert’s conclusions and is seeking a second bite at the proverbial apple by way of this motion.  Additionally, he contends the proposed examination does not comply with CCP §2023.310(b).

 

Hampton Teddy, in reply, contends its orthopedic surgeon viewed and examined the burns, but is not qualified to testify concerning the future care contemplated by Plaintiff in connection with his burns.  HT notes that Plaintiff has retained a burn expert, and notes that its orthopedist’s notes in connection with Plaintiff’s burns do not constitute an examination of the burns.  It contends the proposed examination complies with the Code and should be permitted to go forward. 

 

b.     Law Governing Additional IMEs in Personal Injury Lawsuits

Except for defense physicals in personal injury cases (in which one examination is permitted as a matter of course) and exams arranged by stipulation, a court order is required for a physical or mental examination. Such order may be made only after notice and hearing, and for “good cause shown.”  (CCP §2032.320(a).) 

 

The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action.  (CCP §2032.020(a).)  This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation.  Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337.  Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.”  See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837, wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer.  Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged. 

 

Where the plaintiff's injuries are complex, several exams may be necessary by specialists in different fields. There is no limit on the number of physical or mental exams that may be ordered on a showing of good cause.  The good cause requirement checks any potential harassment of the plaintiff.  See Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255.

 

The burden is on the moving party to show (by declarations or other evidence) that the examinee's condition is “in controversy” in the action.  The moving party must also establish good cause for the examination(s) sought.  A court order for physical or mental examination must be based on a showing of “good cause” (CCP § 2032.320(a)): (1) relevancy to the subject matter; and (2) specific facts justifying discovery: i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere.  (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)  The purpose is to protect an examinee's privacy by preventing annoying “fishing expeditions” - i.e., one party may not compel another to undergo psychiatric testing “solely on the basis of speculation that something of interest may surface.”  (Id.)

 

The notice of motion must state the time, place, identity and speciality of the examiner, and the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination.”  (CCP § 2032.310(b).)  The requirement for specification of the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination” apparently requires disclosure of whatever diagnostic tests and procedures will be utilized (x-rays, blood and urine samples, etc.).  (See CCP § 2032.220(c).)  The notice of motion must be accompanied by a separate document setting forth the discovery request, the Declarations must state facts showing that “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to arrange the examination by stipulation was unsuccessful.  (CCP § 2032.310(b).) 

 

c.     Analysis

Plaintiff’s claimed injuries are clearly complex.  Importantly, Plaintiff is calling both a burn expert and an orthopedic expert.  Plaintiff cannot, under the circumstances, be heard to complain about sitting for defense IMEs with both burn and orthopedic experts. 

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s argument about the lack of evidence in support of the motion is unavailing, as it is common sense that a burn expert would need to examine the plaintiff in order to form competent opinions about necessary future care.  Notably, if Defendant’s retained expert did NOT examine Plaintiff, Plaintiff would likely seek to exclude the expert’s opinions at trial based on lack of foundation and lack of personal knowledge. 

 

The Court also finds it is common sense that an orthopedic expert may have reviewed and made notes about Plaintiff’s burns, but the orthopedic expert is not, by definition, a burn expert, and would lack the expert credentials to testify about future necessary burn care. 

 

While it would have been preferable for HT to have sought this examination sooner, the Court finds the examination is necessary and meets the test set forth above. 

 

d.     §2023.310(b)

Plaintiff argues the motion violates CCP §2023.310(b), in that it (a) improperly demands videotaping, (b) fails to identify specific body parts that will be the subject of the examination, and (c) fails to provide details concerning any tests or procedures to be performed. 

 

i.              Videotape

Plaintiff is correct that neither the Code nor case law permits videotaping of a defense medical examination.  California Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.530 explicitly allows for the audio recording of mental examinations but does not mention videotaping.  Case law further supports this interpretation.  In   Edmiston v. Superior Court, the California Supreme Court held that the statute providing for court-ordered medical examinations does not authorize videotaping, and the imposition of such a condition was improper.  Edmiston v. Superior Court (1978) 22 Cal.3d 699. The court emphasized that the legislature had directed that medical examinations be reported by means other than videotaping.  Id.  Similarly, in   Ramirez v. MacAdam, the court reiterated that the Civil Discovery Act does not permit videotaping of physical examinations, and any change to allow videotaping would need to be determined by the legislature.  Ramirez v. MacAdam (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 1638.  Additionally, in Randy's Trucking, Inc. v. Superior Court of Kern County, the court prohibited videotaping of a neuropsychological examination, instead allowing for audio recording to ensure the examiner adheres to the court's parameters.  Randy's Trucking, Inc. v. Superior Court of Kern County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 818.

 

Cross-Defendant’s reply is silent in this regard.  Cross-Defendant must remove the demand for videotaping from the description of the IME.

 

ii.             Specific Body Parts

§2032.310(a) requires the party seeking the examination to specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.  Plaintiff contends this means the demand must indicate the body parts to be examined.  Plaintiff cites Carpenter v. Superior Court (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 249 in support of his position.  Nothing in Carpenter, however, mentions body parts.  TH’s demand indicates the examination will be of “the body parts to which Plaintiff sustained burns as a result of the incident, including but not limited to Plaintiff’s upper extremities, including face, forearms/hands and head.”  The Court finds this is sufficient to “specify” the scope of the examination; the examination will be of the face, forearms/hands, and head, and if it comes to light that there are burns on other areas, those areas will also be examined.

 

iii.            Tests and Procedures

Plaintiff also complains that the demand does not indicate what tests and procedures will be performed.  Carpenter clearly requires this disclosure in the demand.  It appears, however, that this will be a physical examination without tests and/or procedures.  The expert may not conduct any tests or procedures that are not disclosed in the demand.

 

iv.            Lost Wages and Expenses

Finally, Plaintiff contends he will lose a day’s wages and will have to incur expenses if he is required to sit for this additional examination.  He seeks an order that TH be required to pay these expenses. 

 

CCP §2032.320(e) provides:

(e) If the place of the examination is more than 75 miles from the residence of the person to be examined, an order to submit to it shall be entered only if both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) The court determines that there is good cause for the travel involved.

(2) The order is conditioned on the advancement by the moving party of the reasonable expenses and costs to the examinee for travel to the place of examination.

 

The proposed examination is slightly more than 75 miles from Plaintiff’s home.  The Court finds there is good cause to permit HT to have the examination conducted at the office of its chosen retained expert.  HT must advance the expenses and costs associated with travel to the place of examination.  Plaintiff cites no authority for the position that this would include lost wages, which are not “costs…for travel.”   The request for lost wages is therefore denied.

 

e.     Dr. Light

As a final note, the parties discuss whether or not Dr. Light violated the parties’ stipulation in connection with Plaintiff’s neurological examination.  The issue is not before the Court and the Court is not making any findings in this regard.

 

f.      Conclusion

The motion is granted.  The examination is set for 1/07/25 as per the notice of motion.  The details of the examination are per the notice of motion, with the exception of the fact that the examination cannot be videotaped.  If the parties have issues relating to the specifics of the examination, they must meet and confer in good faith to resolve the issues without the need for additional court guidance.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Jennifer Small filed this suit against numerous defendants for damages arising out of an assault by a third party at Plaintiff’s apartment building.  The specifics of the assault will be discussed in detail below.    

 

Plaintiff’s operative First Amended Complaint includes causes of action for negligence, breach of contract (Civil Code §1941.3(c)), breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and breach of the implied warranty of habitability.  Plaintiff’s complaint includes a prayer for punitive damages. 

 

2.     Defendants’ Facts

Many of the facts relating to this motion are undisputed.  If a fact is disputed, the Court will present each party’s contention in this statement. 

 

Plaintiff was a tenant in Defendants’ two-story, four-unit building in Avalon; her tenancy commenced in February of 2000.  Philip Lopez, whose girlfriend, Sandra Gutierrez, attacked Plaintiff, had been a tenant in the building since December of 2019.  Plaintiff was in Unit B, which was downstairs, and Lopez occupied Unit C, which was upstairs.  In order to access the upstairs units, Lopez had to use an outdoor walkway that passed about ten feet from the front door of Plaintiff’s unit.    

 

Gutierrez was Lopez’s girlfriend and sometimes stayed in his apartment, but was not on the lease and was not a tenant in the building.  Lopez and Gutierrez caused a noise disturbance on 8/12/21, which caused Plaintiff to call the sheriff.  Plaintiff called about another such disturbance on 8/22/21.  Plaintiff called about other noise disturbances on 8/23/21 and 8/28/21. 

 

Plaintiff texted Defendants’ agent about the incidents, and the agent said to contact the police if there was a need to do so.  Plaintiff indicated she would be staying inside from now on.  Defendants’ agent went to the sheriff’s department and signed a trespass arrest authorization, which authorized the department to arrest Gutierrez for trespass if she was on the property without consent or lawful reason.  There were no further noise or other incidents. 

 

On 9/22/21, Plaintiff was returning to her apartment after going on a walk around 5:20 p.m., when she heard Gutierrez, who appeared to be on something.  She contacted Defendants’ agent, who advised her to call the police.  She called the sheriff’s department from inside her unit with the door closed and locked.  While still on the phone, she unlocked her door and stepped outside; she was recording with her iPad.  Gutierrez came downstairs, the call with the sheriff ended, and Gutierrez then struck Plaintiff multiple times while Plaintiff was on her own walkway.  Lopez pulled Gutierrez off of Plaintiff.  The sheriff’s department arrived less than two minutes later. 

 

3.     Plaintiff’s Facts

Plaintiff submitted her own statement of facts with his opposition papers, and Defendants submitted a response with their reply.  Those additional facts consist of a series of facts about habitability issues on the property.  Plaintiff also provides evidence that Defendants knew Lopez was arrested in August of 2021 (Defendants dispute this and say Gutierrez, not Lopez, was arrested, which appears to be correct) for being under the influence and disturbing the peace, and that all the tenants reported to Defendants that 40-50 people were coming and going from Lopez’s apartment for five minutes at a time throughout the night.  Plaintiff provides evidence that both Plaintiff and Defendants understood Lopez to be selling drugs out of his apartment.  Plaintiff reported to Defendants’ agent, on 9/16/21, that the tenant in Unit D had gotten into a violent fight the night before, which rendered her scared to leave her apartment.  Gutierrez had been spending the night OUTSIDE of Lopez’s apartment, in the common area, starting on 8/10/21.  While staying in the common area, Gutierrez yells and screams and had thrown objects at residents, including Plaintiff. 

 

Gutierrez was sometimes entering the property because Lopez let her in, and was sometimes entering the property because the entry door was unsecured.  Gutierrez was arrested on the property on 8/28/21.    

 

4.     Evidentiary Objections

Defendants filed objections with their reply papers.  The objections fail to comply with CRC 3.1354(b)(3), which requires the objecting party to quote or set forth the objectionable statement or material.  Notably, this is a requirement in addition to the requirement, per 3.1354(b)(2), that the objecting party state the exhibit, title, page, and line number of the material objected to.  Defendants complied with (b)(2), but not (b)(3). 

 

Compounding the matter, Defendants arguably complied with 3.1354(b)(4), which requires the objecting party to “state the grounds for each objection.”  Defendants did so, however, by citing the Evidence Code sections pursuant to which they are objecting, rather than using more typical terms such as “relevance,” “hearsay,” “lack of foundation,” etc.

 

For the Court to meaningfully rule on the objections, the Court would have to go back and forth between the objections themselves, Plaintiff’s submitted evidence, and the Evidence Code.  The purpose of the Rules of Court is to simplify this process so the Court can review ONE document when ruling on asserted objections.

 

The Court declines to rule on the objections in light of the defect. 

 

5.     Analysis

a.     Summary Adjudication

Defendants seek summary adjudication of five issues.  Defendants, in their separate statement, list forty facts that they contend entitle them to judgment as a matter of law on the first cause of action for negligence.  They then indicate those same forty facts entitle them to judgment as a matter of law on each of the other four issues.

 

CRC 3.1350(d)(2) makes clear that the separate statement must include only facts pertinent to disposition of the motion.  Plaintiff’s negligence cause of action is based on the assertion that Defendants failed to evict Lopez, failed to hire security guards, and failed to secure doors.  This is not the same as the allegations that support each of the other three causes of action.  The second cause of action is premised on lack of locking mechanisms.  The third cause of action is premised on Defendants’ maintenance of nuisance conditions on the property.  The fourth cause of action is premised on Defendants’ failure to property maintain common areas and secure the property. 

 

The Court will not pick through the separate statement and determine which facts apply to which issues to be adjudicated.  The motion will therefore either be granted or denied in its entirety, and summary adjudication will not be entertained. 

 

a.     Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

b.     Negligence

Plaintiff’s operative First Amended Complaint alleges Defendants were negligent because they failed to secure the locks and hinges on the outside door, hire a security guard, and/or evict Lopez from the property. 

 

Defendants’ moving papers argue they had no duty to evict Lopez, no duty to hire a security guard, and no duty to install additional lighting.  Defendants’ moving papers, as they relate to the cause of action for negligence, never mention or discuss Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendants were obligated to secure the locks and hinges on the outside door.  Defendants therefore failed to meet their moving burden to establish Plaintiff’s first cause of action fails as a matter of law.  The motion for summary adjudication of the first cause of action is denied.    

 

c.     Second Cause of Action, Violation of Civil Code §1941.3(c)

In an abundance of caution, the Court has considered Plaintiff’s second cause of action.  The Court has done so because the second cause of action is premised on Defendants’ failure to ensure all locks complied with the Civil Code.  This cause of action therefore overlaps with Plaintiff’s theory in connection with the first cause of action, which Defendants failed to address in their moving papers in connection with the first cause of action.

 

Defendants, in their motion for summary adjudication of the second cause of action, argue Plaintiff cannot prevail on the cause of action because proper locking mechanisms would not have prevented the subject attack.  Defendants do not, however, attempt to negate Plaintiff’s allegation that the locks were not working.  Defendants fail to provide authority for the position that a cause of action for violation of Civil Code §1941.3(c) cannot be pled if the violation of the section does not give rise to specified damages.  The Code is notably silent in this regard.  Absent on point authority, the Court finds Defendants failed to meet their burden in this regard either. 

 

d.     Additional Issues

As noted above, the Court declines to rule on the alternative motion for summary adjudication, as Defendants’ separate statement fails to comply with the Rules of Court.

 

e.     Conclusion

Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied.  Their alternative motion for summary adjudication is also denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV00491    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Cary Atwood initiated this action against Defendants, Allyn Emery and Loma Law Group, APC on 3/15/23.  Plaintiff’s original complaint included causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty, conversion, unjust enrichment, breach of contract, equitable estoppel, quantum meruit, and accounting.  The crux of the original complaint was that Plaintiff agreed to generate cases for Defendants, and Defendant would pay her for those referrals.  Defendants failed to pay, resulting in a balance due in excess of $400,000.

 

Defendants intended to challenge the original complaint, and the parties’ meet and confer efforts resulted in the filing of a First Amended Complaint.  The FAC was filed on 8/18/23, and the FAC added Lyran Consulting, Inc. as an additional plaintiff.  The same causes of action from the original complaint were pled, and the main difference from the original complaint to the FAC was that the FAC pleads the existence of two contracts, one between Atwood and Defendants, and the other between Lyran and Defendants.   

 

On 3/26/24, the Court sustained a demurrer to the FAC.  The Court granted leave in part and denied leave in part.  On 4/15/24, Plaintiff filed the Second Amended Complaint.  The SAC omits any claims by Lyran, and asserts only claims for breach of contract and quantum meriut on behalf of Atwood.

 

  1. Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares the parties met and conferred extensively before Defendants filed their demurrer.  The meet and confer requirement is satisfied. 

 

c.     Requests for Judicial Notice

Defendants seek judicial notice of various documents from the Secretary of State’s website concerning Lyran Consulting, Inc.  The RJN is granted.

 

d.     First Cause of Action, Breach of Contract

In connection with Defendants’ demurrer to the First Amended Complaint, the Court ruled, in pertinent part:

As a related argument, Defendants argue Lyran is not a California corporation and therefore cannot maintain its lawsuit against Defendants.  Defendants cite Corporations Code §§2105 and 2203 in support of this position.  §2203(c) provides that a foreign corporation “shall not maintain any action or proceeding upon any intrastate business so transacted in any court of this state” unless it complies with certain requirements.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, argue there is no proof that Lyran is not currently in good standing.  The judicially noticeable documents, however, show that, as of 10/18/23, Lyran’s status was “terminated.”  In an abundance of caution, the Court reviewed the current Secretary of State website, and it continues to show Lyran’s status as “terminated.” 

 

The demurrer to Lyran’s cause of action for breach of contract is sustained.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiffs intend to and do revive Lyran’s status with the Secretary of State.

 

Plaintiffs, instead of alleging Lyran’s status was revived, omitted claims by Lyran and asserted only claims by Atwood.  The causes are premised on the allegation that Lyran assigned all claims against Defendants to Atwood. 

 

Defendants demur to the breach of contract cause of action, correctly noting that the assignee of a terminated California corporation stands in the shoes of the terminated California corporation and therefore is also prevented from suing based on the corporation’s lack of registration to do business in California. 

 

Under California law, a foreign corporation that transacts intrastate business without obtaining a certificate of qualification from the Secretary of State cannot maintain an action in California courts until it complies with the qualification requirements and pays the necessary fees and penalties (The Capital Gold Group, Inc. v. Nortier, 176 Cal.App.4th 1119 (2009)).  This rule also applies to assignees of such foreign corporations. Specifically, an assignee for collection purposes of a foreign corporation that has not complied with the requirements for doing business in California is also barred from suing (Thorner v. Selective Cam Transmission Co., 180 Cal.App.2d 89 (1960)).

 

In the case of   Thorner v. Selective Cam Transmission Co., the court held that an assignee of a foreign corporation is barred from suing if the corporation itself would be barred for failing to comply with the requirements for doing business in the state (Thorner v. Selective Cam Transmission Co., 180 Cal.App.2d 89 (1960)), (Cal-Western Business Services, Inc. v. Corning Capital Group, 221 Cal.App.4th 304 (2013))[3]. This principle was reaffirmed in Cal-Western Business Services, Inc. v. Corning Capital Group, where the court emphasized that allowing an assignee to sue would frustrate the purpose of the statute (Cal-Western Business Services, Inc. v. Corning Capital Group, 221 Cal.App.4th 304 (2013)).

 

Therefore, if the foreign corporation has not obtained the necessary certificate of qualification to do business in California, its assignee would also be barred from maintaining an action in California courts.  Defendants’ judicially noticeable documents establish Lyran is not qualified to do business in California, and therefore Atwood’s claims against Defendants are precluded.  The demurrer to the breach of contract cause of action is sustained.  Because this is the Second Amended Complaint, because the demurrer is unopposed, and because this is a purely legal issue, leave to amend is denied. 

e.     Second Cause of Action, Quantum Meruit

Plaintiff’s quantum meruit cause of action rises and falls with the breach of contract cause of action.  See White Lighting Co. v. Woolfson, 68 Cal.2d 336, 346 (1968).  The demurrer to the second cause of action is therefore also sustained.  Again, in light of the lack of opposition and the fact that this is a purely legal issue, leave to amend is denied.

Case Number: 24LBCV00790    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, OOCL (USA), Inc., as agent for Orient Overseas Container Line, filed this action against Defendant, Fenix Marine Services Ltd., a Delaware Corporation, for common counts.  Plaintiff filed its complaint on 4/17/24.

 

  1. Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted

a.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant propounded RFAs on Plaintiff on 8/27/24.  Defendant granted Plaintiff an extension of time to respond through 10/16/24.  On 10/16/24, Plaintiff asked for a couple of additional weeks, but Defendant refused.  On 11/04/24, Defendant filed this motion to deem RFAs admitted.

 

Opposition to the motion was due on or before 12/04/24.  On 12/06/24, Plaintiff’s attorney filed an untimely declaration in opposition to the motion.  Counsel declares he served responses on 11/07/24, but because that response appears not to have been effective, he is re-serving the responses by first class mail concurrently with his declaration. 

 

Defendant filed timely reply papers on 12/10/24.  Defendant argues the motion should be substantively granted because Plaintiff’s untimely responses are not in substantial compliance with the Code.  It seeks, in the alternative, an order compelling Plaintiff to serve fully code-compliant responses to the subject RFAs. 

 

b.     Analysis

CCP §2033.280(c) requires the Court to deem RFAs admitted if the responding party fails to serve a response “in substantial compliance with the Code” prior to the hearing.  In response to numerous RFAs, Plaintiff stated that the RFA is neither admitted nor denied, then gave an explanation for why it is neither admitted nor denied. 

 

§2033.220 provides:

(a) Each answer in a response to requests for admission shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.

(b) Each answer shall:

(1) Admit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true, either as expressed in the request itself or as reasonably and clearly qualified by the responding party.

(2) Deny so much of the matter involved in the request as is untrue.

(3) Specify so much of the matter involved in the request as to the truth of which the responding party lacks sufficient information or knowledge.

(c) If a responding party gives lack of information or knowledge as a reason for a failure to admit all or part of a request for admission, that party shall state in the answer that a reasonable inquiry concerning the matter in the particular request has been made, and that the information known or readily obtainable is insufficient to enable that party to admit the matter.

 

Defendant is correct in noting that Plaintiff’s responses, which state the RFAs are neither admitted nor denied and then give a reason why, are not code-compliant.  The responses do not fall into any of the above categories. 

 

The Court finds, however, that deeming them admitted would be unduly harsh.  As noted above, the motion must be denied if the requests are in “substantial compliance with the Code.”  There is no case law interpreting this mandate, but the Court finds it means the motion must be denied even if the responses are less than perfect, as “substantial compliance” would lack meaning otherwise. 

 

The motion to deem the RFAs admitted is denied.  Plaintiff must, however, serve supplemental responses in full compliance with the Code within ten days. 

 

Sanctions are not sought and therefore none are imposed.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.
