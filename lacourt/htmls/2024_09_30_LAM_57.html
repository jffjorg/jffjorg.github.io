DEPARTMENT 57 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Inform the clerk if you submit on the tentative ruling. If moving and opposing parties submit, no appearance is necessary.

Case Number: 20STCV48692    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 57

Hicham Zerhouni ("Zerhouni") and Wafae Bencherkroun (collectively, "the Plaintiffs") owned a 2016 Mercedes Benz S550 ("the Mercedes"), which was insured under an automobile insurance policy ("the Policy") issued by State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company ("State Farm").   In March 2020, the Plaintiffs authorized Adam Lowe, a friend of Zerhouni's, to drive the Mercedes.  On March 26, 2020, Lowe informed the Plaintiffs that, while driving the Mercedes, he was involved in a collision in which the Mercedes rolled over ("the "Crash").   The next day, the Plaintiffs made a claim to State Farm under the Policy for coverage of the damage to the Mercedes that resulted from the Crash.  

 

After investigating the Crash, State Farm denied coverage.  In denying coverage, State Farm concluded that (1) the Crash did not occur as reported and Plaintiff's claimed losses arising from the Crash thus were not covered under the Policy, and (2) Plaintiffs breached their obligations under the Policy by misrepresenting and concealing information that was material to State Farm's investigation.   Following State Farm's denial of coverage, the Plaintiffs sued State Farm.  Their operative Second Amended Complaint ("SAC") asserts causes of action against State Farm for (1) breach of contract in denying coverage under the Policy for the Crash, and (2) bad faith denial of coverage.  Also, as relevant here, Plaintiffs are seeking an award of punitive damages on their claim for bad faith denial of coverage.  Pending before the Court is the Plaintiffs' motion for an order pursuant to Civil Code Section 3295(c) permitting them to conduct pretrial discovery into profits State Farm earned from its its allegedly wrongful course of conduct in denying the Plaintiffs' claim and State Farm's financial condition. (All statutory references herein are to the Civil Code.) That pretrial discovery is intended to provide evidence under Section 3294 bearing on the amount of punitive damages the jury could award Plaintiffs if the jury first determines that State Farm acted maliciously, fraudulently, or with oppression in denying Plaintiffs' claim. The Court is denying Plaintiffs' Section 3295(c) motion. The general rule in Section 3295(c) is that pretrial discovery into the defendant's profits generated from allegedly bad acts and the defendant's financial condition is not allowed for purposes of a claim under Section 3294 that the defendant is liable for punitive damages. Section 3295(c) contains an exception to that rule for circumstances when, upon a motion by the plaintiff and based on affidavits presented by both the plaintiff and the defendant, "the court finds . .. that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim [for punitive damages] pursuant to Section 3294. The phrase "substantial probability" for purposes of Section 3295(c) motions has been interpreted to mean "very likely," or "a strong likelihood," which is a higher standard of proof tha "more likely than not," which corresponds to the preponderance of evidence standard that generally applies in civil litigation and that would have applied to Section 3295(c) motions as well had the Legislature used the phrase "reasonable probability," or simply the word "probability." (Jabro v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 754, 758 ["Jabro"].) In determining whether the plaintiff has satisfied the substantial probability standard, the court is to weigh the competing affidavits submitted in connection with the Section 3295(c) motion. (Ibid.) In deciding the Plaintiffs' Section 3295(c) motion in this case, this Court has evaluated the affidavits of Hicham Zerhouni and David Easton that the Plaintiffs submitted in support of their motion. Zerhouni's declaration is the same one that the Plaintiffs submitted in support of their successful opposition to State Farm's motion for summary judgement. The Court considered the Easton Declaration even though, as State Farm correctly observed in objections to it, Plaintiffs did not timely file the Easton Declaration. The Court is overruling State Farm's other objections to the Easton Declaration. The Court notes that Plaintiffs failed to file a reply brief in support of their motion. State Farm apparently was served with the reply brief because State Farm objected to it on the ground that it was untimely filed. The reply brief is not on the Court's docket in the case. On the other side of the ledger, the Court evaluated the affidavit of Ashley Troche and excerpts from the deposition transcripts of Amanda O'Berry and Lola Hogan attached to the affidavit of Angela Martin that State Farm submitted along with its opposition to the Plaintiffs' motion. Having weighed the competing affidavits submitted by both sides, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have failed to establish that there is a substantial probability that they will prevail on their Section 3294 claim that State Farm acted maliciously, fraudulently, and with oppression and therefore is liable to Plaintiffs for punitive damages. In denying State Farm's summary judgment motion earlier in this case, the Court determined that Zerhouni's declaration alone created a disputed issue of material fact that precluded entry of summary judgment for State Farm on Plaintiff's claims for bad faith denial of coverage and punitive damages. Had the Easton Declaration been part of the Plaintiffs' package in opposition to State Farm's summary judgment motion, there would have been even more disputed facts precluding entry of summary judgment. But the existence of disputed issues of material fact on whether State Farm could be liable for punitive damages is not the same thing as facts establishing a substantial probability that State Farm will be held for liable punitive damages. (Jabro, supra, 95 Cal.App.4th at p. 758 [noting distinction between summary judgement standard and Section 3295(c) standard].) The Plaintiffs were able to fend off summary judgment on their entitlement to punitive damages against State Farm. They have not shown, however, that there is a substantial probability that a jury will find them entitled to such an award.

Case Number: 23STCV16049    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 57

The unopposed motion of Plaintiff Firestone Plaza, LLC ("Firestone") for an award of $19,740 in attorney's fees it incurred in connection with this action against Defendants George Subrai and Prabhjyoti Subrai (collectively, "the Subrais") is GRANTED.  Firestone prevailed in its breach of contract action against the Subrais.  The parties' contract states that the party who prevails in an action on the contract is entitled to recover its reasonable attorney's fees.  In light of that provision in the contract, Firestone is entitled to its reasonable attorney's fees under Civil Code 1717.  In the Court's view, the hourly billing rates of Firestone's attorneys Rosalinda V. Amash and Jerid R. Maybaum are reasonable and the amount of time that Ms. Amash and Mr. Maybaum say they billed in this action is reasonable as well.  

Case Number: 24STCV11428    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 57

 

Plaintiff Nader Ghadoushi (“Ghadoushi”) sued  Defendants Secured Properties, Inc. (“Secured Properties”), Ryan Yatman (“Ryan”), Sherry Yatman (“Sherry”), and Paymen Rahimi (“Rahimi”).[1] Ghadoushi’s Complaint alleges that he entered into an agreement with Secured Properties in 2017 pursuant to which Ghadoushi, as an associate licensee, was to receive 70% of all fees or commissions on certain sales or leases of real property that Secured Properties brokered (“Commission Agreement”).  The Complaint further alleges that in August 2022, Securities Properties, as a broker, closed escrow on the sale of real property located at 3251 West 6th Street in Los Angeles (“the Subject Property”) that fell with the ambit of the Commission Agreement.  The total commissions for the sale of the Subject Property sale were $293,125,000.  Thus, per the Commission Agreement, Ghadoushi alleges that he was supposed to receive 70% of that figure, or $205,187.50.  Ghadoushi alleges, however, that Secured Properties, through its directors, officers, and  alter egos Ryan and Chu, paid Ghadoushi only $97,708,33 and distributed the rest to Chu, Sherry, and Rahimi.  As pertinent here, the Complaint alleges that Secured Properties, Chu, and Ryan paid to Rahimi $100,000 of the amount owed to Ghadoushi.  

Pending before the Court is the demurrer of Rahimi to the third and fourth causes of action in the Complaint, which are for money had and received and conversion, respectively, and are asserted against all of the Defendants.   Rahimi contends in the demurrer that the Complaint fails to allege sufficient facts to establish those causes of action against him.  Rahimi also has moved to strike Paragraph 65 of the Complaint and Paragraph 3 in the Complaint’s Prayer for Relief, which together relate to Ghadoushi’s request for an award punitive damages against Rahimi on Ghadoushi’s conversion claim.   Rahimi contends in the motion to strike the allegations in the Complaint are insufficient to support such an award. 

The Court is overruling the demurrer and denying the motion to strike.  Rahimi is directed to file an answer to the Complaint by October 14, 2024.

 DEMURRER

    A. Money Had and Received

“A cause of action for money had and received is stated if it is alleged that the defendant ‘is indebted to the plaintiff in a certain sum ‘for money had and received by the defendant for the use of the plaintiff.’”  (Avidor v. Sutter’s Place, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1439, 1454, internal citations omitted [“Avidor”].)   The cause of action is “viable” when the defendant has received money which belongs to the plaintiff, and which  “equity and good conscience” dictate should be paid by the defendant to the plaintiff.  (Ibid; Gutierrez v. Girardi (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 925, 937.)  Based on precedent specifying the elements of a cause of action for money had and received, juries are instructed in CACI No. 370  that the plaintiff must prove that the defendant received money “intended to be used for the benefit of [the plaintiff],” that the money was not used for the plaintiff's benefit, and that the defendant has not given the money to the plaintiff.”  (Avidor, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 1453, internal citations omitted.) 

In the Court’s view, the Complaint adequately alleges the elements of a cause of action for money had and received against Rahimi.  Specifically, the Complaint alleges that Rahimi owes a certain sum of money, $100,000, to Ghadoushi that was intended for Ghadoushi and that Rahimi has not given to Ghadoushi.


Rahimi argues that the Complaint fails to allege that the Commission Agreement binds him and therefore he is not indebted to Ghadoushi.   Rahimi’s argument misses the mark.  Whether Rahimi is a party to the Commission Agreement is not dispositive.  What matters is whether Rahimi has money that belongs Ghadoushi and has not given that money to Ghadoushi.  The Complaint alleges as much and thus withstands Rahimi’s demurrer.

 Also unavailing is Rahimi’s assertion in the demurrer that the money he received reflected the payment by Secured Properties, Ryan, and Chu of a debt they owed on a loan he made to them.  Perhaps that assertion will prove to be true in due course in this action.  But when ruling on a demurrer, the Court cannot consider matters based on evidence outside of the complaint.  (Mt. Hawley Ins. Co. v. Lopez (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1385, 1426).  Rahim’s contention that the money he received was owed to him is such a matter – the Complaint makes no mention of it -- and thus is beyond the Court’s purview at this time.


B. B   Conversion

 “Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant's conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property right; and (3) [resulting] damages.”  (IIG Wireless, Inc. v. Yi (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 630, 650.)  While a wrongful intent is not required, the act constituting conversion must be knowing or intentional.  (Taylor v. Forte Hotels Int’l (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 1119, 1124.) “Money can be the subject of an action for conversion if a specific sum capable of identification is involved.” (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 452, 61 Cal.Rptr.2d 707; see also (PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 395.)  

 

In the Court’s view, the elements of conversion are met here.  The Complaint alleges that Ghadoushi had a right to possess property in the form of money under the Commission Agreement, Rahimi wrongfully and knowingly took possession of $100,000 worth of that money, and Ghadoushi was damaged because Rahimi has not returned the money to Ghadoushi.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 18-20, 25, 57, 61-63, 65.)  Rahimi’s argument that the Complaint fails to allege that he did anything wrong is mistaken.  Paragraphs 57 and 65 accuse him of wrongfully and knowingly taking money that belongs to Ghadoushi.[2]

 

MOTION TO STRIKE        

  Punitive damages may be recovered on a successful conversion claim if the plaintiff proves that the defendant acted with malice, fraud, or oppression in converting the plaintiff’s property.  (Krusi v. Bear, Stearns & Co. (1983) 144 Cal.App.3d 664, 678.)  In considering a motion to strike allegations bearing on punitive damages, the court must consider those allegations holistically and in light of the asserted factual context of the entire complaint.  (Perkins v. Superior Court (1981) 117 Cal.App.3d 1, 6; Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255

 

The allegations in Ghadoushi’s Complaint against Rahimi, if true, would support an award of punitive damages against Rahimi on the conversion claim.  The Complaint alleges that the Rahimi conspired with the other Defendants to maliciously and oppressively deprive Ghadoushi of money that rightfully belonged to Ghadoushi.   When the allegations directly related to punitive damages are considered against the backdrop of the Complaint as a whole, there is enough therein on which a jury could reasonably conclude that punitive damages should be assessed against Rahimi.

 


[1] Because Ryan Yatman and Sherry Yatman have the same last name, the Court will refer to them herein by the first names to avoid confusion.  No disrespect is intended.

[2] Paragraph 25 of the Complaint alleges that Rahimi received $100,000 of the money that was owed to Ghadoushi.  Paragraph 57 alleges that Rahimi received “up to” $100,000 of that money.  The lack of certainty in the exact amount of money converted by Rahimi in Paragraph 57 may run counter to the requirement referenced above that a claim for conversion of money must allege a specific sum.   Rahimi does not advance this argument in the demurrer, however.  In any event, the certainty of the sum converted in Paragraph 25 would seemingly satisfy the specificity requirement.
