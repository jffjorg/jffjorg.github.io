DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV01740    Hearing Date: January 22, 2024    Dept: W

JOSEPH SHRAUGER, et al. v. CEP AMERICA, et al.

 

DEFENDANTS MATTHEW LARREW, D.O. AND CEP AMERICA-CALIFORNIA’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        January 22, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22STCV01740

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Matthew Larrew, D.O. and CEP America-California  

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 14, 2022, Plaintiffs Joseph Shrauger, individually, and as successor in interest and heir to decedent Joseph Shrauger, Priscilla Shrauger, individually, and as successor in interest and heir to decedent Joseph Shrauger, and Limbonia Arredondo, individually, and as successor in interest and heir to decedent Joseph Shraugher filed a complaint for elder and dependent abuse/neglect, willful misconduct, general negligence, professional negligence, unfair business practices, and violation of Patients’ Bill of Rights. Plaintiffs filed a second amended complaint on July 5, 2023.  

 

Plaintiffs allege decedent Joseph Shrauger (“Mr. Shrauger”) was admitted to Camarillo Healthcare Center on September 12, 2020 and remained there until October 19, 2020. Plaintiffs allege during Mr. Shrauger’s stay there, Camarillo and its employees wantonly, and recklessly ignored Mr. Shrauger’s custodial, medical, emotional, and human needs. Plaintiffs further allege Camarillo and its employees fraudulently misrepresented that they would provide necessary staff, medical care and services to their patients. As a result, Plaintiffs allege Mr. Shrauger died on October 25, 2020.

 

Plaintiffs dismissed several defendants on February 14, 2023. The complaint remains against CEP America, Inc., Dignity Health dba St. John’s Pleasant Valley Hospital, Camarillo Community Care Inc. dba Camarillo Healthcare Center, Matthew Larrew, DO, Vinod Valiveti, MD, Ivan Kirk T. Acayan, and Anthony Shrauger.

 

Plaintiffs named Albert Lopez as Doe Defendant 1, Irma Rodarte as Doe Defendant 2, Mary Shrauger as Doe Defendant 3, and Limbonia Alonzo as Doe Defendant 4.

 

Plaintiff and Dignity Health dba St. John’s Pleasant Valley Hospital entered into a good faith settlement on August 7, 2023. Plaintiff Limbonia Arredondo was dismissed on January 9, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Matthew Larrew, D.O. and CEP America-California Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Matthew Larrew, D.O. and CEP America-California move this court for summary judgment against the Plaintiffs Joseph Shrauger, individually, and as successor in interest and heir to decedent Joseph Shrauger; Priscilla Shrauger, individually, and as successor in interest and heir to decedent Joseph Shrauger; Limbonia Arredondo[1], individually, and as successor in interest and heir to decedent Joseph Shrauger, and in favor of Moving Defendants.

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

The elements of a professional negligence medical malpractice cause of action are: “(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional’s negligence.” (Borrayo v. Avery (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 304, 310 (quoting Hanson v. Grode (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 601, 606.)  Thus, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”¿¿(Munro v. Regents of University of California¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).)¿ An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.¿¿(Starr v.¿Mooslin¿(1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.)¿

 

Defendants argue there are no facts that Plaintiffs can allege to show that  Defendants breached their duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise with regard to the care and treatment of Decedent and because there was no negligent conduct, there is no way of showing a proximal causal connection between the alleged negligent conduct and Plaintiffs’ alleged injuries. To support this contention, Defendants present the declaration of Michael S. Ritter, M.D., a Board-Certified Emergency Room Physician. Dr. Ritter has been certified by the American Board of Emergency Medicine since 1995. (Ritter Decl. ¶1.) Since completing his residency, Dr. Ritters full-time practice has been as an Emergency Medicine specialist in a general acute hospital emergency department. (Ritter Decl. ¶2.) Dr. Ritter has made several publications in the field of emergency medicine and has spoken at numerous educational seminars. (Ritter Decl. ¶2.)

 

Dr. Ritter testifies based on his review of the medical records as well as his professional training, education, background, and experience, it is his opinion Dr. Larrew and CEP America-California complied with the applicable standard of care and there is no act or omission by either Dr. Larrew or CEP America-California that caused or contributed to Mr. Shrauger’s death. Dr. Ritter opines Dr. Larrew appropriately obtained a history of the patient’s condition and performed an appropriate physical examination. (Ritter Decl. ¶15.) Moreover, Dr. Larrew’s order for a diagnostic studies including, but not limited to head and abdominal CT scans, labs and abdominal paracentesis were all appropriate to rule in/out the cause of Mr. Shrauger’s altered level of consciousness. (Ritter Decl. ¶15.) Following receipt of the lab values and most of the diagnostic studies, Dr. Larrew appropriately and within the standard of care elected to request admission of the patient for further treatment, which was also appropriate given the potential diagnosis. (Ritter Decl. ¶16.) Thereafter, Mr. Shrauger was thereafter admitted to St. John’s Pleasant Valley Hospital. Dr. Larrew did not provide any care to the patient following admission. (Ritter Decl. ¶¶15, 16.) The final diagnosis for Mr. Shrauger was acute respiratory failure with hypoxia; suspicion of volume overload from IV fluids and pneumonia; septic shock, likely due to infection from pneumonia and possible UTI; anemia and encephalopathy; pneumonia and Urinary Tract Infection; acute kidney injury likely due to sepsis and then worsened by septic shock; elevated troponins likely due to sepsis and shock; hepatic encephalopathy; cirrhosis of the liver; and diabetes mellitus. (Ritter Decl. ¶17.) As a result, the actions of Matthew Larrew, D.O. and CEP met the standard of care in the treatment of the patient. (Ritter Decl. ¶18.)

 

.

 

Defendants have met their burden of showing, by expert declaration, they did not breach the standard of care or cause Plaintiff’s injury.  The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show, by contrary expert declaration, that a triable issue of fact exists. Plaintiff has failed to file an opposition. Therefore, Defendants are entitled to summary judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Matthew Larrew, D.O. and CEP America-California Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Case Number: 22VECV01096    Hearing Date: January 22, 2024    Dept: W

TANNETTE GATES v. warner center condominiums, et al.

 

DEFENDANT TOPA INSURANCE COMPANY’S DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S third AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        January 22, 2024                               Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01096

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Topa Insurance Company

Responding Party:     No opposition.

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Martino Decl. ¶4.)  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 3, 2022, Plaintiff Tannette Gates filed a complaint against Warner Center Condominiums, Ross Morgan & Company, Inc., Amber Hill, Valerie Vincent, Joanne Catran, Mimi Himes, Afsaneh Safaei, Arash Safaei, and Hippo Analytics, Inc. for damages arising out of a kitchen pipe exploding in the unit upstairs and water from the upstairs unit damaging Plaintiffs. On January 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint asserting causes of action for: (1) Breach of CCRs; (2) Enforcement of Equitable Servitudes; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (4) Nuisance; (5) Negligence; (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (7) Breach of Contract and Implied Covenant of Duty of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (8) Third-Party Beneficiary Breach of Contract; (9) Professional Negligence and (Negligent Misrepresentation (10) Violation of Business and Professional Code § 17200; (11) Declaratory Relief; and (12) Unjust Enrichment against. Plaintiff also named Topa Insurance Company as a defendant.

 

On January 30, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Amber Hill, Valerie Vincent, Joanne Catran, and Mimi Himes.

 

Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint on May 11, 2023. On September 7, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Hippo Analytics, Inc..  Although the court’s docket does not reflect that plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint until January 4,2024, plaintiff apparently served a copy of the Third Amended Complaint on Defendant Topa, because on November 28, 2023, Topa filed a demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint.   Plaintiff filed no opposition. 

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Topa Insurance Company’s Demurrer to the Third Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.  

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

In support of their demurrer, Defendant Topa requests this court take judicial notice of several court documents (RJN, Exhs. 1-3.) The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code 452(d).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Topa Insurance Company demurs to the breach of contract cause of action alleged against Defendant on the grounds Plaintiff has failed to quote any of the terms of the alleged contract or agreement with Topa. Additionally, Plaintiff has failed to attach a copy of the contract she contends she entered into with Topa.

 

To recover damages for a breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for non-performance; (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) The pleadings must specify that the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct. (CCP § 430.10(g).) Further, “[a] written contract may be pleaded by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect. [Citation.] In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms. This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’  [Citation.]” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489 [citing 4 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (4th ed. 1997) Pleading, §§ 479-480, pp. 572-73].)

 

Defendant Topa argues Plaintiff’s Third Amended Complaint is not clear as to what purported contract she is referring to. She contends there is a Hippo contract, that she entered into with “Hippo and/or Topa.” (TAC ¶ 105.) As a result, it is still unclear whether there are one or two contracts. Moreover, Hippo has now been dismissed from the instant action. Plaintiff has also failed to quote any of the terms of the alleged contract or agreement or attach a copy of the contract she contends she entered into with “Hippo and/or Topa.”

 

Upon review of the TAC, Plaintiff has attached the insurance contract she contends has been breached by Topa.   (TAC, Exh. B.) The HOMEOWNERS INSURANCE DECLARATIONS PAGE of Exhibit B, lists Topa as the Underwriter. The Plaintiff has also included the Homeowners Condominium Unit-Owners Policy Provisions which delineate the coverage purchased by plaintiff, which was signed by Topa’s president.   Based on this attachment, plaintiff has adequately alleged a cause of action for breach of contract against Topa.

 

As a result, Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.


Case Number: 22VECV01134    Hearing Date: January 22, 2024    Dept: W

YOSEF COHEN, et al. v. KAMBIZ KAMANGAR, et al.

 

DEFENDANT KAMBIZ KAMANGAR’S MOTION FOR ORDER THAT REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION (SET ONE) BE DEEMED ADMITTED AND FOR MANDATORY MONETARY SANCTIONS [CCP § 2033.280(B)(C)]

 

Date of Hearing:        January 22, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01134

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Kambiz Kamangar

Responding Party:     No opposition   

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiffs Yosef Cohen and Vahe Ter-Galstyan filed a complaint against Defendants World Tech Toys, Inc., World Trading 23, Inc., Kevork Kouyoumjian, Kambiz Kamangar, and Kamir Distribution, LLC asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Oral Joint Venture Agreement; (2) Unlawful, Fraudulent & Unfair Business Practices; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (4) Fraud.

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendant Kamangar made fraudulent representations to Plaintiff Cohen regarding a business opportunity arising from the Pandemic. Plaintiffs allege Defendant Kamangar represented to Plaintiff Cohen Defendant KOUYOUMJIAN had extensive contacts in China as a result of his companies, World Trading 23, Inc. and World Tech Toys, Inc., which were involved in importing goods from China for sale in the United States and actively encouraged Plaintiff Cohen and Plaintiff Ter-Galstanyan to order the gloves. Plaintiffs allege Defendants failed to perform in accordance with the term so of the Agreement as well as their fiduciary duty.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Kambiz Kamangar’s Motion for Order that Requests for Admission (Set One) be Deemed Admitted and for Mandatory Monetary Sanctions [CCP §2033.280(B)(C) is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant Kambiz Kamangar requests this court take judicial notice of (2) the Criminal Docket retrieved from LexisNexis for Case No. 1:21-CR444, USA v. Cohen in United States District Court, New York Southern (Exh. 1); (2) the guilty Judgment entered against Plaintiff Yosef Cohen in Case No. 1:21-CR444, USA v. Cohen in United States District Court, New York Southern (Exh. 2); and (3) the guilty Judgment entered against Plaintiff Vahe Ter-Galstanyan in Case No. 1:21-CR444, USA v. Cohen in United States District Court, New York Southern (Exh. 3).

 

The court GRANTS Defendant Kamangar’s request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Kambiz Kamangar moves the court for an order that the truth of each matter specified in each Requests for Admission (Set One) served on Plaintiffs Yosef Cohen and Vahe Ter-Galstanyan on September 7, 2023, be deemed admitted and conclusively established for all purposes in this action. Defendant Kamangar also requests that mandatory monetary sanctions in the total amount of $3,287.95 be imposed against Plaintiffs Yosef Cohen and Vahe Ter-Galstanyan as the reasonable costs and attorneys’ fees incurred by Defendant Kambiz Kamangar for these proceedings.

 

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the truth of any matters specified in the requests and the genuineness of any documents be deemed admitted, as well as a monetary sanction. (CCP §2033.280(b).) The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants that party relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (CCP §2033.280(a)(1)-(2).) The court shall grant the motion “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response … in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (CCP §2033.280(c); St. Mary v. Sup. Ct. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 777-78.)

 

 On July 3, 2023, counsel for Defendant Kamangar became aware that Plaintiffs Yosef Cohen and Vahe Ter-Galstanyan had pled guilty to felonies involving money laundering when counsel for Kouyumjian reached out to Plaintiff’s counsel (copying Kamangar counsel) to determine whether Plaintiffs would be dismissing this case, considering their criminal conviction status. (Cohen Decl. ¶3, Exh. C.) On September 7, 2023, Defendant Kambiz Kamangar served Plaintiffs, and each of them, their own specific discovery including Requests for Admission (Set One). (Cohen Decl. ¶4, Exhs. A, B.) On October 9, 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel sent an email stating that he was aware that responses were due but, because Plaintiffs had been (and continue to be) incarcerated, he sought an extension. (Cohen Decl. ¶5, Exh. D.) Defense counsel granted the extension until November 8, 2023. (Cohen Decl. ¶6, Exh. E.) On November 14, 2023, having received no responses or updates, Plaintiffs were advised that Defendant Kamangar would also be moving to compel responses, deem Plaintiffs’ respective RFAs admitted and seek sanctions. (Cohen Decl. ¶7, Exh. G.)

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have failed to timely respond to Defendant Kamangar’s properly served discovery request. The court therefore grants Defendant Kamangar’s Motion for Order that Requests for Admissions (Set One) be Deemed Admitted is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (CCP §2033.280(c).) 

 

Defendant seeks $3,287.95 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. Defendant seeks $61.65 for reservation fees, $22.30 and $4 for filing fees, $1,600.00 for four hours of preparing the motion, $1,000.00 for 2.5 hours to review any opposition and prepare a reply. (Cohen Decl. ¶9.) Counsel’s billing rate is $400. (Cohen Decl. 9.)

 

The court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,687.95.
