Case Number: 23STLC07370    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Thursday, October 10, 2024

Case Name:                             LARRY A. PELUSO, Plaintiff in Interpleader, v. AMERICAN SPINE, P.C.; SIMONMED IMAGING, P.C.; CARSON ORTHOPEDIC NEUROLOGICAL MEDICAL GROUP, INC.; CARSON CHIROPRACTIC OFFICE, INC.; MAURICE ADAMS; and DOES 1 through 5, Inclusive.

Case No.:                                23STCV07370

Motion:                                   Motion to Enforce Settlement

Moving Party:                         Defendant Maurice Adams

Responding Party:                   Plaintiff Larry A. Peluso

Notice:                                    NOT OK

 

 

Tentative Ruling:                  Defendant’s Motions are DENIED.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Larry A. Peluso (“Plaintiff”) filed this action in pro se against defendants American Spine, P.C., Simonmed Imaging, P.C., Carson Orthopedic Neurological Medical Group, Inc., Carson Chiropractic Office, Inc., and Maurice Adams on November 17, 2023. Plaintiff, an attorney, alleges he holds $9,000.00 in trust remaining after a personal injury settlement for his former client, defendant Adams. The $9,000.00 is subject to $14,000.00 in competing claims. (Compl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff seeks to interplead the funds.

 

On August 21, 2024, defendant Adams (“Defendant”), also self-represented, filed a “MOTION TO – Response to Complaint Motion to Claim Funds in Lawsuit” [sic]. On October 1, 2024, Defendant filed a “MOTION TO – Dispute Plaintiffs Motion Amended Complaint” [sic]. Defendant reserved a hearing for a Motion to Enforce Settlement through the Court Reservation System.

 

Defendant did not file effective proofs of service for either motion. Both proofs of service state he served “Defendant James Antonucci’s Motion to Dispute Memo of Costs”. Neither that person nor that motion is before the Court. The POS also attests to transmission by fax and “ONLY BY ELECTRONIC TRANSMISSION”, a contradiction in terms. Moreover, although Plaintiff appears to be an attorney, he is self-represented here. A self-represented party may not be served electronically except upon express consent or court order. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.251(a) [electronic service rules subject to section 1010.6].)

 

The Court cannot act on these motions until they are properly served, particularly in the absence of an opposition.

 

Furthermore, the motions are unintelligible, and the Court is unable to determine what relief is being requested or the grounds for relief.  

 

Defendant’s Motions are DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.

ANALYSIS:

 

 

 

I.                    Background

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff Mayra Sandoval (Plaintiff) filed suit against Defendants South Bay Auto Group Inc. (South Bay); Quality Acceptance, LLC (Quality); and the Western Surety Company (Western; collectively Defendants). Plaintiff filed the operative first amended complaint (FAC) on September 11, 2024, wherein she alleged six causes of action for the violation of the Consumers Legal Remedies Act, civil theft, fraud and deceit, negligent misrepresentation, violations of the Unfair Competition Law, and violations of Vehicle Code section 11711.

 

Plaintiff alleged that, on September 4, 2023, she purchased a 2016 Ford Explorer (subject vehicle) from South Bay. (FAC ¶ 9). After the purchase, South Bay assigned the purchase contract to Quality. (FAC ¶ 10). Upon delivery, the subject vehicle presented with certain non-conformities including issues with the transmission, crankshaft, check engine light, and engine control module. Plaintiff reported the problem to South Bay, but it took no action. (FAC ¶¶12-13)

 

Plaintiff also alleges that South Bay overcharged her for the subject vehicles, license fee according to Revenue and Taxation Code section 10752. (FAC ¶14). Defendants demur to the claim for civil theft (COA 2), fraud and deceit (COA 3), and violation of the Unfair Competition Law (COA 5). Defendants also move to strike the punitive damages allegations and prayer.

 

As a preliminary manner, Plaintiff has alleged that Quality accepted assignment of Plaintiff’s purchase contract from South Bay. That contract, in turn, provided that,  

 

ANY HOLDER OF THIS CONSUMER CREDIT CONTRACT IS SUBJECT TO ALL CLAIMS AND DEFENSES WHICH THE DEBTOR COULD ASSERT AGAINST THE SELLER OF GOODS OR SERVICES OBTAINED PURSUANT HERETO OR WITH THE PROCEEDS HEREOF, RECOVERY HEREUNDER BY THE DEBTOR SHALL NOT EXCEED AMOUNTS PAID BY THE DEBTOR HEREUNDER.

 

(FAC, ¶7 [capitalization original].) This is sufficient to permit Plaintiff allege claims against Quality for South Bay’s conduct.

 

II.                 Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice of Exhibit 1, Department of Motor Vehicles Vehicle Industry Registration Procedures Manual, Section 3.035 is GRANTED. (Evidence Code §§ 451(f) and 453.)

 

III.              Legal Standard Demurrer

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (See Schultz v. Harney (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1622, as modified on denial of reh’g (Sept. 29, 1994).) The court “take[s] as true all properly pleaded material facts, but not conclusions of fact or law asserted in the complaint.” (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 916.)

 

At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against them. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “The allegations of the pleading demurred to must be regarded as true.” (South Shore Land Co. v. Peterson (1964) 266 Cal.App.2d 725, 732.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (Ibid. [internal citations omitted].)

 

IV.              Demurrer

 

A.    Second Cause of Action for Civil Theft under Penal Code section 496 (Section 496)

 

Section 496, subdivision (a) defines the criminal offense of what is commonly referred to as receiving stolen property. It provides in relevant part: “Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained,” is subject to incarceration. Section 496, subdivision (c), in turn provides civil liability to such persons.

 

Penal Code section 484 states “[e]very person who shall feloniously steal, take, carry, lease or drive away the personal property of another, or who shall fraudulently appropriate property which has been entrusted to him or her, or who shall knowingly and designedly, by any false or fraudulent representation or pretense, defraud any other person of money, labor or real property. . . is guilty of theft.” (Pen. Code, §484, subd. (a).)

 

Thus, “the elements required to show a violation of section 496(a) are simply that (i) property was stolen or obtained in a manner constituting theft, (ii) the defendant knew the property was so stolen or obtained, and (iii) the defendant received or had possession of the stolen property.” (Switzer v. Wood (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 116, 126.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not alleged conduct which falls under section 496 because she has not alleged South Bay obtained the money in a manner constituting theft. Plaintiff states South Bay Obtained money via “theft, and then withheld, converted, kept and otherwise misappropriated the Plaintiff’s money constituting civil theft.” (FAC, ¶34.) However, this is a legal conclusion. Plaintiff has not alleged any foundational facts demonstrating theft of money or putting South Bay on notice of what conduct constituted theft. The only foundational fact is the general allegation of South Bay’s alleged overcharging a vehicle license fee. (FAC, ¶23.) However, Plaintiff fails to allege the prerequisite facts showing an intent to deprive Plaintiff of the overcharged amount.

 

The demurrer to second cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

B.     Third Cause of action for Fraud and Deceit

 

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal. 4th 631, 638 (Lazar).) “[F]raud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. . . .This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Lazar, supra, 12 Cal. 4th at 645 [internal quotation marks and ellipses omitted].)

The law holds plaintiffs to a higher standard where there is a corporate defendant. In that case plaintiff must “allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Id. at p. 645; See also Wald v. TruSpeed Motorcars, LLC (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 378 [applying the heightened corporate pleading standing to an LLC].)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not alleged a fraud claim with sufficient particularity. Plaintiff alleges that South Bay misrepresented that the subject vehicle had been inspected and had no mechanical problems. (FAC ¶¶ 11, 37-39). She does not allege who made this misrepresentation, what form the misrepresentation came in (i.e., written, or oral), or how that person had the authority to speak upon South Bay’s behalf. Plaintiff also alleges that both prior to and after the execution of the contract South Bay made misrepresentations. (FAC, ¶37.) It is unclear when the misrepresentation occurred. Plaintiff also alleges South Bay misrepresented the vehicle registration fee. Again, she does not allege who made the misrepresentation, or the foundational facts demonstrating that South Bay knew the misrepresentation was false.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

C.     Fifth Cause of Action for Unfair Competition

 

“The Unfair Practices Act [the Act] is found in Business and Professions Code, Section 17000 et seq.” (Framers Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 377, 383 (Farmers).) An unfair practice is any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business practices or acts and unfair, deceptive, untrue, or misleading advertising. (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq. (“Section 17200”); see also Podolsky v. First Healthcare Corp. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 632, 647.)

 

A claim premised on the unlawful prong “borrows” violations of other laws and authorizes a separate action pursuant to unfair competition. (Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1383, as modified on denial of reh’g (Feb. 24, 2012).) To state a claim based on an ‘unlawful’ business practice, plaintiff must allege specific unlawful conduct, that the conduct is a business practice, and that because of the practice, defendants has received ill-gotten gains. (Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Trial Claims and Def. Ch. 14(I)-B.)

 

There is a split of authority on the unfair prong. (Jolley v. Chase Home Fin., LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 907, as modified on denial of reh’g (Mar. 7, 2013) [collecting cases].) On the one hand, some cases hold unfair practice “is one that offends established public policy, that is immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous, or substantially injurious to consumers or that has an impact on the victim that outweighs defendant’s reasons, justifications, and motives for the practice.” (Ibid.) On the other hand, some cases hold the claim must be “tethered to specific constitutional, statutory or regulatory provisions.” (Ibid.) As a statutory cause of action, allegations of unfair business practices must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation. (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.) The statutory violations must be delineated and related to a business activity. (Ibid.)  

 

A theory for unlawful practices occurs when a person makes, disseminates, or causes to be disseminated to the public a statement concerning professional services which is untrue, and which is either known or should reasonably be known to be untrue. (Bus. & Prof. Code, §17500) “An unlawful business practice or act within the meaning of the UCL is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Bernardo v. Planned Parenthood Federation of America (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 322, 351.) A plaintiff needs to identify statutory, regulatory, or decisional law that the defendant has violated. (Id. at p. 352.)

 

Finally, a defendant violates the fraudulent prong of the UCL by engaging in conduct by which “members of the public are likely to be deceived.” (Committee on Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 211, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in Branick v. Downey Sav. & Loan Ass’n (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.)

 

Here, Plaintiff appears to allege a claim premised on either the fraud or unlawful prong. As already discussed, Plaintiff has not alleged a claim for fraud, so a claim under the Act premised on fraud also fails. However, Plaintiff has alleged unlawful conduct sufficient to justify a claim under the unlawful prong. Plaintiff alleges that South Bay violated the Consumers Legal Remedies act by misrepresenting the subject vehicle. (FAC, ¶¶ 21-24.) Plaintiff also alleges that this is at least one basis for her claim under the Act. (FAC, ¶98.) This is sufficient.

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

V.                Legal Standard Motion to Strike

 

The court may strike out any “irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., §437) Motions may also target pleadings or parts of pleadings that are not filed or drawn in conformity with applicable laws, rules, or orders. (Code Civ. Proc., §437 subd., (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., §437.) “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)

 

To succeed on a motion to strike punitive damages allegations, it must be said as a matter of law that the alleged behavior was not so vile, base, or contemptible that it would not be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228-1229.) Punitive damages are available in non-contract actions where a defendant is guilty of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c) [defining each term].)

 

Regarding a corporation, “[a] corporation is not deemed to ratify misconduct, and thus become liable for punitive damages, unless its officer, director, or managing agent actually knew about the misconduct and its malicious character. A ‘managing agent’ is an employee with authority to establish corporate policy, that is, the broad principles and rules of general application which govern corporate conduct.” (Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 163.)

 

VI.              Motion to Strike

 

The issue is whether Plaintiff has allegations supporting her claim for punitive damages. She does not.

 

Here, as already discussed, Plaintiff has not alleged fraudulent conduct. Nor has Plaintiff alleged malice or oppression. Plaintiff has essentially alleged intentional torts, but nothing beyond that conduct. The mere allegation that an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal. App. 3d 159, 166.) Nor has Plaintiff alleged any conduct by a specific employee let alone an officer, director, or managing agent which could support that Defendants had ratified the improper conduct.

 

The motion to strike is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. The motion to strike paragraph 26, lines 22-24, and the prayer for punitive damages is GRANTED. The motion to strike paragraph 48, lines 21-24, is denied as moot based on the court’s ruling on the demurrer.  The motion to strike paragraph 4 of Prayer, line 19 is GRANTED.

 

VII.           Conclusion & Order

 

The Demurrer is SUSTAINED in part and OVERRULED in part, as follows: 

 

The Demurrer to the second cause of action for civil theft and the third cause of action as for fraud and deceit is SUSTAINED WITH THIRTY (30) DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND. The Demurrer to the fifth cause of action for violations of the Unfair Competition Law is OVERRULED.

  

The Motion to Strike is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part, as follows:

 

 The Motion to Strike paragraphs 26, and the prayer for punitive damages is GRANTED. The Motion to Strike paragraph 48 is DENIED as MOOT based on the court’s ruling on the Demurrer.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On July 19, 2024, Plaintiff Zohrab Steve Haddad (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint against Defendant Vardan Antonyan (Defendant) with three causes of action for: (1) Malicious Prosecution, (2) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (3) Perjury. Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $8,490 for legal fees and costs. This case concerns alleged harassment by Defendant related to Plaintiff’s involvement with the homeowner’s association where both Plaintiff and Defendant own condominiums.

 

On August 14, 2024, Defendant filed an Answer, denying Plaintiff’s allegations.

 

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Change Venue from Spring Street Courthouse to the Burbank Courthouse. On September 9, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD:

 

“Venue is determined based on the complaint on file at the time the motion to change venue is made. [Citations.]’ [Citation.]”  (Dow AgroSciences, LLC v. Superior Court (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 1067, 1076.)¿ CCP § 397(a) states: “The court may, on motion, change the place of trial in the following cases: (a) When the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court.”

 

“Except as otherwise provided by law and subject to the power of the court to transfer actions or proceedings as provided in this title, the superior court in the county where the defendants or some of them reside at the commencement of the action is the proper court for the trial of the action…Subject to subdivision (b), if a defendant has contracted to perform an obligation in a particular county, the superior court in the county where the obligation is to be performed, where the contract in fact was entered into, or where the defendant or any defendant resides at the commencement of the action is a proper court for the trial of an action founded on that obligation,¿ and the county where the obligation is incurred is the county where it is to be performed unless there is a special contract in writing to the contrary.”  (CCP § 395(a).)¿¿ 

 

The burden is on the moving party to establish facts justifying the transfer.  (Mission Imports, Inc. v. Superior Court (1982) 31 Cal.3d 921, 928.)  Absent “an affirmative showing to the contrary the presumption is that the county in which the title of the action shows that it is brought is, prima facie, the proper county for the commencement and trial of the action.”  (Id.; Fontaine v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 830, 836.)

 

The Code of Civil Procedure, section 397 also provides: “The court may, on motion, change the place of trial in the following cases:

 

(a)   When the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court. 

(b)   When there is reason to believe that an impartial trial cannot be had therein.

(c)   When the convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice would be promoted by the change. 

(d)   When from any cause there is no judge of the court qualified to act. 

(e)   When a proceeding for dissolution of marriage has been filed in the county in which the petitioner has been a resident for three months next preceding the commencement of the proceeding, and the respondent at the time of the commencement of the proceeding is a resident of another county in this state, to the county of the respondent’s residence when the ends of justice would be promoted by the change.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

As an initial matter, the Court should note that Plaintiff did not file or include a memorandum of points and authorities. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.111(a), (b).) Therefore, it is unclear what legal authorities Petitioner is relying on to support his motion.  

 

Plaintiff cites to Code of Civil Procedure § 394, but this section applies to an action or proceeding against a county, city or local agency, which is not applicable in the instant action against Defendant, an individual.

 

Plaintiff contends that transfer to Burbank Courthouse is in the interest of justice because (1) the Burbank Courthouse might provide an earlier hearing date than the current hearing date of January 1, 2026, and (2) Burbank Courthouse is located more conveniently to Plaintiff, who resides in Van Nuys, CA, Defendant, who resides in Sylmar, CA, and witnesses.

 

The court disagrees with Plaintiff that change of venue is proper. First, Code of Civil Procedure,  § 397, subdivisions (a), (b), (d), and (e) do not apply because there is no evidence, and Plaintiff does not argue, that (1) this court is not the proper court, (2) impartial trial cannot be had herein, (3) there is no judge of the court qualified to act, or (4) that the case involves a dissolution of marriage proceeding where respondent’s location justifies the change. With respect to Code of Civil Procedure § 397, subdivision (c), Plaintiff asserts the Burbank Courthouse offers a more convenient location for witnesses because an earlier hearing date “might be possible,” and the change would increase the chances that Plaintiff, Defendant and witnesses can attend the hearing. (Haddad Decl. ¶ 1.) However, the Court in its discretion finds this mere possibility and minor change in location within the proper court is unpersuasive to justify a change of venue in the interest of justice.

 

Accordingly, the court DENIES the motion to change venue.

 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Change Venue is DENIED.

 

Moving party is to give notice.
