DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV02320    Hearing Date: May 16, 2024    Dept: 73

05/16/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

RENE MARTINEZ v. FARMERS FINANCIAL SOLUTIONS, LLC, et al. (19STCV02320)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Patrick Garofalo (Law Offices of Patrick Garofalo), Rachel Nelson (Nelson Law Office APC)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party: Stephen Ronk (Gordon Rees Scully Mansukhani, LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S motion FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed 04/29/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $379,649.25. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This is a wrongful termination action arising from plaintiff Rene Martinez’s (“Plaintiff’s”) employment with defendant Farmers Financial Solutions, LLC (“Farmers”) from February 2015 through March 2018. 

 

On January 23, 2019, Plaintiff filed this action’s Complaint against defendants Farmers, Steve Klein (“Klein”), and Jimmy Coicoechea (“Coicoechea”). The Complaint asserts the following causes of action against the defendants: 

1.     Discrimination in Violation of Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12940 et seq.; 

2.     Failure to Prevent Discrimination in violation of Cal. Gov. Code §§ 12940 Et Seq.; 

3.     Retaliation; 

4.     Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy; 

5.     Retaliation in Violation of Cal. Labor Code § 1102.5; 

6.     Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; 

7.     Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; 

8.     Failure to Provide Accurate Wage Statements in Violation of Cal. Labor Code §§ 210, 226; 

9.     Failure to Pay Wages Upon Termination in Violation of Cal. Labor Code §§ 201, 203; 

10.  Failure to Pay Overtime in Violation of Cal. Labor Code §§ 510, 1194; 

11.  Failure to Provide Rest Periods in Violation of Industrial Welfare Commission Wage Orders and Cal. Labor Code § 226.7; and  

12.  Failure to Provide Meal Periods in Violation of Cal. Labor Code §§ 226.7, 512 

 

On March 30, 2022, Plaintiff dismissed without prejudice causes of action six and seven.

 

On April 19, 2022, the Court granted summary adjudication as to causes of action one, two, three, eight, nine, ten, eleven, and twelve. (See Court’s 4/19/22 Minute Order.)

 

On January 30, 2024, the parties reached a settlement in this case except for attorneys fees excluding costs. (See Court’s 01/31/24 Minute Order.) The parties requested the court to retain jurisdiction to hear the attorneys’ fees motion. (Ibid.)

 

On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Attorneys’ Fees, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s counsel took this case on contingency

·       Plaintiff Is Entitled to An Award of Plaintiff Attorney’s Fees Pursuant to Labor Code §1102.5(j)

o   Effective January 1, 2021, Assembly Bill 1947 modified Labor Code section 1102.5 to, in part, include a provision allowing a court to award attorney fees to a prevailing plaintiff.

·       The Requested Attorney’s Fees are Reasonable

o   Plaintiff’s counsels hourly rates : $525/hour and $285/hour

o   Plaintiff is seeking an award of attorney’s fees in the sum of $776,548.50, which represents a two times multiplier justified because of the length and highly adversarial nature of this litigation, as well as the amount of time and effort required to finally reached a settlement on the eve of trial.

 

On April 29, 2024, the parties filed a Joint Stipulation to continue the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees. To allow Farmers the opportunity to timely file an opposition, the parties stipulate the hearing be continued from May 9, 2024 at 8:30 a.m. to May 16, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

 

On May 3, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

“The verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board Of Trustees Of California State University (2005) 132 Cal. App. 4th 359, 396.)¿ If the motion is supported by evidence, the opposing party must respond with specific evidence showing that the fees are unreasonable.¿ (Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys. v. California Ins. Guarantee Ass’n (2008) 163 Cal. App. 4th 550, 560–63.)¿ The Court has discretion to reduce fees that result from inefficient or duplicative use of time.¿ (Horsford, supra at 395.)¿¿¿

Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) “While courts take judicial notice of public records, they do not take notice of the truth of matters stated therein.” (Jolley v. D.     Discussion

 

a.     Whether Plaintiff Can Seek Attorneys’ Fees

 

First, the Court finds Labor Code § 1102.5(j) allows the Court to award attorney’s fees to the prevailing plaintiff in an action for violation of this statute.

 

Subsection 1102.5(j) became effective on January 1, 2021. Defendant asserts that the legislative history bars retroactive application. The history cited by Defendant, however, is in relation to the extension of the statute of limitations extension found in Labor Code § 98.7(a)(1). “It has been authoritatively held (Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 455 [20 Cal.Rptr. 321, 369 P.2d 937]) that a new statute authorizing an award of attorney fees applies to actions not yet final at the time the statute becomes effective.” (California Housing Finance Agency v. E.R. Fairway Associates (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1508, 1513.) The Court will apply the subsection to this action as it was pending when the statute became effective. 

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiff is not entitled to attorney’s fees because Plaintiff is not the prevailing party. However, Defendant does not provide any authority in support. Plaintiff settled this case and under California law, the “prevailing party” includes the party with a net monetary recovery. [California Code of Civil Procedure § 1032(a)(4)].

 

Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff is entitled to seek attorney’s fees.

 

b.     Reasonableness of Fees¿ 

 

Plaintiff moves for attorney’s fees in the lodestar amount of $388,274.25. Plaintiff’s counsel declares that 963.25 hours at $285.00 to $525.00 per hour were spent prosecuting this action. Plaintiff also requests the Court award a 2.0 multiplier, thus Plaintiff is seeking an award of attorney’s fees in the sum of $776,548.50

 

                                                    i.     Hourly Rate

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.” (PLCM Grp. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The ‘experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in his court, and while his judgment is of course subject to review, it will not be disturbed unless the appellate court is convinced that it is clearly wrong’ — meaning that it abused its discretion.” (Id.) 

 

Plaintiff seeks to recover fees for two attorneys that billed time in connection with this case. Their hourly rates are as follows: 

 

·        Patrick Garofalo : $525/hour 

·        Rachel Nelson : $285/hour 

 

The Court finds that the rates requested by Plaintiff is reasonable and commensurate with rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and expertise. In Defendant’s opposition, Defendant does not dispute counsel’s hourly rate. Mr. Garofalo and Ms. Nelson attest to their experience and reasonableness of the rates. (Garofalo Decl., ¶¶ 8-17; Nelson Decl., ¶¶ 4-7.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court does not reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

                                                   ii.     Number of Hours

 

A prevailing party’s verified billing invoices are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were necessarily incurred.¿ (Hadley v.¿Krepel¿(1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence.”¿ (Lunada¿Biomedical v. Nunez¿(2014) 240 Cal.App.4th 459, 488.)¿ 

 

The Court has reviewed Defendant’s objections to Plaintiffs’ counsel’s billed hours finds most of them to be unavailing. Defendant objects to time counsel spent on discovery, opposing the MSJ and other motions. The Court has independently reviewed the time entries and does not find they represent time spent on unreasonable or unnecessary tasks.  

 

In addition, while Defendant argues that the hours spent prosecuting the unsuccessful non-Labor code claims should not be included because they were unnecessary to prosecute Plaintiff’s Labor Code, Defendant does not point to any specific time entries or activities that were only related to the unsuccessful claims. Apportionment is permissible where the opposing party has identified work that was “chargeable only to her unsuccessful causes of action.” (See Cordero-Sacks v. Housing Authority of City of Los Angeles (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1267, 1286.) Thus, the Court does not reduce any hours billed on this basis.

 

Defendant next asserts that on January 14, 2020, Plaintiff’s counsel billed to attend the FINRA deposition of Rene Martinez. However, Defendant contends the FINRA deposition was unrelated to this civil action and Defendant had no involvement. Mr. Garofalo billed 11 hours to attend this deposition and Ms. Nelson billed 10 hours. (Exhibit List ISO Motion, Exh. D, Exh. E.) Plaintiff does not argue this issue in their reply, thereby conceding such billed hours were not reasonably incurred. Therefore, the Court reduces Plaintiff’s request by 11 hours at the rate of $525/hour and 10 hours at the rate of $285/hour, for a total of $8,625.

 

The Court will allow 942.25 hours for the work of Mr. Garofalo and Ms. Nelson, for a total of $379,649.25. 

 

c.      Multiplier

 

The Court is permitted, but not required, to apply a multiplier to an award for attorney’s fees. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1138.) Relevant factors to determine whether an enhancement is appropriate include (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award. (Id. at 1132.) 

 

Plaintiff requests the Court award a 2.0 multiplier to the fee award. Plaintiff has not shown that the case was particularly novel and complex. Though counsel represented Plaintiff on a contingent basis, the Court does not find a contingency warrants increasing fees by a 2.0 multiplier. Since the Court does not find a multiplier appropriate, counsel will be awarded the fees reasonably incurred.

 

Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $379,649.25. 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $379,649.25.

“A party desiring to depose an expert witness described in subdivision (a) shall pay the expert's reasonable and customary hourly or daily fee for any time spent at the deposition from the time noticed in the deposition subpoena, or from the time of the arrival of the expert witness should that time be later than the time noticed in the deposition subpoena, until the time the expert witness is dismissed from the deposition, regardless of whether the expert is actually deposed by any party attending the deposition.” (Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) § 2034.430(b).) “Except as provided in subdivision (f), this section applies to an expert witness, other than a party or an employee of a party, who is any of the following: (1) An expert described in subdivision (b) of Section 2034.210 . . . .” (CCP § 2034.430(a).) 

 

“If any expert designated by a party under subdivision (a) is a party or an employee of a party, or has been retained by a party for the purpose of forming and expressing an opinion in anticipation of the litigation or in preparation for the trial of the action, the designation of that witness shall include or be accompanied by an expert witness declaration under Section 2034.260.” (CCP § 2034.210(b).) “Any party may demand a mutual and simultaneous exchange by all parties of a list containing the name and address of any natural person, including one who is a party, whose oral or deposition testimony in the form of an expert opinion any party expects to offer in evidence at the trial.” (CCP § 2034.210(a).) 

 

“Misuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (b) Using a discovery method in a manner that does not comply with its specified procedures. (c) Employing a discovery method in a manner or to an extent that causes unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense . . . .” (CCP § 2030.010.)

II.ANALYSIS

 

A.     Defendant’s Motion for Reconsideration

 

a.     Review by a Different Judge

 

As an initial matter, the Court addresses whether a judge who did not make the initial ruling can consider this motion.

 

Generally, a motion for reconsideration must be made to the same judge.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a); Marriage of Oliverez (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1248.)  “However, there are narrow exceptions to this general rule.”  (Marriage of Oliverez, supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at 1248.)  “‘[W]here the judge who made the initial ruling is unavailable to reconsider the motion, a different judge may entertain the reconsideration motion.’”  (Id. (quoting Ziller Electronics Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1232) (alteration in original).)  “Another exception is when the facts have changed or when the judge has considered further evidence and law.”  (Id.)  “Additionally, a second judge may reverse a prior ruling of another judge if the record shows that it was based on inadvertence, mistake, or fraud.”  (Id. at 1248-49.)  “Mere disagreement . . . with the prior trial judge’s ruling, however, is not enough to overturn that ruling.”  (Id. at 1249.)   

 

Here, Defendant’s motion moves for reconsideration of the December 12, 2023 order made in this Department, Department 73, by Judge Timothy Dillon. However, the Court finds that Judge Dillon is unavailable to hear the instant Motion due to his temporary reassignment to another Department. Therefore, the Court finds that the assigned judge to Department 73, Judge Rolf Treu, may consider this motion.

 

b.     Discussion

 

The moving party must present new facts, circumstances or law in order to grant a motion for reconsideration.  (See CCP § 1008(a); see also Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.)  The party seeking reconsideration of an order shall state by affidavit what application was made before, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts or circumstances are claimed to be shown.  (CCP § 1008(a).)  Further, “…the party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” (Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457 [emphasis added].)  The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.) 

 

On July 31, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion to Quash and other relief arguing improper service of process. Defendant argued that Plaintiff had not shown reasonable diligence before utilizing substituted service on a person other than the registered agent for process for defendant.

 

On December 12, 2023, the Court issued an order denying Defendant’s Motion to Quash in its entirety. The Court stated: “Defendant is an entity. Plaintiff served Defendant by substituted service through a registered process server. Plaintiff thereby enjoys a presumption of proper service. Under section 415.20, subdivision (a), due diligence and prior attempts at service are not necessary when an entity is being served. Therefore, based on the information at hand, Plaintiff properly served Defendant.” (See Court’s 12/12/23 Order.)

 

Defendant asserts that since the hearing of the motion, Defendant reviewed video from security cameras posted on his residential property, which shows no process server having come to the property at the time and date indicated in the proof of service of summons that was filed with this Court. Defendant also now provides the declaration of Terry Davis, with whom the process server allegedly left copies of the summons and complaint, stating that she would not have even been awake at the early hour the process server claims to have delivered the documents. Defendant asserts that in light of this new evidence the Court should reconsider its motion denying Defendant’s motion to quash summons.

 

However, the Court finds Defendant has not presented a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the aforementioned evidence earlier.¿(Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 674, 690.)  The evidence that Defendant relies upon in the current motion was available at the time of the original motion. Courts have properly denied motions for reconsideration where it was based on evidence that could have been presented in connection with the original motion.  (Morris v. AGFA Corp. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1460;¿Hennigan v. White (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 395, 406.) 

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s motion for reconsideration.

BAHADOR MIKHAK v. CHRISTINE SNELL (23STCV12939)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Neville Johnson (Johnson & Johnson LLP)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Howard King, Jackson Trugman (King, Holmes, Paterno & Soriano LLP)

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS FEES (filed 5/09/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Award of Attorneys’ fees and costs is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 6, 2023, Plaintiff Bahador Mikhak (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Christine Snell (“Snell” or “Defendant”) for defamation.

The Complaint alleges the following. Snell along with her sister, Julie Skujins (“Skujins”), fabricated a false domestic violence incident against Plaintiff to damage his reputation and to prevent him from gaining custody of his child. (Complaint, ¶ 3.) Skujins is the mother of Plaintiff’s child and she had been residing at Plaintiff’s home with their child. (Id., ¶.)

On the evening of April 9, 2022, Plaintiff arrived at his home and found Snell with Skujins on his property. (Id., ¶ 9.) Plaintiff alleges a discussion arose and Skujins pushed Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff turned to speak to Snell and then saw Skujins had thrown herself on the ground. (Id., ¶ 11.) Skujins called 9-1-1 and police arrived. (Id., ¶ 12.) Police officers handcuffed Plaintiff and took statements from Skujins, Snell and Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 13.) Snell gave false and harmful statements to the police, such as that Plaintiff had physically harmed Skujins in the past. (Id., ¶ 14.) Plaintiff suffered damages as a result of Snell’s false statements. (Id., ¶ 27.)

On March 21, 2024, this Court entered an order granting Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike (“anti-SLAPP”) motion.

On March 27, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion for Award of Attorneys’ fees and costs, arguing:

·       Defendant moves for an order pursuant to CCP § 425.16 compelling Plaintiff to reimburse Defendant for $60,285.19, which includes fees through the contested anti-SLAPP proceeding ($43,600.00), reasonable costs ($6,372.69), and fees in connection with this Motion ($10,312.50).

·       Defendant moves on the grounds that the Court granted Defendant’s anti-SLAPP motion, Defendant is entitled to attorneys’ fees and costs pursuant to CCP § 425.16(c), and the fees and costs incurred are reasonable. 

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The amount of time that Defendant’s counsel claims he spent is excessive and the hourly rate claimed by Defendant’s counsel is unreasonable.

 

Defendant filed a reply, arguing:

·       There is no basis for Plaintiff’s vague and general arguments in the Opposition, that the fees and costs incurred are “excessive” or “unreasonable.” Plaintiff cites no evidence or authority even suggesting that counsel’s hours or rate should be reduced.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

California Municipal Code section 151. 10 provides: “Any person who demands, accepts or retains any payment of rent in excess of the maximum rent or maximum adjusted rent in violation of the provisions of this chapter. or any regulations or orders promulgated hereunder, shall be liable in a civil action to the person from whom such payment is demanded, accepted or retained for damages of three times the amount by which the payment or payments demanded, accepted or retained exceed the maximum rent or maximum adjusted rent which could be lawfully demanded, accepted or retained together with reasonable attorneys' fees and costs as determined by the court."

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

B.     Discussion

Defendant moves for attorney fees and costs in the amount of $60,285.19 incurred in connection with her successful anti-SLAPP motion and the instant motion for attorney fees and costs. 

 

a.     Hourly Rate

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) 

 

Defendant seeks to recover fees for one attorney and one paralegal that billed time in connection with Defendant’s anti-SLAPP and the instant motion. Their hourly rates are as follows: 

 

·        Jackson Trugman (Attorney): $625/hour 

·        Karen Sloane (Paralegal): $250/hour 

 

The Court finds that the rates requested by Defendant is reasonable and commensurate with rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and expertise. Plaintiff’s argument that counsel’s hourly rate is unreasonable is without support or citation to any authority. Trugman attests to his experience and reasonableness of the rates. (Trugman Decl., ¶¶ 16-19.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

b.     Reasonable Hours Incurred

 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.”  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) 

 

While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff argues the hours Defendant incurred is unreasonable. However, Plaintiff does not specify the basis for this assertion or provide authority in support. Defendant asserts the total time spent on the motion for leave to file the anti-SLAPP motion was 14.75 hours, the anti-SLAPP motion took 29.75 hours, and reply and evidentiary objections to Plaintiff’s Opposition to the anti-SLAPP motion took 17.5 hours, to prepare and attend the hearing took 8.75 hours and the instant motion took 16.5 hours. (Trugman Decl., ¶ 15.) 

 

Having analyzed the instant motion, the anti-SLAPP motion, and pleadings, the Court finds that the fees sought by Defendants are reasonable and do not include excessive time billed or duplicative entries. “Parties who litigate with no holds barred in cases such as this, in which the prevailing party is entitled to a fee award, assume the risk they will have to reimburse the excessive expenses they force upon their adversaries.” (Stokus v. Marsh (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 647, 653-654.) Here, the Court does not find that the time billed by defense counsel was excessive.

 

In Plaintiff’s opposition, he also argues that Defendant did not present evidence such as billing statements or receipts to substantiate some of the charges, such as the Legal Research/Westlaw cost. However, under California law, Defendant is not required to attach copies of bills, invoices or statements. (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal. App. 4th 1258, 1267.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Award of Attorneys’ fees and costs is GRANTED.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Defendant’s motion for attorney fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Defendant $60,285.19 in attorney fees and costs.

EMMA T. MALLOY v. EARL HALL (24STCV04870)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Gary Farwell (The Law Offices of Gary A. Farwell)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Eric Davis (Lara & Davis LLP)

 

Demurrer to complaint (filed 04/12/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The demurrer to Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

The demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff Emma T. Malloy (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Earl Hall (“Defendant”) alleging the following causes of action:

            C/A 1: Financial Elder Abuse

            C/A 2: Quiet Title

            C/A 3: Fraud

            C/A 4: Declaratory Relief

            C/A 5: Punitive Damages

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On November 28, 1969, Charlie and Emma T. Malloy purchased the residential property located at 1751 W. 38th Street, Los Angeles, CA 90062. (FAC, ¶ 10.) Title recorded under Charlie and Emma T. Malloy continued to remain in both names until October 31, 2002, when a Quitclaim Deed was signed, transferring title from Charlie Malloy and Emma T. Malloy to Defendant Earl Hall. (FAC, ¶ 11.) Plaintiff became aware of the fraudulent transfer of title to the Defendant in May 2019, when her daughter, Tracy Hall, learned that her brother, Defendant, had taken loans out on the property. (FAC, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff did not agree nor intend to ever quitclaim her property to the Defendant and does not remember signing the Quitclaim Deed, nor did Plaintiff agree to the multiple encumbrances identified in the Title Point Property Search prior to this fraudulent real property transfer. (FAC, ¶ 16.)

 

On February 26, 2024, Defendant filed a Demurrer to the FAC, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action are facially barred by the statutes of limitations

·       Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for punitive damages fails because punitive damages are a legal remedy, not a valid cause of action.

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he called Plaintiff’s counsel to meet and confer regarding the instant motion, but Plaintiff’s counsel did not answer or return his call. (Davis Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) He states he also sent Plaintiff’s counsel a letter outlining the deficiencies in the Complaint, but Plaintiff’s counsel did not respond. (Davis Decl., ¶¶ 3-4) While the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

C.     Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests the Court take judicial notice of the following:

1.     Trust Deed recorded on June 13, 1984 as Instrument No. 84703287 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

2.     Trust Deed recorded on August 29, 1986 as Instrument No. 861135722 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

3.     Trust Deed recorded on December 9, 1987 as Instrument No. 871943799 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

4.     Trust Deed recorded on May 11, 1989 as Instrument No. 89756981 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

5.     Trust Deed recorded on October 25, 1991 as Instrument No. 1688751 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California;

6.     Trust Deed recorded on October 25, 1991 as Instrument No. 1688752 in the Official Records of the County Recorder of Los Angeles County, State of California.

 

The Court may take judicial notice of “facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452(h).) While courts take judicial notice of public records, they do not take judicial notice of the truth of matters stated therein. [Citation.] ‘When judicial notice is taken of a document,…the truthfulness and proper interpretation of the document are disputable.’ [Citation.]” (Herrera v. Deutsche National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.) Further, “courts have taken judicial notice of the existence and recordation of real property records...[because] [t]he official act of recordation and the common use of a notary public in the execution of such documents assure their reliability, and the maintenance of the documents in the recorder’s office makes their existence and text capable of ready confirmation, thereby placing such documents beyond reasonable dispute.” (Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, 264.)

 

The Court judicially notices all of Defendant’s requests. 

 

D.     Demurrer to First, Second, Third and Fourth Causes of Action: Statute of Limitations

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s causes of actions are facially barred by the statutes of limitations.

For a statute of limitations to bar a claim on demurrer, “the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.” (Committee for Green Foothills v. Santa Clara County Bd. of Supervisors (2010) 48 Cal.4th 32, 42, internal quotation marks omitted.) In general, a statute of limitations begins to run “when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements,” namely, wrongdoing, causation, and resulting harm. (Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 397 (Norgart).)  

 

The statute of limitations for financial abuse of an elder or dependent adult is four years after the plaintiff discovers, or through the exercise of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts constituting the financial abuse. (Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code§ 15657.7).

 

The statute of limitations for a cause of action for fraud is three years. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (d).) If an action is brought more than three years after commission of the fraud, the plaintiff has the burden of pleading and proving that he did not make the discovery until within three years prior to the filing of his complaint. (Hobart v. Hobart Estate Co. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 412, 437.)

 

A claim for declaratory relief is subject to the same limitations period as the legal or equitable claim on which it is based. (Bank of N.Y. Mellon v. Citibank, N.A. (2017) 8 Cal. App.5th 935, 943; Ginsburg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal. App. 4th 873, 883.) Similarly, the theory of relief underlying the action for quiet title determines which statute of limitations applies. (Leeper v. Beltrami (1959) 53 Cal. 2d 195, 214; Ankoanda v. Walker-Smith (1996) 44 Cal. App. 4th 610, 615.)

 

Here, the Complaint alleges that Plaintiff discovered the facts constituting the alleged fraudulent transfer of property in May 2019. The Complaint states: “Plaintiff became aware of the fraudulent transfer of title to the Defendant in May 2019, when her daughter, Tracy Hall, learned that her brother, Earl Hall, had taken loans out on the property.” (Compl., ¶¶ 13, 24.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s first cause of action for financial elder abuse would have needed to be brought by May 2023, within four years of her discovery. Plaintiff’s third cause of action for fraud would have needed to be brought by May 2022, within three years of her discovery. Plaintiff’s second cause of action for quiet title and declaratory relief are based on Plaintiff’s financial elder abuse or fraud claims and subject to the same statute of limitations. Here, Plaintiff filed this action February 27, 2024. Thus, over four years have passed since the discovery of the alleged financial abuse, which is past the statute of limitations for Plaintiff’s first, second, third, and fourth causes of action.

 

Thus, on the face of the Complaint, Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action are barred under the statute of limitations, absent tolling under an applicable doctrine. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.  

E.     Fifth Cause of Action: Punitive Damages

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for punitive damages.

 

A request for punitive damages is not a cause of action but a type of remedy. While punitive damages can be sought in a lawsuit, they are incident to a cause of action and cannot constitute the basis thereof. (Gold v. Los Angeles Democratic League (1975) 49 Cal. App. 3d 365, 373.)  “A punitive damage claim depends upon a viable claim for compensatory damages for its vitality.” (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1137, as modified on denial of reh'g (Feb. 27, 2014).)  

 

Therefore, Plaintiff cannot name her fifth cause of action “punitive damages.”  Plaintiff must place any request for punitive damages in the prayer for relief section.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED as to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action without leave to amend.

 

F.      Conclusion

 

The demurrer to Plaintiff’s first, second, third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

The demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
