DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 22STCV29999    Hearing Date: August 12, 2024    Dept: 56

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

MARKELLA THANOU,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

BEVERLY HILLS REHABILITATION

CENTRE, LLC; REHAB CENTRE OF

BEVERLY HILLS; ROGER T. WANG, M.D.; and DOES 1 TO 20,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  22STCV29999

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Date: August 12, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Beverly Hills Rehabilitation Centre, LLC (named as “Beverly Hills Rehabilitation Centre, LLC” and “Rehab Centre of Beverly Hills”) (“BHRC”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Markella Thanou (“Plaintiff”)

 

            The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

             This case stems from a complaint ("Complaint") filed by Plaintiff against Defendants BHRC and Roger T. Wang, MD on September 14, 2022.  The Complaint asserts causes of action for (1) general negligence; (2) professional negligence; (3) wrongful death; (4) elder abuse; and (5) violation of the Eighth Amendment.

 

            On April 26, 2024, BHRC filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (the “Motion for Judgment”) as to the causes of action in the Complaint for general negligence, professional negligence, wrongful death, elder abuse and violation of the Eighth Amendment.  On the same day, BHRC filed a Motion to Strike certain portions of the Complaint. 

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Motion for Judgment on August 5, 2024, and BHRC filed a reply on August 6, 2024.  No opposition to the Motion to Strike has been filed.

 

MEET AND CONFER

             BHRC has complied with the statutory obligation to meet and confer.  

 

DISCUSSION

Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

Legal Standard 

            A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint or the complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(c)(1)(B).)¿¿¿ 

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings is analogous to a general demurrer. (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.)  Like demurrers, motions for judgment on the pleadings challenge the legal sufficiency of the allegations, not their veracity.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿¿ 

¿ 

All facts alleged in the complaint are deemed admitted, and the complaint is given a reasonable interpretation as a whole and all parts in their context.  (Lance, 44 Cal.App.4th at 198.)  The court does not, however, do so for “conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or [judicially noticed] facts…”  (Stevenson Real Estate Servs., Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Servs., Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-20.)¿ 

¿ 

Any defects must either appear on the face of the pleading, or else be taken by judicial notice.  (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-22.)  A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be granted when, “under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Southern California Edison Co. v. City of Victorville (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 218, 227.)¿ 

 

Timing 

            A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be brought by a defendant at any time after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438(f)).  Unless a court orders otherwise, a motion for judgment on the pleadings may not be made after entry of a pre-trial conference order, (Cal. Rules of Court 3.720-3.730) or 30 days before the initial trial date, whichever is later.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(e).  This case has not yet been set for trial, and no pre-trial conference order was entered in this case.  The Motion for Judgment is, therefore, timely.  

 

Plaintiff filed an untimely opposition to the Motion for Judgment.  Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 1005, subsection (b), a timely opposition was due nine court days before the hearing date of August 12, 2024; that is, on or before July 30, 2024.  Plaintiff did not serve an opposition until August 5, 2024, which is six days after the expiration of the statutory deadline to file a timely opposition.  Nevertheless, the court has the discretion to consider late or defective papers in favor of the strong policy favoring the disposition of the case on the merits.  (Kapitanski v. Von’s (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 29, 32.)  BHRC has not demonstrated that it has suffered prejudice from the late filing of the opposition.  In any event, the Court has also considered BHRC’s reply brief.

 

Motion

In BHRC’s Motion for Judgment, BHRC argues that the Complaint is devoid of any facts or allegations related to Plaintiff’s relationship with the decedent, Demetrios Fotinatos (“Decedent”).  BHRC thus contends that Plaintiff lacks standing to bring this action. 

 

Additionally, BHRC contends that the Complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim for general negligence, professional negligence, wrongful death, elder abuse and violation of the Eighth Amendment. 

 

Opposition

In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that she is the surviving spouse of the Decedent, and submits a declaration stating that she is “the spouse of the deceased Mr. Demetrios Fotinatos”, attaching therewith a copy of a License and Certificate of Marriage.  (Declaration of Markella Thanou, ¶ 3; Exh. 1.)  Plaintiff thus contends that she has satisfied the standing requirement. 

 

Moreover, Plaintiff contends that the Complaint sufficiently states causes of action for wrongful death, general negligence and professional negligence.  Plaintiff asserts that the Complaint alleged that the Decedent was admitted to BHRC’s facility, that BHRC owed the Decedent a legal duty to use due care, that BHRC breached that duty by failing to take reasonable care in the feeding of the deceased, and that the breach was a proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury and death of the Decedent.

 

Reply

In reply, BHRC argues that Plaintiff’s declaration falls short of establishing standing as she merely asserted, “I am the spouse of deceased…”  The declaration, however, fails to assert under penalty of perjury that she also remained married to the Decedent at the time of his death.  Thus, the declaration does not establish standing to bring a wrongful death cause of action, as it is unclear whether the marriage was dissolved prior to the Decedent’s passing. 

 

BHRC further asserts that Plaintiff lacks standing to bring the causes of action for general negligence and professional negligence as she has failed to comply with the specific requirements of Code of Civil Procedure, section 377.32, in order to proceed as the Decedent’s successor in interest.  That is, she has not filed an affidavit or declaration demonstrating that she is Decedent’s successor in interest and that no other person has a superior right to commence this proceeding.

 

BHRC also points out that Plaintiff did not oppose or address BHRC’s arguments regarding the failure to state causes of action for elder abuse and violation of the Eighth Amendment, and asserts that Plaintiff thus concedes to the merit of BHRC’s arguments.

 

1st Cause of Action: Wrongful Death

The elements of a wrongful death action are a tort (negligence or other wrongful act), resulting death (causation), and loss (damages) suffered by the heirs.  When the tort is negligence, plaintiff must, of course, prove defendant's duty and breach thereof.  (Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968; Jacoves v. United Merchandising Corp. (1992) 9 Cal.App.4th 88, 105, 11.)

 

Only those persons expressly set forth in Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP) § 377.60 have standing to maintain a wrongful death action.  (Ceja v. Rudolph & Sletten, Inc. (2013) 56 Cal.4th 1113, 1118; Holguin v. Flores (2004) 122 Cal.App.4th 428, 437-438.)  CCP § 377.60 limits the persons who may assert a wrongful death claim to: the victim decedent's surviving spouse, domestic partner, children and issue of deceased children; or, if there is no surviving issue, the persons, including the surviving spouse or domestic partner, who would be entitled to succeed to the estate if the victim had died intestate.  (Prob. Code, § 6400, et seq.)  To qualify as a "surviving spouse" in a wrongful death action, Plaintiff must establish that they were married to the decedent at the time of the decedent's death.  (Prob. Code, § 78.)

 

Here, while Plaintiff indicated under Section 12 of the Complaint that “Plaintiff is the wife of the deceased, Demetrios Fontinatos”, nowhere is it specifically alleged that Plaintiff was a “surviving spouse”, that is, that she was married to Decedent at the time of death.  The category of persons eligible to bring wrongful death actions is strictly construed. (Soto v. BorgWarner Morse TEC Inc. (2015) 239 Cal. App. 4th 165, 188.)  A former spouse is not an heir and thus has no standing to bring a wrongful death action.  (Villacampa v. Russel (1986) 178 Cal. App. 3d 906, 908-909.)  A plaintiff must plead and prove his or her status as a proper statutory wrongful death claimant. (Nelson v. County of Los Angeles (2003) 113 Cal. App. 4th 783, 789.) 

 

Plaintiff attempts to cure the defect in her Complaint by submitting a declaration in her opposition stating that she is the spouse of the Decedent and attaching a copy of the Certificate of Marriage.  In ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, a judge is confined to the facts alleged in the challenged pleading.  Presenting extrinsic evidence is not proper.  (Gerawan Farming, Inc. v Lyons (2000) 24 Cal.4th 468, 515–516; Sykora v State Dep't of State Hosps. (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 1530, 1534.)  In any case, as in the Complaint, Plaintiff’s declaration is also insufficient to establish standing as a “surviving spouse” since it does not set forth that she remained married to the Decedent at the time of his death, and it is unclear whether or not her marriage with the Decedent was dissolved prior to the Decedent’s passing.

 

Moreover, the Complaint does not sufficiently state facts to establish BHRC’s duty of care.  While Plaintiff, in her opposition, asserts that the Decedent was admitted to BHRC’s facility, and that BHRC thus owed a duty of care, the Complaint does not sufficiently set forth those allegations.  Rather, in a conclusory fashion, the Complaint merely states that “Defendants, and DOES 1 to 20 were negligent in the diagnosis, care and treatment of Mr. Demetrios Fotinatos”, but without stating facts to support that BHRC had a duty of care in the first place.

 

Additionally, the facts are wanting as to allegations to support damages suffered by Plaintiff as a result of Decedent’s death.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that as alleged, Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute wrongful death and fails to establish standing to bring a wrongful death cause of action.  

 

2nd and 3rd Causes of Action: General Negligence and Professional Negligence

Foremost, a “survival action” is distinct from a wrongful death action. A wrongful death action is an independent claim on behalf of the decedent's heirs for damages they personally suffered on account of the death (CCP § 377.60 et seq.); a survival action (e.g. personal injury action), on the other hand, “survives” to decedent's estate for the purpose of recovering damages that would have been awardable personally to decedent had he or she lived (CCP § 377.20 et seq.)  Unlike a cause of action for wrongful death, “a survivor cause of action is not a new cause of action that vests in the heirs on the death of the decedent.  It is instead a separate and distinct cause of action which belonged to the decedent before death but, by statute, survives that event.  (Grant v. McAuliffe (1953) 41 Cal.2d 859, 864.)

 

If a person dies having a cause of action for injuries suffered during life, the claim “survives” to their estate under CCP § 377.20 and may be prosecuted by a duly appointed executor, administrator, or guardian on behalf of the estate.  (CCP §§ 377.20, 377.30; Adams v. Sup.Ct. (Centinella Freeman Regional Med. Ctr.) (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 71, 78-79.)  If there is no personal representative for the estate (e.g., estate not probated or probate completed), the “survival” action may be prosecuted by the decedent's “successor in interest”—i.e., the person(s) who succeeds, by will or intestacy, to the cause of action or to the particular item of property that is the subject of the action. (CCP §§ 377.10, 377.11, 377.30, 377.31; Olson v. Toy (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 818, 821.)

 

An action that survives the death of a person passes to the decedent’s successor-in-interest; and an action may be commenced by the decedent’s personal representative, or, if none, by the decedent’s successor-in-interest. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.30.)  

 

Here, the Complaint, broadly construed, appears to attempt to allege a survival action of negligence on behalf of Decedent in that Plaintiff alleges that Decedent’s injuries and death were proximately caused by the negligence of BHRC.  Therefore, Plaintiff must make a motion pursuant to CCP section 377.30 and comply with the requirements of CCP section 377.32 in order to obtain an order to act as the Decedent’s personal representative, and more clearly state a survival cause of action on behalf of the Decedent.

 

Going into the elements of the causes of action, the elements of general negligence are: (1) legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care;  (2) breach of duty; (3) causation;  and (4) damge.  (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318; (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.)  The elements of professional negligence, on the other hand, are: (1) duty of care owed plaintiff to use skill, prudence and diligence as other members of profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) breach; (3) causation;  and (4) damage.  (Burgess v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal. 4th 1064, 1077; County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318; Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 746-47; Powell v. Kleinman (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 112, 122. 

 

As discussed above under the wrongful death cause of action, the Complaint does not sufficiently state facts to establish BHRC’s duty of care.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that the Complaint similarly fails to sufficiently state a cause of action for negligence, and Plaintiff fails to establish standing to bring the cause of action on behalf of the Decedent.

 

            4th Cause of Action: Elder Abuse

The elements of elder abuse are: (1) defendant had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care; (2) defendant  knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs; (3) defendant denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult's basic needs; and (4) the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering.  (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 407 (“the facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.”)  Here, Plaintiff has similarly failed to adequately plead the underlying conduct required to state a claim for elder abuse, or facts sufficient to demonstrate that BHRC abused or neglected Decedent, nor does the Complaint set forth in the first place that BHRC had the responsibility to meet the basic needs of the Decedent as an elder or dependent adult.

 

Moreover, Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657.3, subdivision (d), delineates who has standing to bring an elder abuse lawsuit after the death of an elder or dependent adult. Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657.3, subdivision (d) states: “Upon petition, after the death of the elder or dependent adult, the right to maintain an action shall be transferred to the personal representative of the decedent, or if none, to the person or persons entitled to succeed to the decedent’s estate.”  Plaintiff thus fails to comport with the statutory requirements to support a standing to bring a cause of action for elder abuse.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for elder abuse, and similarly fails to establish standing to bring the cause of action.

 

5th Cause of Action: Violation of the Eighth Amendment

The Eighth Amendment prohibits certain types of punishment: excessive bail, excessive fines, and cruel and unusual punishments.  (Austin v. United States, 509 U.S. 602, 609 (1993) [The purpose of the Eighth Amendment, putting the Bail Clause to one side, was to limit the government’s power to punish.”]; Timbs v. Indiana, No. 17-1091, slip op. at 2 (U.S. Feb. 20, 2019) [“Like the Eighth Amendment’s proscriptions of ‘cruel and unusual punishment’ and '[e]xcessive bail,’ the protection against excessive fines guards against abuses of government’s punitive or criminal-law-enforcement authority.”]  These prohibitions were intended to protect persons convicted of crimes from government abuses of power.   (Ingraham v. Wright, 430 U.S. 651, 666 (1977); Weems v. United States (1910) 217 U.S. 349, 372–73.)

 

Clearly, the Eighth Amendment does not apply to the Plaintiff's claims, as there is no government involvement in this civil action.

 

In sum, the Motion for Judgment is GRANTED in its entirety for failure to state a cause of action.

 

Leave to Amend

Leave to amend should be given if there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured.  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.¿¿(Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.¿ [Citation.]¿ Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given."  (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

Based on the allegations in the Complaint, read as a whole, there is a reasonable possibility that Plaintiff can state the first through the fourth causes of action, but not the fifth cause of action for violation of the Eighth Amendment.  The Court will, therefore, grant leave to amend the Complaint so as to give Plaintiff an opportunity to establish her standing to bring the causes of action and to sufficiently state her causes of action, except as to the fifth cause of action.  Additionally, if Plaintiff desires to bring survival causes of action as described above, Plaintiff must comply with the necessary statutory requirements to do so.

 

Motion to Strike

Generally, a motion to strike all or a portion of a pleading must be filed within the time allowed to respond to that pleading.  (CCP § 435(b)(1); Cal Rules of Ct 3.1322(b); City & County of San Francisco v Strahlendorf (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1911, 1913.)  Therefore, a motion to strike a complaint or cross-complaint must generally be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint.  (CCP §§ 412.20(a)(3), 432.10).  A defendant may not file a motion to strike after filing an answer. A judge has the discretion, however, to consider the merits of the motion under the judge's authority to strike improper matter from pleadings at any time.  (CCP § 436(a);  CPF Agency Corp. v R&S Towing Serv. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1014, 1020–1021.) 

 

In any event, the Court incorporates the foregoing discussion and analysis of the Court as to the Motion for Judgment.  Since the Court has GRANTED the Motion for Judgement in its entirety, the Motion to Strike is MOOT.

                       

RULING

Based on all the foregoing, the court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion for Judgment in its entirety, with 30 days leave to amend.  The Motion to Strike is MOOT.

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

RONDA JONES,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

B & B STABLES, et al.,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  23STCV21443

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND COMPLAINT

 

Date: August 12, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Ronda Jones (“Plaintiff”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendants B & B Stables; Robert D. Buell; and Mary D. Buell (collectively, “Defendants”)

 

            The Court has considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

             On September 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint (“Complaint”) against Defendants for (1) Violation of Civil Rights - Unruh Act, Civil Code § 51; (2) Violation of the California Business and Professions Code - §17200, et seq.; and (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.  Plaintiff’s matter arises out of a business/patron relationship wherein Plaintiff was allegedly treated differently based on her race, and was evicted from a stable despite tendering full monthly payment of her stable rent at all times during her tenancy. 

 

            On February 1, 2024, the Court dismissed Plaintiff’s Complaint without prejudice, for Plaintiff’s failure to submit a request for entry of default.  (2/1/24 Minute Order.)  Plaintiff filed a Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Dismissal on February 26, 2024, which the Court granted on June 10, 2024.  In the June 10, 2024 Minute Order, the Court also allowed Plaintiff thirty days to file a motion to amend the Complaint due to the passing of Robert Buell (one of the Defendants), in order to name the Estate of Robert Buell or another representative as a defendant in place of Robert Buell.

 

            On July 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint (the “Motion”) to add the Estate of Robert Buell as Defendant.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests leave to add a cause of action for breach of contract.  Defendants filed an opposition on             July 29, 2024, and Plaintiff filed a reply on August 2, 2024.

             

DISCUSSION

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on such terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading …”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)  Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties in the same lawsuit.  Thus, the court's discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.  (Nestle v. Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939; Mabie v. Hyatt (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 581, 596 [“If discovery and investigation develop factual grounds justifying a timely amendment to a pleading, leave to amend must be liberally granted.”].)  The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified: “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend; and, where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.”  (Morgan v. Sup.Ct. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530; Mabie v. Hyatt, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at p. 596.) 

 

Courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial,” absent prejudice to the adverse party.  (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761, 135.)  If the plaintiff is the party seeking leave to amend (knowing the trial will be delayed), proximity to the trial date is not ground for denial.  As long as no prejudice to the defendant is shown, the liberal policy regarding amendment prevails and it is an abuse of discretion to refuse the amendment.  (Mesler v. Bragg Mgmt. Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 297.)  

 

If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  (Hirsa v. Sup. Ct. (Vickers) (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence or added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc. (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488; see also P & D Consultants, Inc. v. City of Carlsbad (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 1332, 1345.)  

 

            Here, the Court finds no indication that the amendment's purpose is dilatory. At this stage, the Defendants would not be prejudiced by allowing the amendment sought since a trial has not yet been set, nor has an answer been filed.  Moreover, the amendments are based upon the same set of facts already pleaded.  (City of Stanton v. Cox (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 1557, 1563 [“If new facts are being alleged, prejudice may easily result because of the inability of the other party to investigate the validity of the factual allegations while engaged in trial or to call rebuttal witnesses. If the same set of facts supports merely a different theory…no prejudice can result.”].)

 

            In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not comply with California Rules of Court (“CRC”) rule 3.1324.

 

Pursuant to CRC rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend must: (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered; and (2) state what allegations are proposed to be deleted from or added to the previous pleading and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, such allegations are located.  CRC rule 3.1324(b) requires a separate declaration that accompanies the motion, stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

 

            Here, Plaintiff attached a Proposed First Amended Complaint to her Motion and indicated that the only changes made were the addition of the phrase “the Estate of” preceding the name Robert D. Buell, as well as the new Breach of Contract cause of action.  In her reply, Plaintiff provided a more detailed description of the proposed changes to the Complaint by indicating the page, paragraph and line number where the changes in the allegations are located, and by highlighting them in bold typeface.  (Reply, pp. 3-4.)

 

The Court, thus, finds that Plaintiff has substantially complied with the procedural requirements of CRC rule 3.1324, and any minor deviation shall give way to the liberal policy regarding allowance of amendments.  Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, the courts have held that “there is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.”  (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296-97; Ventura v. ABM Industries, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 268.)

           

RULING

            In view of the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the Motion.  Plaintiff shall have 10 days’ leave to file an amended complaint.

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

DENNIS DEVAUGHN, KEITH ANDERSON, CHRISTINA FAVELA,

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

 

TURNING POINT NETWORK LLC, LISA PERONA REYES, TRESOR TULANDA, KEVIN LEWIS,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                

 

 

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION.            

 

 

      CASE NO.:  24STCV03808

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER RESTRICTING DISCOVERY AND TO LIMIT THE EXTENT AND USE OF DISCOVERY METHOD

 

Date: August 12, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants and Cross-Complainants TURNING POINT NETWORK LLC (“TPN”), LISA PERONA REYES, TRESOR TULANDA, KEVIN LEWIS (collectively, “Defendants”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs and Cross-Defendants DENNIS DEVAUGHN, KEITH ANDERSON, CHRISTINA FAVELA (collectively, “Plaintiffs”)

 

            The Court has considered the moving and opposition papers.  No reply has been filed.  Any reply to the opposition was required to have been filed and served at least five court days prior to the hearing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

BACKGROUND

             This action arises from an alleged agreement between Plaintiffs and Defendants.  On February 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants asserting the following causes of action: (1) declaratory relief; (2) damages for breach of partnership contract; and (3) fraud.

 

            On May 29, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion for Protective Order Restricting Discovery and to Limit the Extent and Use of Discovery Method (the “Motion”).  On July 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition.

             

DISCUSSION

            Defendants bring this Motion on the basis of Welfare and Institutions Code (“WIC”) section 5328.  Defendants contend that under WIC § 5328, all information and records obtained in the course of providing services to either voluntary or involuntary recipients shall be confidential.  On this basis, Defendants move for a protective order prohibiting Plaintiffs from issuing a subpoena to the California Department of Developmental Services (“DDS”) as well as to persons employed by the DDS regarding TPN or TPN’s billing or any other records.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs point out that they do not intend to propound discovery on the DDS, but rather they intend to subpoena the administrative and financial records of TPN from regional centers.  (Declaration of Freddie Fletcher in Opposition to the Motion (“Fletcher Decl.”), ¶ 29.) Plaintiffs also argue that they have no intention of seeking confidential information and records of the recipients of services, but instead they intend to subpoena the financial records of TPN – a provider of services.  (Id., ¶ 32.) 

 

            As an initial matter, the Court infers from the declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel that no subpoena or any discovery has yet been served at the time of the filing of the Motion.  (Id., ¶¶ 25-26.) 

 

            California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 2020.010 permits discovery of non-party witnesses through oral and written depositions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.010(a)(1), (2); Hawkins v. TACA International Airlines, S.A. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 466, 476.) A deposition subpoena may command the attendance and testimony of the deponent, the production of business records, or both. (Id., § 2020.020.) CCP section 1987.1 provides that “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by [a party or a witness] . . . may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.”

 

            Thus, a party or non-party, after service of a subpoena, may seek an order from the court quashing or modifying it and requesting a protective order.  Defendants cite no basis or authority to support this Motion seeking to prevent discovery prior to its service, apart from their reliance on WIC section 5328.

 

WIC section 5328, subdivision (a) provides, in relevant part, “[a]ll information and records obtained in the course of providing services under ... to either voluntary or involuntary recipients of services are confidential.”  The statute, however, goes on to delineate numerous exceptions pursuant to which otherwise confidential information “shall be disclosed.”  (Id., § 5328, subd.(a)(1)–(27).) Pursuant to one such exception, confidential information is disclosable “[t]o the courts, as necessary to the administration of justice.”  (Id., § 5328, subd. (a)(6).)  This provision “contemplates use of the information or records ‘as necessary to the administration of justice’ in some pending judicial action or proceeding.” (County of Riverside v. Superior Court (1974) 42 Cal.App.3d. 478, 481.)  Therefore, “[t]he clear language of Welfare and Institutions Code section 5328, subdivision [(a)(6)] authorizes the disclosure of the evidence ... unless such evidence is otherwise nondisclosable.”  (Mavroudis v. Superior Court (1980) 102 Cal.App.3d 594, 602.)

 

Moreover, the Court takes notice of the Regional Center of Orange County's Guidelines for Access to Vendor Records, which classifies “documents which contain confidential consumer information” as confidential, and states that “[c]onfidential records shall be produced pursuant to a valid subpoena, with identifying consumer information redacted.”  (Fletcher Decl., ¶ 21.)

 

Therefore, even assuming that records sought to be discovered are confidential, there are exceptions to allow their discovery pursuant to a valid subpoena, and procedures are in place to protect the confidentiality of the information, including redaction of consumer information.

 

RULING

Accordingly, the Court finds the Motion is without basis, and is therefore DENIED.
