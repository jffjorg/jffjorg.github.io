DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV02691    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: B

 

 

Hearing:             11/05/2024

Re:                         19STCV02691 Ryan Nathan Goldstein vs Aria Servatjoo, et al.

 

 

Plaintiff Ryan Nathan Goldstein’s Request for Default Judgment

 

Defendant: Aria Servatjoo

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff Ryan Nathan Goldstein’s request for default judgment against Defendant Aria Servatjoo is DENIED.

           

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from an alleged January 20, 2017, assault. Plaintiff Ryan Nathan Goldstein (“Plaintiff”) alleges that Defendant Aria Servatjoo (“Aria”) became belligerent and assaulted Plaintiff. Plaintiff further alleges that Defendants Parviz Servatjoo (“Parviz”) and Nahid Servatjoo (“Nahid”) knew Aria was violent and dangerous and failed to prevent Aria from injuring Plaintiff.

 

On January 18, 2019, Plaintiff filed a complaint alleging six causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) assault; (3) battery; (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (5) negligent infliction of emotional distress; and (6) negligent supervision.

 

On June 7, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On August 27, 2024, Defendants Parviz and Nahid were dismissed.

 

On October 23, 2024, Defendant Aria (hereinafter “Defendant”) entered default. At the same time Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendant.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1800.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff seeks damages in the total amount of $230,675.00, representing $28,532.00 in special damages, $200,000.00 in general damages, and $2,143.00 in costs.

 

Plaintiff fails to provide admissible evidence supporting their claim for special damages. Plaintiff’s declaration claims to provide medical bills as Exhibits A through D, however, no such medical bills are provided.

 

Additionally, Plaintiff seeks general damages in an amount 7x greater than pleaded special damages. Plaintiff appears to seek punitive damages based on the case law they provide in Exhibit B. However, the request for default judgment fails to pray for punitive damages. Punitive damages that are not properly pleaded may not be awarded. (CCP §425.115.) Additionally, Plaintiff must serve Defendant with a a statement of damages identifying punitive damages. (Ibid.) Plaintiff must also submit evidence that punitive damages are not excessive in light of Defendant’s ability to pay. (Cummings Medical Corp v. Occupational Medical Corp. (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1291)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED. This constitutes Plaintiff’s fifth default judgment denial for deficiencies. Accordingly, the Court dismisses the action without prejudice for failure to prosecute and timely obtain default judgment pursuant to CCP sections 583.410 and CRC 3.110(h).

Re:                         ANTHONY RAMIREZ, A MINOR BY AND THROUGH HIS GUARDIAN AD LITEM, MARY TUCCIO, et al. vs NORWALK-LA MIRADA UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT (20STCV33026)

 

 

Petitioner Mary Tuccio’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Mary Tuccio’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a November 22, 2019, incident in which Claimant Anthony Ramirez (“Claimant”) alleges Defendant Norwalk-La Mirada Unified School District (“Defendant”) negligently owned and maintained public property, causing Claimant to sustain injury as a result.

 

On August 31, 2020, Claimant filed an action against Defendant for four causes of action: (1) dangerous condition of public property; (2) negligence; (3) negligent infliction of emotional distress; and (4) negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of an employee.

 

On September 1, 2020, Petitioner Mary Tuccio (“Petitioner”)’s application for appointment as Claimant’s guardian ad litem was granted.

 

On October 18, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability on behalf of Claimant.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $99,000.00 from Defendant.

 

Legal Standard

 

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Id., at p. 1606.) 

 

Probate Code section 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows: 

 

The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code, § 3601, subd. (a).) 

 

In cases pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955, subd. (a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955, subd. (a)(2).)

 

Discussion

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $99,000.00. Of the $99,000 settlement, $10,678.19 is to be deducted for medical expenses, $24,750.00 for attorney’s fees, and $29,488.19 for costs. Therefore, Claimant’s proposed net settlement amount is $34,083.62.

 

The Petition sets forth information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise pursuant to CRC Rule 7.950. (MC-350 Attachment 8 [medical records].) Additionally, attorney’s fees represent 25% of the total settlement agreement. (MC-350 Attachment 13a.) Claimant’s counsel provides their retainer agreement as Attachment 17a.

 

The Petition requests an additional order, requiring Petitioner to use Claimant’s settlement for Claimant’s private school tuition and “nothing else.” (MC-350 Item 20.) The order would also require Petitioner to inform the Court immediately if Claimant ceases to attend private school. The Court finds this additional order reasonable and in the best interest of Claimant.

 

However, the Court must deny the Petition for deficiencies. Counsel completed Item 17e, indicating that they represent or are employed by another party or insurance carrier in this matter. Despite completing this item, Counsel fails to provide the required Attachment 17e identifying and explaining this relationship.

 

This is Petitioner’s first denial for deficiencies. Petitioner and Counsel are advised that further deficient filings risk sanctions pursuant to CCP § 128.7 and CRC Rule 2.30.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES the petition to confirm minor’s compromise.

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits.  (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1332(c).)  The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance. (Ibid.) Good cause includes the unavailability of an essential lay or expert witness, party, or trial counsel; “the substitution of trial counsel, but only where there is an affirmative showing that the substitution is required in the interests of justice;” the addition of a new party; a party’s excused inability to obtain evidence; or a significant, unanticipated change in the case. (Ibid.)

 

Furthermore, the Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted:

 

(1) The proximity of the trial date;

(2) Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party;

(3) The length of the continuance requested;

(4) The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance;

(5) The prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance;

(6) If the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay;

(7) The court's calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials;

(8) Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial;

(9) Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance;

(10) Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and

(11) Any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.

 

(CRC Rule 3.1332(d)(1)-(11).)

 

III.              Discussion

 

Cross-Defendant moves for an order continuing the trial date from November 25, 2024, to May 26, 2025, or any other date thereafter.

 

Cross-Defendant represents that additional time is needed to complete essential discovery, including: (1) taking Plaintiff’s deposition; (2) compelling Plaintiff to provide verified, substantive responses to Cross-Defendant’s written discovery requests; and (3) if necessary after completing Plaintiff’s deposition and reviewing Plaintiff’s substantive, verified discovery responses, conducting an independent medical examination of Plaintiff. (Motion p. 2:6-11.) Cross-Defendant also requests additional time to seek dismissal of this matter. (Ibid.)

 

Cross-Defendant represents that Plaintiff has remained unresponsive to their counsel and all other parties in this matter since March 2023. (Motion p. 2:17.) Plaintiff’s counsel was relieved on March 7, 2024. (Ibid. at 2:22.) Cross-Defendant has diligently attempted to obtain discovery and Plaintiff’s deposition, yet Plaintiff continuously fails to respond or appear. (Englet ¶ 4.) The discovery cut-off date in this matter was October 25, 2024. Furthermore, Cross-Defendant seeks terminating sanctions based on Plaintiff’s complete failure to cooperate, to be ruled on by the Court shortly. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)

 

The Court considers the circumstances of the instant case and the arguments presented in the Motion. Although the trial date has been continued on three previous occasions, due to the unavailability of Plaintiff, the proximity of the trial date, lack of opposition, and Cross-Defendant's intention to seek terminating sanctions, the Court finds that there is good cause to continue the trial.

 

For this reason, Cross-Defendant’s Motion to Continue Trial Date is GRANTED.
