I.            INTRODUCTION

On March 4, 2022, plaintiffs Xueyun Zou (“Zou”) and DL Trucking, Inc. (“DL Trucking”) filed this action against defendants Yao Guo (“Guo”), individually and as trustee of the Guo Protection Trust, and Gosdom, Inc. (“Gosdom”). In 2019, Guo allegedly portrayed herself as a movie producer of the films “Wally’s Wonderland” and “Fast & Furious 9” and fraudulently induced Plaintiff, who is 80-years old, into “investing” $400,000 in the production of those movies. These investments were memorialized in a Loan & Security Agreement and a Secured Promissory Note.

On August 29, 2023, DL Trucking filed the First Amended Complaint. The FAC omitted Zou as a plaintiff after the Honorable Colin P. Leis sustained Guo and Gosdom’s demurrers to all of Zou’s claims on the grounds that DL Trucking – not Zou – was a party to the Loan & Security Agreement and the Secured Promissory Note. DL Trucking alleges that it has received $30,000 from Guo but is still owed over $400,000 and 1% of the net revenues related to Wally’s Wonderland. (FAC, ¶ 19.)

On October 18, 2023, Gosdom filed a cross-complaint against Zou’s children, Keqing Deng (“K. Deng”) and Donghong Deng aka Jennifer Deng (“D. Deng”). Gosdom claims that Guo was “duped into making investment and loans” to nonparties Remington Chase and Kevin Robl, who held themselves out to be “elite” film financers but were allegedly running a Ponzi scheme. Gosdom claims that it paid $110,000 to DL Trucking and alleges that D. Deng and K. Deng misappropriated $80,000. Gosdom claims that D. Deng portrayed herself as an officer of DL Trucking and instructed Gosdom to make payments to D. Deng’s brother, K. Deng, for tax purposes.

On December 5, 2023, the clerk, at Gosdom’s request, entered default against K. Deng.

On January 5, 2024, DL Trucking and D. Deng (collectively, “Moving Parties”) filed this motion to quash Gosdom’s deposition subpoena to East West Bank, which seeks all documents relating to K. Deng’s bank accounts from January 2019 to the present, including copies of all cancelled checks written to D. Deng or DL Trucking. A notice to consumer was mailed to K. Deng at 209 Avenida Melisenda in San Dimas, California (the “San Dimas Address”), on December 8, 2023. Moving Parties argue that the notice to consumer was defective because K. Deng does not live at the San Dimas Address. According to Zou and D. Deng, who are K. Deng’s mother and sister, respectively, K. Deng lives in China and has never lived at the San Dimas Address; instead, only Zou and D. Deng reside at the San Dimas Address. Therefore, Moving Parties contend, the subpoena is void. DL Trucking and D. Deng also argue that the subpoena violates D. Deng and DL Trucking’s right to privacy and contend that the subpoena should be limited to documents from December 6, 2019, to the present, and exclude any bank records not connected to the subject matter of the case.

In opposition, Gosdom argues that the defective notice to consumer is irrelevant because Moving Parties lack standing to object to the subpoena. A subpoenaing party must give special notice and adhere to additional procedures when seeking the “personal records” of a “consumer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3.) Notice must be served on the consumer “personally, or at his or her last known address, or in accordance with Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 1010) of Title 14 of Part 3, or, if he or she is a party, to his or her attorney of record. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (b)(1).)  These additional requirements are intended to protect that person's right of privacy and allow the person the opportunity to seek a court order to quash or limit the subpoena before the records are disclosed. (Sasson v. Katash (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 119, 124.) Failure to comply with those requirements “shall be sufficient basis for the witness to refuse to produce the personal records sought by a subpoena duces tecum.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (k).)

Gosdom does not explain why it believes it complied with section 1985.3 despite Zou and D. Deng’s declarations stating that K. Deng does not live at the San Dimas Address, nor has he ever lived there. (Deng Decl., ¶ 4.)  It would undercut the purpose of section 1985.3 if a party could issue a subpoena for a consumer’s personal records and send a notice to an incorrect address, then insist that only that consumer had the standing to object on the grounds of improper notice when the consumer was not given notice of the subpoena as required by statute.

Furthermore, Gosdom’s subpoena is overbroad. Gosdom does not submit evidence that any payments were actually made to K. Deng’s accounts at East West Bank and it is implausible that the sample set of data in Guo’s declaration is the only information obtainable from Gosdom’s banking institution. In addition, Guo only declares that K. Deng wired $300,000 in December 2019 to the attorney-client trust account of counsel for Remington Chase and Kevin Robl. (Opp., Guo Decl., ¶ 3,) To the extent that Gosdom claims that it paid $110,000 to K. Deng from May 2020 to September 2021, this assertion is unsupported by any evidence.

Last, even if there was evidence to support the existence of these transactions, only records from May 2020 to the present and records reflecting transactions between K. Deng, Guo, Gosdom, Moving Parties and Zou would be relevant or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. 

In light of the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the motion to quash.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO COMPEL ANSWERS TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

January 31st, 2024

 

On October 27, 2022, plaintiff Ray Gomez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Benjamin Pena (“Defendant”) arising from a motor vehicle collision that occurred on November 12, 2021. On July 6, 2023 Defendant served Request for Production of Documents (Set One) on Plaintiff. No responses were received.  On August 15 and October 31, 2023, defense counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a letter requesting verified responses, but Plaintiff did not serve any responses. Defendant filed this motion on December 15, 2023.  

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) A party that fails to serve timely responses waives any objections to the request, including ones based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Id.)

Plaintiff did not oppose the Motion and it is undisputed responses were not served. Defendant’s motion is GRANTED and Plaintiff is ordered to serve verified responses without objections to Defendant’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) within 20 days of the date of this Order.

The Code of Civil Procedure provides that the court shall impose a monetary sanction against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) The Court finds evidence of neither. Accordingly, Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED and imposed against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $235 consisting of 1 hour at defense counsel’s hourly rate of $175 and $60 in filing fees, to be paid within 20 days of the date of this Order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO STRIKE  

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

January 31, 2024

 

 

 

 

On August 11, 2023, plaintiff Nora D. Giralt (“Plaintiff”) filed this action asserting causes of action for fraud, conversion, and breach of contract against defendants Norma Perez (“Defendant”) and Maria Gabriela Posadas. Plaintiff alleges that she gave $36,880 to Defendant after Defendant promised she would “invest the money and would then return the money to [Plaintiff] with 100% interest.” (See, e.g., Compl., ¶¶ 7, 17, 27, 36.)

On November 27, 2023, Defendant filed a responsive pleading with title “Answer to complaint AND COUNTERCLAIMS.DENIALS AND OBJECTIONS TO PLEADINGS CCP SECTION § 430.50.”

On December 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to strike Defendant’s counterclaims on the grounds: (1) that counterclaims have been abolished in California and that any cross-claims must be asserted in a separate cross-complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.80 and (2) Defendant’s attempt to bring cross-claims on behalf of co-defendant Gabriela Posadas and Does 1 through 10 constitutes the unauthorized practice of law because Defendant is self-represented and not an attorney.

On December 29, 2023, Defendant filed an amended responsive pleading entitled “First Amendment -Answer to Complaint-DENIALS AND OBJECTIONS TO PLEADINGS CCP SECTION § 430.50” (referred to herein as the “First Amended Answer”).

Defendant’s First Amended Answer renders Plaintiff’s motion to strike moot. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 472(a), “[a] party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time . . . after a . . . motion to strike is filed but before the . . . motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the . . . motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 472, subd. (a).)

Here, the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion to strike was scheduled for January 31,2024.  Therefore, any opposition brief had to be filed by January 18, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b) [opposition brief to motion due 9 court days before hearing date].) Accordingly, Defendant’s First Amended Answer is timely and Plaintiff’s motion to strike is taken off calendar as moot.
