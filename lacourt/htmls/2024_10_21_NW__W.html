DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV02916    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: W

LIDA SARKISIAN, et al. vs LEISURE HEALTHCARE, LLC DBA LEISURE GLEN POST ACUTE CARE CENTER, et al.

 

defendant’s petition to compel binding arbitration and to stay the superior court matter pending the hearing on the petition

 

Date of Hearing:        October 21, 2024                              Trial Date:       None

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV02916

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Leisure Healthcare, LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Lida Sarkisian (individually and as successor in interest to Susan Khodagulyan), Andranik Sarkisian, Gilbert Sarkisian, and Henrick Sarkisian

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiffs Lida Sarkisian, individually and as successor in interest to Susan Khodagulyan, Andranik Sarkisyan, Gilbert Sarkisyan and Henrik Sarkisyan against Leisure Healthcare LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center and B.V. General, Inc. dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center for survivor action – elder abuse (Welfare & Institutions Code §15600, et seq.), survivor action – negligence, and wrongful death. Decedent Susan Khodagulyan was admitted to Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center shortly after suffering a fall and being injured. Plaintiffs allege Defendants were aware of Decedent presented a fall risk. However, Defendants failed to staff the facility properly and as a result, the Decedent suffered a fall a few days later. By May 2023, Plaintiffs allege Decedent has suffered three or more falls in a three month period. During her residency, the Decedent continued to fall and as a result of the injuries sustained, Decedent passed away on January 27, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Leisure Healthcare, LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center’s Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration and to Stay the Superior Court Matter Pending the Hearing on the Petition is DENIED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Leisure Healthcare, LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center petitions the court for an order compelling Plaintiffs to arbitrate the controversy alleged in the complaint in binding arbitration and to stay the instant matter.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy . . . the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists,” unless the court finds that the right to compel arbitration has been “waived by the petitioner,” or that “grounds exist for recission” of the arbitration agreement. (CCP § 1281.2(a), (b).) 

 

A written arbitration agreement is “valid, enforceable and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” (CCP § 1281.) “The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.) “The general rule is that only a party to an arbitration agreement may enforce it.” (Ronay Family Limited Partnership v. Tweed (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 830, 837.) 

 

Defendants seek to compel Plaintiffs to arbitrate the claims alleged in their complaint against Defendants pursuant to the Arbitration Agreement entered into by Plaintiff Lida Sarkisian, in her individual capacity and in her representative capacity for the Decedent. Defendants argue the Arbitration Agreement was executed by the Decedent’s self-appointed agent, Lida Sarkisian, which provides that any dispute between the Resident (Decedent) and Defendants, including any action for injury or death arising from negligence, intentional tort and/or statutory causes of action will be determined by submission to binding arbitration. (Exh. A, Article 2.) The Arbitration Agreement further provides such agreement will be binding on all parties including the Resident’s heirs who bring any claims individually or in a representative capacity and the Resident’s representatives, agents, and heirs who execute this Agreement are doing so not only in their representative capacity for the Resident, but also in their individual capacity. (Exh. A, Article 4.) Defendants provide evidence Lida Sarkisian executed the Arbitration Agreement and signed the Agreement under penalty of perjury. (Exh. A.) Defendants also provide evidence that Lida Sarkisian executed a Declaration of Healthcare Agent form that attests, under the penalty of perjury, that she was Decedent’s appointed healthcare agent and thus, was authorized to execute the Admission Agreement and the Arbitration Agreement on the decedent’s behalf. (Exh. C.)

 

Plaintiffs do not dispute the existence of the Arbitration Agreement or that it was entered into by Plaintiff Lida Sarkisian. However, Plaintiffs oppose the motion on several grounds.

 

Agency

 

First, Plaintiffs oppose the motion on the grounds there is no evidence that any conduct on the part of the Decedent created an actual or ostensible agency in Lida Sarkisian. Plaintiffs cite Kinder v. Capistrano Beach Care Center, LLC (2023) 91 Cal. App. 5th 804 to support this contention. In Kinder, the arbitration agreement entered into on behalf of the resident and the residential skilled nursing facility was entered into on behalf of the resident’s adult children. The court found, however, the facility presented no evidence that the mother did anything to lead her children to believe they had the actual or ostensible authority to enter into arbitration agreements on her behalf. The court further found that a “defendant cannot prove a plaintiff consented to arbitration merely by showing the plaintiff stood idly by while the purported agent signed on his or her behalf.” (Kinder v. Capistrano Beach Care Center, LLC (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 804, 816.)

 

However, unlike Kinder, Defendants here have presented a declaration, under the penalty of perjury, attesting that an agency relationship had been created between the principal and the purported agent. As such, this goes beyond the representations of the purported agent alone and the burden now shifts to Plaintiffs. Plaintiff have not sufficiently demonstrated that this Appointment of Authorized Representative did not create actual agency. Nonetheless, for the reasons stated below, the court denies the motion to compel arbitration.

 

Healthcare Decision

 

Next, Plaintiffs oppose the motion on the grounds the execution of an arbitration agreement is not a “Healthcare Decision” within the authority of a “Healthcare Agent”. Plaintiffs contend even if Lida Sarkissian was her mother’s “healthcare agent” that would not render the Arbitration Agreement valid. Plaintiffs cite Harrod v. Country Oaks Partners LLC (2024) 15 Cal.5th 939 to support this contention. In Harrod, the Supreme Court stated “interpreting the term ‘health care decision’ to exclude optional and separate agreements to arbitrate fits best with the Legislature's decision to use that term in the Health Care Decisions Law to describe the scope of authority not only for those … who act pursuant to powers of attorney for health care, but also for surrogates, including next of kin or close friends.” (Harrod v. Country Oaks Partners, LLC (2024) 15 Cal.5th 939, 958.) However, the Supreme Court in Harrod did not only base its decision in Health Care Decisions Law but also the power of attorney that was executed in that case. There, the power of attorney defined health care decisions (§4617) and decisions after death (§4683) and authorized the power of attorney to (1) “consent, refuse consent, or withdraw consent to any medical care,” including care to artificially sustain life; (2) “choose or reject [the principal's] physician, other health care professionals or health care facilities;” (3) “receive and consent to the release of medical information;” and (4) authorize organ donation, an autopsy, and disposal of remains.” (Id. at p. 947-48.) The Supreme Court acknowledged that Civil Code section 2319 [necessary authority] “embodies the notion of implied authority—that an agent expressly granted a specific power should have sufficient authority to effectuate it.” (Id. at p. 960.) However, that power is limited. For a power of attorney is to be viewed as binding on the principal “’it must appear that the act done by the agent was in the exercise of the power delegated, and within its limits.’ [Citation.] “Put another way, an implied power ‘ “must be within the ultimate objective of the principal ....” ’ ” (Id. at p. 961.) The Supreme Court ultimately decided a power of attorney does not include the delegated authority to sign a optional, separate arbitration agreement.

 

Here, the facts differ. There is no power of attorney. Plaintiffs did sign a separate arbitration agreement, but also an agency declaration that gave Lida power to not only make healthcare decisions but to also the power of “signing documents on behalf of the Resident [Lida].” This Agency Declaration, unlike the Power of Attorney in Harrod, explicitly authorized healthcare decisions to be made by Lida and signing documents on the Decedent’s behalf, which included the Admission Agreement and Arbitration Agreement.

 

Parties to the Arbitration Agreement

 

Plaintiffs next oppose the motion on the grounds Andranik Sarkisian, Gilbert Sarkisian and Henrick Sarkisian are not parties to the arbitration agreement and therefore, not bound by its terms. Plaintiffs contend other than by general reference to unidentified “family members” or “heirs,” Plaintiffs Andranik Sarkisian, Gilbert Sarkisian and Henrick Sarkisian are not parties to the arbitration agreement, and there is no evidence that any of these Plaintiffs ever authorized anyone to execute or enter this arbitration agreement on their behalf.  There is also no evidence that the Decedent ever authorized Lida Sarkisian to bind either the Decedent or any of her children to the arbitration agreement. Plaintiffs cite Flores v. Evergreen at San Diego LLC (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 581 to support their contention. In Flores, a husband signed various documents for his wife who was admitted to the skilled nursing facility, which included two arbitration agreements. When the facility brought a compel arbitration, the trial court denied the petition finding there was no evidence that the wife did anything which caused the facility to believe that her husband was her agent and therefore could not be compelled to arbitration. The Court of Appeal agreed, finding there was no evidence that the wife gave authority for the husband to enter the arbitration agreements on her behalf and although she later signed a general power of attorney, that was created after the husband signed the admission documents.

 

Defendants rely on Ruiz v. Podolsky (2010) 50 Cal.4th 838 to bind the other parties to arbitration. Ruiz carved out an exception to the general rule that only parties to arbitration agreements are bound. In Ruiz, the California Supreme Court held that Code of Civil Procedure section 1295 permits patients who consent to arbitration to bind their heirs in actions for wrongful death where the language of the agreement manifests an intent to do so. The court notes another case, Avila v. Southern California Specialty Care, Inc. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 835, which has stated that while an action that’s primary basis for the wrongful death claim sounds in professional negligence as defined by MICRA, then the exception in Ruiz applies. However, if the primary basis is under the Elder Abuse Act, then section 1295 does not apply and neither does Ruiz’s exception. (Avila v. Southern California Specialty Care, Inc. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 835, 842.) Here, Plaintiffs have brought three causes of action – Elder Abuse, Negligence, and Negligence. Like Avila, it appears that the primary basis for Plaintiffs’ claims is rooted in violation of elder abuse.  The complaint alleges Defendants were negligent when they knew of the Decedent’s condition and failed to provide Decedent with the safety and protection she needed, was a substantial factor in causing multiple harms to Decedent.

 

As such, the court cannot find that Andranik Sarkisian, Gilbert Sarkisian and Henrick Sarkisian are bound by the arbitration agreement.

 

Conflicting Rulings

 

Lastly, Plaintiffs oppose the motion on the grounds that for the reasons discussed above, there may be conflicting outcomes between the survival causes of action and the wrongful death.

 

Section 1281.2(c), provides an exception to mandatory arbitration where “[a] party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.” All three requirements must be satisfied before section 1281.2, subdivision (c), may be invoked to deny a motion to compel arbitration. (Avila, supra, 20 Cal.App.5th at p. 845.) “For purposes of the statute, a third party is one who is neither bound by nor entitled to enforce the arbitration agreement.” (Daniels v. Sunrise Senior Living, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 674, 679.)

 

As discussed above, Andranik Sarkisian, Gilbert Sarkisian and Henrick Sarkisian are not bound by the arbitration agreement. As the wrongful death claim is based on the same facts as the survivorship claims, there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on common issues of law and fact as between the claims. Given the possibility of conflicting rulings, the court finds that no claims should be sent to arbitration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2(c).

 

Accordingly, Defendant Leisure Healthcare, LLC dba Leisure Glen Post Acute Care Center’s Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration and to Stay the Superior Court Matter Pending the Hearing on the Petition is DENIED.
