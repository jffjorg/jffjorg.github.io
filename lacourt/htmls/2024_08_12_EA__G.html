Case Number: 18PSCV00182    Hearing Date: August 12, 2024    Dept: G

Defendant Sunny Way Corporation’s Motion to Lift Bankruptcy Stay

Respondent: Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, and Juan Liu, and Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc.

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant Sunny Way Corporation’s Motion to Lift Bankruptcy Stay is DENIED as the Court exercises its discretion to stay proceedings.

BACKGROUND

This is an action for contractual fraud. Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma and Yunhan Chen are Chinese nationals. In 2015, they entered into an agreement with Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, and Juan Liu in which they agreed to form and invest funds in Plaintiff Topstar Freight Inc. (Topstar) for the purpose of obtaining an EB-5 investor visa for Chen. Pursuant to the agreement, Ma and Chen invested $800,000 in Topstar. Subsequently, Ma and Chen allegedly discovered Zhang and the Lius had managed Topstar for their own benefit and were transferring Topstar’s profits to Defendant Sunny Way Trucking Inc. (Sunny Way Trucking). They also allege Zhang and the Lius refused to return their investments.

On December 12, 2018, Ma, Chen, and Topstar (collectively, the Topstar Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Zhang, the Lius, Sunny Way Trucking, and Does 1-20, alleging the following causes of action: (1) anticipatory breach of contract, (2) money had and received, (3) conversion, (4) breach of contract, (5) money had and received, (6) conversion, (7) intentional misrepresentation, (8) negligent misrepresentation, (9) fraudulent concealment, (10) breach of fiduciary duty, (11) constructive fraud, (12) unfair competition, and (13) declaratory relief.

On July 7, 2021, the Topstar Plaintiffs filed an amendment to the Complaint that replaced Doe 4 with Defendant Sunny Way Corporation (Sunny Way).

On May 28, 2024, the Court stayed the present action following notice of pending bankruptcy proceedings that involve Sunny Way Trucking.

On June 10, 2024, Sunny Way Corporation filed the present motion.

A hearing on the present motion is set for August 12, 2024, along with a status conference re: bankruptcy and a hearing on motions to be admitted pro hac vice.

ANALYSIS

Sunny Way Corporation (Sunny Way) moves to lift the pending stay of proceedings as to Sunny Way. For the following reasons, the court DENIES their motion.

Legal Standard

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency.” (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.)  They also have inherent powers to manage and fashion procedures to control litigation and ensure the orderly administration of justice. (Cottle v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1376-79; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a)(3) and (a)(5).) But when a defendant commences bankruptcy proceedings in federal court, all civil proceedings against that defendant are automatically stayed. (Higgins v. Superior Court (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 973, 979.) This automatic stay “does not apply to non-debtor codefendants.” (Cross v. Cooper (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 357, 365, fn. 2.)

Discussion

In this case, Sunny Way argues the court should lift the stay for Sunny Way because Sunny Way is not involved in Sunny Way Trucking’s bankruptcy proceedings. (Motion, p. 3:18-24.) But while Sunny Way is correct in arguing the automatic bankruptcy stay is inapplicable to Sunny Way as a non-debtor defendant, the court nonetheless retains the discretion to stay proceedings to manage and fashion procedures to control litigation and ensure the orderly administration of justice.

Here, the Complaint alleges thirteen (13) causes of action against Defendant Sunny Way Corporation.  Sunny Way Trucking was Zhang and the Lius’ company. (Complaint, ¶ 26.) It alleges Zhang and the Lius required Ma and Chen to funnel some of their investments to Topstar through Sunny Way Trucking. (Complaint, ¶ 27.) It further alleges Zhang and the Lius siphoned off Topstar’s profits by creating an agency agreement between Topstar and Sunny Way Trucking. (Complaint, ¶ 28.) And last, the Complaint alleges each defendant, including Sunny Way Trucking and Sunny Way Corporation, is the agent, employee, representative, or alter ego of the other defendants. (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

Since Defendant Sunny Way Corporation is identified in multiple causes of action and allegations, the court finds there is a significant risk of duplicative proceedings if the stay is lifted against Sunny Way Corporation while proceedings against Sunny Way Trucking remain stayed.

Therefore, the court exercises its discretion to keep proceedings stayed against Sunny Way Corporation.

Accordingly, Sunny Way’s motion is DENIED.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Sunny Way’s motion to lift the stay of proceedings as to Sunny Way is DENIED.

Case Number: 21PSCV00617    Hearing Date: August 12, 2024    Dept: G

Defendants Fleet Capital, Inc., Sam Ostayan, and Inco Builders’ Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

Respondent: Plaintiff Diana Culkin

Plaintiff Diana Culkin’s Motion to Quash Non-Monetary Status

Respondent: Defendant Best Alliance Foreclosure and Lien Services Corp.

TENTATIVE RULING

The Motion for Summary Judgment filed by Defendants Fleet Capital, Inc., Sam Ostayan, and Inco Builders is GRANTED IN PART as to the first, second, and seventh causes of action against Defendants Inco Builders and Sam Ostayan and DENIED IN PART on all other grounds.

Plaintiff Diana Culkin’s Motion to Quash Non-Monetary Status is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This is a wrongful foreclosure action. On July 30, 2021, Plaintiff Diana Culkin, also known as Diana Jannette Marmolejo Corona, filed a complaint against Defendants Fleet Capital, Inc. (Fleet Capital), Best Alliance Foreclosure and Lien Services Corp. (Best Alliance), Inco Builders (Inco), and Does 1-25, alleging the following causes of action: (1) violation of California’s non judicial foreclosure statutes, (2) wrongful foreclosure, (3) conversion, (4) unfair business practices, and (5) declaratory relief.

On February 9, 2022, Inco filed a cross-complaint against Culkin, Michael Culkin, and Roes 1-10, alleging the following causes of action: (1) trespass and (2) slander of title.

On February 23, 2022, Fleet Capital and Inco filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the Complaint. On August 4, 2022, the court granted the motion with leave to amend.

On October 7, 2022, Culkin filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against the same defendants and Sam Ostayan, alleging the following causes of action: (1) violation of California’s nonjudicial foreclosure statutes, (2) wrongful foreclosure, (3) quiet title, (4) unfair business practices, and (5) declaratory relief. On January 5, 2023, the court sustained a demurrer to the FAC by Fleet Capital, Inco, and Ostayan.

On February 24, 2023, the court denied Fleet Capital and Inco’s motion to dismiss Culkin’s action for failure to file a timely amended complaint and deemed Culkin’s Second Amended Complaint (SAC) filed as of that date. The SAC is against the same defendants and alleges the following causes of action: (1) violation of California’s nonjudicial foreclosure statutes, (2) wrongful foreclosure, (3) quiet title, (4) promissory estoppel, (5) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (6) violation of the Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, (7) unfair business practices, and (8) declaratory relief.

On April 24, 2023, Culkin and Best Alliance entered into a stipulation with regards to Best Alliance’s nonmonetary status.

On May 4, 2023, the court sustained a demurrer and granted a motion to strike by Fleet Capital, Inco, and Ostayan as to the SAC. As a result, the operative SAC now alleges the remaining causes of action: (1) violation of California’s nonjudicial foreclosure statutes against Fleet Capital, Best Alliance, and Ostayan; (2) wrongful foreclosure against Fleet Capital, Best Alliance, Ostayan, and Inco; (3) quiet title against Best Alliance and Inco; (4) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing against Best Alliance; (6) violation of the Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act against Best Alliance; (7) unfair business practices against Fleet Capital, Best Alliance, and Ostayan; and (8) declaratory relief against Best Alliance.

On May 21, 2024, Fleet Capital, Ostayan, and Inco (collectively, Defendants) filed the present motion for summary judgment or adjudication.

On July 10, 2024, Culkin filed the present motion to quash.

A hearing on the present motions is set for August 12, 2024, along with a trial setting conference and case management conference on September 4, 2024.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendants ask the court to take judicial notice of seven (7) documents: (1) a grant deed recorded May 2, 2018, (2) a grant deed recorded January 14, 2019, (3) a notice of default and election to sell under deed of trust recorded February 17, 2021, (4) a notice of trustee sale recorded May 20, 2021, (5) a trustee’s deed upon sale recorded June 22, 2021, (6) this court’s January 5, 2023 ruling on Defendants’ demurrer to Culkin’s FAC, and (7) this court’s May 4, 2023 ruling on Defendants’ demurrer to Culkin’s SAC.

The existence and factual contents of documents including recorded deeds of trust, notices of default and trustee’s sale, and trustee’s deeds upon sale can be noticed under Evidence Code sections 452, subdivisions (c) and (h), and section 453. (Yvanova v. New Century Mortgage Corp., 62 Cal.4th 919, 924, fn. 1.) Furthermore, the court may take judicial notice of its own rulings. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

Accordingly, Defendants’ requests are GRANTED.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Defendants’ objections to Culkin’s evidence are SUSTAINED as to Nos. 1, 2, and 3.

To the extent the court treats Culkin’s motion to quash Luis Alvarado’s declaration of mailing filed in support Defendants’ motion for summary judgment as an objection, the court finds Culkin has failed to articulate a legal basis for such an objection.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR ADJUDICATION

Defendants move for summary judgment or summary adjudication of Culkin’s remaining causes of action on the grounds that it is undisputed (1) Defendants gave timely notice of the notice of the default and (2) Culkin did not tender funds prior to the foreclosure sale. For the following reasons, the Court GRANTS their motion in part as to the first, second, and seventh causes of action against Ostayan and Inco and DENIES their motion on all other grounds.

Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

Discussion

Here, the gravamen of Culkin’s first cause of action for violation of California’s nonjudicial foreclosure statutes, second cause of action for wrongful foreclosure, third cause of action for quiet title, and seventh cause of action for unfair business practices is the claim that Defendants improperly foreclosed on a property located at 20556 Rancho La Foresta Road, Covina, California 91724 (Covina Property).

Culkin’s first cause of action alleges Defendants committed the following violations: (1) recording a notice of default when no default occurred (SAC, ¶ 52); (2) failing to provide proper notice of sums due pursuant to Civil Code section 2966 (SAC, ¶ 53, 58, 59, 63); (3) failing to sufficiently contact Culkin in person or by telephone before filing notice of default pursuant to Civil Code section 2923.5 (SAC, ¶ 54); (4) failing to provide notice of assignment pursuant to Civil Code section 2932.5 (SAC, ¶ 66); (5) failing to provide notice of default pursuant to Civil Code section 2924b, subdivision (c)(1) (SAC, ¶ 75, 78); and (6) improperly notarizing the trustee’s deed upon sale pursuant to Government Code section 8224 (SAC, ¶ 79.) The court previously found the second, third, fourth, and sixth allegations were inapplicable and failed as a matter of law. (05/04/2023 Notice of Ruling, 7-8.)

Culkin’s second cause of action alleges the non-judicial foreclosure of the Covina Property was wrongful and should be set aside on the grounds alleged in the first cause of action. (SAC, ¶ 86-89.) Culkin also alleges the foreclosure was wrongful as Defendants rejected Culkin’s attempt to tender payment. (SAC, ¶ 91.) Culkin’s remaining causes of action for quiet title and unfair business practices are derivative of Culkin’s first two causes of action as both are based on the same allegations of wrongful foreclosure. (SAC, ¶ 99-100, 128-129, 131-132.)

Thus, the court finds the only remaining issues as framed by the pleadings are:


(1) whether Culkin defaulted as stated in the notice of default,
 (2) whether the notice of default was properly noticed, and
 (3) whether Culkin adequately tendered payment prior to foreclosure.

As to the first issue, it is undisputed Best Alliance recorded a notice of default and election to sell under deed of trust against the Covina Property on February 17, 2021. (Defendants’ Separate Statement (DSS), ¶ 5; RJN, Ex. 3.) The notice states it is based on Culkin’s failure to pay $7,357.51 as of February 10, 2021. (RJN, Ex. 3, p. 2.) It further states that payment has not been made of the following:

“INSTALLMENT OF PRINCIPAL AND/OR INTEREST WHICH BECAME DUE 6/2/2018; PLUS ALL LATE CHARGES AND ADVANCES AS SET FORTH IN SAID NOTE AND DEED OF TRUST, PLUS ALL SUBSEQUENT INSTALLMENTS OF PRINCIPAL, INTEREST, DEFAULT INTEREST, ENFORCEMENT COSTS AND CHARGES, COLLECTION COSTS AND CHARGES, IMPOUNDS, CHARGES, ADVANCES, LATE CHARGES, OBLIGATIONS AND INDEBTEDNESS AS THEY BECOME DUE, AND TRUSTEE'S FEES AND COSTS PURSUANT TO SAID NOTE AND DEED OF TRUST, LESS CREDITS AND/OR OFFSETS. REINSTATEMENT WILL REQUIRE ALL SENIOR LIENS, PROPERTY TAXES, AND HAZARD INSURANCE (NAMING LENDER AS AN INSURED) TO BE IN A CURRENT AND PAID STATUS AND RELIABLE WRITTEN EVIDENCE CONFIRMING IT.” (RJN, Ex. 3, p. 3.)

But while Defendants have established it is undisputed that they recorded a notice of default against Culkin for failing to make installment payments, they failed to establish Culkin’s failure to do so actually constituted a breach of the underlying promissory note and deed of trust. In fact, a copy of the promissory note and deed of trust at issue were not included in Defendants’ evidence or cited to in Defendants’ separate statement. Instead, Defendants appear to rely on the allegations of the SAC. (DSS, ¶ 2.) On April 10, 2018, the SAC alleges Culkin Construction executed a promissory note for a $37,500.00 loan that was secured by a deed of trust recorded against the Covina Property on May 2, 2018. (SAC, ¶ 9(c), (d).) The holders of the note allegedly represented to Culkin that the principal would not become due until Culkin sold or refinanced the Covina Property, whichever came first. (SAC, ¶ 10.) The note also allegedly included language stating it was subject to the provisions of Civil Code section 2966 and failed to include any payment schedule. (SAC, ¶ 12.) After the note allegedly expired on its terms in July 2018, Culkin allegedly entered into a forbearance agreement with the note holders. (SAC, ¶ 11.)

Based on these allegations, it does not appear the note required installment payments, but instead required a single balloon payment to be made by the time the agreement expired in July 2018 or by the time Culkin sold or refinanced the property, whichever occurred first. It is well established that foreclosure proceedings may only be initiated for the grounds explicitly stated in the notice for default. (Miller v. Cote (1982) 127 Cal.App.3d 888, 894 [“[A]ppellants are bound by their notice of default; they cannot assert any ground of default other than that stated in the notice.”].) And when the grounds stated in the notice of default do not constitute a default, the subsequent foreclosure proceedings are defective and improper. (See id., at p. 896 [holding trial court could enjoin pending foreclosure sale due to fatally defective notice of default].) Here, because there is a triable issue of fact with regards to whether Culkin was required to make installment payments, a triable issue of fact also exists with regards to whether there was a proper basis for the notice of default that triggered the nonjudicial foreclosure proceedings at issue. Thus, the court finds Defendant Fleet is not entitled to summary judgment or summary adjudication of these causes of action.

As to Ostayan and Inco, the SAC alleges Fleet and Inco are each the alter ego of Ostayan. (SAC, ¶ 5.) “Two requirements must be met to invoke the alter ego doctrine: (1) ‘[T]here must be such a unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its equitable owner that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist’; and (2) ‘there must be an¿inequitable result¿if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.’” (Turman v. Superior Court of Orange County (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 969, 980-981, quoting Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538.) “Factors a trial court may consider when deciding unity of interest and whether the fiction of a separate existence would promote fraud and injustice include the following: ‘Commingling of funds and other assets . . . ; the treatment by an individual of the assets of the corporation as his own [citations]; . . . the failure to maintain . . . adequate corporate records . . . ; . . .  sole ownership of all of the stock in a corporation by ... the members of a family [citations]; . . .  the use of a corporation as a mere shell, instrumentality or conduit for a single venture or the business of an individual . . .  ; . . .  [the] concealment of personal business activities [citations]; the use of the corporate entity to procure labor, services or merchandise for another person or entity [citations]; . . .  or the use of a corporation as a subterfuge of illegal transactions.” (Ming-Hsiang Kao v. Holiday (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 199, 206, quoting Associated Vendors, Inc. v. Oakland Meat Co. (1962) 210 Cal.App.2d 825, 838-840.)

Here, Defendants point to the following material facts. In a declaration, Ostayan states Ostayan, Fleet, and Inco each maintain separate bank accounts, business transactions, assets, and debts. (DSS, ¶ 31; Ostayan Decl., ¶ 7.) Ostayan also stated that at all times relevant, Ostayan acted as an officer for Fleet or Inco. (DSS, ¶ 32-33.) In opposition, Culkin failed to dispute these facts with admissible evidence. As a result, the court finds there are no triable issues of material fact that establish Inco and Fleet were Ostayan’s alter egos. Since the SAC bases Ostayan and Inco’s liability solely on Fleet’s actions, Ostayan and Inco are entitled to summary judgment on the first, second, and seventh causes of action.

Last, as to the third cause of action for quiet title against Inco, Defendants argue this cause of action fails because it is undisputed Culkin failed to tender full payment of the debt before the sale of the Covina Property. (Motion, p. 12:20-28.) “[A] plaintiff seeking to quiet title in the face of a foreclosure must allege tender or an offer of tender of the amount borrowed.” (Mangindin v. Washington Mut. Bank (N.D. Cal. 2009) 637 F.Supp.2d 700, 712.) The tender requirement is excused, however, “when the borrower is not in default and there is no basis for the foreclosure.” (Turner v. Seterus, Inc. (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 516, 526, quoting 5 Miller & Starr, Cal. Real Estate (4th ed. 2017) § 13:256, pp. 13-1101 to 13-1102.) As acknowledged above, the court has found that a dispute of material fact exists with regard to whether Culkin was in default by failing to make installment payments. Thus, the court finds that this argument fails, and Inco is not entitled to summary judgment or summery adjudication on this ground.

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion is GRANTED as to the first, second, and seventh causes of action against Inco and Ostayan and DENIED on all other grounds.

MOTION TO QUASH NON-MONETARY STATUS

Culkin moves to quash the stipulation between Best Alliance and Culkin that recognizes Best Alliance’s nonmonetary status pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473. But while Culkin seems to be arguing that the court should revisit its prior ruling where it “granted” a stipulation in favor of Best Alliance (Motion, p. 2:1-4), the court notes it did not make a finding of non-monetary status. Instead, the court merely approved a stipulation wherein Culkin, through counsel, agreed not to object to Best Alliance’s nonmonetary status. Culkin does not point to any authority that allows Culkin to unilaterally withdraw from the agreement. Nor does Culkin establish that Culkin was unaware of Best Alliance’s alleged involvement before Culkin entered into the stipulation.

Accordingly, Culkin’s motion is DENIED.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS IN PART Defendants’ motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication as to the first, second, and seventh causes of action against Inco and Ostayan and DENIES it on all other grounds.

Furthermore, the court DENIES Culkin’s motion to quash Best Alliance’s nonmonetary status.
