DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01606    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Nicolas Justin Somers,

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

Jovito Gilberto Palacios Labastida and Does 1-10,

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

                         22VECV01606                              107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:       

Motion for Terminating Sanctions

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Deny 

 

Defendant Jovito Gilberto Palacios Labastida (“Defendant”) moves for terminating sanctions dismissing Plaintiff Nicolas Justin Somers’ (“Plaintiff”) complaint.

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On November 24, 2020, Plaintiff and Defendant were involved in a motor vehicle accident. (Complaint p. 5.) On October 17, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant and Does 1 to 10 alleging causes of action for motor vehicle and general negligence. (Complaint pp. 4-5.)

 

On October 14, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for terminating sanctions (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed no opposition. On November 6, 2024, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition.

 

II.              LEGAL STANDARD 

 

“If [a] party . . . fails to obey an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against that party deponent[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(h).)  “The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc.¿(2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) “Discovery sanctions ‘should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.’” (Id.) “If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted”. (Id.)

 

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc., supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.) The court should consider the totality of the circumstances, including “conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.” (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)

¿¿ 

For example, the Third District held that terminating sanctions were warranted when a party repeatedly failed to propound adequate discovery responses, even after the court imposed monetary sanctions. (Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrations, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1096, 1099, 1106.)

¿¿¿ 

Before any sanctions may be imposed the court must make an express finding that there has been a willful failure of the party to serve the required discovery responses. (Fairfield v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 113, 118.) The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 788; Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250, 252-253.)¿¿ 

 

III.            DISCUSSION 

 

Defendant seeks an order dismissing Plaintiff’s complaint with prejudice. (Memorandum at p. 3.) Defendant states that he has “been trying to obtain Plaintiff’s deposition since October 2023, yet Plaintiff has refused and failed to appear at any of the five properly noticed depositions.” (Memorandum p. 1.) On September 3, 2024, the court granted Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition within twenty days of the hearing. (Memorandum at p. 1; Matteis Declaration ¶ 2.) On September 5, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with a notice of deposition and notice to produce documents for inspection and copying. (Memorandum at pp. 1-2; Matteis Declaration ¶ 3.) The noticed deposition was scheduled for September 18, 2024, but Plaintiff failed to respond. (Memorandum pp. 1-2.) On September 24, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff a meet and confer letter inquiring about Plaintiff’s non-appearance at the deposition. (Memorandum p. 2; Matteis Declaration ¶ 5.) Plaintiff did not respond. (Memorandum p. 2; Matteis Declaration ¶ 6.)

 

Plaintiff failed to appear at Defendant’s five noticed depositions, but this appears to be the first time Plaintiff has failed to comply with this court’s discovery order. And, although Defendant filed motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Specially Prepared Interrogatories, and Demand for Identification and Inspection of Documents, Set One, Defendant later withdrew those motions because Plaintiff provided responses. (Notice of Withdrawal filed 06/22/23.)

 

Further, [t]he discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc., supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.) Defendant did not request, nor did this court impose, sanctions against Plaintiff for his failure to attend the deposition. (Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition Memorandum of Points and Authorities; 09/03/24 Minute Order.) Accordingly, there has been no “incremental approach to discovery sanctions” here and the court declines to award the ultimate sanction of termination.

 

IV.            CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies the Motion.

I.                BACKGROUND¿ 

 

Plaintiff Kamran Dehdashtinejad (“Plaintiff”) was injured when a Suspension Body Fitness Trainer Strap (“G-Strap”) at a premises located at 7320 Woodlake Avenue, Suite 200, West Hills, CA 91307 (the “Premises”) “failed, tore, ripped, pulled apart, broke and/or ruptured.” (Complaint ¶¶ 7, 13-14.) On August 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Lurie Physical Therapy, P.C. dba Balance and Vestibular Center, Saskia Anna Van Ommeren Egberts, and Does 1 through 25, alleging causes of action for premises liability and negligence.

 

On September 12, 2024, Defendant Saskia Anna Van Ommeren Egberts demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint. On October 22, 2024, this court overruled the demurrer. (10/22/24 Minute Order p. 7.)

 

On October 16, 2024, Defendant Lurie Physical Therapy, P.C. (“Defendant”) demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint. On October 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On November 5, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

II.             JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of the following exhibits:

  1. Defendant’s Articles of Incorporation filed with the California Secretary of State (Ex. A.)
  2. Defendant’s Statement of Information for the years ending in 2022 and 2024. (Ex. B.)
  3. Defendant Joshua Lurie’s licensing information from the Physical Therapy Board of California. (Ex. C.)
  4. Defendant Saskia Van Ommeren-Egberts’ licensing information from the Physical Therapy Board of California. (Ex. D.)
  5. “Notice of Authorization Services” dated June 22, 2023, issued by Lakeside Community Healthcare, by Dr. Daniel Ahoubim. (Ex. E.)
  6. “Notice of Authorization of Services” dated August 2, 2023, issued by Lakeside Community Healthcare by Dr. Daniel Ahoubim. (Ex. F.)

Defendant cites Evidence Code section 452, subsections (d), (g), and (h). (Request for Judicial Notice pp. 1-2.)

 

Plaintiff objects to Defendant’s requests for judicial notice, arguing the court should not take judicial notice of unauthenticated documents prepared by private parties, unauthenticated website printouts from licensing agencies, or Plaintiff’s unauthenticated medical records. (Plaintiff’s Objections to Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice p. 2.)

 

Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d) permits the court to take judicial notice of “Records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States.” Evidence code section 452, subdivision (g) permits the court to take judicial notice of “Facts and propositions that are of such common knowledge within the territorial jurisdiction of the court that they cannot reasonably be the subject of dispute.” Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h) permits the court to take judicial notice of “Facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”

 

None of these exhibits are court records. Thus, Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d) does not apply.

“Subdivision (c) of Evidence code section 452 authorizes the taking of judicial notice of ‘official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.’” (People v. Thacker (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 594, 597.)  “However, to be judicially noticed pursuant to subdivision (c) the matter must meet the test of an official act.” (Id. at p. 598.) In People v. Thacker, the court stated “We have found no authority and none has been cited for the proposition that materials prepared by private parties and merely on file with state agencies may be judicially noticed pursuant to subdivision (c). (Id.)

 

The documents in Exhibits A and B appear to be materials prepared by private parties and filed with the California Secretary of State. Accordingly, the documents are not official acts under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c). Nor are the documents matters of common knowledge or not reasonably subject to dispute. (See Evid. Code § 452(g), (h).) Accordingly, this court denies Defendant’s request to take judicial notice of Defendant’s Articles of Incorporation and Statement of Information.

 

The court may take judicial notice of a license issued by a state agency under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c), “which permits judicial notice of ‘official acts of . . . executive . . . departments . . . of any state of the United States.” (Arroyo v. Plosay (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 279, 296 [stating a “Hospital’s license issued by the State Department of Public Health was properly subject to judicial notice under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c)[.]”].) Accordingly, this court grants Defendant’s request and takes judicial notice of Joshua Lurie’s and Saskia Van Ommeren-Egberts’ licensing information from the Physical Therapy Board of California.

 

The Notices of Authorization do not fall under any of the categories listed in Evidence Code section 452. As relevant here, they are not common knowledge, nor are they not reasonably subject to dispute. (See Evid. Code § 452(g), (h).) Accordingly, this court denies Defendant’s request to take judicial notice of the Notices of Authorization.

 

III.           PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

  1. Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40(a).)

 

Plaintiff submitted an acknowledgment of receipt showing Defendant’s counsel acknowledged receipt of the summons and complaint on September 16, 2024. (Notice and Acknowledgement of Receipt filed 09/17/24.) Defendant filed this demurrer on October 16, 2024. Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is timely.

  1. Meet and Confer

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating “[c]ounsel for the parties met-and-conferred telephonically but were unable to reach an agreement.” (Chen Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

This court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

IV.           LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)

 

V.              DISCUSSION

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state causes of action for premises liability and negligence because the complaint is subject to the Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (“MICRA”). (Demurrer p. 7.) Defendant further argues that “Plaintiff attempts to evade MICRA by bringing this suit under the cause of action of ordinary negligence and premises liability rather than professional negligence.” (Demurrer p. 7.)

 

MICRA applies to “action[s] for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person’s alleged professional negligence[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5; Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital (2016) 63 Cal.4th 75, 81.) “Health care provider” is defined as “ any person licensed or certified pursuant to Division 2 (commencing with Section 500) of the Business and Professions Code, or licensed pursuant to the Osteopathic Initiative Act, or the Chiropractic Initiative Act, or licensed pursuant to Chapter 2.5 (commencing with Section 1440) of Division 2 of the Health and Safety Code; and any clinic, health dispensary, or health facility, licensed pursuant to Division 2 (commencing with Section 1200) of the Health and Safety Code” and “includes the legal representatives of a health care provider”. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5(1).) Physical therapists are licensed pursuant to Division 2 of the Business and Professions Code. (See Bus. & Prof. Code § 2620.)

 

“Professional negligence” has four elements: “(1) ‘a negligent act or omission to act by a health care provider in the rendering of professional services,’ (2) ‘which act or omission is the proximate cause of injury or wrongful death,’ (3) ‘provided that such services are within the scope of services for which the provider is licensed,’ and (4) ‘which are not within any restriction imposed by the licensing agency or licensed hospital.’” (Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital, supra, 63 Cal.4th at p. 84.)

 

Claims brought against health care providers based upon the provider’s alleged professional negligence are governed by a different statute of limitations than other personal injury claims. (Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital, supra, 63 Cal.4th at p. 79.)  “In an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person's alleged professional negligence, the time for the commencement of action shall be three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5.)

 

In Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital, supra, 63 Cal.4th at p. 79, a patient was injured when the hospital bed’s rail collapsed. Plaintiff sued the hospital, alleging it negligently failed to inspect and maintain the equipment. (Id.) The hospital demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint, arguing it was barred by section 340.5’s statute of limitations. (Id. at p. 80.) The trial court agreed with the hospital and sustained the demurrer without leave to amend. (Id.) The Court of Appeal stated that “whether negligence in maintaining hospital equipment or premises qualifies as professional negligence depends on the nature of the relationship between the equipment or premises in question and the provision of medical care to the plaintiff.” (Id. at p. 88.) And “if the act or omission that led to the plaintiff's injuries was negligence in the maintenance of equipment that, under the prevailing standard of care, was reasonably required to treat or accommodate a physical or mental condition of the patient, the plaintiff's claim is one of professional negligence under section 340.5. But section 340.5 does not extend to negligence in the maintenance of equipment and premises that are merely convenient for, or incidental to, the provision of medical care to a patient.” (Id. at p. 88.) The patient alleged in the complaint that “she was injured when the latch on the rail on her hospital bed malfunctioned” and the hospital “negligently failed to discover or repair the malfunctioning latch and negligently failed to warn her that it was not working properly.” (Id. at p. 89.) The Court also considered factual concessions in the patient’s brief and oral argument, where the patient explained the doctor “made a ‘medical decision’ to order that the rails on her bed be raised, that this order followed from a ‘medical assessment’ of her condition, and she was thereafter injured when, ‘while grasping [the] rail and attempting to exit the bed, the siderail collapsed causing Plaintiff to fall to the floor and injure herself.” (Id.) The Court stated that, in essence, the patient was alleging that the hospital “failed to properly implement the doctor’s order, which was based on a medical assessment of her condition, that the rails on her bed be raised.” (Id.) The Court of Appeal held that the negligent act or omission occurred “in the rendering of professional services” and Plaintiff’s claim was barred by the statute of limitations because the patient filed the complaint more than one year after the patient discovered the injury. (Id. at pp. 79-80, 89.)

 

In contrast, in Johnson v. Open Door Community Health Centers (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 153, 156, a patient was injured when she tripped on a scale on her way out of the treatment room. The patient filed a complaint almost two years later. (Id.) The defendant sought summary judgment, arguing the patient’s complaint was barred by MICRA’s one-year statute of limitations. (Id.) The trial court agreed with the defendant. (Id. at pp. 156-157.) On appeal, the Court distinguished Johnson from Flores, noting that the Flores patient was “injured during the provision of medical care, through the breach of a duty owed only to patients” while the patient in Johnson “was injured after her care was completed, allegedly as a result of a breach of duties owed generally to all visitors to the . . . Clinic.” (Id. at p. 160.) The patient did not allege the defendant’s failure to maintain the scale affected the quality of her medical treatment, that improper placement of the scale caused her to fall off and injure herself, or that defendant failed to properly calibrate the scale. (Id.) Instead, Plaintiff alleged the scale posed a tripping hazard, “implicating [defendant’s] duty to all users of its facility, including patients, employees, and other invitees, to maintain safe premises.” (Id.) The Court of Appeal held MICRA’s one-year statute of limitations did not apply and reversed the trial court’s ruling. (Id. at pp. 160-162.)

 

Defendant argues this case is like Flores because “there should be no dispute that Plaintiff was undergoing care pursuant to a prescription from a medical care provider”, “there should be no dispute that Plaintiff was receiving care from a licensed physical therapist at the time of the incident” and “[t]he incident arose out of the provision of medical services prescribed by a medical physician – specifically the G-strap snapped while Plaintiff was undergoing prescribed care.” (Demurrer p. 9.)

 

In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co., supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at p. 994.) Plaintiff alleges Defendants “designed manufactured, produced, assembled, distributed, constructed, tested, inspected, controlled, fabricated, analyzed, recommended, merchandised, advertised, promoted, serviced, repaired, maintained packaged, marketed, sold and/or installed certain EQUIPMENT at the PREMISES, including but not limited to the ‘G-STRAP’ and offered the same for use to their business invitees, clientele and/or other authorized members of the general public.” (Complaint ¶ 8.) Plaintiff further alleges they were using the G-Strap with Defendants’ consent, permission, or direction and Defendants “intended that said PREMISES and/or EQUIPMENT, including the G-STRAP, be used by their business invitees, clientele and/or other authorized members of the general public and each of them knew that said EQUIPMENT, including the G-STRAP, would be used by their business invitees, clientele and/or other authorized members of the general public.” (Complaint ¶¶ 9, 12.)

 

Unlike Flores, Plaintiff does not allege in the complaint that the injury occurred because Defendants made a medical decision to treat Plaintiff with the G-Strap or that the G-Strap was used as part of a medical assessment of Plaintiff’s condition.

 

Accordingly, this court is unable to conclude – based on the face of the complaint – that Plaintiff’s cause of action arises from “a negligent act or omission to act by a health care provider in the rendering of professional services.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5(2).)

 

Moreover, assuming MICRA applies, Plaintiff’s complaint is not barred by the statute of limitations. In Flores and Johnson, the patients filed their complaints more than a year after the patients discovered their injuries. (Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital, supra, 63 Cal.4th at pp. 79-80; Johnson v. Open Door Community Health Centers, supra, 15 Cal.App.5th at p. 156.) Thus, if MICRA applied, the patients’ complaints in those cases were barred by section 340.5’s one-year statute of limitations. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 340.5.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges the incident occurred on August 30, 2023. (Complaint ¶¶ 14, 20.) Plaintiff filed the complaint on August 21, 2024. So, Plaintiff filed the complaint within section 340.5’s one-year statute of limitations.

 

Defendant also cites MICRA’s notice requirement. Code of Civil Procedure section 364, subdivision (a) states that “No action based upon the health care provider's professional negligence may be commenced unless the defendant has been given at least 90 days' prior notice of the intention to commence the action.” However, Code of Civil Procedure section 365 states that “Failure to comply with this chapter shall not invalidate any proceedings of any court of this state, nor shall it affect the jurisdiction of the court to render a judgment therein. However, failure to comply with such provisions by any attorney at law shall be grounds for professional discipline . . . ” Accordingly, assuming Plaintiff was required to, but did not, give Defendant ninety days’ notice,  Plaintiff’s failure to give notice does not  prevent this court from rendering a judgment in this case.

 

Plaintiff alleges in the complaint that they are seeking “general/noneconomic damages in excess of any limits under Civil Code section 3333.2 pursuant to Johnson v. Open Door Community Health Centers, 15 21 Cal. App. 5th 153 (Cal. Ct. App. 2017).” (Complaint ¶ 22.) Code of Civil Procedure section 3333.2 imposes limitations on noneconomic damages in actions for injuries “against a health care provider or health care institution based on professional negligence.” Accordingly, although it is unclear from the face of the complaint, if this court finds at a later date that Plaintiff’s causes of action arise from “a negligent act or omission to act by a health care provider in the rendering of professional services” then Plaintiff’s damages may be limited by section 3333.2. (Code Civ. Proc., § 3333.2(j)(4).)

 

VI.           CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court overrules Defendant’s demurrer.
