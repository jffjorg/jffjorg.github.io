Case Number: 21STCV27304    Hearing Date: September 4, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

BLANCA CALDERON;

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

 

REGENTS OF THE UNIVERSITY OF CALIFORNIA;

 

                              Defendant.

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER

 

 

 

Action Filed:  07/26/21

Trial Date:  12/02/24

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             September 4, 2024

Moving Party:             Regents of the University of California

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Blanca Calderon

 

Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

 

The court has considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

The court DENIES Defendants’ motion for summary judgment with respect to the Complaint.

The court DENIES Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication.

 

Background

Plaintiff Blanca Calderon (“Plaintiff”) worked for Regents of the University of California (“UCLA” or “Defendant”) from 2007 through May 14, 2019 as a housekeeper at its UCLA Santa Monica Medical Center. Plaintiff claims that she suffered various work-related injuries during her course of work, including injuries to her back, shoulders, middle fingers, elbow hand and wrist. Plaintiff claims that starting in 2010, her injuries and work restrictions often became permanent, and after medical leave, she informed Defendant that her restrictions were permanent. In doing so, Plaintiff requested reasonable accommodations, but Plaintiff claims that Defendant, each time, refused to provide any reasonable accommodation for Plaintiff’s injuries, while also failing to even initiate any kind of interactive process with Plaintiff to determine if there were any accommodations it could provide, whether in her role as a housekeep or any other, so long as such work was within her restrictions and would not injure her further. Plaintiff claims that as a result of Defendant’s refusal to accommodate Plaintiff, Plaintiff returned to work, all the while being forced to lift weight outside of and in violation of her physical work restrictions, causing further physical injury and harm.

The complaint further alleges that on or about May 13, 2019, Plaintiff’s supervisor Fletes, called Plaintiff into his office. While in his office, Fletes informed Plaintiff that she was being terminated because, as Fletes explained, Defendant could no longer accommodate Plaintiff’s restrictions given that they were now “permanent.” Plaintiff alleges that was a pretext for terminating her based on her disability as her injuries were permanent for the last ten years.

The complaint contains the following causes of action: 1) disability discrimination, 2) failure to reasonably accommodate, 3) failure to provide medical leave, 4) failure to engage in the interactive process, 5) failure to maintain a workplace free from discrimination and retaliation, 6) retaliation, and 7) wrongful termination.

 

On April 25, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed the seventh cause of action for wrongful termination.

On November 17, 2023, Defendant filed this motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication. Plaintiff filed an opposition on August 19, 2024, and Defendant replied on August 27, 2024.

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing motions for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent's claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment must be denied where the moving party's evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition (Leyva v. Sup. Ct. (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475) or where the opposition is weak (Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384, 387).

I.                    First Cause of Action – Disability Discrimination (Issue Nos. 1-2) and Sixth Cause of Action – Retaliation (Issue Nos. 9-10)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action for disability discrimination and sixth cause of action for retaliation, on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff cannot establish a discriminatory or retaliatory motive; and (2) Plaintiff did not suffer an adverse employment action.

 

California’s Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) prohibits employers from discriminating against employees on the basis of disability. (Govt. Code, § 12940.) In order to establish a prima facie case of FEHA disability discrimination, the employee-plaintiff must prove: (1) she suffered from a disability; (2) with or without reasonable accommodation, she could perform the essential functions of the employment position she held or desired; and (3) that she was subjected to an adverse employment action because of her disability. (Jensen v. Wells Fargo Bank (2000) 85 Cal. App. 4th 245, 254.) 

 

To establish retaliation under FEHA, a plaintiff must show that “(1) he or she engaged in a ‘protected activity,’ (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer’s action.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042.) A retaliation claim can be brought by an employee who has complained of conduct reasonably believed to be discriminatory. (Id. at 1043; see also Kelley v. Conco Companies (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 191, 209-10.) “[A]lthough an adverse employment action must materially affect the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment to be actionable, the determination of whether a particular action or course of conduct rises to the level of actionable conduct should take into account the unique circumstances of the affected employee as well as the workplace context of the claim.” (Yanowitz, supra, 36 Cal.4th at 1052.)  

 

At summary judgment, California follows the burden shifting analysis of McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792 to determine whether there are triable issues of fact for resolution by a jury. First, the employer must carry the burden of showing the employee’s action has no merit. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) It may do so by presenting evidence either: (1) negating an essential element of the employee’s claim; or (2) showing some legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the action taken against the employee. (Caldwell v. Paramount Unified School Dist. (1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 189, 202.) If defendant meets its burden, the burden then shifts to the plaintiff to produce substantial evidence that the employer’s showing was untrue or pretextual by raising at least an inference of discrimination or retaliation.  (Hersant v. California Department of Social Services (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 997, 1004-1005.) 

 

Adverse Employment Action 

 

Here, Defendant argues Plaintiff cannot establish that Plaintiff suffered an adverse employment action to establish her causes of action for discrimination and retaliation on the basis of a disability. 

 

An adverse employment action is generally an action that materially affects the terms, conditions, or privileges of employment. (See Featherstone v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 1150, 1161.)

 

Defendant presents evidence that Plaintiff was never disciplined, never issued a performance warning, never received a decrease in pay or salary, and never demoted. (UMF 17-20.) On the contrary, Plaintiff was only ever given positive performance reviews, even when she did not perform tasks associated with regular duty. (UMF 21.) Moreover, Defendant argues, in January 2021, Plaintiff resigned from her employment with Defendant and applied for retirement. (UMF 75; Exh. E: Plaintiff’s Depo., Vol. 1, 44:2-6, Ex. 1; Exh. N: Plaintiff’s Depo., Vol. 3, Exs. 25-29.)

 

Defendant also presents evidence that Plaintiff was placed on a finite leave of absence in May 2019 given permanent restrictions regarding the use of her hands, during which time Defendant sought to find a new assignment for Plaintiff. (UMF 45; Exh. G: Sanchez Depo., 108:7-25; 110:11-20; 170:2-10.) Plaintiff remained in contact with Defendant’s disability management team for over a year after she was placed on medical leave to inquire about her return to work. (UMF 46.) Defendant argues that a leave of absence is a known form of accommodation. It does not constitute an adverse action. See, e.g., Ravel v. Hewlett-Packard Enter., Inc., 228 F.Supp.3d 1086, 1094 (E.D. Cal. 2017) [“a reasonable accommodation cannot be a materially adverse employment action”].

 

In the absence of a controlling California court decision, a court may consider the reasoning reflected in federal cases that apply analogous statutes or rules, such as cases interpreting similar federal employment and antidiscrimination laws. (Pollock v. Tri-Modal Distribution Services, Inc. (2021) 11 Cal.5th 918, 936.) However, in Ravel, the plaintiff’s disability leave was considered reasonable under the ADA. A "reasonable accommodation must be effective, in enabling the employee to perform the duties of [her] position." (Ravel, supra, 228 F. Supp. 3d at 1093.) Here, there is no evidence presented that the leave was reasonable. As such, the court cannot determine as a matter of law whether the leave may constitute an adverse action.

Nevertheless, the court finds that Defendant has met its burden of showing that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for disability discrimination under FEHA has no merit by showing that at least one element of that cause of action (i.e., adverse employment action) cannot be established.  The evidence suggests that Plaintiff resigned in January 2021 when she applied for her retirement.

In opposition, to meet her burden, Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to reasonably accommodate Plaintiff, and forced her on unnecessary, permanent leave. Plaintiff asserts these actions resulted in her constructive discharge. Following Defendant’s May 13, 2019 decision to place Plaintiff on a medical leave of absence, it was clear to Plaintiff she no longer had a job given Defendant’s confirmation that she would no longer be allowed to work her custodian position. (PAMF 25-27.) At no time since May 2019 has Defendant ever offered her any work Plaintiff can perform. (PAMF 32.) Indeed, in 2020, Defendant informed Plaintiff that, because of her restrictions, she would never be allowed to return to her old job as a custodian. (PAMF 33.)

Based on the evidence presented in her opposition, Plaintiff has established that there is a triable issue of material fact regarding whether Plaintiff was subjected to adverse employment actions. “Constructive discharge, like actual discharge, is a materially adverse employment action.” (St. Myers v. Dignity Health (2019) 44 Cal.App.5th 301, 315.) Furthermore, “[w]hether conditions were so intolerable as to justify a reasonable employee’s decision to resign is normally a question of fact.” (Vasquez v. Franklin Mgmt. Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 827.) 

Next, as to issue No. 1, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has no evidence of any conduct from which one could infer “that it is more likely than not that [any]... actions were based on a prohibited discriminatory criterion.” (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 354-355.) Defendant argues that Plaintiff only proffers speculation to support her allegations; she has no evidence that would even suggest a discriminatory motive. Defendant argues Plaintiff’s suspicions of improper motives based entirely on conjecture and speculation are insufficient to raise a triable issue of fact to withstand summary judgment.

However, Defendant has failed to submit or cite to any evidence for this assertion. As a result, Defendant has not carried its initial burden on summary judgment. In any event, the proximity of seeking accommodations and being put on leave presents triable issues of fact.

II. Second Cause of Action – Failure to Reasonably Accommodate in Violation of Government Code section 12940(m) (Issue No. 3)

 

            Defendant moves for summary adjudication as to the second cause of action for failure to reasonably accommodate, on the ground that it accommodated Plaintiff.

           

“The essential elements of a failure to accommodate claim are: (1) the plaintiff has a disability covered by the FEHA; (2) the plaintiff is a qualified individual (i.e., he or she can perform the essential functions of the position); and (3) the employer failed to reasonably accommodate the plaintiff's disability. (Jensen v. Wells Fargo Bank (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 245, 256.)” (Wilson v. County of Orange (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1185, 1192.) A reasonable accommodation as “a modification or adjustment to the workplace that enables the employee to perform the essential functions of the job held or desired.” (Nadaf-Rahrov v. Neiman Marcus Group, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 952, 974.)  

 

“Under FEHA, ‘… an employer who knows of the disability of an employee has an affirmative duty to make known to the employee other suitable job opportunities with the employer and to determine whether the employee is interested in, and qualified for, those positions, if the employer can do so without undue hardship or if the employer offers similar assistance or benefit to other disabled or nondisabled employees or has a policy of offering such assistance or benefit to any other employees.’ ” (Hanson v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 215, 225, quoting Prilliman v. United Air Lines (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 935, 950-51.)

  

Here, Defendant presents evidence that it accommodated Plaintiff with various leaves of absence, including those alleged in Plaintiff’s complaint, during her employment. (UMF 23-24.) When Plaintiff was released to work with restrictions, Defendant scheduled her for modified duty shifts, which meant she only performed light duty tasks based on the needs of the day. (UMF 26-27, 30, 33-34.) Defendant also moved Plaintiff’s shift from the first shift in the morning to the second shift, when the work is less demanding due to a lower volume of work. (UMF 28.) Plaintiff admits that the only people who know what she was doing during her shift are her former supervisors, and Plaintiff’s supervisors testified that they assigned Plaintiff to modified duty and had no problem accommodating her. (UMF 29.) Plaintiff admits that her supervisors told her several times that she could call the office if she ever needed help with any tasks. (UMF 57.) Plaintiff admits that the supervisor would respond by telling her that they would send someone over as soon as possible. (UMF 68.)

Defendant also argues that Plaintiff claims that even though her supervisor told her that someone would handle what she could not do, Plaintiff was still under a time deadline to complete full duty tasks. (UMF 59.) But Plaintiff has no evidence that she was personally required to complete the cleaning of a patient room within 30 minutes. (UMF 60.) Plaintiff has no evidence that anyone with supervisory authority over her expected or instructed Plaintiff to complete any task within a certain amount of time or that was outside her restrictions. (UMF 83.) In fact, Defendant states, Plaintiff admitted that Defendant never reprimanded her for taking longer than 30 minutes to clean a room. (UMF 62.) Thus, Defendant argues, to the extent Plaintiff chose to do anything outside her restrictions, Plaintiff did so voluntarily and on her own accord. (UMF 67.)

The Court finds that Defendant has met its burden of showing that Plaintiff’s second cause of action for failure to provide reasonable accommodation under FEHA has no merit by showing that at least one element of that cause of action (i.e., defendant failed to accommodate Plaintiff’s accommodation) cannot be established.   Therefore, the burden shifts to the Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant failed to reasonably accommodate Plaintiff by consistently forcing her to perform work in violation of her known physical work restrictions. (PAMF 9-24.) For example: Plaintiff had to clean rooms and areas by herself, which forced her to perform work in violation of her restrictions (PAMF 9); whenever Plaintiff asked for help, no one would come to help her; the supervisor would just say there was no one available to help her (PAMF 12); from at least 2015 through 2019, for a portion of each of her shifts, Defendant often had Plaintiff disinfect patient rooms using a UV light disinfecting machine called a Xenex machine, which weighed approximately 140 pounds. (PAMF 14.)

 

Lastly, Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to reasonably accommodate Plaintiff by failing to continue to provide Plaintiff with the same duties she performed for nearly a decade.

Indeed, per Defendant’s own admissions, it suffered no undue hardship in providing her these jobs and could have provided such jobs to her for the indefinite future. (PAMF 29.)

 

The court finds that Plaintiff has met her burden to show there are triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant provided reasonable accommodations in that Plaintiff would work in shifts alone and when she would ask for help, no one was available. Further, Defendant put Plaintiff on leave and did not offer her another job. A lengthy delay in in providing an accommodation qualifies as a refusal to provide the accommodation. (Auburn Woods I Homeowners Assn. v. Fair Employment & Housing Com. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1578, 1599.) “When the reasons for a delay in offering a reasonable accommodation are subject to dispute, the matter is left for the trier of fact to resolve.” (Id.)

 

Summary adjudication is denied as to this cause of action.

 

III. Third Cause of Action – Failure to Provide Medical Leave in Violation of Government Code section 12945.2 (Issue No. 4)

Defendant argues is entitled to summary adjudication as to the third cause of action because Defendant provided Plaintiff with all requested medical leave without interference or retaliation.

“The CFRA is contained within the California Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) (Gov. Code, § 12900 et seq.), which is intended to give employees an opportunity to leave work for certain medical reasons without jeopardizing job security.” (Rogers v.¿City. of Los Angeles¿(2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 480, 487.) Government Code section 12945.2 entitles eligible employees to take up to 12 unpaid workweeks in a 12-month period for family and medical leave to care for children, parents, or spouses, or to recover from their own serious health condition.

A CFRA interference claim consists of the following elements: (1) the employee’s entitlement to leave rights; and (2) Defendant’s interference with or denial of those rights. (Soria v. Univision Radio Los Angeles, Inc., 5 Cal.App.5th 570, 601 (2016).)

The elements of a cause of action for retaliation in violation of CFRA are: (1) the defendant was an employer covered by CFRA; (2) the plaintiff was an employee eligible to take CFRA leave; (3) the plaintiff exercised her right to take leave for a qualifying CFRA purpose; and (4) the plaintiff suffered an adverse employment action. (Faust v. California Portland Cement Co. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 864, 885.)

Defendant presents evidence that Plaintiff admits that Defendant permitted her to take all medically authorized leave recommended by her doctors and that she was provided medical leave on multiple occasions. (UMF 24.) Plaintiff cannot identify any instance where she was denied a prescribed medical leave. (UMF 24.) After every leave of absence, Plaintiff was allowed to return to her previous position and in fact did return with no change in pay or title. (UMF 19, 25.) Plaintiff admits she never received a negative performance review, she was never disciplined, she was never demoted, and she was never subjected to a pay cut. (UMF 17-20.) She received positive performance reviews, even when she did not perform tasks associated with regular duty. (UMF 21.) Finally, Plaintiff voluntarily resigned her employment and retired. (UMF 75-80.)

            However, as noted above, Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of material fact as to whether she suffered adverse employment action after she took leave; as such, this would also raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff for exercising her CFRA rights.

 

            Thus, Defendant is not entitled to summary adjudication as to this cause of action.

 

IV. Fourth Cause of Action – Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process in Violation of Government Code section 12940(n) (Issue Nos. 5-7)

 

Defendant also moves for summary adjudication of the fourth cause of action for failure to engage in the interactive process, arguing that it engaged in good faith and Plaintiff was the cause of any breakdown in the process.

 

It is unlawful for an employer to “fail to engage in a timely, good faith interactive process” with a disabled employee to determine effective reasonable accommodations. (Gov. Code, § 12940(n).) 

 

             “[T]he employer cannot prevail on summary judgment on a claim of failure to reasonably accommodate unless it establishes through undisputed facts that “1) reasonable accommodation was offered and refused, 2) there simply was no vacant position within the employer’s organization for which the disabled employee was qualified and which disabled employee was capable of performing with or without accommodation, or (3) the employer did everything in its power to find a reasonable accommodation, but the informal interactive process broke down because the employee failed to engage in discussions in good faith. [Citation.]” (King v. United Parcel Service, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 426, 442.)

Defendant argues it engaged with Plaintiff in the interactive process in several ways: it granted all leaves of absence she or her physicians requested, it placed Plaintiff on modified or light duty according to restrictions recommended by her doctors, and it instructed Plaintiff to only perform those tasks that she could do and to call for help for anything she felt she could not do or that was unsafe (irrespective of whether it was within her restrictions). (UMF 23-38, 30-45, 57.) Defendant’s disability management team remained in consistent contact with Plaintiff regarding her restrictions over the years. (UMF 35, 46, 87.)

Furthermore, in May 2019, Plaintiff was placed on a medical leave of absence given new permanent restrictions to use of her hands, and Defendant initiated the reassignment process. (UMF 45.) Defendant asked Plaintiff for an updated resume to assist Defendant in those efforts. (UMF 47, 89.) Defendant required Plaintiff’s updated resume as part of the interactive process to determine what positions Plaintiff was minimally qualified to perform. (UMF 10.) However, Plaintiff never submitted her resume despite multiple requests to do so. (UMF 51.) Plaintiff admits that she never prepared a resume. (UMF 88.)

The court finds that Defendant has not met its burden to show there are no triable issues of fact as to whether it engaged in the interactive process. Defendant argues that by failing to provide her resume, Plaintiff caused the breakdown in the interactive process. However, triable issue of material fact exists as to whether there was a breakdown in the communication and who initiated it. The interactive process is a “continuing duty and the fact that the employer took some steps to identify a reasonable accommodation does not absolve the employer of liability for failure to engage in the interactive process if it is responsible for a later breakdown in the process.” (Zamora v. Sec. Indus. Specialists, Inc. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 1, 41.) As noted above under King, in order to find Defendant is entitled to summary judgment, Defendant must show it did everything in its power to find a reasonable accommodation, but the informal interactive process broke down because the Plaintiff failed to engage in discussions in good faith. Defendant did not show that it did everything in its power to find Plaintiff a reasonable accommodation before putting her on leave. Instead, it merely states that Plaintiff failed to engage in the interactive process because she did not submit a resume. Nevertheless, Defendant has not shown it looked for jobs that it thought would be suited for Plaintiff. “Suggesting that the disabled employee check available job postings, as SIS did when it terminated Zamora, does not satisfy the employer's duty to reassign or transfer a disabled employee to a vacant position, since the employer is in a better position than the employee to know what jobs are vacant or may become vacant in the near future to which the employee may be assigned.” (Spitzer v. The Good Guys, Inc. (2000) 80 Cal.App.4th 1376, 1389-1390

 

Summary adjudication is denied as to this cause of action.

 

V. Fifth Cause of Action – Failure to Maintain a Workplace Free from Discrimination and Retaliation in Violation of Government Code section 12940(k) (Issue No. 8)

 

Defendant argues is entitled to summary adjudication as to the fifth cause of action because Plaintiff’s underlying claim fails.

 

 “An actionable claim under section 12940, subdivision (k) is dependent on a claim of actual discrimination: ‘Employers should not be held liable to employees for failure to take necessary steps to prevent such conduct, except where the actions took place and were not prevented.’” (Scotch v. Art Inst. of California (2019) 173 Cal.App.4th 986, 1021.)¿¿¿ 

 

            However, as noted above, there are triable issues of material fact as to the claim for discrimination. Thus, the underlying claim has not failed.

 

            Summary adjudication is denied on this basis.

 

VI. Whether Plaintiff is Entitled to Damages under the Workers’ Compensation Exclusivity Rule (Issue No. 11)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff is not entitled to damages against Defendant because Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the workers’ compensation exclusivity rule.

 

The Workers Compensation Act (“WCA”) governs compensation to employees for injuries incurred in the course and scope of their employment. (See Cal. Const., art. XIV, § 4; Cal. Labor Code, § 3201; Charles J. Vacanti, M.D., Inc. v. State Compensation Insurance Fund (2001) 24 Cal.4th 800, 810.) Compensation under the WCA is an employee’s exclusive remedy against an employer for injuries sustained out of and in the course of employment. (See Cal. Labor Code, §§ 3600(a), 3602(a).) Whether exclusivity bars a cause of action against an employer depends on whether the alleged injury falls within the scope of the exclusive remedy provisions. (See Charles J. Vacanti, M.D., Inc., supra, 24 Cal.4th at 811.) The exclusive remedy provisions apply only in cases of industrial personal injury or death. (Shoemaker v. Myers (1990) 52 Cal.3d 1, 16.) The exclusive remedy provisions do not apply where the dual capacity doctrine applies. (See id.) The dual capacity doctrine applies where the injury stems from an employer-employee relationship that is distinct and invokes a different set of obligations than the employer’s usual duties to its employee. (See Id.; Cole v. Fair Oaks Fire Protection District (1987) 43 Cal.3d 148, 161-62.) Causes of action predicated upon an injury that is collateral to or derivative of an injury compensable by the exclusive remedies of the WCA may be subject to the exclusivity bar. (Id.) “While common law tort claims are generally preempted [citation], claims for separate injuries under distinct statutory schemes are not.” (Veguez v. Governing Board of Long Beach Unified School District (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 406, 418.)¿¿ 

 

“Even where an injury is otherwise compensable under the workers’ compensation system, a cause of action seeking damages based on the injury may nevertheless be allowable where the employer’s conduct falls outside the compensation bargain: ‘if the alleged injury falls within the scope of the exclusive remedy provisions, then courts consider whether the alleged acts or motives that establish the elements of the cause of action fall outside the risks encompassed within the compensation bargain.’”¿ (Light v. Department of Parks & Recreation (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 75, 96 (Light).)¿ Thus, “unlawful discrimination and retaliation in violation of FEHA falls outside the compensation bargain and therefore claims of intentional infliction of emotional distress based on such discrimination and retaliation are not subject to workers’ compensation exclusivity.”¿ (Id. at p. 101.)¿  

 

Disability discrimination is not preempted by workers’ compensation law.¿ (City of Moorpark v. Sup Ct. (1998) 18 Cal. 4th 1143, 1154; Meninga v Raley’s Inc. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 79, 91; Fretland v. County of Humboldt (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 1478, 1484-1485).¿ 

 

In City of Moorpark, the plaintiff sued, “alleging causes of action for discrimination in violation of the FEHA, wrongful termination in violation of public policy (common law wrongful discharge), breach of contract, and intentional and/or negligent infliction of emotional distress.” (City of Moorpark, 18 Cal.4th at 1149.) The defendants demurred, arguing in part that, because the plaintiff’s disability was work related, “section 132a provided her exclusive remedy.” (Id.) The trial court disagreed and overruled the demurrers to the FEHA and common law wrongful discharge causes of action. The defendants petitioned the appellate court for a writ of mandate, which the court denied. The Supreme Court thereafter affirmed the judgment. 

 

In its analysis, City of Moorpark explained that “the existence of a workers’ compensation remedy does not by itself establish that the remedy is exclusive. Rather, the scope of workers’ compensation exclusivity depends on the terms of the exclusive remedy provisions.” (Id., at 1154.) As a result, City of Moorpark concluded that “section 132a does not provide an exclusive remedy and does not preclude an employee from pursuing FEHA and common law wrongful discharge remedies.” (Id., at 1158.)   

 

In Meninga, the plaintiff “unquestionably received workers' compensation benefits due solely to emotional injury resulting from the discriminatory treatment she alleges,” but later asserted FEHA claims against the defendant.¿ (Meninga, supra, 216 Cal. App. 3d 79.)¿ The defendant argued that the plaintiff’s FEHA claims are barred because “workers’ compensation provides the exclusive remedy.”¿ (Id. at 89.)¿ The Meninga court acknowledged that “at first glance “defendants' argument has some appeal given the exclusive remedy language of¿Labor Code sections 3601, subdivision (a), and 3602, subdivision. (Id.) However, the court rejected that argument, reasoning “[t]his approach would not only render the FEHA cause of action for employment discrimination a non sequitur where disability resulted; it would also make a hollow promise out of the public policy, stated in FEHA, of eliminating employment discrimination.”¿(Id.) 

  

In Fretland, the plaintiff filed causes of action following an alleged wrongful termination because of medical problems with his lower back, the plaintiff was re-hired with accommodations. (Fretland, supra, 69 Cal.App.4th at 1482.) The plaintiff raised a FEHA violation claim based on discrimination based upon disability, and defendants argued that plaintiff’s recovery was solely and exclusively limited to workers’ compensation. (Id. at 1484.) Citing City of Moorpark, the Court of Appeal held that work-related injury discrimination is not a normal risk of the compensation bargain and found that the trial court erred in finding that plaintiff’s FEHA claim was barred by the exclusive remedy provisions of the workers’ compensation laws. (Id. at 1484, 1492.) 

 

In the instant case, Plaintiff alleges FEHA claims based on Defendant’s alleged discriminatory practices – i.e., discrimination, retaliation, failure to accommodate, based on discrimination of her disability. Therefore, the crux of her claims revolve around Plaintiff’s alleged discriminatory employment practices – not the recovery of tort claims for any injury to herself while at the workplace. As the Moorpark and Meninga courts have explained, given the public policy reasons behind the adoption of FEHA – i.e., the protection of civil rights and elimination of discriminatory practices, the remedies of FEHA are meant to supplement the workers’ compensation scheme. Since a “workers’ compensation claim cannot address her employer’s liability for employment discrimination,” the Plaintiff’s pending claim of worker’s compensation benefits does not bar her FEHA claims.    

            Summary adjudication is denied on this basis.

Lastly, the court notes that in the reply, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s evidence is inadmissible and that Plaintiff mischaracterizes the evidence. But Defendant has not objected to any of Plaintiff’s evidence. It is not the court’s task to rummage through the evidence in order to determine which piece of evidence is inadmissible and on what basis. Further, the court has reviewed Plaintiff’s evidence it has relied on for this ruling and has determined that it supports Plaintiff’s arguments. Any evidence which does not support Plaintiff’s arguments was not relied upon by the court.

 

Similarly, the Court will not consider Defendant’s improper reply separate statement.  (See e.g. Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 252 (“The deficiencies carried over to the reply papers, which included a 297–page reply separate statement. There is no provision in the statute for this.”); San Diego Watercrafts, Inc. v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 308, 313 (“While the code provides for reply papers, it makes no allowance for submitting additional evidence or filing a supplemental separate statement. (§ 437c, subd. (b).) This is consistent with the requirement supporting papers and the separate statement be served with the original motion. (§ 437c, subd. (a).).”); Moore v. William Jessup University (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 427, 432 n.3. (“Generally, a party moving for summary judgment may not rely on new evidence filed with its reply paper.”).)  

 

In sum, the motion for summary judgment or in the alternative, summary adjudication is denied.

It is so ordered.

Background

            Plaintiff Boyd Horan filed this action on February 18, 2022, against defendants City of Artesia, William Rawlings, Tony Lima, Monica Manalo, Ali Taj, and Does 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for (1) Violation of Cal. Labor Code §1102.5; (2) Retaliation In Violation of FEHA (Cal. Gov’t Code §12940, et seq.); and (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress. On April 8, 2022, defendants Tony Lima, Monica Manalo and Ali Taj filed a demurrer to the Complaint which was rendered moot by the filing of a First Amended Complaint. On February 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). On April 11, 2024, the

Court sustained the demurrer to the third and sixth causes of action with leave to amend. The Court also sustained the demurrer without leave to amend as to the fifth cause of action. On May 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).

 

            On May 1, 2024, defendants filed a demurrer to the SAC.  The SAC is the operative complaint and alleges six causes of action for (1) Violation of Cal. Labor Code §1102.5; (2) Retaliation In Violation of FEHA (Cal. Gov’t Code §12940, et seq.); (3) Hostile Work Environment; (4) Failure to Prevent Harassment; (5) Negligent Hiring; and (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.  This dispute arises out of Plaintiff’s alleged wrongful termination. (SAC, ¶¶ 10-16.)

 

            On July 29, 2024, defendant William Rawlings (“Defendant”) filed a demurrer to the SAC. Plaintiff filed an opposition on August 21, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on August 27, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Discussion

Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to in person or by telephone for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.41.) 

Here, the parties declare that they met and conferred via telephone on July 15, 2024. (Williams Decl. ¶¶ 3-5.) Plaintiff’s counsel provides evidence showing that the parties met and conferred by telephone. (Id.; Ex. A and Ex. B.) Therefore, the meet and confer requirement was satisfied.

 

Judicial Notice

Under Evidence Code section 452, the court may take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy,” and must take judicial notice of such facts and propositions if a party so requests and provides sufficient notice to the adverse party. (Evid. Code, §§ 452, subd. (h) & 453.)

However, the court does not “take judicial notice of the truth of factual matters asserted therein.” (Ramsden v. Western Union (1977) 71 Cal.App.3d 873, 879.) “To go beyond notice of the existence of a document to an interpretation of its meaning constitutes improper consideration of evidentiary matters.” (Middlebrook-Anderson Co. v. Southwest Sav. & Loan Assn. (1971) 18 Cal.App.3d 1023, 1038.)

Here, Defendant requests the court take judicial notice of the following documents:

Attached as Exhibit “C” to the Declaration of Warren M. Williams, Plaintiff’s Claim for Money Damages, dated October 7, 2021.

Attached as Exhibit “D” to the Declaration of Warren M. Williams, City of Artesia’s Claim Rejection Notice, dated October 28, 2022.

The court GRANTS the requests.

 

Demurrer

Defendant William Rawlings argues that Plaintiff should dismiss the individual Rawlings from this lawsuit because the causes of action are not applicable to Rawlings, but to the City of Artesia, presuming they have merit. Further, Defendant William Rawlings demurs to the third and sixth causes of action of the Second Amended Complaint on the grounds that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (CCP §§ 430.10(e).)

 

Third Cause of Action – Hostile Work Environment

Defendant argues that Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action for hostile work environment because he does not identify any protected characteristic or harassing conduct. (Dem. p. 8.) In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the fact that he is not a member of a protected class does not bar his ability to assert the hostile work environment cause of action. (Opp. p. 3.) Plaintiff further argues that “[a]s long as a plaintiff perceived harassment based on a protected category that was severe and pervasive enough to create an abusive environment, a claim for hostile work environment can stand.” (Opp. p. 3.) In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff concedes that he is not a member of a protected class and was not personally subjected to harassing comments or conduct because of his association with or advocacy on behalf of others which results in the failure of the cause of action. (Reply p. 2.)

The elements of a cause of action for harassment based on hostile work environment are: “(1) he was a member of a protected class; (2) he was subjected to unwelcome racial [or other protected status] harassment; (3) the harassment was based on race [or other protected status]; (4) the harassment unreasonably interfered with his work performance by creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment; and (5) [Defendant] is liable for the harassment. (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 876.)

The SAC alleges “while he was employed by the City, he was subjected to harassing conduct that was severe or pervasive. That environment was primarily created by Defendant Rawlings.” (SAC ¶ 49.) The SAC further alleges “Specific instances of Rawlings systematic and continuous harassment include, but are not limited to, those acts alleged in Paragraphs 9 through 11 above, and are incorporated herein.” (FAC ¶ 50.)

Specifically, the SAC alleges “Rawlings managed the City’s employees through fear, intimidation, and harassment. Rawlings went out of his way to systematically target employees based on various protected categories, including pregnancy, gender, familial status, and medical issues. Rawlings would yell at threaten to sue, threaten to fire, and generally intimidate employees based on these protected categories. As the Human Resources Manager, Horan was the one whom the employees would come to complain about Rawlings. Horan frequently sat through meetings with employees that were in tears and inconsolable due to the harassment by Rawlings. When Horan confronted Rawlings with these complaints on behalf of employees of protected categories, Rawlings would turn his abuse towards Horan. Rawlings would yell at Horan, threaten Horan’s job, and would force Horan to engage in conduct that Horan believed to be immoral or unlawful.” (SAC ¶ 9.)

Plaintiff’s facts are insufficient to state a cause of action for hostile work environment as they remain conclusory. The Thompson v. City of Monrovia case is instructive. In Thompson v. City of Monrovia, appellant Thompson was a white police officer who sued the respondent police department for harassment and hostile work environment arising from offensive remarks and behavior directed at an African American colleague. (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 863.) The Court of Appeal reasoned that “a plaintiff like Thompson, who is not within the protected class, may satisfy the “protected class” requirement “based on [his] association with or advocacy on behalf of protected employees.” (Id. at 876-877.) The Court of Appeal further reasoned that “if Thompson produced evidence that he was personally “subjected to unwelcome racial comments as a result of [his] association with or advocacy for protected employees,” he would satisfy the second and third requirements (that he was subjected to unwelcome racial harassment and the harassment was based on race).” (Id. at 878.)

Just as in Thompson, here, the alleged facts fail to show that Plaintiff was “specifically targeted [because he] associated with and advocated for [a member of a protected class] . . .” [citations omitted.] (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 878.) Even though the SAC alleges, “[a]s the Human Resources Manager, Horan was the one whom the employees would come to complain about Rawlings . . . When Horan confronted Rawlings with these complaints on behalf of employees of protected categories, Rawlings would turn his abuse towards Horan. Rawlings would yell at Horan, threaten Horan’s job, and would force Horan to engage in conduct that Horan believed to be immoral or unlawful”, it fails to provide additional facts as to the conduct that Horan was forced to do or that he was specifically targeted because of his advocacy. (SAC ¶ 9.) 

 “To establish the fourth element—that the harassment created a hostile work environment—Thompson “ ‘must prove that the defendant's conduct would have interfered with a reasonable employee's work performance and would have seriously affected the psychological well-being of a reasonable employee … .’ ” (Aguilar v. Avis Rent A Car System, Inc., supra, 21 Cal.4th at pp. 130–131.) Harassment, which may be verbal, physical, or visual and “communicates an offensive message to the harassed employee” (Roby v. McKesson Corp. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686, 706), “ ‘cannot be occasional, isolated, sporadic, or trivial[;] rather the plaintiff must show a concerted pattern of harassment of a repeated, routine or a generalized nature.’ ” (Aguilar, at p. 131.) Whether the harassment  is sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive environment “must be assessed from the ‘perspective of a reasonable person belonging to the racial or ethnic group of the plaintiff.’” [citation omitted.] (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 877.)

Here, the SAC further alleges in a conclusory manner, “Rawlings’ harassment of Horan and the other employees was so severe and continuous that whenever Rawlings was personally at the office the attitude of Horan and the other employees was noticeably depressed and fearful. Horan and the other employees were too afraid to speak with one another. They would simply remain quietly in their offices for fear of drawing the attention of Rawlings. When Rawlings was absent from the office, Horan could tell that the employees were far happier, more communicative and collaborative.” (SAC ¶ 11.) Plaintiff further alleges upon information and belief that any reasonable person in Plaintiff’s circumstance would have considered the work environment to be hostile, intimidating, offensive, oppressive, or abusive.” (FAC ¶ 51.)

Plaintiff alleged that he suffered harassment by Defendant Rawlings in retaliation for his advocating for the rights of the employees in those protected classes. Just as the Court of Appeal in Thompson found that appellant failed to meet any of these elements because “he has not produced evidence that he was subjected to harassing comments or conduct because of his association with or advocacy on behalf of African Americans.” (Thompson v. City of Monrovia (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 860, 878.) Similarly, here, Plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory and fail to produce any facts showing that he was subjected to the harassing comments or conduct because of his advocacy. Moreover, Plaintiff fails to include any facts showing how he advocated for the protected classes.

The court is not yet persuaded that there is no reasonable possibility that Plaintiff can cure the defect identified above.  Because “[l]eave to amend is liberally allowed,” the court will grant Plaintiff leave to file a Third Amended Complaint.  (See, e.g., Kempton v. City of Los Angeles (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1344, 1348.) Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the third cause of action.  The Court grants Plaintiff 20 days leave to amend.

 

Sixth Cause of Action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress [Mislabeled as Seventh Cause of Action on SAC]

Defendant argues that Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress because Plaintiff’s government claim does not include this cause of action and is therefore barred. (Dem. p. 18.) Further, the intentional infliction of emotional distress claims against supervisors arising from wrongful termination in violation of public policy are barred Worker’s Compensation. (Id.) Moreover, Plaintiff has not alleged extreme and outrageous conduct. (Id.) In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s arguments are misguided because Workers Compensation does not bar a claim that arises outside of an employee-employer relationship. (Opp. p.8.)  Further the government claim is not required for claims outside of the scope of employment. (Id.) Additionally, Plaintiff provides examples of the alleged extreme and outrageous conduct. (Id. at p. 10.)

Government Claims Act

The Government Claims Act (the “Act”) governs tort actions against public entities and establishes sovereign immunity, except as provided by the Act or other statute.  The Act was “conceived to strictly limit governmental liability.”  (Williams v. Horvath (1976) 16 Cal.3d 834, 842.) The intent of the Act "is to confine potential governmental liability to rigidly delineated circumstances.” (DiCampli-Mintz v. County of Santa Clara (2012) 55 Cal.4th 983, 991 (internal quotations omitted).)  Under the Act, public entity immunity is the rule and liability the exception.  (Gov. Code, § 815.2; Teter v. City of Newport Beach (2003) 30 Cal.4th 446, 451.)  It is settled law that there is no common law governmental tort liability in this State, and any tort liability is dependent wholly on statute. (Gong v. City of Rosemead (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 363, 370 (Gong).)

The Gong v. City of Rosemead case is instructive. In Gong, appellants sought to impose liability on respondent city for the alleged tortious conduct of its former mayor. (Gong v. City of Rosemead (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 363, 366.) The causes of action included intentional infliction of emotional distress. (Id.) The Court of Appeals found that “[b]ecause [appellants] failed to satisfy the claim presentation requirements of the act with respect to their causes of action for  . . . intentional infliction of emotional distress, and because the City is immune from their promissory estoppel claim, the trial court properly sustained the City's demurrer.” (Id. at 367.)

Here, Plaintiff alleges intentional infliction of emotional distress. The intentional tort claim is defective because it is common law rather than statutory cause of action.  (See Gong, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 370 (there is no common law governmental tort liability in this State; any such tort liability is wholly dependent on statute).) Moreover, the cause of action is also defective because Plaintiff fails to plead compliance with the Tort Claims Act’s claims presentation requirements. (See, e.g., State of California v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1242 (timely claim presentation is required prior to filing lawsuit; complaints that do not allege timely claim presentation are subject to general demurrer).) Nevertheless, Plaintiff’s argument that the Government Claims Act does not apply because “[he] has provided specific allegations that Rawlings’ conduct was in violation of FEHA and beyond the scope of employment” is insufficient to show that the alleged conduct was outside the scope of employment. (Opp. p. 8.)

The City’s demurrer will be sustained, with leave to amend.  In any amended pleading, Plaintiff will need to allege the cause of action arising from statute, rather than common law and plead facts showing the conduct was outside the scope of employment. The Court grants Plaintiff 20 days leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

Defendant William Rawlings moves to strike punitive damages, attorney’s fees and claims of restitution from the Second Amended Complaint.

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer, such as words, phrases, and prayers for damages. (See Code Civ Proc., §§ 435-437.)  A motion to strike can be made to strike irrelevant, false or improper matter inserted in any pleading or to strike any pleading or part thereof not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule or order of the court.  (§ 436.)

The motion to strike is MOOT given that the Court granted leave to amend as to the third and sixth causes of action.

It is so ordered.

Legal Standard

Pro hac vice admission in California is governed by California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40.  To be admitted pro hac vice, one must be “a member in good standing of and eligible to practice before the bar of any United States court or the highest court in any state, territory, or insular possession of the United States.”  California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40(a).  However, in no case shall an attorney appear pro hac vice if the attorney is a resident of California, regularly employed in California, or “regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.”  Ibid.   

 

An attorney seeking pro hac vice admission must file a verified application in both court and the State Bar of California establishing  

 

(1) [t]he applicant's residence and office address; (2)[t]he courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission; (3)[t]hat the applicant is a member in good standing in those courts; (4)[t]hat the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (5)[t]he title of court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel¿pro hac vice¿in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and (6)[t]he name, address, and telephone number of the active member of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.  (California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40(d). )

 

Lastly, repeated applications for pro hac vice appearance may result in denial.  California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40(b).  

 

Discussion

            Merits

            Here, Counsel van Leeuwen and Peyrot have complied with California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40. Counsel Van Leeuwen and Peyrot are not residents of California. (van Leeuwen Decl. ¶ 1; Peyrot Decl. ¶ 1.) Counsel Peyrot is admitted to practice in New York. (Peyrot Decl. ¶ 3.) Counsel van Leeuwen is admitted to practice in New York and District of Columbia. (van Leeuwen Decl. ¶ 3.) Counsel van Leeuwen and Peyrot are members in good standing. (van Leeuwen Decl. ¶ 4; Peyrot Decl. ¶ 4.) Counsel have not been suspended or disbarred in any court. (Id. at ¶ 5; Id. at ¶ 5.) Counsel have not filed pro hac vice applications in the past two years. (Id. at ¶ 6; Id. at ¶ 6.) They also provided the name, address, and telephone number of Alana Yakovlev (SBN: 270910) who is Plaintiff’s counsel of record. (Id. at ¶ 8.)

 

            Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Pro Hac Vice applications for David Van Leeuwen, Esq. and Francois Peyrot, Esq.
