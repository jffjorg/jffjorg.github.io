DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV02487    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANTS' MOTION TO COMPEL FORM INTERROGATORIES - SET ONE, SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES - SET ONE AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION - SET ONE IN PART and GRANTING IN PART

I.                INTRODUCTION

This is a personal injury case filed on October 26, 2023, by Plaintiffs Heriberto Sanchez-Hernandez and Victoria Mendez-Cruz (“Plaintiffs”) against Defendants Harpreet Singh Sandhu, PB Transport Inc., and Does 1–25 (“Defendants”). The Complaint asserts a cause of action for general negligence.

On December 2, 2024, Defendants filed a total of four motions to compel, two for each Plaintiff, addressing Form Interrogatories – Set One, Special Interrogatories – Set One, and Requests for Production – Set One, along with requests for monetary sanctions. On the same date, Plaintiffs filed a combined opposition to Defendants’ motions. On December 30, 2024, Defendants filed a late reply.

The Court reviews Defendants' motions and consolidates them into the following tentative ruling.

II.  INTERROGATORIES – Form Interrogatories– Set One and Special Interrogatories – Set One

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(a), unverified responses are tantamount to no response at all and are subject to a motion to compel initial responses (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 635–36). Objections, however, do not require verification and are sufficient to render the responses timely if served within the statutory period (Blue Ridge Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1988) 202 Cal.App.3d 339, 344–345; Food 4 Less Supermarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 651, 657–658). Where responses consist of a "hybrid" of objections and substantive answers, the failure to provide verifications only affects the substantive responses, leaving the objections intact (Food 4 Less Supermarkets, Inc., supra, 40 Cal.App.4th at p. 658).

Defendants served Requests for Production of Documents – Set One on Plaintiffs Victoria Mendez-Cruz and Heriberto Sanchez-Hernandez on March 6, 2024. The initial deadline to respond was April 9, 2024, after accounting for two additional court days for electronic service. However, an email from Plaintiffs’ counsel dated April 2, 2024, confirms that the parties agreed to extend the response deadline to April 30, 2024. Plaintiffs subsequently served objections-only responses on April 30, 2024, in accordance with the agreed extension. (Opposition, Ex. B.)

Plaintiffs timely served objections-only responses on April 30, 2024, pursuant to an agreed extension. For Form Interrogatory No. 10.3, Plaintiffs included a partial answer, but the response was unverified. Under Appleton, the unverified partial response is deemed no response at all and is properly subject to a motion to compel initial responses. However, for the objections-only responses to the other interrogatories, Plaintiffs complied with their statutory obligations. Defendants' motions to compel initial responses to these objections is procedurally improper and should have been filed as motions to compel further responses under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motions to compel initial responses as to Form Interrogatory No. 10.3, requiring Plaintiffs to serve a verified response within 20 days of this order. The Court however DENIES the motions as to all other Form Interrogatories in Set One and Special Interrogatories - Set One, as the proper procedure would have been to file motions to compel further responses.

 

III.  REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION – RFP Set One

A propounding party may move to compel responses to requests for production of documents where the responding party fails to provide responses. (CCP § 2031.300.) The responding party must provide responses within thirty days after the demand is served. (CCP § 2031.030(c)(2) & (3).) The responding party waives all objections, including privilege and work product, by failing to timely respond to requests for production of documents. (CCP § 2031.300.)

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300, if a party fails to timely respond to requests for the production of documents, they waive all objections, including those based on privilege or work product protection, and the propounding party may move to compel responses. However, where the responding party serves timely objections-only responses, the proper remedy is a motion to compel further responses under section 2031.310, which must be filed within 45 days of service of the objections. This deadline is both mandatory and jurisdictional (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410).

Defendants served Requests for Production of Documents – Set One on Plaintiffs Victoria Mendez-Cruz and Heriberto Sanchez-Hernandez on March 6, 2024. The initial deadline to respond was April 9, 2024, after accounting for two additional court days for electronic service. However, an email from Plaintiffs’ counsel dated April 2, 2024, confirms that the parties agreed to extend the response deadline to April 30, 2024. Plaintiffs subsequently served objections-only responses on April 30, 2024, in accordance with the agreed extension. (Opposition, Ex. B.)

Plaintiffs argue that their responses were timely and code-compliant, and Defendants’ motions to compel responses under section 2031.300 is procedurally improper. Defendants contend that Plaintiffs’ objections waived any privilege claims and justify compelling responses without objections. However, as Plaintiffs timely served their objections, the proper remedy for challenging the sufficiency of these responses is a motion to compel further responses under section 2031.310, not a motion to compel initial responses under section 2031.300 (Board of Registered Nursing v. Superior Court of Orange County (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1020).

Here, Defendants served Requests for Production – Set One on Plaintiffs on March 6, 2024, with responses due on April 9, 2024. Plaintiffs timely served objections-only responses on April 30, 2024, pursuant to an agreed extension. These objections do not require verification and are thus timely and compliant under Blue Ridge Ins. Co. and Food 4 Less Supermarkets, Inc. Defendants' motions to compel initial responses are procedurally improper, as objections were timely asserted and should have been challenged via a motion to compel further responses under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motions to compel initial responses to the request for production as being procedurally improper.

 

V.        SANCTIONS

Sanctions are mandatory under the California Code of Civil Procedure when a party’s failure to respond to discovery necessitates a motion, regardless of whether responses are eventually served, or the motions are resolved as moot. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.030(a), 2030.290(c), 2033.280(c).

Here, Defendants requested monetary sanctions in the amount of $4,060.00 for each motion, totaling $12,180.00. However, as the Court denies Defendants’ motions for being procedurally improper, the Court also DENIES Defendants’ requests for monetary sanctions. 

VI.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court DENIES Defendants’ motions to compel as procedurally improper, except as to the unverified partial response to Form Interrogatory No. 10.3, for which the motion is GRANTED. Plaintiffs are ordered to provide a verified response to Form Interrogatory No. 10.3 within 20 days of this order.

The Court DENIES Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions.

Defendants to give notice.

Case Number: 24AHCV00196    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANTS' DEMURRER TO THE FAC WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND and DENYING MOTION TO STRIKE and ORDER DENYING NON-PARTY O’HARA TRUST’S MOTION TO INTERVENE

I.         INTRODUCTION

This action arises from an alleged negligent real estate transaction. Plaintiffs Daniel J. Carroll and Jana D. Law (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege that Defendants Lloyd Ross (“Ross”) and Douglas Elliman (“Elliman”) (collectively, “Defendants”) failed to inform Plaintiffs of parking restrictions in the City of San Marino and failed to provide the required City of San Marino Information Guide.

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the initial complaint. On August 22, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer with leave to amend.

On September 25, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC). On October 29, 2024, Defendants filed their demurrer. Plaintiffs filed an opposition on December 20, 2024, and Defendants submitted their joint reply on December 27, 2024.

Additionally, on October 9, 2024, Non-Party Stephen and Mary O’Hara Family Trust filed a motion to intervene, asserting that they are the sellers of the property and not parties to this lawsuit.

The Court addresses both the demurrer and the motion to intervene in this single tentative ruling.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

In this case, Defendants’ counsel met and conferred with Plaintiffs’ counsel on October 11, 2024. (Markow Decl., ¶ 2.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendants have satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

Judicial Notice

Defendants request judicial notice of the following documents:

  1. The August 22, 2024, Order of sustaining Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ original Complaint
  2. City Code of San Marino, California, Chapter XV, Article 07, Sections 23 and 27 
  3. City Code of San Marino, California, Chapter XXII, Article 02, Section 03
  4. City Code of San Marino, California, Chapter XXIV, Article 01, Sections 01 through 04
  5. California Civil Code section 2079.16, 

Judicial notice of court records is proper under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d). Judicial notice of municipal codes and legislative enactments is proper under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (b).

As such, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendants demur to both causes of action: (1) Professional Negligence and (2) Negligence, arguing that the First Amended Complaint (FAC) fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain. (Demurrer, p. 3.)

Defendants argue that the FAC is defective for a variety of reasons. First, they assert there was no duty to disclose because they did not owe Plaintiffs a duty of care to disclose San Marino’s parking regulations, as such information is publicly available. (Demurrer, p. 10.) Second, Defendants contend that the parking regulations and the informational guide are matters of public record that Plaintiffs could have accessed independently through reasonable diligence. (Demurrer, p. 9.) Third, Defendants maintain that California law does not impose a duty on real estate professionals to disclose public records unless those records are not reasonably accessible to the buyer. (Demurrer, p. 11.)

Defendants further argue that Plaintiffs’ claim they were unaware of the local ordinances is of no legal consequence, as the information was readily available. (Demurrer, p. 12.) Additionally, Defendants contend that Plaintiffs’ reference to Section 11(N) of the Purchase Agreement is irrelevant, as Defendants are not parties to the agreement. (Demurrer, p. 14.) Finally, Defendants maintain that the terms of the agreement confirm that, as real estate brokers and agents, they did not owe Plaintiffs a duty of care to disclose the local ordinances or provide the informational guide. (Demurrer, p. 14-15.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants were required to provide the informational guide under local regulations, specifically citing the City of San Marino's procedures for residential compliance. According to Plaintiffs, the guide is a mandatory disclosure intended to inform new buyers of the City’s unique ordinances, and Defendants' failure to provide this guide constitutes a breach of duty. (FAC, ¶¶ 21-24; Opposition, p. 3.)

Plaintiffs also contend that Section 11(N) of the Purchase Agreement required Defendants to disclose all material information, including the City’s restrictive parking regulations. They argue that this provision imposes an obligation on Defendants, as real estate brokers and agents, to ensure all necessary disclosures were made, including providing the informational guide. (FAC, ¶¶ 25-26; Opposition, p. 4.)

Finally, Plaintiffs claim that Defendants' failure to provide the informational guide as part of the compliance certificate process violated both statutory and professional obligations. Plaintiffs cite California Civil Code section 2079, which imposes a duty on brokers to investigate and disclose all facts materially affecting the value or desirability of a property. Plaintiffs assert that Defendants’ omission caused them material harm, including financial losses associated with the sale of the property and related substantial damages. (FAC, ¶¶ 35-37; Opposition, p. 6-7.)

The Court, consistent with its prior ruling, finds that Defendants did not owe Plaintiffs a legal duty to disclose the City of San Marino’s parking regulations or the informational guide, as these are matters of public record accessible to Plaintiffs through reasonable diligence. Furthermore, the Court previously determined that the "Notification Form" does not impose a legal duty on Defendants to disclose information that Plaintiffs could have independently discovered. Plaintiffs’ attachment of the Notification Form to the FAC does not cure this deficiency.

Given that the parking restrictions are matters of public record, which Plaintiff could have learned about through minimal effort, the Court remains unpersuaded by Plaintiffs’ arguments. Plaintiffs have failed to address the deficiencies identified in the prior ruling or to establish an actionable cause of action against Defendants. Further amendment cannot create a legal duty where none exists under the facts and law.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer without leave to amend.

Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Here, Defendants move to strike portions of the FAC. Given that the Court has sustained the demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as MOOT.

 

Motion to Intervene

CCP section¿387(a) provides that, “[u]pon¿timely application, any person, who has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both, may intervene in the action or proceeding.¿ An intervention takes place when a third person is permitted to become a party to an action or proceeding between other persons, either by joining the plaintiff in claiming what is sought by the complaint, or by uniting with the defendant in resisting the claims of the plaintiff, or by¿demanding anything adversely to both the plaintiff and the defendant, and is made by complaint, setting forth the grounds upon which the intervention rests, filed by leave of the court and served upon the parties to the action or proceeding who have not appeared in the same manner as upon the commencement of an original action, and upon the attorneys of the parties who have appeared, or upon the party if he has appeared without an attorney, in the manner provided for service of summons or in the manner provided by Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 1010) Title 14 of Part 2.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 387(a).)¿ 

¿           To establish a direct and immediate interest in the litigation for purposes of permissive intervention, a non-party seeking intervention must show that he or she stands to gain or lose by direct operation of the judgment, even if no specific interest in the property or transaction at issue exists.¿ (Simpson Redwood Co. v. State of California¿(1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 1192, 1201.)¿ “Whether the intervener’s interest is sufficiently direct must be decided on the facts of each case¿. . ..¿And section 387 should be liberally construed in favor of intervention.”¿ (Id.¿at 1200.)¿ “In order that a party may be permitted to intervene it is not necessary that his interest in the action be such that he will inevitably be affected by the judgment.¿ It is enough that there be a substantial probability that his interests will also be so affected.¿ ‘The purposes of intervention are to protect the interests of those who may be affected by the judgment¿. . ..’”¿ (Timberidge¿Enterprises, Inc. v. City of Santa Rosa¿(1978) 86 Cal.App.3d 873, 881-82 (citations and emphasis omitted).)¿ 

Here, The O’Hara Trust asserts an interest as the sellers of the property at the center of this litigation. They argue that their rights may be directly affected by the outcome of this case, particularly if liability for failure to disclose rests on facts stemming from their role as sellers.

However, because the Court has sustained the demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint without leave to amend, the underlying action is resolved in favor of Defendants, rendering the motion to intervene moot.

            Accordingly, the Court DENIES O’Hara Trust’s motion to intervene.

 

IV.       ORDER

The Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend.

The Court DENIES Defendants' motion to strike as MOOT.

The Court DENIES Non-Party Motion to Intervene as MOOT.

Defendants to give notice.

             Defendants to submit a proposed judgment consistent with this order within thirty days.

Case Number: 24NNCV02608    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER ON MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL 

I.                Background

This action arises from a contractual dispute between Plaintiff Hong Du ("Plaintiff") and Defendant Ali Hasana Nasab ("Defendant") concerning alleged breaches of partnership obligations. Plaintiff initiated the complaint on July 1, 2024. Defendant subsequently filed a cross-complaint alleging misconduct related to the partnership's dissolution.

On December 4, 2024, Plaintiff's counsel, Shun C. Chen of the Law Offices of Shun C. Chen, APLC, filed a motion to be relieved as counsel of record. Defendant opposes the motion, asserting that the withdrawal is intended to evade pending monetary sanctions and that Plaintiff's counsel's actions have disrupted the litigation's orderly progress. On December 27, 2024, Mr. Chen filed a reply to the opposition.

II.             Legal Standard

The Court may issue an order allowing an attorney to withdraw from representation after notice to the client. (CCP § 284.) The attorney may withdraw from representation if the withdrawal would not result in undue prejudice to the client's interest—i.e., counsel cannot withdraw at a critical point in the litigation. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915; see California Rule of Professional Conduct 3-700.)

The Court has discretion to grant or deny a motion to be relieved as counsel, particularly when granting such a motion would cause undue prejudice to the client's interests or result in “an unreasonable disruption of the orderly processes of justice.” (People v. Ortiz (1990) 51 Cal. 3d 975, 979.) Furthermore, the Court may deny an attorney’s request to withdraw “where such withdrawal would work an injustice or cause undue delay in the proceeding.” (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal. App. 3d 1, 4.) However, this discretion must be exercised reasonably, balancing the client’s right to choose representation with the court’s duty to maintain the orderly administration of justice. (Ibid.)

In making a motion to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with procedures set forth in Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362. The motion must be made using mandatory forms:

1. Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel directed to the client – (MC-051).

2. Declaration “stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship” the reasons that the motion was brought (MC-052).

3. Proposed Order (MC-053).

The forms must be timely filed and served on all parties who have appeared in the case. If these documents are served on the client by mail, there must be a declaration stating either that the address where client was served is “the current residence or business address of the client” or “the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d)(1).)

III.           Discussion

Plaintiff's attorney, Shun C. Chen (“Mr. Chen”), has filed the instant motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 284 and California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, citing a complete breakdown of the attorney-client relationship. Attorney Chen contends that the relationship has deteriorated to the point where effective representation is no longer possible, particularly with respect to discovery obligations and the client's objectives. Chen relies on California Rule of Professional Conduct Rule 3-700(C)(1)(d) and (f), and (C)(6), which permit withdrawal where continued representation would violate legal or ethical obligations. 

Defendant and Cross-Complainant Ali Hasana Nasab ("Nasab") opposes the motion, arguing that Chen’s withdrawal seeks to avoid accountability for discovery violations and pending sanctions.Mr. Chen filed a reply arguing that Defendant lacks standing to oppose the motion, as the issue of withdrawal is solely between attorney and client. He further asserts that the ethical obligations of confidentiality under Aceves v. Superior Court (1996) 51 Cal.App.4th 584, 595, prevent him from disclosing further details about the breakdown in the relationship.

The Court finds that Mr. Chen has complied with all procedural requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 284 and California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, including filing the mandatory MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053 forms and properly serving notice on Plaintiff. The evidence submitted demonstrates a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship, making continued representation untenable.

The Court also notes that the next hearing in this case is scheduled for January 31, 2025, which the Court finds is adequate time for the Plaintiff to retain substitute counsel. The litigation will not be unduly delayed or prejudiced by granting the motion at this juncture. While Defendant raises concerns regarding discovery disputes, these issues do not outweigh the Court's findings of good cause for withdrawal.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds good cause to GRANT counsel’s motion. Therefore, the Court GRANTS Counsel’s motion.

 

IV.           Conclusion and Order

The Court GRANTS Mr. Chen’s motion to be relieved as counsel effective upon filing proof of service of the signed order on Plaintiff.

Counsel is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24NNCV04243    Hearing Date: January 6, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION – SET ONE ADMITTED and MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is a personal injury case brought by Plaintiff Zarui Voskanyan ("Plaintiff") against Defendant Gino Salvatore Guzzardo ("Defendant") and Does 1 through 25. Plaintiff alleges that on January 27, 2024, Defendant negligently operated a motor vehicle, causing a collision with Plaintiff’s vehicle in Glendale, California.

On December 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to deem admitted Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission - Set One pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280. The motion also seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,635.00 against Defendant and Defendant’s counsel. No opposition has been filed.           

            II.        REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . ..”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).)  

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).) 

Here, Plaintiff served RFA - Set One on Defendant on October 1, 2024, making Defendant’s responses due by November 5, 2024. (Voskanyan Decl., ¶ 4) Plaintiff granted multiple extensions, moving the deadline to November 25, 2024, but Defendant failed to provide any responses despite these accommodations. As of the hearing date, Defendant has not served any responses nor opposed this motion.

Accordingly, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280(c), the Court must deem the RFAs admitted.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to deem RFAs Set One admitted.

Sanctions

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].) 

Here, Defendant’s failure to timely serve responses to the RFAs necessitated this motion. Plaintiff’s counsel declares that they expended 1.5 hours preparing the motion, anticipate an additional 0.5 hours to review any opposition and draft a reply, and an additional 0.5 hours to appear for the hearing, for a total of 2.5 hours. Counsel also incurred a $60.00 filing fee, bringing the total requested sanction to $1,635.00. (Voskanyan Decl., ¶¶ 12-13.)

Counsel’s hourly rate is $630, which the Court finds reasonable. However, as no opposition has been filed, the Court reduces the reasonable time spent to 1.0 hour, plus the $60.00 filing fee, resulting in a total sanction amount of $690.00.

The Court GRANTS monetary sanctions in the amount of $690.00 against Defendant within 30 days of this order.

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to deem the RFAs Set One admitted, and awards Plaintiff $690.00 in monetary sanctions to be paid within 30 days of this order.

            Plaintiff is to give notice.
