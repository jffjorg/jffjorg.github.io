DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22BBCV00879    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING VICTOR MANUEL PERRAZA GUEVARA’S MOTION TO ENTER JUDGMENT OR DISMISS

I.         INTRODUCTION

This case arises from a motor vehicle accident involving Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company ("Plaintiff"), Defendant Victor Manuel Perraza Guevara ("Guevara"), and Defendant Jerson Arrana ("Arrana"). Plaintiff alleges negligence against Arrana as the driver and liability against Guevara as the vehicle's registered owner under permissive use statutes.

On April 6, 2024, Plaintiff served Guevara with an offer to compromise pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 998, offering to settle for $4,874.56 in satisfaction of all claims for damages. Guevara accepted the offer on May 3, 2024, and submitted payment on May 28, 2024.

Plaintiff subsequently dismissed Guevara with prejudice but continued the action against Arrana, who had defaulted on May 7, 2024. Guevara now moves to enter judgment in accordance with the CCP §998 offer or, alternatively, to dismiss the case in its entirety, arguing that Plaintiff's dismissal of him alone does not reflect the intended scope of the settlement.

Plaintiff opposes the motion, asserting that Guevara lacks standing because he was dismissed with prejudice and is no longer a party to the case. Plaintiff also contends that Guevara paid before judgment was entered, thereby circumventing the CCP §998 process.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

            The clerk or Judge shall enter judgment according to a C.C.P. 998 offer to allow judgment if the following occur:

i) A written offer by one party to allow judgment to be taken against themselves under certain terms and conditions is served upon any other party, not less than 10 days prior to commencement of trial.

ii) Party receiving the offer accepts it.

iii) Offer and Proof of Acceptance are filed.

C.C.P. 998(b), (b)(1)

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Guevara argues that the CCP §998 settlement required dismissal of all claims, including those against Arrana. However, as a party dismissed from this action with prejudice, Guevara lacks standing to bring forth these arguments. A dismissal with prejudice operates as a final adjudication, terminating the court's jurisdiction over the matter and barring any future litigation on the same cause of action between the same parties (Estate of Redfield (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 1526, 1532; Manning v. Wymer (1969) 273 Cal.App.2d 519, 521).

As a result, Guevara has no standing to bring motions or assert arguments on behalf of himself or any other party in this case. Furthermore, Guevara’s attempt to submit arguments on behalf of the defaulted defendant, Arrana, is procedurally improper.

Accordingly, the Court denies Guevara’s motion in its entirety.

           

IV.       CONCLUSION

            The Court DENIES Guevara’s motion in its entirety.

            Plaintiff is to give notice.

          

Dated: November 20, 2024

                                                                            

JARED D. MOSES
 JUDGE OF THE SUPERIOR COURT

Plaintiff contends that over the course of her tenancy, spanning more than a decade, Defendants failed to address numerous habitability and maintenance issues at the property despite her repeated requests.

On August 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (SAC) alleging seven causes of action for (1) Unlawful Actions by Landlord to Influence Tenant to Vacate [Civ. Code § 1940.2]; (2) Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (3) Unfair Business Practices [Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200]; (4) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (5) Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability; (6) General Negligence; and (7) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

On September 13, 2024, Defendants filed a demurrer to the SAC. Plaintiff filed an opposition on November 6, 2024, and Defendants filed a reply on November 12, 2024.

 Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

In this instance, on August 16, 2024, Defendant’s counsel, Christina Hahn, telephoned Plaintiff’s counsel, Zak Fisher, to meet and confer before filing the demurrer. (Hahn Decl., ¶ 2) Following this discussion, on August 21, 2024, Mr. Fisher sent an email indicating that he would not be amending the complaint. (Hahn Decl., ¶ 3)¿ The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

A.    Special Demurrer – Uncertainty

A defendant may demur to a complaint on the grounds that the pleading is “uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Ibid.) A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed because ambiguities can be clarified through modern discovery procedures. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so vague that the defendant cannot reasonably respond—that is, they cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against them. 

Additionally, the objection of uncertainty does not pertain to a failure to allege sufficient facts. (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) It pertains instead to doubt about what the pleader means by the facts alleged. (Ibid.) A demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues they must meet. (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)

Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s SAC is impermissibly vague and lacks specific details, with each cause of action relying on generalized, conclusory events without clear timing or context. Defendant also demurs to the seventh cause of action for IIED, arguing that Plaintiff has not alleged any specific outrageous actions by Defendants.

Plaintiff, in opposition, argues that her claims are adequately pleaded, and the Defendant’s demurrer lacks merit. 

The Court has reviewed the SAC and finds the following:

Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to repair severe leaks in the laundry room, reported as early as 2012, which persisted and worsened over a decade, causing significant interior damage and potential health hazards. Plaintiff claims that, despite contacting Defendant Paul Raymond, who assured her someone would investigate and fix the issue, Defendants did not address the leak until after serving a notice to quit in 2022 (SAC ¶¶ 18–19)

Plaintiff further alleges that the garage door hinge became non-operational around June 2018. She states she reported this issue to Defendants in November 2017 after the hinge malfunctioned and injured her, again in November 2019, but Defendants only sent a repairman once, failing to resolve the issue. The garage allegedly remained unusable from 2017 through her tenancy (SAC ¶ 20)¿

Additionally, Plaintiff describes a persistent rodent infestation, reporting that she had to pay for exterminator services out-of-pocket without reimbursement from Defendants. She claims Defendants dismissed her concerns, saying, “Thanks for taking care of it,” and took no action to address the infestation themselves (SAC ¶ 22)

Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants refused to change filters for the air conditioning, heating, and furnace, forcing her to hire professionals for maintenance. She notes that Defendants only addressed an urgent repair in January 2022, when a fallen tree damaged the property and a neighboring premises, after pressure from a neighbor (SAC ¶¶ 23-24)

Plaintiff alleges further harassment, including Defendants serving her a 60-day notice to quit despite COVID-19 protections, sending harassing text messages, and insisting she apply for rent-relief programs despite stating she would still be evicted. Plaintiff claims Defendants also engaged in other intimidating actions, such as falsely reporting her dogs to animal control and Defendant Paul Raymond staring ominously at her residence, prompting her to call the Sheriff (SAC ¶¶ 25, 27–29)

Plaintiff contends that these actions collectively aimed to pressure her into vacating the premises, causing her significant emotional distress, including anxiety, depression, and fear of homelessness (SAC ¶¶ 31–32)¿

The Court finds that these allegations are sufficiently clear and specific to provide Defendants with notice of the claims and the timeline of events. A demurrer for uncertainty is disfavored and should only be sustained when the complaint is so ambiguous or unintelligible that the defendant cannot reasonably respond (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616). Here, Plaintiff has presented adequate detail regarding the alleged incidents and their progression over more than a decade, effectively explaining what occurred.

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer as to uncertainty.

 B.    General Demurrer – seventh cause of action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff has failed to state facts sufficient to support the seventh cause of action for IIED. A cause of action for IIED requires: “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-51.) Conduct is “outrageous” when it exceeds all bounds tolerated in a civilized community (Id.). Additionally, IIED requires intentional or reckless conduct—behavior intended to inflict injury or engaged in with realization that injury will result (Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal.3d 197, 210).

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s allegations fail to establish “outrageous” conduct or an intent to cause emotional distress and do not demonstrate that Plaintiff suffered severe distress. Defendant claims the conduct alleged does not meet the high threshold for IIED.

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s allegations support the elements required for an IIED claim at this stage. Plaintiff describes a series of actions by Defendants that, if proven, could be interpreted as “outrageous” and intentionally distressing. The SAC alleges a prolonged pattern of neglect and harassment, including failures to perform necessary repairs, refusal to address infestations, and intimidating actions meant to force Plaintiff out, such as issuing a notice to quit despite COVID-19 protections, filing false animal control reports, and Defendant Paul Raymond’s ominous stares, which led Plaintiff to contact the Sheriff (SAC ¶¶ 18-29)

Plaintiff further alleges that these actions caused her substantial emotional distress, including anxiety, depression, and fear of becoming homeless (SAC ¶¶ 31-32)¿. While Defendant contends Plaintiff’s claims lack specificity regarding the IIED elements, the cumulative nature of the alleged conduct could reasonably be deemed extreme and intolerable in a civilized society. At this stage, the Court finds the pleading sufficient and OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer as to the IIED claim.

The Court OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer as to the IIED claim.

IV.       ORDER

            The Court OVERRULES the demurrer to SAC in its entirety.

Defendants are to respond within 10 days of this order.
