DEPARTMENT 34 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court often posts its tentative several days in advance of the hearing. Please re-check the tentative rulings the day before the hearing to be sure that the Court has not revised the ruling since the time it was posted.

Please call the clerk at (213) 633-0154 by 4:00 pm. the court day before the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative.

Case Number: 22STCV32439    Hearing Date: December 18, 2023    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions

 

Moving Party: Defendant Martin Luther King Jr. – Los Angeles (MLK-LA) Healthcare Corporation

Resp. Party:    None

 

SUBJECT:        Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Provide Verified Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two

 

Moving Party: Defendant Martin Luther King Jr. – Los Angeles (MLK-LA) Healthcare Corporation

Resp. Party:    None

 

SUBJECT:        Motion for Order Deeming the Matters Specified in the Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted

 

Moving Party: Defendant Martin Luther King Jr. – Los Angeles (MLK-LA) Healthcare Corporation

Resp. Party:    None

 

 

The Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED in part. 

 

Defendant Martin Luther King, Jr. Healthcare Corporation is DISMISSED with prejudice from Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint.

 

The other motions filed by Defendant Martin Luther King Jr. Healthcare Corp. are taken off-calendar.

 

        The Case is DISMISSED.

 

 

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On October 4, 2022, Plaintiff Fatimah Muhammad filed her Complaint against Defendant Martin Luther King Jr. Healthcare Corporation, as well as against Nominal Defendants Hakimah Muhammad and Habeeb Muhammad. Plaintiff alleged causes of action for elder abuse, negligence, negligent hiring and supervision, fraud, and wrongful death. Plaintiff Fatimah Muhammad concurrently filed her Declaration as successor in interest pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 377.32.

 

On December 29, 2022, Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint (FAC).

 

On January 31, 2023, Defendant Martin Luther King Jr. Healthcare Corporation (“Defendant”) filed its Answer to the FAC.

 

On February 7, 2023, by request of Plaintiff, the Clerk’s Office dismissed without prejudice the fourth cause of action for fraud from the FAC.

 

On August 4, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

 

On September 11 and 12, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s discovery motions, in which the Court deemed admitted certain requests for admission and compelled Plaintiff to provide initial responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for production of documents.

 

On October 31, 2023, Defendant filed its Motion for Summary Judgment, with concurrent filings in support of it.

 

On November 17, 2023, Defendant filed: (1) Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions; (2) Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Provide Verified Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two; and (3) Motion for Order Deeming the Matters Specified in the Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted. Defendant concurrently filed a proposed order with each of these motions.

 

No oppositions or other responses have been filed to any of these motions.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Motion for Terminating Sanctions

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 gives the court the discretion to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in a misuse of the discovery process. A court may impose terminating sanctions by striking pleadings of the party engaged in misuse of discovery or entering default judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).) A violation of a discovery order is sufficient for the imposition of terminating sanctions. (Collison & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1620.) Terminating sanctions are appropriate when a party persists in disobeying the court's orders. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 795–796.)

 

A terminating sanction is a "drastic measure which should be employed with caution." (Deyo, supra, at p. 793.)

 

"A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction." (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279–280.)

 

While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery." (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 793.) "[A] court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations." (Id.) Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information. (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971.)

 

"A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions . . . (1) absent unusual circumstances, there must be a failure to comply with a court order, and (2) the failure must be willful." (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327; but see Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1291 ["willfulness is no longer a requirement for the imposition of discovery sanctions."].)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Defendant moves the Court to dismiss with prejudice Plaintiff’s FAC and order monetary sanctions against her. (Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions, p. 6:9–13.) Defendant argues that this would be appropriate because Plaintiff has not complied with the Court’s Minute Orders dated September 11 and 12, 2023 in that no discovery responses have been served. (Motion for Terminating Sanctions, p. 5:13–15.) Id., Decl. Moulin, ¶ 9.)

 

        The Court agrees with Defendant’s argument. There is no indication that Plaintiff has served discovery responses or otherwise complied with the Court’s Orders of September 11 and 12, 2023. There is also no indication that any sanction would result in Plaintiff serving responses to Defendant’s discovery requests. Further, considering that the Court has deemed admitted Defendant’s Requests for Admission, the result of a Motion for Summary Judgment is almost a foregone conclusion. 

 

Considering that she did not respond to the discovery requests, obtain new counsel after Plaintiff’s former counsel left the representation, respond to the motions to compel responses, or respond to this motion for terminating sanctions, it appears that Plaintiff has decided not to continue to litigate this case.

 

        The Court will issue terminating sanctions. However, given that Plaintiff has been litigating this matter in propria persona since the time these discovery issues arose, monetary sanctions would not be appropriate here.

 

C.      Conclusion

 

The Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED in part. 

 

Defendant Martin Luther King, Jr. Healthcare Corporation is DISMISSED with prejudice from Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint.

 

The Request for Monetary Sanctions is DENIED.

III.     Legal Standard

 

A.      Renewed Motions

 

“A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on ex parte motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (b).)

 

B.          Motions to Stay Enforcement of Judgment if Other Action Pending

 

“The trial court may, in its discretion, stay the enforcement of a judgment or order if the judgment debtor has another action pending on a disputed claim against the judgment creditor.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 918.5, subd. (a).)

 

“In exercising its discretion under this section, the court shall consider all of the following:

 

“(1) The likelihood of the judgment debtor prevailing in the other action.

 

“(2) The amount of the judgment of the judgment creditor as compared to the amount of the probable recovery of the judgment debtor in the action on the disputed claim.

 

“(3) The financial ability of the judgment creditor to satisfy the judgment if a judgment is rendered against the judgment creditor in the action on the disputed claim.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 918.5, subd. (b).)

 

IV.      Discussion

 

A.      The Parties’ Arguments

 

Judgment Debtor moves the Court again to stay execution of the Judgment in this matter pending resolution of separate, ongoing litigation.

 

Judgment Debtor argues: (1) that there are new facts, evidence, and circumstances in this matter; (2) that there are other cases that also have new facts, evidence, and circumstances; (3) that this renewed motion is timely made; (4) that this renewed motion is not a motion for reconsideration, and thus the Court is not confined to the Minute Order dated August 3, 2023; and (5) that the active litigation, including the filing of a demurrer, warrants a stay pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5, subdivision (b). (Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment, pp. 5:9–10, 5:15–16, 6:26, 8:15–16, 9:16–18, 12:12–13.)

 

Judgment Creditor opposes the Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment, arguing: (1) that all of Judgment Debtor’s prior motions requesting a stay in this matter have been denied; (2) that Judgment Debtor is avoiding sitting for a debtor’s examination; and (3) that the Court should award attorney’s fees and costs in favor of Judgment Creditor and against Judgment Debtor and his counsel because this is a frivolous and bad faith motion. (Opposition, pp. 3:14–21, 4:1–6, 9:15–19.)

 

In his Reply, Judgment Debtor argues: (1) that there are additional facts to consider, including that Judgment Creditor “is currently committing fraud against the Court”; (2) that the Opposition is misleading the Court by attempting to argue that renewed motions are the same as reconsiderations; (3) that the Opposition fails to discuss Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5 and its codification of prior California jurisprudence; (4) that Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5, subdivision (b) was expressly drafted by the Legislature to protect Judgment Debtor in pending litigation against Judgment Creditor; and (5) that Judgment Creditor’s Opposition is frivolous and perjured to avoid the 21-day safe harbor requirement. (Reply, pp. 2:5, 5:24–25, 6:16–17, 9:1–3, 10:3–4.)

 

B.      The Renewed Motion

 

The Court agrees with the proposition that a motion for reconsideration is different from a renewed motion. (Compare Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a). with Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (b).) Yet both motions for reconsideration and renewed motions require an affidavit that states “what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Ibid.)

 

In addition, “[c]ourts have construed section 1008 to require a party filing an application for reconsideration or a renewed application to show diligence with a satisfactory explanation for not having presented the new or different information earlier.” (Even Zohar Constr. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 839, citations omitted; see also Cal. Corr. Peace Officers Ass’n v. Virga (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 30, 46, fn. 15.)

 

Judgment Debtor points to the following items as new or different information:

 

(1)       Judgment Creditor’s filing and then withdrawing of various discovery requests in one of the unrelated matters;

 

(2)       Judgment Debtor’s letter from his current psychologist, who was retained recently;

 

(3)       Judgment Creditor’s safe harbor notice, filed in one of the unrelated matters on September 21, 2023;

 

(4)       Judgment Creditor’s demurrer, filed in one of the unrelated matters on October 12, 2023;

 

(5)       Judgment Debtor’s safe harbor notices, filed in one of the unrelated matters on October 25, 2023;

 

(6)       Judgment Debtor’s written discovery responses, served on Judgment Creditor in September 2023;

 

(7)       Judgment Creditor’s written discovery responses, served on Judgment Debtor on November 8, 2023;

 

(8)       oral testimony from Judgment Debtor, which the prior judicial officer denied;

 

(9)       the “Johnston” evidence (a memorandum and evidence submitted by Judgment Debtor), which the prior judicial officer purportedly did not consider; and

 

(10)    case law submitted by Judgment Debtor, which the prior judicial officer purportedly did not consider.

 

(Decl. Vafa, ¶¶ 5–15.)

 

        Judgment Debtor also seems to argue that the Court should not have sustained an objection to evidence from Jonathan Charles, and that if the Court chooses to do so now, then that would be new or different. (Decl. Vafa, ¶¶ 12, 16.)

 

        The Court has not been presented with sufficiently new or different information to reach the merits of a renewed motion. All of the items that are claimed to be “new or different” are actually: (1) items that Judgment Debtor could have previously presented (by acting with diligence) to the Court at the time the Court made its prior rulings; (2) items that Judgment Debtor and/or Judgment Creditor previously presented to the Court, which the prior judicial officer either accepted, rejected, or chose not to discuss in its rulings; or (3) items that do not have any bearing on relief could be granted pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5.

 

        For this reason alone, the Court could deny the Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment.

 

C.      The Merits

 

Even if the Court were to reach the merits of the Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment, the Court would not grant it.

 

If the Court chose to exercise its discretion under Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5, the Court would need to consider:

 

(1)       The likelihood of the judgment debtor prevailing in the other action.

 

(2)       The amount of the judgment of the judgment creditor as compared to the amount of the probable recovery of the judgment debtor in the action on the disputed claim.

 

(3)       The financial ability of the judgment creditor to satisfy the judgment if a judgment is rendered against the judgment creditor in the action on the disputed claim.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 918.5, subd. (b).)

 

        Based on the limited information presented to the Court, it is unclear what is the likelihood of Judgment Debtor prevailing in either the civil harassment cases (Case No. 23STRO00340 and Appellate Case No. B328215) or the civil damages case for intentional infliction of emotional distress (Case No. Case No. 23SMCV01998). At bottom, the Parties’ respective and relative credibility is highly likely to play a determining factor in these cases. A previous judge of this case had “questions about Defendant’s credibility.”  (Hon. Steven Ellis, Minute Order, 8/3/23, p. 6.)  Currently, this Court does not have any reason to believe that Judgment Debtor is any more likely to prevail than Judgment Creditor in those matters. Because it will be Judgment Debtor’s burden to prove the cases he has brought, the Court cannot say that it is more likely than not that Judgment Debtor will prevail in the other actions.

 

        Similarly, it is difficult to ascertain at this time what the probable recovery of Judgment Debtor would be in the other actions. Unlike causes of action like breach of contract or unlawful receipt of stolen goods — which typically involve allegations of damages that are clear and liquidated at the time the complaint is filed — it is not at all clear at this time what amount of damages Judgment Debtor could or would receive for intentional infliction of emotional distress. Ascertaining such damages is a highly-fact-specific inquiry that the Court cannot conduct (or at least not conduct with a high degree of precision and accuracy) based on the very limited information submitted. Because the Court cannot ascertain Judgment Debtor’s probable recovery, the Court cannot compare such a recovery with the amount of the Judgment that Judgment Creditor has already obtained against Judgment Debtor.

 

        Finally, the prior judicial officer remarked that “neither party has presented much evidence as to [Judgment Creditor’s] ability to satisfy any judgment in the IIED Action.” (Minute Order dated August 3, 2023, p. 6.) No additional information has been provided that would clarify Judgment Creditor’s ability to satisfy any judgment in favor of Judgment Debtor and against Judgment Creditor. While it may have previously been the case that this situation “weighs in [Judgment Debtor’s] favor, as [Judgment Creditor] is certainly in a superior position to present this evidence to the Court but failed to do so,” that is no longer the case because Judgment Debtor has clearly been engaging in discovery. As both Parties should now have this information but have chosen not to share it with the Court, the Court’s current lack of information on this matter inclines the Court away from imposing a discretionary stay. Instead, it appears more prudent for the Court to maintain the status quo because the Court has not been presented with adequate information to justify changing it.

 

        Upon considering each of the factors listed in Code of Civil Procedure section 918.5, subdivision (b), the Court would not be inclined to exercise discretion to stay enforcement of the Judgment in this matter in the alternative scenario where the merits of the renewed motion should be considered.

 

D.     Sanctions

 

        Judgment Creditor requests monetary sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.

 

“A trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. This section also applies to judicial arbitration proceedings under Chapter 2.5 (commencing with Section 1141.10) of Title 3 of Part 3.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (a).)

 

        Given that Judgment Debtor did not meet the standard for filing a renewed motion, it appears to the Court that the renewed motion was frivolous. The Court will order Judgment Debtor and Judgment Debtor’s Counsel to pay reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred “as a result of” the frivolous renewed motion.

 

        In the Opposition, Judgment Creditor requests $7,500.00 in fees and $950.00 in costs. (Opposition, p. 9:15–19.) But in the Declaration of Jason Lonas, Judgment Creditor appears to request $10,800.00 in fees and $950.00 in costs. (Decl. Lonas, ¶¶ 15, 19, 21.) The Court bases the amount of fees requests on the declaration, which explains what work was done and on what.

 

Regarding the costs, all of the costs requested arose from the cost of a court reporter at a debtor’s examination that Judgment Debtor did not attend. Put differently, none of the costs requested directly involve the renewed motion.

 

The Court declines to award the requested costs. Those costs were not incurred for the renewed motion; the costs must have been incurred “as a result of” the frivolous motion in order to be expenses awarded pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, subdivision (a).

 

Regarding the fees, Judgment Creditor’s Counsel declares that they incurred thirty-six hours of work at $300.00 per hour, although only eight hours were incurred on the Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment and four hours are expected to be incurred at the hearing on the renewed motion. (Decl. Lone, ¶ 17.)

 

While the hourly rate requested is reasonable, the number of hours spent on the renewed motion was not reasonable. The Court will award fees for five hours of work (which includes one hour for the reply and one hour for the hearing) at the hourly rate of $300.00.

 

V.     Conclusion

       

The Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment is DENIED.

 

        Judgment Creditor’s Request for Sanctions is GRANTED in part. Sanctions are AWARDED in favor of Judgment Creditor and against Judgment Debtor and Judgment’s Debtor’s Counsel, jointly and severally, in the total amount of $1,500.00.
