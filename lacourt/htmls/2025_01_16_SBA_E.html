Case Number: 24TRCV02972    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

SETAREH NAZARIAN,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

24TRCV02972

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

MERCEDES BENZ USA, LLC.;

MERCEDES BENZ OF SOUTH BAY; and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             January 16, 2025

 

Moving Parties:          Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC

 

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Setareh Nazarian

 

HEARING: Motion to Compel Arbitration

           

The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

           

RULING

           

            The Court grants the Motion to Compel Arbitration.

           

BACKGROUND

 

            On September 9, 2024, Plaintiff Setareh Nazarian filed the Complaint against Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC, alleging causes of action for violations of the Song-Beverly Act, breach of the implied warranty of merchantability, and negligent repair.

 

            On October 11, 2024, Defendant filed the Motion to Compel Arbitration.

 

            On December 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On January 2, 2025, Defendant filed the Reply.

 

JUDICIAL NOTICE

           

The Court may take judicial notice of records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States. (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (d).) The Court may also take judicial notice of “Official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.” (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (c).)

           

            Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of the Complaint. Plaintiff has not objected to this request. Therefore, the Court grants the request.

 

            Plaintiff requests the Court take judicial notice of Ford Motor Warranty Cases (Cal. Ct. App. Apr. 4, 2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, Rosanna Montemayor et al. v. Ford Motor Company, 92 Cal.App.5th 958 (Cal. Ct. App. June 26, 2023), Kielar v. The Superior Court of Placer County, (Cal. Ct. App. August 16, 2023) 94 Cal.App. 5th 614, the California Court of Appeal, Third Appellate District’s August 28, 2023, Response to Petition for Writ of Mandate in Campos et al. v. The Superior Court of Butte County, No. C098848, California Court of Appeal, Third Appellate District’s August 28, 2023, Response to Petition for Writ of Mandate in Ortiz et al. v. The Superior Court of Sacramento County, No. C099135, Yeh v. Superior Ct. of Contra Costa Cnty., (Cal. Ct. App. Sept. 6, 2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 264, and Davis et al. v. Nissan North America, Inc., et al. (Cal. Ct. App. March 15, 2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 825.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The Federal Arbitration Act (the “FAA”) applies in both federal and state courts to contracts evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce. (9 U.S.C. §§ 1-2; Southland Corp. v. Keating (1984) 465 U.S. 1, 12.) The FAA preempts conflicting state law. (Preston v. Ferrer (2008) 552 U.S. 346, 353.) The party that contends the FAA applies bears the burden to demonstrate that the arbitration agreement is in a “‘contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce” (Woolls v. Super. Ct. (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b)¿Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿ 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

The instant case arises out of a lease for a 2023 Mercedes C Class. Defendants argue that Plaintiff signed a lease agreement that reads, in pertinent part:

 

Agreement to Arbitrate:

 

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort or otherwise (including any dispute over the interpretation, scope, or validity of this lease, arbitration section or the arbitrability of any issue), between you and us or any of our employees, agents, successors, assigns, or the vehicle distributor, including Mercedes-Benz USA LLC (each a “Third-Party Beneficiary”), which arises out of or relates to a credit application, this lease, or any resulting transaction or relationship arising out of this lease (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at the election of either you, us, or a Third- Party Beneficiary, be resolved by a neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action. (Ameripour Decl. Exhibit A.)

 

Existence of Valid Arbitration Agreement and Claims Covered by Arbitration Clause¿ 

 

“[T]he petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence . . . .” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.) “In determining whether an arbitration agreement applies to a specific dispute, the court may examine only the agreement itself and the complaint filed by the party refusing arbitration [citation]. The court should attempt to give effect to the parties’ intentions, in light of the usual and ordinary meaning of the contractual language and the circumstances under which the agreement was made.” (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.) “To determine whether a contractual arbitration clause requires arbitration of a particular controversy, the controversy is first identified, and the issue is whether that controversy is within the scope of the contractual arbitration clause.” (Titolo v. Cano (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 310, 316.) “Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration. The court should order them to arbitrate unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.” (California Correctional Peace Officers Ass’n v. State (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205.) 

 

“[A] party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense. [Citation.] In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Giuliano, supra, 149 Cal.App.4th at p. 1284.) 

 

            Third-Party Beneficiary

 

            Defendant argues that it can enforce the arbitration provision in the Plaintiff’s Lease as a third-party beneficiary. (Ronay Family Ltd P’ship v. Tweed, (2013) 216 Cal. App. 4th 830, 836; Macauly v. Norlander (1992) 12 Cal. App. 4th 1, 6-8.) Defendant emphasizes that the arbitration clause in the Lease explicitly includes Defendant as a third-party beneficiary, along with other related parties such as the vehicle distributor.

 

The arbitration provision states that disputes between the lessee and "us or any of our employees, agents, successors, assigns, or the vehicle distributor, including Mercedes-Benz USA LLC," may be resolved through binding arbitration. (Ameripour Decl. Exhibit A.) Defendant argues that this provision demonstrates the parties' intent to make Defendant a beneficiary of the arbitration clause. Furthermore, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s claims arise out of and relate to the Lease and the resulting warranty relationship, further solidifying its right to enforce the arbitration provision.

 

Defendant argues that when a non-signatory falls within the scope of an arbitration agreement due to the nature of the relationship or explicit inclusion, the non-signatory may compel arbitration. (See, Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 625, 636.) Since Plaintiff's claims are tied to the Lease and the warranty relationship, Defendant asserts that it is entitled to enforce the arbitration provision, and the claims must be resolved through arbitration.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant does not qualify as a third-party beneficiary under the California Supreme Court’s Goonewardene test, which requires three elements: (1) the third party must directly benefit from the contract, (2) a motivating purpose of the contract must be to benefit the third party, and (3) allowing the third party to enforce the contract must align with the contract’s objectives and the parties’ reasonable expectations. (Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 829-830.)

 

First, Plaintiff asserts that Defendant does not directly benefit from the lease. Payments under the lease are made directly to the lessor, not to Defendant. While Defendant may receive an indirect benefit through the dealership’s ability to purchase additional vehicles, such incidental benefits are insufficient to satisfy the first element of Goonewardene. (Ngo v. BMW of North America, LLC (2022) 23 F.4th 942, 946.)

 

Second, Plaintiff contends that the motivating purpose of the lease is not to benefit Defendant. The primary purpose of the lease is to transfer possession of the vehicle to the lessee and set payment terms with the lessor, not to confer benefits on Defendant. Plaintiff argues that the dealership and lessee entered into the lease for their own interests, not to provide benefits to Defendant. (See Fuentes v. TMCSF, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 541, 553.)

 

Third, Plaintiff argues that allowing Defendant to bring a breach of contract action against Plaintiffs would be inconsistent with the lease’s objectives because Defendant would not suffer damages from any breach of the lease. None of the lessee’s obligations under the lease affect Defendant, making enforcement of the lease by Defendant unreasonable and outside the parties’ expectations.

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff argues that warranties from a manufacturer, such as Defendant, are independent of the Lease Agreement and cannot be compelled to arbitration under its terms.  Plaintiff emphasizes that manufacturer warranties do not arise from the lease or sales contract but are separate obligations and that the appellate court has regularly rejected the application of third-party beneficiary claims by non-signatory manufacturers. (Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324, 1329; Montemayor et al. v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 961; Kielar v. The Superior Court of Placer County, 94 Cal. App. 5th 614, 620; Yeh v. Superior Ct. of Contra Costa Cnty., 95 Cal.App.5th 264.)

 

Plaintiff contends that the Lease provision does not establish an agreement to arbitrate claims with third-party non-signatories. The claims are unrelated to the Lease's terms and solely focus on obligations arising from the manufacturer’s warranties. Therefore, Plaintiff argues that arbitration should not be compelled under the Lease Agreement.

 

In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff's opposition to arbitration is unfounded because the arbitration provision in the Lease explicitly names Defendant Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC as a third-party beneficiary. Unlike the cases Plaintiff relies on, where manufacturers were not explicitly included, this Lease clearly identifies MBUSA as an intended beneficiary. Defendant asserts that the arbitration provision applies to "any claim or dispute" between Plaintiff and MBUSA, covering all claims arising from the Lease and the manufacturer-consumer relationship. (Ameripour Decl. Exhibit A.)

 

Defendant argues that MBUSA meets the criteria for third-party beneficiary status: (1) MBUSA benefits from the contract, (2) the contract expressly includes MBUSA as a beneficiary, and (3) enforcing the arbitration provision aligns with the contract's objectives. Defendant also notes that the Court in Ford Motor Warranty Cases states, “If the signatories had intended to benefit FMC, such a purpose would have been easy to articulate. They could have simply named FMC—directly or by class as the vehicle’s manufacturer—as a person entitled to compel arbitration.” (Ford Motor Warranty Cases, supra, 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324, 1329.)

 

Defendant emphasizes that doubts about arbitrability should be resolved in favor of arbitration under federal and state precedent. Since the Lease explicitly grants MBUSA the right to compel arbitration and Plaintiff's claims fall within its scope, Defendant requests that the Court compel arbitration and stay the proceedings until arbitration concludes. Exhibits submitted confirm that both state and federal courts have upheld MBUSA's right to compel arbitration under similar agreements.

 

Under the Goonewardene test, the elements required for a finding of third-party beneficiary status are (1) whether the third party would, in fact, benefit from the contract, but also (2) whether a motivating purpose of the contracting parties was to provide a benefit to the third party, and (3) whether permitting a third party to bring its own breach of contract action against a contracting party is consistent with the objectives of the contract and the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties.” (Goonewardene, supra, 6 Cal.5th 817, 829-830.)

 

First, we look to whether the third party would benefit from the contract. While Plaintiff relies on Ngo to argue that Defendant does not benefit from the contract, in Ngo, the arbitration agreement stated that only the plaintiff, the dealership, or the assignee could enforce the arbitration agreement, and therefore, the arbitration clause was not intended to benefit the manufacturer. (See, Ngo, supra, 23 F.4th 942, 946.) Here, however, the arbitration agreement directly states that MBUSA may enforce the arbitration agreement and names MBUSA as a third-party beneficiary. (Ameripour Decl. Exhibit A.) Therefore, the Court finds MBUSA would directly benefit from the contract.

 

Second, we look at whether a motivating purpose of the contracting parties was to provide a benefit to the third party. Defendant argues that the motivating purpose of the lease was to turn over the car to Plaintiff in return for payment. In support of their argument, Plaintiff cites Ngo and Fuentes; however, in both these cases, the arbitration agreement did not list Defendant as a party with the power to enforce the arbitration agreement. (See, Ngo, supra, 23 F.4th 942, 946; Fuentes v. TMCSF, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 541, 551-552.) Here, again, MBUSA is listed as a third-party beneficiary with the power to compel arbitration regarding disputes “which arises out of or relates to a credit application, this lease, or any resulting transaction or relationship arising out of this lease (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract.)” (Ameripour Decl. Exhibit A.) Therefore, the Court finds that the motivating purpose of the arbitration agreement within the contract was to provide a benefit to MBUSA as well as any other signatory or third-party beneficiary.

 

Last, we consider whether permitting a third party to bring its own breach of contract action against a contracting party is consistent with the contract's objectives and the parties' reasonable expectations. “This element calls for a judgment regarding the potential effect that permitting third party enforcement would have on the parties’ contracting goals, rather than a determination whether the parties actually anticipated third party enforcement at the time the contract was entered into.” (Goonewardene, supra, 6 Cal.5th 817, 831.) Defendant argues that MBUSA would not be permitted to bring a breach of contract party against Plaintiff as any breach of the lease would not cause any damage to MBUSA. However, in Ngo, when analyzing the third factor of the Goonewardene test, the Court analyzed the arbitration agreement and not the contract as a whole. (Ngo, supra, 23 F.4th 942, 948.) Here, the arbitration agreement lists MBUSA as an intended third-party beneficiary, and therefore, the Court finds that the parties reasonably expected that MBUSA would have the power to enforce the contract as a third-party beneficiary. (Goonewardene, supra, 6 Cal.5th 817, 831; (Ameripour Decl. Exhibit A.)

 

Thus, the Court finds that MBUSA satisfies all the elements for a finding of third-party beneficiary status under Goonewardene. (Goonewardene, supra, 6 Cal.5th 817, 829-830.)

 

Plaintiff also argues that the warranty claims are not subject to the arbitration provision. Manufacturer warranties are separate and independent from the Lease Agreement and are not "claims or disputes... which arise out of or relate to... this lease." (Ford Motor Warranty Cases, supra, 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324, 1334; Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 961.)  Plaintiff contends that the claims are based solely on MBUSA's warranty obligations and are distinct from the Lease terms. However, the portions of Ford Motor Warranty Cases and Montemayor Plaintiff cites ins support of this argument analyze the doctrine of equitable estoppel, not third-party beneficiaries, and Plaintiff has provided no other authority for his argument to stand on. (Ford Motor Warranty Cases, supra, 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324, 1334; Montemayor, supra, 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 961.) Thus, the Court finds this argument unpersuasive.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Arbitration.
