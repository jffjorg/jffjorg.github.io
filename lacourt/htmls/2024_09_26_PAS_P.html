DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01599    Hearing Date: September 26, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF TRISTAN C. HATFIELD’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE)

I.        INTRODUCTION

       This is an insurance coverage action arising from a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs Tristan C. Hatfield (“Hatfield”) and Lucille Annakilsby Renville (“Renville”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants California Casualty Insurance Company, California Casualty Indemnity Exchange (“Defendants”) and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive on July 12, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (2) Breach of Contract; and (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.  On May 29, 2024, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action without leave to amend and granted Defendant’s motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages.

Plaintiffs allege Renville was involved in a motor vehicle accident with Mathew Thomas MacDougal who was driving a motorcycle. (Compl., ¶7.) Hatfield was the passenger on MacDougal’s motorcycle. (Id.) MacDougal and Hatfield suffered permanent injuries because of the accident. (Id.) Renville was covered by an insurance policy with Defendants and timely notified them of the accident. (Compl., ¶¶8-9.) Hatfield made a demand to settle the case for $100,000.00, which was rejected by Defendants. (Compl., ¶¶10-11.) On August 3, 2017, Hatfield filed a personal injury lawsuit against Renville titled Tristan C. Hatfield  v. Lucille Annakilsby Renville, Case No. BC670821 (“Underlying Action” or “U/A”). (Compl., ¶14.)


The U/A went to trial and a verdict was rendered in Hatfield’s favor for a judgment of $9,082,710.00, costs, prejudgment interest, interest at 10 percent per annum. (Compl., ¶16.) Hatfield made a demand on Defendants to pay the judgment plus costs, prejudgment interest, and postjudgment interest. (Compl., ¶17.) Defendants refused to pay the judgment, costs, prejudgment interest, and postjudgment interest. (Compl., ¶18.) Defendants have paid a partial satisfaction of the judgment in the U/A totaling $380,882.97 consisting of $100,000.00 stated policy limits and two checks for post judgment interest of $280,882.97. (Compl., ¶20.)

          On June 14, 2024, Hatfield filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One). On September 13, 2024,  Defendant California Casualty Indemnity Exchange (“California Indemnity”) filed an opposition. On September 19, 2024, Hatfield filed a reply.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civil Procedure Section 2030.300 governs motions to compel further responses to interrogatories. It provides as follows:

“(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

Motions must be made within 45 days of any response or supplemental response (Subdivision (c)) and must be accompanied by a declaration demonstrating the parties met and conferred to attempt informal resolution; they must also be accompanied by a separate statement or concise outline. (Subdivision (b).)

Subdivision (d) provides for the issuance of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the court finds the party who would be subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make sanctions unjust.

 

On the 45-day limit: Golf & Pro Shop, Inc. v. Superior Court (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 127

 

A responding party is under a statutory obligation to provide responses that are “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.220, subd. (a).) Further, “If only part of an interrogatory is objectionable, the remainder of the interrogatory shall be answered,” and “the specific ground for the objection shall be set forth clearly in the response.” (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.240, subds. (a)-(b), italics added.) That is, to be code-compliant, a response should state all available information available and, if there is additional responsive information that is being withheld based on a privilege, it should so state and identify the privilege under which information is being withheld. Prefacing a substantive response with the words, “subject to and without waiving” all objections is not helpful in terms of understanding whether additional information is being withheld based on a privilege, and which privilege is at issue.

Similarly, Hatfield asserts the privilege objections as to SROG No. 11 are inapplicable for the same reasons set forth regarding SROG No. 9.

 

The Court notes that the moving papers and separate statement indicate Hatfield also seeks further response to SROG No. 10.  However, the moving papers and separate statement do not address SROG No. 10 nor any arguments to support further response to it. Nonetheless, California Indemnity provides SROG No. 10 in its separate statement.

 

In opposition, California Indemnity argues SROG Nos. 8, 9, 10, and 11 asks Defendants to identify when an analysis related to any potential liability in this action was made, which would have come from California Indemnity’s counsel. More specifically, California Indemnity asserts the information sought by SROG Nos. 8, 9, 10, and 11 relate to when California Indemnity received a communication or advice from its attorney regarding their potentiality liability or existence of an open policy.

 

However, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 2030.010, subdivision (b), “An interrogatory is not objectionable because an answer to it involves an opinion or contention that relates to fact or the application of law to fact, or would be based on information obtained or legal theories developed in anticipation of litigation or in preparation for trial.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.010, subd. (b).) As such, California Indemnity’s objections to SROG Nos. 8, 9, 10, and 11 are without merit.

I.         INTRODUCTION

            This action arises out of an alleged slip-and-fall incident involving Plaintiff Bradley Erwin ("Plaintiff") that occurred on January 9, 2022, at 3329 South Bristol Street in Santa Ana, California. Plaintiff alleges that, while lawfully on the premises, he slipped and fell on an unknown liquid substance. Plaintiff claims that Trader Joe’s, Excel Realty Partners, Excel Realty Partners, L.P., and Does 1 through 100 (collectively, "Defendants") negligently owned, maintained, and operated the premises.

As a result of the fall, Plaintiff asserts that he sustained serious injuries and required medical treatment. On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed this lawsuit, asserting causes of action for (1) premises liability and (2) general negligence.

 

PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Complaint. On May 30, 2024, Defendants, specifically, Trader Joe’s (“Defendant Trader Joe’s”) filed a Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

On September 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition to Defendant Trader Joe’s Demurrer, and on September 19, 2024, Defendant Trader Joe’s filed a Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition.

Timeliness of the Demurrer

Plaintiff’s counsel alleges that Defendant Trader Joe’s did not timely file its demurrer, asserting that the defense had 30 days from service to respond to the complaint. (Renge Decl., Exhibit A) Plaintiff served Defendant Trader Joe’s via personal service on April 2, 2024. Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 412.20(a)(3), after personal service of a summons and complaint, a defendant must file a responsive pleading within 30 days. Therefore, the original deadline for Defendant to respond was May 2, 2024. However, on May 1, 2024, Defendant's counsel requested a thirty-day extension to file a responsive pleading, which Plaintiff’s counsel granted. (Renge Decl., Exhibit B) As a result, Defendant’s filing of the demurrer on May 30, 2024, was timely and within the extended deadline agreed upon by both parties.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer, or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

In this instance, the Court finds that Defendant Trader Joe's sufficiently complied with the meet-and-confer requirements. Defense counsel for Trader Joe's first reached out via email on May 1, 2024, requesting potential dates and times for a meet and confer, specifically stating, "Please let us know a few dates/times you are available to discuss." (Renge Decl., ¶2, Exhibit B) On May 17, 2024, defense counsel sent another letter via email, reiterating that the letter constituted a good faith effort to meet and confer and again requesting, "Please provide me with available dates so that we can complete our telephonic meet and confer requirements." (Mathis Decl., ¶3, Exhibit A) Despite these attempts, Plaintiff’s counsel responded on May 20, 2024, without providing any specific dates or times to conduct a telephonic meet and confer. (Renge Decl., Exhibit C)

Accordingly, the Court finds that the defense has made reasonable and sufficient efforts to comply with the meet-and-confer obligations under Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.41 and 435.5.

MOVING PARTY’S ARGUMENT

Defendant Trader Joe’s demurs to the causes of action for (1) premises liability and (2) general negligence, arguing that Plaintiff fails to state sufficient facts to support either cause of action. Additionally, Defendant Trader Joe’s contends that Plaintiff’s complaint is uncertain regarding the specific allegations against each defendant.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

A.    General Demurrer – Failure to State Facts Sufficient to Constitute a Cause of Action

A general demurrer may be taken to a complaint where “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code  Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.)¿

First cause of action: Premise Liability

Defendant Trader Joe’s demurs to the first cause of action for premises liability, arguing that Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to establish the necessary elements for premises liability against a landowner. (Demurrer, pp. 8-10).

The elements of a cause of action for premises liability mirror those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998; Civ. Code, § 1714, subd. (a).) To plead a cause of action for premises liability, Plaintiff must allege that Defendant Trader Joe’s had actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition, or that they could have discovered the condition through the exercise of ordinary care. (See Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206). Constructive knowledge can be imputed if the dangerous condition existed for a sufficient period of time to be discovered by a reasonably prudent person. (Id. at p. 1207).

“A condition is dangerous if it creates a substantial risk of injury when foreseeable users use the property with due care.” (Huffman v. City of Poway (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 975, 991 (citing Gov. Code, § 830, subd. (a).) “A condition is not dangerous if the risk of injury created by the condition is so minor, trivial or insignificant that it arises only when foreseeable users do not use due care.” (Id., citing Gov. Code, § 830.2.) “Whether a given set of facts and circumstances creates a dangerous condition is usually a question of fact.” (Ibid.) “The issue of a dangerous condition becomes a question of law only where reasonable minds can come to only one conclusion.” (Ibid.) “Whether the condition of property posed a substantial risk of injury to foreseeable users exercising due care is an objective standard and is measured by the risk posed to an ordinary foreseeable user.” (Id. at p. 992.)

In this case, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s allegations are conclusory and fail to plead sufficient facts to meet the required elements of a premises liability claim.

Duty:

Plaintiff alleges that Defendants owed him a duty to maintain the premises in a safe condition, claiming that "Defendants breached their duty to Plaintiff by failing to address, alleviate, remove and/or remedy the dangerous condition." (Compl. p.4). The Court finds that this allegation lacks specificity regarding how Defendant Trader Joe’s failed to uphold its duty. Plaintiff has not provided facts regarding the time and manner in which he was on the premises, whether he was shopping at Trader Joe’s, or if the incident occurred during closed hours. Plaintiff also fails to allege where on the premises the incident occurred, e.g. inside the store, the parking lot, the loading dock, etc.  The absence of these factual details renders the claim insufficient to support the contention that Defendant Trader Joe’s owed a duty of care.  

Breach:

Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to remove or remedy the dangerous condition. Specifically, Plaintiff claims that "Defendants breached their duty to Plaintiff by failing to address, alleviate, remove and/or remedy the dangerous condition." (Compl., p. 4.) The Court finds that while the statement that Defendants breached their duty is a legal conclusion, the demurrer admits the truth of all material, ultimate facts alleged in the complaint. As such, Plaintiff is not required to plead detailed evidentiary facts at this stage but must only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise Defendants of the nature of the claim.

Causation:

While Plaintiff alleges that the breach of duty caused severe injuries, the complaint does not include sufficient factual allegations to establish a causal link between Defendant Trader Joe’s actions (or inaction) and the incident. Plaintiff’s general statement that “this caused Plaintiff to suffer severe injuries” (Compl. p. 4) is insufficient to demonstrate how Defendant’s conduct led to the harm suffered.

Damages:

Plaintiff pleads that he received "reasonable and necessary medical care and treatment" as a result of the fall (Compl. p. 4). The Court finds that the damages were sufficiently pled.

Dangerous Condition - Premise Liability

Finally, Plaintiff describes the dangerous condition as an “unknown liquid substance” (Compl. p. 4). While this may suggest a potentially hazardous condition, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not provided sufficient details about the nature of the substance, how long it was present, or whether Trader Joe’s knew or should have known of its existence. A premises liability claim requires more than a conclusory statement about a dangerous condition; Plaintiff must provide facts indicating that the condition posed a substantial risk of harm to foreseeable users exercising due care. (Huffman v. City of Poway (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 975, 991.)

 

Second cause of action: General Negligence

Defendant Trader Joe’s demurs to the second cause of action for general negligence, arguing that Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to establish the necessary elements of negligence. (Demurrer, pp. 11-13).

To state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the following elements: (1) the existence of a legal duty of care, (2) breach of that duty, and (3) proximate cause resulting in an injury. (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671).

Here, Plaintiff alleges that "Defendants so negligently owned, operated, maintained, entrusted, and/or controlled the subject premises." (Compl. p. 5). The Court finds that this allegation is conclusory. Merely stating that Defendants acted negligently does not provide sufficient factual detail to support the claim. There are no specific facts presented regarding how Trader Joe’s conduct constituted negligence or how Defendants' actions led to the alleged harm. Additionally, Plaintiff asserts that "Defendants owed a legal duty, and Defendants breached their duty." (Compl. p. 5). Again, the Court finds that this language is conclusory and lacks sufficient factual support. Plaintiff has not adequately explained how the duty was breached or what specific actions or inactions by Defendant Trader Joe’s amounted to a breach of the duty of care.

Plaintiff also claims that "Defendants were subject to and violated ADA, California Health & Safety Codes" (Compl. p.5), and that "these violations legally caused Plaintiff's damages" (Compl. p. 4). The Court finds that these allegations are similarly conclusory and do not provide sufficient factual details. Plaintiff fails to specify which provisions of the ADA or Health & Safety Codes were allegedly violated, how these violations occurred, or how they directly caused Plaintiff’s injuries.

In conclusion, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not sufficiently plead the elements required to support a claim for general negligence. The allegations are too conclusory and lack the necessary factual specificity to establish that Defendant Trader Joe’s owed and breached a duty of care, or that their conduct proximately caused Plaintiff’s injuries.

Thus, because Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for general negligence, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the first two causes of action with leave to amend.

 

B.    Special Demurrer - Uncertainty

            A defendant may demur to a complaint on the grounds that the pleading is “uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Ibid.) A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed because ambiguities can be clarified through modern discovery procedures. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so vague that the defendant cannot reasonably respond—that is, they cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against them. 

Additionally, the objection of uncertainty does not pertain to a failure to allege sufficient facts. (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) It pertains instead to doubt about what the pleader means by the facts alleged. (Ibid.) A demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues they must meet. (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)

In this case, Defendant Trader Joe’s alleges that Plaintiff’s complaint is uncertain because it fails to specify which defendants are responsible for the alleged wrongful conduct. Plaintiff refers collectively to “Defendants,” which include Trader Joe’s, Excel Realty Partners, Excel Realty Partners, L.P., and Does 1 through 100. By not distinguishing between the individual defendants, Plaintiff fails to provide clear factual allegations identifying the specific actions or inactions attributable to each defendant.

Defendant Trader Joe’s argues that Plaintiff’s use of the term “Defendants” without specificity hinders their ability to respond adequately to the complaint, and that it prejudices their defense. (Demurrer, p. 19.) Although the Court agrees that grouping all defendants together without specifying individual roles makes it challenging for Defendants to ascertain responsibility, Defendant Trader Joe’s can still reasonably determine whether the premises in question are theirs. The Court finds that this lack of specificity does not severely prejudice Defendant Trader Joe’s, and the issue can be remedied by an amendment to the complaint.

Accordingly, the special demurrer for uncertainty is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

IV.       ORDER

            The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to first and second causes of action, as well as the demurrer for uncertainty with leave to amend.  Plaintiff to file his first amended complaint within 20 days of this ruling.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This action was filed on April 14, 2016, and arises from a business dispute that occurred on April 16, 2014, resulting in a physical altercation and ultimately, the shooting of Plaintiffs Arutyun Fitilchyan (Fitilchan), Hrachya Gasparyan (Gasparyan) and Defendant Robert Torosian (Robert).

Plaintiffs Fitilchyan, Galina Azumanova, Gasparyan, and Marine Hovhannisyan filed a complaint against Defendants Akop Torosian aka Jack Torosian (Akop), and Robert. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) assault and battery, (2) conspiracy, (3) negligence, (4) loss of consortium, and (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

The complaint alleges that Fitilchyan was contacted by Akop to perform locksmith services for him. Fitilchyan alleges he was on his way to deliver brochures and schedule the service when he ran low on fuel. On telephoning Akop about his tardiness, Akop began to verbally abuse Fitilchyan, which caused Fitilchyan to decide he was no longer willing to provide the services. Fitilchyan alleges that after this he went to visit his friend Gasparyan at his body shop. Fitilchyan alleges that while the two were having coffee at the body shop, Akop showed up and proceeded to scream obscenities at him and the two of them got into a scuffle which body shop employees broke up. Fitilchyan alleges Akop left but then returned with his brother Robert, who charged Fitilchyan and struck him with his fist. He alleges that Akop then produced a gun and began shooting at them, in response to which Gasparyan retrieved a gun and returned fire.

On June 25, 2024, Robert filed this motion for summary judgment, or in the alterative, summary adjudication. On September 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition. On September 20, 2024, Robert filed a reply.

The motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication is denied as Plaintiffs provide evidence sufficient to show there are triable issues of material fact.

II. LEGAL STANDARD

Summary judgment is appropriately granted where it is shown that an action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action. (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(a).)  

 

A cause of action has no merit if one of the following exists: (1) one or more necessary element cannot be established; (2) a defendant establishes an affirmative defense. (Id., Section §437c(o).)

 

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted where all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue of material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Id., Section 437c(c).)

 

Summary adjudication may be granted where it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim of damages, or an issue of duty. (Id., Section 437c(f).)

 

          III.     DISCUSSION

          As an initial matter, Plaintiffs argue in the opposition that Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is untimely and the Court lacks authority to grant it. The Court already ruled on this issue in its order to advance the motion for summary judgment, and thus, will not readdress this argument as Plaintiffs failed to file a motion for reconsideration, and present no new facts or law.

i.                 Assault and Battery

Defendant Robert moves for summary judgment or adjudication as to the cause of action for assault and battery, on the grounds that he did not intend to cause harm or injure Plaintiffs and he did not make any threats.

“The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.”  (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 669.) Intent is satisfied if the defendant acted with willful disregard of the plaintiff’s rights. (See Ashcraft v. King (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 604, 613.)  

“The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with the intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.”  (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669.)  Physical injury is not required; rather, the tort of assault is complete when anticipation of harm occurs. (See Kiseskey v. Carpenters’ Trust for Southern California (1983) 144 Cal.App.3d 222, 232.) An intent to put the victim in imminent apprehension of a harmful contact is enough. (Lowry v. Standard Oil Co. (1944) 63 Cal. App. 2d 1, 6–7.)

In support of his motion for summary judgment, Defendant declares he only intended to transport his brother, Jack Torosian, to the auto body repair shop. (UMF No. 2.) Defendant also declares had no intention to cause bodily harm to any of Plaintiffs or other persons at the time of the subject incident and that he did not threaten any of the Plaintiffs. (UMF Nos. 3-4.)

Defendant has met his burden on summary judgment or adjudication to present evidence sufficient to show that there are no triable issues of material fact as to whether he intended on committing assault and battery, or whether he made any threats. The burden shifts to Plaintiffs to present triable issues of material fact.

Plaintiffs submitted evidence sufficient to show there are triable issues of material fact. Plaintiffs submit the testimony of witnesses who were present at the incident. Armen Jermakyan was present as the first incident ended, and Jermakyan saw the Torosians return for the second incident. (Jermakyan Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. 3.) Jermakyan saw the two brothers park in a getaway position, blocking the driveway to the multi-business parking, Akop brandishing a gun in an open and obvious fashion, with Robert carrying a metal chain wrapped around his arm, asking for Fitilchyan and Gasparyan. (Id., ¶¶ 3-5.) The brothers appeared “angry and aggressive,” and given the gun and chain, the car being parked in a getaway position with the door open, Jermakyan was in fear of his life and hid. (Id.)

Ashot Mkhitaryan was present during Akop’s first encounter with Plaintiffs. Mkhitaryan tried to de-escalate the situation with Akop, and he texted Akop before to drop it, not to come to the body shop, and that Plaintiffs were not trying to fight with him. (Mkhitaryan Depo., Exhs. 5, 5A, 5B at 56:23-57:16; 57:25-58:2.)

Plaintiffs also submit evidence that Defendant Robert punched Fitilchyan. (Fitilchyan Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. E.)

The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ evidence that Robert showed up at the auto body shop while parked in a getaway position, with a chain wrapped around his arm, accompanied by his brother Akop who was openly brandishing a gun, asking for Plaintiffs, and that Robert punched Fitilchyan, raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant intended to cause injury, and whether Defendant Robert threatened Plaintiffs. Robert wrapped a chain around his arm, asked about Plaintiffs and punched one of the Plaintiffs; the evidence presented tends to show that he intended to cause harm and that he made threats.

Defendant is not entitled to summary judgment as to the cause of action for assault and battery.

ii. Conspiracy

Defendant also moves for summary adjudication as to the cause of action for conspiracy and argues that Plaintiffs cannot prove that he had any knowledge of a plan or design.

To support a conspiracy claim, the following elements must be shown: “(1) the formation and operation of the conspiracy, (2) wrongful conduct in furtherance of the conspiracy, and (3) damages arising from the wrongful conduct.” (Arei II Cases (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1022.) Civil¿conspiracy¿is a “legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves, share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration.” (Kidron v. Movie Acquisition Corp.¿(1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1571, 1581.) The conspiring defendants must also have actual knowledge that a tort is planned and concur in the tortious scheme with knowledge of its unlawful purpose. (Id., at 1582.) “Mere knowledge, acquiescence, or approval of an act, without cooperation or agreement to cooperate is insufficient to establish liability." (Id.)

It must be recognized, however, that because of the very nature of a conspiracy, “its existence must often be inferentially and circumstantially derived from the character of the acts done, the relations of the parties and other facts and circumstances suggestive of concerted action.” (Arei II Cases, supra, 216 Cal.App.4th at 1022.)

To meet his burden, Defendant submits his own declaration, and he declares that he did not have actual knowledge of any plan or design to shoot or injure Plaintiffs. (UMF No. 5.) Defendant states that he accompanied his brother to meet with Fitilchyan for the purpose of discussing a business issue; not with any knowledge that his brother intended to shoot or harm anyone. (UMF Nos. 2, 3.) Defendant also states that he was not aware his brother had a gun. (UMF No. 7.)

Defendant has met his burden on summary judgment to show there is no triable issue of material fact as to whether he had any knowledge of a plan or design to shoot or injure Plaintiffs.

Plaintiffs evidence of the witnesses’ statements that Robert and Akop entered the body shop while openly carrying weapons, parked in a getaway position, angrily asked for Plaintiffs, and Robert punched one plaintiff in the face, provides triable issues of material fact as to Defendant’s knowledge of Akop’s plan.

Further, Plaintiffs submit the testimony of Elda Madatyan, Akop’s ex-wife, who declares that Robert and Akop were very close, and she “regularly observed Jack [aka Akop] share his plans and problems with Robert and would include Robert in everything important, whether personal or business that Jack was dealing with or planning.” (Madatyan Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. D.) Akop “does not plan, execute or take action of any consequence, without Robert’s knowledge, guidance and/or approval.” (Id., ¶ 11.)

The evidence of Defendant’s conduct at the body shop, combined with the fact that Defendants are brothers, and Madatyan’s statement that they are close and confide in each other, provides triable issues of material fact as to whether Robert knew of the plan and he acted in concert with Akop.

Defendant next argues he did not have the requisite intent. The Court incorporates its discussion above as to intent.

Thus, Defendant is not entitled to summary adjudication as to the cause of action for conspiracy.

iii. Negligence

Defendant also moves for summary judgment or adjudication, on the ground that he owed Plaintiffs no duty of care.

“The elements of a cause of action for negligence are well established. They are (a) a legal duty to use due care; (b) a breach of such legal duty; [and] (c) the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917, internal quotations omitted.) Every case is governed by the rule of general application that all persons are required to use ordinary care to prevent others from being harmed as a result of their conduct. (Weirum v. RKO General, Inc. (1975) 15 Cal.3d 40, 46.) “The law imposes a general duty of care on a defendant only when it is the defendant who has " ‘created a risk’ " of harm to the plaintiff, including when " ‘the defendant is responsible for making the plaintiff's position worse.’ " ” (Brown v. U.S. Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 214.) An actor whose conduct has not created a risk of physical or emotional harm to another has no duty of care to the other. (Id.) Generally, one owes no duty to control the conduct of another, nor to warn those endangered by such conduct unless an exception applies. (Id.)

Defendant argues there is no relationship between Defendant and Plaintiffs that gives rise to a duty. Defendant argues he was not the instigator of any physical altercation with Plaintiffs. (UMF No. 12.) Defendant argues he was shot in the leg during the subject incident. (UMF No. 13.) Defendant argues did not have a gun at the time of the subject incident. (UMF No. 14.)

However, the evidence presented shows that Defendant and his brother went to the body shop armed with weapons, despite being told that Plaintiffs did not want to fight, and instigated the incident by showing up with weapons, asking where Plaintiffs were, and punching one Plaintiff in the face. Thus, there are triable issues of material fact as to duty as the evidence suggests that Defendant also created the risk harm and thus, owes a general duty of care to Plaintiffs.

Defendant also argues that the incident was not foreseeable because Defendant had no intention to cause harm and he was not aware his brother carried a gun. The Court disagrees and incorporates its analysis above as to intent. Further, the fact that Robert also brought a weapon, and his brother brandished the gun openly presents triable issues of material fact as to whether he knew his brother brought a gun. The evidence thus suggests that the harm was foreseeable.

Defendant also argues there is no evidence of breach. He contends that Jack and Gasparyan, not Defendant, were the only people armed at the time of the subject incident. (UMF No. 16.) Defendant contends there is no evidence that Defendant caused any harm or had any physical contact with Plaintiffs at the time of the subject incident. (UMF No. 17.)

The evidence presented by Plaintiffs shows that Defendant brought a chain and punched a plaintiff. There are triable issues of material fact as to whether he breached a duty.

iv. Loss of Consortium

“There are four elements to a cause of action for loss of consortium: ‘(1) a valid and lawful marriage between the plaintiff and the person injured at the time of the injury; [¶] (2) a tortious injury to the plaintiff’s spouse; [¶] (3) loss of consortium suffered by the plaintiff; and [¶] (4) the loss was proximately caused by the defendant’s act.’ [Citations.]” (Vanhooser v. Superior Court (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 921, 927 [emphasis removed].)

“Injury is an essential element of any cause of action,” and, therefore, “[w]ithout injury to the spouse, the plaintiff has no loss of consortium claim. Thus, a cause of action is not complete in the sense it is not actionable, without spousal injury.” (Vanhooser, supra, 206 Cal.App.4th at pp. 927-928.)

          Defendant argues that because the underlying claims fail, the loss of consortium claim fails too.

However, as discussed above, Defendant is not entitled to summary adjudication as to the causes of action above. Therefore, Defendant is also not entitled to summary adjudication as to the cause of action for loss of consortium. 

          v. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

          Defendant moves for summary adjudication of the cause of action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, arguing that his conduct was not outrageous, and he did not intend to cause harm.

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.”  (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 903, internal quotations omitted.)  For conduct to be outrageous it must be “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.”  (See Ess v. Eskaton Properties, Inc. (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 120, 130.)  Liability does not extend to mere insults, indignities, threats, annoyances, petty oppressions, or other trivialities.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1051.)  “Severe emotional distress means . . . emotional distress of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable man in a civilized society should be expected to endure it.”  (Fletcher v. Western Nat. Life Ins. Co. (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 376.)  

“[I]t is not enough that the defendant has acted with an intent which is tortious or even criminal, or that he has intended to inflict emotional distress, or even that his conduct has been characterized by ‘malice,’ or a degree of aggravation which would entitle the plaintiff to punitive damages for another tort.”  (Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 488, 496.)  “Liability has been found only where the conduct has been so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all possible bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious, and utterly intolerable in a civilized community.”  (Id.)  The determination of “outrageousness” is generally an issue of fact to be determined by the trier of fact, however, “the court may determine in the first instance, whether the defendant's conduct may reasonably be regarded as so extreme and outrageous as to permit recovery.” (Trerice v. Blue Cross of California (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 878, 883.)

Defendant argues that accompanying his brother to the auto body shop is not outrageous conduct. (UMF No. 2.) Defendant also argues he had no intent to harm Plaintiffs.

The Court incorporates its discussion above as to Defendant’s intent. As to whether Defendant’s conduct was outrageous, the evidence presented by Plaintiffs shows that Robert accompanied Akop to the body shop armed with weapons, parked the car blocking the driveway in a getaway position, and approached the auto body shop yelling out for Plaintiffs, with Robert punching Fitilchyan and Akop then shooting him. “Summary judgment may not be granted unless reasonable minds can draw only one conclusion from the evidence.” (Trerice, supra (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d at 885.) However, when reasonable persons may differ, it is for the jury, subject to the control of the Court, to determine whether, in the particular case, the conduct has been sufficiently extreme and outrageous to result in liability.¿ (Alcorn v. Anbro Engineering, Inc. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 493, 499.) The Court finds it is for the jury to determine whether the conduct was sufficiently extreme and outrageous.

As such, Defendant is not entitled to summary adjudication as to this cause of action.

          vi. Punitive Damages

Defendant argues there is no clear and convincing evidence that he engaged in any act of malice, fraud, or oppression against plaintiffs.

Civil Code § 3294 provides, in pertinent part, as follows: 

 

(a)¿In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant. 

*** 

“Malice” is defined in section 3294(c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”

The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (See, e.g., Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.)  To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.)

While “the ‘clear and convincing’ evidentiary standard is a stringent one, it does not impose on a plaintiff the obligation to ‘prove’ a case for punitive damages at summary judgment.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1053.) However, where the plaintiff's ultimate burden of proof will be by clear and convincing evidence, the higher standard of proof must be taken into account in ruling on a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, since if a plaintiff is to prevail on a claim for punitive damages, it will be necessary that the evidence presented meet the higher evidentiary standard. (Basich v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1112, 1118–1120, 105 Cal.Rptr.2d 153.) Further, summary judgment “on the issue of punitive damages is proper” only “when no reasonable jury could find the plaintiff’s evidence to be clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression.” (Id.) 

          As there are triable issues of material fact as to the cause of action for IIED, there are also triable issues of fact as to whether Defendant’s conduct was malicious to support a claim for punitive damages.

          In the reply, Defendants argue that the wives are not entitled to punitive damages. However, the complaint makes clear that only Plaintiffs Gasparyan and Fitilchyan are seeking punitive damages against Defendants. (Compl., ¶ 40.)

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          The motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication is DENIED.
