DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation.

Ruling:                                    The Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED. 

 

Defendant to give notice. 

 

 

BACKGROUND 

On September 28, 2020, Plaintiff Roland Esparza (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants The Vons Companies, Inc. and Does 1 through 50, inclusive. The Complaint alleges five causes of action: (1) strict product liability – manufacturing defect; (2) strict product liability – design defect; (3) strict product liability – failure to warn; (4) negligence; and (5) breach of implied warranties.

On September 25, 2023, Plaintiff amended his Second Amended Complaint to name Defendant Elan Chemical Company, Inc. (“Defendant”) as Doe 34. Plaintiff alleges Defendant was a manufacturer or distributor of one or more of the parts of the product giving rise to Plaintiff’s alleged injury.

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff and Defendant reached a settlement. (Declaration of Tracy M. Lewis, ¶ 4.) Defendant now moves for Determination of Good Faith Settlement.

 

DISCUSSION 

Applicable Law 

“Any party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors . . . shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more of the alleged tortfeasors.” (Code Civ. Proc., §877.6, subd. (a)(1).) “The determination as to whether a settlement is in good faith is a matter left to the discretion of the trial court.” (Mattco Forge, Inc. v. Arthur Young & Co. (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1337, 1349.) The trial court’s determination of good faith is based on “a number of factors,” including total recovery and proportionate liability, “the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial.” ¿(Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates¿(1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499 (Tech-Bilt); see also Far West Financial Corp. v. D&S Co.¿(1988) 46 Cal.3d 796, 816, fn. 16 [expanding on Tech-Bilt factors].)¿Nevertheless, “when no one objects, the barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient.” (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261 (Grand Terrace).) 

Application to Facts 

Defendant moves for determination of good faith settlement based on a settlement with Plaintiff for a confidential amount. In exchange for payment, Plaintiff released Defendant from all present and prospective claims arising between them as a result of Plaintiff’s alleged injuries. (Declaration of Tracy M. Lewis, ¶ 6.) The settlement also included a provision that it was of paramount importance to both parties to keep the settlement amount confidential, except as to the parties involved. (Declaration of Tracy M. Lewis, ¶ 7.)

Defendant argues that it would have very low proportionate liability, if any at all. Defendant contends it would have avoided liability via demurrer or later summary judgment. (Motion for Determination, pp. 3–4.) Defendant also argues it is only one of over thirty-five named defendants in this action and it may not have even had a single component part used in the finished product at issue, making its potential liability a very small percentage. (Motion for Determination, p. 4.) Defendant also argues that the confidential settlement amount is “within the ballpark” of its potential liability. (Declaration of Tracy M. Lewis, ¶ 9.)

Here, the court has received no objection to the proposed settlement between Plaintiffs and Defendants. Defendant has also circulated a stipulation as to the good faith nature of the settlement and lack of opposition by co-Defendants. (Declaration of Tracy M. Lewis, ¶ 12, Exhibit C.)  Thus, the court has discretion to decide on the motion based on Defendants’ setting forth of grounds of good faith with a brief background declaration. (See Grant Terrace, supra, 192 Cal.App.3d at p. 1261.) The court also notes the lack of opposition because there is no argument or evidence from any of the parties here that this settlement was made in bad faith, and the party asserting bad faith bears the burden of proof. (Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at pp. 499–500.) Additionally, there is no indication that this settlement was the result of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed at the non-settling Defendants. The parties negotiated at arms-length and were each represented by experienced counsel. (Declaration of Tracy M. Lewis, ¶ 5.)

The court finds that due to the lack of objections and evidence of bad faith, as well as Defendant’s very low potential liability, the settlement was fairly agreed upon and made in good faith.

CONCLUSION 

The Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

Defendant to give notice.

BACKGROUND

On May 18, 2023, Madison-MF McCadden CA LLC (Plaintiff) filed an unlawful detainer complaint against Victor Vicente (Defendant), seeking repayment of past due rent pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1161(2). The complaint alleges that Defendant failed to pay Plaintiff rent in the amount of $3,840.00/month from April 1, 2022 and March 1, 2023 ($46,080.00 total). Plaintiff past-due rent, reasonable attorney fees, and forfeiture of the rental agreement.

On June 6, 2023, Defendant demurred claiming Plaintiff’s complaint is improperly unverified. The court overruled Defendant’s demurrer. (07/19/2023 - Minute Order.)

On July 24, 2023, Defendant filed an answer denying all factual allegations and asserting fifteen (15) affirmative defenses including retaliatory eviction and breach of the implied warranty of habitability.

On July 29, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment. On August 23, 2024, the court took the motion off the calendar because Plaintiff filed a Notice of Stay of Proceedings for Bankruptcy on August 22, 2024. (08/23/2024 - Minute Order.)

On August 23, 2024, the court ordered a stay in this case because Defendant filed for Bankruptcy. (08/23/2024 - Minute Order.) Defendant’s Bankruptcy case was dismissed on September 9, 2024. (Motion for Summary Judgment, Exh. 3, at p. 4.)

On September 19, 2024, Defendant filed this motion for summary judgment.

As of October 8, 2024, no opposition has been filed.


DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (c).) A party may also bring a noticed motion for summary adjudication that one or more causes of action has no merit. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

The moving party has the initial burden to show either (1) one or more elements of the cause of actions cannot be established, or (2) a complete affirmative defense to the cause of action exists. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subds. (o), (p)(2).) The moving party may demonstrate that an element of a cause of action cannot be established by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.)

Once the moving party meets its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Id. at p. 849.) The opposing party may not rely upon the mere allegations or denials of the pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, must “set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.; Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) A triable issue of material fact exists if the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the opposing party in accordance with the applicable standard of proof. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 850.)

In analyzing motions for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he pleadings determine the scope of relevant issues on a summary judgment motion.” (Nieto v. Blue Shield of California Life & Health Ins. Co.¿(2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 60, 74.) The court considers all the evidence and all of the inferences reasonably drawn therefrom and must view such evidence in the light most favorable to the opposing party. (Id. at 843; All of US or None - Riverside Chapter v. Hamrick¿(2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 751, 783.)

 Request for Judicial Notice

None.

Evidentiary Objections

None.

Application to Facts

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on the sole cause of action for unlawful detainer. In support, Plaintiff attaches the Declaration of Mark Jaime, Community Manager and agent for Plaintiff, the Written Lease Agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant, the Three-Day Notice of Default, and Notice of Bankruptcy Dismissal. (Jaime Decl., Exhs. 1,2, 3.)


Cause of Action: Unlawful Detainer for Nonpayment of Rent

A tenant is guilty of lawful detainer for nonpayment of rent when: “(1) the tenant is in possession of the premises; (2) that possession is without permission; (3) the tenant is in default for nonpayment of rent; (4) the tenant has been properly served with a written three-day notice; and (5) the default continues after the three-day notice period has elapsed.” (KB Salt Lake III, LLC v. Fitness Internat., LLC (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 1032, 1045; Code Civ. Proc. § 1161, subd. (2).) A section 1161 unlawful detainer action is “subject to the COVID-19 Tenant Relief Act of 2020 (Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 1179.01)) if the neglect or failure to perform other conditions or covenants of the lease or agreement is based upon the COVID-19 rental debt.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1161, subd. (2).)

First, Plaintiff attests that it owns the residential real property located at 1714 N. McCadden Place, #1417, Hollywood, CA 90028 (the Premises). (Compl., at p. 1; Undisputed Material Facts [UMFs] No. 1; Declaration of Mark Jaime [Jaime Decl.], ¶ 2.) Plaintiff attests that Defendant occupies the Premises pursuant to a written rental agreement (the Lease). (Compl., at p. 2; UMFs No. 2; Jaime Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1, at p. 1.) Under the Lease, rent is due in the amount of $3,840.00 on the first of each month. (Compl., at p. 2; UMGs No. 3; Jaime Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 1, at p. 1.) Plaintiff attests that Defendant did not pay monthly rent from April 1, 2022 - March 31, 2023. (UMFs No. 4; Jaime Decl., ¶ 5.)

Next, Plaintiff attests that it served Defendant with the Three-Day Notice (the Notice) on March 21, 2023, demanding past due rent for the period of April 1, 2022 to March 31, 2023. (Compl, at p. 3; UMFs No. 6; Jaime Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff states that because it found no person of suitable age and discretion at the Premises, Plaintiff mailed a copy of the Notice in United States Mail in a sealed envelope with postage fully prepaid and addressed to Defendant at the Premises and posted the Notice in a conspicuous place on the Premises. (Compl., at p. 3; UMFs No. 5; Jaime Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 2, at p. 1.) Plaintiff does not know Defendant’s current place of employment. (Compl., at p. 3; UMFs No. 5; Jaime Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 2, at p. 1.) Plaintiff attests that Defendant has not remedied the default or vacated the Premises as of the filing of the instant motion. (UMFs Nos. 7, 12; Jaime Decl., ¶¶ 8, 12.)

Finally, Plaintiff states it has received no rental or other financial compensation or assistance for the amount demanded in the Notice or for rent which accrued after the Notice date. (UMFs Nos. 8-9; Jaime Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.) Plaintiff also contends that it has no pending applications for rental assistance or other financial compensation for the amount demanded in the Notice or for rent which accrued after the Notice date. (UMFs Nos. 10-11; Jaime Decl., ¶¶ 11-12.)

The court finds that Plaintiff has met its initial burden demonstrating that no triable issue of material fact exists as to Defendant’s unlawful detainer of the Premises. The burden now shifts to Defendant to show a triable issue of material fact exists.

Defendant did not file an opposition. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c, subd. (b)(2) [“Any opposition to the motion shall be served and filed not less than 14 days preceding the noticed or continued date of hearing, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.”].)

Accordingly, the court finds that no triable issue of material fact exists and that Plaintiff is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

 

Plaintiff has established all elements of unlawful detainer

Defendant currently remains in possession of the Premises, without Plaintiff’s permission. Defendant has not paid rent since before April 1, 2022. Plaintiff served Defendant at the Premises by posting the Notice conspicuously and mailing the Notice to Defendant at the Premises. Finally, Defendant continues to default on rent payments.

Accordingly, Defendant has met its burden of establishing unlawful detainer for nonpayment of rent.

The court now address Defendant’s defenses to Plaintiff’s cause of action.

 

Defendant’s defenses fail because Defendant did not allege sufficient facts or provide evidence to support its allegations. 

Defendant’s answer asserted several defenses including retaliatory eviction and breach of the implied warranty of habitability. (Answer, ¶¶ 3-17.)

 

1.     Eleventh Affirmative Defense: Retaliatory Eviction

“[A] landlord may not engage in specified conduct against a tenant who is not in default on rent, including ‘bring[ing] an action to recover possession,’ because of a tenant’s lawful and peaceable exercise of any rights under the law or because of a tenant’s complaints regarding habitability. However, a landlord retains the right to bring an action to recover possession ‘for any lawful cause.’ To recover possession for a good faith, nonretaliatory reason, the landlord must give the tenant notice of such grounds and, if controverted, the landlord must prove the truth of the reason stated.” (Coyne v. De Leo (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 801, 805, quoting Code Civ. Proc. § 1942.5, subds. (a), (d), (f), (g).) The tenant has the burden to prove a retaliatory motive by “a preponderance of the evidence.” (Western Land Office, Inc. v. Cervantes (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 724, 744.)

Defendant’s answer asserts that Plaintiff filed this action because Defendant exercised tenant rights such as engaging in tenant organizing activities. (Answer, ¶ 13.) Defendant provides no further factual allegations or evidence specifying what these activities were. Plaintiff argues it brought this action to seek past-due rent payments, not to retaliate against Defendant. (Motion for Summary Judgment, pg. 9:1-4.)

The court finds that Defendant has not supported its retaliatory eviction defense because Defendant fails to allege sufficient factual allegations and because Defendant is currently in default.

Accordingly, Defendant’s retaliatory eviction defense fails.

 

2.     First and Twelfth Affirmative Defenses: Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

A breach of the implied warranty of habitability “may be raised as a defense in an unlawful detainer action.” (Green v. Superior Court (1974) 10 Cal.3d 616, 619.) ¿“The elements of a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability ‘are the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises’ habitability, notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant’s discovery of the condition, [and that] the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency, and resulting damages.’” (Peviani v. Arbors at California Oaks Property Owner, LLC (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 874, 891, quoting Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1297.)

Defendant contends that Plaintiff breached the implied warranty of habitability, alleging: “mold contamination in the master bedroom, a persistent cockroach infestation, a malfunctioning water heater, soiled or contaminated plumbing connected to the kitchen sink, a defective air conditioning unit, and an inoperable dish washing machine.” (Answer, ¶ 3.) Plaintiff asserts that Defendant has not met “notice” burden because Plaintiff did not allege facts stating Defendant notified Plaintiff of need for repairs before withholding rent. (Motion for Summary Judgment, pg. 10:12-17.)

The court finds that Defendant has failed to establish the notice requirement by not alleging sufficient factual allegations or evidence to counter Plaintiff’s claims. Because Defendant cannot establish the notice element, the court does not go into an in-depth analysis of the other elements.

Accordingly, Defendant’s implied warranty of habitability defense fails.

 

The court concludes that because Defendant fails to show a triable issue of material fact with respect to Plaintiff’s cause of action, summary judgment is granted.

           

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

On October 17, 2023, Plaintiffs Ireli Astorga Nevarez; Francisca Nevarez Sicairos; Juventino Garcia Gallegos; Ireli Garcia, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Ireli Astorga Nevarez; Sandra Garcia, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Ireli Astorga Nevarez; Sofia Garcia Astorga, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Ireli Astorga Nevarez; and Octavio Garcia Astorga, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem (collectively Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Defendants J.K. Residential Services, Inc.; 7520 S. Broadway, LLC; J.K. Properties, Inc.; Haresh Jogani; and Does 1 through 100. The complaint alleges that Plaintiffs suffered uninhabitable substandard housing conditions in the building and unit they rented from Defendants. The alleged conditions include structural deficiencies, defective plumbing, flooring and weather proofing, and cockroach and rat infestations.

On June 18, 2024, Plaintiffs propounded on Defendant Jogani discovery requests including Form Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents and Special Interrogatories.

On July 2, 2024, Defendant Jogani filed a Motion to Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction, or in the Alternative, for Stay Based on Forum Non-Conveniens.

On July 5, 2024, Defendant Jogani filed an answer denying all factual allegations and asserting thirty-seven (37) affirmative defenses.

On July 10, 2024, Defendant Jogani filed an ex parte application requesting court stay discovery until September 11, 2024. On July 12, 2024, the court granted Defendant Jogani’s request.

On July 17, 2024, the court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to continue the hearing on Defendant Jogani’s motion to dismiss and ordered that Defendant Jogani be deposed within 30 days of the order to determine whether the court has jurisdiction over him.

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiffs served 7/17/2024 - Minute Order on Defendant Jogani.

On July 30, 2024, Defendant Jogani filed the instant motion for reconsideration.

On August 5, 2024, Defendants Jogani and J.K. Properties, Inc. filed motions seeking protective orders preventing Defendants from responding to Plaintiffs’ discovery requests.

On August 27, 2024, the court granted Defendant Jogani’s ex parte application to stay his jurisdiction deposition until October 21, 2024.

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition to Defendant Jogani’s motion for reconsideration.

On October 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition to Defendant Jogani’s motions for protective orders.

On October 3, 2024, Defendant Jogani filed replies to Plaintiffs’ oppositions.

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            Legal Standard

A motion for reconsideration asks the court to modify, amend, or revoke its earlier order on a prior motion to the court because of new or different facts, circumstances, or law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).) A motion for reconsideration may be brought by a party or by the court on its own motion. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subds. (a), (c).) 

A party may move for reconsideration based on: (1) new or different facts, (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); see e.g., In re Marriage of LaMoure (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1473 [reconsideration motion granted on new evidence]); (2) new or different circumstances, (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a)); and (3) new or different law, (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a); Baldwin v. Home Sav. of Am. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1196.)

The moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide evidence or different facts earlier. (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.) This requirement extends to motions brought on the basis of “different” law. (Baldwin v. Home Savings of Am. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1200.) The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at trial. (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213.)

A party cannot move for reconsideration based on the court’s erroneous order, i.e., the court’s misinterpretation of facts or law. (See Jones v. P.S. Dev. Co. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 707, 724.) Instead, a party can suggest to the court that it should reconsider its erroneous order on its own motion so long as the suggestion is not made ex parte. (Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1108; In re Marriage of Spector (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 201, 214-15 [emails husband sent to court about erroneous order were not ex parte because wife was copied on all emails].) 

By contrast, the court has statutory authority to reconsider final orders and interim orders based on a change in law. (Civ. Proc., § 1008, subds. (c), (e).) California courts also have a broader constitutional authority to reconsider interim orders that are erroneous that goes beyond Code of Civil Procedure section 1008. (Brown, Winfield & Canzoneri, Inc. v. Superior Court (2010) 47 Cal.4th 1233, 1248 [courts have constitutional authority to reconsider interim orders]; Le Francois, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 1108 [“If a court believes one of its prior interim orders was erroneous, it should be able to correct that error no matter how it came to acquire that belief”].) 

Timeliness 

When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and the court has granted or granted conditionally, on terms, the motion, “any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a) (emphasis added).) 

Here, the court finds that Defendant Jogani filed this motion within by the deadline. The court granted Plaintiffs’ ex parte application on July 17, 2024. (07/17/2024 - Minute Order.) Plaintiffs electronically served notice of the order on Defendant Jogani on July 18, 2024. (07/18/2024 - Notice of Court Ruling.) Defendant Jogani filed his motion to reconsider on July 30, 2024. (Motion to Reconsider, at p. 1; Code Civ. Proc. § 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(B) [noting when motions or papers are served by via electronic mail, the required court-days’ notice is extended by two court days].)

Accordingly, the Defendant Jogani’s motion is timely. 

 

Application to Facts

            Defendant Jogani argues he would have presented the new facts forming the basis of his motion to the court if he had a meaningful opportunity to oppose Plaintiffs’ original ex parte application. Defendant Jogani also contends that if the court were aware of these facts, the court would have decided differently on the ex parte application.

Defendant Jogani alleges the following new facts:

1.     Defendant Haresh is an Apex Deponent under the Apex Doctrine and that Plaintiffs’ fail to meet the requirements for Defendant Jogani’s deposition. Although Defendant Jogani is a director of J.K. Properties, Inc., he is not involved in the daily operation of the company; he does not possess everyday knowledge of what occurs at the property in question; he did not witness any of the alleged habitability issues; and he has no direct, personal or factual knowledge pertaining to any material issues in the case. (Declaration of Ritesh Desai [Desai Decl.], ¶ 2.)

 

2.     The court’s order failed to place limits on the ordered deposition and to set criteria for what would be asked. (Declaration of Armand R. Vosguanian [Vosguanian Decl.], ¶ 4.)

 

3.     Less intrusive discovery means exist to obtain the information Plaintiffs seek such as interrogatories. (Vosguanian Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

4.     Defendant Jogani is neither a citizen nor a resident of the United States and he has no personal assets or residence in California. (Desai Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

5.     Defendant Jogani is a citizen of India, is currently in India, and does not intend to be the U.S. in the next ninety (90) days (from July 30, 2024). (Desai Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

 

Defendant Jogani’s “new facts” regarding citizenship and personal assets are not new facts within the meaning of Section 1008 because Defendant Jogani already had knowledge of these facts and provided no explanation for not revealing them in prior motions.

 

If the “new” matters raised by the moving party’s motion consists of information the moving party always had within his or her possession, such facts do not “constitute new or different facts within the meaning of section 1008.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 213-214.)

Here, Defendant Jogani’s citizenship status and current physical location are not new facts because Defendant Jogani was aware of these facts at the outset of this litigation. Defendant Jogani’s explanation for not presenting this information sooner is that he did not have time to brief the facts and law on Plaintiffs’ ex parte motion. However, Defendant Jogani touched upon these facts in his motion to dismiss for personal jurisdiction. (See Defendant Haresh Jogani’s Motion to Dismiss filed 7/02/2024.) The motion to dismiss explicitly discusses Defendant Jogani’s citizenship, his current physical location, and challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction over him. Thus, Defendant Jogani had knowledge of his Indian citizenship and whether he has assets in California well before the court granted Plaintiffs’ ex parte motion.

Accordingly, the court does not consider these facts for the purpose of this motion.

The apex deponent doctrine does not entitle Defendant Jogani to a reconsideration of the court’s July 17, 2024 grant of Plaintiffs’ ex parte application for a continuance because the doctrine is irrelevant for the purposes of this motion.

 

Under the apex deponent doctrine, the court may grant a protective order prohibiting the deposition of a corporate officer if the moving party shows the officer lacks “personal knowledge of discoverable information” and that the opposing party failed to seek discoverable information through “less intrusive [discovery] methods.” (Liberty Mutual Ins. Co. v. Superior Court, (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1289.) Less intrusive discovery methods include: “interrogatories directed to the high-level official to explore the state of his or her knowledge or involvement in plaintiff’s case; the deposition of lower level employees with appropriate knowledge and involvement in the subject matter of the litigation; and the organizational deposition of the corporation itself, which will require the corporation to produce for deposition the most qualified officer or employee to testify on its behalf as to the specified matters to be raised at the deposition.” (Ibid.) If the opposing party exhausts these methods and makes a “good cause” showing that the “high-level official possesses necessary information to the case, the trial court may then lift the protective order and allow the deposition to proceed.” (Ibid.) 

 However, Liberty Mutual does not stand for the proposition that apex depositions cannot be taken without first exploring less intrusive methods. Rather, Liberty Mutual held that a protective order can be warranted in such circumstances. However, a protective order can only be entered by noticed motion or by stipulation of the parties. (See e.g., St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. v. Superior Court¿(1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 82, 85-86.) It is insufficient to merely object or oppose a motion to compel a deposition on such grounds. (Ibid.)

Here, Defendant Jogani argues that under a different law, the apex deponent doctrine, the court cannot compel him to be deposed. Defendant Jogani presents the declaration of co-defendant Ritesh Desai to support a showing that Defendant Jogani is an apex deponent. Ritesh states that: Although Defendant Jogani is a director of J.K. Properties, Inc., he is not involved in the daily operation of the company; he does not possess everyday knowledge of what occurs at the property in question; he did not witness any of the alleged habitability issues; and he has no direct, personal or factual knowledge pertaining to any material issues in the case. (Desai Decl., ¶ 2.) Defendant Jogani also argues that Plaintiffs have not attempted less intrusive discovery methods before seeking a deposition. Additionally, Defendant Jogani asserts that because the court did not specify criteria or limits on the deposition, the deposition runs the risk of going beyond the issue of jurisdiction and running afoul of the apex deponent doctrine.

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant Jogani’s use of the apex doctrine is misplaced because Plaintiffs personally name Defendant Jogani in this suit. Plaintiffs also note that Defendant Jogani has refused to respond to Plaintiffs’ discovery requests (interrogatories and requests for production), propounded on June 18, 2024. Plaintiffs do not trust Defendant Jogani to comply with other discovery measures because he has yet to reply to the original requests even with a court-ordered extension to reply to Plaintiffs’ initial discovery requests by September 11, 2024. Finally, Plaintiffs assert that the criteria for the court-ordered deposition is clear because Plaintiffs’ ex parte application specifically requested a continuance to “have a fair opportunity to conduct discovery to investigate Mr. Jogani’s assertion that this Court lacks personal jurisdiction over him because he does not have sufficient minimum contacts with the State of California and County of Los Angeles.” (Plaintiffs’ Ex Parte Application for Continue filed 7/15/2024, pg. 2:9-12.)

Although the court acknowledges that Defendant Jogani has presented a different law, the court finds that this law is not relevant to the instant motion. The apex deponent doctrine’s purpose is to prevent deposing of high-level employees lacking knowledge of an entity’s day-to-day operations. Here, the court’s order[1] allows Plaintiffs to exercise their right to conduct discovery regarding “the issue of jurisdiction to develop the facts necessary to sustain [their] burden.” (Mihlon v. Superior Ct. (1985), 169 Cal.App.3d 703, 710.) It matters not that Defendant Jogani is a director of co-defendant J.K. Properties. Defendant Jogani is uniquely positioned with first-hand knowledge to answer questions about his personal connections with Los Angeles County and the Secretary of State of California.[2] Defendant Jogani attempts to mischaracterize and broaden the scope of the court’s July 17, 2024 Minute Order to force the court to apply the apex doctrine. The court did not grant Plaintiffs permission to go on a fishing expedition, and Defendant Jogani cannot avoid this deposition by providing only those facts which he deems are pertinent. While parties must not abuse the discovery process, the discovery the court ordered is expressly limited to a jurisdictional focus.

Accordingly, the court finds that the apex deponent doctrine, though “different” law for the purposes of this motion, does not apply and the scope of Defendant Jogani’s deposition is limited to questions relating to jurisdiction. Therefore, Defendant Jogani is not entitled to reconsideration of the court’s July 17, 2024 order granting Plaintiffs’ ex parte application to continue Defendant Jogani’s motion to dismiss.

           

CONCLUSION

            The motion to reconsider the court’s 7/17/2024 - Minute Order is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On August 16, 2024, Petitioner Blue Hill Specialty Insurance Company (“Blue Hill” or “Petitioner”) filed a Petition to Open a Superior Court File for the Purpose of Establishing Superior Court Jurisdiction over Underinsured Motorist Arbitration pursuant to Insurance Code Section 11580.2, subdivision (f).

            On August 27, 2024, the Court set a hearing on the Petition for today’s date, October 15, 2024.

            On September 11, 2024, Petitioner provided Notice of the Hearing to Respondent Jose Benitez, Jr. (“Respondent”).

            On October 2, 2024,  Petitioner filed the instant Brief re Legal Authority for Establishing Superior Court Jurisdiction over Petition. The Petition and Brief are unopposed.

 

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            Pursuant the California Insurance Code Section 11580.2, subdivision (f): “The policy or an endorsement added thereto shall provide that the determination as to whether the insured shall be legally entitled to recover damages, and if so entitled, the amount thereof, shall be made by agreement between the insured and the insurer or, in the event of disagreement, by arbitration…Title 4 (commencing with Section 2016.010) of Part 4 of the Code of Civil Procedure shall be applicable to these determinations, and all rights, remedies, obligations, liabilities and procedures set forth in Title 4 (commencing with Section 2016.010) of Part 4 of the Code of Civil Procedure shall be available to both the insured and the insurer at any time after the accident, both before and after the commencement of arbitration, if any, with the following limitations: [¶](1) Whenever in Title 4 (commencing with Section 2016.010) of Part 4 of the Code of Civil Procedure, reference is made to the court in which the action is pending, or provision is made for application to the court or obtaining leave of court or approval by the court, the court that shall have jurisdiction for the purposes of this section shall be the superior court of the State of California, in and for any county that is a proper county for the filing of a suit for bodily injury arising out of the accident, against the uninsured motorist, or any county specified in the policy or an endorsement added thereto as a proper county for arbitration or action thereon.” (Cal. Ins. Code, § 11580.2, subd. (f)(1) (emphasis added).)

 

            “[T]he contractual arbitration statute, giving an arbitrator the statutory power to exercise remedies under the Civil Discovery Act, must be reconciled, if possible, with the uninsured motorist law, which not only makes the Civil Discovery Act applicable, but expressly requires that whenever the Civil Discovery Act makes reference to a court, the court that has jurisdiction shall be the superior court. We may harmonize these conflicting provisions by reading the discovery provisions governing uninsured motorist arbitrations as an exception to the discovery provisions governing contractual arbitrations generally.” (Miranda v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 913, 924; see also Briggs v. Resolution Remedies (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1401, fn. 6, stating “We recognize that Insurance Code section 11580.2, subdivision (f) commits discovery disputes in uninsured motorist arbitrations to the superior court.”)

 

Application to Facts

            Petitioner Blue Hill Specialty Insurance Company (hereinafter “Petitioner”) moves for an order establishing jurisdiction and opening a Superior Court file, secure a case number, and conduct discovery in the underinsured motorist arbitration between Petitioner and Respondent Jose Benitez, Jr. (“Respondent”).

 

            Here, Petitioner contends it is involved in a pending arbitration proceeding with Respondent  pursuant to the underinsured motorist provisions of Respondent’s insurance policy with Petitioner. Although the coverage at issue is an underinsured motorist, “uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage shall be offered as a single coverage” under California Insurance Code Section 11580.2, subdivision (n). Furthermore, the case and statutory law supports the Superior Court having jurisdiction over discovery disputes in underinsured motorist arbitration. Lastly, Respondent had notice of the Petition through his purported counsel and has not opposed the present Petition.

           

 

           

CONCLUSION

            The Petition to Open a Superior Court File for the Purpose of Establishing Superior Court Jurisdiction over Underinsured Motorist Arbitration is GRANTED.
