Case Number: 21AVCV00032    Hearing Date: April 23, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Maricela Aguilar (“Plaintiff”) alleges that on or about August 31, 2019, she purchased a 2019 Chevrolet Colorado bearing the VIN number 1GCPTEE11K1319151 (the “Subject Vehicle”) from Antelope Valley Chevrolet, one of Defendant General Motors, LLC (“Defendant”)’s authorized dealerships, for a total contract price of $56,343.00. Plaintiff further alleges that (1) Defendant gave an express bumper-to-bumper limited warranty covering 3 years or 36,000 miles whichever occurred first and an express limited warranty covering certain powertrain components for a period of 5 years or 100,000 miles whichever occurred first and, during the warranty period, Defendant agreed it would, at no cost to plaintiff, repair or replace all covered parts on plaintiffs vehicle that malfunctioned or failed under normal use during the applicable warranty period as a result of defective materials or workmanship; (2) on or about August 24, 2020, at or near 20,935 miles on the odometer, Plaintiff returned the Subject Vehicle to one of Defendant’s authorized repair facility for warranty repairs due to defects, nonconformities, and/or malfunctions consisting of a persistent lack of power and general sluggishness as well as lubricant leaking from the front axle area; (3) in or about November 2020, the braking system of the Subject Vehicle malfunctioned; (4) while Defendant repaired the defects related to lack of power and lubricant leaking from the axle, Defendant would not diagnose, inspect, or assess the Subject Vehicle for issues related to the braking system unless Plaintiff paid a fee in violation of the warranties provided with the Subject Vehicle; and (5) Plaintiff called Defendant’s customer service center after Defendant’s refusal to repair the breaks and requested that Defendant repurchase the vehicle and Defendant refused to do so violating the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

 

On January 13, 2021, Plaintiff filed her Complaint for Damages for Breach of Express and Implied Warranties, alleging three counts of breach: (1) Breach of Express Warranty – Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act; (2) Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability – Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act; and (3) Breach of Warranty to Repair Defects – Uniform Commercial Code (“UCC”).

 

On February 24, 2021, Defendant filed its Answer to the Complaint.

 

On April 12, 2021, Bank of the West (“BOTW”) filed a Complaint in case number 21AVCV00276. against Plaintiff alleging four causes of action related to non-payment of the Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) for the Subject Vehicle: (1) Breach of RISC; (2) Money Lent; (3) Recovery of Personal Property (Claim and Deliver); and (4) Conversion.

 

On June 30, 2021, Plaintiff filed three documents: (1) her Answer to Bank of the West’s Complaint, (2) a Cross-Complaint in 21AVCV00276, and (2) a Notice of Related case in this action as to 21AVCV00276.

 

On July 02, 2021, the two cases were deemed related.

 

On August 13, 201, BMO filed its First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) in 21AVCV00276.

 

On September 13, 2021, BMO filed a Third-Party Cross-Complaint as to Defendant and Antelope Valley Chevrolet, Inc. (“AV Chevrolet”).

 

On September 17, 2021, BMO filed its Answer to Plaintiff’s Cross-Complaint.

 

On October 27, 2021, AV Chevrolet filed two documents: (1) its Answer to BMO’s Cross-Complaint in 21AVCV00276, and (2) a Cross-Complaint in 21AVCV00276 as to Defendant.

 

On November 04, 2021, Defendant filed its Answer to BMO’s Cross-Complaint in 21AVCV00276.

 

On November 29, 2021, AV Chevrolet filed a Second Answer to BMO’s Cross-Complaint. It appears the difference is in whether BMO’s Cross-Complaint was verified.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Attorney’s Fees – A prevailing party in entitled to recover costs, including attorneys’ fees, as a matter of right. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1032(a)(4), 1032(b), 1033.5.)¿ 

¿ 

The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. A computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award. The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)¿ Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary. (Id. at 48, n.23.) After the trial court has performed the lodestar calculations, it shall consider whether the total award so calculated under all of the circumstances of the case is more than a reasonable amount and, if so, shall reduce the section 1717 award so that it is a reasonable figure.¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-96.)¿ 

¿ 

The factors considered in determining the modification of the lodestar include the nature and difficulty of the litigation, the amount of money involved, the skill required and employed to handle the case, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case. (EnPalm, LLC v. Teitler Family Trust (2008) 162 Cal. App. 4th 770, 774 (emphasis in original).) A negative modifier was appropriate when duplicative work had been performed. (Thayer v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 819.)¿ 

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – As an initial matter, Defendant presents that this motion is untimely as Cal. Rules of Court 3.1702 provides 60 days after notice of entry of judgment, or 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first. Defendant cites to DeSaulles v. Community Hospital of Monterey Peninsula (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1140, 1155 (“DeSaulles”) for support that a settlement serves as a judgment.

 

In Reply, Plaintiff presents that Defendant’s reliance on Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1702 is misplaced because the resolution of this case stemmed from a settlement, not a judgment and Defendant’s reliance on DeSaulles is misplaced as Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1032 allows a prevailing party to recover costs and the question answered by the DeSaulles court was whether a plaintiff who voluntarily dismisses an action after entering into a monetary settlement is a prevailing party under section 1032 and no dismissal has been filed in this case.

 

Here, Plaintiff accepted Defendant’s Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 998 offer which reads in pertinent part:

 

GM will also pay Plaintiff's attorney fees, expenses. and costs in the amount of $10,000.

 

Alternatively, at Plaintiff's election, Plaintiff's attorney's fees, expenses and costs that have been reasonably incurred pursuant to California Civil Code § 1794(d) may be determined by the Court via noticed motion. For purposes of any such motion, GM will agree that Plaintiff is the prevailing Party.

 

(Decl. James S. Kostas in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys Fees, Exh. A.)

 

Plaintiff, under her elected alternative, is the prevailing party.

 

A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk under Code of Civil Procedure 664.5 or the date of service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal, or within 180 days after entry of judgment, whichever is first.

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(a)(1); see Hydratec, Inc. v. Sun Valley 260 Orchard & Vineyard Co. (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 924, 929 [failure to comply with time limits for memorandum of costs constitutes waiver of claim of costs]; Douglas v. Willis (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 287, 290 [“The time limit is mandatory and failure to timely file and serve a cost bill may result in waiver of costs. [Citation.].”].)

 

Here, the Court record shows that no memorandum of costs has been filed with the Court. While Plaintiff attached a Memorandum of Costs to her papers in support of this motion, the copy attached is not file-stamped. (See Decl. James S. Kostas in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys Fees, Exh. B.) This document will be addressed by the Court as the Attached Memorandum of Costs. The Court notes that Plaintiff has used Judicial Council of California Form MC-010 for the Attached Memorandum of Costs, approved for optional use, but has not provided the full document (i.e., no page 2 out of 2, substituted by counsel for a different Proof of Service). (See Decl. James S. Kostas in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys Fees, Exh. B.) The Court further notes that the Attached Memorandum of Costs does not have any documentation providing a basis for the requested costs; however, "[d]ocumentation must be submitted only when a party dissatisfied with the costs claimed in the memorandum challenges them by filing a motion to tax costs” and the current context is such that a motion to tax costs could not be filed as a Memorandum of Costs was not filed with the Court.

 

There is no evidence in the Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 998 offer that the requirement for a Memorandum of Costs had been waived by the parties. (Decl. James S. Kostas in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys Fees, Exh. A.)

 

The Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 998 offer reads, as related to dismissal:

 

Plaintiff will file a Request for Dismissal of the entire action, with prejudice, within five business days after receiving all payments from GM due Plaintiff and his counsel.

 

(Decl. James S. Kostas in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys Fees, Exh. A.)

 

At this time, no Request for Dismissal has been entered. Thus, pursuant to Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(a)(1), no written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal or entry of judgment has occurred.

 

Thus, the Court must deny the request for costs and anticipated costs without prejudice.

 

However, the court encourages the parties to meet and confer regarding the allowable costs and come to resolution of this issue to avoid incurring additional attorney's fees to litigate this relatively minor matter. This is particularly true where all said attorney's fees may be imposed on only one party.

 

-----

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Maricela Aguilar’s Motion for Award of Attorney Fees, Litigation Costs, and Other Expenses is DENIED without prejudice.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Summary Judgment – The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co.¿(2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Cal. Code Civ. Proc.¿§437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to¿any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”¿ (Juge¿v. County of Sacramento¿(1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67 (“Juge”), citing¿FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima¿(1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Cal.¿Code Civ.¿Proc.¿§437c(p)(2);¿Scalf¿v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc.¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v.¿Paetkau¿(1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Evidentiary Objections – The Court does not rule on the evidentiary objections as they are not material to its disposition of the motions. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(q).)

 

Application – Art Mortuary Transport has been named as “Doe 2.” “Doe 2” is included in all causes of action in the SAC and, as such, Art Mortuary Transport is included in all causes of action.

 

The basis for Art Mortuary Transport’s Motion for Summary Judgment is that Art Mortuary Transport was not a substantial factor in the causation of injury to Plaintiffs. Art Mortuary Trasport presents that the only cause of action against it is General Negligence. However, as discussed ante, all causes of action in the SAC are directed against Art Mortuary Transport.

 

The Court need not delve into a lengthy analysis of causation as summary judgment is not appropriate as Art Mortuary Transport does not address one of the two negligence causes of action brought against it or the Res Ipsa Loquitur cause of action brought against it, and, as such, fails to meet its initial burden by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense as to the causes of action alleged against it.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

Conclusion

 

Defendant Art Mortuary Transport, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.
