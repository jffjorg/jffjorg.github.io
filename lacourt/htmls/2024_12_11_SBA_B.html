DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV02175    Hearing Date: December 11, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

DIANE MA,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

24TRCV02175

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

LUXE DEVELOPMENT, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          December 11, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      (1) Defendant Luxe Development, Inc., (2) defendant Oren Levy

Responding Party:                  None

(1)   Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default

(2)   Motion to Set Aside Entry of Default

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motions are GRANTED.  The defaults entered on August 20, 2024 against defendants Luxe Development, Inc. and Oren Levy are set aside and vacated.  Defendants are ordered to file a responsive pleading by Dec. 20, 2024.

BACKGROUND

On June 28, 2024, plaintiff Diane Ma filed a complaint against Luxe Development, Inc., Oren Levy, Luxe Home Solutions, Inc., Tariq Hassan Hill, and Business Alliance Insurance Company for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) recovery on surety bond, (4) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing (insurance bad faith), (5) intentional misrepresentation, (6), (7) statutory violation of Bus. & Prof. Code §§7025-7034 and 7125.2, (8) conversion, and (9) unfair business practices. 

On July 10, 2024, defendant Tariq Hassan Hill filed a notice of stay of proceedings based on his Ch. 7 bankruptcy filing.

On August 15, 2024, a default was entered against Luxe Home Solutions, Inc.

On August 20, 2024, defaults were entered against Luxe Development, Inc. and Oren Levy.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §473(b) states, in relevant part:  “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . .”

DISCUSSION

            Defendants Luxe Development, Inc. and Oren Levy request that the Court set aside the defaults entered on August 20, 2024, pursuant to CCP §473, under discretionary and/or mandatory relief.

            Defendants argue that the defaults entered against them were the result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, and/or excusable neglect.  Defendants assert that plaintiff’s counsel surreptitiously took defendants’ defaults while the parties were engaged in settlement discussions.  Defense counsel Jeremy Smith explains the chronology of counsel’s communications.  Defendants were served on July 17, 2024.  On July 25, plaintiff’s counsel had a telephone call with defense counsel where defense counsel gave plaintiff’s counsel permission to coordinate a site visit with defendant directly.  In an email that same date, plaintiff’s counsel requested a copy of the purchase and sale agreement from defendant to a third party, a list of the subcontractors that were used by defendant, and a copy of the subcontracts.  Defense counsel responded that defendant would provide a detailed scope of work and the documents requested by plaintiff.  Plaintiff’s counsel requested more information.  Counsel then engaged in settlement discussions.  On July 26, plaintiff’s counsel emailed copies of change orders and punch list of issues.  On August 7, defendant Levy emailed plaintiff’s counsel directly trying to coordinate the inspection.  On August 16, defendant emailed plaintiff’s counsel “a thorough and detailed cost breakdown of what was remaining” and that plaintiff “does not pay any additional cost for work remaining” and an available start date of August 27.  Plaintiff’s counsel replied that defendant’s email was not sufficiently detailed, and that defendant needed to set forth the detailed nature of the proposed work.  On August 20, defaults were entered against defendants.  On August 22, “having no clue that [plaintiff’s attorney] filed a request for entry of default,” defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel attaching a revised scope of work, requesting that he “confirm it is acceptable so we can finalize the agreement to resolve this matter and help your client finish her home renovation.”  Having not heard from plaintiff’s counsel, defense counsel sent another email to plaintiff’s counsel.  On August 26, plaintiff’s counsel sent an email to defense counsel stating that plaintiff “no longer wishes for [defendant]” to complete the scope of work and making a settlement offer.  On September 5, plaintiff’s counsel emailed defense counsel and “first disclosed that ‘during our settlement discussions, a default was taken and entered against both of [the defendants].’”

            There is no opposition. 

            The Court rules as follows:  The Court notes that “the negotiation of a possible settlement does not excuse the failure to proceed with due diligence in the filing of pleadings, absent some understanding that pleadings need not be filed while negotiations are in process.”  Turley v. Turley (1967) 254 Cal. App. 2d 169, 173.  But, also, “[t]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).  Further, “’any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.’”  Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 975, 980 (citation omitted).  The Court finds that the defaults were entered against defendants as a result of their and their attorney’s mistake, surprise, and/or excusable neglect.

            The motions are thus GRANTED.

           

ORDER

            The motions are GRANTED.  The Court sets aside and vacates the defaults entered on August 20, 2024 against Luxe Development, Inc. and Oren Levy.

Defendants are ordered to file a responsive pleading by Dec. 20, 2024.

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED and the action is STAYED.

BACKGROUND

On September 4, 2024, Andre Abed filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. for violations under Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. requests that the Court compel arbitration and to stay the case.

The complaint alleges that on January 13, 2023, plaintiff purchased a 2023 Tesla Model 3.  Complaint, ¶5.  After plaintiff took possession of the vehicle and during the warranty period, the vehicle contained defects that substantially impair the use, safety, and/or value of the vehicle.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff provided defendant with sufficient opportunity to service or repair the vehicle.  Id., ¶13.  Defendant was unable and/or failed to service or repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts.  Id., ¶14.

            Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant Tesla presents evidence that on January 23, 2023, plaintiff leased a 2023 Tesla Model 3 from Tesla.  Defendant asserts that the lease contains an arbitration provision, which states in part:  “Please carefully read this provision, which applies to any dispute between you and Tesla, Inc. and its affiliates. . . . [Y]ou agree that any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you and Tesla will not be decided by a judge or jury but instead by a single arbitrator in an arbitration administered by the [AAA] under its Consumer Arbitration Rules. . . .”  The provision contains an opt out clause and states, “[i]f you do not opt out, this agreement to arbitrate overrides any different arbitration agreement between us, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract.”  Raymond Kim decl., Exh. 1.

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

The Court finds that an arbitration agreement exists between plaintiff and defendant Tesla (both the manufacturer and seller of the vehicle) and that plaintiff did not opt-out. 

            Scope of agreement

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims against it fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration provision as they arise directly out of the relationship between plaintiff as lessee/consumer and Tesla as the manufacturer/lessor/warrantor/repair facility.

The Court finds that the scope of the arbitration provision covers plaintiff’s claims against Tesla as they relate to the relationship between plaintiff and Tesla, including manufacture, lease, warranty, and repairs to the vehicle.

Enforceability

                        Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

Defendant argues that the arbitration provision is enforceable, citing to California Supreme Court case Sanchez v. Valencia Holding, Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, which, defendant asserts, “upheld the entirety of a substantially similar arbitration provision against a wide array of unconscionability challenges.”

Plaintiff did not file an opposition and has made no argument that the arbitration provision is unconscionable.

The Court finds that even if the arbitration provision is an adhesion contract, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration provision.   The Court further finds little to no substantive unconscionability as the arbitration provision does not lack mutuality.

The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

ORDER

The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

The action is STAYED.
