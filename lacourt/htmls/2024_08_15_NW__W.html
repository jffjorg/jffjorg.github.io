DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV15600    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: W

MICHAEL COROMINAS, et al. v. WEST HILLS HOSPITAL AND MEDICAL CENTER, et al.

 

motion FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT  

 

Date of Hearing:        August 15, 2024                    Trial Date:       August 25, 2025

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        21STCV15600

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Jessica Thomas, M.D.

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Michael Corominas and Lisa Corominas

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 26, 2023, Plaintiffs Michael Corominas and Lisa Corominas filed a complaint against Defendants West Hills Hospital and Medical Center, Martin M. Mortazavi, M.D., Jessica Thomas, M.D., Paul Diehl, M.D., Dean Wilson, Jr., P.A., Specialty Imagining Center, Tejpal Singh, M.D., Providence Cedars-Sina Tarzana Medical Center, Chris Y. Wang, M.D., Jamie Soo Moi Eng, M.D., Exer Urgent Care, Michael H. Lee, M.D., and California Institute of Neuroscience for medical negligence and loss of consortium. Plaintiffs allege that further testing and workup should have been performed during the ER visit including testing for signs and symptoms and were negligent in failing to do so.  

 

Exer Urgent Care moved for summary judgment and judgment was entered in its favor on February 8, 2024. Dr. Lee also moved for summary judgment and judgment was entered in his favor on March 22, 2024. Plaintiff dismissed Specialty Imaging Center on April 4, 2024. Dr. Wang and Dr. Eng were dismissed on May 7, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication by Defendant Thomas, M.D. is DENIED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Defendant Jessica Thomas, M.D., in reply, submits objections to the declaration of Michael Ritter, M.D. Objection Nos. 1-5 are OVERRULED. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Jessica Thomas, M.D. (“Dr. Thomas”) moves this court under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c for an order granting summary judgment/summary adjudication as to Plaintiffs’ causes of action for professional negligence and loss of consortium on the grounds (1) Plaintiff’s complaint against Dr. Thomas is time-barred; (2) Dr. Thomas’ care and treatment of Plaintiff Michael Corominas at all times conformed to the standard of care for emergency medicine physicians; (3) no negligence on the part of Dr. Thomas was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff Michael Corominas’ alleged injuries; and (4) Plaintiff Lisa Corominas’ loss of consortium claim is dependent on Plaintiff Michael’s claim for medical malpractice and therefore, must fail.

 

The elements of medical malpractice are:¿“(1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.”¿¿(Simmons v. West Covina Medical Clinic¿(1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 696, 701-02 (citations omitted).)¿ “Both the standard of care and defendants’ breach must normally be established by expert testimony in a medical malpractice case.”¿¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

Thus, in a medical malpractice case, “[w]hen a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.”¿¿(Munro v. Regents of University of California¿(1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985 (citations omitted).)¿ An expert declaration, if uncontradicted, is conclusive proof as to the prevailing standard of care and the propriety of the particular conduct of the health care provider.¿¿(Starr v.¿Mooslin¿(1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 988, 999.)¿

 

Time-Barred

 

Dr. Thomas first moves for summary judgment on the grounds Dr. Thomas was not properly served with a 90-day notice extending the statute of limitations. Dr. Thomas asserts Mr. Corominas knew upon discharge from West Hills Hospital on February 21, 2020 that he had been rendered paraplegic and accordingly, this lawsuit had to have been brought by February 21, 2021, absent service a 90-day notice under Code of Civil Procedure section 364.

 

Section 364 provides that “[n]o action based upon the health care provider’s professional negligence may be commenced unless the defendant has been given at least 90 days’ prior notice of the intention to commence the action.” (CCP § 364(a).) Section 365 makes it clear that a failure to comply “shall not invalidate any proceedings of any court of this state, nor shall it affect the jurisdiction of the court to render a judgment therein.” (CCP § 365.) Instead, a failure to comply is merely a ground for discipline for the attorney who failed to comply. (See CCP §365; see also Toigo v. Hayashida (1980) 103 Cal.App.3d 267, 268-69.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 364(d) tolls the statute of limitations by ninety days when the notice of intent to sue is served during the last ninety days of the limitations period. (CCP § 364(d); Woods v. Young (1991) 53 Cal.3d 315, 325.)  With respect to the one-year statute of limitations, this means that a plaintiff has one year and ninety days in which to file the lawsuit if the plaintiff serves a notice of intent within the last ninety days of the limitations period.  (Woods, supra, 53 Cal.3d at 325.)

 

Dr. Thomas argues Plaintiffs improperly served their 90-day Notice to Dr. Thomas by mailing the notice to the hospital where Dr. Thomas works. (Charles Decl., Exh. B.)  In Hanooka v. Pivko (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 1553, the patient mailed a notice of intent to commence action against the health care providers at the hospital where they maintained staff privileges. The court found serving the physicians at the hospital did not comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 364 because doing so would “run counter to the purposes of giving a health care provider notice of the imminence of suit and promoting prelitigation settlement, which can only be effectuated if the health care provider has actual notice.” (Hanooka v. Pivko (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 1553, 1559.) As a result, because Section 364 notice was invalid, Dr. Thomas contends the statute of limitations ran on February 21, 2021, and Plaintiffs’ complaint was filed April 26, 2021.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue Emergency Rule 9 tolled the statute of limitations thereby extending Plaintiff’s deadline to file suit. Per Emergency Rule 9, and for statutes of limitations extending beyond 180 days, “the statutes of limitations and repose for civil causes of action that exceed 180 days are tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.”  Cal. Rules of Court, appen. I, emergency rule 9 (a); see Ables v. A. Ghazale Brothers, Inc. (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 823, 825 (restating rule).  Taking Dr. Thomas’ February 21, 2020 as the date of accrual, the cause of action would have been tolled 178 days, bringing the statute of limitations to August 18, 2021. As the complaint was filed April 26, 2021, the court finds the complaint timely. The court notes Plaintiffs did fail to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 364 by failing to properly serve Dr. Thomas the 90-day notice per Hanooka

 

Standard of Care and Causation

 

In support of their motion for summary judgment, Dr. Thomas presents the expert declaration of Roy Antelyes, M.D., Board Certified Emergency Medicine physician. Dr. Antelyes received his medical degree from the University of Southern California in 1977 and has been Board-Certified in Emergency Medicine since 1983. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Antelyes completed his residency at Huntington Memorial Hospital and UCLA Medical Center. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Antelyes also holds several administrative positions including Associate Director, Huntington Memorial Hospital Emergency Group, Inc. and Pediatric Liaison Physician, Huntington Memorial Hospital Emergency Medical Group, Inc. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶3.) Dr. Antelyes testifies based on his education, training, and substantial experience as an emergency physician, as well as his review of the aforementioned records, the care and treatment of Mr. Corominas received from Dr. Thomas complied with the applicable standard of care and nothing in the emergency department physicians did or failed to do was a substantial factor in causing Mr. Corominas’ ultimate outcome. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶¶6-11.) Dr. Antelyes testifies upon Mr. Corominas presentation at West Hills Hospital on January 30, 2020, Dr. Thomas saw Mr. Corominas for his complaints of gradually worsening back pain over the past week after doing workouts the day prior to onset. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) Physical examination of Mr. Corominas revealed intermittent full body spasms, diffuse lower back tenderness with palpable spasm, sensation intact throughout the lower extremities, strength 5/5 bilateral ankles, difficulty moving at the hip secondary to pain, and post void residual of 381. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) Dr. Thomas discussed the case with Dr. Diehl, the hospitalist, who agreed to admit the patient for continued pain management. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) Dr. Thomas also spoke with Dr. Mortazavi, a neurosurgeon, who agreed to consult. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) Thereafter, Mr. Corominas was admitted to Dr. Diehl for further care with Dr. Mortazavi for neurosurgery consult. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) An MRI revealed an epidural abscess, for which Dr. Mortazavi operated. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) At discharge on February 21, 2020, the patient had no movement of his lower extremities. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶9.) Given Mr. Corominas originally had no signs of cord compression or cauda equina syndrome and declined an MRI when he saw Dr. Wang, Dr. Thomas acted appropriately when Mr. Corominas was re-admitted to the hospital. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶10.) Dr. Thomas admitted Mr. Corominas to Dr. Diehl for further care with Dr. Mortazavi. (Dr. Antelyes Decl. ¶10.)

 

Accordingly, Dr. Thomas has met their burden of showing, by expert declaration, they did not breach the standard of care or cause Plaintiff’s injury. The burden shifts to Plaintiff to show, by contrary expert declaration, that a triable issue of fact exists.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff submits the expert declaration of Michel S. Ritter, M.D., a Board-Certified physician in Emergency Medicine. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Ritter received his medical degree from the University of California, Irvine in 1991 and completed his residency in emergency medicine at the University of California, Irvine in 1994. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶1.) Dr. Ritter is an Associate Clinical Professor in the Department of Emergency Medicine at UCI and holds several administrative positions including Director, Emergency Department Medical Education at Mission Hospital and Emergency Department Attending Physician at Mission Hospital, Regional Medical Center, and Children’s Hospital in Mission Viejo, California. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶¶2-3.) Dr. Ritter opines based his education, training, and substantial experience as an emergency medicine physician, Dr. Thomas’ conduct fell below the standard of care and was a substantial cause of Plaintiff’s spine infection as a result of no antibiotics, leading to worsening sepsis and ultimately, leading to his outcome of paraplegia. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶21.) Specifically, when Mr. Corominas presented at West Hills Hospital on January 30, 2020 with an MRI report, laboratory revealed a white blood cell count of 37,450, blood sugar of 498 and elevated absolute metamyelocytes. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶12.) Mr. Corominas was subsequently diagnosed with sepsis with septic shock secondary to multiple staphylococcus aureus abscesses in the epidural spine and acute kidney injury but Dr. Thomas was not told of the positive sepsis screening by the nursing staff. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶13.) Dr. Thomas also did not order any testing for determining the inflammation markers of Mr. Corominas or the sedimentation rate to rule out an infection. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶14.) Based on the extremely elevated white blood cell count that Mr. Corominas presented with on January 30, 2020, the standard of care required Dr. Thomas to also look at inflammatory markers to rule out infection as opposed to an elevated white cell count due to steroid use. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶16.) If she had done so, Dr. Ritter opines Dr. Thomas would have been presented with lab results showing high CRP and procalcitonin levels and therefore, would have known that Mr. Corominas was dealing with a severe infection and would have started him on a broad-spectrum antibiotics to treat or at least slow down the infection. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶16.) Moreover, since Mr. Corominas was taking Prednisone, a steroid, and there was concern about an elevated white blood cell count, the standard of care required Dr. Thomas to order a sedimentation rate. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶17.) If the sedimentation rate was high, this would have led Dr. Thomas to suspect an infection and would have led to a full sepsis workup. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶17.) Furthermore, due to Mr. Corominas high blood sugar level, the standard of care would have been for Dr. Thomas to question whether the high blood sugar level was due to an infection and if she had considered such, additional labs would have revealed an infection. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶18.) Dr. Thomas also asserts that the MRI report, which Dr. Thomas reviewed when Mr. Corominas presented at the hospital, had an abnormality that would not be there unless for an infection and the standard of care required Mr. Thomas to consult with a radiologist to review the MRI. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶19.) Nothing in the records indicate she ever considered having this consultation. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶19.) Lastly, the standard of care required Dr. Thomas to carefully review the labs that had been resulted on January 30, 2020. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶20.) There is nothing in the record that indicates Dr. Thomas considered the results including the elevated metamyelocyte. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶20.) Accordingly, Dr. Riter opines Dr. Thomas’ conduct fell below the standard of care and was a substantial factor in Mr. Corominas’ damages.

 

The court finds Plaintiffs have met their burden in establishing a triable issue of material fact. Dr. Thomas argues that Dr. Ritter’s declaration is inadmissible as unsupported speculation. The court disagrees. While courts liberally construe the declarations for the plaintiff’s experts and plaintiff is entitled to all favorable inferences derived from the expert’s declaration, “these principles in no way eliminate the need for some form of ‘reasoned explanation,’ and it remains the case that any inferences must ‘reasonably be derived from’ the declaration.”  (Fernandez v. Alexander (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 770, 782.) Dr. Ritter’s declaration is supported by a reasoned explanation of the underlying facts including if Dr. Thomas had looked at the inflammatory markers as required based on the elevated white blood cell count, Dr. Thomas would have discovered Mr. Corominas was dealing with a severe infection and that broad-spectrum antibiotics would have treated the spine infection or at least slowed down the infectious process. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶16.) Dr. Ritter opines that the infection as a result of Dr. Thomas’ breach worsened, which led to sepsis and ultimately led to paraplegia. (Dr. Ritter Decl. ¶21.) Dr. Thomas also argues that Dr. Ritter is discounting Dr. Thomas’ deposition testimony that infection was part of her differential diagnosis for this patient. However, this is not cited to in Dr. Thomas’ separate statement or provided in Exhibit B.

 

Since the court has found a triable issue of material fact for the professional negligence claims, Ms. Corominas’ loss of consortium claim still stands.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication by Defendant Thomas, M.D. is DENIED.

 

 


Case Number: 22VECV02589    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: W

FED REPORTER, INC. v. LESTER SNYDER

 

PLAINTIFF FED REPORTER, INC.’S MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER PURSUANT TO COURT’S MARCH 22, 2024 “PROTECTIVE ORDER-CONFIDENTIAL DESIGNATION ONLY” AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS OF $3,200 AGAINST dEFENDANT LESTER SNDYER

 

Date of Hearing:        August 15, 2024                                Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV02589

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Fed Reporter, Inc.

Responding Party:     Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lester Snyder  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 28, 2022, Plaintiff Fed Reporter, Inc. filed a complaint against Lester Snyder for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and breach of implied duty to perform with reasonable care. In October 2016, Fed Reporter engaged Mr. Snyder as an independent contractor to serve as a consultant in connection with Fed Reporter’s development and marketing of a software program. Plaintiff alleges after lengthy prior negotiations, the parties memorialized the terms of their agreement by executing a detailed written contract titled “Consulting Service Agreement”; however, Defendant Snyder repeatedly failed to perform his consulting services in a satisfactory manner and as contracted for by Fed Reporter. Plaintiff further alleges thereafter Defendant Snyder repudiated the express terms of the Consulting Agreement by falsely asserting: that he was the owner of the Product’s intellectual property; that he had licensed said Product to Fed Reporter; and that he was entitled to lifetime or “evergreen” royalty payments for Fed Reporter’s licensing of the Product. Following extensive settlement discussions, Defendant Snyder accepted a settlement offer that was made by Fed Reporter. However, Defendant Snyder later reneged on the settlement agreement, thereby forcing plaintiff to file suit.

 

On Marh 7, 2023, Snyder filed a cross-complaint against Fed Reporter, Inc. for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, breach of implied duty to perform with reasonable care, unjust enrichment, and declaratory relief.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff Fed Reporter, Inc.’s Motion for Protective Order Pursuant to Court’s March 22, 2024 “Protective Order-Confidential Designation Only” is GRANTED. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Fed Reporter, Inc. moves for the entry of a protective order in response to the “Notice of Objection by Lester Snyder to the Confidential Designation of Documents Produced by Fed Reporter” served by Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lester Snyder on May 14, 2024 and to uphold Fed Reporter’s “CONFIDENTIAL” designation as to three documents that have been produced by Fed Reported and designated as “CONFIDENTIAL” per for this court’s Protective Order – Confidential Designation Only entered on March 22, 2024. Fed Reporter also requests sanctions in the amount of $3,200.00 be imposed against Mr. Snyder, and payable to Fed Reporter.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020 provides that “[t]he court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (CCP § 2017.020(a).)  “The court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.”  (Id.) 

 

A responding party may move for a protective order with respect to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, requests for admissions, and depositions and a court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.”  (CCP §§ 2025.420, 2030.090, 2031.060, 2033.080.)  The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks.  (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Fed Reporter argues good cause exists for the requested protective order because these three documents -- (1) SmartCECL CR Accounting and Estimation Documentation 10-17-2021” (Bates-stamped FED REPORTER 06474-6517); (2) SmartCECL CR Accounting and Estimation Documentation” (Bates-stamped FED REPORTER 06518-6549); and (3) SmartCECL CR Accounting and Estimation Documentation_02_01_22” (Bates-stamped FED REPORTER 06550-6579) -- contain Fed Reporter’s proprietary and/or confidential information regarding its Smart CECL product. Moreover, Mr. Snyder obtained the three documents at issue when Fed Reporter produced them, subject to a “CONFIDENTIAL” designation pursuant to the Court’s 3/22/24 Protective Order. Thus, Mr. Snyder acquired these documents under circumstances where there was indeed a duty to maintain their secrecy and limit their use/disclosure pursuant to said Protective Order.

 

In opposition, Mr. Snyder argues the parties entered into a Consulting Service Agreement whereby Fed Reporter was granted conditional rights to use Snyder’s intellectual property for the SmartCECL product and any replacement products, subject to royalty payments and the agreement’s confidentiality obligations were explicitly set to expire one year after the contract’s termination, which occurred on October 20, 2022. As such, Fed Reporter now improperly seeks to maintain a “CONFIDENTIAL” designation on certain documents produced during litigation, despite the expiration of these obligations. Mr. Snyder also argues the protective order explicitly states that it does not alter or extend the contractual confidentiality obligations, which expired on October 20, 2023, and therefore, the court should enforce the terms of the protective order as written. In addition, he presents evidence that he had versions of these same three disputed documents in his own possession prior to the commencement of litigation – a point which defendant concedes in its reply brief. 

 

The court finds Fed Reporter has demonstrated good cause for the “CONFIDENTIAL” designation of the three documents. While it is true that Mr. Snyder is no longer contractually obligated by the terms of the agreement, Mr. Snyder may still be ordered to comply with the protective order to protect trade secrets. The party moving for a protective order has the burden of proof to establish by preponderance of the evidence the need for a protective order. (Stadish v. Sup. Ct. (1999) 71 Cal.4th 1130, 1145.) A party seeking to defend a trade secret must present an affidavit listing the declarant’s qualifications to give an opinion, identifying the trade secret, identifying the documents that may disclose it, and presenting evidence that the trade secret is actually a trade secret. (Id.) Fed Reporter has done so. (See Park Decl.) Moreover, Snyder has failed to show that his “legal possession” of the documents prior to litigation would bar Fed Reporter’s request for a “CONFIDENTIAL” designation of the three documents. Fed Reporter has also submitted evidence of their participation in the creation of the three documents. (See Park Decl. ¶¶6-7.) Mr. Snyder also fails to demonstrate that marking these three documents as “CONFIDENTIAL” would unfairly limit competition and innovation.

 

Sanctions

 

Both parties request sanctions. Fed Reporter seeks sanctions in the amount of $3,200.00 for 3 hours preparing the motion, another three hours reviewing the opposition and preparing the reply, and another 2 hours preparing for and attending court at counsel’s hourly rate of $400.00. (Hollenkamp Decl. ¶9.)

 

Defendant Snyder requests sanctions for opposing Fed Reporter’s motion in the amount of $3,035.00, including a $60 filing fee, $1,575 paid for research support, and $1,400 for attorney consultation, totaling $3,035.00. (Snyder Decl. ¶9.)

 

Because both parties’ positions were substantially justified, the court declines to award sanctions to either party.


Case Number: 24VECV00600    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: W

XAVIER PEREIRA vs JENNIFER JEANN PARKER, et al.

 

defendant ean holdings, llc and enterprise rent-a-car company of los angeles, llc’s demurrer to plaintiff’s complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        August 15, 2024                                Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV00600

 

Moving Party:            Defendants EAN Holdings, LLC and Enterprise Rent-A-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC

Responding Party:     No opposition  

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action involves a motor vehicle collision. Plaintiff Xavier Pereira alleges Defendants EAN Holdings, LLC and Enterprise Rent-A-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC (“Enterprise Entities”) rented their vehicle to Defendant Jennifer Jeann Parker without insurance and Ms. Parker did not have her own insurance coverage when Ms. Parker crashed into Plaintiff’s vehicle while it was parked outside Plaintiff’s residence. On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Enterprise and Parker for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence.

 

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff’s request for entry of default against Defendant Jennifer Jeann Parker was entered.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Ean Holdings, LLC and Enterprise Rent-A-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendants EAN Holdings, LLC and Enterprise Rent-A-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC request this court take judicial notice of (1) the most recent Statement of Information for EAN filed with the California Secretary of State and that it states it is in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles (Exh. B); and (2) the most recent Statement of Information for Enterprise filed with the California Secretary of State and that it states it is in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles (Exh. C). Based on this request, Defendants request this court take judicial notice of the fact that Enterprise and EAN are in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles and of the fact that Enterprise and EAN are affiliated with one another.

 

The court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice of Exhibits B and C. (Evid. Code §452(c).) However, the court declines to take judicial notice of the fact that Enterprise Entities are in the business of renting and/or leasing vehicles or the relationship between EAN and Enterprise as it is not “such common knowledge” or “facts and propositions” that it cannot or is not reasonably be the subject of dispute.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants EAN Holdings, LLC and Enterprise Rent-A-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC (“Enterprise Entities”) demur to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Enterprise Entities and is uncertain.

 

Failure to State Facts Sufficient

 

Enterprise Entities first demur to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiff's complaint does not allege sufficient material facts to state a cause of action against the Enterprise Entities. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that the Enterprise Entities were employees and agents at the time of the Incident. However, Plaintiff’s complaint fails to allege an employer. Moreover, Plaintiff fails to provide any factual support for his claims, a common theme thus far in the pleading. For there is no mention as to how the Enterprise Entities permitted operation of the subject vehicle or how it had the requisite knowledge of Parker's incompetence or unfitness to drive. Enterprise Entities also contend Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged permissive use.

 

The court disagrees. The court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the required elements of negligence for the purposes of a demurrer. Plaintiff is not required to identify and allege the exact nature of the wrongful act. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see also ibid. [“Negligence may be alleged in general terms; that is, it is sufficient to allege an act was negligently done without stating the particular omission which rendered it negligent.”].) As such, while bare, Plaintiff’s claim of negligent entrustment and permissive use are sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer.

 

Graves Amendment

 

Enterprise Entities next demur to the complaint on the grounds a rental car company and its affiliates cannot be held vicariously liable for the acts/omissions of its renters, and only faces potential liability for its own independent acts of negligence or criminal conduct.

 

The Graves Amendment provides immunity from liability under state law to vehicle owners who rent or lease their vehicle, unless there is negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner. (49 U.S.C. § 30106(a).) Because the court has not taken judicial notice of those facts and the complaint fails to allege Defendants are engaged in the business of renting or leasing motor vehicles, the court therefore cannot determine that the Graves Amendment is applicable at the pleading stage. This argument may be better suited for summary judgment or the like.

 

Uncertainty

 

Lastly, Enterprise Entities argue the complaint is uncertain. For example, it is uncertain whether Plaintiff is really pleading course and scope liability or agency liability, as there is no allegation of a principal in the pleading. Additionally, while the first cause of action for motor vehicle negligence solely names Enterprise under the theory of negligent entrustment, the second cause of action for general negligence appears to be alleging negligent entrustment against both Enterprise Entities.

 

The court overrules Enterprise Entities demurrer for uncertainty because the complaint is not so uncertain that Defendant cannot reasonably respond thereto. Any ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. (Khoury v. Maly's of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

Accordingly, Enterprise Entities’ Demurrer to the Complaint is OVERRULED.
