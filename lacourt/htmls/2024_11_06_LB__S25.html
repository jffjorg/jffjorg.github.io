DEPARTMENT S25 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Imed Hsini v. Mohsen Ghariani, et al.
LASC Case No. 24LBCV00194
Hearing Date: September 11, 2024
Request for Default Judgment by Court
 
 
In preparation for the September 11, 2024 default judgment hearing, the court has identified several concerns with Plaintiff’s request: 
 

  1. Request for Entry of Default was entered on May 9, 2024 which is less than 30 days after service of the summons and complaint to Defendant Danielle Canissa and CDW Logistics, LLC. (C.C.P. §587);
 
  1. Plaintiff checked the wrong box and should have checked “Court Judgment” in the “Request For” section on the 1st page. (See 7/29/2024 Request for Entry of Default/Judgment);
 
  1. No DOEs dismissal filed;
 
  1. No attached/adequate and/or authenticated exhibits provided with Plaintiff’s Declaration. Exhibits should demonstrate, e.g. costs for title and insurance for two vehicles, power of attorney, agreement entered into with Defendants to purchase trucks and provide logistic and shipping services to clients;
 
  1. No contractual or statutory basis provided by Plaintiff as to recovery of attorney fees. Also, attorney fees requested is more than what is allowed in a contract for recovery of reasonable attorney fees under Local Rule of the Court, rule 3.214(a);  
 
  1. Prayer for complaint does not specify damages claimed;
 
  1. No notice of punitive damages as required under Code Civ. Proc., § 425.11, subd. (c).  Appears Plaintiff may be waiving punitive damages based upon proposed judgment;
 
  1. Proposed Judgment (UD-100) incorrect calculations as to attorney fees and prejudgment interest calculations.


Case Number: 23LBCV00132    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: S25

Procedural Background

On January 23, 2023, Plaintiff Tolani Lawal, through her Guardian ad Litem Vanyel Lawrence, as a wrongful death beneficiary of Decedent Ayomipo Oluwatobi Lawal filed a complaint against Defendants City of Long Beach, County of Los Angeles, David Anthony Jones and Does 1 through 100. Plaintiff alleges two causes of action: (1) negligence/wrongful death and (2) dangerous condition of public property. On February 18, 2022, at or around 7:40 am at the intersection of Oregon Avenue and 17th Street in the City of Long Beach a collision occurred between a bicycle ridden by Ayomipo Lawal (“Decedent”) and a City-owned vehicle driven by Defendant and City employee David Anthony Jones (“Defendant Jones”); Defendant Jones was working within the course and scope of his employment at the time of incident. (Compl., ¶¶ 3, 6, 12-15). Decedent died because of his injuries and Plaintiff Tolani Lawal is his heir. (Exh. A, Government Claim, p. 3; Exh. B, Compl., ¶¶ 1-3, 6, 12-15; Zinger Decl., ¶¶ 4-15.) On June 11, 2024, the Court denied Defendants’ motion for summary judgment. (June 11, 2024 Minute Order – Ruling on Submitted Matter.) On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Settlement.

Legal Standard

“The guardian or conservator of the estate or guardian ad litem so appearing for any minor, person who lacks legal capacity to make decisions . . . has been appointed shall have power, with the approval of the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, to compromise the same, to agree to the order or judgment to be entered therein for or against the ward or conservatee, and to satisfy any judgment or order in favor of the ward or conservatee or release or discharge any claim of the ward or conservatee pursuant to that compromise.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(3).) “A petition for court approval of a compromise of, or a covenant not to sue or enforce judgment on, a minor's disputed claim; a compromise or settlement of a pending action or proceeding to which a minor or person with a disability is a party; or the disposition of the proceeds of a judgment for a minor or person with a disability under Probate Code sections 3500 and 3600-3613 or Code of Civil Procedure section 372 must be verified by the petitioner and must contain a full disclosure of all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise, covenant, settlement, or disposition. Except as provided in rule 7.950.5, the petition must be submitted on a completed Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or Person With a Disability (form MC-350).” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.)

A court approving a minor and/or person with a disability’s compromise will order and direct payment of reasonable expenses – including attorney’s fees out of the total settlement amount. (Prob. Code, § 3601.) Under Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.955(a)(1), the Court “must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” Also, the court must give weight to the terms of any existing representation agreement. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.955(a)(2).) Other non-exclusive factors to consider include: (1) the fact that a person with a disability is involved, and her particular circumstances; (2) the length and nature of representation; and (3) the time and labor required. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.955(b)(1-14).) “The rule permits, but does not require, the court to allow attorney’s fees in an amount specified in a contingency fee agreement. The amount of attorney’s fees allowed by the court must meet the reasonableness standard of section 3601 no matter how they are determined.” (Advisory Com. Comment to rule 7.955.)

Analysis

The Court has reviewed the proposed settlement and finds that the settlement itself is fair and reasonable. The minor claimant here, Tolani Lawal, has agreed to dismiss her claims and will receive $50,000.00 from Defendants City of Long Beach and David Anthony Jones (“Defendants”). (Pet. ¶ 10.) No other settlement payments are also being made to any person other than the claimant to settle claims arising out of the same incident or accident that resulted in the claimant’s injury. (Pet., ¶ 11.a.)

The amount of attorney’s fees requested, represents 25 percent of the gross settlement to minor claimant, is $12,500.00. (See Pet., ¶ 13.a; Attachment 13a [Attorney Fee Declaration] ¶ 30.) The Claimant, through her parent and Guardian ad litem, Vanyel Lawrence, agreed to a 45 percent contingency fee arrangement at any time after a lawsuit is filed. (See Attachment 17.a [Retainer Agreement and Agreement and Consent and Authorization to Associate Co-Counsel] at pp. 1 and 3.) In relevant part, the retainer agreement provides:

“At any time within sixty (60) days of execution of this agreement by Client, Client shall pay Law Firm as its fee the sum of Thirty-Three and One Third Percent (33 1/3%) of any recovery obtained. At any time after sixty (60) days of execution of this agreement by Client, Client shall pay Law Firm as its fee the sum of Forty Percent (40%) of any recovery obtained. At

any time after a lawsuit or demand of arbitration is filed, Client shall pay Law Firm as its fee the sum of Forty-Five Percent (45%) of any recovery obtained.”

(Attachment 17.a at pp. 1 and 3 [“THE ATTORNEYS’ FEES PAID BY CLIENT ARE NOT INCREASED BY THIS ASSOCIATION”].) The petition further seeks $13,225.75 to pay costs other than medical expenses. (Pet. ¶ 13.b; Attachment 13.b at p. 2.) Costs and litigation expenses are provided for under Retainer Agreement. (See Attachment 17.a at p. 1.) After payment of all fees and expenses, Claimant is left with a balance of $24,274.25.

Tentative Ruling

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s counsel has set forth sufficient facts breaking down and explaining the legal fees and costs incurred in litigating this matter. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.955(b).) The Court approves the instant petition approve minor’s compromise and to deposit funds in blocked account.

Plaintiff Ruben Rodriguez-Ponce’s Motion to Compel Defendant Jose Luis Tenorio to Respond to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Monetary Sanction

Legal Standard

Note: This motion was continued on the Court’s own motion from October 24, 2024 to November 6, 2024.

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to respond at all, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a court order compelling answers thereto. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290.) All that needs to be shown is that the discovery was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. The moving party is not required to show a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the matter informally before filing this motion. A motion to compel initial discovery responses need not show good cause, meeting and conferring, or timely filing, and need not be accompanied by a separate statement. (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pac. Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The failure to timely respond also waives all objections.¿¿

On motion, a party may be relieved from its waiver of objections if: (1) the party subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance “with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240” and (2) “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a)(1)-(2).)

Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290, subd. (c), provides that, "[t]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust." Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010, subd. (d), provides that a misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010, subd. (h), states that a misuse of the discovery process includes making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery. A court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct under Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771.)

Analysis

In the instant motion, Plaintiff states that Form Interrogatories, Set One, was served on Defendant on May 3, 2024, and that Plaintiff failed to respond by the June 5, 2024 deadline. (Knypstra Decl., ¶¶ 4, 5, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff asserts that although not required to meet and confer prior to filing a motion to compel initial responses, Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to obtain discovery multiple times, on June 18, 25, July 11, 25, 30 of this year. (Knypstra Decl., ¶¶ 7-14, Exs. 2-8.) Despite Defendant’s counsel stating that he would provide an update on the efforts made to obtain responses, no update was provided and no response to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, has been served. (Knypstra Decl., ¶ 15.)

Tentative Ruling

The motion is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to provide objection-less responses to the Form Interrogatories, Set One, within 20 days from the date of the hearing. The Court declines to award monetary sanctions against Defendant in this instance; however, further continued failure by Defendant to timely respond to discovery requests or engage in the discovery process may result in future monetary sanctions.

Plaintiff Ruben Rodriguez-Ponce’s Motion to Deem Admitted the Truth of the Matters in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, to Defendant Jose Luis Tenorio, and Request for Monetary Sanction

Procedural History

Note: This motion was continued on the Court’s own motion from October 24, 2024 to November 6, 2024.

On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Jose Luis Tenorio, Beacon Funding Corporation, Inner State Towing, LLC, and Does 1 to 10, alleging a sole cause of action for motor vehicle negligence. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants were negligent and caused Plaintiff injuries on August 26, 2022 at E. 7th Street and Orange Ave., City of Long Beach, CA. (Compl., at p. 4.) On March 29, 2024, Defendants Jose Luis Tenorio and Inner State Roadside Assistance, LLC filed an answer to the complaint. On May 1, 2024, Defendant Beacon Funding Corporation filed a general denial. On September 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for an order deeming Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, admitted. As of October 18, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

Legal Standard

If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply:

(a) The party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product . . . . The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2033.210, 2033.220, and 2033.230.

(2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

(b) The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed

admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).”

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280.)

The court “shall” grant the motion to deem requests for admission admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

Analysis

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff served Requests for Admissions, Set One, on Defendant Tenorio. Defendant failed to respond by the June 5, 2024 deadline. (Knypstra Decl., ¶¶ 4, 5, Ex. 1.) Although not required to meet and confer prior to filing a motion to deem Requests for Admissions admitted, Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to obtain discovery multiple times, on June 18, 25, July 11, 25, 30 of this year. (Knypstra Decl., ¶¶ 7-14, Exs. 2-8.) Despite Defendant Tenorio’s counsel stating that he would provide an update on the efforts made to obtain responses, no update was provided and no response to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, has been served. (Knypstra Decl., ¶ 15.)

No opposition has been filed by Defendant.

Tentative Ruling

The motion is GRANTED and all matters specified in Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One, are deemed admitted. Because Defendant did not file an opposition, the Court removes the 1 hour that Plaintiff’s counsel anticipates spending on preparing a reply to Defendant’s opposition. The Court awards monetary sanctions of $2,450.00 in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant and Defendant’s counsel jointly, payable within 30 days of this order.
