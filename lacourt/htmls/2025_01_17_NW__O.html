ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a Song-Beverly Act case arising out of Plaintiff Rosendo Rodriguez’s (“Plaintiff”) lease of a 2023 Ford F-150 manufactured by Defendant Ford Motor Company (“Defendant”). (See Complaint, ¶ 8.)

Plaintiff now seeks to compel Defendant’s further responses to his request for production of documents, set one (“RFPs”).

///

///

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On May 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Implied Warranty; (3) Violation of Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2.

On June 7, 2024, Defendant filed its Answer.

On August 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Defendant’s Further Responses to its RFPs (“Motion”).

 On August 21, 2024, the Court granted the parties’ Stipulated Protective Order.

On November 7, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion and issued the Song-Beverly Act Joint Case Management Conference Statement Addendum (“Addendum”) on November 18, 2024.

 On January 6, 2025, Defendant filed its Opposition.

On January 10, 2025, Plaintiff filed its Reply.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Request for Production of Documents

In responding to a request for production of documents, a party must provide one of the following: (1) a legally adequate statement of compliance; (2) a statement of inability to comply; or (3) an objection to the particular demand. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210(a).)[1] If any objections are made, they must: (1) “[i]dentify with particularity any document … falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made”; and (2) “[s]et forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground for, the objection.”¿(Id. at § 2031.240(b).) A statement that the responding party is unable to comply with a particular demand “shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand”; “shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party”;  and “shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Id. at § 2031.230.) 

A party who believes a responding party has served (1) an incomplete statement of compliance, (2) an inadequate, incomplete, or evasive statement of compliance, or (3) an unmeritorious objection can move for an order compelling further responses but must “set forth specific acts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (See id. at §§ 2031.310(a)-(b)(1).)

IV.          MEET AND CONFER

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b)(1), and 2033.290(b)(1).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Id. at § 2016.040.) However, a discovery motion should not be denied automatically based upon the reason that the moving parties failed to meet and confer in good faith. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 434.)

Plaintiff’s counsel filed a declaration attesting to Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts, which consisted of two letters, the last of which was sent on August 5, 2024. (See Declaration of Roy Enav filed August 13, 2024, ¶¶ 18-20, 23-34, Exs. 5-8.) The Court does not find Plaintiff has fulfilled his meet and confer obligations. First, the Court does not generally consider an exchange of letters a sufficient attempt to avoid this Court’s intervention. Second, in postponing the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion and issuing its Addendum, the Court specifically stated “[c]ounsel are ordered to meet and confer regarding the substance of the pending Motion to Compel Further with the Addendum in mind BEFORE either party files any additional briefing in connection with the Motion to Compel Further.” (Minute Order dated November 7, 2024.)

Plaintiff does not address the Court’s November 7, 2024, Minute Order in its Reply and does not appear to have made any additional efforts to meet and confer with Defendant as required by the same, issued between the filing of Plaintiff’s Motion and the upcoming hearing on the matter. For its part, Defendant’s Opposition makes no mention of any such efforts either. For that reason alone, the Court denies Plaintiff’s Motion.

Plaintiff’s Motion is also denied for failing to address Defendant’s claims that it was not served with the Motion. Defendant’s counsel declares “[o]n October 23, 2024, Ford sent Plaintiffs a meet and confer letter demanding Plaintiff withdraw the present Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, which noted that the motion is premature and that it was never served upon Ford’s counsel.  Plaintiff’s counsel never responded to this letter.” (Declaration of Colin Cronin filed January 6, 2025, ¶ 7, Ex. 7.) As was the case with the Court’s issuance of its Addendum, Plaintiff does not address Defendant’s claims or offer any evidence to refute them, such as a copy of the email purporting to properly serve Defendant with the Motion. (See Reply.)

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Further Responses to his RFPs.
