DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20VECV01388    Hearing Date: February 1, 2024    Dept: W

MARK A. LIKER v. ELIEZER APPEL, et al.

 

CROSS-DEFENDANT MARK A. LIKER’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES AND COSTS PURSUANT TO C.C.P. 425.16(C)(1)

 

Date of Hearing:        February 1, 2024                                            Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                     Case No.:        20VECV01388

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Mark A. Liker 

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Mark A. Liker alleges Defendant Eliezer Appel, in his capacity as a California licensed attorney, instructed Plaintiff to purchase certain real property for $250,000.00 to Defendant Cochran Inc. and promised Plaintiff a return on his investment as well as plaintiff would take ownership of the property or that the property would secure Plaintiff’s investment. Plaintiff further alleges Eliezer Appel concealed from Plaintiff the fact that plaintiff did not own the property or the company that took title to the property that is, the Defendant Lockhart Development, Inc., and that Eliezer Appel took Plaintiff’s investment for his own gain.

 

On November 24, 2020, Plaintiff Mark A. Liker filed a complaint against Defendants Eliezer Appel, Lockhart Development, Inc., and Cochran Inc. asserting a single cause of action for fraud. Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint on August 30, 2023, asserting new causes of action – breach of contract, negligent misrepresentation, intentional misrepresentation, conspiracy to commit fraud, and breach of attorney fiduciary duty/duty of loyalty.

 

On February 11, 2021, Lockhart Development, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Mark A. Liker asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Written Contract; (2) Intentional or Negligent Interference with Contractual Relations; (3) Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (5) Declaratory Relief; (6) Implied Contractual Indemnity; and (7) Slander of Title.

 

On October 20, 2022, Appel filed a cross-complaint against Liker, the California Neurological Institute, and Avi Marciano asserting causes of action for (1) Violation of Physician’s Medical Practice Act (MPA); (2) Undue Influence (CA Welfare & Institutions Code 15610.70); (3) Negligent Infliction of Extreme Emotional Distress (NIED); (4) Intentional Infliction of Extreme Emotional Distress (IIED); (5) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; and (6) Civil Extortion.     

 

This action is related to Marc A. Larocque v. Christine Larocque Franz, et al. (LC105233).

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Mark A. Liker’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Mark A. Liker moves the court for an award of attorney fees and costs against Cross-Complainant Lockhart pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. Liker makes the motion on the grounds that on November 7, 2023, the Second District Court of Appeal affirmed the anti-SLAPP order and judgment entered July 30, 2021 by this court after granting Mark A. Liker’s Special Motion to Strike, and that an award of attorney fees and costs to Liker as prevailing party in the appeal is mandatory.

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c), a prevailing defendant is entitled to recover attorneys’ fees and costs associated with the motion. Under CRC 3.1702 and CRC 8.104, a request for attorneys’ fees must be made within 60 days of service of the notice of entry of judgment. A defendant may only recover fees and costs related to the motion to strike. (Lafayette Morehouse, Inc. v. Chronicle Publishing Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1383.) This includes fees associated with bringing the motion for fees. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1141 (“an award of fees may include not only the fees incurred with respect to the underlying claim, but also the fees incurred in enforcing the right to mandatory fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16.”).) Additionally, “[a]ny fee award must also include those incurred on appeal. [Citation.]” (Trapp v. Naiman¿(2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 113, 122.)¿

 

On July 30, 2021, this court granted Liker’s anti-SLAPP motion and on November 7, 2023, the Court of Appeal affirmed this court’s order. Accordingly, Liker’s motion for attorneys’ fees is proper and Liker is entitled to recover attorneys’ fees and costs.

 

The court further finds the attorney fees and costs sought herein reasonable. Liker requests $14,717.50 in attorney fees and $596.10 in costs. Counsel’s hourly rate is $400. (Arnold Decl., ¶6.) Based on Mr. Arnold’s experience, his hourly rate is reasonable in the community of practice. Moreover, an appeal from an anti-SLAPP motion is complex in nature and requires the submission of declarations and evidence akin to a motion for summary judgment or a preliminary injunction.¿Counsel for Liker also provides a billing statement listing the tasks, time incurred, and costs incurred by Liker in this action for the anti-SLAPP Motion to date. (Arnold Decl., Exh. B.) The billing statement of the attorney fees and costs incurred on the motion and the declaration by counsel demonstrate the reasonableness of the time spent on this case.

 

Accordingly, Liker’s unopposed motion for attorneys’ fees is granted in the total amount of $14,717.50 in attorney fees and $596.10 in costs.


Case Number: 23VECV04368    Hearing Date: February 1, 2024    Dept: W

RAYMOND TORRES, et al. v. SUSAN C. BEAULIEN, AN INDIVIDUAL, AND AS TRUSTEE OF THE EDWARD C. LUSNICH & SUSAN C. BEAULIEN TRUST, DATED AUGUST 19, 2006, et al.

 

DEFENDANT’s demurrer with motion to strike

 

Date of Hearing:        February 1, 2024                               Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04368

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Susan C. Beaulien, an individual, and as Trustee of The Edward C. Lusnich & Susan C. Beaulien Trust dated August 19, 2006, and Roberta C. Turitz, as an individual and as Trustee of The Turitz Family Trust dated 9/18/2009

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Raymond Torres

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Portillo Decl. ¶¶5-8.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 2, 2023, Plaintiffs Raymond Torres, Elva Amador, and Ramon Torres filed a complaint against Defendants Susan C. Beaulien, an individual, and as Trustee of The Edward C. Lusnich & Susan C. Beaulien Trust dated August 19, 2006, and Roberta C. Turitz, as an individual and as Trustee of The Turitz Family Trust dated 9/18/2009, Buildium, LLC dba 24/7 Real Estate Leasing & Management and Omega Management for 1. Breach of Contract; 2. Statutory Breach of Warranty of Habitability (Civil Code §§ 1941, 1941.1, and 1942.4); 3. Tortious Breach of Warranty of Habitability; 4. Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; 5. Private Nuisance; 6. Negligence; 7. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; 8. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; 9. Violation of Business & Professions Code §17200, et seq.; 10. Violation of LAMC 45.33; 11. Breach of Covenant of Good Faith & Fair Dealing; 12. Violation of Civil Code §1942.4; and 13. Violation of Civil Code 1942.5.

 

Plaintiffs and Defendants entered into a written lease agreement for certain real property located in Winnetka, California. Plaintiff alleges throughout the duration of their tenancy, the Plaintiffs encountered a multitude of habitability issues within the Subject Property however Defendants have refused to resolve the issues through mediation or address any of the myriad of habitability issues.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED, in part. Defendants’ Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Susan C. Beaulien, an individual, and as Trustee of The Edward C. Lusnich & Susan C. Beaulien Trust dated August 19, 2006, and Roberta C. Turitz, as an individual and as Trustee of The Turitz Family Trust dated 9/18/2009 demur to the first, fifth, seventh, eighth, eleventh, and twelfth causes of action on the grounds the causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to support a claim against Defendants and are duplicative.

 

Breach of Contract

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds Plaintiffs fail to allege the existence of any contract between them and Buildium or Omega.

 

To state a claim for breach of contract, a Plaintiff must allege sufficient facts to establish 1) a contract between the parties 2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance 3) defendant’s breach 4) damages to plaintiff from the breach. (See e.g. Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal. App. 4th 1171, 1178; CACI No. 303.)  A contract must be pled verbatim in the body of the complaint, be attached to the complaint and incorporated by reference, or be pled according to its legal effect.  (Bowden v. Robinson (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 705, 718.)

 

Defendants argue they are the indemnitors of Buildium or Omega as Defendants signed contracts with Buildium and Omega for property management services, and Defendants agreed to indemnify those entities as part of the contracts.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue they have adequately alleged the existence of a pertinent and enforceable contract between themselves and the Defendants, including Buildium and Omega. Plaintiffs point to the residential lease agreement attached to the complaint, which involves Buildium DBA 24/7 Real Estate Leasing & Management as signatories. Plaintiffs further argue the existence of such indemnity agreements does not negate the direct contractual relationship established between the Plaintiffs and all named Defendants.

 

The court agrees Plaintiff does not sufficiently allege breach of contract against Buildium or Omega. 24/7 Real Estate Leasing & Management signed the lease agreement; however, as brokers. The lease agreement explicitly provides ‘Real estate brokers who are not also Landlord under this Agreement are not parties to the Agreement between Landlord and Tenant.’  As such, the complaint fails to allege facts against Buildium or Omega.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Nuisance

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for private nuisance on the grounds the cause of action is devoid of intentional acts taken by them.

 

“The essence of a private nuisance is an interference with the use and enjoyment of land.” (Friends of H Street v. City of Sacramento (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 152, 160.) Thus to allege a cause of action for private nuisance, the plaintiff must allege injury specific to the use and enjoyment of his land. (See Adams v. MHC Colony Park Limited Partnership (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 601, 610.) 

 

Defendants argue the only reference to Turitz in the entire Complaint is as a co-owner of the Property. Moreover, Plaintiffs' cause of action for private nuisance is based on the same facts and legal theory that forms the basis for the negligence cause of action.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the cause of action for private nuisance is distinct from negligence. Plaintiff contends the Defendants’ actions demonstrate not just negligence but also an intentional disregard for Plaintiffs’ health and safety, constituting a private nuisance.

 

The court agrees the complaint does not allege facts sufficient to state a claim of private nuisance against Turitz. The complaint does not allege how Turitz, as co-owner, interfered with Plaintiff’s use and enjoyment of the property.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants demur to the seventh cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress on the grounds Plaintiffs did not plead sufficient facts evidencing that they suffered severe emotional distress.

 

The elements for a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) an actual and proximate causal link between the tortious conduct and the emotional distress.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050.)   

 

Defendants argue there are no allegations as to the duration or extent of the alleged symptoms. It appears all of the Plaintiffs have combined their alleged symptoms without specifying the length or intensity that each of them experienced such symptoms.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the allegations have sufficiently demonstrated such actions indeed exceed all bounds of that is usually tolerated in a civilized community, and on its face, the complaint has sufficiently plead intentional and outrageous conduct throughout Plaintiffs tenancy by Defendants.

 

The court finds the complaint sufficiently alleges the nature of the emotional distress. “[T]he mere allegation that the plaintiffs suffered severe emotional distress, without facts indicating the nature or extent of any mental suffering incurred as a result of the defendant's alleged outrageous conduct, fail[s] to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.” (Pitman v. City of Oakland (1988) 197 Cal.App.3d 1037, 1047.) The complaint alleges “Plaintiffs have suffered and/or continue to suffer illness, physical injury, property damages, emotional harm, anguish, depression, fearfulness, anxiety, nightmares, difficulty sleeping, embarrassment, shame, and other economic damages in an amount to be determined according to proof at trial.” (Compl. ¶173.) This is sufficient.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendants demur to the eighth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress on the grounds this cause of action is duplicative of the sixth cause of action for negligence.

 

A demurrer may be sustained when a cause of action is duplicative of another cause of action and thus adds nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery. (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.) Notably, the doctrine of “negligent infliction of emotional distress” is not a separate cause of action. It simply allows certain persons to recover damages for emotional distress only on a negligence cause of action even though they were not otherwise injured or harmed. (See Molien v. Kaiser Foundation Hospitals (1980) 27 Cal.3d 916, 928.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the Plaintiffs NIED claim is substantively distinct from their general negligence claim. While the negligence claim addresses the Defendants’ failure to maintain the property, leading to physical hazards and property damage, the NIED claim specifically addresses the Defendants’ egregious conduct that has caused severe emotional and psychological harm to the Plaintiffs.

 

The court agrees the negligence cause of action and NIED cause of action are based on the same facts. The causes of action share the same elements of duty, breach, causation and damages. Both of the causes of action allege Defendants owed Plaintiffs a duty of care when addressing the reported defects in the Subject Property and breached that duty by never resolving the issues.

 

 As such, the NIED claim is duplicative of the first cause of action for negligence.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Defendants demur to the eleventh cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing on the grounds the cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.  

 

The covenant of good faith and fair dealing is implied by law in every contract, and it acts “as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party’s rights to the benefits of the contract.” (Racine & Laramie, Ltd. v. Department of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1026, 1031-1032.) “If allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded ….” (Green Valley Landowners Assn. v. City of Vallejo (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 425, 433.)

 

Defendants argue the complaint fails to allege Beaulien and Turitz acted unfairly or in bad faith and moreover, the cause of action for breach of good faith alleges the same breach and damages as those sought by the breach of contract cause of action.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the Defendants’ actions have gone beyond “unfair” or “in bad faith” as their conduct demonstrates a systematic effort to undermine the Plaintiffs ability to enjoy the benefits of the leased premises.

 

The court finds the allegations do not go beyond a breach of contract. As noted above, if allegations do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach, the cause of action is duplicative. The eleventh cause of action simply alleges Plaintiff fully performed their contractual obligations under the lease and Defendants prevented Plaintiffs rights to receive the benefits of the contract in that by and through the breaches of habitability and quiet enjoyment.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the eleventh cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Violation of Civil Code §1942.4

 

Defendants demur to the twelfth cause of action for Violation of Civil Code §1942.4 on the grounds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Civil Code section 1942.4 establishes a statutory right of action for damages against landlords who demand or collect rent when: “(1) the dwelling is untenantable…; (2) a public officer inspects the premises and gives the landlord written notice that it must abate the nuisance or repair the property; (3) the conditions have not been remedied within 35 days of the notice; and (4) the substandard conditions were not caused by the tenant’s acts or omissions.” (See Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1298; Civ. Code, § 1942.4(a).) 

 

Defendants argue the complaint fails to allege that any inspection of the premises took place. In opposition, Plaintiff argues the complaint states multiple inspections were conducted and thereafter a notice was issued to Defendants and no remedial action was taken.

 

The court agrees the complaint does not sufficiently allege a public officer inspected the premises. Rather, one has to infer that the notice issued to Defendants on August 21, 2023 was based upon an inspection and that Defendants did not remediate the condition within 35 days of the notice.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the twelfth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

 

Motion to Strike[1]

 

Defendants move to strike paragraph 180 of the negligent infliction of emotional distress cause of action on the grounds the NIED claim is essentially a negligence claim and California law specifically disallows recovery of punitive damages based upon mere negligence.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff states Defendants Motion to Strike must be denied as moot because following the parties meet and confer efforts, Plaintiffs’ have agreed to remove Paragraph 180 of Plaintiffs Complaint.

 

In reply, Defendants disagree with this contention. Regardless, the court finds the complaint does not state facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages and GRANTS the motion to strike paragraph 180.  Further, it appears plaintiffs have agreed to remove this paragraph.
