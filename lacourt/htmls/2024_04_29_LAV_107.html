DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03276    Hearing Date: April 29, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

JW Seals, Jr.,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

Reel Security California, Inc.

Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  23VECV03276          107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

(1) Demurrer to FAC

(2) Motion to Strike

 

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

I. BACKGROUND

Plaintiff JW Seals, Jr. (“Plaintiff”) was employed as a security guard from January 15, 2022 by defendant Reel Security California, Inc. (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”). (FAC pp. 1–3.) This lawsuit arises out of that employment.

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”). On November 7, 2023, Defendant demurred to the Complaint, which demurrer was sustained with leave to amend on January 2, 2024.

On February 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (the “FAC”) alleging causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) violations of labor laws, (3) false representation, (4) negligence, (5) failure to respond to emergencies, (6) whistleblowing and retaliation protections, and (7) documenting and reporting violations.

On March 26, Defendant filed the instant demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and a motion to strike (the “Motion to Strike”). On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff opposed both the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike, and on April 22, 2024, Defendant replied to Plaintiff’s oppositions.

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41(a) and §¿435.5(a) require parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike.

 

Defendant’s counsel submits with the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike declarations stating that after exchanging correspondences regarding the Demurrer and the Motion to Strike, “Plaintiff made it clear he would not substantively meet and confer.” (Crossin Decl. ¶ 3.) We remind Plaintiff of his obligation to meet and confer with Defendant in good faith.

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings 

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Similarly, per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” which is 30 days. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 412.20, subd. (a)(3).)

¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196 (disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176).)

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of service indicating that the FAC was served on Defendant on February 26, 2024. The Demurrer and Motion to Strike are set to be heard on April 29, 2024. Based on these dates, we are satisfied that all filings in the instant matter were timely filed.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to each cause of action on the grounds that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action under California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e); on the grounds that it is uncertain under California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f); and, as to the first cause of action, as on the grounds that it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract upon which the action is founded is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct under California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (g). (Demurrer pp. 3–4.)

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10, subd. (a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.) 

 

1.      The First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

 

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) “Further, the complaint must indicate on its face whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct.” (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458–59. For a written contract, the plaintiff may plead it “by its terms—set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference—or by its legal effect.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489, abrogated on other grounds in Freeman v. Quicken Loans Inc. (2012) 566 U.S. 624.) To plead a contract’s legal effect, the plaintiff’s pleading must include “the substance of its relevant terms.” (Ibid.)¿

 

Plaintiff pleads in the FAC that Parties entered “an employment agreement…, which included specific assurances regarding scheduling flexibility….” (FAC ¶ 1.) He pleads that Parties had an “initially agreed-upon flexible scheduling arrangements” but that Defendant later “breach[ed] the terms of their agreement.” (Id. at ¶ 3.) Plaintiff pleads that at the outset of his employment with Defendant, he “communicated his scheduling constraints due to concurrent employment commitments, to which the Defendant raised no objections,” but “[d]espite specific promises made by the Defendant regarding flexible scheduling to accommodate [Plaintiff’s] other employment, the Defendant failed to honor these commitments, potentially constituting a breach of the parties' employment agreement.” (Id. at ¶¶ 12–13.) Plaintiff alleges that “[d]espite initial assurances of a flexible work schedule, [Plaintiff] consistently faced scheduling issues, contradicting the agreed-upon terms.” (Id. at ¶ 21.) Plaintiff pleads that “[u]pon the commencement of the professional relationship with the Defendant, clear and unequivocal promises were made, constituting the foundation of our mutual employment contract. These commitments explicitly endorsed a scheduling arrangement tailored to accommodate my disclosed concurrent employment obligations, thereby establishing the contractual framework.” (Id. at ¶ 32.) Plaintiff further pleads that “Defendant consistently deviated from the agreed-upon scheduling flexibility, constituting a material breach of our contractual provision”; that “Defendant's failure to uphold the promised scheduling flexibility constitutes a violation not only of the explicit contract terms but also of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing inherent in [Parties’] contractual relationship”; and that “Defendant's actions, marked by a continued disregard for the agreed contract terms, have resulted in the erosion of [Plaintiff’s] contractual rights and inflicted substantial harm, constituting a direct and unequivocal breach of our contract.” (Id. at ¶¶ 33–35.)

 

It is unclear from these pleadings in the FAC whether the employment agreement that Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached is written, oral, or implied by conduct.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s first cause of action for breach of contract.

 

2.      The Second Cause of Action for Violations of Labor Laws

 

Plaintiff alleges violations of the Fair Labor Standards Act (“FLSA”) “and relevant state labor laws,” which Plaintiff specifically identifies to be California Labor Code section 512, subdivision (a) and California Labor Code section 510, subdivision (a). (FAC ¶¶ 38, 40, 42.) Plaintiff further alleges that “Defendant's ongoing negligence in maintaining a safe and adequately supervised work environment contravenes Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) standards outlined in the Occupational Safety and Health Act of 1970.” (Id. at ¶ 43.)

 

Defendant contends that the FAC’s failure to identify which specific standards of the FSLA and OSHA are alleged to have been violated makes the FAC fatally uncertain. (Motion p. 8.) Defendant further contends that the FAC lacks sufficient detail about allegedly withheld wages and allegedly unprovided meal periods for its allegations of violations of California Labor Code sections 510, subdivision (a) and 512, subdivision (a), respectively, to be certain. We agree that without specifying which provisions of the FSLA and OSHA Plaintiff is alleging Defendant violated and without additional factual background regarding alleged violations of California Labor Code sections 510, subdivision (a) and 512, subdivision (a), this cause of action is uncertain.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s second cause of action for violations of labor laws.

 

3.      The Third Cause of Action for False Representations

 

In ruling on a demurrer, a court should “ignore erroneous or confusing labels if the complaint pleads facts which would entitle the plaintiff to relief.” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 387.) Although Plaintiff labels the FAC’s third cause of action as “false representations,” we understand from context and his argument that he means to bring a cause of action for fraud, of which false representation may be the first element.

 

“The elements of fraud are (1) the defendant made a false representation as to a past or existing material fact; (2) the defendant knew the representation was false at the time it was made; (3) in making the representation, the defendant intended to deceive the plaintiff; (4) the plaintiff justifiably and reasonably relied on the representation; and (5) the plaintiff suffered resulting damages.” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank National Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 189, 199–200, citing Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.) “[F]raud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184, quoting Lazar v. Superior Court, supra.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Ibid.) “A plaintiff's burden in asserting a fraud claim against a corporate employer is even greater. In such a case, the plaintiff must allege the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.” (Lazar v. Superior Court, supra, at 645.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant is liable for false representation because it “engaged in a willful pattern of misrepresentation, either knowingly or negligently, by disseminating inaccurate information regarding travel distances for assignments.” (FAC p. 9.) The FAC includes no other specifics about the fraud or false representations that it alleges Defendant made. These allegations thus lack sufficient specificity to meet the heightened pleading requirements for fraud.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s third cause of action for fraud.

 

4.      The Fourth Cause of Action for Negligence

 

“The following elements must be pleaded to state a cause of action for negligence: (1) a legal duty of care toward the plaintiff; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) legal causation; and (4) damages.” (Century Surety Co. v. Crosby Insurance, Inc. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 116, 127.) “Whatever its measure in a given case, it is fundamental that “damages which are speculative, remote, imaginary, contingent, or merely possible cannot serve as a legal basis for recovery.” (Piscitelli v. Friedenberg (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 953, 989.)  “Damages may not be based upon sheer speculation or surmise, and the mere possibility or even probability that damage will result from wrongful conduct does not render it actionable.” (Ferguson v. Lieff, Cabraser, Heimann & Bernstein (2003) 30 Cal.4th 1037, 1048.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Defendant is liable for negligence because “Defendant's failure to establish an effective coordination system with other security teams demonstrates a profound disregard for established safety protocols and constitutes a severe level of negligence. This negligence resulted in an inherently unsecured and perilous working environment, escalating threats to my safety and overall well-being.” (FAC p. 11.) Plaintiff additionally alleges that Defendant’s actions “directly exposed [him] to potential danger and risks.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff does not allege any harm that has resulted from Plaintiff’s actions that is certain and present but, rather, alleges only harms that were possible. However, possible harms are not actionable as damages and Plaintiff therefore does not plead any damages for purposes of stating a cause of action for negligence.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s fourth cause of action for negligence.

 

5.      The Fifth Cause of Action for Failure to Respond to Emergencies

 

As we explain in our discussion above of the third cause of action, the erroneous label of a cause of action should be ignored if the intent and remedy sought is clear. (McBride v. Boughton, supra,123 Cal.App.4th at 387.) Although Plaintiff labels the FAC’s fifth cause of action as “failure to respond to emergencies,” which not a cognizable cause of action, we understand from context and his argument that he means to bring a cause of action for negligence.

 

As stated above, the elements for a cause of action for negligence are “(1) a legal duty of care toward the plaintiff; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) legal causation; and (4) damages.” (Century Surety Co. v. Crosby Insurance, Inc. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 116, 127.) “[D]amages for negligently inflicted emotional distress may be recovered in the absence of physical injury or impact, and [] a cause of action to recover damages for negligently inflicted emotional distress will lie… in cases where a duty arising from a preexisting relationship is negligently breached.” (Burgess v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1064, 1074.) However, where the nature of parties’ relationship does not involve protection from emotional or physical harm, the defendant’s negligence in discharging its duties cannot give rise to liability for emotional distress damages. (Gonzales v. Personal Storage, Inc. (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 464, 478.) Further, when the underlying wrongdoing is “merely negligent, rather than intentional,” a plaintiff who “incurring neither physical impact nor physical damage, and whose loss (other than emotional distress) is solely economic, is entitled neither to punitive damages nor to a recovery for emotional distress.” (Branch v. Homefed Bank (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 793, 799–800.)

 

The FAC alleges that Defendant failed “to provide appropriate, timely, and necessary support in response to urgent emergency calls” and describes an example “during a strenuous 15-hour shift wherein [Plaintiff] found [him]self abandoned without relief or any form of supportive assistance,” which is later described as “a situation fraught with risk and potential harm.” (FAC pp. 12–13.) Consequently, the FAC seeks “redress… for emotional distress, potential harm, and other consequential damages.” (Id. at p. 14.) The FAC emphasizes “the substantial burden that this negligence has placed on me, both physically and emotionally. The disregard for my safety and well-being by the Defendant has caused considerable stress and anxiety, contributing to an overall diminishment in my quality of life.” (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff thereby alleges emotional distress damages. These damages are based on his claim for negligence, and so he must additionally show physical damages in order to qualify for emotional distress damages. However, Plaintiff does not allege any other damages that are present, realized, and certain, but rather only alleges other harms that were “possible.” As noted above in our discussion of the fourth cause of action for damages, “the mere possibility or even probability that damage will result from wrongful conduct does not render it actionable.” (Ferguson v. Lieff, Cabraser, Heimann & Bernstein, supra, 30 Cal.4th at 1048.) Therefore, Plaintiff fails to allege any damages that will support his claim for emotional distress damages, and Plaintiff therefore fails to allege any damages.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s fifth cause of action for failure to respond to emergencies.

 

6.      The Sixth Cause of Action for Whistleblowing and Retaliation Protections

 

California Labor Code section 1102.5 is “California's general whistleblower statute.” (McVeigh v. Recology San Francisco (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 443, 468. “A claim for violation of Labor Code section 1102.5 requires (1) the plaintiff establish a prima facie case of retaliation, (2) the defendant provide a legitimate, nonretaliatory explanation for its acts, and (3) the plaintiff show this explanation is merely a pretext for the retaliation. To establish the first element, the plaintiff must show (1) the plaintiff engaged in protected activity, (2) the defendant subjected the plaintiff to an adverse employment action, and (3) there is a causal link between the two.” (Ross v. County of Riverside (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 580, 591–92, internal citations omitted.) “[California Labor Code section 1102.5] provides whistleblower protections to employees who disclose wrongdoing to authorities.” (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 709.) “[California Labor Code section] 1102.5, subdivision (b), concerns employees who report to public agencies. It does not protect [a] plaintiff[] who reported his suspicions directly to his employer.” (Green v. Ralee Engineering Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 66, 77.)

 

The FAC alleges that “[f]ollowing the reporting of issues to the Defendant, [Plaintiff] experienced implicit retaliation through further scheduling issues and inappropriate treatment, potentially infringing whistleblower protection laws.” (FAC p. 4.) The FAC later repeats that Plaintiff reported “Defendant’s violations,” but it does not allege to whom Plaintiff made such reports. (Id. at p. 14.) As Defendant is a private employer, a report of any violations of law made by Plaintiff to only Defendant was not protected activity within the meaning of California Labor Code section 1102.5. Absent an allegation that Plaintiff made any report “to a government or law enforcement agency, to a person with authority over the employee or another employee who has the authority to investigate, discover, or correct the violation or noncompliance,” the FAC therefore fails to plead all the elements of a cause of action for violation of California Labor Code section 1102.5. (Cal. Labor Code § 1102.5, subd. (b).)

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s sixth cause of action for whistleblowing and retaliation protections.

 

7.      The Seventh Cause of Action for Documenting and Reporting Violations

 

The FAC alleges that “Defendant's blatant disregard for properly documenting and timely reporting of labor law violations, non-compliance with wage and hour statutes, and neglect of health and safety norms constitutes a grave breach of key California employment laws and regulations”; that “Defendant's disregard for accurately documenting and promptly reporting labor law violations, wage and hour infractions, and breaches of health and safety standards amount to a gross violation of numerous pivotal California employment laws and regulations”; and that “Defendant's actions amount to serious transgressions of several employment laws and regulations.” (FAC pp. 17–18.) The FAC references “sections 226 and 6404 of the California Labor Code”; Brinker Restaurant Corp. v. Superior Court (2012) 53 Cal.4th 1004’s discussion of “state-mandated break periods,” which interprets Industrial Welfare Commission Wage Order No. 5; Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, which interprets California Labor Code section 2698, et seq., or the Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004; Thurman v. Bayshore Transit Management, Inc. (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 1112, which interprets California Labor Code section 512, which is now preempted by International Brotherhood of Teamsters, Local 2785 v. Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration (2021 9th Cir.) 986 F.3d 841, and Industrial Welfare Commission Wage Order No. 9.

 

California Labor Code section 226 requires employers to provide itemized wage statements to employees. (Cal. Labor Code § 226.) California Labor Code section 6404 states, “[n]o employer shall occupy or maintain any place of employment that is not safe and healthful.” (Cal. Labor Code § 6404.) Plaintiff does not allege that Defendant failed to provide him with any itemized wage statements, nor does he allege that the place of his employment with Defendant was ever not safe and healthful. Plaintiff therefore fails to state a cause of action for violation of either California Labor Code section 226 or California Labor Code section 6404.

 

Industrial Welfare Commission Wage Order No. 5 applies to only “persons employed in the public housekeeping industry” and is therefore not relevant to Plaintiff’s claims. Industrial Welfare Commission Wage Order No. 9 applies to only “persons employed in the transportation industry” and is similarly therefore not relevant to Plaintiff’s claims. Plaintiff does not bring any claims under the Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act of 2004, which is therefore not relevant to his claims.

 

The FAC alleges “several” or “numerous” violations by Plaintiff of “California employment laws and regulations,” but beyond those discussed immediately above, it is unclear which laws and regulations Plaintiff is alleging that Defendant violated.

 

Accordingly, this count SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the FAC’s seventh cause of action for documenting and reporting violations.

 

  1. The Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike a portion of the Complaint’s prayer for relief which seeks “reimbursement of the costs incurred in initiating and prosecuting this suit, inclusive of attorney’s fees and other associated legal costs.” (Motion to Strike p. 2.)

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. at § 437.) “[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Sup. Ct. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (b) provides, “[e]xcept as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1032, subd. (b).) “Attorney fees can be awarded as costs to the prevailing party when authorized by contract.” (R.P. Richards, Inc. v. Chartered Const. Corp. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 146, 158.) “A party may not recover attorney fees unless expressly authorized by statute or contract.” (Hom v. Petrou (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 459, 454.) Additionally, a “party who has not retained counsel… therefore cannot qualify for an award of attorney fees.” (Leiper v. Gallegos (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 284, 294.) This is true even of unrepresented litigants who are themselves attorneys. “[P]ro se litigants should not recover fees, regardless of their professional status.” (Trope v. Katz (1995) 11 Cal.4th 274, 286.)

 

The FAC does not allege that any contract between Parties authorizes the award of attorneys’ fees. Additionally, Plaintiff is currently unrepresented by an attorney and has been so since the initiation of this lawsuit. Therefore, he cannot qualify for an award of attorney’s fees.

 

Plaintiff may, however, seek to recover his costs, exclusive of attorneys’ fees, incurred by this lawsuit.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion to Strike as to only the words “attorney’s fees and” in the FAC’s prayer for relief.

 

  1. Leave to Amend

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245). The same is true following the granting of a motion to strike. “Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question. A pleading may be stricken only upon terms the court deems proper, that is, terms that are just. It is generally an abuse of discretion to deny leave to amend, because the drastic step of denial of the opportunity to correct the curable defect effectively terminates the pleader's action.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146–47.)

 

Much of the defects in the FAC are a result of too little detail and specific allegations. We observe that Plaintiff has only amended the Complaint once and find there is a reasonable possibility that Plaintiff could amend the FAC to add such needed detail and specific allegations.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS leave to amend.

I. BACKGROUND

On November 29, 2021, plaintiff Ira Mandel (“Plaintiff”) purchased a certain used vehicle (the “Vehicle”) from defendant Haute, LLC d/b/a Vegas Auto Gallery Lotus Cars Las Vegas (“Defendant”). (Complaint p. 2). In doing so, Parties entered a purchase agreement (the “Purchase Agreement”) and an arbitration agreement (the “Arbitration Agreement”). (Opposition pp. 1–2.) Plaintiff later learned that the Vehicle’s odometer had been “rolled back” so that it showed significantly lower mileage than it actually possessed. (Complaint, supra, at p. 3.) Plaintiff notified Defendant of the odometer “roll back” and demanded that Defendant rescind the purchase agreement, but Defendant refused. (Ibid.)

 

On November 28, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) intentional misrepresentation, (2) negligent misrepresentation, (3) violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200, et seq, and (4) violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act.

 

On March 29, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel arbitration (the “Motion”). On April 2, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion, and on April 18, 2024, Defendant replied to Plaintiff’s opposition.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract. (Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Inv. Brokerage Co. v. Hock Inv. Co. (1998) 68 Cal. App.4th 83, 88.) The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413; Provencio v. WMA Securities, Inc., 125 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1031.) This court is empowered by California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so. 

 

The court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that:

 

(a)    The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or

 

(b)   Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.

 

(c)    A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. For purposes of this section, a pending court action or special proceeding includes an action or proceeding initiated by the party refusing to arbitrate after the petition to compel arbitration has been filed, but on or before the date of the hearing on the petition. This subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.

 

(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)

 

Thus, “Section 1281.2 requires a court to order arbitration if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate exists.” (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841.)

 

California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971–72.) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.)

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

Defendant seeks an order compelling arbitration of all of Plaintiff’s claims and staying the action pending the outcome of arbitration. (Motion p. 2.)

 

  1. Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

When presented with a petition to compel arbitration, the trial court's first task is to determine whether the parties have in fact agreed to arbitrate the dispute. (Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Inv. Brokerage Co. v. Hock Inv. Co., supra, 68 Cal. App.4th at 88.) Arbitration remains fundamentally contractual, and “[t]he policy favoring arbitration cannot displace the necessity for a voluntary agreement to arbitrate.” (Avery v. Integrated Healthcare Holdings, Inc. (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 50, 59, emphasis original, internal quotation marks omitted.) “[A]bsent a clear agreement to submit disputes to arbitration, courts will not infer that the right to a jury trial has been waived.” (Adajar v. RWR Homes, Inc. (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 563, 569, internal citations and quotations omitted.)

 

“Several contracts relating to the same matters, between the same parties, and made as parts of substantially one transaction, are to be taken together.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 1642.) “[California] Civil Code section 1642 is most frequently applied to writings executed contemporaneously.” (R.W.L. Enterprises v. Oldcastle, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 1019, 1027.) “Whether a document is incorporated into the contract depends on the parties’ intent as it existed at the time of contracting. For the terms of another document to be incorporated into the document executed by the parties the reference must be clear and unequivocal. The contract need not recite that it incorporates another document, so long as it guides the reader to the incorporated document. To be construed together, the separate instruments must be so interrelated as to be considered one contract.” (Id. at 1027–28, emphasis omitted, internal citations omitted.)

 

“The parol evidence doctrine prohibits the introduction of extrinsic evidence to vary or contradict the terms of an integrated written instrument.” (Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Medical Center (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1379, 1385.) “Terms set forth in a writing intended by the parties as a final expression of their agreement with respect to the terms included therein may not be contradicted by evidence of a prior agreement or of a contemporaneous oral agreement.” (Cal. Civ. Proc. § 1856, subd. (a).) “In contract law, ‘integration’ means the extent to which a writing constitutes the parties' final expression of their agreement.” (Esbensen v. Userware Internat, Inc. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 631, 636.) “An integration may be partial, as well as complete; that is, the parties may intend that a writing finally and completely express certain terms of their agreement rather than the agreement in its entirety.” (Ibid.) “The crucial issue in determining whether there has been an integration is whether the parties intended their writing to serve as the exclusive embodiment of their agreement.” (Grey v. American Management Services (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 803, 807.) “The existence of an integration clause is a key factor in divining that intent,” although it is “not conclusive.” (Ibid.; Kanno v. Marwit Capital Partners II, L.P. (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 987, 1001.) “In determining [whether an agreement is integrated], the court must consider not only whether the written instrument contains an integration clause, but also examine the collateral agreement itself to determine whether it was intended to be a part of the bargain.” (Wagner v. Glendale Adventist Medical Center, supra at 1386.) “However, in determining the issue of integration, the collateral agreement will be examined only insofar as it does not directly contradict an express term of the written agreement; ‘it cannot reasonably be presumed that the parties intended to integrate two directly contradictory terms in the same agreement.’” (Ibid., quoting Gerlund v. Electronic Dispensers International (1987) 190 Cal.App.3d 263, 271.)

 

Plaintiff admits that “[a]t the time [he] signed the Purchase Agreement, [he] also signed an Arbitration Agreement.” (Opposition p. 2.) He contends, however, that disputes arising out of the Purchase Agreement are not subject to the terms of the Arbitration Agreement because the Purchase Agreement contains an integration clause and because it does not expressly incorporate the Arbitration Agreement. (Ibid.) Plaintiff’s opposition raises no other arguments against the enforcement of the Arbitration Agreement’s terms in this lawsuit. It is therefore undisputed that the Arbitration Agreement exists and that if the instant lawsuit is subject to it, it would mandate that this dispute be resolved by arbitration.

 

The integration clause of the Purchase Agreement reads,

 

[Plaintiff] agrees that this [Purchase] Agreement includes all of the terms and conditions on all pages of this [Purchase] Agreement hereof, that this [Purchase] Agreement cancels and supersedes any prior agreement including oral agreements, and as of the date below comprises; together with any retain installment sale contract the complete and exclusive statement of the terms of the agreement relating to the subject matter covered by this [Purchase] Agreement.

 

(Starr Decl. Exh. 1.)

 

The Purchase Agreement also includes terms which read, “[y]ou agree to sign such agreements or documents as we may require to effect the terms and conditions of payments shown in this [Purchase] Agreement”; and “[i]n the event that any of the terms and conditions of this [Purchase] Agreement are inconsistent with the terms and conditions of any other document signed as part of this transaction between [Plaintiff] and [Defendant], with the exception of the retail installment sales agreement, the terms of this [Purchase] Agreement shall apply.” (Ibid.)

 

The Purchase Agreement does not mention arbitration. Parties agree that the Purchase Agreement and the Arbitration Agreement were executed simultaneously. (Opposition p. 2; Reply p. 3.)

 

We find that the Purchase Agreement incorporates the Arbitration Agreement, and that the Arbitration Agreement is therefore enforceable in any dispute arising under the Purchase Agreement. First, we note that the parol evidence rule does not refer to contemporaneously executed written agreements but rather, refers to only prior agreements and contemporaneous oral agreements. The Purchase Agreement, by referring to additional “agreements or documents” “signed as part of this transaction,” is clear and unequivocal that additional agreements are to be considered part of the same instrument. The Purchase Agreement does not have to specifically name the Arbitration Agreement to “guide[] the reader to the incorporated document.” Thus, the Purchase Agreement and the Arbitration Agreement may be considered as a single instrument executed in two parts, and the integration clause of the Arbitration Agreement therefore covers both agreements, integrating both into a single set of transaction documents.

 

Even if, however, we do not consider the Purchase Agreement to incorporate the Arbitration Agreement, we still find that the Arbitration Agreement is enforceable in any dispute arising under the Purchase Agreement. While it does contain an integration clause, this is not determinative of the agreement being fully integrated. The integration clause only specifically “cancels and supersedes” prior agreements, not contemporaneous agreements, and the agreement later references contemporaneously executed agreements that are “required to effect the terms of” the Purchase Agreement. Finally, the Arbitration Agreement supplements but does not contradict the terms of the Purchase Agreement; the Arbitration Agreement adds terms that are not inconsistent with the Purchase Agreement terms. In such case, we therefore find that the Purchase Agreement is only partially integrated and that the Arbitration Agreement may supplement its terms.

 

Accordingly, we find that the Arbitration Agreement exists and that disputes arising under the Purchase Agreement are subject to it. We are therefore required to GRANT the Motion for arbitration pursuant to the terms of the Arbitration Agreement.

 

  1. Stay of Proceedings

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.4 provides in pertinent part,

 

If a court of competent jurisdiction, whether in this State or not, has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.

 

“[W]hen there is a severance of arbitrable from inarbitrable claims, the trial court has the discretion to stay proceedings on the inarbitrable claims pending resolution of the arbitration.” (Cruz v. PacifiCare Health Systems, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 303, 320.) “A stay is appropriate where in the absence of a stay, the continuation of the proceedings in the trial court disrupts the arbitration proceedings and can render them ineffective.” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hosp. v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 693.)  When a court grants a petition to compel arbitration, the court must also order a stay of pending litigation, while the related arbitration is proceeding and until the arbitration is resolved. (C.C.P. § 1281.4; Gaines v Fidelity Nat'l Title Ins. Co. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1081, 1091-1092; Thomas v Westlake (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 605, 620.   A stay is not automatic. (Dial 800 v Fesbinder (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 32, 45.) Accordingly, all proceedings are STAYED pending the outcome of arbitration. Defendants are hereby ORDERED to file and serve on Plaintiff a notice of the stay forthwith. (C.C.P. § 1281.4; Cal Rules of Ct 3.650(a), (b)(2).)
