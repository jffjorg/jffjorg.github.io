DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV13615    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

AVE I GROUP, LLC

                          

         vs.

 

EQUITY PARTNERS, LTD, et al.

 

 Case No.:  21STCV13615 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED. Excluding CAMs, commissions, and late fees, Plaintiff suffered damages in unpaid base rent of $306,384 for the breach of the Richie Lease and $107,639 for the Lee Lease.

 

On 4/9/2021, Plaintiff Ave I Group, LLC (Plaintiff) filed suit against Equity Partners, LTD and Christopher Lee, alleging: (1) breach of lease and (2) breach of guaranty of lease.

 

Defendants asserted a Cross-Complaint for: (1) Breach of Lease; and (2) Detrimental Reliance.

 

On 8/22/2023, the Court granted Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the issue of reformation, and Defendants/Cross-Complainants claim for detrimental reliance.

 

Now, Plaintiff moves for summary judgment, or alternatively, summary adjudication, of: (1) its cause of action for “Breach of Lease” against Richie P.C. and Equity LTD; (2) its claims for “Breach of Guaranty” against the guarantors of the foregoing Leases, Defendants/Cross-Complainants Darren Richie (Richie) Christopher Lee (Lee); and (3) Richie P.C., Richie, Equity LTD, and Lee’s (collectively “Defendants) causes of action for “Breach of Lease.

 

Factual Background

 

This action involves two companion leases (the Leases) in a brand new mixed-use property located at 219 Avenue I, Redondo Beach, CA 90277, called “The Oshens” (hereinafter, the Property). Both of the leases were negotiated at the same time by the same agents and brokers. The dispute concerns whether or not there it was mistake (whether unilateral or mutual) that the Lease for Unit 101 provided it was to commence the “earlier” of May 1, 2020, or the date that Plaintiff obtained a “Conditional Use Permit” from the City of Redondo Beach (the City) for the occupancy of the premises, instead of the “later” of May 1, 2020 or Lessor obtaining a Certificate of Occupancy (CofO), as provided in the Lease for Unit 103.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

CCP section 437c, subdivision (q) provides:

 

In granting or denying a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, the court need rule only on those objections to evidence that it deems material to its disposition of the motion. Objections to evidence that are not ruled on for purposes of the motion shall be preserved for appellate review.

 

            Based on CCP section 437c, subdivision (q), the Court declines to rule on the submitted objections.

 

Discussion

 

             Plaintiff argues that it is entitled to summary judgment because the Court’s order of reformation is binding and this leaves no disputed facts concerning Defendants’ breach of the Leases and Guarantees.

 

            On 8/22/2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication. As part of that ruling, the Court determined that the Lease for Unit 101 needed to be reformed to reflect the “Commencement Date” of the Lease as being May 1, 2020, or when Ave I obtained a CofO is now binding on the parties. As such, the Court ordered that the Lease for Unit 101 be reformed at Paragraph 1.3 as follows:

 

1.3 Term: five (5) years and zero (0) months (“Original Term”) commencing the later May 1, 2020 or Lessor obtaining Certificate of Occupancy for unit 103 at 219 Avenue I, Redondo Beach (“Commencement Date”) and ending … 5-years thereafter (“Expiration Date”)

 

The Court also agreed that Defendants’ second cause of action for “Detrimental Reliance,” which is asserted in their respective Cross-Complainants is meritless since Ave I made no promises that are inconsistent with the leases at issue in this action.

 

Based on this ruling, Plaintiff argues that the terms of the lease and guaranty are clear and plain and establish Defendants’ breach of the Leases and Guarantees.

 

A.    Breach of Contract Claim

 

The elements of Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim are as follows: (1) existence of the lease agreements; (2) Ave I’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) Richie P.C. and Equity LTD’s breach of their respective leases with Ave I; and (4) damages to Ave I as a result of the breach of the Leases. (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402; see also CACI 303.)

 

In order to establish its breach of contract claims, Plaintiff submitted the following evidence and argument:

 

-         It is undisputed that Ave I, on the one hand, and Richie P.C. and Equity LTD, on the other hand, entered into the companion Leases at issue in this action. (See UMF No. 7.)

 

-         The Leases provided that Ave I was required to “use its best commercially reasonable efforts to deliver possession of the Premises to Lessee by the Commencement Date.” (See Trial Exs. Nos. 37 & 38 to AOE, The Leases at ¶ 3.3.)

 

-          If in the event Ave I was unable to deliver possession of the Premises within 60 days after the Commencement Date, Richie P.C. and Equity LTD would have the right to cancel the Leases after providing Ave I 10 days’ notice after the end of the 60-day period following the Commencement Date. (See Trial Exs. Nos. 37 & 38 to AOE, Leases at ¶ 3.3.)

 

-         The Commencement Date for both Leases the later of May 1, 2020, or Lessor obtaining the CofO. (See UMF Nos. 11 & 30)

 

-         On October 2, 2020, which was prior to the “Commencement Date” of the Leases, Richie P.C. and Equity LTD provided notice of their unilateral termination of the Leases and demand for the return of their security deposit. (See UMF No. 10.)

 

-         Ave I obtained the CofO on November 12, 2020. (See UMF No. 14.)

 

-         Despite Richie P.C. and Equity LTD’s attempt to unilaterally terminate the Leases, Ave I provided Richie P.C. and Equity LTD “Notice of Commencement” of the Leases on November 17, 2020. (See UMF No. 15.)

 

-         In response to Ave I’s “Notice of Commencement” of the Leases, Richie P.C. and Equity LTD informed Ave I of their intention not to take possession of the Premises and of their intention not to pay rent under the Leases. (See UMF No. 16.)

 

-         Ave I was able to relet the Premises after Richie P.C. and Equity LTD stipulated to grant Ave I possession of the Premises in order to allow Ave I to mitigate its damages. (See Trial Ex. No. 62 to AOE, Fully Executed Stipulation for Possession of Units 101 and 103.)

 

-         As part of the parties’ stipulation, it was agreed that Ave I retained all of its rights and remedies under the Leases, including but not limited to its rights and remedies under Civil Code section 1951.2.

 

-         Ave I suffered damages due to Richie P.C.’s breach of the Lease for Unit 101 in the amount of $569.554.00. (See UMF No. 24.)

 

-         Ave I’s damages due to Equity LTD’s breach of the Lease for Unit 103 totals $202,213.00. (See UMF No. 29.)

 

-         Ave I’s damages is a simple matter of arithmetic as set forth in detail in the spreadsheets attached to the AOE as Exhibits “F” & “G,” and confirmed by Ave I’s managing member, Fred Mir. (See Mir Decl. at ¶¶ 14-16.)

 

Plaintiff’s evidence supports a reasonable inference that Defendants Richie P.C. and Equity LTD breached the lease at issue, and that Plaintiff suffered damages due to Defendants breach of the Leases. More specifically, Plaintiff submitted evidence that supports a reasonable inference that Plaintiff used its best commercially reasonable efforts to deliver of the Premises to Lessee by the Commencement Date, that the Commencement date was November 12, 2020 when it obtained the CofO, and that it had 60 days from November 12, 2020 to deliver possession of the premises. In the event, Plaintiff was unable to deliver possession of the Premises within 60 days after the Commencement Date, Defendants would have the right to cancel the Leases after providing Ave I 10 days’ notice after the end of the 60-day period following the Commencement Date. However, Defendants issued a notice of termination on October 2, 2020, prior to the Commencement Date. 

 

Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendants to disclose a triable issue of material fact.

 

In opposition, Defendants devote a considerable portion of their opposition to relitigating the reformation issue already decided by this Court in its previous ruling to Plaintiff’s MSA. Defendants did not seek reconsideration of that ruling at the time, and the Court declines to reconsider that ruling here based on contentions raised in an opposition to another motion. Accordingly, the Court limits its analysis to Defendants’ remaining contentions.

 

Defendants also contend that triable issues of material fact exist because: (1) Plaintiff’s evidence regarding the commencement date and possession of the property is flawed; and (2) Plaintiff’s damages calculations are flawed.

 

As to the first contention, Defendants argue that “Ave I’s evidence is improperly authenticated and unexecuted and does not establish any permanent ability for Defendants to occupy and possess the Units.” (Opp., 7: 15-16.) In particular, Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot authenticate the unexecuted Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCOO) submitted to show that the Leases commenced and Defendants were in breach. However, first, the CofO is authenticated by Fred Mir, the managing member of Ave I, who explains in his declaration that he was directly involved in seeking to secure the CofO. Second, and importantly, even setting this aside, Plaintiff’s evidence still establishes an anticipatory breach prior to the TCOO being issued. As such, Plaintiff’s evidence still supports a reasonable inference of breach, regardless of whether or not the CofO is authenticated. (See Reply, 9: 3-10:11.) 

 

Defendants also argue a lease “Commencement Date” based on issuance of the CofO is illusory. However, specifying the “Commencement Date” of the Leases to be the “later” of May 1, 2020, or issuance of the CofO does not grant Plaintiff an unfettered or arbitrary right to terminate the Leases. To the contrary, by specifying the “Commencement Date” in this manner, Ave I undertook an express, if not implied, duty to exercise good faith in securing the CofO. (See California Lettuce Growers v. Union Sugar Co. (1955) 45 Cal.2d 474, 484 [“[W]here a contract confers on one party a discretionary power affecting the rights of the other, a duty is imposed to exercise that discretion in good faith and in accordance with fair dealing.”].) Plaintiff submitted evidence that shows that it worked to secure the CofO for the property, and the delay in the issuance of the CofO was because the City of Redondo Beach effectively closed down during the pandemic. (See Mir Decl. at ¶ 6.) Defendants did not submit any evidence that could support a reasonable inference that Plaintiff did not act in good faith to secure the CofO. Moreover, while Defendants argue that the “Commencement Date” nullifies the “time is of the essence provision,” such provisions generally imply that any delays or failures to meet deadlines are considered material breaches. (See Gold Mining & Water Co. v. Swinerton (1943) 23 Cal.2d 19, 27.) However, when a commencement date, such as here, is dependent on obtaining a CofO, a time of the essence clause remains fully effective since it would be triggered by the issuance of the CofO.

 

            As to the second contention, Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s damages are uncertain in the following ways: (1) “Ave I’s evidence prevents Defendants from adequately challenging their duty to mitigate damages and from accurately calculating any purported damages.” (Opp., 8: 13-14.) For example, to show their mitigation of damages, Plaintiff submitted copies of leases for Units 100 and 102, yet Defendants were to lease units 101 and 103; and (2) Plaintiff’s additional CAM charges are improper, because Defendants’ total rent already included those charges, and that the late charges clause is invalid.

 

            However, as to the first point, Defendants note that while the numbering of the units at issue has changed, the actual physical units remain the same. In other words, the lease for “Units 100 and 102” are, in fact, the same physical premises as those in Defendants’ leases, the numbering of the units has just been changed. (See Exh. G to AOE.)

           

            As to the second point, the Court agrees.

 

A liquidated damages clause becomes an unenforceable penalty ‘if it bears no reasonable relationship to the range of actual damages that the parties could have anticipated would flow from a breach.” (Purcell v. Schweitzer (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 969, 974. “[A litigant seeking the benefits of a clause purporting to fix liquidated damages must plead and prove that the clause is valid under the facts which then existed.”]. (Id., at 975.)

 

Here, the Court agrees that Plaintiff cannot establish that the “Late Charges” provisions have any reasonable relationship to the damages it incurred, since it merely calculated those damages at 10% of rent for both Richie Litigation and Equity Partners. (Trial Ex. 37 at p. 37-12, § 13.4; Trial Ex. 38, at p. 38-12, § 13.4.)

 

Moreover, the Court finds the CAM charges to be improper. This is because Plaintiff has not adequately shown that the damages are not duplicative. In reply, Plaintiff contends that the lease expressly provides for the recovery of CAMs. However, Defendants argument isn’t that CAM damages aren’t recoverable, but that Defendants’ total rent already reflects those charges.  Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff has not met its burden to show that those costs are recoverable.

 

As such, the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to prevail on this claim, but the damages calculations are to be adjusted to remove the duplicative CAM charges, commissions, and the late charges. However, this damages amount is certain. Excusing CAMs, commissions, and late fees, Ave I suffered damages in unpaid base rent of $306,384 for the breach of the Richie Lease and $107,639 for the Lee Lease. (See Supp. Mir Decl. at ¶ 5.)

 

B.    Breach of Guaranty

 

As for breach of guaranty, the elements are as follows: (1) there is a guaranty of the Lease, (2) the tenant or tenants defaulted on the Lease, and (3) the guarantor of the Lease failed to perform under the guaranty. (Gray1 CPB, LLC v. Kolokotronis (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 480, 486; Central Building, LLC v. Cooper (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 1053, 1058-1064; Civ. Code 2807 [“A surety who has assumed liability for payment or performance is liable to the creditor immediately upon the default of the principal, and without demand or notice.”].)

 

In order to establish its breach of guaranty argument, Plaintiff submitted the following evidence and argument:

 

-         It is undisputed that Richie personally guaranteed the Richie P.C. Lease and Lee personally guaranteed the Equity LTD Lease. (See UMF Nos. 31 & 32.)

 

-         Tenant failed to perform its obligations under the Lease by failing to pay rent owed under the Lease.

 

-         Guarantor failed to make good on the obligations of the Lease as required by the Guaranty. (See UMF Nos. 33 & 34.)

 

-         Ave I is entitled to damages in the amount of $569.554.00 personally against Richie and $202,213.00 personally against Lee for their breach of the personal guarantees at issue.

 

Plaintiff’s evidence supports a reasonable inference that Defendants breached the guarantee, and that Plaintiff is entitled to damages in the amount of $569.554.00 personally against Richie and $202,213.00 personally against Lee for their breach of the personal guarantees at issue. More specifically, Plaintiff submitted evidence which shows that Richie personally  guaranteed the Richie P.C. Lease and Lee personally guaranteed the Equity LTD Lease, and submitted evidence which, as set forth above, supports a reasonable inference that Defendants breached the leases. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendants to disclose a triable issue of material fact.

 

In opposition, Defendants did not submit any evidence specific to this claim. Accordingly, the ruling set forth above applies equally here.

 

C.    Cross-Complaints

 

Defendants filed Cross-Complaints asserting causes of action for “Breach of Lease” and “Detrimental Reliance.” In its August 22, 2023 Order, the Court previously adjudicated Defendants causes of action for “Detrimental Reliance” finding they lacked merit. This leaves Defendants sole and remaining causes of action for “Breach of Lease.”

 

Under these claims, Defendants allege that Plaintiff breached the Leases by failing to obtain the CofO until November 2020, which Defendants assert was “too late” and in violation of Paragraph 13.6(a) of the Leases and the language of the Leases specifying that “time is of the essence.” (See Richie Defendant Cross-Complaint at ¶¶ 9, 12-18, 26; Equity Defendant Cross-Complainants at ¶ 9-14, 21.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant cannot establish this claim because these causes of action ignore the express language of the Leases which provided that Ave I was required to deliver possession of the Premises on the “Commencement Date,” which has been established at the later of May 1, 2020, or issuance of the CofO. (See Leases at ¶ 2.2.) As such, by the express terms of the Leases, Plaintiff could not be in breach of the Lease agreements prior to the defined “Commencement Date,” which ultimately was established by the issuance of the CofO for the Property.

 

Plaintiff further argues that Defendants’ reliance upon 13.6 of the Lease is misplaced. This provision provides as follows:

 

 13.6 Breach by Lessor (a) Notice of Breach. Lessor shall not be deemed in breach of this Lease unless Lessor fails within a reasonable time to perform an obligation required to be performed by Lessor. For purposes of this Paragraph, a reasonable time shall in no event be less than 30 days after receipt by Lessor, and any Lender whose name and address shall have been furnished to Lessee in writing for such purpose, of written notice specifying wherein such obligation of Lessor has not been performed; provided however, that if the nature of Lessor’s obligation is such that more than 30 days are reasonably required for its performance, then Lessor shall not be in breach if performance is commenced within such 30 day period and thereafter diligently pursued to completion.

 

(b) Performance by Lessee on Behalf of Lessor. In the event that neither Lessor nor Lender cures said breach within 30 days after receipt of said notice, or having commenced said cure they do not diligently pursue it to completion, then Lessee may elect to cure said breach at Lessee’s expense and offset from Rent the actual and reasonable cost to perform such cure, provided however, that such offset shall not exceed an amount equal to the greater of one month’s Base Rent/TOTAL RENT or the Security Deposit, reserving Lessee’s right to reimbursement from Lessor for any such expense in excess of such offset. Lessee shall document the cost of said cure and supply said documentation to Lessor.

 

            Plaintiff argues that this provision has no application since it was not in breach of the Leases because it was only required to deliver possession of the Premises after the “Commencement Date,” which was the later of May 1, 2020, or issuance of the CofO. As for the latter event (i.e., issuance of the CofO), the Leases do not specify a specific date for their issuance as it was understood that the timing of the issuance of the CofO was up to the City. The “Commencement Date” of the Leases was established by the City’s issuance of the CofO, which was issued on November 12, 2020.  As such, they could not be in breach of lease when Defendants provided unilateral notice of termination on October 2, 2020 because the Leases did not commence until November 12, 2020, which is the date the City issued the CofO for the Property.

 

            Plaintiff’s evidence supports a reasonable inference that Defendants cannot establish their breach of lease claims. More specifically, Plaintiff’s claims supports a reasonable inference that they were not in breach of the lease because Plaintiff was only required to deliver possession of the Premises after the “Commencement Date,” which was the later of May 1, 2020, or issuance of the CofO. The Leases do not specify a specific date for the issuance of the CofO as it was understood that the timing of the issuance of the CofO was up to the City, and Plaintiff used its best commercially reasonable efforts to deliver of the Premises to Lessee by the Commencement Date.

 

            Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendants to disclose a triable issue of material fact.

 

            In opposition, Defendants relied on the same evidence set forth above. For the same reasons, the Court finds no triable issue as to any issue.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is granted. Excluding CAMs, commissions, and late fees, Plaintiff suffered damages in unpaid base rent of $306,384 for the breach of the Richie Lease and $107,639 for the Lee Lease.

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: 23STCV17642    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JUAN M CUARENTA 

 

 

         vs.

 

CLAUDIA MIZUTANI et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV17642  

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is GRANTED.

 

            On 7/27/2023, Plaintiff Juan M. Cuarenta (Plaintiff), representing himself in propria persona filed suit against Claudia Mizutani (Defendant), alleging: (1) slander of title; (2) cancellation of instrument; (3) constructive trust; and (4) declaratory relief.

 

            Now, Defendant moves for a judgment on the pleadings.

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s motion is barred by res judicata/collateral estoppel.

 

The doctrine of res judicata precludes the re-litigation of certain matters which have been resolved in a prior proceeding under certain circumstances. (Brinton v. Bankers Pension Services, Inc. (1999) 76 Cal.App.4th 550, 556.) “Res judicata, or claim preclusion, prevents relitigation of the same cause of action in a second suit between the same parties or parties in privity with them.” (Mycogen Corp. v. Monsanto Co. (2002) 28 Cal.4th 888, 896.) “Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, precludes relitigation of issues argued and decided in prior proceedings.” (Ibid.)  

 

“Issue preclusion prohibits the relitigation of issues argued and decided in a previous case, even if the second suit raises different causes of action.”  (DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber (2015) 61 Cal.4th 813, 824.)  “Under issue preclusion, the prior judgment conclusively resolves an issue actually litigated and determined in the first action.”  (Id.)  “[I]ssue preclusion applies: (1) after final adjudication (2) of an identical issue (3) actually litigated and necessarily decided in the first suit and (4) asserted against one who was a party in the first suit or one in privity with that party.”  (Id. at 825.) 

 

            On 8/12/2020, Defendant here filed a separate action in Case No. 20STCV30584 against Plaintiff in this case, involving real property located at 6533 San Marcus Street Paramount, California 90723 (Subject Property). The causes of action asserted were for: (1) partition; (2) quiet title; (3) breach of fiduciary duty; (4) negligence; (5) cancellation of instrument; and (6) resulting trust.

 

After a bench trial, judgment was entered in favor of Defendant on all causes of action and Defendant and Plaintiff were each determined to be 50% owners of the Subject Property.

 

            Now, in pro per Plaintiff in this case, has filed a lawsuit alleging that he is the true owner of the property, and that Defendant improperly obtained title to the property with Plaintiff’s consent.

 

            After review, the Court agrees that Plaintiff’s claim is barred by collateral estoppel. First, the issue here is identical to that in the former proceeding, i.e., ownership of the Subject Property; (2) the issue was actually, and necessarily, litigated on the merits in the prior proceeding; and (2) that decision is final and on the merits. (DKN Holdings LLC, supra, 61 Cal.4th at p. 824.)

 

            Plaintiff’s non-opposition to the motion is considered a concession on the merits.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is granted.

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: BC510798    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

THE BEST SERVICE CO., INC.

 

         vs.

 

CUAUHTEMOC TRUJILLO

 

 Case No.:  BC510798

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: May 3, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion to quash service of Summons and Complaint is GRANTED.

 

On 6/3/2013, the Best Service Co. Inc. (Plaintiff) filed suit against Cuauhtemoc Trujillo (Defendant), alleging: (1) breach of contract; and (2) common counts.

 

Now, Defendant moves to quash service of Summons and Complaint.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that service of the Summons and Complaint must be quashed due to lack of personal jurisdiction for failure to serve Summons and Complaint.

 

 “A defendant . . . may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes: (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her. . . .”  CCP § 418.10(a).  A court lacks jurisdiction over a party if there has not been proper service of process. See Ruttenberg v. Ruttenberg (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 801, 808.    

 

“[C]ompliance with the statutory procedures for service of process is essential to establish personal jurisdiction. [Citation.]” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1444.) “[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that the service was proper” but only if it “complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.” (Id. at 1441-1442.) When a defendant moves to quash service of the summons and complaint, the plaintiff has “the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) “A court lacks jurisdiction over a party if there has not been proper service of process.” (See Ruttenberg v. Ruttenberg (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 801, 808.) 

 

Here, the Proof of Service states that service was effected on November 17, 2023 by leaving copies thereof with Rosa Mendoza at 1226 S. Record Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90023.

 

Defendant submitted evidence that his mother is Rosa Mendoza, and she resides at 1226 S. Record Street. (Trujillo Decl. ¶ 3.) Defendant submitted evidence that he is 48 years old, and has not resided with his mother since 2000, prior to being married. (Id. ¶  4.) He stated that “At no time since the filing of plaintiff's Complaint in this 10 year old case have I resided at 1226 S. Record. My wife and I have four children. I live with my wife and children in our own residence, not with my mother, and not at 1226 S. Record.” (Id.)

 

            Defendant further states:

 

Prior to, on and after June 9, 2013, I resided with my wife and children at 4835 Genevieve Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90041 ("4835 Genevieve"), which is in the Eagle Rock community of Los Angeles. I, my wife and children all received our mail at our residence at 4835 Genevieve. My children attended the local schools in Eagle Rock. My oldest daughter graduated Eagle Rock High School in 2019. My wife, all of my children and I have always resided together, but never at 1226 S. Record. At this time, we no longer reside at the foregoing residence at 4835 Genevieve.

 

            (Id. ¶ 5.)

 

            Defendant’s wife also submitted a declaration asserting the same. (See Valencia Decl.)

 

CCP section 415.20 provides:

                       

(a) In lieu of personal delivery of a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served as specified in Section 416.10, 416.20, 416.30, 416.40, or 416.50, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint during usual office hours in his or her office or, if no physical address is known, at his or her usual mailing address, other than a United States Postal Service post office box, with the person who is apparently in charge thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left. When service is effected by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at a mailing address, it shall be left with a person at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.

(b) If a copy of the summons and complaint cannot with reasonable diligence be personally delivered to the person to be served, as specified in Section 416.60, 416.70, 416.80, or 416.90, a summons may be served by leaving a copy of the summons and complaint at the person’s dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, in the presence of a competent member of the household or a person apparently in charge of his or her office, place of business, or usual mailing address other than a United States Postal Service post office box, at least 18 years of age, who shall be informed of the contents thereof, and by thereafter mailing a copy of the summons and of the complaint by first-class mail, postage prepaid to the person to be served at the place where a copy of the summons and complaint were left. Service of a summons in this manner is deemed complete on the 10th day after the mailing.

 

Here, Plaintiff has not submitted any evidence to indicate that Defendant was served at his dwelling house, usual place of abode, usual place of business, or usual mailing address. Moreover, Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition. Accordingly, the preponderance of evidence indicates that service was defective.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to quash service of summons and Complaint is granted. Given that this motion could have been avoided by meeting and conferring with Plaintiff regarding service, the Court urges the parties to attempt informal resolution of issues moving forward. 

 

It is so ordered.
