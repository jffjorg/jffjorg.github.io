DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02962    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Harris Davis,

 

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

Norms, et al., 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   23VECV02962               107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

  Motion for Summary Judgment

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:  Granted

 

Defendant Norms Restaurants, LLC moves for summary judgment. This court grants the motion. 

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On or about October 25, 2021, Plaintiff Harris Davis (“Plaintiff”) sustained injuries after he slipped and fell on Defendant’s property located at or near 13636 Sherman Way, Van Nuys, CA 91405 (the “Subject Property”). (Complaint ¶¶ 7, 11.) On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Norms, Norms Restaurants, LLC, and Does 1-100, alleging a single cause of action for premises liability – negligence.

 

On May 28, 2024, Defendant Norms Restaurants, LLC (“Defendant”) filed a motion to deem its Requests for Admission (“RFAs”), Set One, admitted. (Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted p. 7.) On July 16, 2024, this court granted Defendant’s motion. (07/16/24 Minute Order.)

 

On August 13, 2024, Defenant filed a motion for summary judgment (the “Motion”). Plaintiff filed no opposition.

 

II.              JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of:

 

1.     Plaintiff's Complaint, filed on July 10, 2023. (Ex. A.)

 

2.     Defendant's Motion to Deem Request for Admission (Set 1) Admitted filed on May 28, 2024. (Ex. C.)

 

3.     Defendant's Notice of Non-Opposition filed on June 14, 2024. (Ex. D.)

 

4.     The Court's July 16, 2024, Order granting Defendant's Motion to Deem Request for Admission (Set One) Admitted. (Ex. E.)

 

5.     Defendant's Request for Admission (Set One), served on Plaintiff on October 18, 2023, which were deemed admitted pursuant to the Court's ruling on July 16, 2024. (Ex. B.)

 

The court need not take judicial notice of documents that are part of the case file. Accordingly, this court denies Defendant’s requests for judicial notice.

 

III.            LEGAL STANDARD

 

A.    Summary Judgment 

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in¿an¿action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c(a)(1).) The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure §¿437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿”There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 843.)

¿¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the moving party must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c(p)(2).) “A cause of action ‘cannot be established’ if the undisputed facts presented by the defendant prove the contrary of the plaintiff's allegations¿as a matter of law.” (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1597.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the moving party has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c(p)(2).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 162-163.)

 

B.    Requests for Admission

 

“A deemed admitted order establishes, by judicial fiat, that a nonresponding party has responded to the requests by admitting the truth of all matters contained therein.” (Inzunza v. Naranjo (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 736, 742.) “Any matter deemed to have been admitted ‘is conclusively established against the party making the admission’ but ‘is binding only on the party’ that made the admission.” (Id.) A “court’s order deeming the requested admissions to be admitted is . . . conclusive and may not be ‘contested through contradictory evidence.” (People v. $2,709 United States Currency (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 1278, 1286.)

 

C.    Premises Liability

 

A cause of action for premises liability requires showing: (1) duty; (2) breach; (3) causation; and (4) damages. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (“Ortega”) (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.)

 

IV.            DISCUSSION

 

Defendant argues summary judgment should be granted in its favor because this court granted its motion to deem its RFAs admitted as to Plaintiff. (Motion p. 4.)  And, therefore, “Plaintiff admitted that there was no dangerous condition on the premises on October 25, 2021, when the incident occurred and that Norms was not negligent with respect to the subject incident” and “Plaintiff admitted he was not paying attention when he tripped at the premises, and that the mat he allegedly tripped over . . . did not create a tripping hazard. (Motion p. 7.)

 

A.    Dangerous Condition

 

A cause of action for premises liability requires showing a dangerous condition existed and that the defendant had actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition. (See Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1206; Caloroso v. Hathaway (2004) 122 Cal.App.922, 926-927.)

 

Defendant submits its RFA No. 1, which states: “Admit that there was no dangerous condition at the PREMISES on October 25, 2021, when the INCIDENT occurred. (For purposes of these requests, the term “INCIDENT” includes the circumstances and events surrounding the alleged accident, injury, or other occurrence giving rise to this action or lawsuit; the term “PREMISES” refers to the location of the INCIDENT – Norms Restaurant located at 13636 Sherman Way, Van Nuys, CA 91405.)” (Ex. B p. 2.) RFA No. 1 was deemed admitted as to Plaintiff by this court’s order. (07/16/24 Minute Order; Defendant’s Motion to Deem RFA’s Admitted p. 7, Ex. A p. 2.) Accordingly, Defendant has met its burden of showing there was no dangerous condition at the Subject Property when the incident occurred.

 

Plaintiff submits no evidence supporting his burden of showing a dangerous condition existed at the Subject Property. Moreover, a “court’s order deeming the requested admissions to be admitted is . . . conclusive and may not be ‘contested through contradictory evidence.” (People v. $2,709 United States Currency, supra, 231 Cal.App.4th at p. 1286.)

 

Accordingly, there is no triable issue of material fact as to whether a dangerous condition existed on the Subject Property when the incident occurred.

 

B.    Negligence

 

“It is well established in California that although a store owner is not an insurer of the safety of its patrons, the owner does owe them a duty to exercise reasonable care in keeping the premises reasonably safe.” (Ortega, supra, 26 Cal.4th at p. 1205.) “In order to establish liability on a negligence theory, a plaintiff must prove duty, breach, causation and damages.” (Id.)

 

Defendant submits its RFA No. 2, which states: “Admit that NORMS RESTAURUANT, LLC was not negligent with respect to the subject INCIDENT.” (Ex. B. p. 2.) RFA No. 2 was deemed admitted as to Plaintiff by this court’s order. (07/16/24 Minute Order; Defendant’s Motion to Deem RFA’s Admitted p. 7, Ex. A p. 2.) Accordingly, Defendant has met its burden of showing Defendant was not negligent with respect to the incident.

 

Plaintiff submits no evidence supporting his burden of showing Defendant was negligent. Moreover, a “court’s order deeming the requested admissions to be admitted is . . . conclusive and may not be ‘contested through contradictory evidence.” (People v. $2,709 United States Currency, supra, 231 Cal.App.4th at p. 1286.)

 

Accordingly, there is no triable issue of material fact as to whether Defendant was negligent.

 

V.              SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

This court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend.

 

I.                BACKGROUND¿ 

 

On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff Marilyn Freeman (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant EZ Storage of Van Nuys and Does 1 to 100 (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging causes of action for breach of contract, fraud, and intentional infliction of emotional distress.

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint alleging causes of action against Defendants for breach of contract, fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligence, and negligent infliction of emotional distress. 

On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint alleging causes of action against Defendants for breach of contract, fraud, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and general negligence and negligent infliction of emotional distress. (Second Amended Complaint “SAC”.)

On September 20, 2024, Defendant EZ Storage of Van Nuys (“Defendant”) demurred to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint. No opposition has been filed.

II.             PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENTS

 

  1. Timeliness

“A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40(a).)

The parties stipulated to Defendant filing a responsive pleading by September 20, 2024. (09/16/24 Minute Order; Gasparian Declaration ¶ 6.) Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer is timely.

  1. Meet and Confer

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer.¿The demurring party is required to file and serve with the demurrer a declaration either confirming that parties were unable to resolve the issues raised by the demurrer despite having met and conferred or that the opposing party failed to meet and confer with the demurring party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41(a)(3).)¿

 

Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating counsel met and conferred with Plaintiff and “Plaintiff maintained her position that the SAC was sufficiently plead and that she will leave it to the court to make a determination.” (Gasparian Declaration ¶ 7.) Defendant’s counsel also sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff outlining the bases for Defendant’s demurrer and motion to strike. (Gasparian Declaration ¶ 8.)

 

This court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

A party may demur to a complaint because the complaint “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).) A party may also demur to a complaint because “[t]he pleading is uncertain.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “’[U]ncertain’ includes ambiguous and unintelligible.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)

 

Demurrer for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Against a demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to “set forth in [her] complaint the essential facts of [her] case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of [her] cause of action.” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co. (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 841, 858.)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION¿ 

 

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s first, second, and third causes of action.

 

  1. Breach of Contract

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s second amended complaint fails to state a claim for breach of contract. (Demurrer p. 10.)

 

The elements of a breach of contract claim are: (1) existence of a contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) resulting damage. (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N. Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

 

Plaintiff alleges the existence of a contract by alleging that “[i]n or about December 30, 2015, Plaintiff Marilyn Freeman entered into a contract with Defendant EZ Storage of Van Nuys, LP and Does 1 to 100 regarding Plaintiff’s rental of Storage Unit #140 which is located at 15330 Hattaras Street, Van Nuys, CA 91411.” (SAC p. 6.)

 

Plaintiff alleges she performed under the contract or she had an excuse for her nonperformance by alleging “[e]ach month, plaintiff provided to defendant(s) a letter stating that she was excused from said rental payments pursuant to the city, county and state regulations put into effect during the Covid-19 pandemic and because she was ill due to Covid-19” and “[o]ne such law was Article 14.05 of the City of Los Angeles Municipal Code: Temporary Protection of Occupants of Self-Storage Facilities During the Covid-19 Pandemic.” (SAC p. 6.) Plaintiff also alleges “Plaintiff has performed all obligations to defendant except those obligations plaintiff was prevented or excused form performing.” (Complaint p. 10.)

 

Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached the covenant of good faith and fair dealing by letting “Plaintiff’s storage unit become infested with mice, failed to notify Plaintiff that there could possibly be mice in her storage unit and sought to cover up the presence of said mice/rodents.” Plaintiff also alleges “Defendant pressured Plaintiff to pay rent despite the Covid-19 rent moratorium on self-storage units[,] Defendant sought to sell Plaintiff’s possessions three times in 2022[, and] Defendant did this in violation of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing.” (SAC p. 9.)

 

Plaintiff further alleges Defendant breached the covenant to abide by laws of the governing jurisdictions by alleging Defendant “did not follow the laws of the City of Los Angeles.” (Complaint p. 10.) Plaintiff further alleges “Defendant did not follow Article 14.05 Of the City of Los Angeles Municipal Code . . . Defendant repeatedly attempted to collect money that Plaintiff was temporarily excused from paying, locked Plaintiff out of her storage locker, disabled Plaintiff’s gate code so she could not open the gate to freely access the premises and repeatedly instituted proceedings to sell her possessions in violation of Article 14.05 and in violation of the contract/rental agreement.” (Complaint p. 10.)

 

Plaintiff also alleges that “[a]t some point in 2021, defendants put their padlock on plaintiff’s storage unit so plaintiff could not access her possessions” and [t]his padlock was on Plaintiff’s storage locker throughout most of 2021. Plaintiff’s code no longer worked to open the entrance gate to the facility. Plaintiff was locked out of her storage unit and was deprived of the use and enjoyment of her possessions . . . Defendant did not respond to plaintiff’s demands that she be allowed access to her possessions.” (SAC p. 7.)

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges damages including $300,000.00 for replacement of damaged and loss possessions, damages in excess of $400,000 for each violation of Article 14.05, and $350,000 in punitive damages.

 

Defendant argues the Occupancy Agreement (the “Agreement”) shows that Plaintiff stated she stored less than $7,500.00 worth of goods in the storage unit, she is solely responsible for the goods, and the Agreement states that the “[o]wner has no duty of care, custody or control over, nor management of, nor responsibility for, nor knowledge of such goods.” (Demurrer p. 5.)  Defendant also states the Agreement states the owner is not responsible or liable for loss or damage to the property “from any cause whatsoever.” (Demurrer p. 5.) Defendant argues “Plaintiff’s outrageously high claims of property loss is directly inconsistent with the limited value of property specifically provided in the Agreement and bargained for component of the low monthly rent in exchange for releasing the Owner from liability; coupled with Plaintiff’s election to not obtain insurance Plaintiff assumed the risk of potential damage to her property and cannot now allege deliberate fraudulent conduct on the part of Defendant.” (Demurrer p. 6.) The Agreement is attached to Plaintiff’s second amended complaint as Exhibit A. This court finds that to the extent there is a dispute over the amount of Plaintiff’s damages, that is not the proper subject of a demurrer.

 

Defendant further argues that Article 14.05 was repealed on June 23, 2022, and “[a]ny communications by Owner regarding payment of rent began in the summer of 2022 and Notices of Lien Sale were sent in August and October 2022, after Article 14.05 was repealed and Plaintiff acknowledges that the lien sales did not actually proceed.” (Demurrer p. 6.) However, Plaintiff alleges Defendant put a padlock on her storage unit in 2021, which was before Plaintiff alleges Article 14.05 was repealed.

 

Finally, Defendant argues that Plaintiff improperly alleges a breach of the covenant of habitability. The covenant of habitability applies to residential leases. (See Green v. Superior Court (1974) 10 Cal.3d 616, 629 [“For the reasons discussed at length above, we believe that the traditional common law rule has outlived its usefulness; we agree with the Hinson court’s determination that modern conditions compel the recognition of a common law implied warranty of habitability in residential leases.”]) Here, the Agreement provided that “[t]he Space [was] to be used for storage only.” (Ex. A. p. 2.) Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendant breached the covenant of habitability are inapplicable here.

 

Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a breach of contract cause of action by alleging the existence of a contract, Plaintiff’s performance or excuse for non-performance, Defendant’s breach of the contract by placing a padlock on Plaintiff’s storage unit in 2021, and damages.

 

  1. Fraud

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s second amended complaint fails to state a claim for fraud. (Demurrer p. 11.)

 

The elements of fraud are: (1) a misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent to defraud; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. (Beckwith v. Dahl (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1060.) Fraud elements must be alleged with particularity. (Id.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which ‘show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. v. Dana Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 979, 993.)

 

Plaintiff alleges Defendant made a misrepresentation, by alleging Defendant represented it “would keep [the] premises clean, safe and mice-free, rodent-free and pest-free” but “[t]hese representations were in fact false.” (SAC p. 13.)  Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant had knowledge of its falsity because, when Defendant made the representation, “defendant knew they were false.”  (SAC p. 11.) Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant had an intent to defraud, alleging “Defendant made the representations with the intent to defraud and induce plaintiff to act as described[.]” (SAC p. 11.) Plaintiff alleges she justifiably relied on Defendant’s misrepresentation by alleging “[d]ue to Defendant’s fraud and fraudulent representations, Plaintiff was induced to enter into the rental agreement, to pay rent on the storage unit and to store her possessions there.” (SAC p. 13.) Finally, Plaintiff alleges resulting damages, alleging that “Plaintiff has suffered the loss of her personal property in the amount of $300,000.00. (SAC p. 13.)

 

However, Plaintiff’s allegations are not plead with particularity. Plaintiff alleges Defendant made a misrepresentation, but she does not allege how, when, where, and by what means.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged a fraud cause of action.

 

  1. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s second amended complaint fails to state a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. (Demurrer p. 14.)

 

¿“The elements of a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress are (1) the defendant engages in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard for the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffers extreme or severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant's extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the plaintiff's extreme or severe emotional distress.” (So v. Shin (2012) 212 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 671.) 

 

A defendant’s conduct is “outrageous” when it is so “extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1051.) Behavior may be considered outrageous if a defendant: (1) “abuses a relation or position which gives him power to damage the plaintiff’s interest”; (2) “knows the plaintiff is susceptible to injuries through mental distress”; or (3) “acts intentionally or unreasonably with the recognition that the acts are likely to result in illness through mental distress.” (McDaniel v. Gile (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 363, 372.)

 

Plaintiff alleges “Defendant[‘s] conduct was extreme, outrageous and reckless.” (SAC p. 15.) Plaintiff incorporates Paragraphs 1 through 15 of her second amended complaint to the third cause of action. (SAC p. 15.) Paragraphs 1 through 15 allege Defendant put a padlock on Plaintiff’s storage unit, violated Article 14.05 by attempting to collect unpaid rent and sending Plaintiff Notices of Liens of Sale, and by allowing mice to infest the storage facility. (SAC pp. 6-9.) Liability for intentional infliction of emotional distress “does not extend to mere insults, indignities, threats, annoyances, petty oppressions, or other trivialities.” (Hughes v. Pair, supra, (2009) 46 Cal.4th at p. 1051.) Further, “[s]evere emotional distress means ‘emotional distress of such substantial quality or enduring quality that no reasonable [person] in civilized society should be expected to endure it.’” (Id.) This court finds that Plaintiff’s allegations do not rise to the level of extreme or outrageous conduct.

 

Plaintiff further alleges she “suffered severe emotional distress” and “Defendant[‘s] conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s emotional distress.” (SAC p. 15.)

 

Plaintiff has not alleged Defendant’s conduct was extreme or outrageous. Accordingly, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action.

 

  1. Uncertainty

 

Defendant further argues Plaintiff’s first, second, and third causes of action fail because they are uncertain. (Demurrer pp. 9-13.)

 

“Even as against a special demurrer, a plaintiff is required only to ‘set forth in [her] complaint the essential facts of [her] case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant of the nature, source, and extent of [her] cause of action.’” (Elder v. Pac. Bell Tel. Co., supra, 205 Cal.App.4th at p. 858.)

 

This court finds that Plaintiff has set forth the essential facts in her second amended complaint with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint Defendant with the nature, source, and extent of her cause of action. Thus, Plaintiff’s second amended complaint is not uncertain.

 

V.              LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303-304.)¿ 

 

This court finds that there is a reasonable possibility that Plaintiff can successfully amend the complaint. Accordingly, this court grants Plaintiff leave to amend.

 

VI.           MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Defendant seeks an order striking portions of Plaintiff’s second amended complaint, including Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. (Motion to Strike p. 2.) This court has sustained Defendant’s demurrer. Accordingly, the motion to strike is moot.

 

VII.         CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court sustains the demurrer and grants Plaintiff ten days leave to amend. This court denies Defendant’s motion to strike because it is now moot.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

Motion to Compel Arbitration

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:  Denied

 

Defendants Brookdale Gardens of Tarzana and BLC Gardens-Tarzana, L.P. move to compel arbitration of the claims brought by Plaintiff Yvette Guar-Arie.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Yvette Gur-Arie received care at Brookdale Gardens of Tarzana, a Residential Care Facility for the Elderly, and Tarzana Medical Center aka Providence Cedars-Sinai Medical Center. (Complaint ¶ 1.) On June 7, 2024, Plaintiff Yvette Gur-Arie filed a complaint against Defendants Brookdale Gardens of Tarzana, BLC Gardens-Tarzana, L.P., Providence Cedars-Sinai Tarzana Medical Center, Tarzana Medical Center, LLC, Oren N. Hirsch, MD, Sharon George, MD., Shannon M. Smith, MD, and Does 1 through 200, alleging two causes of action for elder abuse/neglect and negligence/professional negligence.

 

On October 3, 2024, Defendants Brookdale Gardens of Tarzana and BLC Gardens-Tarzana, L.P. (“Defendants”) filed a motion to compel arbitration. On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On October 29, 2024, Defendants filed a reply.

 

II.             DISCUSSION

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) “[T]he right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.” (Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Inv. Brokerage Co. v. Hock Inv. Co. (1998) 68 Cal. App.4th 83, 88.)

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413; Provencio v. WMA Securities, Inc., 125 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1031.) The court is empowered by California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.)

   

“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971–72.) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld ‘unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.’” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.) Notwithstanding that strong policy, the United States Supreme Court has held, “[A]rbitration is a matter of contract and a party cannot be required to submit to arbitration any dispute which he has not agreed so to submit.” (AT & T Technologies, Inc. v. Communications Workers of America (1986) 475 U.S. 643, 648.)

 

The hearing and determination of a petition to compel arbitration “in the manner and upon the notice provided by law for the making and hearing of motions” generally means that “the facts are to be proven by affidavit or declaration and documentary evidence with oral testimony taken only in the court's discretion.” (Sonic-Calabasas A, Inc. v. Moreno (2013) 57 Cal.4th 1109, 1157.)

 

  1. Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp., supra, 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff must arbitrate their dispute because, at the time of Plaintiff’s admission to Brookdale Gardens, Plaintiff’s representative, Joshua Gur-Arie, executed an arbitration agreement. (Motion p. 3.)

 

Defendants submit the Residency Agreement (the “Agreement”) that was signed by Joshua Gur-Arie on March 25, 2019. (Ex. A p. 18.) The Agreement contains an arbitration provision, which states:

 

Any and all claims or controversies arising out of, or in any way relating to, this Agreement or your stay at the Community, excluding any action for eviction, and including disputes regarding interpretation of this Agreement, whether arising out of State or Federal law, whether existing or arising in the future, whether for statutory, compensatory or punitive damages and whether sounding in breach of contract, tort or breach of statutory duties, irrespective of the basis for the duty or the legal theories upon which the claim is asserted, shall be submitted to binding arbitration, as provided below, and shall not be filed in a court of law. The parties to this Agreement further understand that a jury will not decide their case. The California Code of Civil Procedure 1280 et seq. concerning arbitration shall govern the procedure, except if inconsistent with this Arbitration Provision or expressly stated otherwise in this Agreement. Further, nothing in this Agreement is to be construed to contradict any applicable California statutory or regulatory grievance, challenge or mediation procedure. Any party who demands arbitration must do so for all claims or controversies that are known, or reasonably should have been known, by the date of the demand for arbitration, and if learned of during the course of the arbitration proceeding shall amend the claims or controversies to reflect the same. All current damages and reasonably foreseeable damages arising out of such claims or controversies shall also be incorporated into the initial demand or amendment thereto.

 

(Ex. A. pp. 9-10.)

 

Defendants also submit the Durable General Power of Attorney, which granted Joshua Gur-Arie “full legal authority to handle all financial and business affairs for [Yvette B. Gur-Arie]” and the power to “sign, seal, execute, deliver, create and acknowledge any documents on behalf of [Yvette V. Gur-Arie.]” (Ex. 2 pp. 1-2.) Joshua Gur-Arie acknowledges that his signature is on the Agreement. (Joshua Gur-Arie Declaration ¶ 5.)

 

In Harrod v. Country Oaks Partners, LLC (2024) 15 Cal.5th 939, 956, the Court stated “[t]he power to make decisions about ‘claims and litigation,’ moreover, includes the power to “submit to arbitration ... with respect to a claim or litigation” and to ‘execute and file or deliver a ... waiver, ... agreement, or other instrument in connection with the prosecution, settlement, or defense of a claim or litigation.’” Here, although the Durable Power of Attorney did not reference “claims and litigation” it granted Joshua Gur-Arie “full authority to handle all financial and business affairs” and the power to “sign, seal, execute, deliver, create and acknowledge any documents on behalf of [Yvette V. Gur-Arie.]” (Ex. 2 pp. 1-2.) Moreover, Plaintiff does not dispute that the Durable Power of Attorney gave Joshua Gur-Arie the authority to sign the Agreement on behalf of Plaintiff.

 

Accordingly, Defendants have met their burden of proving a valid arbitration agreement exists.

 

  1. Plaintiff’s Arguments

 

Plaintiff argues the Agreement is substantively and procedurally unconscionable. Plaintiff also argues the Agreement is against public policy and therefore void. (Opposition p. 12.) Finally, Plaintiff argues this court should exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce the Agreement because this action involves third parties who were not parties to the Agreement.

 

  1. Unconscionability

 

The doctrine of unconscionability applies to arbitration agreements. (Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.) Civil Code section 1670.5, subdivision (a) provides, “If the court as a matter of law finds the contract or any clause of the contract to have been unconscionable at the time it was made the court may refuse to enforce the contract.”

 

“Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element, the former focusing on ‘oppression’ or ‘surprise’ due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on ‘overly harsh’ or ‘one-sided’ results. The prevailing view is that procedural and substantive unconscionability must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability. But they need not be present in the same degree. The more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” (Peng v. First Republic Bank, supra, 219 Cal.App.4th at p. 1469, internal citations omitted, emphasis original.) “Ordinary contracts of adhesion, although they are indispensable facts of modern life that are generally enforced, contain a degree of procedural unconscionability.” (Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1244.)

 

    1. Substantive Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the Agreement is unfairly one-sided because it “requires residents to give up their constitutional right to a jury trial without reciprocity as to Brookdale and its owners/operators.” (Opposition p. 8.) But the Agreement states that “[a]ny and all claims or controversies” are subject to arbitration and does not expressly limit arbitration to claims brought by Plaintiff. (Ex. A. p. 9.) Accordingly, this court finds that the Agreement is not unfairly one-sided.

 

Next, Plaintiff argues the Agreement illegally limits damages because it “requires each party to bear its own attorney fees and costs, and ‘expressly waive any right to recover attorney fees or costs.’” (Opposition p. 9.) Plaintiff cites Bickel v. Sunrise Assisted Living (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 1, 10, where the Court of Appeal upheld a trial court’s ruling that “a waiver of recovery of attorney fees and costs in connection with arbitration of elder abuse claims . . .  was contrary to public policy.” The Court cited California Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657, which states “Where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that a defendant is liable for physical abuse as defined in Section 15610.63, neglect as defined in Section 15610.57, or abandonment as defined in Section 15610.05, and that the defendant has been guilty of recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of this abuse, the following shall apply, in addition to all other remedies otherwise provided by law: (a) The court shall award to the plaintiff reasonable attorney's fees and costs.” The Court stated that “the rights established in Section 15657 were unwaivable and, as a result, the arbitration agreement in the instant case could not be used as a vehicle for the waiver of such important statutory rights.” (Bickel v. Sunrise Assisted Living, supra, 206 Cal.App.4th at p. 12.) The Court held “[w]here a provision in an arbitration agreement seeks to waive such rights, as was the case here, the provision is contrary to public policy and may be severed.” (Id.)

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges a cause of action for elder abuse/neglect. (Complaint p. 1.) Like Bickel, the Agreement here contains a waiver of attorneys’ fees, stating “[t]he parties shall bear their own attorneys’ fees and costs and hereby expressly waive any right to recovery attorney fees or costs, unless otherwise provided for by state or federal statute.” (Ex. A, pp. 10-11.) Accordingly, the Agreement’s attorneys’ fees provision violates Bickel.

 

Finally, Plaintiff argues the Agreement unlawfully requires the arbitration proceedings to remain confidential. (Opposition p. 3.) Plaintiff cites Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.310(a), which states “Notwithstanding any other provision of law, it is the policy of the State of California that confidential settlement agreements are disfavored in any civil action the factual foundation for which establishes a cause of action for a violation of the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Chapter 11(commencing with Section 15600) of Part 3 of Division 9 of the Welfare and Institutions Code).” Plaintiff also cites Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.320(f), which states “A stipulated protective order may not be recognized or enforced by the court to prevent disclosure of information filed with the court pursuant to subdivision (b), absent a showing of any of the following: (1) The information is privileged under existing law. (2) The information is not evidence of abuse of an elder or dependent adult as described in Sections 15610.30, 15610.57, and 15610.63 of the Welfare and Institutions Code. (3) The party seeking to uphold the confidentiality of the information has demonstrated that there is a substantial probability that prejudice will result from the disclosure and that the party's interest in the information cannot be adequately protected through redaction.” (Opposition p. 4.)

 

The Agreement states “[t]he arbitration provision shall remain confidential in all respects . . .” (Ex. A. p. 12.)  This court finds that the Agreement’s confidentiality provision is disfavored under section 2017.310(a) and is also unenforceable, absent a showing of the requirements set forth in section 2017.320(f).

 

Plaintiff argues the unconscionable provisions should not be severed from the arbitration agreement.

 

“The overarching inquiry is whether ‘the interests of justice . . . would be furthered’ by severance.” (Martinez v. Master Protection Corp. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 107, 119.) “If the central purpose of the contract is tainted with illegality, then the contract as a whole cannot be enforced. If the illegality is collateral to the main purpose of the contract, and the illegal provision can be extirpated from the contract by means of severance or restriction, then such severance and restriction are appropriate.” (Id.)

 

In Martinez v. Master Protection Corp., supra, 118 Cal.App.4th at p. 119, the arbitration agreement contained an unlawful provision shortening the statute of limitations, a cost-sharing provision, and an unconscionably unilateral arbitration clause. The Court noted that the defects “indicate a systematic effort to impose arbitration on an employee . . . as an inferior forum that works to the employer’s advantage.” (Id.) The Court held that the provisions could not be cured “by striking or limiting application of [the] provisions . . . [r]ather, the arbitration agreement is so ‘permeated’ by unconscionability it could only be saved, if at all, by a reformation beyond our authority.” (Id.) 

 

In contrast to Martinez, the attorneys’ fees and confidentiality provisions do not permeate the agreement and can be cured by striking those provisions from the Agreement. Accordingly, this court finds that the attorneys’ fees and confidentiality provisions can be severed from the Agreement.

 

    1. Procedural Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the Agreement is procedurally unconscionable because it was signed under oppressive and coercive conditions. (Opposition p. 5.) Plaintiff argues the conditions were oppressive and coercive because Joshua Gur-Arie signed the Agreement the day after Plaintiff was admitted to Brookdale and “[b]y delaying presentation of the agreement until after her move-in date, Brookdale ensured that [Plaintiff’s] move was already complete, such that the burden to negotiate any of its provisions and/or cancel the admission altogether (the only leverage available) would have been too high.” (Opposition p. 5.)

 

“A procedural unconscionability analysis ‘begins with an inquiry into whether the contract is one of adhesion.” (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho, (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 116.) “An adhesive contract is standardized, generally on a preprinted form, and offered by the party with superior bargaining power ‘on a take-it-or-leave-it basis.” (Id.) Procedural unconscionability also “focus[es] on oppression and surprise due to unequal bargaining power.” (Swain v. LaserAway Medical Group, Inc. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 59, 69.) “[O]pression occurs where a contract involves lack of negotiation and meaningful choice, surprise where the allegedly unconscionable provision is hidden within a prolix printed form.” (Id.)

 

The Agreement appears to be standardized, on a preprinted form, and offered by Defendants, who were the party with superior bargaining power. (Ex. A.) Accordingly, the Agreement is an adhesive contract.

 

However, Plaintiff’s argument that the Agreement is procedurally unconscionable because it was signed the day after Plaintiff’s admission and Plaintiff’s representative therefore had no choice but to agree is unpersuasive. The Agreement contained an opt-out provision, stating “[i[f you or your legal representative chooses to opt out of [the arbitration provision] of this Agreement please indicate by signing below.” (Ex. A. p. 11.) Further, the Agreement stated “[e]ven if you or your legal representative chooses not to opt out of the arbitration provisions above, you may withdraw your agreement to arbitrate within thirty (30) days after signing this Agreement by giving written notice of your withdrawal to us.” (Ex. A. p. 11.) Accordingly, Plaintiff’s representative could have opted out of the arbitration provision without cancelling Plaintiff’s admission to the facility.

 

Thus, this court finds that there is a small degree of procedural unconscionability because the Agreement is an adhesion contract but finds Plaintiff’s argument that Plaintiff had no choice but to agree to the arbitration provision meritless.

 

  1. Public Policy

 

Plaintiff argues that pursuant to Harris v. University Village Thousand Oaks, CCRC, LLC (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 847 and Code of Civil Procedure section 1953(a)(4), the Agreement is void as against public policy.

 

Civil Code section 1953(a) states “Any provision of a lease or rental agreement of a dwelling by which the lessee agrees to modify or waive any of the following rights shall be void as contrary to public policy . . . [including her] procedural rights in litigation in any action involving [her] rights and obligations as a tenant.”

 

In Harris v. University Village Thousand Oaks, CCRC, LLC (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 847, 850-851, residents filed an action against a continuing care retirement community, alleging “false representations regarding facility security, the amount of future increases in monthly fees, and whether monthly fees included the cost to charge electric vehicles.” (Id. at 851.) The residents later appealed from a judgment confirming an arbitration award. (Id. at 850.) On Appeal, the Court noted that “Civil Code section 1953, subdivision (a)(4), ‘establishes the general rule that a tenant of residential premises cannot validly agree, in a residential lease agreement, to binding arbitration to resolve disputes regarding [their] rights and obligations as a tenant.’” (Id. at p. 852.) The Court held that section 1953 “applies to the continuing care contracts here because the fees paid by appellants include payment for the right to live in a residence” and “[b]ecause the allegations in the complaint . . . include violations of ‘rights and obligations as a tenant’ [citation] the arbitration agreements are void.” (Id. at 853.)

 

Defendants argue Harris does not apply here because the arbitration agreement exempts eviction and Plaintiff’s causes of action “do not relate to her tenancy, but, rather, to the services and care she received while residing at the Brookdale facility.” (Reply p. 10.)

 

Plaintiff argues Harris applies here because the complaint alleges Defendants failed to supervise Plaintiff, causing her to sustain four broken bones, and Defendants failed to report changes in Plaintiff’s changes to Plaintiff’s physician and unauthorized representatives. (Opposition p. 4.)

 

Unlike Harris, this case does not involve Plaintiff’s tenant’s rights. In contrast, Plaintiff alleges claims for elder abuse/neglect and negligence/professional negligence which are based on Defendants’ failure to supervise and care for Plaintiff and Defendants’ failure to report Plaintiff’s injuries. (Complaint p. 1, ¶¶ 18, 20, 21-30.) Accordingly, this court finds that Harris is distinguishable and does not apply here.

 

  1. Third Parties

 

Plaintiff argues this court “may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement, and may order joinder of all parties into a single action if it determines that a party to the arbitration agreement (i.e., the Brookdale Defendants) is also a party to a pending court action with a third party (i.e. the Tarzana Medical Center Defendants), arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions, and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.” (Opposition p. 10.) Plaintiff further argues “there is a substantial risk of an arbitrator and jury arriving at conflicting findings of fact.” (Opposition p. 11.) Plaintiff suggests “the trier(s) of fact will have to decide the extent to which each of the defendants contributed to the cause of Mrs. Gur-Arie’s injuries, and the factual findings made by the different triers of fact may conflict.” (Opposition p. 11.) For example, an arbitrator could determine the Tarzana Medical Center Defendants . . . are liable for the injuries suffered by [Plaintiff]; whereas, at trial, the jury could determine that the Brookdale Defendants . . . were the responsible tortfeasors.” And “[b]ecause §1431.2 dictates that each defendant only bears liability for its proportionate share of non-economic damages, [Plaintiff] could even be deprived of any favorable verdict – despite a finding of wrongdoing – because the respective triers of fact could come to inconsistent verdicts.” (Opposition p. 11.)

 

Defendants argue inconsistent verdicts and duplication of efforts are unlikely because “[t]he alleged acts of negligence by each defendant pose different lines of inquiry.” (Reply p. 12.) Defendants argue that Plaintiff alleges the Brookdale Gardens Defendants’ negligence resulted in Plaintiff’s fractures and their subsequent failure to administer care increased Plaintiff’s pain and suffering. (Reply p. 12.) In contrast, Defendants argue the Tarzana Medical Center Defendants negligently failed to diagnose Plaintiff’s condition. (Reply p. 12.) Accordingly, Defendants argue that “the determination of liability for the physicians’ failure to diagnose two broken legs is not impacted by anything that Brookdale Gardens is alleged to have done.” (Reply p. 14.)

 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2(c).)

 

In Birl v. Heritage Care, LLC (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1315-1316, a patient underwent surgery at a Kaiser Permanente Hospital (“Kaiser”). The patient was transferred to Brier Oak Terrace Care Center nursing facility (“Brier”) where he developed an infection. (Id at 1316.) The patient was then readmitted to Kaiser, where Plaintiffs alleged he was improperly cared for. (Id.) The patient was transferred to the Fountain View Subacute and Nursing Center, where “he was again allegedly improperly cared for and developed another infection.” (Id.) He was later transferred back to Kaiser’s emergency room. (Id.) He was later discharged and admitted to Heritage nursing facility (“Heritage”). (Id.) Heritage personnel failed to care for the patient. (Id.) The patient was then transferred to Harbor-UCLA Hospital, where he later died. (Id.)  The patient’s successors in interest filed a complaint alleging various causes of action. (Id. at p. 1317.) Heritage filed a petition to compel arbitration, which the trial court denied. (Id.)

 

On appeal, Heritage argued the trial court abused its discretion in refusing to compel arbitration because there was a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. (Birl v. Heritage Care, LLC, supra, 172 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1319.) The Court of Appeal upheld the trial court’s ruling. (Id. at p. 1323) The Court first noted that some of the defendants were third parties to the arbitration agreement with Heritage. (Id. at p. 1319.) Next, the Court noted that the Kaiser and Kaiser affiliate defendants were involved in the same transaction as Heritage because the Kaiser and Kaiser affiliate doctors were the patient’s physicians at Heritage. (Id. at pp. 1316, 1320.) Further, the Court noted that all codefendants were involved in a series of related transactions because their “tortious conduct . . . all contributed to the injuries suffered by [the patient] prior to his death.” (Id. at 1320.) The Court noted that “It is of no consequence that Heritage's services were separated by time from the sequential services provided by the other long-term care facilities. A temporal separation does not necessarily negate the existence of the requisite “series of related transactions.” [Citation] Likewise, all the codefendants contributed to the cause of the injuries suffered by [the patient’s] wife and two daughters, as alleged in their individual causes of action for loss of consortium and the negligent infliction of emotional distress.” (Id.)

 

Here, Defendants are party to a pending proceeding with third parties, Providence Cedars Sinai Tarzana Medical Center, Tarzana Medical Center, LLC, Oren N. Hirsch MD, Sharon George, MD, and Shannon M. Smith, MD, who were not parties to the arbitration agreement. (Complaint p. 1; Ex. A.) Thus, section 1281.2, subdivision (c)’s first element is satisfied.

 

Further, this case involves a series of related transactions involving all of the defendants. Plaintiff alleges Defendants neglected Plaintiff, resulting in Plaintiff suffering two broken legs. (Complaint ¶ 4.) Plaintiff also alleges Defendants “fail[ed] to advise Tarzan Medical Center staff that any traumatic event had occurred” and Defendants’ “failure to provide such information significantly delayed an accurate diagnosis regarding the cause of [Plaintiff’s] swollen legs and her pain.” (Complaint ¶ 31.) Next, Plaintiff alleges Defendants Providence Cedars-Sinai Tarzana Medical Center and Tarzana Medical Center employed personnel who provided services to Plaintiff. (Complaint ¶ 6.) Plaintiff alleges Providence Cedars-Sinai Tarzan Medical Center, Tarzana Medical Center, LLC, Oren N. Hirsch, MD, Sharon George, MD, and Shannon M. Smith, MD provided health care to Plaintiff during her admission to Tarzana Medical Center from June 18, 2023 through June 23, 2023 and failed to properly diagnose, care, and treat Plaintiff’s two broken legs. (Complaint ¶¶ 64-67.) Accordingly, like Birl, this case involves a series of related transactions where Plaintiff alleges all codefendants contributed to injuries resulting from her two broken legs. (Complaint ¶¶ 47, 58.) Accordingly, this court finds that this case involves a series of related transactions.

 

This court also finds there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.

The Court noted in Birl that “if the trier of fact is the same for all defendants, as would occur if all defendants are joined in the court action, then the possibility of conflicting rulings concerning fault and apportionment of damages [citation] would not occur.” (Birl v. Heritage Care, LLC, supra, 172 Cal.App.4th at p. 1321.) Likewise, here, Plaintiff is seeking special damages for medical and related expenses. (Complaint p. 20.) If the trier of facts is the same for all defendants, then that trier of fact can apportion each Defendants’ responsibility for damages according to each Defendants’ liability. If the trier of facts is different, then Plaintiff could receive duplicate damages for her medical expenses. Alternatively, if the trier of facts is different, Plaintiff could receive no damages if each trier of facts finds that defendant(s) –  who are not before that trier of fact – are responsible for Plaintiff’s injuries and damages.

 

This creates a risk of conflicting rulings on common issues of law or fact. Accordingly, this court finds that section 1281.2(c) applies.

 

  1. Federal Arbitration Act

 

“The Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) (9 U.S.C. § 1 et seq.) does not include a provision comparable to section 1281.2(c) and therefore requires courts to enforce written arbitration agreements even if there is pending litigation involving a third party that may result in conflicting rulings.” (Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.)

 

Defendants argue the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies here because “the parties’ Residency Agreement contains a Pharmacy Services Agreement, which describes medications that were obtained from other states.” (Ex. A; Ex.C; Motion p. 7.) Defendants further argue “the facility indisputably obtained supplies and medical equipment from vendors outside of California, accepted residents from outside of California, and advertised on the internet, just like the defendant in Scott.” (Motion p. 7.)

 

In Scott v. Yoho, (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 392, 395, the plaintiffs filed a complaint after a patient consulted with defendants for plastic surgery procedures, underwent the procedure, and died following the surgery. The Court of Appeal held that there was a sufficient nexus with interstate commerce and the FAA applied to the arbitration agreement because there was “uncontroverted evidence” that showed 20 percent of the medical supplies were shipped from out of state, the doctor used materials during the procedure that originated from outside California, the defendants advertised on the internet and communicated with out-of-state patients by telephone, mail, and email, about 5 percent of defendants’ patients were from out-of-state, and the doctor’s declaration stated “My practice contracted (via U.S. mail or the internet) with approximately 15 companies, insurance providers, entities and/or other public or private corporations whose places of business are outside the State of California. My practice regularly sends and/or receives letters, drafts, checks, inquiries, and written and telephonic communications from outside of California, and from various other states, which represents approximately 10% of all such communications in my practice.” (Id. at 401-402.)

 

The defendants in Scott v. Yoho relied on “evidence” and a declaration by the doctor showing “the contracts evidenced a transaction involving interstate commerce.” (Scott v. Yoho, supra, 248 Cal.App.4th at 397.) Here, Defendants argue the Pharmacy Services Agreement describes medications obtained from other states. (Ex. C.) But the Pharmacy Services Agreement does not mention where Defendants obtained the medications. (Ex. C.) Defendants further argue “the facility indisputably obtained supplies and medical equipment from vendors outside of California, accepted residents from outside of California, and advertised on the internet.” (Motion p. 7.) But Defendants cite no evidence supporting these contentions. Accordingly, this court cannot determine that the Agreement has a sufficient nexus with interstate commerce.

 

  1. Federal Preemption

 

However, even if the FAA applied here, it would not preempt section 1281.2(c) because the Agreement shows that the parties agreed to arbitrate their disputes in accordance with California law.

 

Citing Volt Info. Sciences v. Leland Stanford Jr. U. (1989) 489 U.S. 468, 474-476, Plaintiff argues the Federal Arbitration Act (the “FAA”) does not preempt Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2(c). (Opposition p. 13.)

 

Defendants argue Volt does not apply because the Agreement “is specific to where California law will apply, once the arbitration process has begun” and “[a]pplication of the FAA will not impact that.” (Reply pp. 14-15.)

 

In Volt, the arbitration provision “contained a choice-of-law clause providing that ‘[t]he Contract shall be governed by the law of the place where the Project is located.’” (Volt Info. Sciences v. Leland Stanford Jr. U., supra, 489 U.S. at p. 470.) The Supreme Court noted that it was undisputed the contract fell under the FAA because it involved interstate commerce, but stated that “even if  §§ 3 and 4 of the FAA are fully applicable in state-court proceedings, they do not prevent application of Cal. Civ. Proc. Code Ann. § 1281.2(c) to stay arbitration where, as here, the parties have agreed to arbitrate in accordance with California law.” (Id. at p. 477.)

 

In Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group, supra, 213 Cal.App.4th at p. 969, an arbitration agreement contained three provisions referencing California law. First, the arbitration agreement stated “[j]udgment entered upon the award rendered may be enforced by appropriate judicial action pursuant to the California Code of Civil Procedure.” (Id.) Second, the arbitration agreement stated, “[b]y executing this Agreement you are agreeing to have any dispute arising out of this Agreement included in the arbitration of disputes provision decided by neutral arbitration as provided by California law.” (Id.) Third, the arbitration agreement stated “[i]n any event each Tenant shall be entitled to discovery in accordance with California Code of Civil Procedure Section 1283.05.” (Id.) The Court held that “These repeated references to California's procedural rules on arbitration establish the parties intended section 1281.2(c) to apply in this case; a specific reference to section 1281.2(c) was not required.” (Id.)

 

Like the arbitration agreement in Acquire II, Ltd., the Agreement here repeatedly references California law. First, the Agreement states that “The California Code of Civil Procedure 1280, et seq. concerning arbitration shall govern the procedure, except if inconsistent with this arbitration provision or expressly stated otherwise in this agreement.” (Ex. A. p. 10.)  The Agreement further states that “[d]iscovery in the arbitration proceeding shall be governed by the California Code of Civil Procedure” and “[t]he arbitrator shall apply the California Code of Evidence and California Code of Civil Procedure except where otherwise stated in this agreement.” (Ex. A p. 10.) Finally, the Agreement provides that “the arbitrator shall apply, and the arbitration decision shall be consistent with, California law except as otherwise stated in this Arbitration Provision.” (Ex. A p. 10.)  The Agreement is silent as to the FAA. (Ex. A.)

 

Defendants argue the Agreement’s references to California law only “go into effect once arbitration begins” and do not “indicate what applies when, as here, one party refuses to arbitrate.” (Reply p. 14.) However, the provisions in Acquire II, Ltd. could also be read as only applying during and after arbitration. For example, one provision in Acquire II, Ltd. provided that “by executing this Agreement you are agreeing to have any dispute arising out of this Agreement included in the arbitration of disputes provision decided by neutral arbitration as provided by California law.” (Acquire II, Ltd. v. Colton Real Estate Group, supra, 213 Cal.App.4th at p. 969 (Emphasis added).)

 

Accordingly, because the parties agreed to arbitrate in accordance with California law, (Ex. A, p. 10) the FAA does not preempt applying 1281.2(c) to this case.

 

III.           CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, this court denies Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration.
