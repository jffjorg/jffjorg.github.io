DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV24054    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: 73

12/20/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

PEACE OTUONYE, et al. v. MONARK, L.P., et al. (22STCV24054) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  Sedoo Manu (Divine Law Office)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Nia Harbour (Phillips, Spallas & Angstadt, LLC) 

 

PETITION TO CONFIRM MINOR’S COMPROMISE (filed 11/25/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise for Claimant Godspower Otuonye is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 26, 2022, Plaintiffs Peace Otuonye, Individually and as guardian ad litem for Godspower Otuonye, Lisa Otuonye, and Elisha Otuonye, filed this action against Defendants Monark, L.P., and Swami International, Inc.

 

Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint on July 10, 2023, alleging the following causes of action:

1.     Negligent Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability

2.     Intentional Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability

3.     Nuisance (Negligent)

4.     Nuisance (Intentional)

5.     Negligence 

6.     Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

7.     Breach of Implied Covenant of Quiet Use and Enjoyment

8.     Negligent Violation of Statutory Duty

9.     Intentional Violation of Statutory Duty

10.  Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq.

  

The SAC alleges that Plaintiffs were residents of a rented dwelling located at 12615 Oxford Avenue #C, Hawthorne California 90250 (the “Premises”) owned, managed, and operated by Defendants, from approximately December 2019 through December 2022. (SAC, ¶ 1.) Plaintiffs allege multiple habitability violations and substandard conditions including: Recurring water leaks and water intrusion; flooding of the premises, including ceilings, walls and floors; constructions debris; toilet leakages, overflow and flooding; sewage from toilet inundating Plaintiffs’ bedroom and soaking the carpet and mattress. (SAC, ¶ 2.)

 

On October 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Notice of Settlement of the Entire Case.

 

On November 25, 2024, Parent and Guardian Ad Litem Peace Otuonye (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise on behalf of Claimant Godspower Otuonye (Claimant”). No opposition was filed.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 372, any settlement of a claim made by a minor or adult with a disability must be approved by the court.¿If the court is satisfied that the settlement is in the best interest of the claimant, then the court shall approve the settlement.¿(See Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

A petition for court approval of a compromise or covenant not to sue under Code of Civil Procedure section 372 must comply with California Rules of Court, Rules 7.950, 7.951, and 7.952. The petition must be verified by the petitioner and contain a full disclosure of all information that has “any bearing upon the reasonableness” of the compromise or the covenant.¿(Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.950.)¿ The person compromising the claim on behalf of the minor or person who lacks capacity, and the represented person, must attend the hearing on compromise of the claim unless the court for good cause dispenses with their personal appearance. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.952.)¿¿¿ 

 

Defendants have offered to settle this matter in the total amount of $250,000. Of the total settlement payment of $250,000, the total settlement amount for Claimant/Godspower Otuonye is $3,000. The petition indicates that the adult Plaintiff, Peace Otuonye, is receiving a higher settlement because the majority of quantifiable damages were directly incurred by the adult Plaintiff, including: 1. All rental payments made for the defective premises; 2. Personal property damage to items purchased solely by Petitioner; 3. Lost wages and economic damages; 4. Out-of-pocket expenses related to the habitability issues. (Petition, p. 11.) Therefore, Petitioner represents that the settlement allocation reflects this, with the minor Claimant’s portion primarily representing compensation for discomfort and annoyance during their occupancy. (Ibid.) The declaration submitted by Claimant’s counsel states that counsel waives all attorney fees for services rendered to the minor and counsel does not request any attorney fees, costs or expenses from minor Claimant. Thus, the total settlement balance for Claimant is $3,000.00. The Court finds that the settlement is in Claimant’s best interests and approves the settlement. 

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise.

 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise for Claimant Godspower Otuonye is GRANTED.


Case Number: 23STCV08305    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: 73

KENT TRAN v. UTOMO TANI, et al. (23STCV08305)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendants/moving party:  Sally Chan (West Themis Law, PC)

Counsel for Defendants/Cross-Complainant/opposing party: T. Randolph Catanese (Catanese and Wells)

 

CROSS-DEFENDANTS’ Demurrer AND MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE SECOND AMENDED CROSS-complaint (filed 11/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second Amended Cross-Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On April 13, 2023, Plaintiff Kent Tran (“Plaintiff”) filed his Complaint against Defendants Utomi Tani aka Jimmy Tani (“Jimmy”); Yong Sook Kim; Jimmy Tani, Jr.; Lan Sam; Goodland Global Farm Inc.; JT and KT Development LLC; Maie JT & KT Development LLC; True Garlic, Inc.; Goodland Global Mining, Inc.; and nominal Defendant Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc. dba NBC Seafood Restaurant (“collectively Defendants”).

 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint alleging the following causes of action:

1)      Intentional misrepresentation/fraud

2)      Conversion/embezzlement

3)      Declaratory relief

4)      Partition by sale

5)      Violation of Welfare and Institutions Code § 15600 et seq – Financial Elder Abuse

6)      Intentional infliction of emotional distress

7)      Breach of fiduciary duty

8)      Conspiracy

9)      Fraudulent transfer/constructive fraud

10)  Negligent misrepresentation/fraud

11)  Fraud by concealment

12)  Breach of written contract

13)  Breach of oral contract

14)  Inspection of records

15)  Accounting

16)  Unjust enrichment

17)  Involuntary dissolution of corporations

18)  Fraudulent transfer

19)  CA Penal Code Section § 496(A)

20)  Cancellation of instrument pursuant to Civ. Code § 3412

 

The SAC alleges the following. In or about 2008, Plaintiff and Jimmy formed several companies, including Goodland Global Farm Inc., (“Goodland”), JT and KT Development LLC, and Maie JT and KT Development LLC. Plaintiff and Jimmy had agreed to own the companies equally as partners. Plaintiff and Jimmy also decided to jointly invest in another company, NBC Seafood Restaurant (“NBC”) with Kam Choi Lin, Chang Ho Chen, Wei Lin Zheng and Wai Hung Szeto. Plaintiff alleges that Jimmy engaged in fraud and falsely misrepresented to Plaintiff, among other things, that Plaintiff owned 50% of the Companies when he actually did not, that the Companies were in need of loans and not profitable in order to induce Plaintiff to obtain personal loans, for Plaintiff to sign documents that were related to taking out loans in 2021, and that Plaintiff needed to vacate and sell his home to save the Companies.

 

On February 15, 2024, Defendants (“Cross-Complainants”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Kam Choi Lin, Chang Ho Chen, Wei Lin Zheng and Wai Hung Szeto (collectively “Cross-Defendants”).

 

On September 19, 2024, Cross-Complainants filed a Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SACC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1)      Equitable Indemnity and Contribution

2)      Indemnity

3)      Declaratory relief

 

Cross-Complainants, Cross-Defendants and Plaintiff are shareholders of NBC Seafood Restaurant. The SACC alleges that that any liability held against them pertaining to Plaintiff’s allegations, is due to the negligence, carelessness, acts, or omissions of Cross-Defendants. Cross-Complainants are seeking indemnification and contribution for any loss suffered and damages awarded to Plaintiff. Cross-Complainants also seek a declaration that Jimmy has not violated the Bylaws of Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc., and that his actions or omissions were ratified and consented to by Cross-Defendants.

 

On November 19, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed a Demurrer and Motion to Strike to the SACC, arguing:

·       The SACC fails to state sufficient facts as to the first and second causes of action for indemnity, and the third cause of action for declaratory relief. The SACC is also uncertain.

·       Cross-Defendants reiterate that there is a pending contentious case in Department 19 between TANI and LIN, CHEN, ZHENG, and SZETO regarding TANI’s embezzlement of NBC funds and other allegations of fraudulent wrongdoing under Case No. 22STCV30356. Despite TANI's attempts to consolidate the cases, he failed to establish a sufficient relationship between his claims and the actions of the Cross-Defendants. Hon. Judge Stephanie Bowick, in a Minute Order dated June 22, 2023 (Exhibit E), held that the Cross-Complaint and Complaint against TANI in Tani v. Lin et al., Case No. 22STCV30356, and Tran v. Tani et al., Case No. 23STCV08305, are not related.

·       Motion to Strike

o   The SACC alleges that Cross-Complainant Tani acquired a 19.4% interest in NBC Seafood Restaurant in 2021, which contradicts prior allegations in Case No. 22STCV30356, where Tani claimed a 41.67% ownership since 2008. This inconsistency remains unexplained and renders the 2AXC a sham pleading under California law.

o   SACC paragraphs 18-30 incorporate vague and unsupported claims of “unity of interest” and alter ego liability without specific facts to establish Cross-Defendants’ fault or liability. Such generalized assertions fail to meet pleading standards and do not provide sufficient grounds for equitable indemnity or contribution, as previously ruled by the Court.

 

Cross-Complainants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The SACC contains new specific factual allegations to address the Court’s previous order sustaining the prior Cross-Complaint.

·       Cross-Complainants allege that all acts or omissions by TANI or the other Cross-Complainants as alleged in the Second Amended Complaint were consented to and ratified by the Cross-Defendants and therefore Cross-Defendants are liable to the Cross-Complainants under causes of action for Equitable Indemnity and Contribution, Indemnity, and Declaratory Relief as pled in the SACC.

·       Motion to Strike

o   Cross-Defendants list three "grounds" for the Motion in their notice: (1) Sham Pleading; (2) Failure to State Specific Facts; and (3) Inconsistency with Prior Court Rulings. The SACC is not a "sham" pleading. Further, "Failure to State Specific Facts" and "Inconsistency with Prior Court Rulings" are not proper grounds for a motion to strike.

 

Cross-Defendants filed a reply, arguing:

·       The SACC fails to state valid causes of action for equitable relief.

·       The SACC lacks specific allegations of fault by the Cross-Defendants, fails to establish joint legal obligation or resulting damages attributable to Cross-Defendants.

·       Cross-Defendants maintain the Motion to Strike should be granted.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Cross-Defendants assert the parties held a meet and confer call with counsel for Cross-Complainants on October 15, 2024. The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

C.      First and Second Causes of Action for Equitable Indemnity and Contribution; and Indemnity

 

Cross-Defendants argue that the SACC fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute the equitable indemnity and contribution; and indemnity causes of action.

“To state a claim for equitable indemnity, a defendant must allege the same harm for which he may be held liable is properly attributable—at least in part—to the cross-defendant.”  (Platt v. Coldwell Banker Residential Real Estate Services (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 1439, 1445, fn.7.)

 

Cross-Defendants argue the SACC lacks specific allegations of fault, contractual duty and resulting damages. A valid claim of indemnity requires that there must be a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor and resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is contractually or equitably responsible. (Bailey v. Safeway, Inc., (2011) 199 Cal. App. 4th 206.)

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants assert that the SACC sufficiently alleges Cross-Defendants ratified and consented to the transfer of Plaintiff KENT’s shares and the payment of dividend payout to TANI. Specifically, the SACC alleges the following:

 

“[T]he SAC allege that TANI wrongfully acquired TRAN's 19.4% equity interest in GOLDEN PARTNER.” (SACC, ¶ 21.)

 

“The transfer of TRAN's 19.4% equity interest in GOLDEN PARTNER to TANI and TANI' s acquisition of those shares was consented to and ratified by the Cross-Defendants by means of Cross-Defendants' execution of GOLDEN PARTNER corporate minutes dated January 1, 2021, which approved the transfer of TRAN's 19.4% ownership of GOLDEN PARTNER to TANI.” (SACC, ¶ 22.)

 

“[T]he SAC alleges that TANI embezzled KENT's expected 2021 shareholder dividend payout of $552,900 by creating fake corporate meetings claiming that KENT, LIN, CHEN, SZETO and ZHENG approved of JIMMY taking KENT's entire 19.4% of NBC in a non-existent NBC meeting on January 1st, 2021." (SACC, ¶ 23.)

 

“The payment of a shareholder dividend payout of $1,168,500 to TANI for year 2021 was consented to and ratified by the Cross-Defendants by means of Cross-Defendants' execution of GOLDEN PARTNER corporate minutes dated January 1, 2021, approving the transfer of TRAN's 19.4% ownership of GOLDEN PARTNER to TANI.” (SACC, ¶ 24.)

 

“In addition to the above, all Cross-Defendants received K-1s from GOLDEN PARTNER's certified public accountants and bookkeepers reflecting their relative equity holdings in GOLDEN PARTNER based upon the transfer of TRAN's 19.4% equity interest to TANI and consented to and ratified this activity as they accepted financial compensation based upon these equity holdings and did not object at any time. Cross-Defendants knew of, consented to and ratified the information in GOLDEN PARTNER tax reporting documents such as K-1s which identified the payments to TANI from GOLDEN PARTNER identified in the SAC by TRAN as illegal.” (SACC, ¶ 25.)

 

“In regular meetings of the shareholders of GOLDEN PARTNER (including TANI, TRAN, and all Cross-Defendants as well as GOLDEN PARTNER's financial advisors) the shareholders of GOLDEN PARTNER discussed the relative stock positions held by each shareholder, how each shareholder's share of the income of GOLDEN PARTNER should be allocated and reflected on K-1 statements issued by GOLDEN PARTNER, and how payments should be made to the shareholders pursuant to the K-1 statements.” (SACC, ¶ 26.)

 

Cross-Complainants contend that equitable indemnity “requires no contractual relationship,” and “ ‘is premised on a joint legal obligation to another for damages’ ”; it is “subject to allocation of fault principles and comparative equitable apportionment of loss.” (C.W. Howe Partners Inc. v. Mooradian, (2019) 43 Cal. App. 5th 688, 700.) Cross-Complainants also contend the SACC contains new allegations explaining how each Cross-Complainant has standing. Cross-Complainants argue that the SAC ties the alleged conduct of TANI to all the other Cross-Complainants by means of alter ego and conspiracy allegations (citing to paragraphs 20, 21, 131, 132, and 133 of the SAC). Therefore, because the SAC alleges that all Cross-Defendants are liable for the alleged actions of TANI and seeks damages from all Cross-Defendants, Cross-Defendants have standing to bring the SACC. Cross-Complainants also contend the SACC was amended to show how Cross-Defendants’ specific actions caused the damages claimed, alleging that “Cross-Defendants' actions in executing the January 1, 2021, GOLDEN PARTNER corporate minutes approving the transfer of TRAN's ownership in GOLDEN PARTNER to TANI caused the damages alleged by TRAN in the SAC." (SACC, ¶ 22.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that the SACC alleges sufficient facts. Accordingly, the demurrer to the first and second cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

D.     Third Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

 

An action for declaratory relief lies when there is an actual bona fide dispute between parties as to a legal obligation arising under the circumstances specified in CCP §1060 and, in addition, the controversy must be justiciable - i.e., presents a question as to which there is more than one answer. (Western Motors Servicing Corp. v. Land Development & Inv. Co. (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 509.). “Actual controversy” is a controversy which admits of definitive and conclusive relief by judgment within the field of judicial administration, as distinguished from an advisory opinion on a particular or hypothetical state of facts. The judgment must decree, not suggest, what the parties may or may not do. (Selby Realty Co. v. San Buenaventura (1973) 10 Cal.3d 110.) A mere difference of opinion is not an “actual controversy” within §1060. The summary judgment procedure is appropriate in declaratory relief actions since ‘The propriety of the application of declaratory relief lies in the trial court's function to render such a judgment when only legal issues are presented for its determination.’ (Spencer v. Hibernia Bank, 186 Cal.App.2d 702, 712.)

 

The SACC states: “An actual controversy has arisen and now exists between Cross-Complainant TANI and Cross-Defendants concerning their respective rights and duties under the Bylaws of GOLDEN PARTNER. Plaintiff TRAN has alleged in the SAC that TANI has violated various provisions of the Bylaws of GOLDEN PARTNER. TANI is informed and believes, and based thereon alleges that all acts or omissions by him were ratified and consented to by the Cross-Defendants in their role as directors, officers and shareholders of GOLDEN PARTNER.” (SACC, ¶ 40.) “Cross-Complainant TANI desires a judicial determination of his rights and obligations under the Bylaws of GOLDEN PARTNER and a judicial declaration that TANI has not violated the Bylaws of GOLDEN PARTNER and that that all acts or omissions by him were ratified and consented to by the Cross-Defendants in their role as directors, officers and shareholders of GOLDEN PARTNER.” (SACC, ¶ 41.)

 

As stated above, the Court finds that the SACC states sufficient allegations at this stage as to Cross-Complainants alleged ratification of Tani’s actions. While Cross-Defendants argue the SACC “does not provide any evidence that Cross-Defendants ratified or endorsed Tani’s actions” (Motion, p. 21.), at this stage, Cross-Defendants are not required to provide evidence. The Court finds Cross-Complainants have alleged an actual controversy.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

E.      Motion to Strike

 

a.      Legal Standard

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

b.      Discussion

 

Cross-Defendants request the Court to strike portions of the SACC as follows:

1.      Paragraphs 18-30

2.      Paragraphs 23-27

 

Cross-Defendants argue Paragraphs 18-30 of the SACC should be stricken because “these paragraphs incorporate vague and unsupported claims of “unity of interest” and alter ego liability without specific facts to establish Cross-Defendants’ fault or liability. Such generalized assertions fail to meet pleading standards and do not provide sufficient grounds for equitable indemnity or contribution, as previously ruled by the Court.” (Motion, p. 3.) However, the Court does not find these arguments as proper grounds for a Motion to Strike and instead are arguments better suited for a demurrer. Further, as stated above, the Court finds that the Cross-Complainants additional allegations sufficient at this time to support its causes of action for indemnity and contribution.

 

Next, Cross-Defendants argue Paragraphs 23-27 of the SACC should be stricken because they are sham pleadings. A subsequent complaint is considered a “sham” pleading if the amendment contains allegations that contradict the earlier pleading or that omit previously pled facts that rendered the initial complaint deficient. (Owens v. Kings Supermarket (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 379, 383-84.) Cross-Defendants argue that these allegations are contradictory to allegations made in the Complaint in the case Tani v. Lin, et al., LASC Case No. 22STCV30356. Specifically, the Complaint in the Tani v. Lin, et al. case states that “Shares of common stock have been issued by the Company as follows: TANI - 37,500 shares; LIN - 37,500 shares; CHEN - 5,000 shares; ZHENG - 5,000 shares; and SZETO - 5,000 shares." (Motion, p. 9.) The Court does not find the allegations are inconsistent, as the allegations in the Tani v. Lin, et al. Complaint does not specify that the TANI’s shares were issued at that amount at the time of the company’s inception.

 

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Strike is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second Amended Cross-Complaint’s first, second and third causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Cross-Defendants’ Motion to Strike is DENIED.

DAVIS v. STARS BEHAVIORAL HEALTH GROUP, INC., et al. (23STCV25804)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Geoffrey Lyon (Lyon Law)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  David Hoiles, Jr. (Jackson Lewis PC)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES TO DEFENDANT STARS BEHAVIORAL GROUP, INC., SET ONE; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS (filed 11/22/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories to Defendant Star Behavioral Group, Inc., Set One, Nos. 6 and 11 is GRANTED in part. Defendants are ordered to provide a Belaire notice to the employees identified, and, as to all those that do not opt out, to further provide the relevant contact information to Plaintiff. Request for sanctions is DENIED.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff Anita Davis filed this action against Defendants Stars Behavioral Health Group, Inc. and Donte Kelley.  

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Wrongful termination in violation of public policy

2.      Family leave retaliation

3.      Paid sick leave discrimination

4.      Paid sick leave retaliation

5.      Family leave discrimination

6.      Retaliation for requesting and using accommodations for disabilities

7.      Failure to engage in the interactive process

8.      Failure to reasonably accommodate

9.      Disability discrimination

10.  Discrimination based on race

11.  Sex discrimination

12.  Harassment based on race

13.  Sexual harassment

14.  Retaliation for opposing FEHA violations

15.  Failure to prevent and stop harassment, discrimination and retaliation

16.  Whistleblower retaliation

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff was employed by Defendants from approximately October 11, 2021, through Plaintiff’s termination on or about May 8, 2023. Plaintiff’s job title was “Peer Advocate Coordinator.” Plaintiff’s responsibilities included supervising a team of subordinates who interacted directly with patients, advising the subordinates on the proper care to administer. Plaintiff worked under immediate supervisor Director of Peer and Parent Partners Donte Kelley. Employer is a mental health and social services care provider that connects patients with care coordinators to provide specialized treatment. Plaintiff alleges multiple FEHA and Labor Code violations, including harassment by Kelley and retaliatory termination.

 

On November 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 6 and 11; and request for sanctions.

·       Special Interrogatory No. 6 seeks Identities of Employees Similar to Plaintiff Who Took Leave and Were Terminated

·       Special Interrogatory No. 11 seeks Identities of Employees Who Were Terminated After Taking Disability Leave

·       The information is relevant and discoverable.

·       Plaintiff seeks sanctions in the amount of $1760.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s interrogatories are procedurally improper because they contain subparts and are compound in direct contravention of Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.060.

·       Plaintiff’s interrogatories are substantively improper as they seek irrelevant, private information regarding third party employees’ identities, medical information, contact information, and personnel information. The interrogatories are also completely overbroad as to time (i.e., five years) and are ambiguous as to what Plaintiff refers to as, “intermittent FMLA, CFRA, pregnancy disability leave, or reasonable accommodation disability leave” and “more than 2 continuous weeks of medical leave.”

 

Plaintiff filed a reply, arguing:

·       “Me too” Evidence of Medical Discrimination Against Third-Party Employees Discoverable With Notice and Opportunity to Opt Out

·       The Interrogatories are Not Compound According to Caselaw Permitting “Me Too” Discovery

·       The Interrogatories are Not Overbroad Under Cases Addressing Temporal Proximity Between Protected Conduct and Adverse Employment Actions

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel declares that the parties met and conferred telephonically and exchanged emails regarding alleged deficiencies in the discovery responses. (Lyon Decl., Exs. 4-8.) The Court finds that counsel has satisfied the meet and confer requirements under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.      Legal Standard

 

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

 

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).) 

 

It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

  

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure fully to answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

Furthermore, to the extent there is any doubt in whether these records should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) 

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Stars Behavioral Health Group, Inc. to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 6 and 11.

 

The interrogatories at issue are as follows:

 

Special Interrogatory No. 6: “IDENTIFY all of YOUR employees with the same job title as PLAINTIFF who in the last five years took intermittent FMLA, CFRA, pregnancy disability leave, or reasonable accommodation disability leave, or more than 2 continuous weeks of medical leave, and were terminated within three months of taking such leave.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Special Interrogatories No. 6: “Respondent objects to this Interrogatory on the grounds: it is premature and employed in a manner or to an extent that causes unwarranted annoyance or undue burden and expense in light of the fact the Propounding Party’s prosecution of the pending action in state court violates her agreement to submit her claims to binding arbitration before a neutral arbitrator; it violates Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.060(f) in that it contains subparts or is a compound question; it is vague and ambiguous as to the phrases “same job title as PLAINTIFF who in the last five years had delays in providing medical certification for paid time off,” and “subsequently terminated or subjected to any resultant discipline,” requiring Respondent to speculate as to its intended meaning and the scope of the Interrogatory; it is overly broad as to time and scope; it calls for a legal conclusion; and it exceeds the permissible scope of discovery to the extent it seeks information protected from disclosure by third-party privacy rights.”

 

Special Interrogatory No. 11: “IDENTIFY all current and former employees, from rank and file, to supervisors, working under Director of Peer and Parent Partners Donte Kelley, Employee Services Manager Claudia Constancio, Administrator Tina Binda, and/or Human Resources Manager Jin Choi, who in the last five years were terminated within three months of taking more than two consecutive weeks of disability leave.”

 

Defendant’s Response to Special Interrogatories No. 11: “Respondent objects to this Interrogatory on the grounds: it is premature and employed in a manner or to an extent that causes unwarranted annoyance or undue burden and expense in light of the fact the Propounding Party’s prosecution of the pending action in state court violates her agreement to submit her claims to binding arbitration before a neutral arbitrator; it violates Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.060(f) in that it contains subparts or is a compound question; it is vague and ambiguous as to the term and phrases “rank and file,” “working under Director of Peer and Parent Partners Donte Kelley and Employee Services Manager Claudia Constancio,” and “terminated within three months of taking more than two consecutive weeks of disability leave,” requiring Respondent to speculate as to its intended meaning and the scope of the Interrogatory; it is overly broad as to time and scope; it calls for a legal conclusion; and it exceeds the permissible scope of discovery to the extent it seeks information protected from disclosure by third-party privacy rights.”

 

Plaintiff asserts these interrogatories are directed toward obtaining comparator or “me too” evidence, for other persons whose employment have been terminated for the same or similar circumstances as Plaintiff. In a FEHA retaliation claim, “[e]vidence that an employer treated ‘similarly situated’ employees outside the plaintiff's protected class ‘more favorably’ is probative of the employer's discriminatory or retaliatory intent.” (Gupta v. Trustees of California State University¿(2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 510, 519.) The data must be directed at showing disparate treatment between employees similarly situated to the plaintiff in all relevant respects, including having similar jobs and displaying similar conduct. (See id. at 519-20.) As such, the sought discovery may serve as evidence on Plaintiff’s case with respect to FEHA claims, to support that the proffered reasons for discipline and termination were a pretext for discriminatory and retaliatory animus. (See Iwekaogwu v. City of Los Angeles (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 803, 816.) 

 

In opposition, Defendant first argues the interrogatories are compound, overbroad and ambiguous.

 

However, the Court does not find the interrogatories improperly compound. An interrogatory is only compound if covers multiple subject matters. Questions regarding the same subject should be allowed although they include an `and' or `or. (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1292). Identification of responding party using question such as State your first name, middle name and last name, and your current address and telephone number is not compound because it only involves one subject matter of an individual's identity. (Id.) Here, the interrogatories cover one subject matter. The Court also does not find the interrogatories are ambiguous or overly broad, as the scope of time is limited to five years.

 

Defendant next argues the sought discovery is violative of the right of privacy for those employees whose information is sought. The court applies the framework of Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn.¿(1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 35-40, as articulated in Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Superior Court: (1) the claimant must possess a “legally protected privacy interest”; (2) the privacy claimant must possess a reasonable expectation of privacy under the particular circumstances; (3) the invasion of privacy complained of must be “serious” in nature, scope, and actual or potential impact to constitute an “egregious” breach of social norms; and (4) the court balances that interest against other competing or countervailing interests in a balancing test. ((2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 370-71.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks the contact information for specific former or current employees of Defendant who took medical leave. The information falls under the rubric of personnel information, as this information would presumably be confidential and contained in personnel files. “[T]he balance will favor privacy for confidential information in third party personnel files unless the litigant can show a compelling need for the particular documents¿and that the information cannot reasonably be obtained through depositions or from nonconfidential sources.” (Life Technologies Corp. v. Superior Court¿(2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 640, 652,¿disapproved of on other grounds by¿Williams v. Superior Court¿(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531 (party seeking discovery of private information need not always establish a compelling interest or compelling need without regard to other considerations articulated in Hill; only obvious invasions of interests fundamental to personal autonomy must be supported by a compelling interest).) The foregoing requests thus go beyond the mere listing of contact information for all of Defendant’s former employees within the listed timeframe.

 

The court finds that privacy concerns under the circumstances would require employees whose information is sought to be given prior notice and an opportunity to resist disclosure of the information.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 6 and 11 is GRANTED in part. Defendants are ordered to provide a Belaire notice to the employees identified, and, as to all those that do not opt out, to further provide the relevant contact information to Plaintiff.

 

D.     Sanctions

 

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.) 

 

Sanctions may be imposed for misuse of discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.030, subd. (a).) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., section 2023.010, subd. (d).) Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 provides that a court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery even if no opposition was filed.  

 

Here, both parties request sanctions. The Court does not find that the parties engaged in the misuse of the discovery process and declines to impose sanctions.

 

III. DISPOSITION

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories to Defendant Star Behavioral Group, Inc., Set One, Nos. 6 and 11 is GRANTED in part. Defendants are ordered to provide a Belaire notice to the employees identified, and, as to all those that do not opt out, to further provide the relevant contact information to Plaintiff. Request for sanctions is DENIED.

MAJMA v. WHITE OAK ENCINO HOMEOWNERS ASSOCIATION, et al. (23STCV29602) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Robert Bisno (Law Offices of Robert Bisno)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Eurus Cady (Law Offices of Scott Stratman) 

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 11/20/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion For Leave to File First Amended Complaint.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 4, 2023, Plaintiff Safoura Majma filed this action against Defendants White Oak Encino Homeowners Association aka White Oak Encino HOA; Ryan Moorman; Joseph Barker; Sam Bronshteyn; Jordan Ceglia; Sonia Garibay and SF Valley Management, Inc.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Breach of CC&Rs; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Breach of Statutory Duty to Maintain Common Area (Civ. Code § 4775); (4) Negligence; (5) Declaratory Relief; and (6) Injunctive Relief.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. White Oak Encino HOA is a homeowners association that is required to maintain the common area of the common interest development located at 17711 Margate Street in Encino, California (the “Project”), pursuant to both the applicable Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions (“CC&Rs”) for the Project and applicable law (see, e.g., Civ. Code § 4775). Plaintiff owns and resides in Unit #106 within the Project (“Plaintiff’s Unit”). White Oak Encino HOA and the Directors, and SF Valley Management, have failed and refused to repair and maintain the roof of the building within the Project where Plaintiff’s Unit is located, despite Plaintiff’s multiple requests that they do so. As a result, water has intruded through the roof of said building and ultimately into Plaintiff’s Unit, causing significant damage.

 

On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint, arguing:

·       The proposed FAC adds 3 additional Plaintiffs, all members of Plaintiff’s family living in the property, 1 additional defendant, a contractor working under Defendants or some of them and 4 causes of action, Fraud/Fraudulent Concealment/Fraud in the in the Inducement, Violation of Penal Code 496, Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

·       This lawsuit was filed in December 2023 and attorney Robert H. Bisno substituted in as counsel on August 30, 2024.

·       Trial in this matter should not be delayed by the FAC as Defendants have not yet filed Responses to Plaintiff’s discovery.

·       The additional causes of action and parties will enable Plaintiffs to seek complete relief, have a trial on the merits based upon all known facts and legal theories tried in one lawsuit. The Statute of Limitations has not yet run on Defendants transgressions and for Plaintiffs to bring a new lawsuit for the proposed additional causes of action would result in a waste of Court resources and attorney time.

 

Defendant’s filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s motion is untimely in that the 3 new Plaintiffs are all members of the original Plaintiff’s family living in the subject property. Clearly their claims were known, or should have been known, and could have been and should have been filed as part of the original Complaint. While Plaintiff’s new counsel may not have been dilatory, the new Plaintiffs themselves have been dilatory and unreasonably delayed the assertion of their claims to the prejudice of the Defendants.

·       Defendants are prejudiced by the proposed amendments.

 

Plaintiff filed a reply maintaining that the motion should be granted.

 

II. DISCUSSION

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” 

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.) Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. 

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend complies with Rules 3.1324(a) and (b) of the California Rules of Court. Plaintiff included a copy of the proposed First Amended Complaint, and has identified the proposed amendments to be added. (Bisno Decl., Ex. A, p. 24.) Plaintiff also provided a separate declaration specifying the effect of the amendments, why they are necessary and proper, and the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Bisno Decl., ¶¶¿10-14.) Plaintiff’s counsel asserts: “After reviewing the file when I took over the case, on or about August 30, 2024, visiting the Property several times, speaking with Plaintiff and her husband, Hamid Majma, seeing Plaintiff’s children, Kayeh Majma and Kamia Majma, reviewing information provided by Plaintiff, a contractor and an environmental consultant, I determined that the 4 additional causes of action sought in the FAC were factually and legally supportable and would permit a full litigation of all matters between the parties.” (Bisno Decl., ¶ 14.) 

 

Defendants argue in opposition that they will be prejudiced because the scope of litigation will be expanded by the proposed amendments and trial in this matter is set for June 2025.

 

The Court does not find Defendants’ arguments persuasive. As Plaintiff argues, the case is still in its early stage as Defendants have not yet filed responses to Plaintiff’s discovery. Further, if a plaintiff wants to expand the scope of a complaint to add additional legitimate legal theories, it is proper to do so and discovery is still open.

 

Based on the foregoing and the judicial policy favoring amendment, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion For Leave to File First Amended Complaint.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion For Leave to File First Amended Complaint.


Case Number: 24STCV03507    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: 73

12/20/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

GONZALEZ v. DEL TACO, LLC (24STCV03507)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Giuliano Dorian (Levin & Nalbandyan, LLP)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Andrew Satenberg (Manatt, Phelps & Phillips, LLP)

 

DEFENDANT’S motion to compel arbitration (filed 10/04/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration. Pursuant to CCP section 1281.4, all proceedings are STAYED pending the outcome of arbitration. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff Guadalupe Gonzalez filed this action against Defendant Del Taco, LLC.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Discrimination in violation of the California Family Rights Act

2.      Retaliation in violation of the California Family Rights Act

3.      Failure to reinstate in violation of the California Family Rights Act

4.      Disability discrimination in violation of FEHA

5.      Failure to provide reasonable accommodations in violation of FEHA

6.      Failure to engage in a good faith interactive process in violation of FEHA

7.      Retaliation in violation of FEHA

8.      Sex and gender discrimination in violation of FEHA

9.      Failure to prevent discrimination and retaliation in violation of FEHA

10.  Failure to reinstate in violation of Pregnancy Disability Leave Law

11.  Failure to provide reasonable accommodations in violation of Pregnancy Disability Leave Law

12.  Retaliation in violation of Pregnancy Disability Leave Law

13.  Wrongful termination in violation of public policy

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about June 18, 2015, Plaintiff began her employment with Defendant as a general manager. In or around April 2023, Plaintiff found out she was pregnant and proceeded to notify her employer of her pregnancy. Plaintiff’s doctor placed her on work restrictions of no standing for long periods of time and frequent breaks whenever possible. However, these restrictions were rarely accommodated because Defendant’s corporate location was understaffed, requiring Plaintiff to attend to the restaurant’s customers on the store floor and ensure that employees were completing tasks. In or around the middle of April 2023, Plaintiff submitted documents requesting Family and Medical Leave Act leave to Defendant’s Human Resources Personnel. Defendant’s Human Resources approved Plaintiff’s leave to begin on April 29, 2023, with an expected return to work date of July 22, 2023. On or about July 23, 2023, Plaintiff extended her leave to bond with her baby. Defendant approved Plaintiff’s baby bonding leave and her new expected return to work date was September 19, 2023. On or about July 24, 2023, Plaintiff was terminated, while she was still on leave.

 

On October 4, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration arguing:

·       Defendant brings this Motion on the grounds that Plaintiff consented to various versions of the Arbitration Agreement no less than six times during her employment with Defendant.

·       Plaintiff electronically signed the operative, superseding version of the Arbitration Agreement on August 24, 2022.

·       The Arbitration Agreement requires, to the maximum extent allowed by law, that all disputes between the parties be resolved through binding arbitration. The Arbitration Agreement is expressly governed by the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. § 1 et seq. (the “FAA”), covers all of Plaintiff’s claims in her Complaint for Damages (the “Complaint”), and is valid, enforceable, and not unconscionable.

·       The Court should compel Plaintiff to attend arbitration and stay proceedings.

 

On December 13, 2024, Defendant filed a Notice of Non-Opposition:

·       Defendant states that on December 4, 2024, counsel for plaintiff advised Del Taco’s counsel that they did not intend to oppose the Motion and would be initiating the arbitration process.

·       Therefore, Defendant requests that the Court grant the Motion in its entirety

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A written agreement where the parties agree to arbitration is valid, enforceable and 

irrevocable unless there is a contract defense. (C.C.P. § 1281.) “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿¿(C.C.P. § 1281.2.)  

¿ 

“[T]he petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence…”¿¿(Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc.¿(2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.)¿¿“In determining whether an arbitration agreement applies to a specific dispute, the court may examine only the agreement itself and the complaint filed by the party refusing arbitration [citation]. The court should attempt to give effect to the parties' intentions, in light of the usual and ordinary meaning of the contractual language and the circumstances under which the agreement was made.”¿¿(Weeks v. Crow¿(1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.) “To determine whether a contractual arbitration clause requires arbitration of a particular controversy, the controversy is first identified and the issue is whether that controversy is within the scope of the contractual arbitration clause.”¿(Titolo¿v. Cano¿(2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 310, 316.) “Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration. The court should order them to arbitrate unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.”¿(California Correctional Peace Officers¿Ass'n¿v. State¿(2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

There is strong public policy in favor of both arbitration of disputes and resolving any doubts concerning the scope of arbitrable disputes in favor of arbitration.¿(Moncharsh¿v.¿Heily¿&¿Blase¿(1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 9.) Arbitration agreements should be liberally interpreted, and arbitration should be ordered unless the agreement clearly does not apply to the dispute in question.¿¿(Vianna¿v. Doctors’ Management Co.¿(1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1186, 1189.)¿ 

 

B.      Existence of an Agreement to Arbitrate

 

The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)

 

Defendant presents an executed arbitration agreement (“the Agreement”) that is applicable to Plaintiff’s claims. (Brogdon Decl., Ex. A.) The Agreement states, in relevant part, “[t]o the maximum extent allowed by law, [Defendant] and [Plaintiff] mutually consent to the resolution by binding arbitration of all claims or causes of action … that [Defendant] may have against [Plaintiff], or that [Plaintiff] may have against [Defendant]….” (Brogdon Decl., at ¶ 8, Ex. A, “Claims Covered by this Agreement.”)

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges various causes of action for violations of the FEHA, CFRA and Pregnancy Disability Leave Law. The Agreement encompasses all of Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant, including “claims for discrimination or harassment, including, but not limited to … sex … medical condition, disability … pregnancy … claims for retaliation … claims for violation of … the California Fair Employment and Housing Act, the California Family Rights Act and the California Labor Code.” (Id., Ex. A, “Claims Covered by this Agreement.”). Accordingly, Defendant has met its burden of showing that a valid agreement to arbitrate exists between the parties. 

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition. Arbitration can be denied only where the party objecting to arbitration proves a defense to enforcement of the parties’ agreement to arbitrate, such as unconscionability. The objecting party bears the burden of proving that the arbitration agreement is both procedurally and substantively unconscionable. (Pinnacle Museum Tower Ass'n v. Pinnacle Market Dev. (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 235.) In fact, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition, stating that on December 4, 2024, counsel for Plaintiff advised defense counsel that they did not intend to oppose the Motion and would be initiating the arbitration process.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration. Pursuant to CCP section 1281.4, all proceedings are STAYED pending the outcome of arbitration. 

 

C.      Conclusion

 

For these foregoing reasons, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration. Pursuant to CCP section 1281.4, all proceedings are STAYED pending the outcome of arbitration.
