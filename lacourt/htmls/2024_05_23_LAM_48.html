[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO TRIFURCATE AS MOOT

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 23, 2024

 

On April 24, 2024, Plaintiff Hyung Koo filed a motion to trifurcate the trial in this action into (1) bench trial on single enterprise theory, (2) bench trial on joint venture theory, and (3) jury trial on negligence and other issues.

Plaintiff asserts that a finding of single enterprise or joint venture (among Defendants Central Fitness, Central Plaza LLC, Jamison Realty Advisors LLC, and Jamison Services Inc.) will be binding for liability in Phase Three.  (Motion at p. 8.)  However, on May 17, 2024, the Court granted summary judgment in favor of Central Plaza LLC, Jamison Realty Advisors LLC, and Jamison Services Inc.  Accordingly, there is no longer a need for a bench trial on the single enterprise or joint venture theories for these Defendants as they relate to Central Fitness.

The motion to trifurcate is DENIED AS MOOT.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 23, 2024

 

On November 1, 2021, Plaintiff Simon Parsakar filed this action against Defendant Shlomo Meiri for breach of a commercial lease.

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiff filed a motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.  The motion sought summary judgment or adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of lease of action, as well as Defendant’s second, fifth, twelfth, fifteenth, twenty-fifth, and twenty-sixth affirmative defenses.

On October 25, 2022, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment, but it granted summary adjudication of Defendant’s twenty-fifth affirmative defense of breach of contract.

On October 3, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to file a first amended complaint (“FAC”).  The FAC added alter ego allegations against Defendants Adriana Sahar Meiri and Adriana Sahar LLC.  It also added eight new causes of action that are additional legal theories for recovery of the same damages (unpaid rent) alleged in the original Complaint.

On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed another motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.  This motion again seeks summary judgment or adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of lease of action, as well as Defendant’s second, fifth, twelfth, fifteenth, twenty-fifth, and twenty-sixth affirmative defenses.  The substance of Plaintiff’s present motion is substantially similar to his prior motion, which the Court largely denied.

“A party shall not move for summary judgment based on issues asserted in a prior motion for summary adjudication and denied by the court unless that party establishes, to the satisfaction of the court, newly discovered facts or circumstances or a change of law supporting the issues reasserted in the summary judgment motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(2).)

The present motion makes no mention of the Court’s October 25, 2022 order on the first motion.  Plaintiff does not clearly present any new facts, new circumstances, or changes in law.

To the extent that Plaintiff raises new arguments about the applicability of Ordinance 186606 (Motion at pp. 7-8), this is not a new fact, new circumstance, or change in law.  Rather, Plaintiff is implicitly arguing that the Court incorrectly interpreted Ordinance 186606 in its prior order—without referring to the prior order.  Additionally, although Plaintiff may have served a demand letter on or around March 3, 2023, that is still a demand for the same rent demanded on or about October 18, 2021.  (Complaint ¶ 19; FAC ¶ 22.)

Plaintiff wholly failed to address the Court’s order on the first motion and did not clearly explain or establish, to the Court’s satisfaction, any “newly discovered facts or circumstances or a change of law supporting the issues reasserted in the summary judgment motion.”

The motion for summary judgment/adjudication is DENIED.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO SEAL

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 23, 2024

 

On April 23, 2024, Cross-Defendant Stacy Summers filed a motion for summary judgment (scheduled for hearing on July 9, 2024) and a motion to seal certain documents.  Cross-Complainants filed a notice of non-opposition.

The Court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it finds that (1) there is an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record, (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record, (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced absent sealing, (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, and (5) no less restrictive means exists to achieve the overriding interest.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)  A motion seeking an order sealing records must be accompanied by a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)

A record must not be filed under seal without a court order, and a party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(a)-(b).)  The party requesting sealing must also file a public redacted version and lodge conditionally under seal with the court a complete, unredacted version.  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(4)-(b)(5).)  Additionally, pursuant to Department 48’s courtroom procedures, if a motion requires reliance on confidential and/or lodged filings, the unredacted versions must be emailed to smcdept48@lacourt.org, in accordance with Department 48’s Courtroom Information, available on the court’s website (www.lacourt.org).

Cross-Defendant lodged redacted copies of the Motion for Summary Judgment, Exhibit A to the Declaration of Larry R. Jackson in Support of the Motion, and Statement of Undisputed Facts in Support of the Motion.  Cross-Defendant did not provide the Court with unredacted electronic copies.  Because the documents were not properly lodged with the Court, the Court is unable to review them.

Even if Cross-Defendant had followed the proper procedures, the accompanying declaration does not contain facts sufficient to justify the sealing.  (See California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)  Cross-Defendant’s counsel declares only that the motion refers to testimony that has been designated as Confidential under the Protective Order, and the Protective Order “requires any such documents to be submitted under seal with the Court by any party desiring to cite to them in support of moving papers.”  (Jackson Decl. ¶ 3.)  However, “[t]he court must not permit a record to be filed under seal based solely on the agreement or stipulation of the parties.”  (California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(a).)

The motion to seal is DENIED, without prejudice to the filing of a renewed motion that satisfies all requirements for sealing.

For any future motions that rely on confidential and/or lodged filings, the unredacted versions must be emailed to smcdept48@lacourt.org, in accordance with Department 48’s Courtroom Information, available on the court’s website (www.lacourt.org).

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO TAX COSTS

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

May 23, 2024

 

On April 23, 2024, the Court granted summary judgment in favor of Defendant State Farm General Insurance Company and against Plaintiff Jennifer Bornoff.

On April 26, 2024, Defendant filed a memorandum of costs.  On April 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to tax costs.  The Court entered judgment in favor of Defendant on May 16, 2024.

If a plaintiff rejects a defendant’s settlement offer under Code of Civil Procedure section 998 and the plaintiff fails to obtain a more favorable judgment, the plaintiff may not recover their post-offer costs and must pay the defendant’s costs from the time of the offer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (c)(1).)  The court may also award the defendant their reasonable costs for expert witnesses.  (Ibid.)

On December 23, 2022, Defendant served a section 998 offer on Plaintiff.  (McKeon Decl., Ex. A.)  Plaintiff did not respond, and she did not obtain a more favorable judgment.  (McKeon Decl. ¶ 2.)  Accordingly, Defendant is entitled to its costs, including its expert fees.

Plaintiff moves to tax the expert witness fees and some electronic filing fees.  “A ‘verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of the propriety’ of the items listed on it, and the burden is on the party challenging these costs to demonstrate that they were not reasonable or necessary.”  (Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1486 (Adams).)  “[I]f the correctness of the memorandum is challenged either in whole or in part by the affidavit or other evidence of the contesting party, the burden is then on the party claiming the costs and disbursements to show that the items charged were for matters necessarily relevant and material to the issues involved in the action.”  (Oak Grove School Dist. of Santa Clara County v. City Title Ins. Co. (1963) 217 Cal.App.2d 678, 699.)

Plaintiff argues that “Defendant will be unable to show any evidence that the expert was paid for anything more than a convenient or beneficial purpose to their preparation of the Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication.”  (Motion at p. 4.)  Plaintiff notes that “Defendant is the party that served a request for expert disclosure and merely hired an expert in the event the case went at trial.”  (Ibid.)  On January 22 and 23, 2024, Defendant’s “bad faith” expert witness reviewed the insurance claim files for the two claims at issue in this case and the two deposition transcripts of State Farm’s employees in preparation for the trial, which was then scheduled for March 18, 2024.  (McKeon Decl. ¶ 5.)  On February 7, 2024, the parties stipulated to a continuance of the trial date.  It was reasonable for Defendant to prepare an expert witness in January 2024 for a March 18, 2024 trial date, and Defendant is entitled to its costs “actually incurred and reasonably necessary in . . . preparation for trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (c)(1).)

Plaintiff also argues that the electronic filing fees are “quite frankly excessive.”  (Motion at p. 4.)  In reply, Defendant provides invoices that support the costs.  (McKeon Decl. ¶ 3 & Ex. B.)  “Fees for the electronic filing or service of documents through an electronic filing service provider” are recoverable if—as here—the court requires electronic filing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(14).)

The motion to tax costs is DENIED.
