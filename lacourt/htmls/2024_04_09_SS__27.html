DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV02938    Hearing Date: April 9, 2024    Dept: 27

Complaint Filed: 1/25/2021 

Trial Date: 7/15/2024 

¿¿¿ 

Hon. Lee S. Arian 

Department 27 

Tentative Ruling 

¿¿ 

Hearing Date: 4/9/2024 at 1:30 p.m. 

Case No./Name: 21STCV02938 HEDYEH HAMEDANIZADEH vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES 

Motion: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AN AMENDED COMPLAINT 

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendant Glenoaks Business Park 

Notice: Sufficient 

¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AN AMENDED COMPLAINT IS DENIED 

 

Background 

On January 25, 2021, Plaintiff filed the current trip and fall lawsuit, claiming she tripped over a bolt protruding from the concrete in the driveway of a property owned by Defendant GLENOAKS BUSINESS PARK and occupied by Defendant COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES. Plaintiff asserts that recent depositions of GLENOAKS' person most knowledgeable (PMK) and written discovery have unveiled facts that justify punitive damages. Plaintiff is now requesting the Court's approval to amend the complaint to seek punitive damages against Defendant GLENOAKS. Defendant argues that Plaintiff had knowledge of the facts supporting punitive damages for over two years but chose to seek an amendment only several months before the scheduled trial date. 

 

Standard 

 

When a party moves to amend a pleading, “courts generally should permit amendment to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial. [Citations.]” (Melican v. Regents of University of California (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 168, 175.) In ruling on this type of motion, prejudice to another party is the main concern. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486.) The type of prejudice the court is to be concerned with should be something beyond simply having to cope with a potentially successful new legal theory of recovery that has been revealed during discovery. (Ibid.) Instead, the court should look for delays in the trial date, loss of critical evidence, extensive increase in the costs of preparation and other similar circumstances that create prejudice to another party. (Melican, supra, 151 Cal.App.4th at p. 176.) 

 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(3).)¿ A motion to amend a pleading must also be supported by a declaration which specifies the following: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b).) 

Analysis 

Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration, incorrectly titled a “Declaration for Additional Discovery,” to support the Motion. The Declaration fails to specify when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b)(3).) In the Motion, Plaintiff cites multiple pieces of evidence to support the punitive damages allegations: a deposition on February 26, 2024, a request for admissions served on November 6, 2023, and another deposition on November 21, 2022. It remains unclear, however, whether the facts justifying punitive damages were discovered in 2022 or 2024. Moreover, and most significantly, Plaintiff failed to offer justifications in the declaration for the delay in seeking the amendment, as required by Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b)(4). 

Additionally, Plaintiff did not specify what specific allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, nor did Plaintiff detail the exact location of the proposed additions by page, paragraph, and line number. The motion conveys Plaintiff's intention to amend the complaint to include punitive damages against Defendant Glenoaks and to add certain facts supporting the prayer for punitive damages. However, Plaintiff failed to specify in the Declaration these facts. 

Thus, the present motion is denied without prejudice for failure to comply with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324.

Case No./Name.: 21STCV12624 MARGARET BOCK vs CITY OF LA MIRADA 

Motion Name: Motion to Compel Further Testimony at Deposition 

Moving Party: Defendant WEST COAST ARBORISTS, INC 

Responding Party: Defendant CITY OF LA MIRADA 

Notice: Sufficient 

¿¿¿¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER TESTIMONY AT DEPOSITION IS DENIED. 

 

Background 

On April 2, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present trip and fall case against Defendant City of La Mirada. La Mirada filed a cross-complaint against Defendant West Coast Arborists (WCA) for indemnity. On December 7, 2023, Defendant WCA took the deposition of Marlin Munoz, an employee of the City of La Mirada, during which counsel for the City of La Mirada instructed Munoz not to answer two lines of questioning because they were duplicative and harassing. Consequently, WCA filed a motion to compel Munoz to provide further testimony. Currently, the last day to hear discovery motions is February 11, 2024, but the motion to compel is set to be heard on April 9, 2024. 

Legal Standard and Analysis 

Pursuant to CCP § 2024.020, "except as otherwise provided in this section, any party shall be entitled as a matter of right … to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action. A continuance or postponement of the trial date does not operate to reopen discovery proceedings." 

The trial date controlling the discovery cut-off is February 26, 2024. Therefore, the discovery cut-off date was January 27, 2024, and the last date to have motions concerning discovery heard was on or before February 11, 2024. The current discovery motion is set to be heard on April 9, 2024. 

Although the final status conference (FSC) and the trial date were continued from February 9, 2024, to July 15, 2024 and from February 26, 2024, to July 29, 2024, respectively, the court specified in its January 18, 2024 minute order that the discovery deadline remains tied to the February 26, 2024 trial date unless a stipulation or noticed motion is filed. However, no motion to reopen discovery or a stipulation to reopen discovery was filed by Defendant after the Court issued its order on January 18, 2024. 

When the hearing for a discovery motion is held after the discovery motion hearing cut-off date, the Court cannot grant a motion to compel without first deciding on a motion to reopen discovery. (Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1588.) Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to compel is DENIED.

¿¿ 

Hearing Date: 4/9/2024 at 1:30 p.m. 

Case No./Name: 21STCV19391 PEDRAM PANAHI vs ALEJANDRO BARRAZA MENDEZ, 

Motion: MOTIONS TO COMPEL AND FOR SANCTIONS 

Moving Party: Defendant Hannah McCormack 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS, SET ONE, FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, SET ONE, ARE DENIED. 

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS IS GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART. 

 

Background 

 

On May 20, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present auto accident case. On December 8, 2023, Defendant Hannah McCormack served Requests for Admission, Set One, Requests for Production, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Plaintiff. Plaintiff served responses to the discovery requests at issue on January 9, 2023. Plaintiff’s responses to Requests for Production consist of only objections, responses to Form Interrogatories contained only fact-specific responses, and responses to Requests for Admission consist of objections and fact-specific responses to each request. The discovery responses were unverified, and Defendant gave Plaintiff 10 days to provide the verifications. After receiving no verifications, Defendant moves the Court to compel verified discovery responses to the requests at issue. Defendant argues that the motion should be denied because there was no meet and confer, no informal discovery conference held, and verified responses have already been served. 

Legal Standard 

 

  1. Requests for Production and Interrogatories 

 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and, upon declaration, any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.) 

 

  1. Request for Admissions 

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ CCP § 2033.010.¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”¿ CCP § 2033.250(a). 

¿¿ 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c). Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to deem RFA admitted does not have any meet and confer requirements. 

 

  1. Hybrid Responses 

Verifications are generally required, unless the response “contain[s] only objections.” (See §§ 2030.250(a), 2031.250(a) and 2033.240(a).) Unverified responses to discovery are tantamount to no responses at all. (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632.) When a discovery response served within the statutory deadline includes both objections and unverified fact-specific answers, verification is not necessary for the objections to be deemed timely. There is no requirement for objections to be verified and objections must be considered timely notwithstanding the lack of verification. (Food 4 Less Supermarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 651, 657.) Only the fact-specific responses must be verified. A party may file a motion for orders and sanctions regarding a portion of the response that contains unverified fact-specific responses. This does not constitute a waiver of objections. The only scenario where a lack of verification leads to both an untimely response and a waiver of objections is if the initial, unverified response served within the timeline does not contain any objections. (Id.) 

Analysis 

For a motion to compel initial responses, meet and confer and an IDC is not required. These are requirements for a motion to compel further. However, Plaintiff, in its opposition, stated that verified responses were provided prior to the hearing, and Defendant, in the reply, does not dispute this. Thus, the present motions to compel are moot and therefore DENIED. The matter of sanctions still remains at issue. 

Since Plaintiff's responses to requests for admission consist only of objections, Plaintiff was not required to serve verifications. Thus, the sanction relating to the Request for Admission is DENIED. However, Plaintiff's responses to requests for production and form interrogatories are fact-specific and therefore required verification. Plaintiff’s lack of verification compelled Defendant to incur attorney fees to file the present motions. Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $735.00 per motion. The Court finds this amount reasonable. The Court ORDERS Plaintiff and his counsel, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $1470 to Defendant within 20 days of today’s date.

Motion: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL 

Moving Party: Plaintiff’s Counsel Daniel Moossai 

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Insufficient 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL IS DENIED. 

¿¿¿ 

¿ 

Background 

¿¿ 

Attorney Daniel Moossai represents Plaintiff.  Moosai moves to be relieved as counsel, citing an irremediable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. No opposition has been filed. 

¿¿ 

Legal Standard 

¿¿ 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.) 

¿¿ 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).) 

¿¿ 

Analysis and Conclusion 

¿¿ 

Moosai has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Moosai seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).) 

 

However, the Court cannot grant the motion because no proof of service of the motion on Plaintiff has been filed. Moreover, the next hearing for an Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal for Failure to File Proof of Service and Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal for Failure to File Default Judgment as to SDLA Courier Service, Inc. is scheduled for April 15, 2024. This timeframe is insufficient for Plaintiff to retain new counsel or be prepared to represent himself.  Accordingly, the motion is denied.
