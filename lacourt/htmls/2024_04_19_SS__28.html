DEPARTMENT 28 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SSCDEPT28@lacourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.     If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect.

                                       Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.  This does not excuse a moving party's need to do one of the following: appear; submit; or take a matter off calendar by canceling the motion in the case reservation system before issuance of the tentative ruling if the matter moving party does not intend to proceed.    
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  


Case Number: 21STCV47154    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the petitioning papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On December 28, 2021, Plaintiff Saghar Yadegar, by and through her guardian ad litem Samira Yadegar (“Plaintiff”), filed this action against Defendants Nestor R. Gonzalez, M.D. (“Gonzalez”), Zachary Barnard, M.D. (“Barnard”), Cedars-Sinai Health System (“Cedars-Sinai”), and Does 1-50 for medical negligence. 

On May 17, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint. 

On June 20, 2022, Gonzalez and Cedars-Sinai filed an answer. 

On June 21, 2022, the Court dismissed Barnard with prejudice at Plaintiff’s request. On December 28, 2023, the Court dismissed Gonzalez with prejudice at Plaintiff’s request. 

On August 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed a notice of settlement. 

On January 19, 2024, Petitioner Samira Yadegar (Petitioner) filed a revised petition to approve the compromise of a claim of a person with a disability, set for February 8, 2024. 

On February 8, 2024, the Court heard argument and continued the matter to March 18, 2024. 

On February 29 and March 13, 2024, Petitioner filed supplements to the revised petition. 

On March 18, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on the revised petition to April 19, 2024.  The Court ordered Petitioner’s counsel to file a supplemental declaration concerning the outcome of a Probate Court hearing scheduled for April 12, 2024 in Plaintiff’s conservatorship case. 

No trial date is currently scheduled. 

PETITIONER’S REQUEST 

Petitioner asks the Court to approve the compromise of the pending action of Plaintiff Saghar Yadegar. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

The parties to a lawsuit may settle the claim of a person who lacks the capacity to make decisions only with court approval.  (Prob. Code., §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372; see Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1337.) 

A petition for court approval of a compromise or covenant not to sue under Code of Civil Procedure section 372 must comply with California Rules of Court, rules 7.950, 7.951 and 7.952. The petition must be verified by the petitioner and contain a full disclosure of all information that has “any bearing upon the reasonableness” of the compromise or the covenant. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.) 

The person compromising the claim on behalf of the person who lacks capacity, and the represented person, must attend the hearing on compromise of the claim unless the court for good cause dispenses with their personal appearance. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.952.) 

DISCUSSION 

A.   Civil Code section 3333.1 

          Petitioner asserts that Civil Code section 3333.1 (“§ 3333.1”) applies to all the medical care which Plaintiff has received as a result of Defendants’ alleged negligence.  Blue Shield of California paid for Plaintiff’s extensive medical care. Petitioner states that Plaintiff has not received Medi-Cal or Medicare benefits in connection with her injuries and she is not the beneficiary of a self-funded ERISA plan.  No other insurance plan has paid for Plaintiff’s care and no providers have rendered care related to Plaintiff’s injuries on the promise of future payment specifically from Plaintiff’s recovery, e.g. on a lien basis. (Verified Supplement to Revised Petition for Compromise of Claim pp. 2-4; see Z. Haning et al., Cal. Practice Guide: Personal Injury (Rutter 2023) ¶¶ 3:2050 – 3:2068, pp. 3-394 to 398 (Cal. Practice Guide: Personal Injury).)   

          “The § 3333.1 reimbursement/subrogation bar applies whenever plaintiffs have ‘demonstrably’ had their damages recovery ‘reduced to reflect [§ 3333.1] collateral source contributions’ . . . notwithstanding that there was no trial, no evidence of collateral source benefits introduced, and no jury consideration of the issue.  Thus, a damages recovery by pretrial settlement triggers the bar when it is shown that the settlement sum excluded compensation for losses paid by the collateral source provider.”  (Cal. Practice Guide: Personal Injury, supra, ¶ 3:2068, p. 3-397; see Graham v. WCAB (1989) 210 Cal.App.3d 499, 508.)    

          Petitioner has provided a June 24, 2022 letter from The Rawlings Company’s subrogation division stating: “As you know, we are writing on behalf of Blue Shield of California.  We are closing this file and are no longer pursuing this matter.”  The letter suggests that the settlement amount does not include compensation for losses paid by Blue Shield of California; if it did, The Rawlings Company presumably would pursue reimbursement of those payments. 

          Based on Petitioner’s supplemental filing, it appears that under Civil Code section 3333.1, Plaintiff’s medical expenses need not be reimbursed from the settlement proceeds. 

B.   Conservatorship case 

Petitioner’s March 13, 2024 supplemental filing states that on February 29, 2024, Petitioner filed (1) a Petition to Authorize Conservator to Marshal Assets Resulting From Compromise of Medical Malpractice Settlement, (2) a Petition for Order to File Bond for Conservator, (3) a Petition to Invest in Mutual Funds and U.S. Government Bonds with Maturity Dates Later than Five Years, and (4) a Petition to Set Review Hearing Date for Filing of the First and Current Account of Conservator in the conservatorship case, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 21STPB11784. The hearing on the petitions was scheduled for April 12, 2024 in Department ST62. 

In light of the April 12, 2024 date for the conservatorship petitions, on March 18, 2024 the Court continued the hearing on the petition to approve the compromise of Plaintiff’s claim to April 19, 2024.  The Court ordered Petitioner’s counsel to file a supplemental declaration or other pleading addressing the Probate Court’s rulings. 

Petitioner has not yet filed a supplemental declaration or pleading describing the Probate Court’s rulings.  Therefore, the Court will continue the hearing once again. 

C.   Proposed payment of $250,000.00 to Petitioner 

Petitioner argues that Plaintiff has the right to the reasonable value of nursing services required by the Defendants’ tortious conduct even if a family member provided the services without an agreement or expectation of payment.  According to Petitioner, “[w]here services in the way of attendance and nursing are rendered by a member of the plaintiff's family, the amount for which the defendant is liable is the amount for which reasonably competent nursing and attendance by others could have been obtained.”  (Verified Supplement to Revised Petition for Compromise of Claim p. 6, citing Hanif v. Housing Authority (1988) 200 Cal.App.3d 635, 644-645.)  Petitioner acknowledges that the right belongs to Plaintiff, not Petitioner. 

Petitioner nonetheless argues that it is appropriate to pay her $250,000.00 out of the settlement funds because she has acted as Plaintiff’s primary caregiver since July 13, 2021 and assists with essentially all of Plaintiff’s activities of daily living. 

Petitioner also argues that the Court hearing this civil matter may approve the requested compensation rather than the Court hearing the conservatorship case.  Petitioner concedes that Probate Code section 2641 would require the conservatorship Court to address the issue if the settlement funds were “currently within the [conservatorship].”  Petitioner argues, however, that “the present situation is distinguishable in that it involves a settlement arising out of civil litigation and that payment is not being requested from the conservatorship assets, but rather from the settlement funds being tendered by Defendant Cedars-Sinai Health System.” 

The Court greatly appreciates Petitioner’s services to Plaintiff.  However, the Court that is hearing the conservatorship case should consider the request to transfer $250,000.00 of Plaintiff's assets to Petitioner, who is also Plaintiff's conservator. 

Anticipating that the Court might defer to the Probate Court on this issue, Petitioner asks the Court to “carve out” the proposed $250,000.00 payment to Petitioner and approve the revised petition “such that the remainder of the compromise process may go forward, including the funding of annuities, payments of legal fees and expenses in [connection] with the medical negligence case, and the lump sum payment of the remaining net proceeds to the conservatorship estate.”  Petitioner asks that the Court allow $250,000.00 to “remain in Plaintiff’s counsel’s trust account pending final judicial determinations, not unlike when there is a disputed lien pending resolution.” 

The Court declines to approve the compromise of Plaintiff’s action without determining the distribution of all the settlement funds. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court CONTINUES the hearing on the revised petition to approve the compromise of Plaintiff Saghar Yadegar’s claims filed by Petitioner Samira Yadegar on January 19, 2024, as supplemented by Petitioner’s February 29, 2024 and March 13, 2024 filings.  The Court will provide a continued hearing date at the April 19, 2024 hearing. 

Petitioner is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Petitioner is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 22STCV03821    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On January 31, 2022, Plaintiff Iris Alcala (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Super Center Concepts, Inc., Superior Grocers, Inc., and Does 1-50 for general negligence and premises liability. 

On September 2, 2022, Defendant Super Center Concepts, Inc. dba Superior Grocers (“Defendant”) filed an answer. 

On February 13, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for protective order and for sanctions to be heard on April 19, 2024. On April 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.  On April 12, 2024, Defendant filed a reply. 

Trial is currently scheduled for June 24, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Defendant asks the Court to allow Plaintiff to inspect Defendant’s premises only between 7:00 a.m. and 9:00 a.m. on a Monday, Tuesday, Thursday or Friday.  Defendant also asks the Court to impose sanctions on Plaintiff. 

Plaintiff asks the Court to deny the motion and impose sanctions on Defendant. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.060 provides in part: 

“(a) When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

“(b) The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense. This protective order may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions: 

* * * 

“(4) That the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling be made only on specified terms and conditions. 

* * * 

“(h) Except as provided in subdivision (i), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion for a protective order, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. 

“(i) (1) Notwithstanding subdivision (h), absent exceptional circumstances, the court shall not impose sanctions on a party or any attorney of a party for failure to provide electronically stored information that has been lost, damaged, altered, or overwritten as the result of the routine, good faith operation of an electronic information system. 

“(2) This subdivision shall not be construed to alter any obligation to preserve discoverable information.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subds. (a), (b)(4), (h), (i).) 

DISCUSSION 

Plaintiff alleges that on November 16, 2020, she slipped and fell on a slippery substance near the checkout line of Defendant’s premises, sustaining injuries.  (Complaint p. 6.)  On October 10, 2023, Plaintiff served an amended request to inspect Defendant’s premises, including “testing, inspecting, measuring, and/or photographing and videotaping the surface of the [premises],” on December 13, 2023 at 12:00 p.m. 

According to Defendant, Plaintiff wishes to inspect Defendant’s premises at 11:00 a.m., which is a busy time for the store.  Defendant argues that the inspection would involve placement of a liquid hazard on the floor, barricades, and redirection of traffic, drawing customer attention. Defendant has asked Plaintiff to conduct the inspection before the store opens or soon after it opens to minimize disruption.  The parties have met and conferred in an attempt to resolve this dispute, without success.  Therefore, Defendant asks the Court to issue a protective order allowing Plaintiff to inspect Defendant’s premises only between 7:00 a.m. and 9:00 a.m. on a Monday, Tuesday, Thursday or Friday. 

Plaintiff responds that Defendant has failed to demonstrate that an inspection at 11:00 a.m. or afterward would impose unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense on Defendant.  Plaintiff also argues that “inspections generally occur during business hours to replicate the environment at the time of the incident.”  According to Plaintiff, the accident occurred at 7:00 in the evening. 

The Court finds good cause and grants Defendant’s motion.  Plaintiff has not shown that an inspection during the time frame Defendant requests will deprive Plaintiff of discovery to which she is entitled. 

Sanctions are mandatory under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.060, subdivision (h), absent a finding that that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.  Defendant has not explained how it calculated the amount of the sanctions it is requesting ($707.50).  The Court therefore finds that it would be unjust to grant Defendant’s request for sanctions. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Super Center Concepts, Inc. dba Superior Grocers’s motion for a protective order.  Plaintiff Iris Alcala’s inspection of Defendant’s premises will take place between 7:00 a.m. and 9:00 a.m. on a Monday, Tuesday, Thursday or Friday.

The Court denies both parties' requests for sanctions. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling. 

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling with the Court within five days.

Case Number: 22STCV40921    Hearing Date: April 19, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On December 29, 2022, Plaintiff Elias Acevedo Holguin (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Bryan James Hammer (“Hammer”), Super T Transport, Inc. (“Super T”), 99 Cents Only Stores, LLC (“99 Cents Only”), PP Tango CA LLC, Link Logistics Real Estate LLC, Allied Universal Security Services Universal Protection Service LPP, Realty Associates Fund X, LP, Washington Food Center Association, and Does 1- 100 for battery and negligence. 

On July 11, 2023, 99 Cents Only filed an answer and a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants Hammer, Super T, and Roes 1-10 for total equitable indemnity, partial equitable indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief. 

On August 15, 2023, the clerk entered Super T’s default on the complaint. On September 1, 2023, the Court set aside the default based on the stipulation of Plaintiff and Super T. 

On September 19, 2023, Super T filed an answer to Plaintiff's complaint. On October 16, 2023, Hammer filed an answer to Plaintiff's complaint. 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed (1) a motion to compel responses to demands for inspection and copying, set one, and for sanctions, and (2) a motion to compel responses to form interrogatories, set one, and for sanctions.  The motions were set for hearing on April 19, 2024.  On April 8, 2024, Super T filed oppositions.  On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed replies. 

On April 12, 2024, 99 Cents Only filed a notice of stay of proceedings (bankruptcy). 

Trial is currently scheduled for June 27, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Plaintiff asks the Court to compel Super T to respond to Plaintiff’s demands for inspection and copying, set one, and form interrogatories, set one.  Plaintiff also asks the Court to impose sanctions on Super T. 

Super T asks the Court to deny Plaintiff's motions and to impose sanctions on Plaintiff. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

A.   Inspection demand 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300 provides: 

“If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, the following rules shall apply: 

“(a) The party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010). The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: 

“(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. 

“(2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. 

“(b) The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand. 

“(c) Except as provided in subdivision (d), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey the order compelling a response, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). 

“(d) (1) Notwithstanding subdivision (c), absent exceptional circumstances, the court shall not impose sanctions on a party or any attorney of a party for failure to provide electronically stored information that has been lost, damaged, altered, or overwritten as a result of the routine, good faith operation of an electronic information system. 

“(2) This subdivision shall not be construed to alter any obligation to preserve discoverable information.” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) 

B.   Interrogatories 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290 provides: 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply: 

“(a) The party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010). The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: 

“(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. 

“(2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. 

“(b) The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories. 

“(c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey an order compelling answers, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290.) 

DISCUSSION 

Plaintiff argues that on August 29, 2023, he served demands for inspection and copying, set one, and form interrogatories, set one, on Super T.  Super T did not provide timely responses and had not provided responses by the time Plaintiff filed these motions on March 19, 2024.  Plaintiff argues the Court should therefore compel Super T’s responses, without objections, to these discovery requests. 

In response, Super T argues that (1) it was in default when Plaintiff served the discovery requests and therefore the service was improper, (2) Plaintiff failed to confirm the electronic service address of Service T’s counsel before electronically serving the discovery requests, making the service ineffective, and (3) Plaintiff failed to meet and confer before filing these motions, entitling Super T to sanctions. 

The Court denies Plaintiff’s motions to compel.  Super T was in default from August 15, 2023 to September 1, 2023.  Plaintiff attempted to serve the discovery requests on August 29, 2023, while Super T was in default.  As a result, the service was not effective and Super T was not required to respond to the discovery which Plaintiff attempted to serve on August 29, 2024. 

The Court grants sanctions against Plaintiff based on Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.300, subdivision (c), and 2030.290, subdivision (c).  Super T requests sanctions of $1,750.00 on each motion apparently based on five hours of attorney time at a rate of $350.00 per hour.  The Court grants $750.00 in sanctions on both motions based on three hours of attorney time at a reasonable rate of $250.00 per hour. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff Elias Acevedo Holguin’s motion to compel responses to demands for inspection and copying, set one. 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff Elias Acevedo Holguin’s motion to compel responses to form interrogatories, set one. 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff Elias Acevedo Holguin’s requests for sanctions. 

The Court GRANTS IN PART Defendant Super T Transport, Inc.’s requests for sanctions and orders Plaintiff Elias Acevedo Holguin and his counsel to pay Defendant Super T Transport, Inc. $750.00 by May 20, 2024.  In all other respects, the Court DENIES Defendant Super T Transport, Inc.'s requests for sanctions. 

Moving party is to give notice of the Court’s ruling. 

Moving party is to file proof of service of the Court’s ruling within five days.
