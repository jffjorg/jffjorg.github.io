DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV20750    Hearing Date: June 21, 2024    Dept: 73

6/21/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

FULLER, et al. v. SKETCHBOX LLC, et al. (22STCV20750)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  Virginia Ksadzhikyan (B B Law Group LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/ opposing party: Alton Burkhalter (Burkhalter Kessler Clement & George LLP)

 

(1)   PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER ANSWERS TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE PROPOUNDED ON DEFENDANT JON GOUGEON (filed on 05/06/2024)

(2)   PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER ANSWERS TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE PROPOUNDED ON DEFENDANT SKETCHBOX LLC (filed on 05/07/2024)

(3)   PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SETS ONE AND TWO, PROPOUNDED OND DEFENDANT SKETCHBOX LLC (filed on 05/06/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57 propounded on both Defendants Jon Gougeon and Sketchbox LLC is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 38 propounded on both Defendants Jon Gougeon and Sketchbox LLC is DENIED.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 127-130 propounded on Defendant Sketchbox LLC is GRANTED.  

 

Defendants shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

The Court DENIES the parties’ requests for sanctions.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 24, 2022, Plaintiffs Torie Fuller (“Fuller”) and Shady Peach Solutions, LLC brought this action against Defendants Sketchbox LLC (“Sketchbox”) and Jon Gougeon (“Gougeon”).

 

On July 13, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) asserting 7 causes of action for: (1) breach of oral contract; (2) breach of implied contract; (3) breach of written contract; (4) breach of the implied duty of good faith and fair dealing; (5) fraudulent inducement by misrepresentation; (6) accounting and (7) declaratory relief.

 

The SAC alleges the following. Defendant Gougeon formed Sketchbox on or about May 2015, as a single member Limited Liability Company, with Gougeon being its only member. Shortly after the company was formed, Fuller began assisting Gougeon in operating and managing Sketchbox. On or around September 2015, Gougeon represented to Fuller that he intended to sell Sketchbox when it reached 100,000 subscribes, and promised to pay Plaintiff 10% of the profits from the sale in exchange for Plaintiff’s services, which included assisting managing Sketchbox to make it operational and functional, including but not limited, to assisting in customer services, billing, operations, and later supervision of employees. On or about 2016, Gougen and Fuller modified their oral agreement and agreed that Fuller would be given a 10% profit share interest in Sketchbox, which she would receive through periodic profit share distributions. However, Fuller alleges that Defendants made periodic profit share distributions to Fuller, but they rarely included the full amount owed to Plaintiffs. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants promised to pay the remainder of Plaintiffs’ payments at a later time when additional funds came in. On or about April 11, 2022, Defendants terminated Plaintiffs’ services with Defendants and have refused to pay any of the debt owed to Plaintiff.

 

A.     Motions to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded on Defendant Jon Gougeon and Defendant Sketchbox

 

On March 20, 2024, Plaintiffs initially filed the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded on Defendant Jon Gougeon and its Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded on Defendant Sketchbox. On May 6, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an amended Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded on Defendant Jon Gougeon. On May 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an amended Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded on Defendant Sketchbox. In both motions, Plaintiffs argue:

·       Plaintiffs seek further response to Request Nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, 57 propounded on both Defendants.

·       Plaintiff initiated an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) with the Court. At the IDC, the Court conveyed that Defendants should provide supplemental responses and Defendants agreed to do so. (Ksadzhikyan Decl., ¶ 11 & Exh. 6.) On March 8, 2024, Defendants produced supplemental verified responses. Defendants did not, however, provide any further response and refused to provide responsive documents.

·       Plaintiffs request that the court impose a monetary sanction in the amount of $4,287.

Defendants filed oppositions to both motions, arguing:

·       The Disputed Discovery Requests Seek Information Neither Relevant to the Cause of Action for Breach of Written Contract Nor Likely to Lead to the Discovery of Admissible Evidence.

·       Plaintiffs’ request for sanctions should be denied and instead sanctions should be imposed against Plaintiffs in the amount of $5,000. In the alternative, the Court should not impose sanctions against Gougeon.

 

Plaintiffs filed replies to both motions maintaining that Defendants must provide further response and produce responsive documents.

 

B.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Sets One and Two, Propounded on Defendant Sketchbox

 

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.” 

 

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that: 

“(1)   A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. 

  (2)   A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. 

  (3)   An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”   

 

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”   

 

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

B.     Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.) 

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel submitted a declaration stated the parties met and conferred as to Defendants’ allegedly deficient discovery responses. (Ksadzhikyan Decl., ¶¶ 6-14.) The parties were unable to resolve these issues and the parties attended an Informal Discovery Conference with the Court to address these issues. After the IDC, Defendants indicated they would provide supplemental responses. On March 8, 2024, Defendants produced supplemental responses. Plaintiffs state Defendants did not provide additional responses to RFP Nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, or 57 or Special Rogs 127-130. Thus, Plaintiffs filed the instant motions. (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiffs’ failed to meaningfully meet and confer after receiving Defendants’ supplemental responses. However, the Court will analyze the motions on the merits despite this deficiency because it recognizes that the parties previously met and conferred on these issues and Defendants’ supplemental responses did not include any new responses to the discovery requests at issue.

 

 

C.     Discussion

                                                    i.     Motions to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Propounded on Defendant Jon Gougeon and Defendant Sketchbox

 

Plaintiffs move to compel Defendants Jon Gougeon and Sketchbox to provide further responses to RFP Nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 38, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57.

 

As an initial matter, to the extent Defendants asserts a privacy privilege, they must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].)

 

Further, the Court notes that to the extent Defendants believe that no further documents exist or that the documents have already been produced, Defendants are ordered to state that fact.  

 

1.     RFP Nos. 14-15

 

RFP No 14 seeks: Please produce all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA from YOUR bank that RELATE TO payments YOU made to PLAINTIFF.

 

RFP No. 15 seeks: Please produce all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA from YOUR bank that RELATES TO payments YOU made to Plaintiff Shady Peach.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. The lawsuit pertains to whether Defendants made all payments allegedly owed to Plaintiff. Thus, the requested information is relevant and the request is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 14 and 15 is GRANTED.  

 

2.     RFP Nos. 21, 36, 37

 

RFP Nos. 21, 36 and 37 seek Defendants’ tax returns.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. Plaintiff is alleging that an agreement was reached entitling Plaintiffs to 10% of Sketchbox, LLC’s profits. In order to calculate damages – i.e., what 10% of the profits was – information from Sketchbox, LLC’s tax returns would be relevant to show Sketchbox, LLC’s income and expenses – i.e., its profit.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 21, 36 and 37 is GRANTED.  

 

3.     RFP No. 34

 

RFP No. 34 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS related to YOUR termination, suspension, or modification of any and all contracts entered into with PLAINTIFF.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. The responsive documents would likely contain evidence pertaining to Defendant’s reasons and justifications for the termination, including whether it was done to avoid fulfilling Defendants’ contractual obligations.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 34 is GRANTED.  

 

4.     RFP Nos. 38-39

 

RFP No. 38 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and Defendant Sketchbox, LLC’s accountant.

 

RFP No. 39 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and Mark Dionne that REFER or RELATE to PLAINTIFF.

 

The Court finds Request No. 38 is overbroad as it pertains to “all” communications between Defendants and its accountant. Plaintiff should limit the scope of the request as to subject matter or time.

 

The Court finds Request No. 39 is sufficiently limited as to subject matter as it only requests communications relating to Plaintiff. The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 38 is DENIED. Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 39 is GRANTED.  

5.     RFP Nos. 43-46

 

RFP Nos. 43-46 seek communications between Defendant and Christopher Burke, an attorney that worked for Sketchbox, relating to Plaintiff, Plaintiffs’ profit share interest, Plaintiffs’ salary, and Plaintiffs’ employment.

 

The Court finds this information may lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The requests seek communications or other documents that are likely to provide evidence as to what Defendants knew Plaintiff was entitled to, whether and to what extent Defendants acknowledged that they had an obligation to make profit share distributions to Plaintiffs, what monies were owed to Plaintiffs, and what was actually paid to Plaintiffs. To the extent Defendants asserts a privacy privilege, they must submit a privilege log for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Defendants have not submitted a privilege log to assist the Court in evaluating these claims.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 43 to 46 is GRANTED.  

 

6.     RFP Nos. 50-51

 

RFP No. 50 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain profit share distributions made to Defendant JON GOUGEON.

 

RFP No. 51 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER to, RELATE to, or pertain salary payments made to Defendant JON GOUGEON.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. Plaintiffs allege that payments were made to Plaintiffs constituting partial payment of the profit share to which Plaintiffs were entitled, but that Defendants did not make the whole payments as required. The requested information is relevant to show the extent to which Defendants obtained a profit.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 50 and 51 is GRANTED.  

 

7.     RFP Nos. 54, 57

 

RFP No. 54 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and MIKE BENDER that REFER or RELATE to PLAINTIFF.

 

RFP No. 57 seeks: Any and all DOCUMENTS, COMMUNICATIONS, and ELECTRONIC DATA that REFER, RELATE, or pertain to all communications between YOU and MIKE BENDER that REFER or RELATE to PLAINTIFF’s employment.

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. Mark Bender is an employee that worked for Defendants. These requests seek communications that are likely to provide evidence as to what Defendants knew Plaintiff was entitled to, whether and to what extent Defendants acknowledged that they had an obligation to make profit share distributions to Plaintiff, what monies were owed to Plaintiff, what work was performed by Plaintiff, and what was actually paid to Plaintiff. Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 54 and 57 is GRANTED.  

 

                                                    i.     Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Sets One and Two, Propounded on Defendant Sketchbox

 

Plaintiffs move to compel Defendant Sketchbox to provide further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 127-130.

 

Rog No. 127 requests: “IDENTIFY each date on which SKETCHBOX, LLC profit share distributions were made to Defendant JON GOUGEON.”

 

Rog No. 128 requests: “For every date on which SKETCHBOX, LLC profit share distributions were made to Defendant JON GOUGEON, IDENTIFY the amount of each profit share distribution received by Defendant JON GOUGEON.”

 

Rog No. 129 requests: “IDENTIFY each date on which Defendant JON GOUGEON received a salary from SKETCHBOX, LLC.”

 

Rog No. 130 requests: “For every date on which Defendant JON GOUGEON received a salary from SKETCHBOX, LLC., IDENTIFY the amount of each salary payment received by Defendant JON GOUGEON.”

 

The Court finds there is good cause to compel discovery. The Court does not find Defendants’ overbroad, irrelevant, or vague objections persuasive. The requested information is relevant to show the extent to which Defendants obtained a profit. The requested information could also be relevant in calculating damages owed to Plaintiff, to evaluate evidence as to Defendants’ claimed profits or to potentially impeach testimony that minimize or deny the company’s profits. The Court also does not find Defendants’ privacy objection persuasive and Defendants fail to seek a protective order.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 127-130 is GRANTED.  

 

D.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Defendants request that the Court award monetary sanctions against Defendants and its attorney of record in the total sum of $11,040. In opposition, Defendants requests sanctions of $5,000 on grounds that Defendants brought this motion without substantial justification.¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiffs’ motion is granted and denied in part. The Court does not grant either parties’ request to impose sanctions.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents nos. 14, 15, 21, 34, 36, 37, 39, 43, 44, 45, 46, 50, 51, 54, and 57 propounded on both Defendants Jon Gougeon and Sketchbox LLC is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to request for production of documents no. 38 propounded on both Defendants Jon Gougeon and Sketchbox LLC is DENIED.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories Nos. 127-130 propounded on Defendant Sketchbox LLC is GRANTED.  

 

Defendants shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

The Court DENIES the parties’ requests for sanctions.

DENNYS RENE RIVAS-WILLIAMS v. A & A GRANITE MARBLE, et al. (23STCV08523)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Raphael Metzger (Metzger Law Group), Alan Brayton (Brayton Purcell LLP)

 

Counsel for Defendant Caesarstone LTD/moving party: James Spratt (Lydecker)

 

DEFENDANT CAESARSTONE LTD’S MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFF’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT (filed 01/17/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Caesarstone LTD’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On April 17, 2023, Plaintiff Dennys Rene Rivas-Williams (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Caesarstone LTD (“Caesarstone”) and 80 other named defendants.

 

On December 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence, (2) Strict Liability – Warning Defect, (3) Strict Liability – Design Defect, (4) Fraudulent Concealment and (5) Breach of Implied Warranties. The FAC alleges that from April 2015 to 2022, Plaintiff worked with stone products that were manufactured, distributed, and/or supplied by all named Defendants. (FAC, ¶¶ 118-120.) As a result of working with the stone products, Plaintiff alleges he developed silicosis and other related injuries. (FAC, ¶¶ 120-122.)

 

On May 13, 2024, Defendant Caesarstone LTD filed a motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s FAC.

·       Caesarstone argues that punitive damages should be stricken because there are no specific allegations regarding Caesarstone. Thus, any allegations of punitive damages lack particularity.

·       Caesarstone also asserts that allegations relating to other silica-containing stone products to be determined during discovery should be stricken. It argues that under the Bockrath standard for toxic tort cases, a plaintiff must specifically identify each product to which he was exposed for each defendant.

 

In opposition to the motion to strike, Plaintiff argues:

·       As to punitive damages, the allegations of fraud support the punitive damages allegations and Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient under California appellate toxic injury cases involving dangerous products.

 

 

In reply, Caesarstone maintains that as to punitive damages, Plaintiff has failed to allege facts sufficient to support punitive damages. As to the other chemical products language, Plaintiff does not provide any argument in opposition.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard for Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., (CCP) § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id.¿§¿437.)¿

¿ 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

Prior to filing a motion to strike, the moving party is required to satisfy their meet and confer obligations and demonstrate that they so satisfied their meet and confer obligation by submitting a declaration. (CCP § 435.5.)

 

Here, counsel for Caesarstone submitted a declaration stating she sent Plaintiff’s counsel a correspondence outlining the issues raised in the motion to strike. (Brice Decl., ¶ 3.) Caesarstone’s counsel states the parties were unable to reach an informal resolution. (Ibid.) While Caesarstone does not specify whether the parties met and conferred in-person or by telephone as required by CPP § 435.5, the Court finds the moving party’s attempts have satisfied their meet and confer obligations.

 

C.     Reference to Other Silica-Containing Stone Products

 

Caesarstone request the Court to strike allegations relating to other silica-containing stone products to be determined during discovery as follows:

1.     Page 40, line 25: “And other silica-containing stone products to be identified in the course of discovery.”

 

Caesarstone argues that this allegation violates the principles set forth in Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc., where the court held that the plaintiff must allege “each product that caused the injury.” ((1999) 21 Cal. 4th 71.) The¿Bockrath¿¿court held that a plaintiff who alleged facts to support all of the following has sufficiently pled his complaint in toxic exposure matters: (1) Plaintiff was exposed to each toxic material claimed to cause a specific illness, (2)¿toxins entered Plaintiff’s body as a result of exposure to each defendants’ product, (3) Plaintiff suffers from a specific illness, and that each toxin which entered his body was a substantial factor in bringing about or aggravating the illness. (Id.¿at 79-80.) The¿Bockrath¿court further noted that the plaintiff may allege these facts in a conclusory fashion if their knowledge of the precise cause is¿limited, and¿may plead doe defendants if their knowledge of all defendants is limited. (Id.¿at 80-81.)¿ 

 

Therefore, it does not follow from¿Bockrath¿that the phrase “other silica-containing stone products to be identified in the course of discovery” must be stricken from the FAC. Instead,¿Bockrath,¿taken as a whole, appears¿to stand for the proposition that a toxic tort plaintiff must allege facts as succinctly as he knows how at case inception, and that alleging facts in a conclusory fashion by, for example, not specifying all alleged toxic products, is permissible if plaintiff does not have sufficient knowledge.

 

Here, Plaintiff pleads the specific products manufactured by Caesarstone, including Caesarston Classico, Caesarstone Concetto, Caesarstone Motivo, Caesarstone Supernatural, Caesarstone Metropolitan, exposed Plaintiff to crystalline silica, metals, and other toxins. (FAC, p. 40; ¶¶ 118-120.) Plaintiff alleges he inhaled these toxins in the course of his work as a cutter, fabricator and installer of stone countertops. (FAC, ¶¶ 120-122.) As a result, Plaintiff further alleges he developed silicosis and other conditions that have required medical treatment and hospitalizations, and for which Plaintiff will need to receive a lung transplant. (Ibid.) Therefore, Plaintiff has complied with the pleading standards set forth in Bockrath.

 

Accordingly, Caesarstone’s motion to strike the allegation relating to other silica-containing stone products to be determined during discovery is DENIED.

 

D.     Punitive Damages

 

Caesarstone requests the Court to strike all allegations of punitive damages as follows:

1.     FAC ¿¿ 858, 874 and 989

2.     FAC Prayer for Relief, ¿ 4 : “For punitive damages and according to proof.”

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

Caesarstone argues that Plaintiff has failed to plead punitive damages with the requisite specificity as to Caesarstone. Caesarstone asserts that Plaintiff’s FAC makes conclusory and overbroad allegations against all defendants, for example Plaintiff alleges that defendants “consciously decided to market their stone products with knowledge of their harmful effects and without remedying the toxic effects of their stone products, … and misrepresented the nature of their stone products, by withholding information from Plaintiff regarding toxic and fibrogenic chemicals released from their products.” (FAC, ¶ 857.) Caesarstone argues that Plaintiff does not allege any specific facts as to how defendants allegedly concealed the toxic hazards of its product or how they misrepresented the nature of their products. Caesarstone cites to G. D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22 in support, in which the court found Plaintiff’s allegations too vague to support a claim for punitive damages. For example in G.D. Searle, the complaint alleged that : “defendants…. knew and implicitly represented by the defective products’ presence on the market that they could safely do the jobs for which they were developed; and that defendants . . . wrongfully, knowingly, and willfully acted in complete disregard of plaintiff’s implicit reliance upon defendants’ implicit representations of safety and for which plaintiff seeks punitive damages in the amount of $2,000,000.00.' (Id. at 27.)

 

However, the Court finds that here, Plaintiff has pled sufficient allegations to support punitive damages. While Caesarstone argues that Plaintiff makes conclusory allegations as to all defendants, the FAC contains allegations specific to Caesarstone. (FAC, ¶¶ 256-367.) The FAC alleges that Caesarstone knew its product was causing workers to get sick because in 2023 Caesarstone submitted a formal “Opening Statement” to Australian regulators in response to questions that they posed to Caesarstone in determining whether importation of Caesarstone and other artificial stone products into Australia should be banned due to the dangers of the products. (FAC, ¶¶ 261-271.) Among the questions that Australian regulators posed to Caesarstone in 2023 was the following question: “When did Caesarstone first learn that people working with the product were getting sick as a result?” In its “Opening Statement,” Caesarstone answered the question: “2010.” (FAC, ¶¶ 261-271.) The FAC also alleges on February 16, 2012, Caesarstone filed its Form F-1 Registration Statement under the Securities and Exchange Commission and in the document, Caesarstone acknowledged that “Silicosis and related claims could have a material adverse effect on our business, operating results and financial condition” and Caesarstone went on to describe the hazards of tis product and the risks of silicosis from exposure to its product. (FAC, ¶¶ 280-281.) The FAC further alleges “Caesarstone also concealed the true nature and severity of the hazards of its Caesarstone product by stating in its March 26, 2012 Safety Data Sheet that “Quartz surfaces products are not hazardous as shipped,” although Caesarstone® is not a finished consumer product, but is rather than industrial product that must be sawed, ground, routed, drilled, sanded, and polished in fabricating and installing the product, thereby generating respirable crystalline silica dust that causes silicosis.” (FAC, ¶ 296.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has pled facts as to what Caesarstone knew about the alleged acts that harmed Plaintiff, how Caesarstone knew and how Caesarstone disregarded the Plaintiff’s rights or safety despite this knowledge. The FAC also lists the specific products manufactured by Caesarstone, a description of how Plaintiff’s work as a cutter, fabricator and installer of stone countertops brought him into contact with the product, the health hazards of the product, allegations providing a detailed historical timeline of the Caesarstone’s knowledge of the hazards of the product, and allegations describing Plaintiff’s resulting silicosis and its alleged causal connection to Caesarstone’s products. Thus, Plaintiff’s allegations are specific as to Caesarstone and are not as vague as the allegations in G. D. Searle.

 

Caesarstone further argues should the Plaintiff prevail on a claim for fraud against a corporation, Plaintiff must establish (1) the identity of the individuals who made misrepresentations to plaintiffs, (2) demonstrate that such individuals had authority to speak for the corporation, (3) identify what such individuals said or wrote and when, and (4) specially plead that their damages were proximately caused by the defendants' fraudulent conduct. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) However, as Plaintiff’s case is premised not on an affirmative statement, but on omissions, it is hard to imagine how an individual can be identified who made the omissions. (See Jones v. ConocoPhillips (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187 [requiring a relaxed pleading standard for fraudulent concealment causes of action].)  Thus, Caesarstone’s argument on this basis fails.

 

Therefore, the Court finds Plaintiff has set forth allegations against Caesarstone that would warrant punitive damages. Accordingly, Caesarstone’s motion to strike is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant Caesarstone LTD’s motion to strike is DENIED.

BANK OF THE WEST v. MOSES GALIMBA (BC684287)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Shawn Olson (Olson Law Firm)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Matthew Loker (Loker Law, APC)

 

DEFENDANT’S motion to VACATE AND SET ASIDE DEFAULT AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 05/08/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s motion to set aside default and default judgment is DENIED. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action was filed on November 20, 2017. This is an action to enforce a retail installment sale contract by Defendant Moses Galimba.  Plaintiff Bank of the West is the owner and holder of the contract by Defendant, assigned to Bank of the West by the seller of the 2017 Ford F-350 vehicle.  Plaintiff filed this lawsuit after Defendant failed to make monthly payments when due. Plaintiff’s attempts to obtain possession of its collateral have been ignored.  Per Commercial Code §§ 9601 and 9609, Plaintiff seeks cumulative remedies for the possession of its property as prayed for in its complaint, as well as the damages for the unpaid balance due. 

 

On June 21, 2018, the court entered default judgment against Defendant in the total amount of $96,705.77 plus possession of the “2017, Ford F-350, vehicle identification number” ending in 8009 (the “Subject Vehicle”). 

 

On May 8, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Set Aside and Vacate Dismissal, arguing:

·       Defendant moves to set aside the entry of default and default judgment pursuant to CCP §§ 473.5 and 473(d). Defendant argues that the motion should be granted on statutory grounds, the motion should be granted on equitable grounds, Plaintiff will not suffer prejudice if default is set aside, public policy favors setting aside default, and the interests of justice warrant setting aside default. Defendant asserts that he was the victim of identity theft and his son, Bryan Galimba, fraudulently purchased the Vehicle at issue herein and then intercepted all communications regarding this matter to hide his nefarious conduct. Defendant also argues he was not served properly and this court never acquired personal jurisdiction over Defendant, so a Motion to Quash Service of Summons is proper. Defendant contends that he was not served with the summons and Complaint, but his son Bryan was served, and Defendant did not have actual notice of this action. Defendant attaches a proposed Answer to the instant motion.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends:

·       Defendant’s argument of lack of service is without merit and Plaintiff complied with statutory requirements of service.

o   Defendant was properly served with the Summons and Complaint as required by Code of Civil Procedure § 415.20(b). His Address has remained the same throughout the entire judicial process. The Summons and Complaint were served via substitute service at Defendant’s primary address.

·       Plaintiff asserts that Defendant is not entitled to relief, has not shown excusable neglect, has failed to state the extrinsic mistake justifying relief, and has failed to demonstrate any meritorious defense.

·       Plaintiff contends that public policy favors the finality of judgments.

 

Defendant filed a reply, arguing that the motion should be granted to allow resolution on the merits.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

“The court may set aside a void default judgment at any time under section 473, subdivision (d).” (Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 749.) “In addition, the court has inherent equitable power to set aside a default judgment at any time for extrinsic fraud or mistake.” (Ibid.) “The court may grant relief under its inherent equity power if, because of the fraud of his opponent, the aggrieved party was prevented from presenting his claim or defense to the court.” (Id. at p. 750, citations omitted.) “Fraud is intrinsic and not a valid ground for setting aside a judgment when the party has been given notice of the action and has had an opportunity to present his case and to protect himself from any mistake or fraud of his adversary, but has unreasonably neglected to do so.” (Ibid.) A party seeking equitable relief on the grounds of extrinsic fraud or mistake must show the following three elements: (1) a meritorious defense; (2) a satisfactory excuse for not presenting the defense in the first place; and (3) diligence in seeking to set aside the default judgment once discovered. (Ibid.) Extrinsic mistake “is not so much the fraud or other misconduct of one of the parties as it is the excusable neglect of the defaulting party to appear and present his claim or defense. If that neglect results in an unjust judgment, without a fair adversary hearing, the basis for equitable relief on the ground of extrinsic mistake is present.” (Mechling v. Asbestos Defendants (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1241, 1246.) “When a default judgment has been obtained, equitable relief may be given only in exceptional circumstances.” (Ibid., emphasis in original.)  

“When evaluating a motion to set aside a default judgment on equitable grounds, the court must weigh the reasonableness of the conduct of the moving party in light of the extent of the prejudice to the responding party.” (Lasalle v. Vogel (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 127, 139, internal quotations omitted.) Where there is lack of prejudice to the opposing party, such factor weighs in favor of setting aside default and default judgment. (Gray v. Lawlor (1907) 151 Cal. 352, 357.)  

“When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).) “The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).) “A defendant seeking to set aside a default or default judgment under section 473.5 must show, by affidavit, that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect.” (Shapell Socal Rental Properties, LLC v. Chico’s FAS, Inc. (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 198, 212.)  

 “[T]he filing of a proof of service creates a rebuttable presumption that . . . service was proper.” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441.) “[A] registered process server’s declaration establishes a presumption affecting the burden of producing evidence of the facts stated in the declaration.” (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390.) “[A] registered process server’s declaration of service establishes a presumption that the facts stated in the declaration are true.” (Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.)  

“It is well settled that appellate courts have always been and are favorably disposed toward such action upon the part of the trial courts as will permit, rather than prevent, the adjudication of legal controversies upon their merits.” (Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 525.) “[T]he law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, [and] any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.” (Shapell Socal Rental Properties, LLC v. Chico’s FAS, Inc., supra, 85 Cal.App.5th 198, 212.) “An order denying relief is therefore scrutinized more carefully than an order granting relief and permitting trial on the merits.” (Id. at p. 213.) “[T]he provisions of section 473 . . . are to be liberally construed and sound policy favors the determination of actions on their merits.” (Id. at p. 212, citation and internal quotations omitted.)  

B.     Appropriateness of Setting Aside the Default and Default Judgment

 

Defendant moves to set aside default and default judgment on the grounds that he received no notice of this action and that service was defective. 

 

Relief under Code Civ. Proc., section 473.5 is not available here because default was entered against Defendant more than two years ago. However, equitable relief is still available. Defendant contends the default may be set aside on the grounds of extrinsic fraud or mistake.

 

Defendant argues that his son Bryan Galimba (“Bryan”) had fraudulently purchased the Subject Vehicle in Defendant’s name without Defendant’s knowledge. (Moses Galimba Decl., ¶¶ 2-10.) Defendant further asserts that he did not have actual notice of this lawsuit until late 2023 because Bryan had intercepted documents regarding this matter, including the summons and Complaint. (Moses Galimba Decl., ¶¶ 11-24.)

 

Plaintiff’s proof of service filed shows that on December 1, 2017, the summons and Complaint were served via substituted service at Defendant’s address on a male, approximately 30 years old, around 5’11” and 155 pounds with black hair and brown eyes. (Moses Galimba Decl., ¶¶ 16-17.) Defendant states in his declaration that the description matches Bryan, as Defendant is a senior citizen. (Ibid.) Defendant states in his declaration that he did not receive the summons and Complaint from Bryan, thus, Defendant was not aware of this action. (Moses Galimba Decl., ¶¶ 18-25.) Defendant also provides as evidence a Fraud Affidavit he filed with the Federal Trade Commission asserting that he was the victim of identity theft by Bryan. (Moses Galimba Suppl. Decl., Ex. 2.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant had actual notice of the lawsuit because Defendant’s wife was served with the Application to Sell Dwelling on June 25, 2020; Defendant was personally served the Turnover Order in August 18, 2020; and Defendant was personally served on September 30, 2020 with the Order for Sale of Dwelling. (Oppos., Ex. 1.) Plaintiff argues Defendant cannot now claim that he had no idea that he was a judgment debtor. In response, Defendant argues that he assumed documents were sent to him as a mistake and that he did not believe he was the subject of the lawsuit. (Moses Galimba Decl., ¶¶ 11-12.)

 

Here, the Court finds Defendant has not presented a satisfactory excuse for not presenting his defense in the first place. Defendant only provides his own self-serving declaration in support that he did not receive the summons and Complaint and was thus not aware of this lawsuit. However, Defendant does not dispute that he was served at his primary address or that Bryan did not have authority to accept service. Further, Defendant does not provide a satisfactory explanation as to why he was not aware of this lawsuit after being personally served with the Turnover Order on August 18, 2020 and the Order for Sale of Dwelling on September 30, 2020. Additionally, Defendant does not provide how he discovered the default judgment, but only states he discovered the default judgment sometime in late 2023. Defendant did not file the instant motion until May 8, 2024. Defendant does not explain why he was not diligent in seeking default sooner. In sum, the Court does not find Defendant qualifies for equitable relief from default.

 

Defendant’s motion to set aside default and default judgment is denied. 

 

C.     Appropriateness of Quashing Service of the Summons and Complaint

 

The Court also notes that Defendant’s request to quash service of the summons and complaint is procedurally improper. Defendant filed a motion to set aside default and default judgment, but in the body of the motion, he also included a request for an order quashing service of the summons on the defendant.

 

However, because default and default judgment were entered against Defendant, any request to quash service of the summons and complaint is procedurally improper. (Bailey v. City Bank, N.A. (2021) 66 Cal.App.5th 335, 347.) “The entry of a defendant’s default cuts off the defendant’s right to file pleadings and motions (other than a motion to set aside default under § 473), and it also cuts off the defendant’s right to notices and the service of pleadings or papers.” (Ibid.)

 

Thus, Defendant was only entitled to file a motion to set aside default and did not have standing to quash service of summons and complaint pursuant to Bailey, supra, 66 Cal.App.5th 335, 347. 

 

Further, by filing a motion to set aside default, Defendant has made a general appearance. (Kallman v. Henderson (1965) 234 Cal.App.2d 91, 96.) However, a motion to quash must be made through a special appearance. (Stancil v. Superior Court (2021) 11 Cal.5th 381, 393.) Therefore, by generally appearing, Defendant is improperly requesting to quash service of the summons and complaint.

 

As such, the Court denies any request to quash service of the summons and complaint made in Defendant’s motion.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s motion to set aside default and default judgment is DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
