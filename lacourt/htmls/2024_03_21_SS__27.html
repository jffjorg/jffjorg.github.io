RULING:

DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR UNDERTAKING FOR COSTS IS DENIED

 

Background

On May 19, 2021, Plaintiff filed a medical malpractice case against Defendant. Through discovery, Defendant discovered that Plaintiff currently resides out of state and now moves for an order requiring the out-of-state Plaintiff to file a $100,000 undertaking. Plaintiff argues that Defendant has no basis to assert that there is a reasonable possibility of obtaining judgment in the action or seeking costs in the amount of $100,000.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 1030 provides the process and grounds for when a defendant may move for an order requiring an out-of-state plaintiff to file an undertaking. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (a)). The policy behind the statute is to prevent out-of-state residents from filing frivolous lawsuits against California residents. (Yao v. Superior Court (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 327, 331.) This policy is achieved by allowing a Californian defendant to obtain security for potential fees and costs from the out-of-state plaintiff, which counteracts “the difficulty of enforcing a judgment for costs against a person who is not within the court's jurisdiction.” (Yao, supra, 104 Cal.App.4th at 331, quoting Shannon v. Sims Service Center, Inc. (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 907, 913).

 

For the court to grant a Motion for Undertaking, the following requirements must be met:

(1) The plaintiff must reside out of state or must be a foreign corporation;

(2) There is a reasonable possibility that the moving defendant will obtain judgment in the action; and

(3) The motion must contain an affidavit in support of the grounds for the motion and a memorandum of points and authorities. The affidavit must set “forth the nature and amount of the costs and attorney’s fees the defendant has incurred and expects to incur by the conclusion of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (b)).

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff does not dispute that he resides out of state.[1] The parties disagree as to whether Movants have established a “reasonable possibility that the moving defendant will obtain judgment in the action” and whether Movants’ affidavit “set forth the nature and amount of the costs and attorney's fees the defendant has incurred and expects to incur by the conclusion of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (b)).

 

1.   Reasonable Possibility of Obtaining a Favorable Verdict.

To obtain an order for undertaking, a movant must first establish he has a reasonable possibility of obtaining a verdict in his favor. Actionable negligence involves a legal duty to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury. (United States Liab. Ins. Co. v. Haidinger-Hayes, Inc. (1970) 1 Cal.3d 586, 594.) In a medical negligence action, the medical professional’s duty is to possess and exercise, in both diagnosis and treatment, the reasonable degree of knowledge, skill, and care which is ordinarily possessed and exercised by other members of his profession in similar circumstances.” (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 408; Mann v. Cracchiolo (1985) 38 Cal.3d 18, 36). Thus, one way for a medical professional to obtain a favorable resolution in a medical malpractice action against him is for the medical professional to show he did not breach his duty to possess and exercise the reasonable degree of knowledge, skill, and care which is ordinarily possessed and exercised by other members of his profession in similar circumstances.

 

In Baltayan v. Estate of Getemyan, (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1427, 1432, the Court of Appeal held the parties moving for undertaking had proved they had a reasonable possibility of obtaining a verdict such that a motion for undertaking should have been granted. In reaching its holding, the Court of Appeal considered the following evidence: the movants’ favorable arbitration award (an award which the plaintiff-appellant appealed to the trial court), a police report indicating the litigated injuries were the plaintiff’s own fault, the responding police officer’s deposition testimony about his post-accident interview of the plaintiff, and expert witness testimonies (Ibid.)

 

Defendant has not shown that he has a reasonable possibility of obtaining a favorable verdict.

Defendant's evidence includes a document titled “Designation of Expert Witnesses”, showing that Defendant has retained several experts, and another document titled "Expert Witness Declaration." However, the expert witness declaration is provided by Michael Trotter, Defendant’s attorney, rather than one of Defendant's retained experts. In the declaration, Mr. Trotter lists the names, qualifications, and scope of testimonies but does not include any expert witness testimony regarding Defendant's duty or liability or explanations on how a favorable verdict will be achieved.

Unlike Baltayan v. Estate of Getemyan, supra., a case cited by Defendant, the movant presented the court with various pieces of evidence to assess the merits of the case: Movant’s favorable arbitration award, arbitrator’s letter, a police report indicating the injuries were Plaintiff's own fault, statement from the investigating police officer that plaintiff caused the accident, and Movant’s expert witness testimonies, Defendant here has failed to produce any substantive evidence relating to the merits of the case demonstrating a reasonable possibility that he will prevail.

This case was filed in 2021 and discovery has been conducted. Defendant should have access to medical documents for his expert witnesses to opine on whether Defendant breached his duty to possess and exercise the reasonable degree of knowledge, skill, and care which is ordinarily possessed and exercised by other members of his profession in similar. Yet, no expert witness report has been produced in support of the motion.

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion is DENIED.

Background 

 

On August 17, 2021, Plaintiff filed an auto accident case alleging various injuries. On July 12, 2023, Plaintiff's discovery responses identified RADSTAR STAND-UP MRI (“Radstar”) as a provider for her pre-accident injuries. On August 8, 2023, Defendant subpoenaed Radstar but was notified that Radstar had changed its name to Empire Radiology, LLC (“Empire”). Consequently, on September 5, 2023, Defendant reissued his subpoena to Empire, with a compliance date set for October 15, 2023. However, on September 27, 2023, Plaintiff's counsel objected to Defendant's subpoena for business records from Empire, noting that the request was overbroad and the requested information would disclose Plaintiff's next of kin and their personal details. Plaintiff's counsel consented to comply only if Defendant's counsel narrowed the subpoena scope solely to the films and MRI records. On October 13, 2023, Defendant sent a meet and confer letter, leading to an email exchange between the Parties. From October 16 to 17, the Parties exchanged another pair of emails discussing the scope of the subpoena. Defendant now moves the court to compel Empire to comply with Defendant's subpoena. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

Legal Standard¿and Analysis 

¿  

CCP § 2025.480(a) provides that “[i]f a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information,¿or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” CCP § 2025.480 includes a time limit. If a nonparty disobeys a deposition subpoena, the subpoenaing party may seek a court order compelling the nonparty to comply with the subpoena within¿60 days¿after completion of the¿deposition record. (CCP § 2025.480(b); Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 CA4th 123, 127.) The deposition record is “complete” either on the date documents are specified for production or on the date objections are served. (Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal. App. 5th 1011, 1032-1033.)¿  

 

 “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).)  

 

Meet and Confer

 

“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)    

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.”  (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) This rule is designed “to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order....”  (McElhaney v. Cessna Aircraft Co. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 285, 289.) “This, in turn, will lessen the burden on the court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by litigants through promotion of informal, extrajudicial resolution of discovery disputes.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.)  “A determination of whether an attempt at informal resolution is adequate . . . involves the exercise of discretion.” (Stewart (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th at 1016.)  “The history of the litigation, the nature of the interaction between counsel, the nature of the issues, the type and scope of discovery requested, the prospects for success and other similar factors can be relevant.” (Id.)  

"Meet and confer" or "confer" means to communicate directly and discuss in good faith the issue(s) required under the particular Rule or order… such communication may take place by telephone. The mere sending of a written, electronic, or voice-mail communication, however, does not satisfy a requirement to "meet and confer" or to "confer." Rather, this requirement can be satisfied only through direct dialogue and discussion — either in a face-to-face meeting or in a telephone conversation. Phleger v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc., No. C 07-1686 SBA, 5 (N.D. Cal. Dec. 24, 2007)

 

The Court finds that Defendant’s efforts to meet and confer were insufficient. On September 27, 2023, Plaintiff sent an objection to Defendant’s subpoena. On October 13, 2023, Defendant sent a meet and confer letter, and the Parties had one e-mail exchange regarding the letter and the subpoena. Another e-mail exchange occurred from October 16 to 17, with the last e-mail from Plaintiff asking Defendant for the legal basis for the scope of the subpoena. Defendant did not respond to that e-mail or further engage in meet and confer efforts. The meet and confer process appears incomplete, based on the evidence presented by Defendant, especially the last email exchange between the parties.

A moving party must demonstrate a serious attempt to meet and confer before filing a motion to compel. E-mail communication, while convenient, can sometimes be unreliable – in this day and age of receiving a barrage of e-mails on a daily basis, an email could be overlooked, or, even if seen and opened, could be forgotten when sidelined by a more pressing matter. Sufficient meet and confer requires either a phone call or a face-to-face meeting.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to compel is DENIED without prejudice.

RULING:

 

Summary adjudication as to Affirmative Defenses Nos. 2, 4-10, 12-15 is GRANTED.

 

Summary adjudication as to Affirmative Defenses Nos. 3 and 11 is DENIED.

 

 

Background: 

 

On October 13, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present lawsuit, alleging Defendant reversed her car, hitting Plaintiff when he was walking behind the car. On December 7, 2021, Defendant filed an answer, raising 15 affirmative defenses. After discovery had been conducted, Plaintiff moved the Court for summary adjudication on Affirmative Defenses numbers 2 to 15 because Defendant has no evidence to support these alleged defenses. In Defendant’s opposition, she forfeits all of the affirmative defenses at issue, except for numbers 3, 4, 11, and 13. For those remaining Affirmative Defenses, Defendant contends that there are triable issues of fact. 

 

Affirmative Defenses At Issue 

 

THIRD AFFIRMATIVE DEFENSE: Defendant is informed and believes and upon such information and belief alleges that at all times mentioned herein, Plaintiff was negligent, careless and reckless and unlawfully conducted himself so as to substantially contribute to the alleged injuries and damages of himself, compared to the conduct of all other parties, all of which said negligence either bars in whole or in part the damages sought herein. 

 

FOURTH AFFIRMATIVE DEFENSE: Defendant is informed and believes and upon such information and belief alleges that at all times mentioned herein, if Defendant was negligent, co-defendants, both known and unknown, were negligent, careless, and reckless, and unlawfully conducted themselves so as to substantially contribute to Plaintiff’s claimed injuries and damages, and said negligence, if any, either bars in whole or in part the damages sought herein against Defendant. 

 

ELEVENTH AFFIRMATIVE DEFENSE: Defendant is informed and believes and upon such information and belief alleges that at all times mentioned herein, Plaintiff was negligent, careless, and reckless, and unlawfully conducted himself by: (a) using a public street or highway on foot and failing to yield the right of-way to all vehicles upon the roadway so near as to constitute an immediate hazard, in violation of California Vehicle Code section 21954; (b) walking on a roadway in violation of California Vehicle Code section 21956; (c) suddenly leaving the curb or other place of safety and walk or run into the path of an approaching vehicle; and (d) providing valet parking services on a public right-of-way without a valid Valet Parking Attendant permit, in violation of Los Angeles Municipal Code section 103.203. Plaintiff’s conduct described in this paragraph substantially contributed to his alleged injuries and damages compared to the conduct of all other parties, all of which said negligence either bars in whole or in part the damages sought herein. 

 

THIRTEENTH AFFIRMATIVE DEFENSE: Defendant is informed and believes and upon such information and belief alleges that at all times mentioned herein, Plaintiff has failed to mitigate his damages so as to bar, in whole or in part, the damages sought herein. 

 

Legal Standard¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite its allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve its dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843). “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119).¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519). A defendant/plaintiff moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action [or affirmative defense] has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements... cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).  

¿¿ 

Movant has two methods to shift the burden of proof. In addition to the standard method of presenting evidence that negates an essential element, she may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses to show that the responding party cannot establish an essential element. (Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 590). Once the movant. . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the responding party . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto. CCP § 437c(p)(2).    

 

Affirmative Defenses Nos. 3, 4, 11 

 

1.  Plaintiff's Initial Burden 

 

Plaintiff grouped Affirmative Defenses 3, 4, and 11 together, citing the same Undisputed Material Facts (UMFs) 3, 24, and 25 and referencing the same pieces of evidence: excerpts from Marvin Augustin Cardona's deposition (Ex. B) and Defendant's responses to form interrogatories, Set One (Ex. F) to support his motion for summary adjudication of these three affirmative defenses.  

 

A.  Factually devoid discovery responses 

 

The Court examined Defendant's responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1, which requested Defendant to state all facts, all evidence, and identify all documents supporting each of her affirmative defenses in her answer. Defendant responded that “despite a diligent and good faith attempt to obtain the information sought, at this time, given the present status of litigation, Responding Party does not have sufficient information to fully respond to this interrogatory. Discovery and investigation are ongoing.” (Ex. F.) 

However, moving Party’s initial burden will shift based on factually devoid discovery responses only if: (1) discovery responses fail to set forth facts and identify evidence supporting responding party’s allegations; and (2) the court can reasonably infer from the proffered discovery and responses that the responding party is unable to produce any additional evidence. (Andrews v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 96, 102-103.)  The responding party must have been afforded adequate opportunity for discovery (Union Bank v. Sup.Ct. (Demetry) (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590) or have declared that they have no further information following extensive discovery. (Weber v. John Crane, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1433, 1441-1442.) Moving party does not satisfy its burden of proof by producing discovery responses that do not exclude the possibility that responding party may possess or may reasonably obtain evidence sufficient to establish its claim/defense. (Id. at 1441-1442.)  

 

The evidence Plaintiff relies on to meet his initial burden shows that Defendant's responses were served on 1/31/22, less than two months after her answer was served on 12/7/21. (Ex. F). Given that discovery had only been ongoing for less than 2 months, it is understandable that "Defendant does not have sufficient information to fully respond to the interrogatory.” (Id.) Based on this evidence, the Court cannot reasonably infer that Defendant is incapable of producing any additional evidence. 

 

B.  Affirmative Evidence 

 

Plaintiff introduces excerpts from Cardona's deposition to meet his initial burden against Affirmative Defenses Nos. 3, 4 and 11. The deposition reveals that the accident occurred at night (Cardona Depo. 21:12), with Defendant's car parked on the street. The valet, including Plaintiff and Cardona, parked a car in front of it and beside it. (Cardona Depo. 18:25-19:19). Plaintiff and Cardona observed that the car in front of Defendant's was in the way of her getting out. Cardona asked Plaintiff to move the car that was in front of Defendant. Plaintiff moved around Defendant's car to reach the car in front. As Plaintiff was walking behind Defendant’s car, she began to move and backed into Plaintiff (Cardona Depo. 21:22-22:4, 23:1-10.) 

 

For Affirmative Defense No. 3, concerning Plaintiff's alleged negligence, a reasonable mind could indeed question whether Plaintiff acted negligently based on the evidence provided by Plaintiff himself. Plaintiff's choice to navigate around Defendant's car, particularly in close proximity, especially knowing that Defendant was in the process of maneuvering her vehicle out of the parking spot, raises a triable issue of fact as to Plaintiff’s own negligence. Additionally, the evidence does not demonstrate that Plaintiff communicated with Defendant as he was walking behind her car. These are all triable issues of fact and therefore Plaintiff has not met his initial burden. 

 

For Affirmative Defense No. 11, it is reasonable to infer that the incident occurred on a public street, as the car was parked on a street next to a meter, and Defendant was reversing on a street. Consequently, Plaintiff failed to meet his initial burden concerning Affirmative Defense No. 11, which pertains to failing to yield to a car on a public street and walking on a public street way. 

 

For Affirmative Defense No. 4, the deposition detailing how the incident occurred does not indicate negligence by anyone other than Defendant and Plaintiff. Although Cardona asked Plaintiff to move the car in front of Defendant's car, he did not direct Plaintiff to go behind Defendant's car. Thus, Plaintiff has met his initial burden. 

 

2.  Defendant’s Burden 

 

In meeting her burden, Defendant introduces excerpts of her own deposition where she states that she did not see Plaintiff at any time before the collision. She checked her mirrors and ensured no one was in the vicinity but did not see Plaintiff. She also did not see Plaintiff in her backup camera. Defendant speculated that Plaintiff might have purposefully stepped behind her car, allowing her to hit him (Finkelstein Depo, Exhibit A to Defendant’s Opposition). This introduces a triable issue of fact regarding Plaintiff's own negligence. Was Plaintiff walking so fast or so close to Defendant’s car that she could not see him in the mirrors and camera? This situation presents a triable issue of fact relating to Plaintiff's own negligence.  

 

3.  Conclusion

 

Accordingly, Summary Adjudication as to Affirmative Defense Nos. 3 and 11 is DENIED, and Summary Adjudication as to Affirmative Defense No. 4 is GRANTED. 

 

Affirmative Defense No. 13

 

In meeting his initial burden, Plaintiff references UMFs 33, 34, 35. The supporting evidence are Defendant's responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One (Ex. F), and Defendant’s Responses to Requests for Admission (RFAs), Set Two. (Ex. K.) As previously discussed, Defendant's responses to the Form Interrogatories, Set One, were served only two months after she filed her answer, which does not indicate an inability to acquire additional evidence. Upon reviewing Defendant's responses to the RFAs, the Court observed that RFAs 62 through 99 all relate to Plaintiff's mitigation efforts. (Ex. K.) Defendant’s responses stated that she lacked sufficient information to respond. (Ex. K.)  Defendant’s responses were served on March 7, 2023. (Ex. K.) Given that Plaintiff's requests comprehensively addressed the issue of mitigation, and the responses were provided after substantial discovery, the Court concludes that Plaintiff has fulfilled his initial burden.

 

Defendant failed to present any evidence demonstrating that Plaintiff did not mitigate his damages. In the opposition, Defendant only produced two deposition excerpts related to the incident. Defendant has not met her burden, and therefore, summary adjudication as to Affirmative Defense No. 13 is GRANTED.

 

Conclusion

 

Summary adjudication as to Affirmative Defenses Nos. 2, 4-10, 12-15 is GRANTED.

 

Summary adjudication as to Affirmative Defense Nos. 3 and 11 is DENIED.

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.)  

 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).)  

 

Analysis and Conclusion 

 

Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order) have all been filed and properly served. Counsel seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw. (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) The Court notes that the next hearing is an FSC set for 5/8/24, which should be sufficient time for Plaintiff to retain new counsel. Alternatively, if Plaintiff or new counsel needs additional time to prepare for trial, sufficient time exists for Plaintiff to take action to seek a continuance of the trial. Thus, the present motion to be relieved as counsel is GRANTED, effective as of the date of Notice of this Ruling is provided to Plaintiff.

RULING: 

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MARIA MENDOZA AND JASMIN CASTRO JUAREZ MOTION TO COMPEL AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS IS GRANTED.

 

Background

 

On January 17, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present auto accident case. On April 5, 2023, Plaintiff Jasmin Castro Juarez and Plaintiff Maria Antonia Juarez Mendoza each served Form Interrogatories (Set No. Two) on Defendant. On May 9, 2023, after not receiving a verified response to these form interrogatories, Plaintiff's counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant's counsel, requiring that a verified response to both Plaintiffs’ Form Interrogatories (Set No. Two) be received by May 15, 2023. However, no responses were received by May 15, 2023. Plaintiffs now move the court to compel the discovery responses at issue. No opposition was filed.

 

Legal Standard 

 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ ¿ 

It is undisputed that Defendant failed to provide timely responses. Defendant did not file an opposition indicating that discovery responses were served before the hearing. Thus, the present motions to compel are GRANTED.

 

Sanctions¿ ¿ 

 

Defendant's failure to respond caused Plaintiff to incur attorney's fees in bringing the present motion. Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $1,560 per motion. Taking into account the complexity (or, as the case is, non-complexity) of the issue and the absence of an opposition, the Court exercises its discretion and reduces the amount to $1,500 for both motions. Consequently, Defendant and her counsel are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $1,500 to Plaintiff within 20 days of today’s date.
