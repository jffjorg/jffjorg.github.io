DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01458    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: W

KIMBERLY DAVIS, et al. v. PAIGE HEMMIS, et al.

 

brief: re stay of proceedings, bifuraction and mediation

 

Date of Hearing:        December 4, 2024                             Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01458

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis filed a third amended complaint against Defendants Paige Hemmis, Jason Short, Ameka Benton, Scott Fields, Hallmark Cards, Inc., Crown Media Holdings, Inc., Paige Hemmis, Inc., Aria Music Group, The Oaks of Calabasas Homeowners Association, Melissa Improta, Jason Improta, and Compass California, Inc. for 1) Intentional Interference with Contract; 2) Negligence; 3) Trespass; 4) Violations of Civil Code § 1798.93; 5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 6) Professional Negligence; 7) Unfair Business Practices; 8) Fraudulent Concealment; 9) Negligent Misrepresentation; 10) Fraud; 11) Slander of Title; 12) Constructive Fraud; 13) Wrongful Eviction; 14) Quiet Title; and 15) Conversion.

 

Plaintiffs allege about September 15, 2019, when Plaintiffs were absent from the subject property, Defendants Hemmis and Short unlawfully entered upon the subject property and remained in possession of the property until early July 2022. While Defendants Hemmis and Short were in possession of the property, Plaintiffs were engaged in a dispute with their mortgage lender, US Bank, related to a settlement agreement that was entered into to address the pay off of a mortgage loan. Plaintiffs further allege at some point prior to September 12, 2019, Defendants Hemmis, Short, Improta and Compass engaged in a conspiracy to attempt to steal title to the Plaintiffs’ home. On this point, Plaintiffs claim Hemmis and Short fraudulently forged a lease that purported to have Kimberly Davis' signature on it. As a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged. Plaintiff dismissed Defendants Ameka Benton and Scott Fields.

 

On March 24, 2023, this action was related to Kimberly Davis v. Nationstar, et al. (23VECV01125). On August 18, 2023, Paige Hemmis and Jason Short filed a cross-complaint against Kimberly Ann Davis, Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta, and Jason Improta for breach of contract, money had and received, implied indemnification, express indemnification, and comparative contribution. Cross-Complainants dismissed their cause of action for express indemnification.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The request for a stay is granted, except as to the attorney fee motions, currently set for hearing in December.  The remainder of the case is stayed, pending the resolution of the current appeals, including all discovery.  

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiffs request this court take judicial notice of declarations filed by Plaintiffs in this instant action in support of their opposition to Defendant Hallmark Media United States, LLC’s Special Motion to Strike (RJN, Exhs. 1, 2.) The court GRANTS the request as to the existence of these documents.

 

DISCUSSION

 

On October 9, 2024, the Court ordered the parties to file briefs on the issue of whether the case should be stayed in whole or in part, whether the action should be bifurcated, and the parties’ willingness to engage in private mediation. Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis, Hallmark Defendants, The Oaks HOA, Defendants Paige Hemmis and Jason Short, and Defendants Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta and Jason Improta all filed briefs. Each take their own position on the issues inquired by this Court.  

 

Defendants Paige Hemmis and Jason Short

 

Defendants Paige Hemmis and Jason Short argue a stay of all trial court proceedings should be invoked, including all discovery and depositions, pending the completion of the appellate process. Defendants contend (1) the filing of the appeals by Hallmark Defendants and Plaintiffs created an automatic stay; (2) the automatic stay is to the entire action as the SLAPP motion sought to have stricken all causes of action against Hallmark Defendants and a simple reading of the TAC shows that all the allegations and claims factually overlap; and (3) the resulting burden and expense of not staying the entire action as to all Defendants will be significant.

 

As for bifurcation, Defendants oppose the idea as to require Defendants to participate in more than one jury trial will be expensive, unduly burdensome, and unfair.

 

Defendants agree to private mediation.

 

The Oaks HOA

 

The Oaks HOA argues there should be a full stay of the action as the allegations against the various defendants are intertwined to the degree that all causes of action are affected by the appeals filed by Hallmark Defendant and Plaintiffs. In addition, the interests of judicial efficiency and economy will best be served by staying the entire action. The Oaks HOA maintains there is no way to exclude any one cause of action or party from the mandatory stay without running the risk that there would be substantial interference with the appellate court’s jurisdiction. Moreover, to allow the matter to proceed in part would be at odds with the policy and purpose of the anti-SLAPP statute as the causes of action are “inextricably intertwined”. Thus, to allow the action to proceed against the non-appealing defendants and make a finding on the merits of the inter-related claims prior to disposition of the pending appeals would substantially interfere with the appellant court's ability to conduct the appeal and could result in irreconcilable outcomes or results.

 

The Oaks HOA also does not wish for the claims against it to be bifurcated at this time. The Oaks HOA note all Plaintiffs’ allegations against them are based on allegations that it conspired with the other defendants to create, approve or ratify a fraudulent lease and to bifurcate the claims would require a duplication of judicial resources. 

 

The Oaks HOA is agreeable to private mediation.

 

Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta and Jason Improta

 

Compass California, Inc. dba Compass, Melissa Improta and Jason Improta (“Real Estate Defendants”) submitted their brief in support of an order staying all trial-court proceedings as to all parties in this action or, in the alternative, an order staying all discovery as to all parties in this action until the resolution of the pending appeals. Defendants assert the automatic stay on further trial-court proceedings applies to all parties as each of Plaintiffs’ causes of action are based on the same set of operative facts alleging a supposed conspiracy between the multiple defendants and Plaintiffs’ pleading has made it so that it is virtually impossible to have proceedings on the nine causes of action without crossing into the domain of the six causes of action on appeal. Moreover, Defendants argue the parties currently do not know what the outcome of the appeals will be and it would be the most cost effective to stay all proceedings until the resolution of the appeals. Defendants contend this would save judicial time and resources as well as save the parties time and resources by preventing unnecessary costs from being incurred like costs of duplicative depositions. However, if the court finds a stay on all proceedings is not feasible, Defendants seek a stay on all discovery.

 

As for bifurcation, Defendants argue against bifurcation as it would not resolve the issues presented and would be impossible. It would ultimately result in duplicative trials, needless duplication of resources, duplicative time spent by all parties to prepare for the trials and attend the trials, duplicative costs and fees associated with the trials, and a waste of already limited judicial resources.

 

Defendants are willing to participate in mediation.  

 

Plaintiffs Kimberly Davis and Jonathan Davis

 

Plaintiffs note the action is stayed as to the Hallmark Defendants but argue the other defendants’ reasons for staying the entire action as to them are unavailing. Nonetheless, Plaintiffs would be agreeable to a stay of the instant action on the condition that all proceedings are stayed, including the Plaintiff's pending fee motion and the Defendants' fee motion. The reason for this is that the Plaintiffs would prefer to avoid further unnecessary appeals. Plaintiffs contend it is well-settled law that a court may rule on a request for attorney's fees in connection with an anti-SLAPP motion after the appeal is resolved. (Carpenter v. Jack in the Box Corp. (2007) 151 Cal. App. 4th 454.) Indeed, the Court of Appeals would either eliminate the need for this court to rule on the fee motion or provide substantial guidance on what the outcome of those motions should be. Plaintiffs briefly note that the Hallmark Fee Motion itself wouldn’t past muster but nonetheless, if the court were to grant Hallmark’s Fee Motion, Plaintiffs would engage in substantial briefing and litigation to appeal the decision or post a bond which could all be avoided upon waiting on the Court of Appeals’ decision. Accordingly, Hallmark Defendants would not suffer any prejudice by the approach suggested above.

 

Plaintiffs also briefly argue if the court is not inclined to stay the entire case – including fee motions – it should permit the Plaintiff to continue with prosecuting the case, including propounding discovery and filing a motion for summary judgment as to Paige Hemmis' cross-complaint, which would have the benefit of establishing one of the most critical issues in this litigation – that Ms. Hemmis had no right to be in the Plaintiff's home.

 

Plaintiffs go on to argue why all the other remaining defendants’ contentions that the court should stay the case against them are not well taken. As for Hemmis/Short Defendants, Plaintiffs argue a stay does not suspend the trial court proceedings with respect to claims asserted against nonappealing parties. (Hedwall v. PCMV, LLC (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 564, 580, fn. 11.) Moreover, Hemmis/Short Defendants filed a cross-complaint asserting their own claims for affirmative relief arising out of the same general set of facts. Staying the entire action, including the cross-complaint, is clearly not a matter embraced in this appeal nor would continued litigation on the cross-complaint affect Hallmark's pending appeal in any meaningful way. Moreover, Hemmis/Short Defendants’ provide no authority for their contention that the action must be stayed in its entirety since the allegations of agency and relationship necessarily ties into the Plaintiff's argument against Hemmis.

 

As for the Real Estate Defendants’ contention, Plaintiffs argue the problem with their argument is that virtually all of the cited allegations pertain to a conspiracy between Real Estate Defendants and Hemmis/Short, or with other Defendants, not including Hallmark. Accordingly, the resolution of the appeal does not impact the Real Estate Defendants’ liability. Moreover, any notice that Hallmark Defendants may depose them in the future is speculative and there is nothing wrong with wishing this case to proceed. If the court were to stay only as to discovery, Plaintiffs would potentially have to respond to a motion for summary judgment without complete discovery.

 

Turning to The Oaks HOA, Plaintiffs argue nothing the Hallmark Defendants did has any bearing on the Oaks of HOA liability. Their liability stems from improperly providing Ms. Hemmis with access to the Plaintiff's property and conspiring with the other defendants. The Oaks cannot legitimately point to anything in the Hallmark Defendants appeal that has anything to with their liability.

 

Plaintiffs aver each defendant claimed Plaintiffs would suffer no prejudice by staying the matter; however, Ms. Davis has repeatedly stated she cannot obtain a new loan so long as this case remains pending and Ms. Hemmis’ cloud on the title remains.

 

As for bifurcation, Plaintiffs oppose the notion as trying this case twice would be inordinately expensive, costly, and time-consuming. In addition, Plaintiffs would certainly have to re-call Paige Hemmis as a witness as her conduct in wrongfully occupying the home, which the Hallmark Defendants allegedly ratified, is highly relevant for punitive damages and other purposes.

 

Plaintiffs agree to mediation on the condition that all parties will attend.

 

Hallmark Defendants

 

Hallmark Defendants argue because they are the ones who appealed the SLAPP Order, the case is automatically stayed as to the them. However, the Hallmark Defendants oppose any stay as to the other Defendants or related to the Hallmark Fee Motion. Hallmark Defendants contend the issues to be tried as to the Hallmark Defendants and all of the other Defendants are unlikely to overlap. Furthermore, the claims that remain against the Hallmark Defendants are narrow. Specifically, the SLAPP Order effectively eliminated the Alleged Real Estate Fraud theory as to the Hallmark Defendants finding that there was not “sufficient evidence to find that Hallmark Media ratified the alleged forgery by Ms. Hemmis of a deed or lease or the alleged interference with contract”. (Order, p. 10.) As a result, Hallmark Defendants claim if the Court of Appeals finds in favor of Hallmark on the remaining claims and on timeliness, then the case against the Hallmark Defendants will be dismissed. If the Court of Appeal upholds the Court’s order as to the remaining causes of action against the Hallmark Defendants, and Hemmis is not found liable in the interim, a similar result will occur. And in the event that Hemmis is found liable in the interim, the only issue remaining after appeal will be whether Hemmis was acting as the Hallmark Defendants’ agent and if so, whether the Hallmark Defendants’ ratified her conduct by continuing to display the AHWOF Videos after this lawsuit commenced.

 

Hallmark Defendants further argue the HOA’s argument that a stay “would be at odds” with” the policy of the SLAPP statute” is unfounded. The Legislature enacted the SLAPP statute to promote the “fast and inexpensive unmasking and dismissal” of lawsuits, like this one, that target the exercise of free-speech rights about matters of public interest. Hallmark Defendants claim allowing the causes of action to proceed would serve that end because it would substantially narrow the issues while the appeal is pending and any duplicative discovery is unnecessary. Hallmark Defendants also argue in a footnote on p. 8 that Plaintiffs request is untimely because if they are requesting a complete stay, the request had to be filed by 11/4 (Per Court Order).

 

Hallmark Defendants also argue that the Hallmark Fee Motion should be resolved as calendared. Hallmark Defendants assert “an appeal does not stay proceedings on ancillary or collateral matters which do not affect the judgment [or order] on appeal,” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal. 4th 180, 191) and courts consistently have held that prevailing SLAPP defendants can bring fee motions under Section 425.16(c) while a plaintiff appeals the underlying ruling. (See e.g. Carpenter v. Jack in the Box (2007) 151 Cal. App. 4th 454, 461.) In this case, there is no basis to delay a hearing on the Hallmark Fee Motion because deciding the motion now while the merits of the motion already are briefed and the facts relating to the underlying merits of the SLAPP Motion are still fresh with the Court will lead to the most expedient and just outcome. Furthermore, the outcome will have no impact on the number appeals in this case or adversely impact the judicial economy of the case. Plaintiffs can either appeal after final judgment or stay enforcement by posting an appropriate bond.

 

As for bifurcation, Hallmark Defendants argue the trial should be bifurcated in the interest of judicial economy. According to Hallmark Defendants, bifurcation is appropriate because (1) if Hemmis and Short are found not liable, there can be no liability for the Hallmark Defendants, potentially avoiding the need for trial as to Hallmark altogether; (2) if Hemmis and Short are found liable on only a subset of the claims against Hallmark, the required evidence and trial would be significantly narrowed; and (3) even if liability is found as to Hemmis and Short as to all of the claims alleged against Hallmark, the issues to be tried as to the Hallmark Defendants and all of the other defendants are not significantly overlapping. Specifically, the TAC alleges no facts to show that the Hallmark Defendants were involved in any the underlying conduct or were involved in a conspiracy with the other Defendants. Notably, there are no facts pled in the TAC that the Hallmark Defendants are vicariously liable for the acts of Compass, the Improtas, or the HOA. Moreover, none of the allegations show the Hallmark Defendants were involved in the underlying conduct or conspiracy.

 

In reply, Real Estate Defendants argue because every cause of action incorporates the allegations that the defendants allegedly conspired together and moreover, that every act of Defendants Hemmis and Short was allegedly done within the scope of their agency relationship with the Hallmark Defendants, the issues in this case are all inextricably tied together necessitating a stay.

 

Plaintiffs also filed a reply as to Hallmark Defendants’ brief. Plaintiffs maintain Hallmark cannot enjoy the benefit of the stay and then attempt to participate in this case by making substantive requests (arguing the other parties must litigate against each other and the action should be bifurcated). (See CCP §916.) Because Hallmark has no standing to file a motion to bifurcate, the request should be denied. Plaintiffs go on to argue why bifurcation is not appropriate in this matter including having to call witnesses multiple times, Plaintiffs’ claim for trespass is not dependent on agency and ratification, and Hallmark Defendants have not come close to meeting its burden of establishing that Plaintiff would not be prejudiced.

 

Hallmark Defendants are open to private mediation.

 

Stay the Action

 

Based on the arguments above as well as the authority provided to this court, the court grants the request to stay the action in whole.

 

“Except as provided in Sections 917.1 to 917.9, inclusive . . . the perfecting of an appeal stays proceedings in the trial court upon the judgment or order appealed from or upon the matters embraced therein or affected thereby, including enforcement of the judgment or order, but the trial court may proceed upon any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order.” (CCP § 916(a).) 

 

The trial court is divested of subject matter jurisdiction over any matter embraced in or affected by the appeal during the¿pendency of that appeal. (See Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino¿(2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 197.) Subsequent trial court proceedings on matters embraced in or affected by the appeal are void. (Id. at 198.)  “The purpose of the automatic stay provision of section 916, subdivision (a) ‘is to protect the appellate court's jurisdiction by preserving the status quo until the appeal is decided. The [automatic stay] prevents the trial court from rendering an appeal futile by altering the appealed judgment or order by conducting other proceedings that may affect it.’” (Id. at 189.)

 

“[W]hether a matter is ‘embraced’ in or ‘affected’ by a judgment within the meaning of section 916 depends upon whether postjudgment trial court¿proceedings on the particular matter would have any impact on the ‘effectiveness' of the appeal. If so, the proceedings are stayed; if not, the proceedings are permitted.” (City of Santa Monica v. Stewart¿(2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 43, 79,¿as modified on denial of reh'g (Feb. 28, 2005) (Stewart).)

 

The court finds that a stay is appropriate in this case as to the entire matter because of the substantial interrelationship between the claims involving Halmark, currently on appeal, and claims that remain as to the other defendants.  As plead in the operative complaint, the claims are “inextricably intertwined,” alleging conspiracies and agency relations between all of the groups of defendants.   To allow the case to proceed against certain defendants, without the Hallmark Defendants, would lead to redundancy, inefficiency, and expense.   As just one example, to allow discovery to proceed but to exclude Hallmark from that process could require that all that discovery be redone should Hallmark return to active status in the case after the current appeal is concluded.   While it is true that a trial as to the Hemmis defendants and a jury exoneration of those defendants could relieve Hallmark of any further liability, the converse is also true.  If the jury finds against the Hemmis defendants, Hallmark would not be bound by that finding but would be permitted to its own trial on those same issues. 

 

The court sees no reason, however, to stay resolution of the attorney fee motions.  Those motions are briefed and set to be heard in the near future.  They are ancillary to the current appeal.  Further, if the court rules on those motions, and either party disagrees with the court’s ruling, an appeal can be taken and likely consolidated with the current appeal, ensuring that the entire issue of the anti-SLAPP motion is resolved once and for all, rather than broken up into piecemeal appeals. 

 


Case Number: 23VECV03069    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: W

JOSE MERA, et al. vs MARINE AMIRKHANYAN

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel defendant’s further discovery responses

 

Date of Hearing:        December 4, 2024                 Trial Date:       February 3, 2025  

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV03069

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff LZB Retail Inc.  

Responding Party:     Defendant SWZ, LLC

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 17, 2023, Plaintiff LZB Retail, Inc. filed a complaint against Defendant SWZ, LLC for breach of lease, breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealings, promissory estoppel, negligence and negligent misrepresentation. Plaintiff alleges as the Tenant, entered into a written Lease Agreement with Defendant, as the Landlord whereby Plaintiff agreed to lease a commercial space. Plaintiff further alleges Defendant breached the agreement by failing to give possession at the time agreed upon in the Lease Agreement and upon finally obtaining possession, Plaintiff’s contractor discovered that Defendant had failed to properly seal the Property’s slab. Plaintiff further alleges Defendant has refused to pay Plaintiff towards the cost of the construction of “Tenant’s Work” as agreed upon and Defendant has failed to provide Plaintiff with a sign panel as agreed upon. On October 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint adding a cause of action for false promise.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant SWZ, LLC to Further Respond to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED, in part.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant SWZ, LLC to Further Respond to Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED, in part.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant SWZ, LLC to Further Respond to Request for Admissions, Set One is MOOT.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff moves the court for an order compelling further responses from Defendant SWZ, LLC to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set One), Nos. 1 -53; Form Interrogatories (Set One), Nos. 12.1, 12.6, 12.7, 15.1, and 17.1; and Request for Admissions (Set One), Nos. 3, 8, 12, 13, 14 and 16.

 

The court notes an IDC was held October 10, 2024 whereby this court advised Defendant that there should be supplemental responses to the disputed discovery. SWZ served supplemental responses. In light of Defendant’s supplemental responses to the Request for Admissions, Plaintiff withdraws the motion to compel as to the RFA but does not withdraw its request for sanctions. Plaintiff further argues WZ’s supplemental responses to the disputed SROGs and FROGs continue to be deficient and require further supplementation.

 

As for the SROGs, the court finds the supplemental responses sufficient as to certain responses. Plaintiff notes that some of the SROGs inadvertently cited the original complaint rather than the FAC but claims the SROG nonetheless contained sufficient detail and an unambiguous request for all facts supporting the contention. The court disagrees. Plaintiff may serve supplemental discovery requests citing the correct paragraphs and language of the FAC. Moreover, responses where Defendant’s response is “the parties” is sufficient. The term ‘parties’ is defined.

 

However, the court agrees where the Defendant gives one word responses to the interrogatories, such as “operative fact,”  the response is insufficient. As noted by Plaintiff, “if a question does require the responding party to make reference to a pleading or document, the pleading or document should be identified and summarized so the answer is fully responsive to the question.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 784.) As such, Defendant should identify where in the operative FAC supports the contention. Moreover, the court agrees the responses such as “no documents were identified” (see SROG 29 and 30) are evasive.

 

As for the FROGs, the court finds the responses to FROG 15.1(a) and 15.1(b) sufficient. Plaintiff notes that the responses are not “complete and straightforward” because Defendant will state that it is asserting the affirmative defense as a matter of right and certain claims may be barred by statute of limitations, may contain speculative damage, and so on. However, this is sufficient. Defendant has stated why they are asserting these affirmative defenses in the first sentence. For 15.1(b), the term ‘parties’ is defined and the parties have the addresses and telephone numbers. Moreover, 15.1(c) only asks the responding party to identify the documents and the information of the person who has each document. Defendant has done so. Lastly, Plaintiff has demonstrated the responses were not sufficiently supplemented as to FROG 17.1.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant SWZ, LLC to Further Respond to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED, in part. Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant SWZ, LLC to Further Respond to Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED, in part. Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant SWZ, LLC to Further Respond to Request for Admissions, Set One is MOOT.

 

Sanctions

HUGO PEREYRA vs THE CANBY PLACE HOMEOWNERS association aka the canby place

 

plaintiff’s motions to compel initial discovery

 

Date of Hearing:        December 4, 2024                             Trial Date:       June 9, 2025

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV05472

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Hugo Pereyra

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 8, 2023, Plaintiff Hugo Pereyra filed a complaint against The Canby Place Homeowners Association aka The Canby Place for (1) Injunctive Relief and Damages for Violations of CC&Rs; (2) Injunctive Relief and Damages for Violations of Bylaws; (3) Injunctive Relief and Damages for Breach of Fiduciary Duties; (4) Injunctive Relief and Damages for Negligence; (5) Declaratory and Injunctive Relief; (6) Injunctive Relief to Enforce Right to Copies of Association Records; and (7) Accounting.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Compelling Defendant Canby Place Homeowners Association to Respond to Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Compelling Responses by Defendant Canby Place Homeowners Association to Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Order Compelling Responses by Defendant Canby Place Homeowners Association to Form Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Hugo Pereyra moves the court for an order, pursuant to Section 2031.300 of the Code of Civil Procedure, compelling The Canby Place Homeowners Association to respond to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One; Special Interrogatories, Set One; and Form Interrogatories, Set One. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant and their attorney for failure to serve a timely response.

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may also move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).)

 

On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff propounded discovery onto Defendant including Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Special Interrogatories and Form Interrogatories (all Set One). (Szabo Decl. ¶3, Exh. 1.) The deadline for responding was June 14, 2024. (Szabo Decl. ¶4.) On June 10, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel granted an extension for Defendant to respond to the discovery requests by July 5, 2024. (Szabo Decl. ¶5, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel granted several more requests for extensions on July 3, 2024, July 26, 2024, and August 8, 2024. (Szabo Decl. ¶¶6-9, Exhs. 3-6.) Plaintiff never received any discovery and on September 4, 2024, sent an e-mail to defense counsel demanding they provide responses without objections by September 11, 2024. (Szabo Decl. ¶¶10-11, Exh. 7.) Defendant never responded to Plaintiff’s letter and never produced any of the requested discovery. (Szabo Decl. ¶12.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s unopposed motions for an order compelling Defendant Canby Place Homeowners Association to Respond to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One are GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to produce verified discovery responses and documents, without objection, in twenty days.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant and their attorney of record for having to bring the instant motions. Plaintiff seeks $1,573.47 for their motion to compel responses to their requests for production of documents, $879.97 for their motion to compel responses to their special interrogatories, and $1,611.22 for their motion to compel responses to their form interrogatories.

 

If a propounding party moves for and obtains a court order compelling a response, the court shall impose monetary sanctions against the party failing to timely respond to interrogatories and demands for inspection unless that party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c), 2033.280(c); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) 

 

The court GRANTS the request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $822.50 for the motion to compel requests for production of documents, $660.00 for the motion to compel special interrogatories, and $285.00 for the motion to compel form interrogatories.


Case Number: 24VECV02088    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: W

GLOBAL INNOVATIONS, LLC VS NORMAN J. KRAVETZ

 

PLAINTIFF GLOBAL INNOVATIONS, LLC’S EX PARTE APPLICATION FOR RIGHT TO ATTACH ORDER AND PRE-JUDGMENT WRIT OF ATTACHMENT AGAINST DEFENDANT NORMA J. KRAVETZ

 

Date of Hearing:        December 4, 2024                             Trial Date:       None set. 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV02088

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Global Innovations, LLC

Responding Party:     Defendant Norman Kravetz

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff Global Innovations, LLC filed a complaint against Norman J. Kravetz for breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges on November 9, 2022, Defendant executed a Promissory Note in favor of Plaintiff, in the amount of $1,200.000.00. Plaintiff alleges after Defendant defaulted on the Original Note by failing to make timely payments, Plaintiff and Kravetz executed a First Amendment to Promissory Note on October 20, 2023, which amended certain terms of the Original Note. Plaintiff further alleges in the Amendment to Note, Plaintiff and Defendant stipulated to a balance as of the effective date of the Amended Note of $776,985.55 plus an “Extension Fee” of $19,424.64, payable by October 31, 2023, and a past-due payment of $33,858.98 by October 20, 2023. However, Plaintiff alleges on April 15, 2024, Defendants defaulted on the Agreement by failing to make required payments.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff Global Innovations, LLC’s Ex Parte Application for Right to Attach Order and Pre-Judgment Writ of Attachment Against Defendant Norma J. Kravetz is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of several court documents including a complaint, writ of attachment, motion to continue trial by less than 90 days in Pacific Western Bank v. Norman J. Kravetz et al, LASC Case No. 23STCV09801; complaint filed in LTL Insurance Services Inc. Profit Sharing Plan, et al. v. JH Portfolio Debt Equities, LLC, et al., LASC Case No. 24SMCV02361; complaint and writ of attachment filed in Aaron Kitzman et al. v. Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC et al., LASC Case No. 23VECV04655; complaint filed in RBE Capital Partners Fund LP v. JH Portfolio Debt Equities, LLC, LASC No. 24VECV00463; and complaint filed in Connie Brockamn et al. v. Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC et. al., LASC No. 23VECV05660.

 

Plaintiff seeks judicial notice of these documents to demonstrate Kravetz is personally named as a defendant in at least five other lawsuits with different lenders regarding breach of promissory notes of which enforcement efforts are underway totaling at least $22,880,000.00. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as to the existence of these documents.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff appears ex parte to request that the court issue a right to attach order and prejudgment writ of attachment on Defendant Kravetz in the total amount of $872,708.90. Plaintiff makes the application on the grounds that Plaintiff has a probable validity of prevailing against Kravetz and there is a grave and immediate danger that Plaintiff will suffer irreparable harm due to Kravetz’s self-admitted imminent likelihood of filing for bankruptcy.

 

“Upon the filing of the complaint or at any time thereafter, the plaintiff may apply pursuant to this article for a right to attach order and a writ of attachment by filing an application for the order and writ with the court in which the action is brought.”  (CCP § 484.010.)

 

The application shall be executed under oath and must include: (1) a statement showing that the attachment is sought to secure the recovery on a claim upon which an attachment may be issued; (2) a statement of the amount to be secured by the attachment; (3) a statement that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based; (4) a statement that the applicant has no information or belief that the claim is discharged or that the prosecution of the action is stayed in a proceeding under the Bankruptcy Act (11 U.S.C. section 101 et seq.); and (5) a description of the property to be attached under the writ of attachment and a statement that the plaintiff is informed and believes that such property is subject to attachment.  (CCP § 484.020.) 

 

“The application [for a writ of attachment] shall be supported by an affidavit showing that the plaintiff on the facts presented would be entitled to a judgment on the claim upon which the attachment is based.”  (CCP § 484.030.)  “The declarations in the moving papers must contain evidentiary facts, stated ‘with particularity,’ and based on actual personal knowledge with all documentary evidence properly identified and authenticated.” (Hobbs v. Weiss (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 76, 79–80 [citing CCP § 482.040].) “In contested applications, the court must consider the relative merits of the positions of the respective parties and make a determination of¿the probable outcome of the litigation.” (Hobbs, supra, 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 80.) 

 

The court shall issue a right to attach order if the court finds all of the following: (1) The claim upon which the attachment is based is one upon which an attachment may be issued. (2) The plaintiff has established the probable validity of the claim upon which the attachment is based.  (3) The attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based. (4) The amount to be secured by the attachment is greater than zero. (CCP §484.090.)  

 

Ex Parte

 

To make a showing for an ex parte right to attach order, the moving party must demonstrate that they will suffer great or irreparable injury by the delay of the issuance of the writ of attachment until after a noticed hearing. The moving party demonstrate great or irreparable injury if there is a danger that the property to be attached may be concealed, substantially impaired in value, or made unavailable to the levy of an attachment or it may be inferred that the defendant has failed to pay the debt underlying the requested attachment and the defendant is insolvent in the sense that the defendant is generally not paying his or her debts as those debts become due, unless the debts are subject to a bona fide dispute. (CCP §485.010.)

 

Plaintiff argues there is a significant and immediate danger that Kravetz will conceal, substantially impair, or otherwise make unavailable the property sought to be attached by Plaintiff if an order is delayed until this matter can be heard on notice. Kravetz has admitted he is in default on the Note and Amended Note and is also in default on at least five other loans with different lenders that are the subject of five other lawsuits naming Kravetz as a defendant. He has also admitted that he is experiencing serious financial issues and is unable pay his debts related to all of his loans. (See Enenstein Decl. ¶12, Exh. 1.) Kravetz has also admitted to speaking with bankruptcy counsel, which evidences that he may imminently file for bankruptcy. (See Enenstein Decl. ¶13, Exh. 1.) Plaintiff also points to another action where Kravetz admitted he needed to continue trial because he was unable to pay his counsel as a result of lawsuits and attachments levied against him in that action. (RJN, Exh. 8.)

 

All together, Plaintiff argues this evidence is more than sufficient to show there is a danger that Kravetz will “conceal or substantially impair” the value of his assets, thereby raising an inference “great or irreparable injury” to Plaintiff under Code of Civil Procedure section 485.010(b)(1). Furthermore, the fact that Kravetz admitted that he is not in a position to pay his debts related to all the outstanding loans he’s taken raises a statutory inference of insolvency and “great or irreparable injury” to Plaintiff under Code of Civil Procedure section 485.010(b)(2).

 

In opposition, Kravetz argues Plaintiff's Application fails to provide any evidence of concealment or evidence that any property may be substantially impaired in value without this Application. Kravetz has been in the case since June 2024 and no property has been transferred. Moreover, Plaintiffs Application provides no support that Defendant is insolvent or that any of the other cases he cites to are not subject to a bona fide dispute. For example, Plaintiff pointed to Kitzman v. Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC (LASC Case No. 23VECV04655) for the proposition Kravetz has additional debt that is undisputed. However, Kravetz contends this is untrue as Kravetz filed a cross-complaint in that action and the claims remained disputed in that action  until the parties stipulated to a judgment based on Defendants’ 998 offer.

 

Furthermore, with the exception of Pacific Western Bank v. Kravetz, in all other cases Plaintiff cites, Kravetz was sued only as a guarantor. The monies are owed by separate entities. Kravetz maintains the other lawsuits in which he is a defendant do not render him insolvent. In each lawsuit Plaintiff cites, Kravetz has worked, and continues to work, with lenders to restructure the debt for pay off. Similarly, because Kravetz has a strong payment history with Plaintiff and Plaintiff called the loan due after only 4 days of a missed payment, Defendant disputes the merits of Plaintiff’s claims. Kravetz goes on to argue Plaintiff misused Defendant’s statement that he has consulted with bankruptcy advisor to mislead the court into believing Defendant Kravetz’s bankruptcy was imminent. Kravetz also testifies he has no intention to sell, transfer, or further encumber any of his personal property Plaintiff seeks to attach, pending a future attachment hearing, and will give ample notice if those changes. (Kravetz Decl. ¶1.)

                                                                                            

The court finds Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently show it will suffer “great or irreparable injury” if the issuance of the writ of attachment is delayed by a noticed hearing. There is no evidence of any bankruptcy proceedings before this court. The mere speculation is not enough. Furthermore, although Plaintiff has provided evidence upon which writs were attached to the Vanalden Property, those writs have already been issued. Plaintiff fails to provide how they would suffer irreparable injury if the subject property has already ordered to levy by other plaintiffs.  

 

In the instant matter, however, the court transformed this ex parte application into a noticed motion and has given Kravetz full opportunity to oppose this motion.  Therefore, the question becomes whether plaintiff has established the other bases for a writ of attachment order. 

 

Basis of Attachment

 

As noted above, “an attachment may be issued only in an action on a claim or claims for money, each of which is based upon a contract, express or implied, where the total amount of the claim or claims is a fixed or readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500) exclusive of costs, interest, and attorney's fees.”  (CCP § 483.010(a).)  “An attachment may not be issued on a claim which is secured by any interest in real property arising from agreement ….”  (CCP § 483.010(b).)   

 

In this matter, Plaintiff’s claims are for monies owed upon a contract. Moreover, the amount sought to be attached by Plaintiff is of a fixed or readily ascertainable amount greater than $500. Plaintiff seeks $872,708.90. Lastly, Plaintiff testifies Plaintiff’s claim is not secured by any interest in real property. (See Wazana Decl. ¶13, Exhs. 3, 7.)

 

Lastly, as noted by Plaintiff, “[i]f the action is against a defendant who is a natural person, an attachment may be issued only on a claim which arises out of the conduct by the defendant of a trade, business, or profession. An Attachment may not be issued on a claim against a defendant who is a natural person if the claim is based on the sale or lease of property…used by the defendant primarily for personal, family, or household purpose.” (CCP § 483.010(c).) There is no indication that Defendant intended to use the property primarily for personal, family, or household purposes and during Defendant’s deposition, he admitted he owed Plaintiff money because they “lent money or transferred other obligations” and he has admitted that he has over fifty years of experience in real estate development. (See Enenstein Decl., Exh. 1.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s claim meets the statutory requirements upon which attachment may be issued.

 

Purpose and Amount of Attachment

 

As noted above, to obtain a writ of attachment, Plaintiff must prove that the attachment is not sought for any purpose other than to secure recovery on the claim. (CCP § 484.090.) Here, Counsel testifies the ex parte application is brought solely to secure recovery of monies owed to Plaintiff under the Note and Amendment to the Note that are the subject of Plaintiff’s contractual claim against Defendant Norman J. Kravetz. (Enenstein Decl. ¶2.) The court also notes the total amount sought to be secured is $872,708.90, which is evidently greater than zero.

 

Defendant opposes the amount sought on the grounds it includes a fee request. Defendant contends Plaintiff rationalizes he should be entitled to $25,689.12 in fees for “this application only” but the prerequisite for this rule is that Plaintiff received a judgment and was therefore entitled to use the fee schedule to determine “reasonable fees”. Defendant further contends any request for attorney fees is subject to a proper notice.

 

The court agrees at least that the “fixed or readily ascertainable amount” excludes attorney fees. Regardless, the amount is still fixed and readily ascertainable. However, Section 483.015 allows costs and allowable attorney fees to be included in a plaintiff’s application for a right to attach order and writ of attachment.

 

Probable Validity of Claims

 

“A claim has ‘probable validity’ where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.”  (CCP § 481.190.)  In determining this issue, the court must consider the relative merits of the positions of the respective parties.  (Kemp Bros. Construction, Inc. v. Titan Electric Corp. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1474, 1484.)  The court does not determine whether the claim is actually valid; that determination will be made at trial and is not affected by the decision on the application for the order.  (CCP § 484.050(b).)

 

Plaintiff contends they can sufficiently establish probable validity of their claim. The application is based on a claim for breach of contract. A claim for breach of contract requires a showing of the following elements: (1) the existence of a contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damages to plaintiff therefrom.  (Acoustics, Inc. v. Trepte Construction Co. (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 887, 913.)

 

Plaintiff argues the evidence clearly demonstrates that Plaintiff is more likely than not to obtain a judgement against Kravetz for its breach of contract claim because Kravetz agreed to and entered the terms of the Note and Amendment to Note and has defaulted on the note by failing to make timely payments. (Wazana Decl., ¶¶ 5-22.) Kravetz admitted in his deposition that he agreed to the Note in favor of Plaintiff for $1,200,000.00 and the Note and Amendment to Note show Kravetz signed them. (Enenstein Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exh. 1 at 21:20-23.) Kravetz admitted that he received the $1,200,000.00 from Plaintiff under the Note. (Enenstein Decl., Exh. 1 at 28:23-29:10). Kravetz then made some payments on the Note, but then by failing to make timely payments thereto. (Enenstein Decl., ¶¶ 6-7, Exh. 1 at 30:1-4, 17-20; Wazana Decl., ¶ 14.) After defaulting on the Note, Kravetz then agreed to the Amended Note, whereby Kravetz agreed to pay a total balance of $776,985.55 with 10% interest. (See Enenstein Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. 1 at 28:23-29:10; Wazana Decl., ¶¶ 16-17, Exh. 7.) It also cannot be disputed that Kravetz defaulted on the Note and Promissory Note. (See Enenstein Decl., Exh. 7, 25:17-24.)

 

Under these circumstances, Plaintiff’s evidence shows the following: (1) a contract exists between the parties by way of the Note and Amendment to Note agreements; (2) plaintiff performed under the promissory notes by funding the required payment; (3) Defendant breached his obligations under the Note and Amendment to Note agreements by failing to make the necessary payments; and (4) Plaintiff was damaged as a result.  

 

Exemption

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 485.220(a)(4) requires that “the property sought to be attached, or the portion thereof to be specified in the writ, is not exempt from attachment.” Here, Plaintiff seeks to attach Kravetz’ interests in the Business Center Property, the Vanalden Property, any securities, any stocks, and any bonds as well as attach any interest in money on premises where a trade or business is conducted, plus money in excess of $1,000 located elsewhere or in a deposit account. (See AT-125.) Counsel for Plaintiff attests he is informed and believes that no assets sought to be attached are exempt. (Enenstein Decl., ¶ 16.)

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing that the property listed is exempt from attachment. Specifically, Plaintiff seeks to attach 4535 Vanalden Avenue, Tarzana, 91356 family home and is subject to exemption from attachment. Moreover, Plaintiff seeks to attach the commercial property located at 19350 Business Center Drive, Northridge, 91324, but this property is not owned by Defendant.

 

In Reply, Plaintiff states it will no longer seek to attach the Business center Property since it learned that Kravetz no longer owns it. However, Plaintiff contends the Vanalden Property is still subject to attachment, and even if the homestead exemption applies, it only applies to at most $600,000 of the value of the home. Kravetz’s claimed “necessities of life” exemptions for deposit accounts should be denied outright since he has failed to provide any financial statement as is required by Code of Civil Procedure section 703.530. Similarly, Kravetz’s other exemptions for personal property, wages, and social security should also be denied because he has failed to provide any evidentiary support for such exemptions, as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 484.070. But even if those exemptions apply, they have no effect on this Application since Plaintiff is not seeking to attach his personal property, wages, and social security.

 

The court agrees there is a limit to the homestead exemption. The amount of the homestead exemption is limited to either $300,00.00 or $600,000.00, depending on the median countywide home price.  (See CCP § 704.730 (a).)  Plaintiff maintains at most, Kravetz is entitled to an exemption of only $600,000. Accordingly, the court applies the exemption in the amount of $600,000.00.

 

The court finds Kravetz has not established how the amounts Plaintiff seeks to attach are

necessary for his or his family’s support pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 703.530. Moreover, Plaintiff is not seeking to attach the various personal items listed including household furnishings, appliances, art, heirlooms, jewelry, social security, and wages. As such, these two exemptions are denied.

 

Undertaking

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 489.210 requires the plaintiff to file an undertaking before issuance of a writ of attachment.  Code of Civil Procedure section 489.220 provides, with exceptions, for an undertaking in the amount of $10,000.

 

Plaintiff does not dispute the $10,000.00 amount (See AT-125, Sec. 2(h).)

 

Accordingly, the application is GRANTED in the amount of $872,708.90.  Plaintiff is ordered to post an undertaking in the amount of $10,000.00.


Case Number: 24VECV03148    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: W

VINCENT LEMUS, et al. vs HI DESERT MOBILE HOME PARK, LP

 

defendant hi desert mobile home park, lp’s motion to strike portions of plaintiff’s complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        December 4, 2024                             Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV03148

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Hi Desert Mobile Home Park, LP

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Vicente Lemus, Rose Dodd, Juliet Lemus by and through her guardian ad litem Rose Dodd, Ivory Lemus by and through her guardian ad litem Rose Dodd, and Isiah Lemus by and through his guardian ad litem Rose Dodd  

Meet and Confer:      Yes (Mintesnot Decl. ¶2.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a habitability case. On July 2, 2024, Plaintiffs Vicente Lemus, Rose Dodd, Juliet Lemus by and through her guardian ad litem Rose Dodd, Ivory Lemus by and through her guardian ad litem Rose Dodd, and Isiah Lemus by and through his guardian ad litem Rose Dodd filed a complaint against Hi Desert Mobile Home Park, LP for (1) Breach of Warranty of Habitability; (2) Breach of Warranty of Quiet Enjoyment; (3) Nuisance; (4) Violations of Civil Code section 1941.1, et seq. and (5) Negligence. Plaintiffs allege shortly after taking possession of an apartment from Defendant, Plaintiffs began to experience multiple substandard conditions including deteriorated flooring, cracked walls and ceilings; defective cabinets; faulty and defective electrical wiring and outlets; lack of hot water; improper sewage/garbage disposal, inoperable doors and windows, excessive odors and lack of sanitation in the common areas and infestations of roaches and vermin.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Hi Desert Mobile Home Park, LP’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Hi Desert Mobile Home Park LP moves the court for an order striking portions of Plaintiffs Vicente Lemus, Rose Dodd, Juliet Lemus by and through her guardian ad litem Rose Dodd, Ivory Lemus by and through her guardian ad litem Rose Dodd, and Isiah Lemus by and through his guardian ad litem Rose Dodd complaint.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP § 437.) Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) A plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice.¿ To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.¿(Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.).)¿¿ 

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs’ complaint seeks punitive damages, but Plaintiffs have pled no facts to establish that any conduct by Defendant was performed with malice, oppression or fraud. Specifically, Plaintiffs have made broad allegations and blanket characterizations of Defendant’s conduct without explaining how such conduct arises to the requisite level of culpability to impose punitive damages. At most, Plaintiffs' allegations might rise to the level of negligence.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue they have pled sufficient facts to support their request for punitive damages. The court agrees the allegations are sufficient for the purposes of a motion to strike. The complaint alleges Defendant knew or should have known of the substandard conditions of the subject property and intentionally rented the substandard unit to Plaintiffs. (Compl. ¶34, 43, 60.)  Moreover, when complained to Defendants about the deplorable conditions of the unit, Defendants would make promises to fix but either ignored the complaints or when attempted to fix, Defendants failed to correctly fix the issues.  (Compl. ¶45,63.) Plaintiffs assert this was done intentionally, recklessly, maliciously and with oppression. Taken as a whole and assuming the truth of the well-pleaded allegations, the foregoing is sufficient to defend against a motion to strike.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Hi Desert Mobile Home Park, LP’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiffs’ Complaint is DENIED.
