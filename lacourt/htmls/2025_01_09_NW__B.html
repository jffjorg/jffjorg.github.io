DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 20STCV49896    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: B

Hearing:          01/09/2025

Re:                   20STCV49896 Dolores Cromwell vs Rosa Nunfio

 

 

Defendant-in-Intervention Rosa Nunfio (“Nunfio”)’s Motions to Compel Dolores Cromwell (“Plaintiff”)’s Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set Two, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, is granted. Plaintiff is ordered to serve Defendant with verified, objection-free responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set Two, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, within 20 days of this hearing. Additionally, Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, are ordered to pay $1,942.50 in sanctions to defense counsel within 20 days.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS

 

Interrogatories

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute does not contain a time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

Where there has been no timely response to interrogatories, the demanding party may seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300(b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. (CCP § 2031.300(a).) Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the interrogatories. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.

 

Request for Production of Documents

 

If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production. (CCP § 2025.480(a).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel . . . responses to interrogatories or production demands, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.” (CCP § 2030.290(c).) Similarly, “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (CCP § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant-in-Intervention Rosa Nunfio (“Nunfio”) moves to compel Plaintiff Dolores Cromwell (“Plaintiff”)’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set Two, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Nunfio also seeks sanctions in the amount of $882.50 per motion, comprised of 3.5 hours of work at an hourly rate of $235.00 and a $60.00 filing fee. (Trafton Decl. ¶ 10.)

 

Nunfio moves to compel Plaintiff’s response to discovery because Plaintiff failed to provide any responses. Defendant propounded discovery requests on Plaintiff on March 27, 2024. (Trafton Decl. ¶ 3; Exh A. [discovery service].) Plaintiff never provided responses. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

MARLO HEMPSTEAD VS GAME SPORTS BAR ANd grill, llc, et al.

Date of Hearing:          January 9, 2025                       Trial Date:       None

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         21STCV28253

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Marlo Hempstead

Responding Party:       Defendants 5600 Gateway Center, LLC and H.B.D. Inc.

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 2, 2021, Plaintiff Marlo Hempstead (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Game Sports Bar and Grill, LLC (“Game Sports”), Tyrei Lacyt (“Tyrei”), Sylvia Lacy (“Sylvia”), 5600 Gateway Center, LLC (“5600 Gateway”), H.B.D. Incorporated (“H.B.D”), Burger King Corporation (“Burger King”), and Does 1 to 50, alleging five causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) negligent hiring, training, and supervision; (3) negligent infliction of emotional distress; (4) assault and battery; and (5) premises liability. The complaint alleges that, on or about June 28, 2020, Plaintiff got into a verbal altercation with a security guard employed by Defendants, who then allegedly shot Plaintiff as he attempted to leave the premises. (Complaint ¶¶ 15-19.) As a result, Plaintiff alleges serious injuries and damages. (Ibid. at ¶ 20.)

 

On April 7, 2023, Game Sports, Tyrei, and Sylvia filed a motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to each cause of action. The motion was granted in its entirety on June 29, 2023.

 

On April 11, 2024, Defendants 5600 Gateway and H.B.D. (hereinafter “Defendants”) filed a motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to each cause of action. The motion was granted in its entirety on August 30, 2024.

 

On November 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to vacate judgment granting Defendants’ motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On December 23, 2024, 5600 Gateway and H.B.D. filed their opposition.

 

Plaintiff did not file a reply.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief [from judgment]. [Citation.]” (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.) An application for relief under this section must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect of the moving party or its attorney. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Additionally, an application for relief under this section “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed herein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.” (CCP § 473(b).) Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Ibid.) When dismissal is the attorney’s fault, the six-month period starts to run from the date of the entry of dismissal. (CCP § 473(b); Sugasawara v. Newland (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 294, 295.)

DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendants’ December 23, 2024, evidentiary objections are sustained as to No. 1. Plaintiff claims that he appeared for the hearing on Defendants’ motion for summary judgment on August 30, 2024, and was told by a clerk that summary judgment was denied. (Motion p. 1:20-21.) However, the clerk’s alleged statement constitutes hearsay pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code § 1200 et seq.

 

Defendants’ evidentiary objections are overruled as to No. 2. Plaintiff has personal knowledge of the general nature of his hospital stays and treatments for his kidney. (Cal. Evid. Code §§ 70-702 and 801.) Plaintiff does not need to be a medical expert to understand that he requires surgery. (Ibid.)

 

Statutory Requirements

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff failed to include a proposed pleading. An application for relief under CCP § 473(b) must be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein; otherwise, the application shall not be granted. (CCP § 473(b).) Therefore, the instant motion must be denied.

 

Nonetheless, for both parties’ benefit, the Court now considers the merits of Plaintiff’s motion.

 

Plaintiff’s motion claims that he “vehemently oppose[s]” Defendants’ motion for summary judgment. (Motion p. 1:20-21.) However, Defendants’ motion was already heard and ruled upon at its August 30, 2024, hearing. Any opposition to Defendants’ motion for summary judgment was due fourteen calendar days before its hearing. (See CCP § 1005.) Therefore, Plaintiff was required to file his opposition by August 16, 2024. The Court notes that Plaintiff filed a supposed opposition to Defendants’ motion for summary judgment on November 26, 2024, nearly three months after the motion was heard. Plaintiff’s motion offers no showing of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect which resulted in his failure to oppose Defendants’ motion for summary judgment.

 

 

 

CONCLUSION

 

Therefore, on both procedural and substantive grounds, Plaintiff Marlo Hempstead’s Motion to Vacate Judgment is DENIED.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief [from dismissal]. [Citation.]” (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.) An application for relief under this section must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect of the moving party or its attorney. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Additionally, an application for relief under this section “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed herein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.” (CCP § 473(b).) Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Ibid.) When dismissal is the attorney’s fault, the six-month period starts to run from the date of the entry of dismissal. (CCP § 473(b); Sugasawara v. Newland (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 294, 295.)

DISCUSSION

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff failed to include a proposed pleading. An application for relief under CCP § 473(b) must be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein; otherwise, the application shall not be granted. (CCP § 473(b).) Therefore, the instant motion must be denied.

 

Nonetheless, for both parties’ benefit, the Court now considers the merits of Plaintiff’s motion.

 

Plaintiff argues that the Court should set aside dismissal because Plaintiff did not sign, nor agree to sign, the settlement agreement. (Motion p. 3:6-12.) The parties have resolved their issues as to Cruz’s responses to Rasier’s Special Interrogatories, Set Two, and Request for Production of Documents, Set Two. (Joint Decl. ¶ 17.) The parties have not resolved their issues as to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Requests for Admissions, Set Two. (Ibid. at ¶ 18.)

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

Interrogatories

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 provides, in pertinent part, that “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” (CCP § 2030.300(a).) 

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2030.300(c).) 

 

Request for Admissions

CCP § 2033.290 provides, in pertinent part, that “[o]n receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete. (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (CCP § 2033.290(a).)

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2033.290(c).)

Under California law, the objecting party has the burden of justifying its objections when the propounding party requests that the Court order further responses. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.) Further, under CCP § 2023.010, it is a misuse of discovery to make unmerited objections. (CCP § 2023.010(e).) 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer Requirement

A motion to compel further responses shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP §§ 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b), and 2031.310(b).) Rasier propounded discovery requests upon Cruz on April 26, 2024. (Dance Decl. ¶ 5.) Cruz failed to provide responses by the May 29, 2024 deadline. (Ibid. at ¶ 6.) On June 4, 2024, defense counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter. (Ibid. at ¶ 7; Exh. D [June 4, 2024, correspondence].) Defense counsel granted Plaintiff’s counsel various extensions to serve discovery responses. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7 and 8; Exh. D; Exh. E [June 28, 2024, correspondence].) On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff served their discovery responses. (Ibid. at ¶ 9; Exh. G [Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One].) On July 12, 2024, defense counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding alleged deficiencies in Plaintiff’s responses. (Ibid. at ¶ 10; Exh. H [July 12, 2024, correspondence].) On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff served allegedly defective amended responses to Rasier’s discovery requests. (Ibid. at ¶ 11; Exh. J [Plaintiff’s supplemental responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One].) Defense counsel attended an informal discovery conference on August 26, 2024, in an attempt to resolve these discovery issues, but were ultimately unsuccessful. (Ibid. at ¶ 13.) After additional extensions and conferring, Rasier ultimately filed the instant motions. (See Dance Decl. ¶¶ 15-17.) Finally, counsel meet and conferred regarding discovery issues on December 17 and 20, 2024. (Joint Decl. ¶ 3.) Pursuant to CCP § 2016.040, Rasier presents facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” at an informal resolution. (CCP § 2016.040.) Therefore, Rasier satisfies the meet and confer requirements of CCP §§ 2030.300(b) and 2033.290(b).

 

Form Interrogatories, Set One

Rasier moves to compel Plaintiff’s further response to Form Interrogatories, Set One, No. 17.1. (Motion p. 2:2-7.) No. 17.1 requests additional information for each response to Rasier’s request for admission that is not an unqualified admission. (Exh. H [Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One].)

Form interrogatories were created and approved by the Judicial Council to allow discovery into information regarding an individual’s general background information. A party responding to these interrogatories owes a duty to respond in good faith as best as possible. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783.)

Here, Plaintiff has provided, in total, three initial or amended responses to Form Interrogatories No. 17.1. (See Separate Statement pp. 4-20.) These responses provide extensive information and detail as to nature of the accident, alleged cause of the accident, Plaintiff’s alleged injuries, medical treatment, providers, and costs. (Ibid.) Rasier’s most recent set of requests for admissions contains 362 new requests. (Opposition p. 2:9-13.) Despite this burdensome discovery request, Plaintiff made a good faith effort to provide responses that are “as complete and straightfoward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (CCP § 2030.220(a).)

Therefore, Rasier’s motion to compel Cruz’s further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, is denied.

 

Requests for Admissions, Set One

Rasier moves to compel Cruz’s further responses to Requests for Admissions, Set Two, Nos. 83-84, 290, 330-393, and 396-401. (Joint Decl. ¶ 18.)

Rasier’s motion is denied in its entirety.

First, Rasier fails to set forth No. 83 in its separate statement. Therefore, it is denied. Rasier’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s further response to Requests for Admissions, Set Two, No. 290 is denied for the same.

Monetary Sanctions

I.       BACKGROUND  

On February 14, 2022, Plaintiff Glendis Beker (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants the Hertz Corporation (“Hertz”), Romaine Kirlew (“Kirlew”), and Does 1 through 50 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging three causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) negligence per se; and (3) statutory liability. The complaint arises from an April 29, 2021, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiff allegedly suffered injuries when Defendants, due to negligent operation and ownership of a motor vehicle, collided with Plaintiff’s motor vehicle. (Complaint ¶¶ 13-19.)

On March 27, 2024, Hertz filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On June 6, 2024, the matter was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024. 

On November 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition. 

On November 15, 2024, Hertz filed their reply. 

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Hertz moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff’s claim for negligence against Hertz is barred by the Graves Amendment; and (2) even if the Graves Amendment did not bar Plaintiff’s claim, Plaintiff will not be able to establish the elements of breach and causation due to lack of evidence. (Motion p. 2:14-23.)

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).) 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [it is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) “The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence. 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff’s November 12, 2024, evidentiary objections are sustained as to Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, and 10. The Hall declaration is not signed and was not made under penalty of perjury. Therefore, it is inadmissible pursuant to CCP §§ 98 and 437c. Plaintiff’s objections are also sustained as to Nos. 12 and 14 as the traffic collision report and rental agreement lack foundation and authentication. (Cal. Evid. Code §§ 403, 1400, and 1271.)

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections are overruled as to Nos. 13 and 15.

 

Defendant’s Burden 

Hertz fails to meet their initial burden. 

Hertz moves for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff’s claim for negligence against Hertz is barred by the Graves Amendment; and (2) even if the Graves Amendment did not bar Plaintiff’s claim, Plaintiff will not be able to establish the elements of breach and causation due to lack of evidence. (Motion p. 2:14-23.)

 

The Graves Amendment

Hertz argues that Plaintiff’s claims must fail pursuant to 49 USC § 30106. (Motion p. 7:10-13; the “Graves Amendment” or “Graves”.) The Graves Amendment states in relevant part:

“An owner of a motor vehicle that rents or leases the vehicle to a person (or an affiliate of the owner) shall not be liable under the law of any State or political subdivision thereof, by reason of being the owner of the vehicle (or an affiliate of the owner), for harm to persons or property that results or arises out of the use, operation, or possession of the vehicle during the period of the rental or lease, if--

 

(1) the owner (or an affiliate of the owner) is engaged in the trade or business of renting or leasing motor vehicles; ¿and

(2) there is no negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner (or an affiliate of the owner).” (49 USC § 30106(a) – 49 USC § 30106(a)(2).)”

 

Graves generally preempts all state statutory and common law to the extent those laws hold owners in the business of renting or leasing motor vehicles vicariously liable for the negligence of drivers, except when there is negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner. (29 A.L.R. Fed. 2d 223.) However, not all state statutes are preempted.

First, Hertz must provide evidence that they are engaged in the trade or business of renting or leasing motor vehicles. (49 USC § 30106(a)(1).) Hertz claims that, at the time of the subject incident, they were in the business of renting or leasing motor vehicles. (Hertz Separate Statement of Facts “H-SSF” No. 7.) Plaintiff disputes this fact. (See Plaintiff Response to Hertz Separate Statement of Facts “PR-HSSF” No 7.) As evidence, Hertz solely relies on the declaration of Kirstie F. Hall (“Hall”). As aforementioned, the Hall declaration is unsigned and unsworn. Therefore, the declaration is inadmissible for the purposes of this summary judgment. Consequently, Hertz fails to provide admissible evidence that Plaintiff’s claims are barred by the Graves Amendment.

 

Breach and Causation

Next, Hertz argues that, even if the Graves Amendment does not bar Plaintiff’s claims, Plaintiff cannot provide evidence proving elements of negligence, namely, breach and causation. (Motion p. 11:3-8.)

To succeed on a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must show that: (1) defendant owed a legal duty to plaintiff to use due care; (2) defendant breached that duty; (3) defendant’s breach was the actual or proximate cause of plaintiff’s harm; and (4) plaintiff suffered damages as a result. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318; Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.) Hertz argues that Plaintiff cannot establish a breach of duty or causation because Hertz, a business entity, was not operating the vehicle at the time of the underlying incident. (H-SSF No. 5.) Hertz further claims that there is no evidence of negligent maintenance of the rental car or negligent entrustment to Kirlew. (H-SSF Nos. 9 – 12.)

Plaintiff disputes these claims. (PR-HSSF Nos. 9-12.) As evidence, Hertz relies on the inadmissible Hall declaration and unauthenticated traffic collision report. (See Hertz Exhibit List Exh. C [traffic collision report].) Hertz also relies on its responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One. (See Hertz Exhibit List Exh. H [Hertz’s responses to form interrogatories].) Finally, Hertz also relies on Plaintiff responses to Hertz’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 20.9-20.10, where Plaintiff states that they are unaware of any malfunctions or defects in a vehicle which contributed to the underlying incident. (Hertz Exhibit List Exh. E [Plaintiff’s response Nos. 20.9-20.10].)

However, to succeed on a motion for summary judgment, it is insufficient for movant to merely point out the absence of evidence. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 856.) Instead, the movant must “present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Gaggero, supra, 108 Cal.App.4th at 891.) Here, Hertz fails to provide evidence that Plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence as to Hertz’s negligent maintenance, entrustment, or other wrongdoing in relation to the underlying incident. In fact, discovery in the instant action remains ongoing. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts “P-SSF” No. 20; Exh. N [signed stipulation to continue trial and all related dates]; Movsesian Decl. ¶¶ 23 and 24.) Therefore, it’s very possible that Plaintiff could obtain evidence supporting their negligence claim against Hertz.

Since Hertz failed to meet their burden on summary judgment, Plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc., supra, (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th at 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].)

Accordingly, Hertz’s motion for summary judgment is denied.

 

V.       CONCLUSION

             Hertz fails to meet their initial burden on a motion for summary judgment. Accordingly, Hertz’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

LEGAL STANDARDS

 

Interrogatories

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute does not contain a time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

Where there has been no timely response to interrogatories, the demanding party may seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300(b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. (CCP § 2031.300(a).) Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the interrogatories. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.

 

Request for Production of Documents

 

If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production. (CCP § 2025.480(a).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel . . . responses to interrogatories or production demands, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.” (CCP § 2030.290(c).) Similarly, “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (CCP § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant AJR Investment Partnership, LLC (“Defendant”) moves to compel Plaintiff Parvin Shakibian (“Plaintiff”)’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant also seeks sanctions in the amount of $585.00 per motion, comprised of three hours of work at an hourly rate of $175.00 and a $60.00 filing fee. (Cavanna Decl. ¶ 17.)

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s response to discovery because Plaintiff failed to provide any responses. Defendant propounded discovery requests on Plaintiff on September 6, 2023. (Cavanna Decl. ¶ 3; Exh A. [discovery service].) Plaintiff never provided responses. (Ibid. at ¶ 16.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One are granted. Plaintiff is to serve Defendant with verified, objection-free responses to Special Interrogatories, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, within 20 days of this hearing.

 

Considering Plaintiff’s total failure to comply with their discovery obligations, the Court orders Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions in the reduced amount of $410.00 per motion (reflecting the straightforward nature of this motion and lack of opposition), payable to defense counsel within 20 days of this hearing.
