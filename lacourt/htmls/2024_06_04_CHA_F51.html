DEPARTMENT F51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F51, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2251.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 22CHCV00493    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: F51

JUNE 3, 2024

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22CHCV00493

 

Motion Filed: 7/28/23                                                                               JURY TRIAL: 3/17/25

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Gary G. Topolewski (“Mr. Topolewski”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Bank of the West (on behalf of real party in interest, BMO Harris Bank N.A.) (“Plaintiff”)

NOTICE: OK¿ 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order granting summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication in favor of Mr. Topolewski and against Plaintiff on Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is denied. 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE: Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is granted.

 

BACKGROUND 

 

This action arises out of three business loans extended by Plaintiff to nonmoving defendant Topolewski America, Inc. (the “Company”). Plaintiff allegedly entered into three separate loan agreements with the Company on 7/26/19, 5/14/20, and 7/12/21, each secured by various pieces of the Company’s equipment as collateral. (FAC ¶¶ 14–22.)¿The equipment collateral was allegedly purchased from and insured by nonparty Finning International, Inc. (“Finning”). (Id. at ¶¶ 64, 66.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that the Company breached the loan and security agreements “by, among other things, failing and refusing to allow [Plaintiff] to inspect the Equipment Collateral.” (Id. at ¶ 48.) Plaintiff also alleges that it has reason to believe the Company “never actually had ownership or possession of the Collateral and that [the Company] made continuing misrepresentations to [Plaintiff] concerning [the Company’s] alleged ownership and possession thereof.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that Mr. Topolewski and the Company “are the alter egos of one another, and each is fully responsible and liable for all obligations and debts.” (Id. at ¶ 13.)

 

On 7/6/22, Plaintiff filed its original complaint against Defendants, alleging causes of action for Breach of Contract and Breach of Security Agreement with respect to each of the three loans. Plaintiff’s complaint also pleads causes of action for Recovery of Personal Property; Temporary Restraining Order/Preliminary Injunction; Conversion; and Fraud and Deceit.

 

On 3/29/23, Plaintiff filed its first amended complaint (“FAC”), alleging the same causes of action against Defendants. On 4/28/23, Defendants filed their answer.

 

On 7/28/23, Mr. Topolewski filed the instant motion for summary judgment. On 10/10/23, Plaintiff filed its opposition. On 10/17/23, Mr. Topolewski filed his reply. On 10/19/23, the Court continued the hearing on the instant motion to this date to allow Plaintiff to conduct additional discovery. On 5/20/24, Plaintiff filed a supplemental opposition. On 5/28/24, Mr. Topolewski filed his response thereto.

 

ANALYSIS

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 65, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381–382.) 

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519–1520.)  

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) 

 

A.    Alter Ego Theory

 

Here, Mr. Topolewski argues that he is entitled to summary judgment on Plaintiff’s claims against him because no triable issue of fact exists as to whether he was the alter ego of the Company, and therefore liable for the Company’s alleged misconduct. Ordinarily, shareholders are not personally responsible for corporate liabilities. However, if a corporation has been operated as the “alter ego” of its shareholders, the corporation’s creditors may be permitted to “pierce the corporate veil” and enforce their claims directly against the shareholders. (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 300.)

 

To succeed on an alter ego theory, there are two general requirements: “(1) that there be such unity of interest and ownership that the separate personalities of the corporation and the individual no longer exist and (2) that, if the acts are treated as those of the corporation alone, an inequitable result will follow.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, Mr. Topolewski argues that he “was not a shareholder of TAI at the time the loans were obtained by TAI from Plaintiff.” (MSJ 4:14–15.) Mr. Topolewski further argues that “there is no evidence to suggest that Mr. Topolewski treated TAI’s assets as his own or added or withdrew capital from TAI’s bank accounts at will.” (Id. at 3:21–23.) “The thousands of bank transactions subpoenaed by Plaintiff confirm that there was no comingling of Mr. Topolewski’s personal funds with TAI’s business accounts such that it would suggest one is the alter ego of the other.” (Id. at 4:8–11.) Finally, Mr. Topolewski asserts that no inequitable result would follow should the Court find no alter ego, because “there is no evidence that Mr. Topolewski received the proceeds from the loans. There is no evidence that Mr. Topolewski took possession of the equipment that was purchased.” (Id. at 5:2–3.)

 

Mr. Topolewski supports the above arguments with the declarations of himself, and two employees of the Company. Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Mr. Topolewski has met his initial burden to establish a defense to Plaintiff’s claims against him. The burden therefore shifts to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether Mr. Topolewski is the alter ego of the Company such that he may be held personally liable for its alleged conduct.

 

 Plaintiff argues in opposition that “because Mr. Topolewski personally controlled an account for a company that was represented by Defendants to Plaintiff to be a third party equipment vendor, [Finning,] Mr. Topolewski personally participated, and benefited from, the fraud described in the Complaint.” (Pl.’s Opp. 1:21–23.) Plaintiff asserts that discovery “revealed that several million dollars were transferred from the account designated as belonging to [Finning] to the accounts belonging to Topolewski America and Mr. Topolewski.” (Id. at 8:2–9:1.) “Among those documents produced in the Second Document Production were documents showing that the ‘Finning International’ account that received the original loan funds from the Plaintiff was opened and controlled by defendant, Gary Topolewski. Thus, instead of the Plaintiff’s loan proceeds having been transmitted to what Plaintiff was led to believe was a third party vendor of the Equipment Collateral, the loan proceeds were, in fact, transmitted to a fabricated account controlled by Gary Topolewski.” (Id. at 9:9–15.)

 

Essentially, Plaintiff argues that its discovery efforts revealed that “in furtherance of the alleged alter-ego scheme, … Mr. Topolewski used the Finning International account to distribute [loan] funds to both Topolewski America and his own personal account.” (Id. at 12:2–8.) Finally, Plaintiff argues that granting Mr. Topolewski summary judgment would lead to an inequitable result because “if Mr. Topolewski were able to direct these loan funds at will, in his personal capacity, to the detriment of the Plaintiff.” (Id. at 12:17–20.)

 

In support of its argument, Plaintiff proffers copies of documents subpoenaed from nonparty U.S. Bank showing various transfers of funds from Finning to the Company, from Finning to Mr. Topolewski, and from the Company to Finning. (Ex. 5 to Decl. of Joshua K. Partington.) Plaintiff also proffers signature cards and account documents for Finning’s U.S. Bank account, which are executed by Mr. Topolewski. (Ex. 6 to Partington Decl.)

 

Mr. Topolewski argues on reply that “Plaintiff points to alleged evidence that Gary Topolewski and Finning International, Inc. may be alter egos, but Finning is not a party to this litigation. Gary Topolewski’s relationship with Finning is irrelevant to whether Topolewski is the alter ego of TAI.” (Def.’s Reply 2:3–5.) The Court disagrees and finds that Mr. Topolewski’s relationship with Finning is relevant to Plaintiff’s alter ego claims, particularly where the equipment collateral at the heart of Plaintiff’s allegations were allegedly purchased from and insured by Finning, and Plaintiff presents evidence of the transfer of loan funds between Mr. Topolewski, Finning, and the Company. (FAC ¶¶ 64, 66.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has met its responsive burden to show that a triable issue exists as to whether Mr. Topolewski is the alter ego of the Company. Accordingly, the motion is denied as to this issue.

 

B.     Conversion; Fraud

 

Mr. Topolewski further asserts that Plaintiff cannot maintain its Conversion and Fraud causes of action against him. “Conversion is generally described as the wrongful exercise of dominion over the personal property of another.” (Fremont Indemnity Co., 148 Cal.App.4th at 119.) “The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) The elements that must be pleaded in a cause of action for fraud are: (1) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of its falsity (or “scienter”); (3) intent to defraud (i.e., to induce reliance); (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. (Philipson & Simon v. Gulsvig (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 347, 363.)

 

Here, Mr. Topolewski argues that he has not acted in his personal capacity giving rise to any liability to Plaintiff for conversion or fraud. “Mr. Topolewski did not commit any wrongful acts as relates to the equipment; nor did he dispose of any property rights held by Plaintiff in the equipment.” (MSJ 8:2–4.) Furthermore, “there were no false representations made by Mr. Topolewski: he did not personally receive money from the loans and he did not personally receive the equipment at issue.” (Id. at 9:7–8.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Mr. Topolewski has satisfied his initial burden to negate essential elements of Plaintiff’s ninth and tenth causes of action. The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that “Defendants led the Plaintiff to believe that Topolewski America was purchasing the Equipment Collateral from a third party vendor, Finning International, who also acted as the insurer of the Equipment Collateral.” (Pl.’s Opp. 13:6–8.) “Because Mr. Topolewski was personally responsible, as the owner and controller of the Finning International account, for the transfers of funds from Finning International to Topolewski America and to himself, personally, there exist triable issues of fact as to the Plaintiff’s fraud and conversion claims against Mr. Topolewski.” (Id. at 13:12–15.) “If, as the evidence from Caterpillar and US Bank suggests, the Equipment Collateral does not exist, then Mr. Topolewski perpetrated a fraud in which he directed the Plaintiff, to transmit loan funds to an entity that Plaintiff believed, based on Mr. Topolewski’s representations, was a third party equipment vendor, for the purported purchase of the Equipment Collateral, which funds were then directed to other bank accounts including, but not limited to those owned by Topolewski America and Mr. Topolewski.” (Id. at 13:18–23.)

 

Despite the Court’s continuance of the instant motion to allow Plaintiff to conduct additional discovery, namely to inspect the Equipment Collateral, Plaintiff was unsuccessful in doing so. (Pl.’s Suppl. Opp. 2:12–14.) Nevertheless, the Court finds that Plaintiff has met its responsive burden to show that a triable issue exists as to whether Mr. Topolewski is either directly liable for conversion and fraud as alleged in the FAC, or indirectly liable under an alter ego theory as detailed above. Accordingly, the motion is denied as to these issues.

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is denied.

 

 


Case Number: 22CHCV00494    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: F51

JUNE 3, 2024

 

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22CHCV00494

 

Motion Filed: 11/21/23

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Michael Brang (“Plaintiff”)¿

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant General Motors, LLC (“Defendant”)¿

NOTICE: OK

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: Terminating sanctions against Defendant, and additional monetary sanctions against Defendant and their counsel in the amount of $1,500.00.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is granted in part. The Court orders Defendant to produce its PMK for an additional deposition on Matters of Examination Nos. 2 and 7–9, and to produce documents responsive to Requests for Production Nos. 1–14 and 18–23, as specified in Plaintiff’s 9/21/22 Deposition Notice, within 30 days. The Court also imposes $1,500.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel, to be paid within 45 days.

 

BACKGROUND 

 

Plaintiff brings this action against Defendant under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civil Code § 1790 et seq.) for a vehicle he purchased on 12/2/20, and for which Defendant issued the manufacturer’s warranty. (Compl. ¶¶ 4, 8.)

 

On 7/6/22, Plaintiff filed his complaint, alleging against Defendant the following causes of action: (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; and (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2(b). On 8/5/22, Defendant filed its answer.

 

On 9/5/23, this Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s Person(s) Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) and Custodian of Records. On 11/21/23, Plaintiff filed the instant motion. On 4/10/24, Defendant filed its opposition thereto. On 5/28/24, Plaintiff filed his reply.

//

//

//

ANALYSIS

 

The Court may issue terminating or monetary sanctions, upon noticed motion, “against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030.) However, “A trial court must be cautious when imposing a terminating sanction because the sanction eliminates a party’s fundamental right to a trial, thus implicating due process rights. … Although in extreme cases a court has the authority to order a terminating sanction as a first measure … a terminating sanction should generally not be imposed until the court has attempted less severe alternatives and found them to be unsuccessful and/or the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective.” (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604 [internal citations omitted].)

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that terminating and monetary sanctions are warranted against Defendant because “GM did not produce, and still has not produced, its PMK witness” despite the Court’s 9/5/23 Order to do so. (Pl.’s Mot. 3:5–6.)

 

A.    Terminating Sanctions

 

Plaintiff contends that Defendant should be sanctioned for violating the Court’s 9/5/23 Order by failing to produce its PMK for deposition on or before 10/5/23. (9/5/23 Min. Order.) However, as Defendant notes in opposition, Defendant has since agreed to produce its PMK witness for deposition on 4/30/24. (Def.’s Opp. 3:7–9.) On reply, Plaintiff confirms that the deposition of Defendant’s PMK took place on 4/30/24, but maintains that “at the deposition GM did not produce a witness for all Matters of Examination as ordered by the Court. … GM did not produce a witness for Matter of Examination Nos. 2, 7, 8, and 9. … Also, … GM did not produce documents responsive to Request for Production Nos. 1-14 or 18 -23.” (Pl.’s Reply 1:27–2:6.)

 

The Court therefore finds that Defendant has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by failing to produce its PMK witness for deposition as ordered by the Court on 9/5/23. However, the Court declines to issue terminating sanctions against Defendant for this misuse of the discovery process, as it finds that Defendant’s actions do not rise to the level of misconduct warranting terminating sanctions. Although Defendant eventually complied with the Court’s Order, its compliance was late and insufficient. The Court’s previous Order stated that Defendant “is ordered to produce its Person(s) Most Knowledgeable and Custodian of Records to be deposed, and to produce the documents specified in the deposition notice, on or before 10/5/23.” (9/5/23 Min. Order, p. 1 [emphasis added].)[1]

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court orders Defendant to produce its PMK for an additional deposition on Matters of Examination Nos. 2 and 7–9, and to produce documents responsive to Requests for Production Nos. 1–14 and 18–23, in full compliance of Plaintiff’s 9/21/22 Deposition Notice, within 30 days. (Ex. B to Decl. of Sepehr Daghighian.)

//

//

B.     Monetary Sanctions

 

“The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030, subd. (a).) Here, Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the total amount of $1,500.00 to be imposed on Defendant for Defendant’s violation of the Court’s 9/5/23 Order. Based the foregoing, the Court finds it reasonable to impose monetary sanctions against Defendant and/or its counsel in the amount of $1,500.00.

 

CONCLUSION 

 

The motion is granted in part. The Court orders Defendant to produce its PMK for an additional deposition on Matters of Examination Nos. 2 and 7–9, and to produce documents responsive to Requests for Production Nos. 1–14 and 18–23, as specified in Plaintiff’s 9/21/22 Deposition Notice, within 30 days. The Court also imposes $1,500.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel, to be paid within 45 days.


[1] The Court notes that the 9/5/23 Minute Order appears to erroneously state that Plaintiff is ordered to produce the witness for deposition. However, the correct party ordered to produce its witness for deposition is Defendant.
