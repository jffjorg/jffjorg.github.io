Case Number: 23STCV14660    Hearing Date: May 20, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Monday, May 20, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   23STCV14660

CASE NAME:                        Patrick Muh-En Lu v. Lootably, Inc.  

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Patrick Muh-En Lu

OPPOSING PARTY:             Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lootably, Inc.

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set.

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer to First Amended Cross-Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        29 April 2024

REPLY:                                  13 May 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu’s demurrer to the FACC is overruled. Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu is ordered to file Answer to the FACC in 10 days.  The court sets a (Non-Appearance) OSC Re: Answer to FACC for June 7, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  Moving party to give notice. 

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

on June 23, 2023, Patrick Muh-En Lu (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Lootably, Inc. (“Defendant” or “Lootably”) and Does 1 to 10. Plaintiff seeks declaratory relief regarding whether his shares in Defendant have vested making him a 25% owner.

 

On August 18, 2023, the Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff and Roes 1 to 50. The Cross-Complaint alleges six causes of action: (1) Recission of Employment Agreement, (2) Recission of Stock Restriction Agreement, (3) Intentional Misrepresentation, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and (6) Breach of Contract.

 

On September 18, 2023, the Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filed a demurrer to the Cross-Complaint. Defendant/Cross-Complainant opposes the demurrer. On February 9, 2024, the court sustained the demurrer to the Cross-complaint with leave to amend and found that Delaware law applied to the Employment Agreement, the Stock Restriction Agreement, as well as any other dispute arising from said agreements. (Order of 02/09/2024.)

 

On February 23, 2024, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lootably filed the First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) alleging the same six causes of action. Cross-Defendant now demurs to the FACC. Defendant Lootably opposes the demurrer. The matter is now before the court.

request for JUDICIAL notice

 

The Court may take judicial notice of records of any court of record of the United States. (Evid. Code, § 452(d)(2).) However, the court may only judicially notice the existence of the record, not that its contents are the truth. (Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1565.) 

 

The court may take judicial notice of records of any court of record of the United States. (Evid. Code, § 452(d)(2).) However, the court may only judicially notice the existence of the record, not that its contents are the truth. (Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1565.) 

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu requests judicial notice of the following:

 

1)     CROSS-COMPLAINT BY LOOTABLY, INC., which was filed on August 18, 2023.

 

2)     NOTICE OF DEMURRER AND DEMURRER TO CROSS- COMPLAINT; MEMORANDUM OF POINTS AND AUTHORITIES; DECLARATION OF STEPHEN S. SMITH, which was filed on September 18, 2023.

 

3)     REPLY MEMORANDUM OF POINTS AND AUTHORITIES IN SUPPORT OF DEMURRER TO CROSS-COMPLAINT, which was filed on December 6, 2023.

 

4)     PLAINTIFF/CROSS-DEFENDANT’S SUPPLEMENTAL BRIEF RE: APPLICATION OF DELAWARE LAW, which was filed on January 24, 2024.

A court may take judicial notice of contents of its own records. (Dwan v. Dixon (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 260, 265; Foster v. Gray (1962) 203 Cal.App.2d 434, 439.) Cross-Defendant Lu’s request for judicial notice is granted.

 

Discussion

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.¿ (CCP § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) In evaluating a demurrer, the court accepts the complainant’s properly pled facts as true and ignores contentions, deductions, and conclusory statements. (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1976) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Moreover, the court does not consider whether a plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations or the possible difficulty in making such proof. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿ The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

II.        Demurrer

 

A.        Failure to Meet and Confer

 

The court notes that Cross-Defendant Lu has failed to comply with the mandatory requirements of CCP § 430.41 which requires the parties to meet and confer before filing a demurrer.  Cross-Defendant Lu’s motion is void of any declaration reflecting Cross-Defendant’s efforts to meet and confer prior to filing this Motion. “Any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

However, “[i]f upon review of a declaration under 430.41(a)(3), a court learns no meet and confer has taken place, or concludes further conferences between counsel would likely be productive, it retains the discretion to order counsel to meaningfully discuss the pleadings with an eye toward reducing the number of issues or eliminating the need for a demurrer, and to continue the hearing date to facilitate that effort.” (Dumas v. Los Angeles County Bd. of Supervisors (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 348, 356, fn. 3; see also Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 967– 968 [courts have inherent authority to regulate proceedings in ways consistent with statutes]; CCP § 430.42[“Nothing in this section [§ 430.41] prohibits the court from ordering a conference on its own motion at any time or prevents a party from requesting that the court order a conference to be held.”] [italics added].) 

 

While the court retains the discretion to continue the hearing and order the parties to comply with the meet and confer requirement, the court will proceed to address the Motion on the merits.

 

B.        Summary of Allegations in FACC

 

In July 2022, the Chief Executive Officer of Lootably, Ethan Geczi (“Geczi”) became interested in purchasing the domain “Playfull.com” from its owner, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Patrick Muh-En Lu (“Lu”). (FACC ¶ 7.) When it became clear Plaintiff Lu was not selling the domain name, Geczi entered into discussion with Lu about Lu joining Lootably “based on Lu’s express representation that he had many relationships with individuals who, within their respective companies, had the authority to enter into business agreements on behalf of their respective companies, (hereinafter Lu’s relationship with these individuals are referred to as "decision-maker relationships”) and with whom he, as part of Lootably, intended to substantially grow LOOTABLY’S business.” (FACC ¶ 7.)

 

Between the end of 2022 and the first quarter of 2023, discussions between Lu, Geczi and Joshua Verdehem (“Verdehem”) continued wherein Lu represented that his “decision-maker relationship” would increase Lootably’s business.  Based on these representations, on April 21, 2023, Lootably and Lu entered into a written employment agreement (the “Employment Agreement”) wherein Lu would serve as Lootably’s President and Chief Operating Officer. (FACC ¶ 11.)

Under the Employment Agreement, Lou was granted a restricted stock grant (the “Stock Grant” of One Million shares (1,000,000 shares) of the Company’s Common Stock (the ‘Shares”) at a price equal to the fair market value of Lootably’s common stock as determined by the Board on the date of the grant, pursuant to the terms of a written stock restriction agreement (“Equity Agreement”). (FACC ¶ 12, Ex. A, B.)

 

The FACC alleges that it later became clear to Geczi and Verdehem “that Lu's pre-employment representations about his having "decision-maker relationships" and business relationships with persons who had the authority to enter into business agreements with LOOTABLY, and which he intended to substantially increase in LOOTABLY' s business based on those relationships were false.” (FACC ¶ 16.) “Lu had no such "decision-maker relationships" or business relationships with large companies.” (FACC ¶ 16.)

 

Moreover, the FACC alleges that Lu breached his fiduciary duty owed to Lootably and its stockholders by misappropriating assets and using corporate funds for self-entertainment and self-promotion instead of Lootably’s business development or for a legitimate business pursuit. (FACC ¶ 16.) 

 

Lu also breached the Employment Agreement “by failing to devote his full business time, attention, and best efforts in performing his duties and furthering LOOTABLY's business interests.” (FACC ¶ 17.) During Lu’s employment, Lootably only generated $500.00 in revenue. (FACC ¶16.) On June 12, 2023, Lootably terminated Lu’s employment and he was removed as the Director, President, and Chief Operating Officer of Lootably. (FACC ¶ 17.)

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu now demurs to first to fifth causes of action alleged in the FACC.

 

C.        First and Second Causes of Action – Recission of Employment Agreement and Recission of Stock Restriction Agreement

 

“The remedy of rescission extinguishes the contract (Civ. Code, § 1688) and restores the parties to their former positions by requiring them to return whatever consideration they have received.” (Koenig v. Warner Unified School Dist. (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 43, 59–60.) “A rescission is enforced by a civil action for relief based on rescission (Civ. Code, § 1692) or by asserting rescission as a defense.” (Southern Ins. Co. v. Workers' Comp. Appeals Bd. (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 961, 963–964.) “When the right to rescind exists section 1691 of the Civil Code prescribes the method of procedure which must be followed.” (Neet v Holmes (1944) 25 Cal.2d 447, 457.)

 

(b) A party to a contract may rescind the contract in the following cases:

 

(1)   If the consent of the party rescinding, or of any party jointly contracting with him, was given by mistake, or obtained through duress, menace, fraud, or undue influence, exercised by or with the connivance of the party as to whom he rescinds, or of any other party to the contract jointly interested with such party.

 

(Civ. Code, § 1689(b).)

The first and second causes of action are premised on the fact that “Lu made numerous and repeatedly false representations” about having “decision-maker relationships,” that these representations were false at the time they were made, and that Lu had no reasonable grounds to believe them to be true. (FACC ¶¶ 20, 29.) Lootably seeks to rescind the Employment Agreement and the Stock Agreement on the grounds that Lootably’s consent to enter into said agreement was “obtained by fraudulent misrepresentation of Lu”  because “[n]one of those introductions were with individuals with the authority to enter into business agreements on behalf of their respective companies and/or with individuals or large companies with whom Lu had any business relationship with.”(FACC ¶¶ 22, 25, 30, Ex. A, B, C.)

 

Accordingly, Lootably’s right to request the remedy of rescission is premised on properly pleading a cause of action for Intentional Misrepresentation and/or Negligent Misrepresentation. For the reasons set forth below, Lootably’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action are overruled as the claims for Intentional Misrepresentation and Negligent Misrepresentation are adequately pled.

 

            D.        Third and Fourth Causes of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation and Negligent Misrepresentation

 

“The essential elements of a count for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance,  (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230–231.) “The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true.” (Id. at p. 231.) “Each element of a fraud count must be pleaded with particularity so as to apprise the defendant of the specific grounds for the charge and enable the court to determine whether there is any basis for the cause of action, although less specificity is required if the defendant would likely have greater knowledge of the facts than the plaintiff.” (Id. at p. 231.)

 

Lootably’s third and fourth causes of action are premised on the same operative facts: “Lu made false representations” about having “decision-maker relationships" and “business relationships” and that when Lootably hired Lu, “he intended to use those ‘decision-maker relationships’ and business relationships with large companies that would result in a substantial increase in LOOTABLY's business.” These representations were false because “Lu had no such ‘decision-maker relationships’ and/or business relationships with large companies at the time he made such representations.” (FACC ¶¶ 39, 46.)

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu demurs to the first, second, third, and fourth causes on the ground that under Delaware law, these causes of action fail because the alleged misrepresentations are not actionable. “But, as the Delaware cases (and this Court) have recognized, such predictions of a vague, hoped-for economic success are not actionable as a matter of law. Trenwick Am. Litig. Trust v. Ernst & Young, L.L.P., (Del Ch. 2006) 906 A.2d 168, 209; Great Lakes Chem. Corp. v. Pharmacia Corp., (Del. Ch. 2001) 788 A.2d 544, 554.)” (Demurrer at p. 1:20-24.)

 

Plaintiff Lu is mistaken, however, in assuming that Delaware law governs all of Lootably’s claims. This court ruled that Delaware Law applied to the Employment Agreement and the Stock Restriction Agreement. (Order of 02/09/2024.)

Paragraph 8 of the Employment Agreement states:

 

This Offer Letter shall be governed by the laws of the State of Delaware without regard to conflict-of-law principles.

 

(FACC Ex. A at p. 4, ¶ 8.)

 

Paragraph 12(e) of the Stock Restriction Agreement states:

 

Governing Law. This agreement and actions taken thereunder shall be governed by and construed in accordance with, the laws of the State of Delaware, applied without regard to conflict of law principles.

 

(FACC Ex. B at p. 8, ¶ 12(e) [underline original].)

 

Accordingly, Delaware law applies to any dispute regarding the interpretation of the Employment Agreement and the Stock Rescission Agreement. Here, the misrepresentations occurred before the parties entered into the Agreements and the FACC does not concern the interpretation or content of the Agreements. Therefore, California procedural and substantive law governs the third and fourth causes of action.

 

“The law is well established that actionable misrepresentations must pertain to past or existing material facts. [Citation.] Statements or predictions regarding future events are deemed to be mere opinions which are not actionable.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 146.) The representation by Lu that he would use his “relationship” to grow Lootably’s business is not actionable because such a statement goes to a future event. But the fact that “Lu had no such ‘decision-maker relationships’” is a statement of fact asserting that Lu did not have any contact with persons  who “had the authority to enter into business agreements on behalf of their respective companies” despite representing to Lootably that he in fact did have such present existing relationships. (FACC ¶¶ 7, 8, 39, 46.)

 

The FACC alleges that Plaintiff Lu introduced Lootably to “several companies, Gen.G Esports, TSM, Sky Dance,” reflecting that Plaintiff did have “relationships.” None of the introductions were with a “decision maker” for their company because Lu did not, in fact, have such any relationship. (FACC ¶¶ 8, 9. 10.) This is a representation of a past or existing fact, sufficient to constitute a misrepresentation. “A demurrer does not lie to a portion of a cause of action.” (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682.) Therefore, the fact that Lu failed to grow Lootably’s business is not an actionable representation because it is a representation about a future event.  The allegation that Lootably had no relationship with “decision-makers” is, however, sufficient to sustain the demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action.

 

Therefore, the demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action are overruled.

 

            E.        Fifth Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

"The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Meister v. Mensinger (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 381, 395.) A fiduciary relationship is “any relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.” (Cleveland v. Johnson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1338.) “Such a relation ordinarily arises where a confidence is reposed by one person in the integrity of another.” (Id.) “[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.” (Id.)   

 

The FACC alleges that Lu as the Director, President, and Chief Operating Officer of Lootably, Inc. owed a fiduciary duty to Lootably that precluded Lu from using Lootably’s money “for purposes of self-entertainment and self-promotion wholly unrelated to LOOTABLY' s business interests.” (FACC ¶¶ 51, 52.) “Lu did not exercise the care required of directors and officers and breached the fiduciary duties he owed to LOOTABLY by” “incurring extensive expenses for self-entertainment and self-promotion unrelated to LOOTABLY' s business or business development.” (FACC  53.)

 

Plaintiff Lu demurs to the fifth cause of action on the ground that under Delaware law, a plaintiff may not allege a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty based on obligations expressly addressed by contract. (Nemec v. Shrader, (Del. Ch. 2010) 991 A.2d 1120, 1129.)

 

As stated above, the fifth cause of action does not concern the interpretation of the Employment Agreement or Stock Recission Agreement, or any action taken pursuant to the Stock Recission Agreement. (FACC Ex. A at p. 4, ¶ 8; FACC Ex. B at p. 8, ¶ 12(e).) Paragraph 8 of the Employment Agreement states that the Employment Agreement is “governed by the laws of the State of Delaware” but contains no provision extending the Delaware choice of law provision to claims arising from the Employment Contract. (FACC Ex. A at p. 4, ¶ 8.) Therefore, the court is not persuaded that Delaware law applies to the breach of fiduciary claim in the FACC.

 

Whether Plaintiff Lu breached his fiduciary duties under Corp. Code § 309(a) is a question of fact that should be decided under California law rather than Delaware law. (FACC ¶ 52.) Moreover, Cross-Complainant Lootably fails to cite case law to support the finding that the obligations outlined in Corp. Code § 309(a) arise out of the Employment Agreement or are subsumed under the duties outlined in the Employment Agreement.

 

 As Plaintiff Lu fails to show that the breach of fiduciary duty cause of action is improperly pled or barred under California law, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is overruled.

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu’s demurrer to the FACC is overruled. Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Lu

Background

 

On September 19, 2023, DSS Risk Consultants, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against ArentFox Schiff LLP (“ArentFox”); DSS Equity Holdings, Inc. (“DSS Equity”);  DB Nevada Trust (“DBNT”); Dorna Brown, Trent Brown, and Does 1 to 50.  This action arises out of a dispute regarding escrow funds alleged to have wrongfully been released by the escrow agent, ArentFox, to Dorna Brown and her affiliates, DSS Equity and DBNT in violation of the Membership Interest Purchase Agreement (“MIPA”) and the Escrow Agreement.

 

The operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges six causes of action: (1) conversion, (2) breach of contract, (3) breach of fiduciary duty, (4) violation of Fin. Code § 17414, (5) unjust enrichment, and (6) violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq.  

 

On March 7, 2024, Defendant DBNT moved to quash the summons on the basis that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over DBNT. Plaintiff opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

motion to quash service of summons

 

I.         Legal Standard

CCP § 418.10(a) states: “A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve or file a motion…[t]o quash service of summon on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿ 

When a defendant moves to quash service of process on jurisdictional grounds, the plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts justifying the exercise of jurisdiction.¿(State of Oregon v. Superior Court (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1550, 1557.)¿If the plaintiff meets his or her burden, the burden shifts to the defendant to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable. (Buchanan v. Soto (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 1353, 1362. [“When a motion to quash is properly brought, the burden of proof is placed upon the plaintiff to establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence.”].) This burden must be met by competent evidence in affidavits and authenticated documentary evidence. (Jewish Defense Org. v. Superior Court¿(1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 1045, 1055.) “A court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant only if: (1) the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits; (2) the controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant’s contacts with the forum; and (3) the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice.” (Snowey v. Harrah’s Entertainment, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1054, 1062.)¿¿

III.      Discussion

 

A.        Factual Summary

In 2021, Paul Phillips through his company DSS Risk Consultants, LLC (“Plaintiff”) sought to buy the risk-management company, Development Solutions and Services LLC from Dorna Brown (“Ms. Brown”) and the entities DDS Equity and DBNT (the “Sellers”). (FAC ¶ 5.)

Ira Deitsch, from the law firm ArentFox Schiff LLP, represented sellers while Plaintiff, as the buyer, did not have representation during the sale. (FACC ¶ 6.) Dorna Brown’s son, Trent Brown (“Mr. Brown”), helped negotiate the sale. (FAC ¶ 7, Ex. A, pp. 12-13, § 3(h), (“Post Closing Purchase Price Adjustment”).) ArentFox also served as the escrow agent. (FAC ¶ 10.)

 

The parties signed the Membership Interest Purchase Agreement, which incorporated by reference to the Escrow Agreement, the latter which provided that the $1,005,000.00 placed in escrow by Plaintiff would only be disbursed either by court order or by joint payment instruction signed by both Plaintiff and Ms. Brown or her attorneys. (FAC ¶¶ 9, 21, 22, Ex. A; Ex. B § 4(a)-(b); Ex. C.)

 

In November 2022, Mr. Brown instructed ArentFox to disburse the escrow funds to sellers and ArentFox did so, which Plaintiff alleges was a violation of the Escrow Agreement. (FAC ¶¶ 14, 24, Ex. D at p. 4 [Nov. 3, 2022, email in which Trent Brown wrote to the sellers’ atorney: “Ira — I will send over the allocation instructions to show how to disburse the funds between [a third party and] Dorna’s Trust.”]

 

In April 2023, Plaintiff learned that the escrow funds had already been disbursed, in violation of the Escrow Agreement. (FAC Ex. B.) Plaintiff agreed to place $1,169,950 in escrow until the one-year valuation was complete, allowing the final sale price to be calculated. (FAC ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant DBNT now moves to quash the service of summons on the basis that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over DBNT.

 

B.        Plaintiffs’ Burden to Prove Personal Jurisdiction 

 

In opposition to a motion to quash based upon lack of personal jurisdiction, complainants have the initial burden of filing evidence to show minimum contacts. (See Muckle v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 218, 228.) “It is plaintiff's burden to prove facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of evidence.” (Cornelison v. Chaney (1976) 16 Cal.3d 143, 155.) “This burden must be met by competent evidence in affidavits and authenticated documentary evidence. An unverified complaint may not be considered as an affidavit supplying necessary facts.” (Ziller Electronics Lab GmbH v. Superior Court¿(1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1233.)¿ 

 

Plaintiff does not argue that the court has general jurisdiction over DBNT, but instead argues that the court has specific jurisdiction over DBNT as the dispute concerns the wrongful disbursement of the escrow funds. “A nonresident defendant is subject to specific personal jurisdiction only if (1) the defendant purposefully availed itself of the benefits of conducting activities in the forum state; (2) the controversy arises out of or is related to the defendant's forum contacts; and (3) the exercise of jurisdiction would be fair and reasonable.” (HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1167.)¿¿ 

 

The parties do not dispute that the MIPA was signed by the Plaintiff and the following parties as follows:

 

DSS EQUITY HOLDINGS, INC., a California corporation (“Seller”), (c) DSS RISK CONSULTANTS, LLC, a Nevada limited liability company (“Buyer”), and (d) DB NEVADA TRUST [(“DBNT”)], a Nevada trust of which Dorna Brown is the grantor and Preservation Trust Company is the sole trustee, and CHARLES W. BATES (together, the “Shareholders” and, individually, a “Shareholder”)

 

(FACC Ex. A; Phillips Decl. Ex. A.)

 

The MIPA references the Escrow Agreement as follows:

 

“Escrow Agent” means Arent Fox LLP.

 

“Escrow Agreement” means the escrow agreement by and among the Escrow Agent, Buyer and Seller. “Escrow Amount” means a cash amount equal to thirty percent (30%) of what would be the Closing Purchase Price if there were no Escrow Amount, which shall be available to secure Buyer’s rights under Section 10.

 

“Escrow Fund” means the escrow fund established by the Escrow Agent pursuant to the Escrow Agreement, which shall include the Escrow Amount and any interest and earnings thereon, as the same may be disbursed from time to time in accordance with this Agreement and the Escrow Agreement.

 

(FACC, Ex. A at p. 4 [bold original]; Phillips Decl. Ex. A at p. 4 [bold original].) Accordingly, only Plaintiff (“buyer”) and DSS Equity (“seller”) were parties to the Escrow Agreement. (FACC Ex. B; Phillips Decl. Ex. B.)

 

While DBNT is not a party to the Escrow Agreement, Plaintiff’s evidence establishes that Ms. Brown is the grantor of the DBNT, and Preservation Trust Company (“Preservation Trust”) is the sole trustee of DBNT. This information is confirmed by DBNT. (Schoenblum Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)

 

DBNT was the shareholder of the business that was sold, Development Solutions and Services, (Philips Decl. Ex. A at p. p. 45 (Schedule 1) [listing the DB Nevada Trust as the owner of 40,000 of the sold business’s 41,667 shares or about 96%]).) This fact is confirmed by DBNT. (Scholenblum Decl. ¶ 2.) Charles W. Bates was the other shareholder of Development Solutions and Services. (Philips Decl. Ex. A.)

 

Plaintiff also presents evidence that Mr. Brown intended the escrow funds to be disbursed “between Charlie and Dorna’s Trust.” (Phillips Decl. Ex. C.) Plaintiff presents evidence that ArentFox admitted that on November 4, 2022, it disbursed the escrow fund “in the amount of $22,836.34 to Charles W. Bates” and “ $982,153.66 to Preservation Trust Company.” (RJN Ex. B, SROG No. 6.) ArentFox’s discovery responses are verified by counsel for ArentFox. (CCP § § 2030.250.) Defendant DBNT fails to rebut the representations by ArentFox that Preservation Trust received a portion of the escrow funds at issue in this case. DBNT’s trustee, Jeffrey Schoenblum (Schoenblum), asserts that it did not “disburse any related funds contained in an escrow account” but fails to assert that it never received or held in trust the escrow funds. (Schoenblum Decl. ¶ 4.)

 

While DBNT was not an agent to the Escrow Agreement, the Escrow Agreement provides that in case of a wire transfer, the escrow funds were to be delivered as follows:

 

Seller Bank Name:                             Bank of America N.A.

Bank Address:                                    7315 Durango Drive

Las Vegas, NV 89113

Domestic Routing #: 026009593

Account #: 501026116493

 

Name and Address of Recipient:        Preservation Trust Company, Inc.

3753 Howard Hughes Parkway Las Vegas, NV 89169

F/C: DB Nevada Trust

 

(FACC Ex. B at p. 2.)

 

The facts above support the finding that DBNT received a portion of the escrow funds at issue, in violation of the terms set out in the Escrow Agreement. DBNT fails to rebut this fact. The evidence reflects that DBNT’s role was not limited to being a selling shareholder of Development Solutions and Services, but also the entity that the sellers intended to be the recipient of the escrow funds at issue.

 

For specific jurisdiction, “courts consider the relationship among the defendant, the forum and the litigation.” (Snowey v. Harrah’s Entertainment, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1054, 1062.) A court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant only if: “(1) the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits; (2) the controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant’s contacts with the forum, and (3) the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice.” (Ibid.)¿¿¿ 

 

Here, the evidence supports the findings that the buyer and sellers entered into a transaction for the purposes of selling Development Solutions and Service, a California corporation whose majority shareholder was DBNT. (Philips Decl. Ex. A at p. p. 45 (Schedule 1).) The buyer and seller signed the MIPA and agreed that California law would govern said agreement.  (Phillips Decl. Ex. A.) The MIPA and Escrow Agreement were negotiated “mainly” in California with Los Angeles listed as the place of closing. (Phillips Decl. ¶ 9, Ex. A.)

 

The MIPA created ongoing obligations among the parties that were to take place in California. These included calculating and apportioning the sold business's adjusted value to determine any overpayment; challenging any valuation; raising objections to certain representations; making final payments; and giving instructions to the escrow agent.

 

(Phillips Decl. ¶ 11.) Moreover, the sellers agreed that DBNT would be the entity that would receive the escrow funds on the seller’s behalf. (Phillips Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. B, C.) Moreover, according to Plaintiff, DBNT has refused to return the escrow funds. (Phillips Decl. ¶ 17.) Therefore, the court finds that based on the facts above, DBNT purposefully availed itself of the benefits of conducting business in California when its agents negotiated for the sale of its business and agreed that deposit of the escrow funds. (See Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446; Rivelli v. Hemm (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 380, 399.)

 

C.        Defendants’ Burden to Show that Exercising Personal Jurisdiction Over Defendants Would Offend Notions of Fair Play and Substantial Justice  

 

Once facts showing minimum contacts with the forum state are established, the burden is on Defendants to demonstrate that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable.¿ (See Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 476-477; Buchanan, supra, 241 Ca.App.4th at p. 1362.) “Courts may evaluate the burden on the defendant of appearing in the forum, the forum state's interest in adjudicating the claim, the plaintiff's interest in convenient and effective relief within the forum, judicial economy, and ‘the shared interest of the several States in furthering fundamental substantive social policies.’ ” (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 446 quoting Burger King Corp., supra, 417 U.S. at p. 476.) “An otherwise valid exercise of personal jurisdiction ‘is presumed to be reasonable.’¿[Citation.] Therefore, defendant ‘must present a compelling case that the presence of some other considerations would render jurisdiction unreasonable. Most such considerations usually may be accommodated through means short of finding jurisdiction unconstitutional.’” (Integral Development Corp., supra, 99 Cal.App.4th at 591 quoting Burger King Corp., supra, 417 U.S. at 477.)¿ 

 

Defendant DBNT fails to show that it would be unreasonable for California to exercise jurisdiction over DBNT due to DBNT’s retention of the escrow funds at issue in this action. DBNT fails to show that when Preservation Trust received the escrow funds from ArentFox, it did so for its benefit or that of another entity rather than as a trustee acting for the exclusive benefit of DBNT.

 

For this reason, the court finds that exercising jurisdiction over DBNT would not offend traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice. 

 

As to the other arguments raised by Plaintiff, the court finds they are not dispositive on the issue of jurisdiction. Plaintiff fails to show or cite any legal authority to support the conclusion that when counsel for DBNT accepted service for DBNT, DBNT waived the right to challenge personal jurisdiction. (Boock Supp. Decl. ¶ 7, Ex. 2.) Moreover, while DBNT’s motion is late, the language of CCP § 418.10 is not mandatory and nothing in section 418.10 suggests that DBNT waiver the right to challenge personal jurisdiction by failing to timely file a motion to quash. Section 418.10 also has a “good cause” provision that allows the court discretion to consider untimely motions to quash. (See Air Machine Com SRL v. Superior Court (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 414, 421 [Appellate court exercised its discretion to consider a motion to quash matter service of summons raised when raised for the first time on appeal where application of statute at issue involved a question of law only, and the facts at issue were not in dispute].) Here, the Supplemental Declaration of Todd A. Boock explains why DBNT delayed in bringing this Motion. (Boock Supp. Decl. ¶¶ 6- 10, Ex. 2.)

 

Lastly, the court did not consider if DBNT’s “appearance” in the Ventura action, Brown v. Arent Fox Schiff LLP, Case No. 2023CUPN011223, in the Superior Court of California, County of Ventura, barred DBNT’s right to challenge jurisdiction in this action as counsel for DBNT present evidence that DBNT’s inclusion in that suit was without its consent and DBNT has been dismissed as a party. (Boock Decl. ¶¶ 2-5, 11, Ex. 1; Schoenblum Supp. Decl. ¶¶ 2-4.)

 

Based on the foregoing, DBNT’s Motion to Quash based on lack of personal jurisdiction is denied.  

 

Conclusion

 

DBNT’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons is denied.
