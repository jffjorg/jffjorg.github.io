     CASE NO.: 20STCV36117

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT RODOLFO MARTINEZ’S ATTORNEY’S MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

 

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This a personal injury action arising out of injuries Plaintiff Arsho Haroutanian (“Plaintiff”) suffered when he was getting out of a passenger van allegedly owned by Defendants Access Services (“Access”) and San Gabriel Transit, Inc. (“San Gabriel”), and operated by Rodolfo Martinez (“Martinez”) (collectively, “Defendants”). (See First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶ 9.)

          Martinez’s counsel Kenton Moore (“Moore”) now seeks to be relieved as counsel of record. 

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On September 22, 2020, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) General Negligence; and (2) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention.

On March 4, 2021, Plaintiff filed his FAC, alleging (1) General Negligence; (2) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention; and (3) Violation of Civil Unruh Civil Rights Act.

On August 12, 2021, Access and San Gabriel filed their Answer to the FAC.

On November 17, 2023, Martinez filed his Answer.

          On July 12, 2024, Moore filed the instant Motion to be Relieved as Martinez’s Counsel.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 284(1) [1] allows for a change or substitution of attorney “[u]pon the consent of both client and attorney, filed with the clerk, or entered upon the minutes.” If both parties do not consent to a substitution of attorney, Code of Civil Procedure § 284(2) allows for a substitution “[u]pon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”

Under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, an attorney seeking to withdraw by motion rather than by consent of the client is required to make that motion using approved Judicial Council forms. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(a-e).) The motion requires a declaration stating “in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1).” (Id. at (c).) A proposed order prepared on form MC-053 must also be lodged with the court with the moving papers. (Id. at (e).)

The motion, notice of motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and all other parties who have appeared in the case. (Id. at (d).) If the notice is served by mail or e-mail, the notice must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts that show that either the service address is current or that “[t]he service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.” (Id.) 

IV.     ANALYSIS

A.             Moore’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is Granted.

          Moore moves to be relieved as counsel of record for Martinez because “[t]here has been a breakdown in communication.” (See Declaration of Kenton Moore filed July 12, 2024 (“Moore Decl.”), ¶ 2.)

          Moore has submitted evidence suggesting he is entitled to an order relieving him as Martinez’s counsel. Moore submitted a declaration attesting to the complete breakdown in communication, served the notice by mail and stated the mailing address is the last known address of Martinez, and filed a proposed order on form MC-053. (See Moore Decl.) Martinez hasn’t filed a written opposition.  

V.       CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court GRANTS Moore’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a Song-Beverly Act case between Plaintiff Kenya White (“Plaintiff”) and FCA US LLC (“Defendant”). 

Plaintiff seeks to compel Defendant’s responses to her request for production of documents (“RFPs”), her form and special interrogatories (“FROGS” and “SROGS”, respectively), and also requests an order deeming the truth of the matters admitted in her requests for admissions (“RFAs”).

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; (2) Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; (3) Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; (4) Breach of Express Written Warranty Civil Code Section 1791.2 Subdivision (a); Section 1794; and (5) Breach of The Implied Warranty of Merchantability Civil Code Section 1791.1, Section 1794.

          On March 18, 2024, Defendant filed its Answer.

          On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Motion to Compel Defendant’s Responses to her RFPs (“Motion RFPs”).

          On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Motion to Compel Defendant’s Responses to her FROGS (“Motion FROGS”).

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Motion to Compel Defendant’s Responses to her SROGS (“Motion SROGS”).

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Motion for an Order Deeming the Truth of the Matters in Plaintiff’s RFAs Admitted (“Motion RFAs”).  

          On September 9, 2024, Defendant filed its Opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion FROGS, Motion SROGS, and Motion RFAs. Defendant did not file an opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion RFPs.

          On September 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Replies.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Requests for Production

 A party upon whom a demand for the production of documents has been made has 30 days after service of the demand to respond. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)[1] The propounding and responding parties may agree to extend the time for response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.270(a).) When a party fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party’s remedy is to seek a motion to compel response. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(b).) A responding party that does not provide responses waives the right to object to the demand, including objections based on privilege or work product. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).) 

B.             Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)    

C.             Requests for Admission

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.010.) “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared…” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.250(a).)

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)    

IV.          ANALYSIS

Four motions are presently before the Court. The ruling will address Plaintiff’s Motion RFPs, then its Motion FROGS, then its Motion SROGS, and, finally, its Motion RFAs.

A.             Plaintiff’s Motion RFPs is Granted.

Plaintiff’s counsel declares Plaintiff served her RFPs, FROGS, SROGS, and RFAs on Defendant on May 6, 2024, and that “Defendant has not served any responses or documents as of July 12, 2024.” (Motion RFPs Declaration of Ahmed Yousef filed July 12, 2024, ¶¶ 5-6.)

Defendant did not file an opposition.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion RFPs and orders Defendant to serve Plaintiff with code compliant responses without objection on or before October 25, 2024.

B.             Plaintiff’s Motion FROGS is Granted.

Plaintiff’s counsel declares Plaintiff served her RFPs, FROGS, SROGS, and RFAs on Defendant on May 6, 2024, and that “Defendant has not served any responses or documents as of July 12, 2024.” (Motion FROGS Declaration of Ahmed Yousef filed July 12, 2024, ¶¶ 5-6.)

In opposition, Defendant states “[i]t is factual that FCA US was belated in serving its responses but contends that Plaintiff has suffered no prejudice. The oversight was due to FCA US counsel’s mistake, inadvertence and excusable neglect and FCA US is entitled to relief.” (Opposition to Motion FROGS, pg. 2.)   

While the Court acknowledges Defendant provided responses after Plaintiff’s Motion FROGS, the Court will analyze the Motion FROGS as of the date of its filing on July 12, 2024.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion FROGS and orders Defendant to serve Plaintiff with code compliant responses without objection on or before October 25, 2024.

C.             Plaintiff’s Motion SROGS is Granted.

Plaintiff’s counsel declares Plaintiff served her RFPs, FROGS, SROGS, and RFAs on Defendant on May 6, 2023, and that “Defendant has not served any responses or documents as of July 12, 2024.” (Motion SROGS Declaration of Ahmed Yousef filed July 12, 2024, ¶¶ 5-6.)

In opposition, Defendant states “[i]t is factual that FCA US was belated in serving its responses but contends that Plaintiff has suffered no prejudice. The oversight was due to FCA US counsel’s mistake, inadvertence and excusable neglect and FCA US is entitled to relief.” (Opposition to Motion SROGS, pg. 2.)   

While the Court acknowledges Defendant provided responses after Plaintiff’s Motion SROGS, the Court will analyze the Motion SROGS as of the date of its filing on July 12, 2024.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion SROGS and orders Defendant to serve Plaintiff with code compliant responses without objection on or before October 25, 2024.

D.            Plaintiff’s Motion RFAs is Granted.

Plaintiff’s counsel declares Plaintiff served her RFPs, FROGS, SROGS, and RFAs on Defendant on May 6, 2023, and that “Defendant has not served any responses or documents as of July 12, 2024.” (Motion RFAs Declaration of Ahmed Yousef filed July 12, 2024, ¶¶ 5-6.)

In opposition, Defendant states “[i]t is factual that FCA US was belated in serving its responses but contends that Plaintiff has suffered no prejudice. The oversight was due to FCA US counsel’s mistake, inadvertence and excusable neglect and FCA US is entitled to relief.” (Opposition to Motion RFAs, pg. 2.)

Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b) & (c) provide that where a responding party has failed to serve a timely response, the Court “shall” order “the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted” “unless it finds [the responding party] has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” Here, other than Defendant’s conclusory, boilerplate argument quoted above, Defendant has not provided any evidence from which this Court might conclude its purported untimely responses are in substantial compliance with section 2033.220.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion RFAs and deems admitted the truth of the matters specified in her RFAs.

V.             SANCTIONS

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction¿. . .¿against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel¿. . .” responses to interrogatories or production demands, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(c).) The court shall also impose a “monetary sanction…on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).) Similarly,¿“[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying¿a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)

For her Motion RFPs, Motion FROGS, and Motion SROGS, Plaintiff seeks “an order directing Defendant to pay Plaintiff $500 for each day following the 10-day compliance period that Defendant does not fully comply with the Court's order.” (E.g., Motion RFPs, pg. 8.) Plaintiff does not further specify (either in any other motion, declaration, or in her Replies) what sanctions she would like awarded or the legal authority for the accrual of sanctions in the manner she has requested.

The Court will not order Defendant to prospectively pay sanctions after it issues the orders contained in this ruling, especially when it appears, if Defendant’s Oppositions are to be believed – notwithstanding Defendant’s failure to provide the Court with proof in the form of a supporting declaration and relevant exhibits – Defendant has served responses (albeit late responses). Without any other sanctions request, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions.

VI.          CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Defendant’s Responses to her RFPs, her FROGS, and her SROGS.

The Court also GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Deeming the Truth of the Matters Admitted in her RFAs.

Defendant is ordered to serve Plaintiff with code compliant responses without objections on or before October 25, 2024.  

IT IS SO ORDERED.
