DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21NWCV00400    Hearing Date: January 26, 2024    Dept: C

The Windmill Co. Inc. vs Fayek Sedrak, et al.

Case No.: 21NWCV00400

Hearing Date: January 26, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

#1

Tentative Ruling

I.                Defendant Fayez Sedrak’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

II.              Defendant O&C Hillside Resources Management Company’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

Background

On June 21, 2021, Plaintiff, The Windmill Co., Inc., (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Fayez Sedrak (“Sedrak”) and O&C Hillside Resources Management Co. (“O&C Hillside”), alleging Breach of Contract, Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, and Declaratory Relief. In a First Amended Complaint filed on February 6, 2023 (“FAC”) Plaintiff alleged that on January 3, 2020, Plaintiff entered into a Sale of Business agreement with O&C Hillside to purchase the business known as ARCO AM PM 6545 located at 13550 Beach Blvd, La Mirada, CA, for $1,590,000.00. The parties opened an Escrow at Ace Escrow where they each signed mutual escrow instructions and Plaintiff deposited $30,000 into Escrow. (Id., ¶¶ 8-9; Benge Decl., Exhibit 1.) Escrow was to close in 90 days. (Benge Decl., Exhibit 2.)

The original escrow for the purchase of the Arco AM/PM was signed on January 3, 2020. The original purchase price was $1,590,000.00. (Benge Decl. ¶¶ 3-4; Exhibits 1-2.) However, during the first escrow period several problems arose, including the necessity by Tesoro (the franchisor) to elect not to exercise their right of first refusal, the Landlord’s unwillingness to sign the assignment and assumption of ground lease, and problems with a Hydrogen facility. (Sedrak Decl. ¶¶ 9, 14, 24; Benge Decl. ¶¶ 10, 17.) During this period, Plaintiff obtained a loan approval note dated October 1, 2020, for $1,110,000.00. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 9.)

For those reasons an amended escrow agreement was signed on January 21, 2021.  Those instructions stated that escrow was to close within ninety days. (Benge Decl., Exhibit 3.) The amended agreement called for a promissory note for $150,000.00 with an interest rate of 0% per annum for eight years. In addition, the Escrow Amendment changed the purchase price to $1,940,000.00. (Benge Decl., Exhibit 3.)

During this time, Plaintiff did not deposit funds into escrow as required by the amended escrow agreement or obtain a loan. However, on March 22, 2021, Defendant Sedrak emailed an Ace Escrow account for an agent named “Debbie” to cancel escrow. (Mashney Decl., Ex. 16.) This cancellation occurred 30 days prior to the close of 90 day window.

On May 25, 2021, Plaintiff sent a demand for specific performance as to all Defendants, which went unanswered. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 17.)

On June 21, 2021, Plaintiff, the Windmill Co., Inc., filed an action against Fayez Sedrak and O&C Hillside Resources Management Co., alleging Breach of Contract, Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, and Declaratory Relief. (See Complaint.)

On August 1, 2021, Defendants emailed a Notice of Default and Second Demand and Notice to Perform Escrow, which gave only five days to deposit the funds. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 18.)

On August 4, 2021, Plaintiff sent the second demand for specific performance. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 20.)

On October 7, 2021, Plaintiff obtained a second loan approval note for $1,940,000.00. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 21.) Plaintiff claims that Defendant O&C Hillside did not sign a 4506T form that Defendant claims is necessary for the loan to be processed.

Legal Standard

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Id.)   

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option, he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66.)

 

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.) 

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or alternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.”

(Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.) 

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.) 

 

Discussion

A.    Defendant Fayek Sedrak’s Motion for Summary Judgment

Defendant Sedrak maintains that he was not a party to the escrow agreement, but rather signed the contract in his capacity as a corporate principal.

The fact that an individual is a corporate principal, agent, or officer of the company does not make the corporate officer-principal a party to the corporation’s contacts.(Bensara v. Marciano, (2001) 92 Cal. App. 4th 987, 990 (holding that plaintiff was not a party to agreements because “he unambiguously signed both as a corporate officer and only as a corporate officer, not in his individual capacity.”) It is an elementary principle of contract law that a person must be a party to a contract to be liable under the contract. (Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co., (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, 576 (non-insurer defendants were not parties to the insurance agreements and therefore were not subject to a duty of good faith and fair dealing); Tuchscher Development Enterprises, Inc. v. San Diego Unified Port Dist., (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 1219, 1243 (nonparty to exclusive negotiating agreement not under contractual duty to refrain from negotiating.) Defendant argues that Plaintiff has no basis to claim that Mr. Sedrak is responsible for a breach of contract to which he is not a party. The Court notes, that Defendant Sedrak is not an individual signee to the contract. Further, Defendant Sedrak also contends that he is not the alter ego of O&C Resources Management Company.

The alter ego doctrine does not guard every unsatisfied creditor of a corporation but instead affords protection where some conduct amounting to bad faith makes it inequitable for the corporate owner to hide behind the corporate form. Difficulty in enforcing a judgment or collecting a debt does not satisfy this standard. (Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court, (2000) 83 Cal. App. 4th 523, 539.)

Defendant Sedrak argues that Plaintiff has not made any showing the Defendant Sedrak abused the corporation’s privileges, committed any fraud, or engaged in bad faith.

Defendant Sedrak has made a showing that as a non-party to the contract, he cannot be held liable.  Accordingly, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to make an affirmative showing that Defendant Sedrak was a party to the contract.

The conditions under which a corporate entity may be disregarded, or the corporation be regarded as the alter ego of the stockholders, necessarily vary according to the circumstances in each case since the doctrine is essentially an equitable one and for that reason is particularly within the province of the trial court. Only general rules may be laid down for guidance. The two requirements are (1) that there be such unity of interest and ownership that the separate personalities of the corporation and the individual no longer exist, and (2) that, if the acts are treated as those of the corporation alone, an inequitable result will follow. With respect to the second requirement, it is sufficient that it appear that recognition of the acts as those of a corporation only will produce inequitable results. (Associated Vendors, Inc. v. Oakland Meat Co. (1962) 210 Cal.App.2d 825, 827.)

In Opposition, Plaintiff presents the following evidence for the alter ego theory:

·       Defendant Sedrak testified during his deposition that basically he is “everything” (meaning he was the President, the treasurer, and the secretary) at O&C Hillside Resource Management Company (“O&C”), and he has been holding such titles since 2008. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 29 – Deposition Transcript of Fayez Sedrak, pg. 17, lines 12-17)

·       Defendant Sedrak testified during his deposition that as the one who holds all such titles, he is basically knowledgeable and responsible for all of the major decisions and transactions that pertain to O&C. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 29 – Deposition Transcript of Fayez Sedrak, pg. 17, lines 18-22)

·       Defendant Sedrak testified during his deposition that “I am the organization.” (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 29 – Deposition Transcript of Fayez Sedrak, pg. 63, line 25)

·       Defendant Sedrak appeared as the PMK (Person Most knowledgeable) of O&C.

·       Defendant Sedrak and O&C retained the same counsel, Wendy Benge, at the beginning of this lawsuit.

·       Defendant Sedrak is the sole entity that communicated with TESORO. According to Mashney Decl., Exhibit 3 – Notice of Insufficient Documentation, dated 03/26/2020, Tesoro Refining & Marketing Company, LLC (“TESORO”) sent email to fsedrak@sbcglobal.net. Furthermore, according to the Notice of Denial of Consent” dated 12/23/2020, TESORO also communicated with DSEDRAK.

·       Defendant Sedrak emailed Ace Escrow on 03/04/2021 to cancel escrow. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 16)

The facts of this case are analogous to Lopez v Escamilla, (2022) 79 Cal. App. 5th 646. In Lopez, Defendant admitted that he was his company’ initial director, president, secretary, and treasurer. The company had only one board meeting. Only he signed the company's checks. He was the only shareholder, officer, and board member. (Id. at 651.) The Court agreed with Plaintiff’s arguments that “the corporation and its sole owner had the same interests because the owner was the corporation. Because he was the corporation, only he controlled the company, all its decisions, the litigation, and the decision to default. (Id. at 648.) However, in Lopez, the court found that plaintiff made an affirmative showing that defendant not only controlled the corporation but comingled his own funds with the corporation’s and engaged in fraudulent and inequitable conduct.

Here, Plaintiff does not provide evidence of any fraudulent behavior or comingling of funds. Therefore, the Court need not assess the piercing of the corporate vail argument.

Since Plaintiff cannot provide proof of its alter ego theory or that Defendant Sedrak, by signing the contract, was acting as anything other than O&C Hillside’s corporate officer, Defendant Sedrak’s Motion for Summary Judgement is GRANTED.

 

B.    Defendant O&C Hillside Resources Management Company 

Defendant O&C Hillside’s Motion for Summary Judgment is based on Plaintiff’s Material failure to perform the Amended Escrow Agreement of January 21, 2021. Defendant argues that the 90-day closing date of April 21, 2021 closed without performance and that Plaintiff failed to perform at any time following O&C Hillside’s August 1, 2021, demand for performance. Defendant O&C Hillside also argues that Plaintiff never had a loan commitment to fund the Escrow, and was never ready, willing, or able to close escrow.  

 

a.    First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) The plaintiff must attach the contract at issue to the complaint or plead the material terms to assert a breach of contract claim. (Construction Protective Servs., Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198–99; San Diego Housing Comm’n v. Indust. Indem. Co. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 526, 536.) 

Here, there was a contract that laid out how the parties were to perform, what the breach would be and the damages. However, Defendant O&C Hillside argues that the contract fails, because the material terms of the contract were unspecified.

To establish that a contract exists (the first element), the plaintiff must prove that there has been a “meeting of the [parties'] minds on all material points.” (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 359; Cheema v. L.S. Trucking, Inc. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 1142, 1149.) Whether the parties' minds have met is judged “objective[ly]” by looking to “the outward manifestations or expressions of the parties, ... and not their unexpressed intentions or understandings.” (Alexander v. Codemasters Group Limited (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 129, 141 (Alexander), disapproved on another ground in Reid v. Google (2010) 50 Cal.4th 512 (Reid); Bustamante v. Intuit, Inc. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 199, 208 (Bustamante).) A particular term of a contract is “material” or “essential” if it is necessary (1) in order to “provide a basis for determining the existence of a breach” of that contract, and (2) “for giving an appropriate remedy.” (Bustamante, at p. 209; Alexander, at p. 141.) 

Defendant O&C Hillside contends the January 21, 2021, Amendment (Exhibit “3”) was an agreement to agree on collateral for the Security Agreement and to fill in the blanks of Escrow’s “usual form.” There was no agreement on material terms following the January 21, 2021, Amendment. There was no “usual form” presented to the parties and no such form was ever agreed upon. So-called “usual forms” have blanks where the material terms are to be filled in. There is a total failure to specify (1) the creditor’s rights in the collateral; (2) what would constitute a default; (3) what remedies could be exercised upon default, (4) whether after acquired property was permitted or affected. Instead, these material terms were left to future agreement in filling out the “usual form.”

Defendant O&C Hillside further contends that that Plaintiff did not perform the terms of the contract as Plaintiff did not deposit the funds into escrow by the April 21, 2021 deadline.

The material factors to be ascertained from a contract for the sale of real property are the seller, buyer, the price to be paid, the time and manner of payment, and the property to be transferred, describing it so it may be identified; and that where these matters are sufficiently definite the contract may be specifically enforced. (Magna Development Co. v. Reed (1964) 228 Cal.App.2d 230, 231.) The modern trend of law is to favor the enforcement of contracts, to lean against their unenforceability because of uncertainty, and to carry out the intentions of the parties if this can feasibly be done. Id. at 235.

Uniform Commercial Code Comment 3 to U.C.C. 9108 notes that "[m]uch litigation has arisen over whether a description in a security agreement is sufficient to include after-acquired collateral if the agreement does not explicitly so provide. This question is one of contract interpretation and is not susceptible to a statutory rule." Accordingly, U.C.C. 9108 contains no reference to descriptions of after-acquired collateral. (4 B. Witkin, Summary of California Law, Secured Transactions in Personal Property § 46 (11th ed. 2022).)

Here, the Amended Escrow Instructions signed by the parties on January 21, 2021 was an enforceable contract. (See Mashney Decl., Exhibit 14.) The Court notes that the First Amended Instructions specifically refer to the original Escrow Instructions signed by parties on January 3, 2020. (Mashney Decl., Exhibit 1.) The amended instructions specifically state that the previous instructions are being “modified” – supplemented to reflect the new purchase price, new loan amount and the new balance of funds to be deposited into escrow. Furthermore, the last paragraph of the Amended Escrow Instructions specifically state that all other terms and conditions of the escrow will remain the same.

Furthermore, the Court finds that the material terms are present – seller is identified as O&C Hillside; buyer is identified as Plaintiff; price to be paid is stated as $1,940,000.00; time and manner of payment is stated – “ESCROW SUMMARY” specifically states, “Buyer has deposited with escrow $30,000.00.” “ Buyer will deposit prior to close of escrow $670,000.00.” “Buyer to obtain a new business loan in the amount of $1,090,000.00.” “Owner’s carry loan in the amount of $150,000.00.” “TOTAL CONSIDERATION: $1,940,000.00.” Moreover, the property to be transferred is stated as “ARCO AM PM 6545 13550 Beach Blvd., La Mirada, CA 90638.”

As for the “NEW FINANCING BY SELLER”, the Amended Escrow Instructions specifically states that the note, security agreement, and financing statements are to be prepared by Escrow Holder on “your standard form,” for signature of the parties bearing the terms specified as follows: Promissory note in the amount of $150,000.00 executed by Buyer in favor of Seller. Payment terms are to be 8 years with balloon payment with interest from TBD (date of escrow closing) at the rate of 0 (ZERO)% per annum. Further, the Amended Instructions specifically states that Escrow Holder is authorized to insert interest commencement date, payment dates (which are linked to the interest commencement date), as required at closing, over signature of the parties, without further instructions from them.

Accordingly, the Court finds that the material terms of the contract were present.

Defendant O&C Hillside argues that Plaintiff never deposited the requisite funds into escrow nor obtained the loan, as required by the contract by the date escrow was to close. The Court determines that O&C Hillside has satisfied its initial burden of demonstrating a prima facie case.  The Court now turns to whether Plaintiff performed or was excused from performing the condition precedent.

When the contract contains an express condition precedent, a party alleging breach of contract bears the burden of proving that it performed or was excused from performing the condition precedent. (Code of Civil Procedure § 457.)  Plaintiff argues that it performed its end of the bargain by: (1) attending all training and interviews required by TESORO, (2) paying all required fees to TESORO, (3) obtaining loan approval, and (4) having sufficient cash at hand to close escrow.

Plaintiff, by its own admission, states that it did not have the loan amount for the amended purchase price of $1,940,000.00 until October 7, 2021. (Opp., p. 12.) This date is certainly past the ninety-day threshold of the amended agreement and the five-day threshold of the August 1, 2021 letter. Even considering the accounts Plaintiff cites as exhibits, Plaintiff would not have had the funds to pay the purchase price in full with only $1,348,337.71 on hand. (Mashney Decl., Exhibits 23-27.)

However, Plaintiff does make a showing that 30 days prior to the end of the 90 day escrow period closing, Defendant Sedrak emailed Ace Escrow to cancel the escrow. (Mashney Decl., Ex. 16.) The Court is persuaded that there is a triable issue of fact of whether this canceled escrow prevented Plaintiff from satisfying its obligations.

Further any escrow must remain open until ABC approves the qualifications of the applicant and premises. The escrow holder shall not release any funds in the escrow in exchange for a promissory note or in exchange for any other consideration of less value to the creditors than the funds exchanged. (Section 24074.2 B&P Code.) Plaintiff offers evidence that it attempted to have Michael Geyer —O&C Hillside ABC’s license holder— sign and return the license transfer. (Mashney Decl., Ex 6.) However, Defendant O&C Hillside never responded to this email. Accordingly, there is a triable issue of fact as to whether escrow would have been able to close due to the lack of a transferred ABC license.

Finally, Defendant argues that the January 21, 2021 Amended Escrow Agreement waived all prior claims between the parties.  In support of this argument, Defendant relies upon Oakland Raiders v. Oakland-Alameda County Coliseum, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1175.  There, Plaintiff Oakland Raiders entered into a series of agreements with Defendant Oakland-Alameda County Coliseum, Inc. to finance and construct a stadium in the City of Oakland.  The Oakland Raiders sued for alleged misrepresentation of season ticket sales which it claimed induced the team to leave Los Angeles and enter into agreements with Defendant.  The appellate court concluded that the Oakland Raiders waived its claim of fraudulent inducement, which occurred before the first agreements were reached, by entering into a supplemental agreement with Defendant. 

In contrast to the fraud alleged in Oakland Raiders, Plaintiff Windmill alleges a series of delays that continued beyond the signing of the Amended Escrow Agreement.  “Waiver is the intentional relinquishment of a known right after full knowledge of the facts … Thus, [t]he pivotal issue in a claim of waiver is the intention of the party who allegedly relinquished the known legal right.” (Id. at 1189-90.)  Understood in this way, it can hardly be said that by signing the Amended Escrow Agreement, Plaintiff intentionally relinquished its right to sue over future delays caused by Defendant.  Under these circumstances, the Court determines that the doctrine of waiver is inapplicable.

Accordingly, Defendant O&C Hillside’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

b.    Second Cause of Action: Violation of Good Faith Covenant

The Court now turns its attention to the second cause of action for violation of the good faith covenant.

Defendant O&C Hillside argues that there is no independent claim for Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and there is no claim for interfering with a contract to which a party is a signatory. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s claims predate the January 21, 2021, Amendment.

Plaintiff argues that Defendant O&C Hillside interfered with the contract by not signing the requisite forms necessary to close escrow.

There is no independent claim for the violation of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and the claim is nothing more than a breach of contract. (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc., (2000) 24 Cal. 4th 317, 351; Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc., (1990) 222 Cal. App. 3d 1371, 1393.)

Here, the Court has resolved the breach of contract cause of action.  As there is no independent claim for violation of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, this claim must also present a triable issue of fact. 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the Second Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

c.     Third Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

“Any person interested under a written instrument, excluding a will or a trust, or under a contract, or who desires a declaration of his or her rights or duties with respect to another, or in respect to, in, over or upon property, or with respect to the location of the natural channel of a watercourse, may, in cases of actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties, bring an original action or cross-complaint in the superior court for a declaration of his or her rights and duties in the premises, including a determination of any question of construction or validity arising under the instrument or contract. He or she may ask for a declaration of rights or duties, either alone or with other relief; and the court may make a binding declaration of these rights or duties, whether or not further relief is or could be claimed at the time. The declaration may be either affirmative or negative in form and effect, and the declaration shall have the force of a final judgment. The declaration may be had before there has been any breach of the obligation in respect to which said declaration is sought.” (CCP § 1060.)

 

“The remedies provided by this chapter are cumulative, and shall not be construed as restricting any remedy, provisional or otherwise, provided by law for the benefit of any party to such action, and no judgment under this chapter shall preclude any party from obtaining additional relief based upon the same facts.” (CCP § 1062.)

 

Defendant O&C Hillside argues that declaratory relief cause of action relies on the same facts as the breach of contract cause of action.  However, under CCP § 1062, declaratory relief may still be granted.

The Court has found that there is a triable issue of fact with respect to the breach of contract cause of action. If, after a trial, the court decides to uphold the agreement and order specific performance, there will still be a future relationship between the parties. Since an actual controversy has been established, declaratory relief is required to preserve the rights and obligations of both parties.

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the Third Cause of Action is DENIED.
