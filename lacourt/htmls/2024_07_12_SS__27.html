DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV21526    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27¿ 

¿ 

HEARING ON THIRD PARTY CLAIM 

Hearing Date: 7/12/24¿¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV21526 ADAM COULTER vs STEFANIE AFTON WALL et al. 

Moving Party: Non-Party - Law Offices of Melissa B. Buchman¿ 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient¿¿ 

Ruling: Creditor’s Lien is Valid; Court Will Hear Further Argument re Claim of Exemption 

Background 

On June 8, 2020, Plaintiff Adam Coulter filed the present case. On January 30, 2024, and April 26, 2024, non-party and judgment creditor Law Offices of Melissa B. Buchman filed two notices of lien for $80,539.65 and $18,552.28, respectively, against judgment debtor and plaintiff Adam Coulter. The notices of lien are based on two orders for attorney’s fees against Mr. Coulter from Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. KF 015144, In re Matter of Christina Chacon vs. Adam Coulter, a confidential parentage action.  

On April 26, 2024, the parties in this action filed a notice of settlement. The judgment creditor argues that since the creditor’s lien has not been satisfied, pursuant to CCP § 708.440, judgment in favor of the debtor cannot be enforced and no settlement may be entered without the creditor’s authorization. The creditor further argues that the debtor failed to file a claim of exemption within 30 days after receiving notice of the lien in this case; therefore, debtor Mr. Coulter has waived his claim of exemption. Debtor argues that the lien creditor impermissibly attached an order when filing the notice of lien and further argues that he made an appropriate claim of exemption to the liens. 

Legal Standard 

CCP § 708.440 provides that “(a) unless the judgment creditor’s money judgment is first satisfied or the lien is released, the judgment recovered in the action or special proceeding in favor of the judgment debtor may not be enforced by a writ or otherwise, and no compromise, dismissal, settlement, or satisfaction of the pending action or special proceeding or the judgment procured therein may be entered into by or on behalf of the judgment debtor, without the written consent of the judgment creditor or authorization by order of the court obtained under subdivision (b).” 

CCP § 708.450 states “(a) If a lien is created under this article, the judgment debtor may claim that all or any portion of the money or property that the judgment debtor may recover in the action or special proceeding is exempt from enforcement of a money judgment. The claim shall be made by application on noticed motion to the court in which the action or special proceeding is pending, filed, and served on the judgment creditor not later than 30 days after the judgment debtor has notice of the creation of the lien. Service shall be made personally or by mail ... The failure of the judgment debtor to make a claim of exemption under this section constitutes a waiver of the exemption.” 

Discussion 

Validity of Notice of Lien 

Debtor first contends that creditor impermissibly attached an order when filing a notice of lien. He argues that although the word "judgment" under the statute means “a judgment, order, or decree entered in a court of this state” (CCP § 680.230), litigants do not have license to substitute the word "order" everywhere the word "judgment" appears in the Enforcement of Judgments Law (EJL), regardless of the circumstances or statutory intent. (See Lucky United Properties Investment, Inc. v. Lee (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 125, 143-44). He further argues that Creditor only attached an order and failed to show that there is no judgment; therefore, the lien is defective. 

Creditor filed a reply declaring that a judgment in Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. KF 015144, In re Matter of Christina Chacon vs. Adam Coulter, was entered on June 24, 2019. The two liens relate to post-judgment orders for attorney’s fees. The Court finds that just as pre-judgment orders for monetary sanctions are enforceable under the EJL when there is no judgment, post-judgment orders are similarly enforceable when there is already a judgment. Since the judgment in KF 015144 was entered in 2019, there is no separate judgment relating to the attorney’s fees. Debtor’s argument would entail that any modifications or orders made post judgment would not be capable of being enforced. Furthermore, the purpose of section 708.410 et seq., like the purpose of lien statutes generally, is to secure creditors' rights to money they are owed, and the statute is liberally construed. (Gilman v. Dalby (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 923.) 

Debtor then argues that Creditor failed to serve the January 30, 2024, Notice of Lien on one of Mr. Coulter’s counsel, McNicholas & McNicholas, and therefore Plaintiff has preserved its right to claim exemption. However, the Court is not persuaded because Debtor served the notice of lien by mail to Eric Panitz of Panitz Law, Plaintiff’s original attorney of record. Debtor does not dispute this nor does he dispute that he had actual notice of the lien but contests the validity of the service because the notice of lien was not served on all law firms representing him. As Adaimy v. Ruhl (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 583, 588 points out, service is effective if made on one of multiple attorneys representing a party. Similarly, here, Debtor does not argue that service of the lien on Eric Panitz was defective or that Debtor had no actual notice of the lien, only that one law firm representing Plaintiff was not served. But, Panitz Law was served, and Panitz Law has represented Plaintiff since the inception of the case, and McNicholas & McNicholas only became associated with the case in July 2022. 

Accordingly, the Creditor’s notice of lien is valid in this case. 

Claim of Exemption 

Debtor/Plaintiff argues that after Plaintiff’s counsel was served with the April 26, 2024, Notice of Lien for $18,552.28, Plaintiff’s co-counsel, Panitz Law Group APC, timely served a Claim of Exemption on March 5, 2024. The court does not see how it is conceivable to file a claim of exemption in March against a notice of lien served in April. In any event, the Court will hear from the parties regarding the claim of exemption.

MOTION TO REOPEN DISCOVERY¿ 

Hearing Date: 7/12/24¿¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV24719 JOSHUA PAYTON vs LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY 

Moving Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Responding Party: Defendant LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY 

Notice: Sufficient¿¿ 

Ruling: MOTION TO REOPEN DISCOVERY¿IS GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART 

 

Background 

On June 30, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present case. On September 3, 2020, Defendants filed their answer. The trial date and discovery deadlines have been continued numerous times. On November 27, 2023, the Court continued the trial date to February 21, 2024, and reset all discovery deadlines based on that new trial date.  The trial has since been continued several times, both at the parties' request and due to the unavailability of courtrooms; however, discovery remained closed. Plaintiff now moves the Court to reopen discovery to depose Defendants’ PMK, obtain Defendants’ sub rosa discovery, and designate a toxicology expert. 

Legal Standard 

Except as otherwise provided, “any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for trial of the action.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020(a).)¿ “[A] continuance or postponement of the trial date does not operate to reopen discovery proceedings” unless a motion to reopen discovery is filed and granted pursuant to CCP section 2024.050.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020(b); Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568.)¿ CCP section 2024.050 provides that “[o]n motion of any party, the court may grant leave to complete discovery proceedings, or to have a motion concerning discovery heard, closer to the initial trial date, or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050(a).)¿ 

¿ “The purpose of imposing a time limit on discovery is to expedite and facilitate trial preparation and to prevent delay.¿ Without a cutoff date, the parties could tie up each other and the trial court in discovery and discovery disputes right up to the eve of trial or beyond. Furthermore, . . . to be effective the cutoff date must be firm or some litigants will manipulate the proceedings to avoid the cut-off date.”¿ (Beverly Hosp. v. Superior Court¿(1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 1289, 1295.)¿ The reopening of discovery is a matter that is committed to the trial court’s sound discretion.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050(a), (b).)¿ In exercising that discretion, the trial court considers “any matter relevant to the leave requested,” including: 

  1. The necessity and the reasons for the discovery. 

  1. The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier. 

  1. Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party. 

  1. The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050(b).)¿¿ 

Discussion 

PMK Depositions 

In February 2024, Defendants designated both Shonda Breland and Demetrius Jones as non-retained experts. Plaintiff requested that Defendants’ non-retained expert witnesses be produced for deposition. Defendants communicated to Plaintiff that both Breland and Jones were mistakenly designed as experts when they are both PMKs for Defendant. On February 5, 2024, Defendants’ counsel agreed to produce Jones and Breland as in an email exchange between the parties. (Plf’s Ex. C.) Plaintiff subsequently scheduled Jones and Breland’s depositions, but Defendants subsequently canceled the depositions and refused to produce the PMKs. 

If Breland or Jones are listed as PMKs for Defendants and are expected to testify at trial, then Plaintiff should have the opportunity to depose them, especially when Defendant already agreed to produce the PMKs for deposition. Fairness and due process require that both parties have an equal chance to prepare for trial. Further, the Court does not find Plaintiff to have been tardy, as Plaintiff was unable to take the depositions due to the confusion from Defendant’s initial mis-designation. Defendants contend that Jones is no longer an employee and will not testify at trial. If true, Plaintiff would only need to depose Breland. However, if Jones might testify, Plaintiff should be allowed to depose him as well. Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request to reopen discovery for this limited purpose. 

Designation Of Toxicology Expert 

Plaintiff seeks to reopen discovery under Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.050. However, this statute pertains to the reopening of fact discovery only. The designation of expert witnesses, including a toxicology expert, does not fall under the category of fact discovery and is therefore outside the scope of this motion. 

Sub-rosa Discovery 

Plaintiff argues that reopening discovery is necessary to conduct supplemental discovery on Defendant’s sub rosa surveillance on Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s social media. The Court is unmoved by this argument for the following reasons: First, if this line of reasoning is accepted, it would imply that discovery could never truly close due to the perpetual potential for a party to conduct further surveillance. Such an approach undermines the finality of the discovery process. Second, Plaintiff is not prejudiced by the potential existence of sub rosa surveillance. Plaintiff has full control over his social media presence and complete knowledge of his own whereabouts and activities. Therefore, there is no basis for an ambush at trial. Plaintiff has the ability to anticipate and address any surveillance-related evidence based on his own actions and postings. Third, Plaintiff has not demonstrated diligence in pursuing supplemental discovery. Plaintiff does not dispute that the last time he served any discovery requests to Defendants was in May 2023. However, when the Court issued its November 27, 2023, minute order continuing the trial date, it explicitly stated that no further continuances would be granted. This order established a final discovery cut-off date of January 21, 2024. Plaintiff's failure to seek supplemental discovery near the discovery cut off demonstrates a lack of diligence.  

Conclusion 

The court GRANTS the present motion in part for the limited purpose of taking PMQ depositions.  All other aspects of the motion are denied.

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 7/12/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV45806 DELIA RAMIREZ vs MOKHTAR ZHOOBIN, et al. 

Moving Party: Defendant Mokhtar Zhoobin 

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS IS DENIED. 

 

Background 

This matter arises from an alleged fall at the residence located at 7101 Oso Ave #7, Winnetka, CA 91306. Plaintiff Delia Ramirez was a resident of a facility operated by Defendant Tarzana Treatment Centers, Inc., which leased the home from Defendant Mokhtar Zhoobin. On November 10, 2023, Defendant Mokhtar Zhoobin propounded Form Interrogatories (Set Two) upon Plaintiff Delia Ramirez. No responses were provided by the statutory deadline. On February 7, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set Two, and Requests for Sanctions. On April 11, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion, ordering Plaintiff to serve initial responses and pay sanctions within 20 days. On April 18, 2024, Defendant served the notice of ruling on Plaintiff, but Plaintiff failed to provide any responses.  

On May 13, 2024, Defendant sent a meet and confer letter via certified mail, requesting responses to the discovery propounded by Defendant. As of June 6, 2024, the date of this motion, Defendant has not received any response to the meet and confer letter nor any verified responses. Defendant Zhoobin now moves the Court to issue terminating sanctions against Plaintiff. 

Legal Standard 

The Civil Discovery Act provides for an escalating and “incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc.¿(2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604.)¿Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to and commensurate with the misconduct, and they “should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc.¿(2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) “If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.” (Ibid.; see also, e.g.,¿Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem¿(2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.)¿“Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.¿ But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390 [citation omitted].)¿ 

¿ 

The primary purpose of discovery sanctions is to obtain compliance with the Civil Discovery Act and the Court’s orders. It is not to punish. (Newland v. Super. Ct.¿(1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 608, 613;¿Ghanooni v. Super Shuttle of Los Angeles¿(1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 256, 262.) A discovery sanction should not create a “windfall” for a party or place a party in a better position than it would have been if the opposing party had simply complied with its obligations under the Court’s orders and the Civil Discovery Act. (Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co.¿(2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1194; see also 2 Weil & Brown, California Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023), ¶¶¿8:2214-2220.)¿“[T]the trial court has broad discretion in selecting the appropriate sanction, and we must uphold the trial court's determination absent an abuse of discretion.” (Dept. of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 19.) 

 

Discussion 

The Court examined all the evidence submitted and declines to grant terminating sanctions at this moment for the following reasons: 

  1. The Discovery Act mandates an escalating and incremental approach to discovery sanctions. In this case, Defendant moved from monetary sanctions, as ordered by the Court's April 11, 2024 minute order, straight to the ultimate sanctions. This approach contradicts the incremental sanctions framework envisioned by the Discovery Act, which aims to progressively enforce compliance through increasingly severe penalties. 

  1. There is no history of discovery abuses. Defendant cited the Court's April 11, 2024 order as the only violation by Plaintiff and failed to provide evidence of any other court order violation. A pattern of repeated non-compliance would typically justify harsher sanctions, but in this instance, Defendant has not demonstrated such a pattern. 

  1. The Court's April 11, 2024 order was issued approximately three months ago. This relatively short time period, combined with the absence of lesser sanctions, suggests that lesser sanctions could still be effective. The Discovery Act presumes that escalating penalties will eventually achieve compliance, and there has not been enough time or intermediate steps taken to conclude otherwise. 

  1. Plaintiff's counsel was only recently relieved. Given more time, there is a possibility that Plaintiff would engage more fully in the discovery process. The Court examined the docket and found that Plaintiff had been actively engaged in the discovery process when represented by counsel.  

However, the Court notes that Plaintiff did not oppose the present motion. The Court may reconsider its position if additional efforts to determine Plaintiff’s willingness to engage are made –- here, the only effort to engage Plaintiff prior to filing the terminating sanctions motion was to send a letter to Plaintiff -- and Plaintiff continues to refuse to engage in the discovery process. At this time, the Court denies the terminating sanctions motion without prejudice.

MOTION TO SEAL 

Hearing Date: 7/12/24¿¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV26278 LEANNA PALOMINO, et al. vs 123 MOVING & STORAGE LLC, et al. 

Moving Party: Defendant 123 Moving & Storage LLC and Rafael Rodriguez Sanchez 

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO SEAL IS GRANTED. 

 

Background 

 

This case arises out of a series of collisions that occurred on I-110 S/B, south of Adams Blvd., Los Angeles, CA 90007, on August 12, 2020. Plaintiffs were first rear-ended by Defendants Daveonta Louis Robinson and Donshel Zomalt-Robinson, causing a spin-out. Defendant 123 Moving & Storage LLC Rafael Rodriguez Sanchez’s vehicle then collided with Plaintiffs' vehicle, resulting in serious injuries and damages. 

 

Plaintiffs and Settling Defendants, 123 Moving & Storage LLC and Rafael Rodriguez Sanchez, reached a settlement agreement. The Settlement Agreement includes an express contractual obligation to maintain the confidentiality of the terms of the settlement, including but not limited to the settlement amount. The confidentiality provision was a material term of the settlement, which Settling Defendants specifically considered when agreeing to settle with Plaintiffs. 

 

Settling Defendants now move the Court to seal and maintain the confidentiality of the terms of the settlement, including but not limited to the settlement amount. Specifically, Settling Defendants request the Court to: 

 

  1. Seal Defendants’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement, or portions of the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with Defendants’ Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement that discuss confidential settlement terms, including but not limited to the confidential settlement amount. 

  1. Seal Plaintiffs’ Petitions for Approval of Compromise of Claim of Minor, or portions of it, that discuss confidential settlement terms, including but not limited to the confidential settlement amount. 

  1. Seal all related Orders Approving Compromise of Claim of Minor containing confidential information regarding the terms of the settlement, including but not limited to the confidential settlement amount. 

  1. Direct the Robinson Defendants and their attorneys to maintain the confidentiality of the sealed records by (a) not disseminating the sealed records or information contained therein to third parties or attorneys in their office that are not directly working on this matter, (b) placing the word “CONFIDENTIAL” on any correspondence regarding the sealed records, and (c) following the requirements of Rule 2.550 in connection with any court-filed documents. 

 

Discussion 

 

Pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 2.550 the court may seal a record “only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).) 

 

The contractual obligation not to disclose certain information constitutes an overriding interest within the meaning of Rule 2.550(d). (Publicker Industries, Inc. v. Cohen (3d Cir. 1984) 733 F.2d 1059, 1073; NBC Subsidiary (KNBC–TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1222, fn. 46.) 

 

Here, disclosure of the terms of the settlement agreement would violate the confidentiality clause within the agreement, thus breaching the parties' contractual obligation not to disclose certain information, an overriding interest. The motion is narrowly tailored, as it only seeks to seal the portions of the public document that reveal the confidential terms of the settlement agreement. The parties are not requesting to seal more than what is necessary to protect their agreed-upon confidentiality. 

 

Moreover, no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest of maintaining the confidentiality of the settlement terms. Any attempt to disclose these terms would compromise the confidentiality agreement and the parties' contractual obligations. All the requirements under Rule 2.550(d) of the California Rules of Court are met. Plaintiff did not file an opposition. Therefore, the Court grants the present motion.

MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL DISCOVERY RESPONSES¿AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 7/12/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV23818 KEVIN COLLINS vs LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY¿(MTA) 

Moving Party: Defendant MTA 

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:¿MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL DISCOVERY RESPONSES¿AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED. 

 

Legal Standard 

 

A defendant may make a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling without leave of court at any time. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (a).) A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)¿ 

¿ 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.¿ 

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.) 

 

Discussion 

 

On January 19, 2024, Defendant Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority served its Requests for Productions, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One, on Plaintiff.  The responses were due on February 23, 2024. Plaintiff has not served responses nor requested an extension to respond to discovery. Defendant now moves the court to compel initial responses. 

It is undisputed that the initial discovery responses at issue were not provided by the statutory deadline. No opposition or any other documents were filed indicating that the initial responses were provided prior to the hearing. Thus, the Court grants the present motions. The Court ORDERS Plaintiff to provide complete and verified responses to Defendant’s Requests for Productions, Set One and Form Interrogatories, Set One, without objections within 30 days of today. 

 

Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $1800 per motion. Sanctions are mandatory when Plaintiff's non-response forced Defendant to file the present motions.  Plaintiff did not act with substantial justification in failing to provide responses. In consideration of the simplicity of the issues and the lack of an opposition, the Court grants sanctions in the amount of $1500 for both motions. The Court ORDERS Plaintiff and his counsel, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $1500 to Defendant within 30 days of today’s date.
