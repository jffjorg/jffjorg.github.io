DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Please notify Department 52 via email at smcdept52@lacourt.org and indicate that the parties are submitting on the tentative ruling. Please provide the attorney's name and represented party. Please notify the opposing side via email if submitting on the Court's tentative ruling.



Case Number: 21STCV07923    Hearing Date: November 6, 2023    Dept: 52

Tentative Ruling:   

Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants City of Burbank, the People of the State of California by and Through Joseph H. McDougall, City of Burbank City Attorney’s Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

            Plaintiffs/cross-defendants City of Burbank, the People of the State of California by and through Joseph H. McDougall, City of Burbank City Attorney move for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication, on their complaint and on the third amended cross-complaint by defendants/cross-complainants Barfly, Inc., Baret Lepejian, Lucas Lepejian, and Tayla Lepejian.

Requests for Judicial Notice

Plaintiffs/cross-defendants request judicial notice of 37 facts and exhibits.  Request Nos. 9, 10, 18, 31, 32, and 33 may be court records subject to judicial notice under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d)(1), but they are irrelevant and unnecessary to the court’s analysis.  (See Jordache Enterprises, Inc. v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 18 Cal.4th 739, 748, fn. 6; Appel v. Superior Court (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 329, 342, fn. 6.)  The existence and legal effects of the remaining items are subject to judicial notice under Evidence Code section 452, subdivisions (b), (c), and (d)(1).  The court does not take judicial notice of the truth of facts asserted therein.  (See Sosinsky v. Grant (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1548, 1569.)

            Plaintiffs/cross-defendants’ requests for judicial notice Nos. 9, 10, 18, 31, 32, and 33 are denied.  Plaintiffs/cross-defendants’ requests for judicial notice Nos. 1-8, 11-17, 19-30, and 34-37 are granted.

Defendants/cross-complainants request judicial notice of four court records.  (Opp. RJN.)  They did not submit copies of the first three records.  “A party requesting judicial notice of material under Evidence Code sections 452 or 453 must provide the court and each party with a copy of the material.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1306(c).)  The only record attached, the fourth, is the notice of ruling on plaintiffs’ motion for summary judgment in a case by the County of Los Angeles against several defendants including Barfly, Inc. and Baret Lepejian.  Though it is subject to judicial notice under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d)(1), it is irrelevant and unnecessary to the court’s analysis.  (See Jordache Enterprises, Inc. v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 18 Cal.4th 739, 748, fn. 6; Appel v. Superior Court (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 329, 342, fn. 6.) 

Defendants/cross-complainants’ four requests for judicial notice are denied.

Evidentiary Objections

            Defendants/cross-complainants make 143 pages of evidentiary objections.  They did not number their objections.  When objecting to evidence submitted in support of or opposition to a motion for summary judgment, “[e]ach written objection must be numbered consecutively.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1354(b).)  Consecutively numbering objections—especially such voluminous objections—is necessary for the court to make clear rulings on them.  All of defendants/cross-complainants’ evidentiary objections are overruled.

            Plaintiffs/cross-defendants make five objections to the declaration of Baret Lepejian.  All five objections are overruled.

            Plaintiffs/cross-defendants make 10 objections to the declaration of Lucas Lepejian.  Objection Nos. 1, 3, 4, 5, 8, and 10 are sustained. 

Legal Standard for a Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment “must prove each element of the cause of action on which he seeks judgment.  Once the plaintiff meets this burden, the burden shifts to the defendant to show there is a triable issue of material fact as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 1332, 1337; CCP § 437c(p)(1).)  The court must deny a plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment if the defendant meets its “burden of showing the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to any available affirmative defense.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 568.)

Plaintiffs’ Complaint

            Plaintiffs bring three causes of action against defendants: (1) violation of Burbank Municipal Code section 4-1-101-1, (2) violation of Burbank Municipal Code section 10-1-501l, and (3) abatement of public nuisance.  The complaint alleges defendants continued operating “Tin Horn Flats Saloon/Bar & Grill, located at 2623 West Magnolia Boulevard, Burbank, California” (Comp., ¶ 7) after the County revoked the restaurant’s public health permit (¶ 62) and after the City revoked the restaurant’s conditional use permit (¶ 76).

Plaintiffs’ complaint prays for injunctive relief (a) prohibiting defendants from operating the restaurant “Tin Horn Flats without the necessary use permit and public health permit” (Comp., prayer ¶ 1); (b) permitting the city to disconnect the electricity at Tin Horn Flats (prayer, ¶ 2); (c) permitting the City to padlock the doors to Tin Horn Flats (prayer, ¶ 3); (d) a declaration that defendants’ conduct at Tin Horn Flats constitutes “a continuing public nuisance” (prayer, ¶ 4); (e) “an abatement order requiring Defendants … to bring Tin Horn Flats into compliance” with the applicable law (prayer, ¶ 5); (f) “attorney’s fees and costs of suit” (prayer, ¶ 6); and (g) “any other relief that the Court sees as just and proper” (prayer, ¶ 7). 

Assuming plaintiffs meet their initial burden of proving each element of their three causes of action, defendants meet their burden of showing there is a triable issue of material fact as to an affirmative defense to all three causes of action: mootness.  “As a general rule, when events render a case moot, the trial or appellate court should dismiss it.”  (Davis v. Fresno Unified School District (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 911, 927.)  “[A] case that presents a true controversy at its inception becomes moot ‘ “if before decision it has, through act of the parties or other cause, occurring after the commencement of the action, lost that essential character.” ’ ”  (Fairview Valley Fire, Inc. v. Department of Forestry & Fire Protection (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 1262, 1272.)  “When a plaintiff has received all that he or she has demanded in the complaint, the case is considered ‘moot’; in other words, there is no further relief the court could provide.”  (Schoshinski v. City of Los Angeles (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 780, 791.) 

Defendants present evidence sufficient to show triable issues of fact on whether plaintiffs’ claims are moot.  As discussed above, plaintiffs’ complaint seeks permanent injunctions and declaratory relief regarding defendants’ operation of the Tin Horn Flats restaurant at 2623 West Magnolia Boulevard in the City of Burbank.  (Comp., ¶ 7.)  Defendant Lucas Lepejian “was an employee of Barfly, Inc.” and “managed the day to day operations” of the Tin Horn Flats restaurant.  (L. Lepejian Decl., ¶ 1.)  He states the property on which the restaurant was located was “forcibly taken in a separate eviction action.”  (Id., ¶ 18.)  Finally, he states, “Since surrendering the property Tin Horn Flats was located at in or around June of 2021, I have not returned to the property.”  (Id., ¶ 20.)

Defendant Baret Lepejian also presents evidence to the same effect.  He is and was the chief executive officer of defendant “Barfly, Inc., which operated the restaurant, Tin Horn Flats, previously located at 2623 W Magnolia Boulevard.”  (B. Lepejian Decl., ¶ 1.)  Baret Lepejian states, “Since the eviction action brought by my estranged ex-wife against me in or around June of 2021, Barfly, Inc. has no interest in the property Tin Horn Flats was once located at.”  (Id., ¶ 4.)  He further states, “Since surrendering the property Tin Horn Flats was located at in or around June of 2021, neither myself, my agents, nor employees who operated the restaurant have returned to the property.  Additionally, since that same surrender, I have not intended, nor attempted to open another restaurant, nor do I intend on opening one in the future.”  (B. Lepejian Decl., ¶ 9.) 

This evidence raises triable issues of material fact on the defense of mootness.  If the Tin Horn Flats restaurant in Burbank no longer exists and will never return, the trier of fact could conclude plaintiff already received all that they demanded in the complaint.    

            Plaintiffs rely on the rule that a defendant cannot make a case moot by voluntarily ceasing unlawful conduct.  “[A] defendant cannot automatically moot a case simply by ending its unlawful conduct once sued.  [Citation.]  Otherwise, a defendant could engage in unlawful conduct, stop when sued to have the case declared moot, then pick up where he left off, repeating this cycle until he achieves all his unlawful ends.  Given this concern, … ‘a defendant claiming that its voluntary compliance moots a case bears the formidable burden of showing that it is absolutely clear the allegedly wrongful behavior could not reasonably be expected to recur.’ ”  (Already, LLC v. Nike, Inc. (2013) 568 U.S. 85, 91.)

When determining plaintiffs’ motion to summarily adjudicate its causes of action, the issue is not whether defendants can ultimately make that showing.  The issue is whether they present a triable issue of material fact on their affirmative defense.  They do.  Based on this evidence, a reasonable trier of fact could determine that defendants show it is absolutely clear defendants will never again operate a restaurant in violation of the Burbank Municipal Code or in a manner that constitutes a public nuisance.

Moreover, there is evidence that the only reason for revoking Tin Horn Flats’ public health permit and conditional use permit is that it operated a restaurant in violation of COVID-19 shutdown orders in 2020 and 2021.  Baret Lepejian states, “[T]he only health violations received were related to the events giving rise to this lawsuit,” meaning the COVID-19 shutdown orders.  (B. Lepejian Decl., ¶ 10.)  And there is evidence those shutdown orders are no longer in effect.  Lucas Lepejian states, “[M]erely merely two days after revoking Barfly’s public health permit on that basis, the County officially lifted its restaurant outdoor dining prohibition, declaring that outdoor dining at restaurants no longer posed an immediate risk to the public’s health and safety.”  (L. Lepejian Decl., ¶ 12.)  The trier of fact could reasonably conclude that, because the grounds for revoking Tin Horn Flats’ permit ended long ago, it is absolutely clear that defendants will never again operate a restaurant in violation of the Burbank Municipal Code or in a manner that constitutes a public nuisance.

In their reply brief, plaintiffs assert its causes of action are not moot due to their “claim for payment of the outstanding Administrative citations/civil penalties under BMC 1-1-108-1.”  (Reply, p. 12.)  They rely on Justice Stevens’ concurring opinion in Friends of the Earth, Inc. v. Laidlaw Environmental Services (TOC), Inc. (2000) 528 U.S. 167, which states, “No one contends that a defendant’s postcomplaint conduct could moot a claim for punitive damages; civil penalties should be treated the same way.”  (Id. at p. 197.)  A concurrence is not binding authority. 

Moreover, plaintiffs’ complaint does not seek any payment for outstanding citations or civil penalties.  “The pleadings play a key role in a summary judgment motion.  ‘ “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues” ’  and to frame ‘the outer measure of materiality in a summary judgment proceeding.’ ”  (Hutton v. Fidelity National Title Co. (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 486, 493.)  Above, the court summarized all seven paragraphs of the complaints’ prayer for relief.  None of those seven paragraphs raises the issue of civil penalties or outstanding citations or fines.  The court expresses no opinion on whether plaintiffs may be able to recover the only other relief they seek: attorney fees and costs.  (See Graham v. DaimlerChrysler Corp. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 553 [under “catalyst theory,” plaintiffs may recover attorney fees when case dismissed as moot].)

The court therefore will deny summary adjudication of plaintiffs’ three causes of action against defendants. 

Legal Standard for a Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment

Typically, a defendant moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause of action… cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (CCP § 437c(p)(2).)  Once the defendant does so, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show a triable issue of at least one material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849.) 

For some causes of action that rely on the defendant’s subjective motivation, however, the plaintiff bears the initial burden even on a defendant’s motion for summary judgment.  The third amended cross-complaint’s sole remaining cause of action is a claim under 42 U.S.C. section 1983 for retaliation in violation of the First Amendment.  (In June 2022, cross-complainants requested dismissal of the first through fourth and sixth through ninth causes of action, but specified that they did not dismiss the fifth cause of action for “violation of the First Amendment.”  That is the third cause of action, not the fifth.)

In First Amendment retaliation claims, the plaintiff bears the initial burden of proof.  This rule arises from the United States Supreme Court’s decision in Mt. Healthy City School Dist. Bd. of Educ. v. Doyle (1977) 429 U.S. 274 (Mt. Healthy).  On appeal after a bench trial (id. at p. 276), the Court concluded, “Initially, … the burden was properly placed upon” the plaintiff employee “to show that his conduct was constitutionally protected, and that this conduct was a ‘substantial factor’ or to put it in other words, that it was a ‘motivating factor’ [fn.] in the” defendant employer’s “decision not to rehire him” (id. at p. 287).  “Mt. Healthy assigns to plaintiffs the initial burden of showing that conduct protected by the First Amendment was a ‘ “substantial” ’ or ‘ “motivating” ’ factor in an employer’s adverse employment decision, then assigns to the defendant the burden of showing it would have made the same decision in the absence of the protected conduct.”  (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 714 [discussed in deciding burden-shifting framework for summary judgment of whistleblower retaliation claims].)  

Numerous circuit courts have held that, when a defendant moves for summary judgment, the plaintiff bears the initial burden of showing a prima facie case.  (Walton v. Powell (10th Cir. 2016) 821 F.3d 1204, 1211 [doing so after noting Mt. Healthy was an appeal of “a decision after trial”]; McCue v. Bradstreet (1st Cir. 2015) 807 F.3d 334, 344–345; Wagner v. Jones (8th Cir. 2011) 664 F.3d 259, 270-271; Eckerman v. Tennessee Dept. of Safety (6th Cir. 2010) 636 F.3d 202, 207–208; Ulrich v. City and County of San Francisco (9th Cir. 2002) 308 F.3d 968, 976–977.)  Courts have also applied this standard in contexts other than public employment.  (Bello-Reyes v. Gaynor (9th Cir. 2021) 985 F.3d 696, 702 [in habeas corpus petition, “once a petitioner has made a showing of a First Amendment retaliation claim, ‘the burden shifts to the government to show that it “would have taken the same action even in the absence of the protected conduct” ’ ”].)

In contrast with government employees suing for employment retaliation, a citizen bringing a section 1983 suit for retaliatory government action generally must prove an additional element: that the government lacked probable cause for its action.  In “retaliatory prosecution claims,” the plaintiff must “plead and prove the absence of probable cause for the underlying criminal charge.”  (Nieves v. Bartlett (2019) 139 S.Ct. 1715, 1723 (Nieves).)  The same requirement applies to claims for “retaliatory arrest.”  (Id. at p. 1725.)  “[I]f the plaintiff establishes the absence of probable cause, ‘then the Mt. Healthy test governs: The plaintiff must show that the retaliation was a substantial or motivating factor behind the [arrest], and, if that showing is made, the defendant can prevail only by showing that the [arrest] would have been initiated without respect to retaliation.’ ”  (Ibid.)  The Court concluded there was probable cause for the arrest, so the “retaliation claim cannot survive summary judgment.”  (Ibid.)

Courts have applied the Nieves rule to government actions other than arrest or criminal prosecution.  In Capp v. County of San Diego (9th Cir. 2019) 940 F.3d 1046, 1056, the Ninth Circuit applied the rule to a plaintiff’s claim that the County conducted a child welfare investigation against him in retaliation for his protected speech.  The 11th Circuit applied Nieves to a suit alleging a retaliatory civil action.  (DeMartini v. Town of Gulf Stream (11th Cir. 2019) 942 F.3d 1277, 1303-1304 (DeMartini).)  “[A]s with § 1983 First Amendment retaliation claims arising in the criminal prosecution and arrest context, the presence of probable cause will generally defeat a § 1983 First Amendment retaliation claim based on a civil lawsuit as a matter of law.”  (Id. at p. 1304.) 

Here, cross-defendants submit voluminous undisputed evidence establishing probable cause for their actions against cross-complainants.  It is undisputed that cross-complainants repeatedly violated the laws cross-defendants enforced against them.  Cross-complainants only argue that the public health orders they violated were unconstitutional.  The Court of Appeal decided otherwise.  (County of Los Angeles Department of Public Health v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 478.)

Nieves found one exception to the requirement that the plaintiff prove the government lacked probable cause for its action.  “Although probable cause should generally defeat a retaliatory arrest claim, a narrow qualification is warranted for circumstances where officers have probable cause to make arrests, but typically exercise their discretion not to do so.”  (Nieves, supra, 139 S.Ct. at p. 1727.)  “[T]he no-probable-cause requirement should not apply when a plaintiff presents objective evidence that he was arrested when otherwise similarly situated individuals not engaged in the same sort of protected speech had not been.”  (Ibid.)  “After making the required showing, the plaintiff’s claim may proceed in the same manner as claims where the plaintiff has met the threshold showing of the absence of probable cause.”  (Ibid.) 

It is not clear this exception is available for retaliatory conduct other than arrests.  (See DeMartini, supra, 942 F.3d at p. 1306 [“the Supreme Court has not identified any exceptions to the no-probable-cause requirement in § 1983 First Amendment retaliation claims predicated on criminal prosecutions”].)  Assuming this exception applies, cross-complainants bear the initial burden of proving they fall within it.  Doing so requires them to “present[] objective evidence that” the government took actions against them but not against “otherwise similarly situated individuals not engaged in the same sort of protected speech.”  (Nieves, supra, 139 S.Ct. at p. 1725.) 

Cross-complainants present no such evidence.  Rather than attempting to do so, they argue cross-defendants bore the initial burden of proving they had no animus.  (Opp., pp. 8-10.)  The United States Supreme Court held otherwise because “retaliatory animus by a governmental actor is a subjective condition that is ‘easy to allege and hard to disprove.’ ”  (DeMartini, supra, 942 F.3d at p. 1289, quoting Nieves, supra, 139 S.Ct. at p. 1725.)

Cross-complainants provide zero admissible objective evidence that the City of Burbank enforced the law against them but not other restaurants or businesses who violated the COVID-19 shutdown orders (or ensuing court orders to comply).  Their only attempt to do so is the following statement by Lucas Lepejian: “[R]estaurants located in places such as the Grove that served to-go food, were not penalized or cited when their customers could sit in shared areas, similar to Tin Horn Flats’s, due to the characterization of the shared areas not belonging to a single restaurant.  That characterization was untrue as I personally saw other restaurants within miles of Tin Horn Flats do the same thing as us.”  (L. Lepejian Decl., ¶ 5.)  Cross-defendants properly objected to this testimony on several grounds. 

Even if this testimony were admissible, it would not constitute objective evidence that the City of Burbank did not enforce the law against otherwise similarly situated businesses that did not engage in the same protected speech.  The Grove is in the City of Los Angeles, not Burbank.  That fact is subject to judicial notice and cannot be reasonably disputed.  (Evid. Code, § 452(g), (h).)  What happened at the Grove is irrelevant to whether the City of Burbank can be liable for selectively enforcing the law.

Lucas Lepejian’s statement that other restaurants “within miles of Tin Horn Flats” did “the same thing” as cross-complainants is also insufficient.  For that to constitute objective evidence that the City of Burbank did not enforce the law against other restaurants that did not engage in similar protected speech, cross-complainants must show: (a) those restaurants were in the City of Burbank, (b) the City of Burbank knew those restaurants were also violating the shutdown orders, and (c) those restaurants did not engage in similar protected speech.  Lucas Lepejian’s statement provides as little information as possible.  He does not identify the other restaurants, does not say when he saw them allow outdoor dining, and avoids even saying these unidentified restaurants are in the City of Burbank.  There is no evidence that the City of Burbank knew these unidentified restaurants violated the law and there is no evidence that these unidentified restaurants did not engage in similar protected speech as cross-complainants.

On this record, no reasonable trier of fact could conclude cross-defendants enforced the law against cross-complainants without probable cause or that they selectively enforced the law to retaliate against them for protected speech. 

Disposition

            Plaintiffs City of Burbank, the People of the State of California by and through Joseph H. McDougall, City of Burbank City Attorney’s motion for summary adjudication of their complaint is denied. 

Cross-defendants City of Burbank, the People of the State of California by and through Joseph H. McDougall, City of Burbank City Attorney’s motion for summary adjudication of the third amended cross-complaint is granted.

 


Case Number: 21STCV42411    Hearing Date: November 6, 2023    Dept: 52

Tentative Ruling:

            Plaintiffs Gerald Meehan and Lois Meehan’s Motions to: (1-3) Compel Depositions of Gal Batzri, Nitzan Ben-Nun, and Neta Golani; (4) Compel Defendant Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC to Provide Answers to Special Interrogatories

(1-3) Motions to Compel Depositions      

Plaintiffs Gerald Meehan and Lois Meehan move to compel the depositions of witnesses Gal Batzri, Nitzan Ben-Nun, and Neta Golani.  The motions are moot as to compelling the depositions. 

Plaintiffs seek monetary sanctions.  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though …  the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)  The court, however, finds defendants acted with substantial justification and sanctions would not be just under the circumstances. 

Disposition

Plaintiffs’ motions to compel depositions of Gal Batzri, Nitzan Ben-Nun, and Neta Golani and for sanctions are denied.

(4) Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories

Plaintiffs Gerald Meehan and Lois Meehan move to compel defendant Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC to respond to special interrogatories, set two.

Meet and Confer

            Defendant argues plaintiffs did not meet and confer in good faith before filing this motion.  Doing so is not required when moving to compel responses as opposed to moving to compel further responses.  (Compare CCP §§ 2030.290 with 2030.300(b)(1).)

Defendant’s Untimely Responses

            When the responding party fails to timely respond to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses.  (CCP § 2030.290(b).)  Failing to timely respond waives any objections.  (CCP § 2030.290(a).)  Defendant concedes that it failed to timely respond to plaintiffs’ special interrogatories, set two.  Defendant served responses on September 7, a week after plaintiffs filed this motion.  (Varozian Decl., ¶ 2.) 

The court will exercise its discretion to treat this motion as a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300.  When the opposing party serves untimely responses, “the trial court has the discretion to rule on the motion” and may “treat the motion as one” to compel further responses and “determine that further answers are required.”  (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 409.)

Because its response was untimely, defendant waived its objections.  Defendant’s response includes over a page of general objections.  (Varozian Decl., Ex. A.)  Defendant’s objections to plaintiffs’ special interrogatories, set two, are overruled.

Defendant’s responses are also evasive or incomplete.  “[A] party has a general duty to conduct a reasonable investigation to obtain responsive information and must furnish information from all sources under his or her control.”  (Regency Health Services, Inc. v. Superior Court (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 1496, 1504.)  “Verification of the answers is in effect a declaration that the party has disclosed all information which is available to him.  If only partial answers can be supplied, the answers should reveal all information then available to the party.  If a person cannot furnish details, he should set forth the efforts made to secure the information.  He cannot plead ignorance to information which can be obtained from sources under his control.”  (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 782.)

The interrogatories ask defendant to state contact information for three people who signed declarations in support of papers filed by Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC in another case.  Defendant responded, “This information is unknown to Responding Party.  Investigation and discovery still continue and Responding Party reserves the right to amend this response upon obtaining additional information.”  Defendant neither stated it conducted a reasonable investigation nor set forth any efforts it made to obtain the witnesses’ contact information.

Cleary defendant or its counsel at some point had communications with the three people who submitted declarations on its behalf.  The declarations did not fall from the sky into defense counsel’s office.  Defendant’s curt statement that any contact information is “unknown” to defendant is evasive.

Sanctions

Plaintiffs move for $3,085 in sanctions against defendant Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC and its attorneys, Rouben Varozian and David Beitchman.  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though … the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).)          

The court will grant plaintiffs’ request for sanctions.  “The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2030.290(c).)  Defendant unsuccessfully opposed this motion.  Defendant is also subject to sanctions for making “an unmeritorious objection to discovery.”  (CCP § 2023.010(e).)

Defendant did not act with substantial justification.  Sanctions are just under the circumstances.  Defendant’s delay necessitated this motion, and its untimely responses are insufficient.

Disposition

            Plaintiffs Gerald Meehan and Lois Meehan’s motion to compel defendant Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC to respond to special interrogatories is granted. 

Defendant Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC is ordered to serve further responses without objections to plaintiffs’ special interrogatories, set two, within 20 days.  Defendant Pacific Horizon Holdings, LLC and its counsel David P. Beitchman and Rouben Varozian are ordered to pay plaintiffs $3,085 in sanctions within 20 days.  Defendant and its counsel are jointly and severally liable for the sanctions.
