DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV04391    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: W

fallah v. volkswagen group of america, inc., et al.

 

motioN for summary judgment or in the alternative summary adjudication

 

Date of Hearing:          12/16/2024                             Trial Date:       4/14/2025

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         23VECV04391

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Aria Fallah [no opposition filed]

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

The hearing on Defendant’s motion is continued to March 6, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

Plaintiff Aria Fallah filed this lemon law action against defendants Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. (“VWGA”), Scott Robinson Pontiac, Inc. dba Scott Robinson Honda, and Sherman-Oaks-A, Inc. dba Audi Van Nuys on October 3, 2023. (The latter two defendant were named erroneously in Plaintiff’s initial complaint.)

 

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed her claims against Scott Robinson Honda.

 

On November 12, 2024, VWGA moved for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of three of Plaintiff’s four causes of action. (See Not. of Mot., 2:14-26 [issues for summary adjudication].) VWGA served Plaintiff with its motion via electronic mail on November 11, 2024.

 

VWGA did not provide sufficient notice of its motion.  “Notice of [a] motion [for summary judgment or adjudication] and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437(c)(a)(2).) VWGA served its motion only forty-five (45) days prior.

 

The hearing on VWGA’s motion is continued to Wednesday, March 6, 2024, at 8:30 a.m., with opposition and reply briefing to proceed according to that hearing date.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.


Case Number: 24VECV02088    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: W

GLOBAL INNOVATIONS, LLC VS NORMAN J. KRAVETZ

 

PLAINTIFF GLOBAL INNOVATIONS, LLC’S EX PARTE APPLICATION FOR RIGHT TO ATTACH ORDER AND PRE-JUDGMENT WRIT OF ATTACHMENT AGAINST DEFENDANT NORMA J. KRAVETZ

 

Date of Hearing:        December 16, 2024                          Trial Date:       None set. 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV02088

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Global Innovations, LLC

Responding Party:     Defendant Norman Kravetz

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff Global Innovations, LLC filed a complaint against Norman J. Kravetz for breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges on November 9, 2022, Defendant executed a Promissory Note in favor of Plaintiff, in the amount of $1,200.000.00. Plaintiff alleges after Defendant defaulted on the Original Note by failing to make timely payments, Plaintiff and Kravetz executed a First Amendment to Promissory Note on October 20, 2023, which amended certain terms of the Original Note. Plaintiff further alleges in the Amendment to Note, Plaintiff and Defendant stipulated to a balance as of the effective date of the Amended Note of $776,985.55 plus an “Extension Fee” of $19,424.64, payable by October 31, 2023, and a past-due payment of $33,858.98 by October 20, 2023. However, Plaintiff alleges on April 15, 2024, Defendants defaulted on the Agreement by failing to make required payments.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Plaintiff Global Innovations, LLC’s Ex Parte Application for Right to Attach Order and Pre-Judgment Writ of Attachment Against Defendant Norma J. Kravetz is granted.  

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Plaintiff requests this court take judicial notice of several court documents including a complaint, writ of attachment, motion to continue trial by less than 90 days in Pacific Western Bank v. Norman J. Kravetz et al, LASC Case No. 23STCV09801; complaint filed in LTL Insurance Services Inc. Profit Sharing Plan, et al. v. JH Portfolio Debt Equities, LLC, et al., LASC Case No. 24SMCV02361; complaint and writ of attachment filed in Aaron Kitzman et al. v. Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC et al., LASC Case No. 23VECV04655; complaint filed in RBE Capital Partners Fund LP v. JH Portfolio Debt Equities, LLC, LASC No. 24VECV00463; and complaint filed in Connie Brockamn et al. v. Kravetz Capital Funding, LLC et. al., LASC No. 23VECV05660.

 

Plaintiff seeks judicial notice of these documents to demonstrate Kravetz is personally named as a defendant in at least five other lawsuits with different lenders regarding breach of promissory notes of which enforcement efforts are underway totaling at least $22,880,000.00. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as to the existence of these documents.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff applies for a right to attach order and prejudgment writ of attachment on Defendant Kravetz in the total amount of $872,708.90. Plaintiff makes the application on the grounds that Plaintiff has a probable validity of prevailing against Kravetz and there is a grave and immediate danger that Plaintiff will suffer irreparable harm due to Kravetz’s self-admitted imminent likelihood of filing for bankruptcy.

 

“Upon the filing of the complaint or at any time thereafter, the plaintiff may apply pursuant to this article for a right to attach order and a writ of attachment by filing an application for the order and writ with the court in which the action is brought.”  (CCP § 484.010.)

 

The application shall be executed under oath and must include: (1) a statement showing that the attachment is sought to secure the recovery on a claim upon which an attachment may be issued; (2) a statement of the amount to be secured by the attachment; (3) a statement that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based; (4) a statement that the applicant has no information or belief that the claim is discharged or that the prosecution of the action is stayed in a proceeding under the Bankruptcy Act (11 U.S.C. section 101 et seq.); and (5) a description of the property to be attached under the writ of attachment and a statement that the plaintiff is informed and believes that such property is subject to attachment.  (CCP § 484.020.) 

 

“The application [for a writ of attachment] shall be supported by an affidavit showing that the plaintiff on the facts presented would be entitled to a judgment on the claim upon which the attachment is based.”  (CCP § 484.030.)  “The declarations in the moving papers must contain evidentiary facts, stated ‘with particularity,’ and based on actual personal knowledge with all documentary evidence properly identified and authenticated.” (Hobbs v. Weiss (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 76, 79–80 [citing CCP § 482.040].) “In contested applications, the court must consider the relative merits of the positions of the respective parties and make a determination of¿the probable outcome of the litigation.” (Hobbs, supra, 73 Cal.App.4th at p. 80.) 

 

The court shall issue a right to attach order if the court finds all of the following: (1) The claim upon which the attachment is based is one upon which an attachment may be issued. (2) The plaintiff has established the probable validity of the claim upon which the attachment is based.  (3) The attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based. (4) The amount to be secured by the attachment is greater than zero. (CCP §484.090.)  

 

Basis of Attachment

 

“[A]n attachment may be issued only in an action on a claim or claims for money, each of which is based upon a contract, express or implied, where the total amount of the claim or claims is a fixed or readily ascertainable amount not less than five hundred dollars ($500) exclusive of costs, interest, and attorney's fees.”  (CCP § 483.010(a).)  “An attachment may not be issued on a claim which is secured by any interest in real property arising from agreement ….”  (CCP § 483.010(b).)   

 

In this matter, Plaintiff’s claims are for monies owed upon a contract. Moreover, the amount sought to be attached by Plaintiff is of a fixed or readily ascertainable amount greater than $500. Plaintiff seeks $872,708.90. Plaintiff also testifies Plaintiff’s claim is not secured by any interest in real property. (See Wazana Decl. ¶13, Exhs. 3, 7.)

 

Lastly, as noted by Plaintiff, “[i]f the action is against a defendant who is a natural person, an attachment may be issued only on a claim which arises out of the conduct by the defendant of a trade, business, or profession. An Attachment may not be issued on a claim against a defendant who is a natural person if the claim is based on the sale or lease of property…used by the defendant primarily for personal, family, or household purpose.” (CCP § 483.010(c).) There is no indication that Defendant intended to use the property primarily for personal, family, or household purposes and during Defendant’s deposition, he admitted he owed Plaintiff money because they “lent money or transferred other obligations” and he has admitted that he has over fifty years of experience in real estate development. (See Enenstein Decl., Exh. 1.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s claim meets the statutory requirements upon which attachment may be issued.

 

Purpose and Amount of Attachment

 

To obtain a writ of attachment, Plaintiff must prove that the attachment is not sought for any purpose other than to secure recovery on the claim. (CCP § 484.090.) Here, Counsel testifies the application is brought solely to secure recovery of monies owed to Plaintiff under the Note and Amendment to the Note that are the subject of Plaintiff’s contractual claim against Defendant Norman J. Kravetz. (Enenstein Decl. ¶2.) The court also notes the total amount sought to be secured is $872,708.90, which is evidently greater than zero.

 

Defendant opposes the amount sought on the grounds it includes a fee request. Defendant contends Plaintiff rationalizes he should be entitled to $25,689.12 in fees for “this application only” but the prerequisite for this rule is that Plaintiff received a judgment and was therefore entitled to use the fee schedule to determine “reasonable fees”. Defendant further contends any request for attorney fees is subject to a proper notice.

 

The court agrees at least that the “fixed or readily ascertainable amount” excludes attorney fees. However, Section 483.015 allows costs and allowable attorney fees to be included in a plaintiff’s application for a right to attach order and writ of attachment.

 

Probable Validity of Claims

 

“A claim has ‘probable validity’ where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.”  (CCP § 481.190.)  In determining this issue, the court must consider the relative merits of the positions of the respective parties.  (Kemp Bros. Construction, Inc. v. Titan Electric Corp. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1474, 1484.)  The court does not determine whether the claim is actually valid; that determination will be made at trial and is not affected by the decision on the application for the order.  (CCP § 484.050(b).)

 

Plaintiff contends they can sufficiently establish probable validity of their claim. The application is based on a claim for breach of contract. A claim for breach of contract requires a showing of the following elements: (1) the existence of a contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damages to plaintiff therefrom.  (Acoustics, Inc. v. Trepte Construction Co. (1971) 14 Cal.App.3d 887, 913.)

 

Plaintiff argues the evidence clearly demonstrates that Plaintiff is more likely than not to obtain a judgement against Kravetz for its breach of contract claim because Kravetz agreed to and entered the terms of the Note and Amendment to Note and has defaulted on the note by failing to make timely payments. (Wazana Decl., ¶¶ 5-22.) Kravetz admitted in his deposition that he agreed to the Note in favor of Plaintiff for $1,200,000.00 and the Note and Amendment to Note show Kravetz signed them. (Enenstein Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exh. 1 at 21:20-23.) Kravetz admitted that he received the $1,200,000.00 from Plaintiff under the Note. (Enenstein Decl., Exh. 1 at 28:23-29:10). Kravetz then made some payments on the Note, but then by failing to make timely payments thereto. (Enenstein Decl., ¶¶ 6-7, Exh. 1 at 30:1-4, 17-20; Wazana Decl., ¶ 14.) After defaulting on the Note, Kravetz then agreed to the Amended Note, whereby Kravetz agreed to pay a total balance of $776,985.55 with 10% interest. (See Enenstein Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. 1 at 28:23-29:10; Wazana Decl., ¶¶ 16-17, Exh. 7.) It also cannot be disputed that Kravetz defaulted on the Note and Promissory Note. (See Enenstein Decl., Exh. 7, 25:17-24.)

 

Under these circumstances, Plaintiff’s evidence shows the following: (1) a contract exists between the parties by way of the Note and Amendment to Note agreements; (2) plaintiff performed under the promissory notes by funding the required payment; (3) Defendant breached his obligations under the Note and Amendment to Note agreements by failing to make the necessary payments; and (4) Plaintiff was damaged as a result.  While defendant argues that he has only recently defaulted and that he has had a good history of payments, he does not present evidence to counter plaintiff’s right to an attachment order.  Rather, he focuses on exemptions to the attachment order, which he requests the court to consider.   

 

Exemption

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 485.220(a)(4) requires that “the property sought to be attached, or the portion thereof to be specified in the writ, is not exempt from attachment.” Here, Plaintiff seeks to attach Kravetz’ interests in the Business Center Property, the Vanalden Property, any securities, any stocks, and any bonds as well as attach any interest in money on premises where a trade or business is conducted, plus money in excess of $1,000 located elsewhere or in a deposit account. (See AT-125.) Counsel for Plaintiff attests he is informed and believes that no assets sought to be attached are exempt. (Enenstein Decl., ¶ 16.)

 

Defendant filed an opposition arguing that the property listed is exempt from attachment. Specifically, Plaintiff seeks to attach 4535 Vanalden Avenue, Tarzana, 91356 family home, which is subject to exemption from attachment. Moreover, Plaintiff seeks to attach the commercial property located at 19350 Business Center Drive, Northridge, 91324, but this property is not owned by Defendant.

 

In Reply, Plaintiff states it will no longer seek to attach the Business center Property since it learned that Kravetz no longer owns it. However, Plaintiff contends the Vanalden Property is still subject to attachment, and even if the homestead exemption applies, it only applies to at most $669,474.83 of the value of the home. Defendant disputes this amount, arguing that the proper calculation of the cost of living adjustment allowed increases the exemption to $705,107.  The court agrees with plaintiff that its interpretation of the statutory increase for inflation, more closely tracks the intent of the statute, entitling defendant to a homestead exemption of $669,474.83. 

 

Kravetz’s claimed “necessities of life” exemptions for deposit accounts should be denied outright since he has failed to provide any financial statement as is required by Code of Civil Procedure section 703.530. Plaintiff is not seeking to attach personal property, wages, and social security.  

 

 

Undertaking

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 489.210 requires the plaintiff to file an undertaking before issuance of a writ of attachment.  Code of Civil Procedure section 489.220 provides, with exceptions, for an undertaking in the amount of $10,000.

 

Plaintiff does not dispute the $10,000.00 amount (See AT-125, Sec. 2(h).)

 

Accordingly, the application is GRANTED in the amount of $872,708.90, which includes costs and fees.  Plaintiff is ordered to post an undertaking in the amount of $10,000.00.


Case Number: 24VECV02842    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: W

RICHARD TAVITA vs MILLBRAE CARE CENTER, LLC

 

MOTION TO CHANGE VENUE

 

Date of Hearing:          December 16, 2024                Trial Date:       N/A

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         24VECV02842

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Millbrae Care Center, LLC dba Millbrae Care Center

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Richard Tavita

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff Richard Tavita (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint alleging injuries suffered while residing in Defendant Millbrae Care Center, LLC dba Millbrae Care Center’s (“Defendant”) skilled nursing facility. Plaintiff alleges he is a dependent adult, and was a resident of County of San Mateo, State of the California. Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s facility was located in Sherman Oaks, California.  The Complaint alleges Plaintiff suffered from various pressure injuries, sepsis, and other conditions under Defendant’s care, where Defendant failed to prevent and treat such conditions. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Dependent Adult Abuse (Pursuant to Welfare & Institutions Code § 15600, et. seq.), 2) Negligence, and 3) Violation of Residents’ Rights (Pursuant to Health and Safety Code §1430(b)). 

 

Defendant now moves to transfer venue in this matter to San Mateo County Superior Court. Plaintiff opposes and Defendant replies.

 

The hearing for this motion was originally set for November 5, 2024, but was continued multiple times to the present date. (See Min. Order, 10/15/24, 11/14/24.)

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Motion to Change Venue is DENIED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 395(a), in an action for personal injuries, the “superior court in either the county where the injury occurs or the injury causing death occurs or the county where the defendants, or some of them reside at the commencement of the action, is a proper court for the trial of the action.” 

 

“Venue is determined based on the complaint on file at the time the motion to change venue is made. [Citations.]” (Dow AgroSciences, LLC v. Superior Court (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 1067, 1076.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 397, the discretionary venue statute, provides that “[t]he court may, on motion, change the place of trial . . . (a) [w]hen the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court” and “(c) [w]hen the convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice would the promoted by the change.”

 

The burden is on the moving party to establish facts justifying the transfer.¿ (Mission Imports, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1982) 31 Cal.3d 921, 928.)¿Where a party moves to transfer venue based on the convenience of witnesses, it must submit affidavits showing the names of each witness expected to testify for both parties, the substance of their expected testimony, whether the witness has been deposed or given a statement, the reasons why it would be inconvenient for the witnesses to appear, and the reasons why the ends of justice would be promoted by transfer to a different county.¿ (Juneau v. Juneau (1941) 45 Cal.App.2d 14, 16; Edwards v. Pierson (1957) 156 Cal.App.2d 72, 75 [“The burden rests upon one who seeks a change of venue . . . to prove that both the convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice will be promoted thereby . . . This he must do through affidavits which contain something more than generalities and conclusions”].) ¿ 

 

“ ‘Convenience of witnesses is shown by the fact that the residence of all the witnesses is in the county to which the transfer of the cause is requested. [Citation.] A conclusion that the ends of justice are promoted can be drawn from the fact that by moving the trial closer to the residence of the witnesses, delay and expense in court proceedings are avoided and savings in the witnesses’ time and expenses are effected.’ [Citation.] ‘A motion for a change of the place of trial on the ground that the convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice would be promoted by the change is committed to the sound discretion of the trial court and its determination will not be disturbed on appeal unless it clearly appears, as a matter of law, that there has been an abuse of such discretion.’ [Citations.]” (Rycz v. Superior Court of San Francisco County (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 824, 837.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Motion to Transfer  

 

Defendant argues the case should be transferred to San Mateo County in the interests of justice and for the convenience of non-party witnesses. Defendant argues Millbrae Care Center is located in San Mateo County and its staff members and leadership live in San Mateo County or nearby counties. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, 4.) Millbrae Care Center does not provide care to its patients in Los Angeles County, and no staff members live in Los Angeles County. (Ibid.) Defendant argues that the specified employees have treated Plaintiff and will testify to the issues in this case.  

 

Plaintiff argues that the venue is proper under Code of Civil Procedure section 395, subdivision (a). Plaintiff argues Defendant’s “residence” is its principal place of business located at 13345 Ventura Boulevard Los Angeles, California 91423, within Los Angeles County. Further, Plaintiff argues Defendant failed to provide the required factual showing for motions under Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c).  

 

A more extensive factual showing is required for motions for venue transfer based on the “convenience of witnesses and the ends of justice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 397, subd. (c).) Declarations must contain admissible evidence, not hearsay and conclusions. (Lieppman v. Lieber (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 914, 919; Tutor-Saliba-Perini Joint Venture v. Sup.Ct. (San Diego Unified Port Dist.) (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 736, 744.) “The affidavits in support of the motion for change of venue on this ground must set forth the names of the witnesses, the nature of the testimony expected from each, and the reasons why the attendance of each would be inconvenient.” (Peiser v. Mettler (1958) 50 Cal.2d 594, 607.)

 

The Court finds venue is proper as filed because Defendant’s principal place of business is in Los Angeles County. (Peck Decl., ¶ 3.) The Court further finds that Defendant has failed to make the required factual showing for motions to transfer venue under on Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c).  

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiff that Defendant’s declaration of Abby Ma, Administrator of Defendant, fails to meet the requirements set forth by Juneau v. Juneau (1941) 45 Cal.App.2d 14, 16 and Peiser v. Mettler. The declaration identifies certain employees but fails to state the substance of their expected testimony, whether they have been deposed or have given a statement, the reasons why it would be inconvenient to testify in Los Angeles County, and why “ends of justice” would be promoted by transfer to a different county. (See ibid.) Neither does the declaration of Defendant’s counsel, Richard M. Acosta, meet these requirements. Even if it did, declarations from a party’s counsel regarding the convenience of the witnesses are usually hearsay or conclusions. (Rycz v. Sup.Ct. (McGarry) (2022) 81 CA5th 824, 846, fn. 22.)  

 

Furthermore, for the purposes of determining whether to grant a motion for change of venue under Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c), a court need not give consideration as to the convenience of witnesses who are employees of the litigants, unless the employee is called to testify by the opposing party. (See  

J.C. Millett Co. v. Latchford-Marble Glass Co.¿(1959) 167 Cal.App.2d 218, 227.) Here, in support of its motion, Defendant speaks to the convenience of its own employees. There is no indication Plaintiff seeks to call these employees to testify. Thus, Defendant has failed to make the required factual showing for its Motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 397, subdivision (c). 

 

Order for Expenses and Fees 

 

Defendant requests for attorney’s fees and costs under Code of Civil Procedure section 396b, subdivision (b). However, the Court finds sanctions are unwarranted where Defendant was unsuccessful in its Motion to Transfer. The Court thus denies Defendant’s request for attorney’s fees and costs.  

 

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Court DENIES the Motion to Transfer Venue and Request for Sanctions. 

 

 

Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay

 

Date of Hearing:          December 16, 2024                Trial Date:       N/A     

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         24VECV02842

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Millbrae Care Center, LLC

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Richard Tavita

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action for dependent adult abuse by a long-term nursing facility. On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff Richard Tavita (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Millbrae Care Center (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 200. The Complaint alleges Plaintiff suffered from various pressure injuries, sepsis, and other conditions under Defendant’s care, where Defendant failed to prevent and treat such conditions. The Complaint alleges Defendant was understaffed and failed to adequately train its staff, resulting in Plaintiff’s neglect. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Dependent Adult Abuse (Pursuant to Welfare & Institutions Code § 15600, et. seq.), 2) Negligence, and 3) Violation of Residents’ Rights (Pursuant to Health and Safety Code §1430(b)).

 

On September 9, 2024, Defendant filed this Motion to Compel Arbitration pursuant to arbitration clauses (the “Arbitration Clauses”) Plaintiff purportedly signed with his admission agreement (the “Admission Agreement”) on March 23, 2023. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2-4, Exhs. A-C.)

 

On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed the opposition.

 

On November 5, 2024, Defendant filed the reply.

 

The hearing for this motion was set for November 14, 2024. However, after conferring with Counsel, the Court directed further briefing on the Serafin case, Civil Code section 3388, and the lack of mutual assent to the arbitration agreement. (Min. Order, 11/14/24.) The briefing was ordered to be no more than 7-pages and filed by 11/27/24. Any opposition was ordered to be filed no later than 12/6/24.

 

On November 26, 2024, Defendant filed its supplemental brief. On December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a supplemental opposition.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay is DENIED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; see also Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿ 

 

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)¿¿

 

ANALYSIS

 

Existence of a Valid Agreement

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, Arbitration Clauses are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

 

“With respect to the moving party’s burden to provide evidence of the¿existence¿of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court. (See¿Condee v. Longwood Management Corp.¿(2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218, 105 Cal.Rptr.2d 597 (Condee¿); see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference”].) Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges. [Citation]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC¿(2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.)

 

i.                    Plaintiff’s Capacity

 

Plaintiff argues that he lacked capacity to enter into the Arbitration Clauses when he signed them, rending them invalid. Defendant argues that Plaintiff has provided no evidence supporting his incapacity.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant that Plaintiff has failed to prove he lacked contractual capacity at the time he signed the Arbitration Clauses. As support, Plaintiff merely attached the Complaint. Such allegations are not evidence. The Arbitration Clauses thus may not be invalidated based on lack of capacity. Nevertheless, the Court finds the DRP Agreement and Arbitration are invalid on other grounds.

 

ii.                   Mutual Assent

 

Defendant attaches the Admission Agreement and separate Arbitration Clauses and asserts Plaintiff signed all documents on March 23, 2023. (Ma Decl., ¶¶ 2-4, Exhs. A-C.) Plaintiff argues Defendant did not sign the Dispute Resolution Program Agreement or the incorporated Arbitration Clauses, and thus, there was no mutual assent, rendering them invalid.

 

The Court finds that while Defendant has presented a copy of the Arbitration Clauses, the parties did not mutually assent to arbitration because the DPR Agreement was not signed by Plaintiff.

 

The Court notes that, following the Admissions Agreement, there is a Dispute Resolution Program (DRP) Agreement that incorporates the two Arbitration Clauses. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A (DRP Agreement is page 73 to 86 of the Motion).) The two Arbitration Clauses are for 1) arbitrations relating to medical malpractice and 2) for claims other than medical malpractice. The Arbitration Clauses are incorporated in the DRP Agreement and also attached as Exhibits B and C to the Motion. (Ma Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exhs. B, C.)

 

At issue in the November 14, 2024 hearing for this motion was the legal effect of Defendant’s failure to sign the DRP and Arbitration Clauses. Plaintiff argued that this invalidated the agreement. (Opp., 4-5.) In its supplemental brief, Defendant argues that its failure to sign the agreement does not void the agreement under Serafin v. Balco Properties Ltd., LLC (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 165 and Civil Code section 3388.[1] In Serafin, the Court of Appeal rejected the argument that an arbitration agreement was invalid because there was no signature from the drafter. (Serafin, supra, 235 Cal.App.4th at 176.) Instead, the court noted that evidence of an arbitration agreement’s existence could be based on conduct implying ratification or acceptance. (Id.) In Serafin, the drafter showed intent to be bound by the agreement when it invoked the arbitration process and moved to stay the litigation and compel arbitration. (Id. at 176-77 [“Just as with any written agreement signed by one party, an arbitration agreement can be specifically enforced against the signing party regardless of whether the party seeking enforcement has also signed, provided that the party seeking enforcement has performed or offered to do so.”].)[2]

 

Plaintiff relies on Romo v. Y-3 Holdings, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1153, to argue that Defendant needed to sign the agreement because it contained lines for its signature. However, Romo involved an arbitration agreement within an Employee Handbook where neither the employee nor employer had signed the area immediately following the arbitration agreement. Based on the differing language in the arbitration section and the fact that the arbitration section and remaining sections had spaces for separate signatures, the Court of Appeal found they were separate and severable agreements. (Id. at 1159.) Nowhere does Romo foreclose the rule described in Serafin or Civil Code section 3388 that an agreement can be valid even without a signature from the enforcer.  

 

Based on Serafin and Civil Code section 3388, the Court concludes that Defendant was not required to also sign the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses. Plaintiff sets forth no evidence or argument that Defendant has not performed its duties under the Agreement.

 

Instead, Plaintiff focuses on the fact that the DRP Agreement was not signed at all. Here, the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses are separate from the Admissions Agreement. Article 1 of the DRP Agreement clearly states: “By entering into this DRP Agreement and one or both of the attached arbitration clauses, both parties give up their constitutional right to have any such dispute decided in a court of law before a jury, and instead accept the use of mediation or ultimately arbitration.” (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 73 [emphasis added].) The first Arbitration Clause also contains the following provision: “By entering into this DRP Agreement and arbitration clause, both parties give up their constitutional right to have any such dispute decided in a court of law before a jury . . . .” (Ma Decl., Exh. B [emphasis added].) Plaintiff argues the conjunctive “and”, suggests that both the DRP Agreement and the Arbitration Clauses needed to be signed.

 

“In interpreting contracts, ‘[t]he whole of a contract is to be taken together, so as to give effect to every part, if reasonably practicable, each clause helping to interpret the other.’ (Civ.Code, § 1641.) Additionally: ‘In the construction of a statute or instrument, the office of the judge is simply to ascertain and declare what is in terms or in substance contained therein, not to insert what has been omitted, or to omit what has been inserted; and where there are several provisions or particulars, such a construction is, if possible, to be adopted as will give effect to all.’ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1858.)” (Romo, supra, 87 Cal.App.4th at 1158-59.)

 

The Court notes that the Arbitration Clause pertaining to Medical Malpractice Disputes contains the following provision: “By signing this arbitration clause below, the Resident agrees to be bound by the foregoing arbitration provisions. The Resident acknowledges that he or she has the option of not signing this DRP Agreement and arbitration clause and not being bound by the arbitration provisions contained herein.” (Ma Decl., Exh. B.) The second Arbitration Clause pertaining to disputes other than medical malpractice, also references the DRP Agreement, by stating: “By entering into this arbitration agreement, both parties give up their constitutional right to have any such dispute decided in a court of law before a jury, and instead accept the use of arbitration as set forth in this DRP Agreement.” (Exh. C.)

 

However, based on the language within the DRP Agreement that separates the Agreement and Arbitration Clauses as two separate instruments, the Court concludes that Plaintiff needed to sign the DRP Agreement. Because it was not signed, Defendant has failed to show that there was mutual assent.

 

Although the inquiry would generally end when the arbitration agreement is found invalid, the Court will continue to evaluate the parties’ arguments regarding enforceability.

 

Enforceability

 

            The parties dispute whether the Arbitration Clauses are enforceable under Title 42 Code of Federal Regulations section 483.70, subdivision (n) (now subdivision (m)) and Health & Safety Code section 1599.81, which both govern arbitration agreements by long-term nursing facilities. The Court will address each in turn.

 

i.                    Title 42 Code of Federal Regulations section 483.70, subdivision (m)

 

Title 42 Code of Federal Regulations section 483.70 governs requirements for institutions to participate as nursing facilities in the Medicare and Medicaid programs. (42 C.F.R. § 483.1, subd. (b).) Subdivision (m) sets forth requirements for arbitration clauses for such facilities, including, but not limited to the following: the arbitration cannot be condition of admission, the arbitration agreement must be explained to resident in manner he or she understands, the resident acknowledges he or she understands the agreement, and the arbitration agreement must provide for a neutral arbitrator, provide for selection of venue agreed upon by both parties, and must explicitly grant the resident the right to rescind within 30 days of signing. (42 C.F.R. § 483.70, subd. (m).)

 

Plaintiff argues that the declaration of Abby Ma in support of the Motion fails to state that Defendant complied with these requirements as to Plaintiff specifically, not just as to all patients. Defendant argues the DRP Agreement and the Arbitration Clauses comply with these requirements.

 

The Court finds that the requirements of 42 C.F.R. section 483.70, subdivision (m) are likely met. The DRP Agreement states in multiple places that signing the agreement is not a precondition for admission to Defendant’s facility. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 73-74.) Further, the DRP Agreement states it may be revoked within 30 days of signing and provides for a neutral arbitrator and mutual venue selection. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 73-78.) Defendant has also provided evidence that during the admissions process, Defendant allows their residents the opportunity to review the agreements prior to signing them, explains the documents during the process, and answers questions. (Ma Decl., ¶ 5) Residents are also advised that the Arbitration Clauses are voluntary and not a precondition to admission. (Ibid.) The Court thus finds that the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses do not run afoul of 42 C.F.R. section 483.70, subdivision (m) as to render them unenforceable.

 

ii.                   Health & Safety Code section 1599.81, subdivision (d)

 

Plaintiff argues its Health & Safety Code section 1430, subdivision (b) cause of action may not be compelled to arbitration pursuant to Health & Safety Code section 1599.81, subdivision (d). Defendant argues that the Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) applies to the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses, and the FAA preempts Health & Safety Code sections 1430 and 1599.81 to the extent they limit arbitration.

 

Health & Safety Code section 1430, subdivision (b)(C) states the following: “An agreement by a resident or patient of a skilled nursing facility or intermediate care facility to waive that resident’s or patient’s rights to sue pursuant to this subdivision is void as contrary to public policy.” Health & Safety Code section 1599.81, subdivision (d) states the following: “In the event the contract contains an arbitration clause, the contract attachment pertaining to arbitration shall contain notice that under Section 1430, the patient may not waive his or her ability to sue for violation of the Patient's Bill of Rights.”

 

The Court finds that DRP Agreement explicitly states the FAA applies. (Ma Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A, p. 80.) The Court further finds that Health & Safety Code sections 1430 and 1599.81 are preempted by the FAA to the extent they prevent arbitration. (See Valley View Health Care, Inc. v. Chapman (E.D. Cal. 2014) 992 F.Supp.2d 1016, 1040–1041 [“[Section 1430, subdivision (b)(C)] effectively ‘prohibits outright arbitration’ of Patient Bill of Rights claims and as a ‘conflicting rule is displaced by the FAA.’ [Citation.] [Section 1430, subdivision (b)(C)] violates and is preempted by the FAA as an obstacle to accomplishment and execution of FAA purposes. . . . Section[] 1599.81(d) fall[s] as contrary to the FAA to the extent that [it] require[s] notice of the bar on arbitration of Patient's Bill of Rights claims.”].) Thus, while the DRP Agreement and the Arbitration Clause on medical malpractice claims contain a section 1599.81 notice, the Court finds Plaintiff’s section 1430 cause of action arbitrable due to FAA preemption. The Court finds Plaintiff’s argument insufficient to render the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses unenforceable.

 

            The Court does not address other issues of enforceability because no other issues were raised by the parties. The Court finds that, based on the parties’ arguments, the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses are not unenforceable on Plaintiff’s asserted grounds. Yet the DRP Agreement and Arbitration Clauses are still invalid due to a lack of mutual assent—there is no valid agreement to enforce. The Court thus denies the Motion to Compel.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay is DENIED.
