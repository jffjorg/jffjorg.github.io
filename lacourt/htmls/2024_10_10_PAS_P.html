DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21GDCV00585    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF CREDITORS ADJUSTMENT BUREAU INC.’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES (SET FOUR)

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is breach of contract action arising from a written agreement between State Compensation Insurance Fund (“Assignor” or “SCIF”) and Defendants Bedrock Group, Inc. a/k/a Bedrock Group Inc. (“Bedrock”), Frontiers Group, Inc. a/k/a Frontiers Group Inc. (“Frontiers”) (collectively, “Defendants”), wherein Assignor agreed to provide workers compensation insurance to Defendants in exchange for payment of premiums for the periods of 3/30/18-3/30/19 and 3/30/19-11/7/19. Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive on April 27, 2021, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Open Book Account; (3) Account Stated; and (4) Reasonable Value.

 

          Plaintiff alleges SCIF assigned its rights to the agreement between SCIF and Defendants for payments of premiums. (Compl., ¶¶1, 4, 11.) Defendants accepted workers compensation insurance policies bearing Policy Nos. 9156407-18 (“First Policy”) and 9156407-19 (“Second Policy”) (collectively, “Policies”), which provided for payment of premiums. (Id. at ¶12.) Defendants breached the First Policy by failing to pay the premiums leaving a balance of $160,494.25. (Id. at ¶14.) Defendants breached the Second Policy by failing to submit to and allow SCIF to access the records of Defendants’ business operations and payroll for purposes of conducting an audit, thus the estimated balance owed is $739.56. (Id. at ¶15.) The interest rate from the Due Date on the Policies is ten (10 percent per annum. (Id.)

 

          On June 22, 2021, Defendant Bedrock filed an Answer.

 

          On March 11, 2024, Defendant Bedrock’s Counsel Peter Frederick Lindborg filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. The motion was taken off calendar on May 25, 2024.

 

          On April 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set Four). On June 21, 2024, Defendant Bedrock filed an opposition. No reply has been filed.

 

          The jury trial is set for December 3, 2024.

 

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civil Procedure Section 2030.300 governs motions to compel further responses to interrogatories. It provides as follows:

“(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

Motions must be made within 45 days of any response or supplemental response (Subdivision (c)) and must be accompanied by a declaration demonstrating the parties met and conferred to attempt informal resolution; they must also be accompanied by a separate statement or concise outline. (Subdivision (b).)

 

On the 45-day limit: Golf & Pro Shop, Inc. v. Superior Court (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 127

 

A responding party is under a statutory obligation to provide responses that are “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.220, subd. (a).) Further, “If only part of an interrogatory is objectionable, the remainder of the interrogatory shall be answered,” and “the specific ground for the objection shall be set forth clearly in the response.” (Code Civ. Proc. §2030.240, subds. (a)-(b), italics added.) That is, to be code-compliant, a response should state all available information available and, if there is additional responsive information that is being withheld based on a privilege, it should so state and identify the privilege under which information is being withheld. Prefacing a substantive response with the words, “subject to and without waiving” all objections is not helpful in terms of understanding whether additional information is being withheld based on a privilege, and which privilege is at issue.

           

III.     ANALYSIS

          On October 3, 2023, Plaintiff served its Fourth Set of Special Interrogatories (“SROGs”) on Defendant Bedrock. (Frischer Decl., ¶2, Ex. 1.) The parties engaged in meet and confer process pertaining to these discovery requests, which included extensions for supplemental responses. (Id. at ¶¶3-6, Exs. 2-5.) On February 5, 2024, Defendant Bedrock’s counsel indicated he would be filing a motion to withdraw as counsel and Plaintiff granted a final extension to provide supplemental responses. (Id. at ¶7, Ex. 6.) Plaintiff avers Defendant Bedrock’s counsel failed to provide any supplemental responses or respond to the final meet and confer efforts. (Id. at ¶8, Ex. 7.)

 

          Plaintiff now moves for an order compelling Defendant Bedrock to provide further responses SROGs Nos. 60 through 210. The following interrogatories seek information pertaining to the check number and amount paid to Defendant Bedrock’s employees; Defendant Bedrock’s contractors’ license numbers; duties and responsibilities of Defendant Bedrock’s contractors from the time period of the Policies. Plaintiff asserts the information sought by these requests are narrowly tailored to ascertain where Defendant Bedrock disagrees with either the auditing or invoicing of the 2018 audited policy and to obtain precise, relevant, and necessary information Defendant Bedrock relies upon in support thereof. Furthermore, Plaintiff argues the discovery requested will allow Assignor SCIF to proceed with the required audit of the Second Policy.

 

In response to these requests, Defendant Bedrock stated:

“Responding Party objects to this Interrogatory on the grounds that it is overbroad, vague, ambiguous, argumentative, is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, and invades the privacy rights of third parties. In particular, the matters at issue in this action have been the subject of an ongoing audit which has been, and is continuing to be, conducted by Propounding Party’s assignor since early 2020 and, during the course of the audit, the scope and number of potential issues between the parties has been narrowed. During the course of that audit, Responding party has produced voluminous documents to Propounding Party’s assignor including, but not limited to, the documents from which the information requested in this  interrogatory can be derived. As the answer to this interrogatory would necessitate the preparation or the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of or from the documents of previously sent by Responding Party to Propounding Party’s assignor including, but not limited to Responding Party’s payroll register, and the burden or expense of preparing or making such compilation, abstract, audit, or summary would be substantially the same for the Propounding Party as for Responding Party, Responding Party directs Propounding to the foregoing documents pursuant to the provisions of CCP § 2030.230, which is incorporated herein by this reference.”

 

In opposition, Defendant Bedrock asserts the present motion seeks an audit by litigation. More specifically, Defendant Bedrock argues no good cause exists for the present motion because Defendant Bedrock has already supplied SCIF with information which responds to every single one of the discovery requests which are the subject of the instant motion during the completed 2018 and 2019 audits.

 

The Court finds that supplemental responses are warranted. First, the requests at issue are not overbroad, vague, and/or ambiguous because they are limited to the time period of the Policies and narrowly tailored to information about specifically named employees and contractors of Defendant Bedrock. Furthermore, Defendant Bedrock makes no argument that it cannot provide an intelligible response to such requests. In fact, Defendant Bedrock simply contends it has provided the information requested to Assignor SCIF already. Moreover, the fact that the information and/or documentation requested by Plaintiff is purportedly available from Assignor SCIF does not relieve Defendant Bedrock from turning the information over “if the information is less easily available from the other source than it is from the party.” ([8:1023] Responding to Interrogatories:, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 8F-5; see also Bunnell v. Superior Court (California Life Ins. Co.) (1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 720, 723-724.) Eventhough Plaintiff does not assert the information is less easily available from Assignor SCIF, the moving papers suggest that Assignor SCIF does not have the information and/or documentation sought in these requests as they keep asking Plaintiff for additional information and documentation to complete the 2019 audit of the Second Policy. (Plf. Sep. Stmt. at 11:22-12:1-9.)

In addition, objections for being “overbroad,” “burdensome,” or “oppressive” are only valid when the responding party is asserting an undue burden based on the “quantum of work required” to satisfy the production. (West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) Defendant Bedrock merely argues the documents are voluminous and an unnecessary expense. Finally, the parties do not dispute initial premiums are based on an estimate of factors and actual earned premiums are determined after the workers compensation policy expires and an audit of the insured’s operation and payroll records is completed. (Mot. at 12:15-19; Opp’n at 2:10-21.) As such, the checks paid to Defendant Bedrock’s employees and contractors, and amount of those checks are reasonable calculated to lead to admissible evidence pertaining to what Defendant Bedrock’s actual earned premiums were for the Second Policy, which Plaintiff argues Defendant Bedrock refuses to provide and SCIF claims it does not have to complete the 2019 audit. (Mot. at 3:20-24; Plf. Sep. Stmt. at 11:22-12:1-9.)

 

Therefore, Defendant Bedrock’s responses to SROG Nos. 60 through 210 are insufficient.

 

 

 

Monetary Sanctions

Code Civil Procedure Section 2030.300, subdivision (d) provides for the issuance of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the court finds the party who would be subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make sanctions unjust.

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel of record, Michael D. Frischer seeks $2,311.95 in sanctions against Defendant Bedrock for (1) 3.5 hours reviewing Defendant Bedrock’s responses, researching and drafting the detailed initial meet and confer, subsequent emails, drafting the instant motion and separate statement; (2) 1.0 hour preparing and for appearance at the hearing at an hourly rate of $500.00; and (3) filing fee for $61.95. (Frischer Decl., ¶¶9-11.) Counsel Frischer’s request is reasonable based on his 30 years of litigation experience and comparable rates set forth in  National Association of Legal Fee Analysis. (Id.) Moreover, Defendant Bedrock did not act with substantial justification by providing meritless objections to the discovery requests at issue as discussed above. Further, Defendant Bedrock does not argue any other circumstance to support the notion that imposing sanctions would be unjust. Therefore, the Court awards Plaintiff sanction in the amount of $2,311.95.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set Four).

 

Defendant Bedrock Group, Inc. is ordered to provide verified supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories (Set Four) Nos. 60 through 210 within 30 days of this order. Defendant is further ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $2,311.95 within 30 days of this order.

 

          Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau Inc. to give notice of this order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF CREDITORS ADJUSTMENT BUREAU INC.’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (SET FOUR)

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is breach of contract action arising from a written agreement between State Compensation Insurance Fund (“Assignor” or “SCIF”) and Defendants Bedrock Group, Inc. a/k/a Bedrock Group Inc. (“Bedrock”), Frontiers Group, Inc. a/k/a Frontiers Group Inc. (“Frontiers”) (collectively, “Defendants”), wherein Assignor agreed to provide workers compensation insurance to Defendants in exchange for payment of premiums for the periods of 3/30/18-3/30/19 and 3/30/19-11/7/19. Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive on April 27, 2021, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Open Book Account; (3) Account Stated; and (4) Reasonable Value.

 

          Plaintiff alleges SCIF assigned its rights to the agreement between SCIF and Defendants for payments of premiums. (Compl., ¶¶1, 4, 11.) Defendants accepted workers compensation insurance policies bearing Policy Nos. 9156407-18 (“First Policy”) and 9156407-19 (“Second Policy”) (collectively, “Policies”), which provided for payment of premiums. (Id. at ¶12.) Defendants breached the First Policy by failing to pay the premiums leaving a balance of $160,494.25. (Id. at ¶14.) Defendants breached the Second Policy by failing to submit to and allow SCIF to access the records of Defendants’ business operations and payroll for purposes of conducting an audit, thus the estimated balance owed is $739.56. (Id. at ¶15.) The interest rate from the Due Date on the Policies is ten (10 percent per annum. (Id.)

 

          On June 22, 2021, Defendant Bedrock filed an Answer.

 

          On March 11, 2024, Defendant Bedrock’s Counsel Peter Frederick Lindborg filed a Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. The motion was taken off calendar on May 25, 2024.

 

          On April 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents (Set Four). On June 21, 2024, Defendant Bedrock filed an opposition. No reply has been filed.

 

          The jury trial is set for December 3, 2024.

 

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          A party responding to requests for inspection must either provide a statement of compliance, represent that it lacks the ability to comply, or object. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210.)

          - If a party responds with a statement of compliance, the statement must indicate whether production will be allowed in whole or in part and that all documents in the responding party’s possession, custody or control to which no objection is being made will be included in the production. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.220.)

          - If a party responds that a particular demand cannot be complied with it must include a representation that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry have been made and that the inability to comply is because the item has never existed, has been lost, stolen, or misplaced, or has never been in or is no longer in the responding party’s possession, custody, or control; such response must also identify the name and address of any person or entity known or believed to have possession, custody, or control of the item or category of item.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.)

          - If a response includes objections, a privilege log identifying documents being withheld must be provided. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240.)

          When a party propounding demands for inspection deems responses to the responses to be incomplete or evasive, or deems objections to be without merit, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses. Such motion must set forth facts showing good cause for the discovery, be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, and include a separate statement. Such motion must also be made within 45 days of verified responses or supplemental responses, or on or before any specific later date the parties have agreed to in writing. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subds. (a)-(c).)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

          On October 3, 2023, Plaintiff served its Fourth Demand for Identification, Production, Inspection and Copying of Documents and Other Things (“Request for Production of Documents” or “RFPs”) on Defendant Bedrock. (Frischer Decl., ¶2, Ex. 1.) The parties engaged in meet and confer process pertaining to these discovery requests, which included extensions for supplemental responses. (Id. at ¶¶3-6, Exs. 2-5.) On February 5, 2024, Defendant Bedrock’s counsel indicated he would be filing a motion to withdraw as counsel and Plaintiff granted a final extension to provide supplemental responses. (Id. at ¶7, Ex. 6.) Plaintiff avers Defendant Bedrock’s counsel failed to provide any supplemental responses or respond to the final meet and confer efforts. (Id. at ¶8, Ex. 7.)

          Plaintiff now moves for an order compelling Defendant Bedrock to provide further responses and to produce documents for Request for Production of Documents (“RFP”) Nos. 57 through 190. The following requests seek Defendant Bedrock’s employees timecards/payroll records and checks issued to employees during the periods the Policies were in effective. Plaintiff contends the requested information and documents are narrowly tailored to ascertain where Defendant Bedrock disagrees with either the auditing or invoicing of the 2018 audited policy and to obtain the documentation Defendant Bedrock relies upon in support thereof. Furthermore, Plaintiff argues the discovery requested will allow Assignor SCIF to proceed with the required audit of the Second Policy.

 

In response to these requests, Defendant Bedrock stated:

“Responding Party objects to this Demand on the grounds that it is overbroad, vague, ambiguous, argumentative, is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, purports to require the production of documents which are reasonably available to the Propounding Party through other means which would not subject Responding Party to unreasonable annoyance, oppression, burden and expense. In particular, the matters at issue in this action have been the subject of an ongoing audit which has been, and is continuing to be, conducted by Propounding Party’s assignor since early 2020 and, during the course of the audit, the scope and number of potential issues between the parties has been narrowed. During the course of that audit, Responding Party has produced voluminous documents to Propounding Party’s assignor including, but not limited to, the documents described in this specific request. Thus, not only are the documents requested [equally] available to Propounding Party, requiring [Responding] party to produce them at this point is not only unduly burdensome, but also exceeds the scope of discovery because the documents requested have no bearing, directly or indirectly, on any of the issues in dispute between the parties which required resolution before the finalization of the audit and therefore, the request is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”

 

In opposition, Defendant Bedrock asserts the present motion seeks an audit by litigation. More specifically, Defendant Bedrock argues no good cause exists for the present motion because Defendant Bedrock has already supplied SCIF with information which responds to every single one of the discovery requests which are the subject of the instant motion during the 2018 and 2019 audits.

 

The Court finds that supplemental responses are warranted. First, the requests at issue are not overbroad, vague, and/or ambiguous because they are limited to the time period of the Policies and narrowly tailored to information about specifically named employees of Defendant Bedrock. Moreover, Defendant Bedrock makes no argument that it cannot provide an intelligible response to such requests. Next, the fact that the information and/or documentation requested by Plaintiff is purportedly available from Assignor SCIF does not relieve Defendant Bedrock from turning the information over “if the information is less easily available from the other source than it is from the party.” ([8:1023] Responding to Interrogatories:, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 8F-5; see also Bunnell v. Superior Court (California Life Ins. Co.) (1967) 254 Cal.App.2d 720, 723-724.) Although Plaintiff does not assert the information is less easily available from SCIF, the moving papers suggest that Assignor SCIF does not have the information and/or documentation sought in these requests as they keep asking Plaintiff for additional information and documentation to complete the 2019 audit of the Second Policy. (Plf. Sep. Stmt. at 12:12-28.) In addition, objections for being “overbroad,” “burdensome,” or “oppressive” are only valid when the responding party is asserting an undue burden based on the “quantum of work required” to satisfy the production. (West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) Defendant Bedrock merely contends the documents are voluminous. Lastly, the parties both agree initial premiums are based on an estimate of factors and actual earned premiums are determined after the workers compensation policy expires and an audit of the insured’s operation and payroll records is completed. (Mot. at 12:15-19; Opp’n at 2:10-21.) As such, the time cards and payroll records requested are reasonable calculated to lead to admissible evidence pertaining to what Defendant Bedrock’s actual earned premiums were for the Second Policy, which Plaintiff argues Defendant Bedrock refuses to provide and SCIF claims it does not have to complete the 2019 audit. (Mot. at 3:20-24; Plf. Sep. Stmt. at 12:12-28.)

 

Therefore, Defendant Bedrock’s responses to and document production for RFP Nos. 57 through 190 are deficient.

 

Monetary Sanctions

Code Civil Procedure Section 2031.310, subdivision (h) provides for the imposition of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes such a motion unless the court finds that the one subject to sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel of record, Michael D. Frischer seeks $2,311.95 in sanctions against Defendant Bedrock for (1) 3.5 hours reviewing Defendant Bedrock’s responses, researching and drafting the detailed initial meet and confer, subsequent emails, drafting the instant motion and separate statement; (2) 1.0 hour preparing and for appearance at the hearing at an hourly rate of $500.00; and (3) filing fee for $61.95. (Frischer Decl., ¶¶9-11.) Counsel Frischer’s request is reasonable based on his 30 years of litigation experience and comparable rates set forth in  National Association of Legal Fee Analysis. (Id.) Moreover, Defendant Bedrock did not act with substantial justification by providing meritless objections to the discovery requests at issue as discussed above. Further, Defendant Bedrock argue any other circumstance to support the imposition of sanctions would be unjust.  

However, the Court notes that Mr. Frischer included the following identical paragraph in each of his declarations for the two motions to compel on calendar for today:

“I expended in excess of three and half(3.5) hours reviewing defendant's responses, researching and drafting the detailed initial meet and confer correspondence as well as the subsequent emails, and in researching and drafting this motion and the separate statement. I anticipate incurring no less than one (1) hour of time preparing and for the appearance at the hearing. The filing fee for this motion is $61.95. Based upon the foregoing, it is respectfully requested that the Court enter an order pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2030.300, 2023.010, and 2023 .030 awarding plaintiff the sum of $2,311.95, representing the reasonable expenses which have been incurred in connection with filing this motion, necessitated solely by defendant's willful failure to properly respond to plaintiffs legitimate discovery.”

The Court awarded sanctions in the amount of $2,311.95 in the companion motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories.  The Court believes that there must be overlap in Mr. Frischer’s efforts and that imposing a second award of $2,311.95, as requested, would amount to a windfall.  The Court, on its own motion in the interests of judicial economy and convenience to the parties, re-calendared one of the discovery motions so that both could be heard on the same day (10/10).  Therefore, there is no need to imposed sanctions for time spent attending two hearings.  In addition, the meet and confer efforts must have encompassed both motions.  Therefore, as to this motion, the Court awards two hours at $500 per hour, plus costs of $61.95 for a total of $1,061.95.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

           Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents (Set Four).

 

          Defendant Bedrock is ordered to provide verified supplemental responses and production of documents to Request for Production of Documents (Set Four) Nos. 57 through 190 within 30 days of this order.

 

          Defendant Bedrock is also ordered to pay sanctions to Plaintiff in the amount of $1,061.95 within 30 days of this order.
