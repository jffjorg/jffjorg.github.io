DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV06935    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Friday, August 2, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   23STCV06935

CASE NAME:                        Omar Penny v. County of Los Angeles, et al.

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendants Jorge Fuentes, Philip Hyden

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Omar Penny

TRIAL DATE:                        10 December 2024

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

OPPOSITION:                        14 June 2024

SUPP. OPPOSITION:             19 July 2024

 

REPLY:                                  20 June 2024

SUPP. REPLY:                       26 July 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Moving Defendants’ MJOP is granted in part and denied in part. As to Defendant Hyden, the Court GRANTS the MJOP on the first, second, and fourteenth causes of action with leave to amend. As to Defendant Fuentes, the Court DENIES the MJOP on the first cause of action for sex and gender discrimination and the fourteenth cause of action for IIED and GRANTS the MJOP on the second and third causes of action for harassment with leave to amend. As to both Defendants Fuentes and Hyden, the Court GRANTS the MJOP on the twelfth cause of action for aiding and abetting discrimination without leave to amend.  Moving Defendants to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

On March 29, 2023, Omar Penny (“Plaintiff”) filed this FEHA action against the County of Los Angeles; Los Angeles County Department of Health Services; LAC+USC Medical Center; The Los Angeles County – USC Medical Auxiliary (the “County Defendants”); Jorge Fuentes; Philip Hyden; Theresa Saracho;  Rosario Tanphanich; Melissa Valenzuela; Nune Abraamyan; Antony Meza; and Does 1 to 25.

 

The Complaint alleges fourteen causes of action:

 

1)     Harassment Based on Sex and Gender in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [Cal. Gov’t Code § 12940(j)];

2)     Harassment Based on Religion in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [Cal. Gov’t Code § 12940(j)];

3)     Harassment Based on Disability in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [Cal. Gov’t Code § 12940(j)];

4)     Discrimination in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [Cal. Gov’t §§ 12940(a)];

5)     Retaliation in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [Cal. Gov't Code §12940(h)];

6)     Violation of Labor Code § 1102.5;

7)     Violation of Labor Code § 6310;

8)     Violation of Health and Safety Code § 1278.5;

9)     Defamation;

10) Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy;

11) Failure to Prevent Harassment and Discrimination from Occurring in Violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [Cal. Gov’t Code §§12940(k)];

12) Aiding and Abetting Discrimination in violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act [ Cal. Gov’t Code §12940(i)];

13) Negligent Hiring and Retention; and

14) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (“IIED”).

 

On May 31, 2024, Defendants Jorge Fuentes (“Fuentes”) and Philip Hyden (“Hyden”) (collectively “Moving Defendants”) filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings (“MJOP”). Plaintiff opposes the Motion. After hearing argument on July 12, 2024, the Court requested supplemental briefing.  The matter is now before the court.

 

motion for judgment on the pleadings[1]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1064.) “In deciding or reviewing a judgment on the pleadings, all properly pleaded material facts are deemed to be true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.” (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) When considering demurrers and judgment on the pleadings, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.)

A motion for judgment on the pleadings does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action. (Id.) “In the case of either a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings, leave to amend should be granted if there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action.” (Gami v. Mullikin Medical Ctr. (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 870, 876.) A non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made any time before or during trial. (Stoops v. Abbassi (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 644, 650.)¿¿

 

II.        Request for Judicial Notice

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) “Taking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning.” (Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374.)

III.      Discussion

 

A.        Failure to Exhaust Administrative Remedies Under FEHA

 

“Under FEHA, the employee must exhaust the administrative remedy provided by the statute by filing a complaint with the Department of Fair Employment and Housing (Department) and must obtain from the Department a notice of right to sue in order to be entitled to file a civil action in court based on violations of the FEHA.” (Romano v. Rockwell Internat., Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 479, 492; see also Gov. Code, §§ 12960, 12965(b).) “The timely filing of an administrative complaint is a prerequisite to the bringing of a civil action for damages under FEHA.” (Romano, at p. 492.)

 

Moving Defendants assert plaintiff failed to exhaust his administrative remedies with respect to the harassment claims in his Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (“EEOC”) charges. (Defendants’ RJN, Ex. A, B.) plaintiff asserts that his administrative charges with the EEOC, filed on August 1, 2022, and December 3, 2021, properly named Moving Defendants as respondents. Plaintiff’s opposition adds that “Plaintiff filed a further administrative charge with DFEH on April 7, 2023. And obtained a right-to sue letter[.]” (Opposition, at p.  4:28-5:1.)

 

            B.        Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to Defendant Hyden

 

The court gave the Parties an opportunity to file supplemental briefs related to whether Plaintiff’s had exhausted his administrative remedies with the DFEH. Plaintiff’s supplemental request for judicial notice reflects the fact Plaintiff filed three charges before the EEOC, but Plaintiff failed to attach the right-to-sue letter from the DFEH. Plaintiff can only point to Defendants RJN Ex. C wherein the DFEH issued a right-to-sue in relation to the EEOC Complaint Number 480-2021-03580C. (Defendants’ RJN Ex. C at p. 61.) Complaint Number 480-2021-035C only alleged claims of harassment, discrimination, and IIED, NIED, and relation as to Defendant Fuentes and did not name Defendant Hyden as Defendant. (Defendants RJN Ex. C.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to Defendant Hyden. Plaintiff asserts he requested all EEOC charges be cross-filed with the DFEH, but there is no evidence that all EEOC charges were in fact cross-filed with the DFEH. The court grants the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to Defendant Hyden with leave to amend as Plaintiff may be able to amend the Complaint to show that the DFEH did in fact issue a right-to-sue letter for all three EEOC charges. Copies of the DFEH’s right-to-sue letters should be attached to any amended pleading.

 

C.        First, Second, and Third Causes of Action based on Harassment by Defendant Fuentes

 

To establish a claim for harassment, an employee must show that he was: (1) subjected to verbal or physical conduct because of the employee’s protected status; (2) the conduct was unwelcome; and (3) the conduct was sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the employee’s employment and create an abusive work environment. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 609.)

 

As to Defendant Fuentes, the Complaint alleges that Fuente was a Senior Physician and Medical Director for County Defendants. (Compl., ¶ 6.) Fuentes routinely held meetings to delegate tasks to Plaintiff, but Plaintiff was not present during these meetings. (Compl.,¶ 25.) At the meetings, co-workers assigned Plaintiff to conduct all sexual assault examinations of male prison inmates and all male patients considered violent, aggressive, hostile, or medically complicated. (Compl, ¶ 25.) Defendant Fuentes always assigned a female nurse as the Charge Nurse, depriving Plaintiff of participating in Charge Nurse pay. (Compl., ¶ 27.) Plaintiff was also the only male nurse practitioner in his Department. (Compl, ¶ 27.) Defendant Fuentes refused to give Plaintiff overtime opportunities despite giving the same opportunity to female co-workers and failed to address Plaintiff’s complaints. (Compl, ¶¶ 37, 40.)

 

In August 2020, Fuentes gave a female co-worker a day shift despite Plaintiff’s seniority and the fact he had placed his name for a day shift on the waitlist in 2018. (Compl., ¶ 42.) When Plaintiff complained to Fuentes about gendered comments made to him, Fuentes told Plaintiff to be “the bigger man’ and suggested that Plaintiff transfer to another location. (Compl., ¶ 48.) When Plaintiff applied for a vaccine exemption, Fuentes told Plaintiff that he would be fired. (Compl., ¶ 76) The Complaint also alleges other offensive conduct by Defendant Fuentes after August 6, 2021, but because the DFEH right-to-sue letter pertains to EEOC Complaint Number 480-2021-03580C filed on August 13, 2021, such conduct is not at issue until Plaintiff can show he has exhausted his administrative remedies as to those claims.

 

Defendants assert that the harassment-based causes of action fail because Plaintiff does not allege sufficient facts to support the harassment claims as many of the claims relate to personnel decisions made by Fuentes:

 

[T]he Legislature intended that commonly necessary personnel management actions such as hiring and firing, job or project assignments, office or work station assignments, promotion or demotion, performance evaluations, the provision of support, the assignment or non-assignment of supervisory functions, deciding who will and who will not attend meetings, deciding who will be laid off, and the like, do not come within the meaning of harassment. . . These actions may retrospectively be found discriminatory if based on improper motives, but in that event the remedies provided by the FEHA are those for discrimination, not harassment. Harassment, by contrast, consists of actions outside the scope of job duties which are not of a type necessary to business and personnel management. This significant distinction underlies the differential treatment of harassment and discrimination in the FEHA.

 

(Janken v. GM Hughes Electronics (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 55, 65 (Janken).)

 

In Roby v. McKesson Corp. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 686 (Roby), the California Supreme Court explained that “[a]lthough discrimination and harassment are separate wrongs, they are sometimes closely interrelated, and even overlapping, particularly with regard to proof.” (Id. at p. 707.) The Court explained that “harassment focuses on situations in which the social environment of the workplace becomes intolerable because the harassment (whether verbal, physical, or visual) communicates an offensive message to the harassed employee.” (Id. at p. 706 [italics original].) Accordingly, “official employment actions [can] constitute[] the evidentiary basis of the harassment cause of action, because the supervisor used those official actions as his means of conveying his offensive message.” (Id. at p. 708 [italics original].) “[I]n some cases the hostile message that constitutes the harassment is conveyed through official employment actions, and therefore evidence that would otherwise be associated with a discrimination claim can form the basis of a harassment claim.” (Ibid.)

Roby supports the conclusion “that some official employment actions done in furtherance of a supervisor's managerial role can also have a secondary effect of communicating a hostile message. This occurs when the actions establish a widespread pattern of bias.” (Roby, supra, 47 Cal.4th at p. 709.) In Miller v. Department of Corrections (2005) 36 Cal.4th 446 (Miller), the California Supreme Court found that evidence of widespread sexual favoritism could support a claim for sexual harassment when the  “demeaning message is conveyed to female employees that they are viewed by management as ‘sexual playthings’ or that the way required for women to get ahead in the workplace is to engage in sexual conduct with their supervisors or the management.” (Id. at p. 451.)

 

The demeaning message in Miller was conveyed through the supervisor’s “sexual favoritism” of female employees who welcomed his sexual advances. (Miller, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 467.) The supervisor blocked the merit-based advancement of female plaintiffs who did not welcome his sexual advances and favored the advancement of those who did. (Ibid.) The supervisor “refused or failed to control” retaliatory harassing conduct instigated by favored female employees against other female employees and “[t]his harassment, apparently retaliatory, included loss of work responsibilities, demeaning comments in the presence of other employees, loss of entitlement to a pay enhancement and to disability accommodation[.]” (Id. at p. 468.) The California Supreme Court concluded that such conduct raised triable issues of fact as whether the supervisor’s “conduct constituted sexual favoritism widespread enough to constitute a hostile work environment” and was sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the employment due to the female employee’s gender. (Id. at p. 468.)

 

The court finds that the Complaint alleges that Fuentes gave Plaintiff less favorable job assignments because he was male. (Compl., ¶¶ 25.) Plaintiff also alleges he was never given the duties of a Charge Nurse and was denied the higher pay rate by Fuentes. (Compl, ¶ 27.) Fuentes dismissed Plaintiff’s request to work overtime, denied his request for a day shift despite having senior priority, and continually ignored Plaintiff’s complaints regarding degrading comments made by other workers related to Plaintiff’s gender. (Compl, ¶¶ 40, 45, 47, 54.) Such facts are sufficient to allege harassment based on gender that was severe or pervasive enough to alter the terms and conditions of employment.

 

In sum, as to Defendant Fuentes, the Court denies the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action for harassment based on sex and gender.  Because the Complaint fails to allege sufficient facts to support a claim for harassment based on disability and religious creed, the Court grants the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the second and third causes of action for harassment with leave to amend.

 

D.        Twelfth Cause of Action- Aiding and Abetting Discrimination

 

“FEHA prohibits ‘any person’ from aiding or abetting workplace discrimination. (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (i).) For that reason, individuals and entities who are not the plaintiff's employer may be liable under FEHA for aiding and abetting the plaintiff's employer's violation of FEHA.” (Smith v. BP Lubricants USA Inc. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 138, 145–146.)

Defendant maintains that the Legislature never intended to impose personal liability upon supervisory employees because an employer acts through its employees, so the element of “concert” is missing to satisfy the cause of action or aiding and abetting. “ ‘The common basis for liability for both conspiracy and aiding and abetting ... is concerted wrongful action.’ [Citation.]  Aiding and abetting thus ‘involves two separate persons, one helping the other.’ ” (Smith v. BP Lubricants USA Inc. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 138, 146 citing Janken, supra, Cal.App.4th 55 at pp. 77-78.) “A corporate employee cannot conspire with his or her corporate employer; that would be tantamount to a person conspiring with himself. Thus, when a corporate employee acts in his or her authorized capacity on behalf of his or her corporate employer, there can be no claim of conspiracy between the corporate employer and the corporate employee.” (Janken, at p. 78.) “[A]iding and abetting involves two separate people, one who helps the other. Thus, a supervisor cannot likely aid and abet his or her employer because the employer can only act through its individual employees.” (Haligowski v. Superior Court (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 983, 996.)

 

Plaintiff’s opposition states Janken stands for the proposition that when “a claim of aiding and abetting cannot be brought against an individual defendant, they mean that the claim cannot be brought against an individual as in singular defendant.” (Opposition, at p. 9:26-27.) Plaintiff fails to cite any case law supporting Plaintiff’s reading of Janken. As the California Supreme Court made clear in Reno v. Baird (1998) 18 Cal.4th 640, “individuals who do not themselves qualify as employers may not be sued under the FEHA for alleged discriminatory acts.” (Id. at p. 663.) “We concluded that the agent-inclusive language of section 12926, subdivision (d) does not impose liability on all agents, including individual employees of the same employer, and adopting that interpretation of section 12926, subdivision (d) would be inconsistent with the provision's express exemption for employers with fewer than five employees.” (Raines v. U.S. Healthworks Medical Group (2023) 15 Cal.5th 268, 276 [italics original].) Therefore, “[managerial and/or supervisory personnel cannot be individually or personally liable to an employee under FEHA, even under an aiding and abetting theory.” (Peterson v. State of California Dept. of Corrections and Rehabilitation (E.D. Cal. 2006) 451 F.Supp.2d 1092, 1113.)

 

The motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the twelfth cause of action is granted without leave to amend.

 

E.        Fourteenth Cause of Action- Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

 

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress requires: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by appellants with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the appellants’ outrageous conduct. [Citation.]  Conduct is considered outrageous when it is ‘so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ [Citation.]” (Belen v. Ryan Seacrest Productions, LLC(2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1145, 1164.) Whether the defendant’s conduct was outrageous, oppressive or malicious is a question of fact for the jury. (Alcorn v. Anbro Engineering, Inc. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 493, 499.)

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s IIED claim against Defendant Fuentes fails because no extreme or outrageous conduct is alleged. The Complaint states: “By engaging in the above-described conduct, Defendants engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress.” (Compl., ¶ 236.) The court finds that because Plaintiff has stated a viable sex and gender harassment claim against Defendant Fuentes and has alleged that Fuentes acted intentionally, the IIED claim is sufficiently pled. The motion for judgment on the pleadings is denied as to the fourteenth cause of action.

 

Conclusion

 

Moving Defendants’ MJOP is granted in part and denied in part. As to Defendant Hyden, the

Court GRANTS the MJOP on the first, second, and fourteenth causes of action with leave to

amend. As to Defendant Fuentes, the Court DENIES the MJOP on the first cause of action for

sex and gender discrimination and the fourteenth cause of action for IIED and GRANTS the

MJOP on the second and third causes of action for harassment with leave to amend. As to both

Defendants Fuentes and Hyden, the Court GRANTS the MJOP on the twelfth cause of action for

aiding and abetting discrimination without leave to amend.  Moving Defendants to give notice.
