DEPARTMENT 31 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling by emailing Dept. 31 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. The email address is smcdept31@lacourt.org. Please do not call the court to submit on the tentative. Please do not submit to the tentative ruling on behalf of the opposing party. Please do not e-mail the Court if you plan to appear and argue.

In deciding whether to submit on the tentative ruling or attend the hearing and present oral argument, please keep the following in mind:

The tentative rulings authored by this court reflect that the court has read and considered all pleadings and evidence timely submitted to the court in connection with the motion, opposition, and reply (if any). Because the pleadings were filed, they are part of the public record.

Oral argument is not an opportunity to simply regurgitate that which a party set forth in its pleadings. Nor, is oral argument an opportunity to "make a record" when there is no court reporter present and the statements and arguments of counsel are already part of the record because they were set forth in the pleadings. Finally, simply because a party or attorney disagrees with the court's analysis and ruling or is not satisfied with it does not necessarily warrant oral argument when no new arguments will be articulated.

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify all other parties email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. If all parties to the motion submit, this tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date and it will be memorialized in a minute order. This tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such document will be considered by the Court.

**Tentative rulings on Motions for Summary Judgment will only be available for review in the courtroom on the day of the hearing.


Case Number: 22STCV06267    Hearing Date: December 4, 2023    Dept: 31

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Kerry Bensinger, Department 31

 

 

HEARING DATE:      December 4, 2023                                                        TRIAL DATE:  Not set

                                                          

CASE:                         Jorge Medrano v. BYD Coach & Bus LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV06267

 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF

DEFENDANT BYD COACH & BUS LLC’S PERSON MOST QUALIFIED

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Jorge Medrano

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant BYD Coach & Bus LLC

-

 

I.          INTRODUCTION

 

            This is a disability discrimination, failure to accommodate, and wrongful termination action.  On February 18, 2022, Plaintiff, Jorge Medrano, filed a Complaint against Defendant, BYD Coach & Bus LLC, for (1) Disability Discrimination (Gov. Code § 12940, et seq.), (2) Retaliation (Gov. Code § 12940(h)), (3) Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodation (Gov. Code § 12940(m)), (4) Failure to Engage in Interactive Process (Gov. Code § 12940(n)), (5) Retaliation in Violation of California Family Rights Act (Gov. Code § 12945.2), (6) Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code § 1102.5 et seq., (7) Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Retaliation (Gov. Code § 12940 et seq.), and (8) Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy.

 

            Relevant Background

                       

            On June 16, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant with a Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Qualified.  The Notice sets forth twenty-seven (27) categories and twenty-five (25) requests for production of documents at deposition.  The deposition was noticed for July 20, 2023.

 

            On June 26, 2023, defense counsel requested Plaintiff’s availability for deposition.  Defense counsel did not mention whether Defendant would appear for the person most qualified (PMQ) deposition. 

 

            On June 29, 2023, defense counsel again inquired about Plaintiff’s availability for deposition.  Plaintiff’s counsel responded on the same day by requesting confirmation of Defendant’s appearance for the PMQ deposition or to provide alternate dates.  Defense counsel indicated that she would be unavailable on the noticed deposition date of July 20, 2023 from 2:00 p.m. and onward.  Plaintiff’s counsel confirmed the deposition would likely require the entire day and again requested alternate dates.

 

            On July 12, 2023, defense counsel confirmed that the noticed PMQ deposition date of July 20, 2023 would not work for a full-day deposition.  Defense counsel asked Plaintiff for alternate dates.

 

            On July 13, 2023, Defendant served an Objection to the Notice.  The Objection was based upon defense counsel’s unavailability for the deposition date.  Defendant did not propose alternate dates. 

 

            On July 31, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel again inquired about possible dates for the PMQ deposition.

 

            On August 3, 2023, defense counsel indicated they were working on dates and hoped to produce one PMQ for all the topics.  In the parties email exchange, a disagreement arose over who was responsible for providing alternate dates for the PMQ deposition, and whether there was a currently noticed PMQ deposition.  Plaintiff’s counsel again requested alternate dates by August 4, 2023.

 

            On August 8, 2023, after Defendant had yet to propose deposition dates, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s PMQ.  Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Defendant and its counsel.

 

            On August 9, 2023, defense counsel notified Plaintiff’s counsel by email that she had been out of state and traveling from August 5-8, 2023, and was thus unavailable.  However, defense counsel was able to speak with Defendant’s PMQ, Kimberly McFerguson, and had obtained her dates of availability.

 

            Thereafter, the parties came to an agreement over a deposition date.  Plaintiff deposed McFerguson on September 27, 2023.  At the deposition, Ms. McFerguson indicated that she was not the PMQ for all deposition topics and identified two other individuals—Jonathan Hernandez and Janie. Li— who may have knowledge of the remaining PMQ topics.

 

            On November 17, 2023, Defendant filed an Opposition, arguing that Plaintiff’s motion was moot given that Ms. McFerguson had been deposed and that the parties were working on deposition dates for the two other individuals.  Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel for persisting with this motion.

 

            On November 22, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Reply, arguing the motion is not moot because Mr. Hernandez and Ms. Li had yet to be deposed.

             

II.        LEGAL STANDARDS

 

Any party may obtain discovery by taking in California the oral deposition of any person.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)  “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action…without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)   

 

            Monetary Sanctions¿ 

¿ 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 is a general statute authorizing the Court to impose discovery sanctions for “misuse of the discovery process,” which includes (without limitation) a variety of conduct such as: making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery; making an evasive response to discovery; and unsuccessfully and without substantial justification making or opposing a motion to compel or limit discovery.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.)¿¿ 

¿ 

            If sanctions are sought, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.040 requires that the notice specify the identity of the person against whom sanctions are sought and the type of sanction requested, that the motion be supported in the points and authorities, and the facts be set forth in a declaration supporting the amount of any monetary sanction.¿¿¿¿ 

 

            “If a motion under [Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450] subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)¿¿¿¿ 

 

Sanctions against counsel:¿ The court in Kwan Software Engineering, Inc. v. Hennings (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 57, 81 (Hennings) noted that discovery sanctions against an attorney are governed by a different standard than sanctions against a party:¿¿¿ 

¿ 

By the terms of the statute, a trial court under section 2023.030(a) may not impose monetary sanctions against a party’s attorney unless the court finds that the attorney “advised” the party to engage in the conduct resulting in sanctions. (§ 2023.030(a); Ghanooni v. Super Shuttle (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 256, 261, 24 Cal.Rptr.2d 501.)¿ “Unlike monetary sanctions against a party, which are based on the party's misuse of the discovery process, monetary sanctions against the party's attorney require a finding the ‘attorney advis[ed] that conduct.’ ” (Ibid.) “It is not enough that the attorney's actions were in some way improper.” (Corns v. Miller (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 195, 200, 226 Cal.Rptr. 247 (Corns).) Because an attorney's advice to a client is “peculiarly within [his or her] knowledge,” the attorney has the burden of showing that he or she did not counsel discovery abuse. (Ibid.) Accordingly, when a party seeking sanctions against an attorney offers sufficient evidence of a misuse of the discovery process, the burden shifts to the attorney to demonstrate that he or she did not recommend that conduct. (Id. at pp. 200–201, 226 Cal.Rptr. 247; Ghanooni, at p. 262, 24 Cal.Rptr.2d 501.)¿

 

III.      DISCUSSION

 

            It is undisputed that Defendant produced Ms. McFerguson for deposition on September 27, 2023. As such, the motion is moot.   

 

            Plaintiff argues the motion is still at issue because Ms. McFerguson stated she was not the PMQ for all the deposition topics and identified Mr. Hernandez and Ms. Li as the persons most qualified for the remaining topics.  Further, Defendant only recently provided deposition dates for Mr. Hernandez and has not yet confirmed Ms. Li’s availability.  (See Reply, Chami Decl., Ex. F.)  However, it appears Defendant is striving to provide available dates for the depositions of Mr. Hernandez and Ms. Li.  Plaintiff does not show that Defendant has engaged in any of the conduct giving rise to a motion to compel a party’s deposition: Defendant has not served an invalid objection, let alone any objection, to the deposition of Mr. Hernandez or Ms. Li, nor has either individual failed to appear for deposition.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)  Given the foregoing, an order compelling Defendant’s deposition is not warranted.

 

            Monetary Sanctions

 

            The parties each request imposition of sanctions against the other and their counsel.         

Plaintiff argues sanctions are warranted against Defendant and its counsel because Defendant objected to the deposition notice based on unavailability.  As such, Defendant was responsible for providing alternate dates yet failed to do so despite numerous requests from Plaintiff.  Plaintiff further argues that Defendant provided deposition dates only because this motion was filed. 

 

            Defendant argues sanctions are warranted against Plaintiff and his counsel because Defendant requested alternate dates when it became clear defense counsel would be unavailable for the July 20, 2023 deposition date.  Defense counsel also states in her declaration that she was in the process of obtaining the requested dates.  Plaintiff was so notified on August 1, 2023.  However, because defense counsel was traveling, and because of Ms. McFerguson’s unavailability, defense counsel did not obtain the dates until August 8, 2023.  (Alameddine Decl., ¶¶ 6-10.)

 

            The Court declines to award (or impose) sanctions.  Plaintiff may be technically correct that, Defendant, as the party objecting to the notice based on unavailability, should have provided alternate dates.  However, Defendant requested the same of Plaintiff prior to serving its written objection to the notice, and only served the objection when Plaintiff did not respond.  In sum, both parties requested alternate dates.  Neither party did not do so until engaging in a lengthy meet and confer process.  This motion could have been avoided altogether if the parties had not unreasonably insisted that the other party “blink first.”

           

IV.       CONCLUSION 

 

            The motion is moot.  The requests for sanctions are denied.

CASE NO.:                 23STCV16161

 

 

DEMURRER WITHOUT MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Illumina, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Linda Mansolillo

 

 

I.          BACKGROUND

 

            This is an employment discrimination and retaliation action. On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff, Linda Mansolillo, filed a Complaint against Defendants, GRAIL, Inc. (“GRAIL”) and Illumina, Inc. (“Illumina”), for (1) Harassment in Violation of FEHA, (2) Discrimination in Violation of FEHA, (3) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA, (4) Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment in Violation of the Fair Employment & Housing Act, (5) Violation of California Equal Pay Act, (6) Discrimination and Retaliation in Violation of California Equal Pay Act, (7) Whistleblower Retaliation, and (8) Labor Code Retaliation. 

 

            The Complaint alleges as follows: Plaintiff was employed by Illumina and GRAIL. Illumina develops gene sequencing technology. GRAIL was formed in 2015 as a subsidiary of Illumina to develop cancer screening from blood tests. In 2016, GRAIL spun off as a separate company to facilitate fundraising from outside investors. In September 2020, Illumina decided to reacquire GRAIL. The acquisition was completed in August 2021. When first proposed, the acquisition spurred on intense scrutiny by the European Union and United States Government. The European Union opened an investigation into the proposed merger. Illumina and GRAIL completed the merger before the investigation was completed. In response, the European Union adopted measures requiring the companies to maintain separate management. Meanwhile, in the United States, the Federal Trade Commission ordered Illumina to divest its acquisition of GRAIL.

 

            From 2016 to 2020, Plaintiff was employed by Illumina. In February 2020, Plaintiff was hired by GRAIL as Director of National Accounts.  Plaintiff’s first year of employment with GRAIL was a success.  Plaintiff received positive performance reviews and was promoted. However, after the May 2021 hiring of a woman named Stephanie Gutendorf as the Senior Vice President of Partnerships, the workplace environment deteriorated. 

 

            Ms. Gutendorf hired her former colleague and good friend, Joe McKenna, as Vice President of Commercial Partnerships. To Plaintiff’s dismay, Plaintiff was never given the opportunity to apply to that position despite being more than qualified. Plaintiff voiced her concerns. Shortly after, in an apparent attempt to deflect Plaintiff’s concerns, Ms. Gutendorf told Plaintiff about a new position and indicated Plaintiff was the best person for it.  Plaintiff eventually assumed the new position with a clear path to promotion to a Vice President level position. 

 

             During Ms. Gutendorf’s tenure at GRAIL, Ms. Gutendorf directly contributed to a sexually charged, hostile work environment.  For instance, Ms. Gutendorf attended a work meeting in inappropriate attire and openly discussed her personal sex life and other unprofessional conduct.  Plaintiff reported Ms. Gutendorf’s behavior to human resources.  GRAIL opened an investigation and, shortly afterwards, Ms. Gutendorf left the company under undisclosed terms. 

           

            After Ms. Gutendorf’s departure from GRAIL, Plaintiff was required to report to Mr. McKenna.  Mr. McKenna suspected that the investigation of Ms. Gutendorf was initiated based on Plaintiff’s report.  In retaliation, Mr. McKenna, along with Chief Financial Officer Aaron Friedan, placed Plaintiff in a new position.  The new position was effectively a demotion since Plaintiff no longer had a clear path to a Vice President level position and required Plaintiff to co-lead with a male colleague with considerably less experience. Plaintiff’s suspicion of retaliation was confirmed when she learned that GRAIL was publicly advertising a position for a Vice President of Customer Success position.  Plaintiff was qualified for the position but was never notified of its availability.  Mr. McKenna was eventually hired for the position.  Further, Mr. McKenna prepared Plaintiff’s year end performance review for 2021 and gave her low ratings without providing any metrics, examples, or specific feedback supporting the rating.  Plaintiff’s performance review sharply contrasted with year-end reviews that Mr. McKenna prepared for male employees.

 

            In addition to retaliation, the hostile work environment under Ms. Gutendorf’s tenure persisted with Mr. McKenna. Plaintiff complained of the retaliatory conduct and discrimination she was experiencing, along with myriad other issues, but her concerns were not met with serious consideration nor were any investigations undertaken. Plaintiff eventually left GRAIL to pursue other opportunities. Prior to filing her Complaint, Plaintiff wrote a letter to Illumina’s interim CEO and board of directors which detailed her experiences while employed at GRAIL.  Plaintiff asked that Illumina take action to end the hostile work environment at its wholly owned subsidiary. Plaintiff also provided a draft copy of this Complaint. In response, Illumina’s outside counsel indicated no action would be taken because of the hold-separate requirement imposed by US antitrust and European competition regulators.

 

            On August 17, 2023, Illumina filed this Demurrer to the entire Complaint, arguing that each cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action as to Illumina.  In the alternative, Illumina requests a stay of the action as to Illumina pursuant to the primary jurisdiction doctrine and equitable abstention doctrine during the pendency of European Commission proceedings and Federal Trade Commission proceedings.

 

            Plaintiff filed an opposition.  Illumina replied. 

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD FOR DEMURRER  

 

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face.  (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.)  “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.  [Citation.]”  (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)  Allegations are to be liberally construed.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  In construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations.  (Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn (1987) 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769.)  

 

A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)  “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.”  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)   

Where the complaint contains substantial factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty will be overruled or plaintiff will be given leave to amend.  (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)  Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully.  (Ibid.)  

 

III.      DISCUSSION

 

A.  Meet and Confer

 

Defense counsel has complied with the meet and confer requirement. (See Declaration of Christina Snider, ¶¶ 5, 6.)

 

B.  Judicial Notice

 

Illumina requests judicial notice of thirteen documents.  Plaintiff opposes generally, but not specifically as to each request.  No matter, as the Court does not rely upon the requested documents in reaching its ruling.  As such, the requests for judicial notice are DENIED as not relevant.  

 

C.  Analysis

 

Illumina does not challenge whether the elements of each cause of action are sufficiently pleaded.  Rather, the main issue to be decided is whether the Complaint sufficiently alleges Illumina’s liability under a joint employment theory or single enterprise theory.  For the reasons stated herein, the Court finds the Complaint is deficient.

 

            1. Joint Employment Theory

 

“California law specifies the elements necessary to define an employer. “To employ” has “three alternative definitions.” (Martinez v. Combs (2010) 49 Cal.4th 35, 64, 109 Cal.Rptr.3d 514, 231 P.3d 259.) “It means: (a) to exercise control over the wages, hours or working conditions, or (b) to suffer or permit to work, or (c) to engage, thereby creating a common law employment relationship.” (Ibid.)” (Castaneda v. Ensign Grp., Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1015, 1019.) 

 

An entity that controls the business enterprise may be an employer even if it did not “directly hire, fire or supervise” the employees. (Guerrero v. Superior Court, supra, 213 Cal.App.4th at p. 950, 153 Cal.Rptr.3d 315.) Multiple entities may be employers where they “control different aspects of the employment relationship.” (Martinez v. Combs, supra, 49 Cal.4th at p. 76, 109 Cal.Rptr.3d 514, 231 P.3d 259.)” (Id.)

 

Courts consider the ‘totality of the circumstances’ to determine the existence of an employer/employee relationship.  “Factors to be taken into account in assessing the relationship of the parties include payment of salary or other employment benefits and Social Security taxes, the ownership of the equipment necessary to performance of the job, the location where the work is performed, the obligation of the defendant to train the employee, the authority of the defendant to hire, transfer, promote, discipline or discharge the employee, the authority to establish work schedules and assignments, the defendant's discretion to determine the amount of compensation earned by the employee, the skill required of the work performed and the extent to which it is done under the direction of a supervisor, whether the work is part of the defendant's regular business operations, the skill required in the particular occupation, the duration of the relationship of the parties, and the duration of the plaintiff's employment. [Citations.] ‘ “Generally, ... the individual factors cannot be applied mechanically as separate tests; they are intertwined and their weight depends often on particular combinations.” ’ ” (Vernon v. State of California (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 114, 124-125, 10 Cal.Rptr.3d 121 (Vernon), fn. omitted.) The most important factor is “the defendant's right to control the means and manner of the workers’ performance.” (Id. at p. 126, 10 Cal.Rptr.3d 121.)”  (St. Myers v. Dignity Health (2019) 44 Cal.App.5th 301, 311-12.)

 

Here, the Complaint alleges as follows:

 

On information and belief, GRAIL is and was at all relevant times a wholly owned subsidiary of ... Illumina.  On information and belief, Illumina, at all relevant times, had and has 100% ownership of interests or a controlling interest in GRAIL and exercises ultimate control over GRAIL’s affairs—no matter the public facing statements made by GRAIL and Illumina asserting that GRAIL is held completely separate from Illumina and that Illumina does not have any material knowledge of or control over GRAIL.  (Complaint, ¶ 3.) 

 

On information and belief, executives and leadership at Illumina were made aware of the allegations detailed below in this Complaint and failed to take any corrective action at GRAIL despite their knowledge of the events alleged.  (Complaint, ¶ 4.)

 

Plaintiff is informed, believes, and based thereon alleges that GRAIL, ILLUMINA, and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive (“Defendants”), do business jointly and are collectively liable under either a joint employer theory or a single enterprise theory for the conduct described herein.  (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

 

Plaintiff is informed, believes, and based thereon alleges that, at all times relevant herein alleged, each of the Defendants designated, including DOES 1 through 100, inclusive, is and was the agent, managing agent, principal, owner, partner, joint venture, representative, manager, servant, employee, and/or co-conspirator of each of the other defendants and was at all times mentioned herein acting within the course and scope of said agency, employment, and/or representation. Plaintiff is further informed, believes, and based thereon alleges that, at all relevant times as herein alleged, all acts or omissions alleged herein were duly committed with the ratification, knowledge, permission, encouragement, authorization, and/or consent of each of the other Defendants herein designated, including DOES 1 through 100, inclusive.  (Complaint, ¶ 7.)

 

The foregoing allegations are conclusory and devoid of any factual support.  There are no factual allegations stating that Illumina exercised control over Plaintiff’s wages, hours or working conditions.  And critically, there are no allegations that Illumina had the right to control the means and manner of Plaintiff’s work performance at GRAIL.  Rather, the Complaint makes clear that the alleged wrongful conduct was committed by GRAIL employees (see, e.g., Complaint, ¶¶ 33-34, 37-38, 42-45, 48-50, 53, 54, 57), while Plaintiff was employed by GRAIL (Complaint, ¶ 20).  In essence, Plaintiff seeks to hold Illumina liable for the alleged conduct based solely on Illumina’s status as the parent company of GRAIL.  However, a parent corporation is not liable for the torts of its subsidiary simply because it owns the subsidiary. (Northern Natural Gas Co. v. Superior Court (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 983, 991; see also Noe v. Superior Court (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 316, 332 [observing there is “no authority suggesting that, under California law, joint employers are generally treated as if they were each other’s agents or that joint employers are normally held jointly liable for Labor Code violation committed by a co-employer]”.)  The Complaint does not sufficiently allege Illumina’s liability pursuant to a joint employer theory.

 

            2. Single Enterprise Theory

 

“An employee who seeks to hold a parent corporation liable for the acts or omissions of its subsidiary on the theory that the two corporate entities constitute a single employer has a heavy burden to meet under both California and federal law. Corporate entities are presumed to have separate existences, and the corporate form will be disregarded only when the ends of justice require this result.”  (Laird v. Cap. Cities/ABC, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 727, 737.)  Courts employ a four-factor test to determine whether two corporations should be considered a single employer.  The factors are (1) interrelation of operations, (2) common management, (3) centralized control of labor relations, and (4) common ownership or financial control.  (Id.)  Further, under this test, common ownership or control alone is never enough to establish parent liability.  Centralized control of labor relations is often deemed the most critical factor.  To satisfy the centralized control prong, a parent must control the day-to-day employment decisions of the subsidiary.  (Id. at p. 738.) 

 

Here, the Complaint again falls short of the mark.  There are no allegations regarding any of the four factors set forth under Laird.  Plaintiff contends the allegation that “[o]n information and belief, Illumina, at all relevant times, had and has 100% ownership of interests or a controlling interest in GRAIL and exercises ultimate control over GRAIL’s affairs” (Complaint, ¶ 3), sufficiently alleges that Illumina and Grail were a single enterprise.  However, this allegation only establishes Illumina’s common ownership or control of GRAIL.  As Laird states,  “common ownership or control alone is never enough to establish parent liability.”  (Laird, 68 Cal.App.4th at p. 738.)

 

The Complaint is deficient.  Having so concluded, the Court does not address Illumina’s alternative request for a stay.

Based on the foregoing, the Demurrer is SUSTAINED.  Leave to amend is GRANTED.

Defendant Illumina, Inc. is ordered to file and serve its responsive pleading within 30 days of service with the amended pleading.
