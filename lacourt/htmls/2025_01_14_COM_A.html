DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

      CASE NO: 23CMCV01624

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOVANT’S MOTION TO QUASH DEPOSITION SUBPOENA

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: January 14, 2025

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 10/09/2023

TRIAL: None set

 

MOVING PARTY:              Non-party Mark Ridley-Thomas

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Plenitude Holdings, LLC

1.     Background

This is a breach of contract action. Plaintiff Plenitude Holdings, LLC (“Plaintiff”) alleges that it and defendant County of Los Angeles (“Defendant”) entered into a lease agreement in 2021. Plaintiff alleges that it sought a six-month extension in its time to perform in exchange for an agreed-upon extension fee of $100,000, which Defendant was contractually obligated to consider in good faith, but that Defendant refused Plaintiff’s request without adequate justification or explanation.

Non-party Mark Ridley-Thomas (“Movant”) was Los Angeles County Supervisor for the 2nd Supervisorial District from 2008 to 2020. Plaintiff previously sought documentary discovery from Movant, which Movant provided on August 2, 2024 and August 15, 2024. On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff served a deposition subpoena on Movant. On November 14, 2024, Movant filed the instant motion to quash.

2.     Requests for Judicial Notice

Movant seeks judicial notice of (1) the United States Attorney’s Office press release regarding Movant’s criminal sentencing; (2) a court order regarding Movant’s pending bail appeal, and (3) the court docket in that appellate proceeding.

Plaintiff seeks judicial notice of four documents: (1)An excerpt from Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors Statement of Proceedings dated November 21, 2017 and related Motion by Supervisor Ridley-Thomas; (2) An excerpt from Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors Statement of Proceedings dated November 24, 2020 and related Motion by Supervisor Ridley-Thomas; (3) An article dated December 2, 2020 from Our Weekly Los Angeles; (4) This court’s Minute Order dated September 5, 2024.

Factual findings in prior judicial orders, opinions, or decisions are not a proper subject of judicial notice. (Kilroy v. State of Calif. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 140, 148.) Nor does the court take judicial notice of matters not relevant to the proceeding. (People ex rel. Lockyer v. Shamrock Foods Co. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 415, 422, fn. 2.) However, pursuant to California Evidence Code 452(c), judicial notice may be taken of “[o]fficial acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.” To that extent, the court therefore takes judicial notice of all of the foregoing without taking judicial notice of the facts set forth therein, with the exception of the Our Weekly Los Angeles article dated December 2, 2020 and the United States Attorney’s Office press release both of which the court declines to take judicial notice.

 

3.     Discussion

Movant moves to quash Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena of Movant on the grounds that Movant was not employed by Defendant during the lease period, nor in other ways involved with the lease during the lease period, and that therefore the deposition of Movant is unnecessary and unduly burdensome. Plaintiff, in opposition, contends that Movant was involved in the negotiations between Plaintiff and Defendant in establishing the lease and therefore is the only person in possession of some of the information sought to be obtained by the deposition.

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a), the court may quash, modify, or direct compliance with a subpoena requiring the attendance of a witness at a deposition, or make any other order “as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands.” A witness may make such a motion to quash a deposition subpoena. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(2).)

A party may obtain discovery of any non-privileged matter that is relevant to the subject matter of the action “if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)  Though Plaintiff has not identified with specificity the matters on which it intends to question Movant at the noticed deposition, the Discovery Act does not require either a records subpoena or a testimony subpoena to be accompanied by an affidavit or declaration showing good cause.  (Ibid., §§ 2020.310, 2020.510, subdiv. (b), and 2020.410; see Terry v. SLICO (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 352, 359—CCP § 2020.510(b) controls over contrary provisions in § 1985(b) and 1987.5 (requiring affidavits showing good cause for production.)

It appears to the court that this deposition may be very brief due to Movant’s contention that he lacks personal knowledge as to any of the issues raised by the pleadings.  However, the court finds that given Movant’s prior employment and involvement in the transactions at issue as the “primary promoter of the project” and his role in having “sponsored every motion and vote before the Los Angeles County Board of Supervisors between 2017 and 2020 (and perhaps even earlier) relating to the development and lease at issue in this litigation,”  Plaintiff’s belief that Movant has or may have testimony that may be relevant to the subject matter of the suit is not unreasonable, particularly given the broad scope of permissible discovery.  Moreover, Movant’s testimony may lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, through the identification of potential witnesses, facts or documents. 

Although Movant argues that Plaintiff’s intention is to embarrass Movant through reference to Movant’s criminal history, the court sees no indication that Plaintiff intends to depose Movant as to this subject.  The court strongly discourages Plaintiff from inquiring into clearly irrelevant and harassing areas.  Based upon the moving papers, Movant’s criminal proceedings and the conduct allegedly giving rise to those proceedings appear to fall into that category.  Movant may object and challenge any such line of questioning should it arise, and/or should the questioning become hostile or otherwise abusive. Movant has not otherwise established that the taking of a deposition would be unnecessary and unduly burdensome, considering Plaintiff’s assertion that Movant is the individual most knowledgeable as to the subjects on which Plaintiff seeks to depose him.

4.    Conclusion 
Based on the foregoing, the court DENIES Movant’s motion to quash deposition subpoena.

Movant is ordered to give notice.

1.     Background

This is a petition for transfer of structured settlement payment rights (the “Petition”). The real party in interest, Kaiwann King (“King”), wishes to transfer some portion of his settlement rights to Asset Security Consulting, LLC (“Petitioner”).

King is in possession of guaranteed monthly payments of $1,038.96, beginning on December 20, 2021 and continuing through November 20, 2051, as well as life-contingent monthly payments of $1,038.96, beginning on December 20, 2051 and continuing through King’s life. These payments arise from a personal injury case that was settled in 2016. (King Decl. ¶ 6.) King wishes to transfer future payments totaling $248,311.44, comprised of 239 monthly payments from January 20, 2032 through November 20, 2051. The discounted present value of the transfer amount has been calculated as $114,965.44, and King will receive $38,195.42 in exchange for the transfer.

2.     Discussion

Insurance Code section 10139.5 requires the direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights to be approved in advance in a final court order. (Ins. Code, §10139.6, subd. (a).) In its order, the Court must make the following express, written findings:

“(1) The transfer is in the best interest of the payee, taking into account the welfare and support of the payee's dependents.

(2) The payee has been advised in writing by the transferee to seek independent professional advice regarding the transfer and has either received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing, the opportunity to receive the advice.

(3) The transferee has complied with the notification requirements pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (f), the transferee has provided the payee with a disclosure form that complies with Section 10136, and the transfer agreement complies with Sections 10136 and 10138.

(4) The transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority.

(5) The payee understands the terms of the transfer agreement, including the terms set forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136.

(6) The payee understands and does not wish to exercise the payee's right to cancel the transfer agreement.”

(Ibid.)

When determining whether the proposed transfer is in the payee’s best interest, taking into account the welfare and support of the payee’s dependents, the Court must consider the totality of the circumstances, including, but not limited to, all of the following factors:

“… (4) The terms of the transaction, including whether the payee is transferring monthly or lump sum payments or all or a portion of his or her future payments.

… (8) Whether the payee has other means of income or support, aside from the structured settlement payments that are the subject of the proposed transfer, sufficient to meet the payee's future financial obligations for maintenance and support of the payee's dependents, specifically including, but not limited to, the payee's child support obligations, if any. The payee shall disclose to the transferee and the court his or her court-ordered child support or maintenance obligations for the court's consideration.

(9) Whether the financial terms of the transaction, including the discount rate applied to determine the amount to be paid to the payee, the expenses and costs of the transaction for both the payee and the transferee, the size of the transaction, the available financial alternatives to the payee to achieve the payee's stated objectives, are fair and reasonable.

(10) Whether the payee completed previous transactions involving the payee's structured settlement payments and the timing and size of the previous transactions and whether the payee was satisfied with any previous transaction.

…”

(Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (b).)

First, the court finds that the transfer is in the best interest of King.  (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(1).) (See King “Best Interest Affidavit”.) 

He  is an adult resident of Los Angeles County. (King Decl. ¶ 3.) King wishes to transfer a settlement amount with a discounted present value of $114,965.44 in exchange for $38,195.42. Were the court to approve the transfer, King’s remaining settlement would constitute 84 guaranteed monthly payments of $1038.96, totaling $56,103.84, plus life-contingent monthly payments of $1038.96 beginning from December 20, 2051 through the end of King’s life.

King is single, has no dependents, and has no child support obligations. (King Decl. ¶ 4-5.) He is twenty years old, is an unemployed student, and has income of $1038.96 per month arising from the settlement. (King Decl. ¶ 3.) He attests that, if the transfer is approved, he intends to use the funds to pay for tuition to become a clinical nutritionist and invest a portion of the transfer price with his specialized fiduciary financial advisory team in order to, after the investments have grown, purchase a home. (King Decl. ¶ 10.) King has disclosed a previous attempt to transfer that was voluntarily dismissed without prejudice on October 9, 2023. (King Decl. ¶ 9.)

King attests that the proposed transferred payments were not intended to be used for future medical expenses. (King Decl., p. 7.)

Second, the court finds that Kim has been advised in writing by Petitioner to seek independent professional advice regarding the transfer and has knowingly waived, in writing, the opportunity to receive that advice.  (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subdiv. (a)(2).)  (See, “Statement of Independent Professional Advice” executed by King.)

Third, the court finds that Petitioner has complied with the notification requirements pursuant to section 10139.5, subdivision (f)(2), Petitioner has provided King with a disclosure form that complies with section 10136, and the transfer agreement complies with sections 10136 and 10138.

Insurance Code section 10139.5, subdivision (f)(2) requires Petitioner to file and serve, not less than 20 days before the hearing on a petition for approval of a transfer of payment rights, a notice of the proposed transfer and the petition for its authorization, a copy of the proposed transfer agreement, a listing of each of the payee’s dependents, disclosures as required by section 10136, and, if available, copies of the annuity contract, any qualified assignment agreement, and the underlying structured settlement agreement.¿¿

Petitioner served King, the payment obligor, and the payment issuer with the Motion for Order Approving Petition for Approval for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights on December 23, 2024, i.e., 20 days before the January 14, 2025 hearing on the petition. (Pet., p. 83 [Proof of Service].) The First Amended Petition includes a copy of the proposed transfer agreement (Pet., Ex. B), a verified chart showing that King has no dependents (Pet., Ex. D), the disclosure statements for both California and New York (Pet., Ex. A), and the underlying qualified assignment agreement and annuity contract (Pet., Ex. H). The court therefore finds that Petitioner has complied with the notice requirements.

Section 10136 requires that the transfer agreement include certain information and meet certain requirements, including that it be written in 12-point type, state it will not be effective until a court enters a final order approving it and that payment can be delayed, and set forth certain information, including the net amount to be paid to the payee.¿ (Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (c).)¿ Section 10138 includes additional requirements, including that a transfer agreement cannot waive the seller/payee’s right to sue, require the seller/payee to indemnify the buyer in claims brought by the seller or on the seller’s behalf contesting the sale, or require the seller/payee to pay the buyer’s attorneys’ fees and costs.¿¿ (Ins. Code, § 10138, subd. (a).)¿ Petitioner has submitted a copy of the “California Disclosure Statement,” which was signed by King. (Pet., Ex. A.) The court finds that this disclosure statement complies with section 10136. The court further finds that the proposed transfer agreement (the “California Structured Settlement Payment Rights Purchase and Sale Agreement”) substantially complies with Sections 10136 and 10138.  (Pet., Ex. B.)

Fourth, the court finds that the transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Fifth, the court finds that King understands the terms of the transfer agreement, including the terms set forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(5); Confirmation of Structured Settlement Payment Transfer Agreement, Pet. Ex. B, C.)

Sixth, the court finds that King understands that he has the right to cancel the transfer agreement and does not wish to exercise that right. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5, subd. (a)(6); King Best Interest Affidavit, Ex. C.)

Based on the findings set forth above, and after considering the circumstances set forth in Insurance Code section 10139.5, subdivision (b)(1)-(b)(15) and King’s declaration, the court determines that the proposed transfer of the structured settlement payment rights should be approved and that the transfer is fair, reasonable, and in the payee’s best interest. The court therefore grants Petitioner’s Motion for Order Approving Petition for Approval for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights.

The court grants petitioner Asset Security Consulting, LLC’s Motion for Order Approving Petition for Approval for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights.

The court orders that the transfer of the structured settlement payment rights set forth in the “California Transfer Agreement,” as attached as Exhibit B to petitioner Asset Security Consulting, LLC’s Motion for Order Approving Petition for Approval for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payment Rights, is approved.

This order shall constitute a final “Qualified Order” pursuant to 26 U.S.C. section 5891.
