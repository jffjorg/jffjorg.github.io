DEPARTMENT 11 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV30808    Hearing Date: October 27, 2023    Dept: 11

21STCV30808 (Sotoodeh)

 

Tentative Ruling Re: Motion for Class Certification

 

Date:                           10/27/23

Time:                          1:45 pm

Moving Party:           Hossein Sotoodeh (“Plaintiff” or “Sotoodeh”)

Opposing Party:        Safe-Guard Products International, LLC (“Defendant” or “Safe-Guard”)

Department:              11

Judge:                        David S. Cunningham III

________________________________________________________________________

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion for class certification is denied without prejudice as to the proposed class.  The proposed class is not alleged in the complaint.  Plaintiff needs to amend the complaint to allege the proposed class, and Defendant needs to be given a chance to file a demurrer or motion to strike.

 

Plaintiff’s motion is denied as to the proposed subclass. Plaintiff fails to meet all certification prongs.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Safe-Guard provides vehicle service contracts (“VSCs”), which are agreements to repair and/or replace covered parts of leased vehicles.

 

Sotoodeh alleges that he obtained a VSC from Safe-Guard for his leased Rolls Royce.  (See Complaint, ¶¶ 6, 8.)  He claims the VSC required Safe-Guard to replace the vehicle’s tires, and charge him a $100 deductible, regardless of the number of tires that need to be replaced during a service visit.  (See id. at ¶¶ 1, 10.)  He alleges that Safe-Guard breached the VSC on multiple occasions by charging him $100 per tire instead of the $100 deductible.  (See id. at ¶¶ 1, 6, 8-20.)

 

The complaint asserts causes of action for breach of contract, violation of the Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”), and violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act (“CLRA”).  (See id. at ¶¶ 32-57.)

 

Here, Sotoodeh moves to certify a class and subclass of similarly situated customers.

 

DISCUSSION

 

The plaintiff bears the burden of demonstrating that class certification is proper.  (See City of San Jose v. Superior Court (1974) 12 Cal.3d 447, 460; see also Caro v. Procter & Gamble Co. (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 644, 654.)  He or she “must demonstrate the existence of an ascertainable and sufficiently numerous class, a well-defined community of interest, and substantial benefits from certification that render proceeding as a class superior to the alternatives.”  (Brinker Restaurant Corp. v. Superior Court (2012) 53 Cal.4th 1004, 1021.)

 

Scope of the Pleadings

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s motion should be denied without prejudice as to the proposed class because:

 

* the proposed class is much broader than the class alleged in the complaint;[1]

 

* it is appropriate to “deny class certification or [to] strike [the] certification motion where the plaintiffs seek certification that is beyond the scope of the pleadings” (Lampe v. Queen of the Valley Medical Center (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 832, 842-843; see also Jones v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 986, 999);

 

* if Plaintiff wants to continue pursuing the proposed class, he needs to amend the complaint; and

 

* Defendant needs to receive an opportunity to file a demurrer or motion to strike.

 

Providing Defendant an opportunity to file a demurrer or motion to strike is important because the proposed class appears to include people who did not suffer damages or actual harm and who may be unable to satisfy other critical elements of the causes of action – e.g., breach, reliance, and causation.  (See Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1388 [instructing that “a cause of action for damages for breach of contract is comprised of the following elements: (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to plaintiff.”], emphasis added; see also, e.g., Stern,, Business & Professions Code Section 17200 Practice (The Rutter Group March 2023 Update) ¶¶ 3:128.1 [citing In re Firearm Cases (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 959 for the proposition that “the ‘unfairness’ prong of the UCL requires proof of causation, i.e., a ‘link between a defendant’s business practice and the alleged harm[]’”]; Naranjo v. Doctors Medical Center of Modesto, Inc. (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1193, 1216 n.7 [noting that “reliance and causation of damages” are necessary elements under the CLRA].)

 

In reply, Plaintiff asserts that the proposed class is alleged in the complaint.  (See Reply, pp. 1-2.)

 

The Court disagrees.  Plaintiff’s own moving brief states that the proposed class is at odds with the complaint class.  Indeed, Plaintiff acknowledges that the complaint needs to be amended.  (See Motion, p. 5 n.10 [stating that “Plaintiff seeks to certify a Class that differs from that contemplated in the complaint” and that he “will promptly move for leave to amend his complaint to conform with the definitions of the proposed Class and Subclass”], emphasis added.)

 

The Court also disagrees with Plaintiff’s case citations.  (See Reply, pp. 3-4.)  None of his cases addresses the situation here.  None of them permits certification of an unalleged class, particularly an unalleged class that is broader than the alleged class and that contains members who arguably cannot meet the elements of the causes of action.  (See Harper v. 24 Hour Fitness, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 966 [analyzing the trial court’s decertification order and the plaintiffs’ post-certification attempts to modify the class definition]; see also Massachusetts Mutual Life Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 1282 [analyzing commonality and the requisite proof for class reliance under the UCL and the CLRA]; Brinker, supra, 53 Cal.4th 1004 [finding that the scope of the defendant’s duties to provide meal and rest periods constituted predominating common legal issues]; Arce v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471 [reversing the trial court’s demurrer ruling because the alleged facts created a “reasonable possibility” that the plaintiff would be able to show predominating common questions at the certification stage]; Sav-On Drug Stores, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 34 Cal.4th 319 [finding that the plaintiffs’ misclassification theories raised common legal questions and were “amenable to class treatment”].)

 

The proposed subclass is a different story.  It is nearly the same as the complaint class.[2]  The Court finds that the motion should go forward as to the proposed subclass and that the certification prongs should be analyzed.

 

Ascertainable Class

 

A class is “ascertainable when it is defined ‘in terms of objective characteristics and common transactional facts’ that make ‘the ultimate identification of class members possible when that identification becomes necessary.’”  (Noel v. Thrifty Payless, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 955, 980.)

 

“The class must be ‘numerous’ in size.  But there is no fixed minimum or maximum number . . . The numerosity analysis is limited to how many individuals fall within the class definition and whether their joinder is impracticable, not how many ‘net’ class members there might be after considering affirmative defenses.” (Edmon & Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civ. Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group June 2023 Update) ¶ 14:21, emphasis in original.)

 

“A party seeking class certification bears the burden of satisfying the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 382, including numerosity, and the trial court is entitled to consider ‘the totality of the evidence in making [the] determination’ of whether a ‘plaintiff has presented substantial evidence of the class action requisites.’” (Soderstedt v. CBIZ S. California, LLC (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 133, 154.)

 

Plaintiff’s showing suffices.  The parties agree that the proposed subclass has at least nine potential members.  (See Motion, pp. 9-10; see also Opposition, p. 18.)  Nine is an adequate number.  (See Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:21.1 [instructing that “[n]o minimum number of plaintiffs is required as a matter of law for maintenance of a state court class action”]; see also id. at ¶ 14:21.3 [recognizing that a class of 10 is permissible]; Hendershot v. Ready to Roll Transportation, Inc. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1213, 1222-1223 [reversing the trial court’s denial of a nine-member class].)  Moreover, Defendant’s person most knowledgeable (“PMK”) found the nine customers through an internal audit (see Archer Decl., ¶¶ 64-65), so “[t]here is no dispute that Defendant possesses the means to identify members.”  (Motion, p. 11; see also Reyes v. San Diego County Bd. of Supervisors (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 1263, 1274-1275 [noting that “the issue is whether there exists sufficient means for identifying class members”], emphasis in original.)[3]

 

Predominating Common Questions

 

“[T]he proponent of certification must show, inter alia, that questions of law or fact common to the class predominate over the questions affecting the individual members[.]” (Washington Mutual Bank, FA v. Superior Court (2001) 24 Cal.4th 906, 913.) This means “each member must not be required to individually litigate numerous and substantial questions to determine his [or her] right to recover following the class judgment[.]”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:11.6.)  “[T]he issues which may be jointly tried, when compared with those requiring separate adjudication, must be sufficiently numerous and substantial to make the class action advantageous to the judicial process and to the litigants.” (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff claims “common questions . . . are likely to arise” because “Defendant itself told Plaintiff that the deductible overcharges he was subjected to were due to a ‘system malfunction in which the computer program used to process claims was inadvertently applying the $100 deductible to all tires and not the claim as should be the case.’”  (Motion, p. 12.)  Plaintiff says “[t]here is no evidence that this ‘system malfunction’ solely impacted Plaintiff.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendant contends:

 

Notably, instead of affirmatively proving the existence of class-wide “system malfunction,” Plaintiff argues there is no evidence that this ‘system malfunction’ solely impacted Plaintiff.” [Citation.] Plaintiff, however, attempts to flip the burden of proof. It is not SafeGuard’s burden of proving the absence of a class-wide “system malfunction,” whatever that might be since, again, it is unexplained by Plaintiff. Rather, it is Plaintiff’s burden of proving that this system malfunction adversely affected the class such that there is a predominating common issue.

 

Without evidence of class wide conduct causing harm to members of the classes, Plaintiff provides no basis for this Court to conclude that the salient factual and legal issues attendant to his three causes of action will not give rise to predominating individualized questions. Without more, questions of whether there has even been harm, much less the extent of that harm for damage calculation purposes, so as to give rise to a claim requires an examination of each class member’s circumstances. Also, without more, claims that class members have been subject to misleading and/or fraudulent advertisement (both of which are required under Plaintiff’s CLRA and fraud-based UCL claim) would need to be assessed on an individualized class member basis.

 

The specter of predominating individualized questions of fact and law are particularly heightened with Plaintiff’s proposed Subclass. While Safe-Guard has identified nine possible instances where customers have been charged an excessive deductible, it is Plaintiff’s contention that there are thousands more members of this Subclass that have not been identified due to so-called “critical gaps and limitations in Defendant’s records.” [Citations.]

 

Safe-Guard disagrees with this speculative assertion. Nevertheless, as made clear by this view of his Subclass, Plaintiff challenges the accuracy of Safe-Guard’s records and intends to seek discovery confirming the same. Assuming the truth of Plaintiff’s assertion, however, would require an individualized examination of each and every one of the more than 300,000 claims at issue to review the underlying records applicable to those claims (and possibly the records of dealers that provided repairs to confirm the same) to determine (1) the accuracy of Plaintiff’s electronic records concerning deductibles and (2) whether a customer was incorrectly assessed a multiple deductible. This cannot be done on a class wide basis. Resolution of claims of each member of the Subclass would require litigation of the facts of each member of that class.

 

(Opposition, pp. 16-17, emphasis in original, footnote omitted.)

 

Plaintiff responds:

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has not provided “evidence” of the extent of Defendant’s class-wide conduct and therefore individualized questions predominate. [Citation.] This is incorrect. In determining whether there is sufficient evidence to support certification, courts are to “consider whether the theory of recovery advanced by the proponents of certification is, as an analytical matter, likely to prove amenable to class treatment.” [Citation.] Plaintiff has submitted sufficient evidence to show that Defendant employed uniform, systemic practices and subjected all Class and Subclass members to common systems, policies and procedures for claims processing, and further, Defendant’s own data supports this claim. [Citations.] . . . Further, it is not Plaintiff’s duty to explain to Defendant its own assertion that Plaintiff’s claim arose due to a system malfunction – Defendant itself stated that it had a system malfunction. [Citation.] The exact scope of that system malfunction is, again, a merits issue.

 

(Reply, p. 11.)

 

The Court agrees with Defendant.  To show that a “system malfunction” occurred, Plaintiff cites paragraph seven of his declaration.  (See Motion, p. 12; see also Reply, p. 11.)  Paragraph seven states that, on May 7, 2021, Safe-Guard corresponded with him, “explain[ing] that the overcharges were the result of ‘a system malfunction in which the computer program used to process claims was inadvertently applying the $100 deductible to all tires and not the claim as should be the case.’”  (Sotoodeh Decl., ¶ 7; see also Pang Decl., Ex. 2.)  The statement is hearsay, and, anyway, it does not show that the malfunction affected other customers.  Plaintiff’s argument – the record lacks evidence that the malfunction “solely impacted Plaintiff” (Motion, p. 12) – amounts to an improper attempt to shift the burden to Defendant and fails to justify an inference of commonality.

 

Plaintiff’s additional evidence is unavailing.  Defendant’s PMK testified.  Plaintiff cites pages 38, 39, and 72 of his testimony.  (See Motion, p. 12; see also Reply, p. 11.)  On those pages, the PMK says Safe-Guard’s underwriting department manually enters all VSCs into Safe-Guard’s CMS computer system (see Pang Decl., Ex. 1, pp. 38-39) and Safe-Guard’s call center enters and processes customer claims through Safe-Guards’ SG-Connect computer system.  (See id. at Ex. 1, p. 72.)  These statements fail to demonstrate a common malfunction and common mischarges.

 

The fact that Defendant’s PMK found nine potential members does not change the result. The PMK reviewed Safe-Guard’s computer systems. Instead of confirming that mischarges and excessive payments took place, his declaration is agnostic, noting that nonparty dealership records need to be examined to determine what really happened.  (See Archer Decl., ¶¶ 64-65.)  Plaintiff fails to cite or attach the dealership records.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s burden unsatisfied.

 

Typical Class Representative

 

“The ‘test of typicality is whether other members have the same or similar injury, whether the action is based on conduct which is not unique to the named plaintiffs, and whether other class members have been injured by the same course of conduct.’”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:29 [quoting Martinez v. Joe’s Crab Shack Holdings (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 362, 375].)

 

“That the purported class representative's claims must be ‘typical’ does not mean they must be identical to the claims of other class members.  It is sufficient that the representative is similarly situated so that he or she will have the motive to litigate on behalf of all class members.”  (Id. at ¶ 14:29.2.)  “Thus, it is not necessary that the class representative have personally incurred all of the damages suffered by each of the other class members.”  (Ibid., emphasis in original.)

 

However, the class representative’s claim is atypical “if it is subject to ‘factually intensive or legally complex unique defenses that pose any significant risk of diverting [plaintiff’s] attention from class issues.’”  (Id. at ¶ 14:35.10 [citing Fireside Bank v. Superior Court (2007) 40 Cal.4th 1069] emphasis in original.)

 

Plaintiff contends “he ‘was subjected to the same alleged wrong, by the same defendant, as the other members of the putative class[.]”  (Motion, p. 13.)

 

Defendant argues:

 

* Plaintiff fails to cite evidence showing that the putative members “were the subject of any conduct that resulted in any harm, much less the same conduct and same harm, concerning the alleged charging of excessive deductibles” (Opposition, p. 14);

 

* in some instances, the dealerships, not Defendant, overcharged Plaintiff (see id. at pp. 14-15); and

 

* unique defenses and cross-claims (unclean hands, breach of contract, estoppel, unjust enrichment, and fraud) apply to Plaintiff (see id. at pp. 15-16).  

 

Plaintiff asserts in reply that “the existence of affirmative defenses” does not render a class representative atypical and that Defendant fails to cite substantial evidence supporting the affirmative defenses.  (Reply, pp. 8-11 [arguing that the affirmative defenses “bear no relation to the ‘major focus of the litigation,’ which concerns whether Defendant’s system, which includes its practices and procedures for claims processing, is susceptible to charging incorrect deductibles and whether multiple deductibles were improperly charged by Defendant”], emphasis in original.)

 

The Court agrees with Defendant’s first point.  As explained above, Plaintiff’s evidence fails to demonstrate a common malfunction and common mischarges. Plaintiff fails to show that the members of the proposed subclass “have the same or similar injury” and “have been injured by the same course of conduct.’”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:29.)

 

It is unnecessary to address Defendant’s other points. 

 

As a matter of guidance, though, it appears premature to decide whether Defendant’s defenses and cross-claims make Plaintiff atypical.  Defendant contends:

 

* Plaintiff sought to replace tires “on vehicles that were used for ‘prohibited commercial purposes’” (Opposition, p. 15); and

 

* he filed substantially more replacement claims than average customers filed (see id. at p. 16).

 

These are merits arguments.  They probably cannot be resolved on the current record given that the parties have not conducted merits discovery yet.  At this time, the record seems too undeveloped.

 

Adequate Representation

 

“The class representative, through qualified counsel, must be capable of ‘vigorously and tenaciously’ protecting the interests of the class members.”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:36.)

 

“The prospective class representative must file a declaration stating that he or she desires to represent the class and understands the fiduciary obligations of serving as class representative.  Counsel’s declaration to that effect will not suffice.”  (Ibid.)

 

Defendant does not challenge the adequacy prong. (See Opposition, pp. 12-18.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s evidence meets his burden. (See Sotoodeh Decl., ¶¶ 10-13; see also Pang Decl., ¶ 7, Ex. 7.)

 

Manageable and Superior

 

“The proponent of class certification must demonstrate that the proposed class action is manageable [citation].”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:11.10.)  “This requires the trial court ‘to carefully weigh the respective benefits and burdens of a class action, and to permit its maintenance only where substantial benefits will be accrued by both litigants and the courts alike.’  [Citation.]”  (Ibid., emphasis in original.) 

 

“In certifying a class action, the court must also conclude that litigation of individual issues, including those arising from affirmative defenses, can be managed fairly and efficiently.”  (Duran v. U.S. Bank Nat. Assn. (2014) 59 Cal. 4th 1, 28-29.)  “Trial courts must pay careful attention to manageability when deciding whether to certify a class action.  In considering whether a class action is a superior device for resolving a controversy, the manageability of individual issues is just as important as the existence of common questions uniting the proposed class.”  (Id. at 29.)

 

“A class action is not ‘superior’ where there are numerous and substantial questions affecting each class member's right to recover, following determination of liability to the class as a whole.”  (Edmon & Karnow, supra, at ¶ 14:46, emphasis in original.)

 

This prong is uncontested.  (See Opposition, pp. 12-18.)

 

Still, the Court finds the prong unsatisfied.  While nine potential members is a manageable number, the class method is inferior since Plaintiff fails to establish predominating common issues and typicality.  

 

Summary

 

Plaintiff’s motion is denied without prejudice as to the proposed class because it is not alleged in the complaint.

 

The motion is denied as to the proposed subclass because Plaintiff fails to show predominating common questions, typicality, and superiority.
