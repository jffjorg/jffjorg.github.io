DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV02007    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Michele Bidinger,

 

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Ralph, Estate of Shaffer, et al.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

  22VECV02007         107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

Plaintiff Michele Bidinger’s attorney Gary Kurtz (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel.

 

Counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff is GRANTED.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

This is a landlord-tenant dispute. Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Estate of Ralph Shaffer, Abrienne Shaffer, Vivian Sypien, Andrew Sypien, and Does 1 to 50 on November 15, 2022. The complaint alleges causes of action for statutory violations and breach of implied covenant of quiet enjoyment.

 

Counsel filed a motion to be relieved as counsel (the “Motion”) on August 30, 2024. No opposition was filed.

 

At a hearing on September 26, 2024, this court granted Counsel’s oral motion to continue the hearing so Counsel could file a revised proposed order. (09/26/24 Minute Order; 09/30/24 Notice of Ruling.)

 

Counsel filed revised proposed orders on September 26, 2024, and October 4, 2024.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 284(2).) California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.     Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051));

2.     A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052));

3.     Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4.     The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) “The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case.” (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 554, 559.)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

(A) Notice—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)

 

A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under California Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-051).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

(B) Declaration—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)

 

The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement by explaining on form MC-052 that “There has been a complete breakdown of the attorney client relationship. Client has stated that she does not want me to be her lawyer and that a lawyer will replace me. After waiting an extended period of time for tht [sic] to occur, and after being unable to respond to discovery or a settlement offer because client refuses to communicate with me, I have no choice but to ask for leave to withdraw.”

 

(C) Service—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d); Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, Rule 4.35

 

The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service or mail. If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

1.     The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

2.     The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.

 

As used in this rule, “current” means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011, subdivision (b) applies.

 

When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 4.35.)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement. Although Counsel did not attach a proof of service to each filing, Counsel filed a proof of service attached to the MC-051 filing that indicates the motion, declaration, and order were served on Plaintiff by mail.  Counsel’s declaration on MC-052 states that the Motion’s filings were served on Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last-known address and the address at which the papers were served was confirmed to be Plaintiff’s current address within the past 30 days by referencing her employment records and an internet search. Counsel also states a courtesy copy will be sent by email to Plaintiff.  Finally, Counsel states Counsel has been unable to confirm that the address is current or to locate a more current address after calling Plaintiff’s last known telephone number, contacting persons familiar with the client, and conducting a Zillow search to see if Plaintiff’s apartment has been on the market recently.

 

(D) Order—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)

 

The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.

 

Counsel’s revised proposed order filed on October 4, 2024, complies with this requirement.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion.

IV.           DEMURRER - LEGAL STANDARD 

¿¿ 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or the facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 745.)¿A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a]¿statement¿of the¿facts¿constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿425.10(a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)¿

 

V.              DEMURRER - DISCUSSION 

 

  1. Joinder

 

“All persons may join in one action as plaintiffs if: (1) They assert any right to relief jointly, severally, or in the alternative, in respect of or arising out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences and if any question of law or fact common to all these persons will arise in the action; or (2) They have a claim, right, or interest adverse to the defendant in the property or controversy which is the subject of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 378.)

 

“A common interest in the subject matter of the action has been found to exist where four plaintiffs sued upon four separate causes of action on common counts for money had and received in connection with purported sales of securities in violation of the Corporate Securities Act. [Citations.] And a right to relief arising out of the same transaction or series of transactions exists where several plaintiffs sue for personal injuries suffered in the same accident [citations]; where two persons are falsely imprisoned by the same acts committed at the same time [citation]; where taxpayers join to challenge the validity of assessments or recover taxes [citations]; where holders of separate oil leases sue for an alleged trespass resulting from the drilling of one well by defendant [citation]; and where plaintiffs whose causes of action are based upon misrepresentation or conspiracy and fraud allege a single scheme, depending on the same basic misrepresentations and leading to a series of transactions exactly similar in kind and manner of operation [citations].” (Moe v. Anderson (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 826, 832.)

 

The complaint alleges twenty-three causes of action for breach of written contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty.

 

Plaintiff Nikal, LTD. (“Nikal”) brings causes of actions for breach of written contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty related to the matters Nikal, Ltd. v. Kyle L. Carper, Nikal Ltd. v. Ashley Goldson, and Nikal Ltd. v. Samantha Kline, et al. (Complaint Causes of Action 1-9.)

 

Plaintiff GNM, L.P. (“GNM”) brings causes of action for breach of written contract related to the matters GNM, LP v. Daniel Zepeda and GNM, LP v. Matthew Stevenson, et al. (Complaint Causes of Action 10-11.)

 

Plaintiff Blue Sky Rainbow, LP (“Blue Sky Rainbow”) brings causes of action for breach of written contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty related to the matter Blue Sky Rainbow, LP v. Sandy Ortiz. (Complaint Causes of Action 12-14.)

 

Plaintiff Animal Kingdom, L.P. (“Animal Kingdom”) brings causes of action for breach of written contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty related to the matter Animal Kingdom, LP v. Monica Marie Castro. (Complaint Causes of Action 15-17.)

 

Plaintiff AJ Rock, LTD (“AJ Rock”) brings causes of action for breach of written contract, legal malpractice, and breach of fiduciary duty related to the matters AJ Rock, LP v. Veronica Quintana and AJ Rock, LP v. Raquel Gonzalez. (Complaint Causes of Action 18-23.)

 

Plaintiffs’ complaint does not assert any right to relief jointly or severally. (Complaint pp. 44-47.) Further, there are five distinct plaintiffs suing for breaches of written contract, legal malpractice, and breaches of fiduciary duty related to separate underlying matters, which occurred at separate times. Accordingly, Plaintiff has not alleged that the causes of action arose out of the “same transaction [or] occurrence.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 378.) Finally, Plaintiffs do not allege that the causes of action involve a “common scheme to defraud a group of plaintiffs, ‘depending upon the same basic misrepresentations, and leading to a series of transactions exactly similar in kind and manner of operation which have affected all of the plaintiffs.” (Moe v. Anderson, supra, 207 Cal.App.4th at pp. 833-834.) Thus, Plaintiffs have not alleged the facts underlying the various causes of action are a related series of transactions or occurrences. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 378.)

 

Defendants’ demurrer mentions severance.  (Demurrer p. 5.) The proper remedy may be to sever the cases or to give Plaintiffs leave to amend. In Moe v. Anderson, the court mentions sustaining a demurrer without leave to amend. Additionally, California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(d) states that a party can challenge misjoinder of parties in a demurrer, but it does not state whether the court can sever the cases or whether the court should sustain with leave to amend.

 

Accordingly, this court sustains Defendants’ demurrer with leave to amend to address, if possible, whether the Plaintiffs here may be joined in one action.

    1. Legal Malpractice

 

“To state a cause of action for legal malpractice, a plaintiff must plead the duty of the attorney to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as members of his or her profession commonly possess and exercise; breach of that duty; a proximate causal connection between the breach and the resulting injury; and actual loss or damage resulting from the attorney's negligence.” (Kasem v. Dion-Kindem (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1399.)

Plaintiffs’ second, fifth, eighth, thirteenth, sixteenth, nineteenth, and twenty-second causes of action are for legal malpractice.

Plaintiffs allege Defendants owed and breached “their duty of care, and have fallen below the applicable standard of care required of attorneys performing legal services of the type rendered by defendants” by “fail[ing] to prepare the manager for testimony”; “fail[ing] to review the tenant ledger in advance. . .”; and “send[ing] a stand-in attorney on the day of trial who was completely unprepared and unfamiliar with the file[.]” (Complaint ¶ 25.) Plaintiffs further state that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . . in representing plaintiff, plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $15,000.” (Complaint ¶ 26.) 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their second cause of action.

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their thirteenth cause of action.

 

                                                  v.     Sixteenth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants owed and breached their “duty of due care, and have fallen below the applicable standard of care required of attorneys performing legal services rendered by defendants” by “fail[ing] to notice that the three-day notice was defective, as a result of which it had to be re-served” and sending an “inexperienced, unprepared, and incompetent” attorney to cover the trial. (Complaint ¶ 117.) Plaintiffs also allege Defendants caused an injury and resulting damages, stating that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . . in representing plaintiff, plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $10,000.00.” (Complaint ¶ 119.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their sixteenth cause of action.

 

                                                vi.     Nineteenth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants owed and breached “their duty of due care, and have fallen below the applicable standard of care required by attorneys performing legal services of the type rendered by defendants” because “[w]hile the Quintana Unlawful Detainer Action was pending, the parties agreed to settle on terms that allowed the tenant to remain in possession, subject to a payment plan; if the tenant defaulted, the landlord was to be able to proceed with lockout immediately. Defendants drafted a stipulation for unlawful detainer judgment which . . . was defective [and a]s a result, when the tenant defaulted, a whole new unlawful detainer case had to be filed and litigated to a conclusion.” (Complaint ¶ 136.) Plaintiffs also allege Defendants caused an injury and resulting damages, stating that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . . plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $25,000.00.” (Complaint ¶ 137.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their nineteenth cause of action.

 

                                               vii.     Twenty-Second Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendants owed and breached “their duty of due care, and have fallen below the applicable standard of care required of attorneys performing legal services of the type rendered by defendants” because “[a]fter running up a bill for $8,468.34, defendants informed plaintiff that the three-day notice was defective, the case would have to be dismissed and the whole process started all over again. They had failed to check or notice whether the three-day notice was in proper form.” (Complaint ¶ 155.) “As a consequence, Plaintiff was required to re-serve the three-day notice and commence a whole new unlawful detainer proceeding in order to evict the tenants.” (Complaint ¶ 155.) Plaintiffs also allege Defendants caused an injury, stating that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . . in representing plaintiff, plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $15,000.00.” (Complaint ¶ 156.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their twenty-second cause of action.

    1. Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

The elements of a breach of fiduciary claim are: (1) existence of a fiduciary duty; (2) breach of the duty; and (3) damage caused by the breach. (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.) The relationship between an attorney and his or her client is a fiduciary relationship. (Fair v. Bakhtiari (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1135, 1140.)

 

Plaintiffs’ third, sixth, ninth, fourteenth, seventeenth, twentieth, and twenty-third causes of action are for breach of fiduciary duty.

 

                                                    i.     Third Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiff’s attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . .” (Complaint ¶ 29.) Plaintiff next alleges that Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by failing to “exercise the degree of care, skill and competence commonly possessed by attorneys in the legal community of Los Angeles” and “fail[ing] to prepare the manager for testimony”; “fail[ing] to review the tenant ledger in advance. . .”; and “send[ing] a stand-in attorney on the day of trial who was completely unprepared and unfamiliar with the file[.]” (Complaint ¶ 30.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege damages caused by the breach, stating that because Defendants sent an unprepared attorney, “plaintiff lost its case and had to start [the] eviction process all over again” and “plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $15,000.00.” (Complaint ¶¶ 30-31.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their third cause of action.

 

                                                  ii.     Sixth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiff’s attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . .” (Complaint ¶ 48.) Plaintiffs next allege Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by failing to “exercise the degree of care, skill and competence commonly possessed by attorneys in the legal community of Los Angeles County” and by “fail[ing] to notice that the three-day notice was defective, for failure to state the number of bedrooms.” (Complaint ¶ 49.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege damages caused by the breach, stating that “[a]s a result, the case had to be dismissed and refiled” and “Defendants caused the loss of at least two months of rent by this delay in the unlawful detainer process” and “plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $10,000.00.” (Complaint ¶¶ 49-50.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their sixth cause of action.

 

                                                iii.     Ninth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiff’s attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . . .” (Complaint ¶ 67.) Plaintiffs next allege Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by failing to “exercise the degree of care, skill and competence commonly possessed by attorneys in the legal community of Los Angeles County” and “[c]ontrary to client instructions, defendants engaged in extensive settlement negotiations instead of proceeding directly to trial.” (Complaint ¶ 68.) Plaintiffs also allege that “[w]hen negotiations failed, defendants were not ready to proceed to trial.” (Complaint ¶ 68.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege damages caused by the breach, stating that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . . in representing plaintiff, plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $15,000.00.” (Complaint ¶ 68.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their ninth cause of action.

 

                                                iv.     Fourteenth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiff’s attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . . .” (Complaint ¶ 102.) Plaintiffs next allege that Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by failing to “exercise the degree of care, skill and competence commonly possessed by attorneys in the legal community of Los Angeles County” and “enter[ing] into a stipulation on behalf of plaintiff on the same date as a default had been submitted for entry.” (Complaint ¶ 103.) Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s a result, the tenant was permitted to remain in the apartment unit and plaintiff was unable to recover possession of the subject unit.” (Complaint ¶ 103.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege damages caused by the breach, stating that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . .  in representing plaintiff, plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $9,836.42.” (Complaint ¶ 104.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their fourteenth cause of action.

 

                                                  v.     Seventeenth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiff’s attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . . .” (Complaint ¶ 121.) Plaintiffs next allege Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by failing to “exercise the degree of care, skill and competence commonly possessed by attorneys in the legal community of Los Angeles County.” (Complaint ¶ 122.) Plaintiffs further allege Defendants “failed to notice that the three-day notice was defective, as a result of which it had to be re-served” and sent an ”inexperienced, unprepared, and incompetent” attorney to cover the trial. (Complaint ¶ 122.) Finally, Plaintiffs allege damages caused by the breach, stating that “[a]s a direct and proximate result of defendants’ . . . breaches of the standard of care . . . in representing plaintiff, plaintiff has suffered damages in the minimum sum of $10,000.00.” (Complaint ¶ 123.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged facts supporting their seventeenth cause of action.

 

                                                vi.     Twentieth Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiff’s attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . . .” (Complaint ¶ 140.) Plaintiffs next allege Defendants breached their fiduciary duty by failing to “exercise the degree of care, skill and competence commonly possessed by attorneys in the legal community of Los Angeles County.” (Complaint ¶ 141.) Plaintiffs allege that “[w]hile the Quintana Unlawful Detainer Action was pending, the parties agreed to settle on terms that allowed the tenant to remain in possession, subject to a payment plan; if the tenant defaulted, the landlord was to be able to proceed with lockout immediately. Defendants drafted a stipulation for unlawful detainer judgment, which, however, was defective, in that it did not make reference to the landlord’s right to immediate recovery of possession of the rental premises upon a default in the payment plan.                                                vii.     Twenty-Third Cause of Action

 

Plaintiffs allege that “[a]s plaintiffs attorneys, defendants owed plaintiff a fiduciary duty . . . .” (Complaint ¶ 149.) VI.           MOTION TO STRIKE

 

“The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

  1. Plaintiffs’ Punitive Damages Claims

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs’ claims for punitive damages should be struck because Plaintiff has not alleged Defendant is guilty of fraud, oppression, or malice. (Motion to Strike p. 11.)

 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.” (Id.) Malice is defined “as conduct ‘intended by the defendant to cause injury to plaintiff, or despicable conduct that is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others.” (Id.) Oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Id.) Fraud is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Id.)

 

Plaintiffs seek punitive damages for their third, sixth, ninth, fourteenth, seventeenth, twentieth, and twenty-third causes of action – which are for breach of fiduciary duty. (Complaint pp. 44-46.)  Plaintiffs argue punitive damages are available in a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty and Defendants’ conduct amounted to oppression and fraud because “plaintiffs were charged fees and costs for services that were clearly subpar.” (Opposition p. 9.) Plaintiffs also argue Defendants’ false representations about their skill and “defendants’ astonishing incompetence” support Plaintiffs’ claims for punitive damages. (Opposition pp. 9-10.)

 

This court finds that Plaintiffs’ allegations do not rise to the level of fraud, oppression, or malice.  Accordingly, this court sustains the motion to strike Plaintiffs’ punitive damages allegations, with leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court, supra, 180 Cal. App.3d at pp. 303-304 [“[A] motion to strike paragraphs in a complaint should be granted with leave to amend where . . . the defect, though one of substance, may possibly be cured by supplying omitted allegations, and the plaintiff has not had a fair opportunity to do so”].)

 

  1. Plaintiffs’ General and Special Damages Claims

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ claims for special damages should be struck because “the evidence presented fails to establish a clear and direct link between the defendant’s alleged actions and the alleged financial losses.” (Motion to Strike p. 12.) Defendant also argues Plaintiff’s claims for general damages should be struck because “the complaint does not specify the amounts [and] what types of damages the amount[s] requested are for[.]”

 

This court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged damages resulting from each of their causes of action, so this court denies Defendants’ request to strike Plaintiffs’ claims for special and general damages.

 

  1. Other Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint

 

Defendants seek to strike portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint because the statements are “irrelevant, false, improper, and/or not drawn in conformity with the laws, local rules, or order of court.” (Motion to Strike.)

 

It is not clear from the face of the complaint and matters this court has taken judicial notice of that Plaintiffs’ statements are irrelevant, false, improper, or not drawn in conformity with the laws, local rules, or order of the court. Accordingly, this court denies Defendants’ motion to strike other portions of Plaintiffs’ complaint.

 

VII.         LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.) Courts generally allow at least one time to amend a complaint, after sustaining a demurrer, even without any request for leave to amend. (McDonald v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal. App. 3d 297, 303-304.)¿ 

 

Plaintiffs have not yet filed an amended complaint and this court finds that there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment.

 

VIII.      CONCLUSION

¿ 

Accordingly, this court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend.
