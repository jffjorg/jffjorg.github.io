COMPLAINT FILED: OCTOBER 13, 2021

I.       BACKGROUND  

On October 13, 2021, Plaintiff Rancho Palos Verdes Estates Community Association (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint for damages and an injunction against Defendants Giovanni Funiciello (“Giovanni”), Christina Funiciello (“Christina”), CSI Services (“CSI”), Frank Colaruotolo (“Colaruotolo”), and Does to 10.

Plaintiff is a homeowners association (“HOA”) that owns, occupies, and controls the common area of Rancho Palos Verdes Estates Community (“RPVE”). (Complaint ¶ 1.) RPVE is a gated community whose entrance lies south of Crest Road. (Ibid.)

Giovanni and Christina Funiciello own a home north of Crest Road, across the street from RPVE. (Complaint ¶ 2.) Plaintiff alleges that five utility poles have been on the Funiciello property since at least 2004 and that, in 2019, Defendants began work to move at least three of these poles to RPVE’s property. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7-8.) As a result, Plaintiff seeks damages and an injunction.

On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for leave to augment their expert witness list.

The motion was taken under submission on November 26, 2024. On December 3, 2024, the Court issued a ruling denying the motion as moot.

On December 12, 2024, Defendants Giovanni and Christina Funiciello (hereinafter “Defendants”) filed the instant motion for reconsideration of the Court’s December 3, 2024, ruling denying Plaintiff’s April 10, 2024, motion for leave to augment expert witness list as moot.

On December 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

On January 7, 2025, Defendants filed their reply.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

C.C.P. §1008(a) provides, as follows:  “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Defendants move for reconsideration of the Court’s December 3, 2024, ruling denying Plaintiff’s April 10, 2024, motion for leave to augment expert witness list on the grounds that the motion was moot. (Motion p. 1:22-2-3.) It appears that Defendant does not wish for the Court to change its denial, but rather issue a correction regarding its grounds for the denial. “. . . Defendants respectfully ask this Court to reconsider its Minute Order and adopt its Tentative Ruling of November 25, 2024, which correctly DENIED Plaintiff’s Motion.” (Motion p. 4:20-24.)

The Court finds Defendants’ request confusing. A motion for reconsideration must be based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law. (CCP § 1008(a).) Here, Defendants fail to provide any new or different facts, circumstances, or law. Instead, Defendants appear to argue that the Court, in its December 3, 2024, Minute Order, denied Plaintiff’s motion as moot on the grounds that discovery was still open. (See December 3, 2024, Minute Order.) In Defendants’ instant motion, Defendants argue that “[d]iscovery is and has been closed since February 13, 2024 . . .” (Motion p. 4:18-22.) However, as noted by the court in the December 3, 2024, Minute Order, on May 30, 2024, after Plaintiff’s filing of their motion to augment expert witness list, the Court advanced and vacated the August 20, 2024, trial date due to the action’s pending reassignment to an independent calendar court. (See December 3, 2024, and May 30, 2024, Minute Orders.) The May 30, 2024, Minute Order, provided no indication that discovery or other cut-off dates remained tied to the August 20, 2024, trial date. (Ibid.) Therefore, in its December 3, 2024, Minute Order, the Court ordered that all discovery cutoff dates were to follow the final trial date of August 25, 2025. (See December 3, 2024, Minute Order.) As a result, Plaintiff’s motion to augment their expert witness list was properly denied as moot since discovery remains open.

Defendants argue that “[t]he parties never agreed to re-open discovery since it closed 30 days before the March 13, 2024[sic] trial date.” (Motion p. 4:17-18.) However, the Court may reopen or change discovery cutoff dates sua sponte, as it did in its December 3, 2024, Minute Order, as it has an inherent power to manage its own calendar. (Walker v. Superior Court (1991) 53 Cal.3d 257, 266-267.) Therefore, on both procedural and substantive grounds, Defendants’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On February 23, 2022, Plaintiff Yu Chol Kim (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants RD America, LLC (“RD America”), Jetro Cash & Carry (“Jetro”), and Does 1 to 20 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for premises liability. The complaint arises from a March 9, 2020, incident, where Plaintiff claims to have incurred injuries and damages after slipping and falling on a piece of fruit on Defendants’ premises.

On December 27, 2022, Plaintiff filed their first amended complaint (“FAC”) adding a cause of action for general negligence.

On March 22, 2024, RD America filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s FAC.

On December 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition. 

RD America has not filed a reply.

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

RD America moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s FAC on the grounds that neither RD America nor its employees created or had actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition upon which Plaintiff bases his causes of action for general negligence and premises liability. (Motion p. 2:8-11.)

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).) 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [it is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) “The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence. 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Defendant’s Burden 

RD America meets their initial burden. 

RD America moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s FAC on the grounds that neither RD America nor its employees created or had actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition upon which Plaintiff bases his causes of action for general negligence and premises liability. (Motion p. 2:8-11.)

To succeed on a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must show that: (1) defendant owed a legal duty to plaintiff to use due care; (2) defendant breached that duty; (3) defendant’s breach was the actual or proximate cause of plaintiff’s harm; and (4) plaintiff suffered damages as a result. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318; Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.) “Superimposed upon the criteria for determining the existence of a duty of care in a particular category of negligence . . . are . . . universal factors”, including foreseeability of harm. (Hamilton v. Gage Bowl (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1706, 1713.)

Premises liability is a form of negligence. (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619.) To succeed on a claim of premises liability, a plaintiff must show that (1) the defendant was the owner, occupant, or lessor of the premises; (2) the defendant was negligent in the use, maintenance, or management of the premises; and (3) such negligence was the cause of injury, damage, loss, or harm to plaintiff. (Ibid.) “A landowner owes a duty to exercise reasonable care to maintain his or her property in such a manner as to avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of injury.” (Barnes v. Black (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 1473, 1478.)

Here, RD America argues that Plaintiff cannot establish a breach of duty because RD America exercised due care in maintaining its premises. The following facts are undisputed: the underlying incident occurred when Plaintiff entered a RD America warehouse and slipped and fell on a piece of produce on the floor, outside of the warehouse, near the front entrance. (Plaintiff’s Response to Separate Statement of Facts “PR-SSF” No. 3; Exh. A [Bubion Decl.] at ¶¶ 3, 5, and 7; Exh. B [FAC]; Exh. C [Plaintiff Depo.] at 59:21-60:4 and 60:16-18; Exh. E [Begando Depo.] at 22:16-21].) The premises are inspected three times per day by senior management: first upon opening, second at midday, and third in the afternoon. During these daily inspections, the entire warehouse, including the parking lot and front entrance,, is inspected “specifically for spills and unsafe conditions.” (PR-SSF No. 7; Exh. G [Gomez Decl.] at ¶ 3.) The inspections are documented on a Senior Manager Daily Safety and Branch Readiness Checklist (“Checklist”). (PR-SSF No. 8; Exh. A at ¶ 7; Exh. G at ¶¶ 3 and 6; Exh. E at 19:1-16, 22:22-23:9, and 24:8-17; Exh. H [Checklist from March 9, 2020].) The “Lot” Checklist section includes the parking lot and front entrance area outside of the store. The “Front End” section includes the front entrance area inside the store. (Ibid.) The Checklists are prepared contemporaneous with the inspections and identify the areas of inspection, time of inspection, any potential hazards that may have been encountered, and how they were remedied, if applicable. (PR-SSF No. 9; Exh. A at ¶ 7; Exh. E at 19:1-16; Exh. G at ¶¶ 3 and 6; Exh. H.) Additionally, all RD America personnel are trained to continuously monitor the premises for any potential slipping hazards as they perform their duties. (PR-SSF No. 10; Exh. G at ¶ 4.) On March 9, 2020, the day of the underlying incident, two inspections were conducted on the premises prior to the alleged incident. (PR-SSF No. 11; Exh. A at ¶ 7; Exh. E at 19:1-21 and 21:11-17; Exh. G at ¶¶ 6-7; Exh. H.) The first inspection occurred at 6:04 a.m. and did not reveal any spilled substances or slipping hazards near the parking lot and front entrance area outside of the store. (PR-SSF No. 12; Exh. A at ¶ 7; Exh. E at 19:1-21, 22:3-7, and 24:24-25:8; Exh. G at ¶¶ 6 and 7; Exh. H.) The second inspection was performed between 1:00 and 1:20 p.m. (PR-SSF No. 13; Exh. A at ¶ 7; Exh. E at 21:11-17, 22:3-7, and 22:22-25:8; Exh. G at ¶¶ 6-9; Exh. H.) During the inspection, inspecting employee Senior Manager Javier Gomez (“Gomez”) did not observe any slipping hazards. (PR-SSF No. 14; Exh. G at ¶¶ 6-9; Exh. H.) Gomez had no knowledge of the alleged piece of produce on the floor until Plaintiff informed RD America personnel of the incident. (PR-SSF No. 15; Exh. A at ¶ 8 Exh. F [Gomez Depo.] at 11:15-25; Exh. G at ¶¶ 6-10; Exh. H.) Plaintiff did not report the incident to RD America staff until three days after the incident, when he reported the same to Assistant Branch Manager Debra Begando (“Begando”). (PR-SSF No. 6; Exh. A at ¶¶ 5 and 7; Exh. C at 55:9-13; Exh. E at 20:11-21:2.)

In a premises liability action, the owner of the premises may prove it exercised reasonable care and inspected the floor within a brief period before the accident with a declaration from a knowledgeable person describing the store’s regular maintenance practices and the record of actual inspections conducted around the time of the plaintiff’s injury. (Gonzalez v. Interstate Cleaning Corporation (2024) 106 Cal.App.5th 1026, 1026.) Here, RD America provides this exact proof with declarations from Assistant Branch Manager Debra Begando and inspecting employee Javier Gomez, in addition to providing the Senior Manager Daily Safety and Branch Readiness Checklist from the day of the incident. Therefore, RD America meets their initial burden.

 

Plaintiff’s Burden

The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show a triable issue of material fact as to RD America’s exercise of due care on the day of the underlying incident.

Plaintiff’s opposition argues two points: (1) the approximate 9 to 20 minutes between Gomez’s inspection and Plaintiff’s slip and fall is enough time to raise a triable issue of fact as to whether RD America had constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition; and (2) RD America engaged in spoilation of critical electronically stored information pursuant to CCP § 2023.030 by failing to preserve the store’s March 9, 2020, surveillance video and, as a result, Plaintiff is prevented from determining whether RD America had actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition. (Opposition p. 3.) The Court now addresses both arguments.

First, Plaintiff argues that the approximate 9 to 20 minutes between Gomez’s inspection and Plaintiff’s fall is enough time to raise a triable issue of fact as to whether RD America knew, or should have known, of the alleged dangerous condition. In support of their argument, Plaintiff relies on Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1210-1211, Tuttle v. Crawford (1936) 8 Cal.2d. 126, Louie v. Hagstrom’s Food Stores (1947) 81 Cal.App.2d 601, 608, and Hale v. Safeway Stores (1954) 129 Cal.App.2d 124.

Whether a dangerous condition has existed long enough for a reasonably prudent person to have discovered it is a question of fact for the jury in a premises liability action, and governing case law does not impose any time limitations. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1207.) “. . . [A] plaintiff in premises liability action may prove a dangerous condition existed for an unreasonable time with circumstantial evidence, and . . . evidence that an inspection has not been made within a particular period of time prior to an accident may warrant an inference that the defective condition existed long enough so that a person exercising reasonable care would have discovered it.” (Ibid. at 1210.) “. . . [A]n owner must have a reasonable time to make an inspection in order to discover the dangerous condition and correct it.” (Ibid. at 1210-1211.) “. . . [I]f a store owner has taken care in the discharge of its duty [to maintain the premises], by inspecting its premises in a reasonable manner, then no breach will be found even if a patron suffers injury.” (Ibid. at 1211.) Here, while Plaintiff argues that the 9 to 20 minutes existing between Gomez’s inspection and Plaintiff’s fall is enough time to raise a triable issue of fact as to whether RD America had actual or constructive knowledge of the alleged dangerous condition, this claim is defeated by the undisputed facts that RD America inspects its premises in a reasonable manner. Again, it is undisputed that RD America inspects the premises, and maintains a Checklist thereof, at least three times per day. (PR-SSF Nos. 7-8; Exh. A at ¶ 7; Exh. G at ¶¶ 3 and 6; Exh. E at 19:1-16, 22:22-23:9, and 24:8-17; Exh. H.) Additionally, it is undisputed that all RD America personnel are trained to continuously monitor the premises for any potential slipping hazards as they perform their duties. (PR-SSF No. 10; Exh. G at ¶ 4.) Therefore, Plaintiff fails to raise a triable issue of material fact as to whether RD America inspects its premises in a reasonable manner. Consequently, pursuant to Ortega, no breach may be found even though Plaintiff suffered injury.

Next, Plaintiff argues that RD America engaged in spoilation of critical electronically stored information pursuant to CCP § 2023.030 by failing to preserve the store’s March 9, 2020, surveillance video and, as a result, Plaintiff is prevented from determining whether RD America had actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition. (Opposition p. 3.) Therefore, Plaintiff requests that, pursuant to CCP § 2023.030(b) and Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, the Court imposes a sanction against RD America and that RD America is deemed, as a matter of law, to have had notice of the produce matter on the floor prior to Plaintiff’s slip and fall on March 9, 2020, and/or that a trier of fact may make a reasonable inference that the March 9, 2020, surveillance footage showed that RD America had notice of the produce matter on the floor prior to Plaintiff’s accident. (Opposition p. 4:1-12.)

CCP § 2023.030(b) states that “[t]he court may impose an issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process.” (CCP § 2023.030(b).) One serious form of discovery abuse potentially warranting discovery sanctions is the “spoilation of evidence,” which is defined as the destruction or alteration of relevant evidence or the failure to preserve evidence for another party’s use in pending or future litigation. (Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardino County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1139.) “’Chief among’ the non-tort remedies for the spoilation of evidence ‘is the evidentiary inference that evidence which one party has destroyed or rendered unavailable was unfavorable to that party.’ [Citation].” (Ibid.) “The duty to preserve material evidence arises not only during litigation but also extends to that period before the litigation when a party reasonably should know that the evidence may be relevant to anticipated litigation.” (Silvestri v. General Motors Corp. (4th Cir. 2001) 271 F.3d 583, 591.)

Here, both Begando and Gomez watched the March 9, 2020, surveillance footage, which purportedly showed Plaintiff’s slip and fall, yet failed to save and preserve the footage. (Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Facts (“P-SSF”) No. 1; Coolidge Decl. Exh. 1 [Begando Depo.] at 11:23-12:22, 15:4-12, 28:4-35:9, 35:11-38; Coolidge Decl. Exh. 2 [Gomez Depo.] at 10:11-23, 12:16-13:17, 19:3-20:5, 22:5-7, 22:12-25:20.) Indeed, Begando and Gomez watched the footage after Plaintiff reported his slip and fall on March 12, 2020. (Ibid.) Therefore, Begando and Gomez watched the footage with reasonable anticipation that it could be relevant to anticipated litigation. Despite this, the video was copied over and erased two weeks after Plaintiff’s incident, such that it was not available to be produced for Plaintiff during discovery. (Ibid.) As a result, RD America engaged in the spoilation of relevant evidence, and the Court imposes an issue sanction ordering that a trier of fact may make a reasonable inference that the March 9, 2020, surveillance footage showed that RD America had notice of the produce matter on the floor prior to Plaintiff’s accident.

To this extent, Plaintiff meets their burden and defeats summary judgment.

 

V.       CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff shows a triable issue of fact as to whether RD America had actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition. Accordingly, RD America’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.

I.       BACKGROUND  

This case is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Mehdi Rostamian (“Plaintiff”) alleges that, on or about September 7, 2021, the Pitbull dog of Defendants Mehrdad Dardashti (“Defendant”) and Mahyar Dardashti (“Mahyar”) suddenly and without notice lunged at and bit Plaintiff’s leg, causing Plaintiff to sustain serious injuries.

On May 25, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Mehrdad, Mahyar, and Does 1 to 50, alleging three causes of action: (1) strict liability pursuant to Cal. Civ. Code § 3342; (2) strict liability for injury caused by domestic animal with dangerous propensities; and (3) negligence.

On October 29, 2024, Defendant Mehrdad Dardashti filed the instant motion for leave to reopen discovery.

On December 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

On January 7, 2025, Defendant filed their reply.

A jury trial is scheduled for February 18, 2025.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARDS

“On motion of any party, the court may grant leave to complete discovery proceedings, or to have a motion concerning discovery heard, closer to the initial trial date, or to reopen discovery after a new trial date has been set.  This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2024.050(a).) 

“In exercising its discretion to grant or deny this motion, the court shall take into consideration any matter relevant to the leave requested, including, but not limited to, the following: (1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery. (2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier. (3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party. (4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action.” (CCP § 2024.050(b)(1)-(4).)   

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to extend or to reopen discovery, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (CCP § 2024.050(c).)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

 Meet and Confer Requirement

            On October 16, 2024, defense counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel asking if Plaintiff would stipulate to the limited depositions of Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s experts. (Gerber Decl. ¶ 32; Exh. 23 [meet and confer].) Plaintiff’s counsel would not agree to the stipulation. (Ibid. at ¶ 33; Exh. 23.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant satisfies the meet and confer requirement of CCP § 2024.050(a).

 

Good Cause

            Defendant moves for leave to reopen discovery to take one-hour depositions from Plaintiff and his experts on the issue of damages. (Motion p. 1:3-14.) Defendant claims that he was previously prevented from obtaining this discovery due to prior defense counsel’s health issues. (Motion p. 1:7-9; Gerber Decl. ¶ 37.) Defendant argues the necessity of these depositions because Plaintiff alleges continued pain and suffering. (Gerber Decl. ¶ 36.) Defendant does not request a continuance for trial. (Gerber Decl. ¶ 40.)

            Trial in this matter has been continued many times. On November 13, 2023, Defendant filed an ex parte application to continue the trial due to defense counsel’s health issues. (Mullen Decl. ¶ 6.) The Court granted a brief continuance to December 20, 2023, but noted that discovery remained closed. (Ibid.) On December 5, 2023, Defendant moved ex parte to reopen discovery. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) The Court denied the motion but granted another continuance to January 23, 2024, to accommodate defense counsel’s health issues. (Ibid.) On January 8, 2024, Defendant again moved ex parte to continue the trial date. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.) The Court denied Defendant’s motion. (Ibid.) On January 17, 2024, Defendant noticed reassignment of lead counsel. (Ibid. at ¶ 9.) On the Court’s own motion, the trial was continued to February 20, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 10.) Due to witness scheduling conflicts, the parties stipulated to continue trial to March 25, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 11.) The Court granted continuance, again noting that discovery remained closed. (Ibid.) On its own motion, the Court continued trial to July 10, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 12.) On July 10, 2024, the trial was continued to November 18, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 15.) At the November 8, 2024, final status conference, defense counsel represented that his client was in Israel and would not be able to attend the November 18, 2024, trial. (Ibid. at ¶ 18.) Trial was continued to the current trial date of February 18, 2025. (See November 8, 2024, Minute Order.) The Court stated that discovery was to remain closed. (Ibid.)

            Here, despite the instant action’s many trial continuances, Defendant lacked diligence in obtaining discovery or obtaining leave to reopen discovery. On October 10, 2023, Defendant noticed Plaintiff’s deposition but never took it. (Gerber Decl. ¶¶ 8 and 10.) Defendant fails to explain why Plaintiff’s deposition never occurred. Discovery has been closed since October 20, 2023. Prior to this application, which was made less than three weeks before the November 18, 2024, trial date, Defendant never sought to depose Plaintiff’s experts. (Mullen Decl. ¶ 4.) In fact, on July 8, 2024, defense counsel indicated that no further discovery was necessary since “all the discovery in the world” would not change Defendant’s insurance policy limits. (Savo Decl. ¶ 14; Exh. A [July 8, 2024, correspondence].) While prior defense counsel struggled with health issues, the Court continued trial in December 2023 so Defendant could obtain new counsel. (See December 6, 2023, Minute Order.) Defendant retained new lead counsel in January 2024. (Mullen Decl. ¶ 9.) Defendant provides no explanation for their failure to request the reopening of discovery in the interim nine months. Furthermore, despite having knowledge of the November 18, 2024, trial date since July 10, 2024, Defendant flew to Israel on November 3, 2024. (Dardashti December 2, 2024, Decl. ¶ 3.) While the Court is sympathetic to Defendant’s family and friends who have been impacted by the region’s hostilities, Defendant, in his untimely declaration regarding the same, fails to explain why he had to travel just a few weeks before another continued trial date. He further fails to explain why he did not inform the Court of his travels until the final status conference on November 8, 2024.

            This case has been pending for over three years. In considering a request to reopen discovery, the Court considers the “diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery . . . and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier.” (CCP § 2024.050(b)(2).) Here, Defendant shows an unexplained lack of diligence in timely seeking the reopening of discovery. Conversely, Plaintiff, who has been prepared for trial since November 2023, would be greatly prejudiced if discovery were reopened weeks before another continued trial date.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s motion for leave to reopen discovery is DENIED.

It is so ordered. Moving party to give notice.
