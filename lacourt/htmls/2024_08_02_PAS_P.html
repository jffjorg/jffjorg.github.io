Case Number: 20GDCV00304    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING THE DEMURRER OF DEFENDANT 360 REMODELING, INC. TO THE FIRST, SECOND, AND THIRD CAUSES OF ACTION IN THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT WITH LEAVE TO AMEND; DENYING THE MOTION TO STRIKE AS MOOT

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This action arises from Plaintiff Anahid Cholakian (“Plaintiff”) sustaining water damage to her home located at 250 West Fairview Avenue, Apartment 302, Glendale, California 91202 (the “Subject Property”). On March 6, 2020, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Legend Construction Services, Inc. (“Legend); Fairview Gardens Community Association (“Fairview”), Farmers Insurance Company, Inc. (“Farmers”), and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) negligence, (4) negligent hiring/retention, and (5) negligent entrustment. None of the causes of actions asserted in the complaint are alleged against the “Doe” defendants.

          On April 20, 2020, Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint which identified Doe 1 as Fire Insurance Exchange (“FIE”).

          On May 13, 2020, pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Defendant Farmers was dismissed from this action without prejudice.

          On November 17, 2021, pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Defendant Farmers was dismissed from this action with prejudice.

          Also, on November 17, 2021, pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Defendant FIE was dismissed from this action with prejudice.

          On August 1, 2022, pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Does 1 to 50 were dismissed from this action without prejudice.

          On November 3, 2022, pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff, Does 1 to 50 were dismissed from this action without prejudice.

          On December 1, 2022, pursuant to a stipulation between the parties, the Court entered an order setting aside the default entered against Defendant Fairview.

          On December 20, 2022, Defendant Fairview filed an answer to the complaint.

          On December 29, 2022, Defendant Fairview filed a cross-complaint against Defendant Legend and ROES 1 to 20 for: (1) indemnity, and (2) contribution.

          On June 6, 2023, Defendant Fairview filed an amendment to cross-complaint which identified 360 Remodeling, Inc. as Roe 1.

          On September 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint which identified 360 Remodeling, Inc. as Doe 2.

          On September 25, 2023, 360 Remodeling, Inc. filed an answer to the cross-complaint filed by Defendant Fairview. Also, on such date, 360 Remodeling, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against Defendant Legend and ROES 75 to 95 for: (1) indemnity, and (2) contribution.

          On October 19, 2023, Defendant 360 Remodeling, Inc. (“360”) filed a demurrer to the third cause of action for negligence in Plaintiff’s complaint, which Plaintiff opposed, and Defendant 360 filed a reply brief.

          On March 11, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court sustained the demurrer of Defendant 360 to the complaint with 20 days leave to amend. (03/11/24 Minute Order.) Plaintiff also made an oral motion to set aside the dismissal of Does 1 through 50 and the Court ordered the dismissals of Does 1 through 50 on August 1, 2022 and November 1, 2022 set aside and vacated. (03/11/24 Minute Order.)

          On March 26, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Margaret L. Oldendorf to the Honorable Jared D. Moses sitting in Department P at Pasadena Courthouse effective April 2, 2024.

          On March 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative first amended complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Legend, Fairview, Farmers, 360, and Does 1 through 50, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) negligence, (2) negligent hiring, and (3) negligent entrustment. While Defendant 360 is not named in the caption of the FAC, Plaintiff indicates that all causes of action in the FAC are asserted against Defendant 360. (FAC at p. 2.)

          On April 22, 2024, Defendant 360 filed the instant demurrer to the first, second, and third causes of action in the FAC, as well as a motion to strike.

          On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed respective oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike, to which Defendant 360 filed respective reply briefs on July 25, 2024.

          The Court notes that Plaintiff’s opposition briefs were filed and served late; however, Plaintiff’s counsel attests to the county wide outage as being the cause of the late filing. (Keusseyan Decl., ¶¶ 2, 4-5.) Thus, the Court will consider Plaintiff’s opposition papers.

 

II.      DEMURRER

A. Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. Section 430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” (Aubry v. Tri-Defendants Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.) 

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.) 

          Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) 

          B. The Meet and Confer Requirement

          Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) A motion to strike also requires a similar meet and confer process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.)

The meet and confer requirement has been met as counsel for Defendant 360, Artin Gholian (“Gholian”), provides a declaration attesting to a telephonic meet and confer discussions concerning the demurrer and motion to strike. (Gholian Decl., ¶ 6.)  

C. Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Defendant 360’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code §§ 452, 453.)  

          D. Demurrer

          Defendant 360 demurs to each cause of action in the FAC on the grounds that each cause of action is time-barred by the applicable statute of limitations. Further, Defendant 360 contends that Plaintiff cannot state a claim for negligence against Defendant 360 as a matter of law.

 

          Pertinent Allegations of the FAC

          The Court finds it necessary to set forth the pertinent allegations of the FAC given that the demurrer is based on the argument concerning the expiration of the statute of limitations. The FAC alleges the following: Defendant Fairview is the homeowner’s association for the Subject Property. (FAC, ¶ 4.) Defendant Fairview hired Defendant 360 to perform roof repairs and complete work in common areas at the Subject Property. (FAC, ¶ 13; Ex. A.) Defendant 360 was the general contractor assigned to the task and was responsible to oversee and supervise the construction activities being performed. (FAC, ¶ 13.) Unbeknownst to Plaintiff at the time, Defendant 360 hired and supervised a subcontractor, Defendant Legend, to complete the work instead. (FAC, ¶ 14.) Defendant 360 remodeling remained the general contractor on the project and was responsible for overseeing the construction activities and work being performed. (FAC, ¶ 14.) Plaintiff believes that Defendant 360 was in constant communication with Defendant Legend throughout the work being performed. (FAC, ¶ 14.)

          Thereafter, on or about June 21, 2019, Plaintiff arrived at her home and discovered water damage throughout the Subject Property. (FAC, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that on June 21, 2019, it rained and Defendants Legend and 360 failed to properly tarp the roof over the Subject Property, which caused significant rainwater to intrude into the Subject Property. (FAC, ¶ 16.) Following the June 21, 2019 date of loss, Defendant Legend made necessary repairs to three other units that had water from rain while Defendant 360 supervised the work to make sure it was completed correctly. (FAC, ¶ 17.) After the follow-up repair work following the June 21, 2019 date of loss, Defendant 360 inspected the roof and did a walk through with Defendant Fairview. (FAC, ¶ 18.)

 

          The Statute of Limitations and Plaintiff’s Delayed Discovery

          Relevant Law Concerning the Statute of Limitations

An action for injury to real property is subject to a three-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338, subd. (b).) “In tort cases, the statute of limitations generally begins to run upon the occurrence of the last event essential to the cause of action, even if the plaintiff is unaware that a cause of action exists.” (Wilshire Westwood Associates v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 732, 739.) “Where the harm at issue is a tortious injury to property, the injury is considered to be to the property itself, rather than to the property owner.” (Ibid.) “[W]here a complaint sets forth, or attempts to set forth, a cause of action against a fictitiously named defendant and his true name is thereafter discovered and is substituted by amendment, for statute of limitations purposes, he is considered a party to the action as of the date of the original pleading.” (Cooper v. Jevne (1976) 56 Cal.App.3d 860, 873.)

          A cause of action accrues at the time when the cause of action is complete with all of its elements.” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 806.) “An important exception to the general rule of accrual is the discovery rule, which postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.” (Id. at p. 807.) “A plaintiff has reason to discover a cause of action when he or she has reason at least to suspect a factual basis for its elements.” (Ibid.) “[S]uspicion of one or more of the elements of a cause of action, coupled with knowledge of any remaining elements, will generally trigger the statute of limitations period.” (Ibid.) “The discovery rule only delays accrual until the plaintiff has, or should have, inquiry notice of the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “In order to rely on the discovery rule for delayed accrual of a cause of action, [a] plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his claim would be barred without the benefit of the discovery rule must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence.” (Id. at p. 808.) “In assessing the sufficiency of the allegations of delayed discovery, the court places the burden on the plaintiff to show diligence; conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer.” (Ibid.)¿¿ 

          Analysis as to the Statute of Limitations Issue

          Here, the operative FAC indicates that the date of loss pertaining to the Subject Property was June 21, 2019. (FAC, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff filed her initial complaint on March 6, 2020, in which Defendant 360 was not named as a defendant and no cause of action was asserted against any doe defendant therein. In her opposition brief, Plaintiff does not dispute that the FAC does not relate back to the filing of the original complaint as it concerns Defendant 360.

          Defendant 360 was not brought into this action until an amendment to the cross-complaint was filed by Defendant Fairview on June 6, 2023. However, Plaintiff did not bring Defendant 360 into this action until September 7, 2023, when Plaintiff amended her original complaint which named Defendant 360 as “Doe 1.”  

          The Court finds that on its face, the FAC against Defendant 360 is barred by the applicable statute of limitations. The Court finds that Plaintiff’s reliance on Fox v. Ethicon-Endo Surgery, Inc., supra, 35 Cal.4th 797 is inapposite as in that the case the plaintiff set forth allegations in the operative first amended complaint as to delayed discovery and reasonable diligence. (Fox v. Ethicon-Endo Surgery, Inc., supra, 35 Cal.4th 797, 805.)

          Here, the FAC only makes a conclusory allegation as to the discovery of Defendant 360’s role in the construction work at issue. (FAC, ¶ 14.) While Plaintiff may have in fact discovered Defendant 360’s involvement well after the filing of the complaint, Plaintiff did not set forth any allegations of reasonable diligence in the FAC. (FAC, ¶¶ 20-92.) The FAC does not allege the time and manner of discovery or the inability to make earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence as to the identity of Defendant 360. Thus, Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to invoke the delayed discovery rule.

          Given that Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts in the FAC to invoke the delayed discovery rule, the Court finds that the FAC is barred on its face as to Defendant 360.

          Due to the Court’s analysis as to the statute of limitations issue above, such analysis is dispositive of the demurrer before the Court.

          The Court does not find that there is no reasonable possibility of successful amendment. The Court therefore SUSTAINS the demurrer of Defendant 360 to the first, second, and third causes of action in the FAC with leave to amend.

 

III.     MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendant 360 moves to strike the allegations of the FAC on the grounds that the allegations asserted against it are barred by the statute of limitations. Given that the Court has sustained the demurrer to the FAC with leave to amend, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as MOOT.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Defendant 360’s demurrer is sustained with 20 days leave to amend as to the first, second, and third causes of action in the FAC.

Defendant 360’s motion to strike is denied as moot.

II.       LEGAL STANDARD      

“When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (a).) “Coordination of civil actions sharing a common question of fact or law is appropriate if one judge hearing all of the actions . . . will promote the ends of justice taking into account whether the common question of fact or law is predominating and significant to the litigation; the convenience of the parties, witnesses, and counsel; the relative development of the actions and the work product of counsel; the efficient utilization; the calendar of the courts; the disadvantages of duplicative and inconsistent rulings, orders, or judgments; and, the likelihood of settlement of the actions without further litigation should coordination be denied.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 404.1.)

“The purpose of consolidation is merely to promote trial convenience and economy by avoiding duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both actions.” (Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485 [citation omitted].) “A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.” (Ibid.) Consolidation does not require identical causes of action in each case, absolute identity of the parties, or identical allegations. (Ibid.) Where common issues are present in two cases then consolidation is appropriate. (Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978-79.) 

A motion to consolidate “[i]s deemed a single motion for the purpose of determining the appropriate filing fee, but memorandums, declarations, and other supporting papers must be filed only in the lowest numbered case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(2)(A).) Moreover, a motion to consolidate “[m]ust be served on all attorneys of record and all nonrepresented partes in all of the cases sought to be consolidated.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.350(a)(2)(B).)

Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g)(1) provides that “[c]ases may not be consolidated unless they are in the same department. A motion to consolidate two or more cases may be noticed and heard after the cases, initially filed in different departments, have been related in a single department, or if the cases were already assigned to that department.”

TCSCA provides a declaration. Attorney Marutzky does not explicitly attest to the Jokake Action—and its related consolidated actions—sharing a common question of law or fact. (Marutzky Decl., ¶¶ 1-10.) Attorney Marutzky, however, does state that TCSCA issued a payment bond and performance bond naming Jokake as principal and which guaranteed Jokake’s payment to subcontractors and supplies and performance on the construction project. (Marutzky Decl., ¶ 4.) Moreover, the complaint in the Travelers Action arises from the construction of Las Encinas Hospital. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Thus, the Court finds that there are common questions of law and fact between the Jokake Action and the Travelers Action.

           The Court, however, cannot grant the instant motion due to procedural irregularities. Here, the Travelers Action is currently pending in Department A at the Burbank Courthouse. Los Angeles County Court Rules, Rule 3.3(g)(1) requires the cases to be related prior to the entry of an order for consolidation. Upon review of the docket in the Jokake Action, there has been no order relating the Jokake Action and the Travelers Action. The Court has also reviewed the docket in the Travelers Action and no order has been entered in such action relating the Jokake Action to the Travelers Action. While the motion to consolidate is unopposed, TSCSA must comply with the procedural requirements pertaining to a motion to consolidate.

 

IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, the motion to consolidate filed by Travelers Casualty and Surety Company of America is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

Case Number: 22AHCV00927    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION FOR LEAVE TO INTERVENE


I.        INTRODUCTION

          This case arises out of a vehicle collision that resulted in the death of Santos Gabino Larrynega (“Decedent”). On October 27, 2022, Plaintiffs Francisca Gutierrez, individually and as successor to Decedent, along with Raul Lareinaga Gutierrez, and Saul Lareinaga Gutierrez filed a complaint against Defendants Manuel Reyes, DLR Express, Inc., and Adrian De La Rosa, alleging causes of action for survival, and wrongful death. The complaint alleges that while Decedent’s vehicle was proceeding straight within his lane, Defendant Reyes’ vehicle negligently and suddenly merged in front of Decedent’s vehicle, suddenly braked and stopped without signaling, and Decedent was unable to stop, and collided with Defendants’ vehicle. The complaint further alleges that Defendant DLR Express was Reyes’ employer, and De La Rosa owned the vehicle Reyes was operating.

 

          Before the Court is a motion by Insurance Company of the West for leave to intervene. The motion is unopposed.  The motion is granted.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 387, subdivision (d)(1) provides that the court shall, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if “a provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene,” or “[t]he person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person’s ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.” Additionally, upon timely application, a court may “permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(2).) A court will determine the timeliness of a motion to intervene based on the date when a nonparty “knew or should have known their interests in the litigation were not being adequately represented.” (Ziani Homeowners Assn. v. Brookfield Ziani LLC (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 274, 282.) 

 

Under Labor Code section¿3852, “[a]ny¿employer who pays, or becomes obligated to pay compensation . . . may likewise make a claim or bring an action against the third person.¿ In the latter event the employer may recover in the same suit, in addition to the total amount of compensation, damage for which he or she was liable including all salary, wage, pension, or other emolument paid to the employee or to his or her dependents.”¿ “If the employer is insured against workmen’s compensation liability, the insurer has the same right of action as the employer, or is subrogated to the employer’s right. In enforcing its cause of action the insurer may . . . join as party plaintiff in the suit previously filed by the employee.”¿¿(Burum¿v. State Compensation Ins. Fund¿(1947) 30 Cal.2d 575, 580-581.)¿¿

 

Further, Labor Code section¿3853¿provides that “[i]f the action is brought by either the employer or employee, the other may, at any time before trial on the facts, join as party plaintiff or shall consolidate his action, if brought independently.”¿¿ 

 

In terms of the form of the petition, Code of Civil Procedure section 387, subdivision (c) provides that a nonparty “shall petition the court for leave to intervene by noticed motion or by ex parte application. The petition shall include a copy of the proposed complaint in intervention or answer in intervention and set forth the grounds upon which intervention rests.” 

 

          III.     DISCUSSION

 

          Proposed Intervenor Insurance Company of the West has satisfied the filing requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 387, subdivision (c) by filing a noticed motion and setting forth the grounds for intervention as well as a copy of its proposed complaint in intervention. (Javan Decl. ¶ 2; Exh. A.)  

 

Proposed Intervenor seeks to intervene in the action under Labor Code section 3853, arguing that Reyes’ case arises out of an industrial injury while Reyes was in the course and scope of his employment with Proposed Intervenor’s insured, DLR Express, and Proposed Intervenor has paid benefits to or on behalf of its insured’s employee and is now subrogated to the rights and claims of its insured to seek reimbursement.

 

Proposed Intervenor is entitled to intervene under Code of Civil Procedure section 387. First, the motion is timely because Labor Code section 3853 allows a nonparty to join as a party plaintiff “at any time before trial on the facts.” Second, Labor Code section 3853 grants Proposed Intervenor the right to intervene under Code of Civil Procedure section 387 because Proposed Intervenor has a right of subrogation as the insurer of Reyes’ employer and has incurred an obligation to continue paying workers’ compensation benefits to Reyes pursuant to his employer’s insurance policy. (Lab. Code § 3852; see also Burum, 30 Cal.2d at 580-581.) Proposed Intervenor may file a complaint-in-intervention to recover for the “total amount of compensation” paid to Reyes and related damages. (Lab. Code. § 3852.) Accordingly, Proposed Intervenor has sufficiently claimed an interest relating to the disposition of the action for purposes of intervention.  

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

          As a result, Insurance Company of the West’s motion for leave to intervene is GRANTED.  

 

Insurance Company of the West is ordered to file the proposed complaint-in-intervention attached as Exhibit A as required under Code of Civil Procedure section 387, subdivision (e) with the Court within 10 days of this order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT FORD MOTOR COMPANY’S MOTION FOR ENTRY OF PROTECTIVE ORDER TO GOVERN PRODUCTION OF CONFIDENTIAL MATERIALS


I.        INTRODUCTION

           This is an action arising from the purchase of an allegedly defective 2019 Ford Transit Connect (the “Subject Vehicle”). On May 9, 2023, Plaintiff Jesus Correa Morales aka Jesus Morales (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Ford Motor Company (“Defendant” or “Ford”), LAD-F, Inc. dba Ford of Downtown L.A. (“DTLA Ford”), and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) violation of the Song-Beverly Act—breach of express warranty, (2) violation of the Song-Beverly Act—breach of implied warranty, (3) violation of the Song-Beverly Act section 1793.2, and (4) negligent repair.

           On June 14, 2023, Defendant filed its answer to the complaint.

           On March 13, 2024, Defendant filed and served the instant Motion for Entry of Protective Order to Govern Production of Confidential Materials. Defendant indicates that the parties do not disagree on the need for a protective order, but they disagree on the form of the protective order. (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 4:3-6.) Defendant requests the entry of a protective order that uses the Los Angeles Superior Court Model Protective Order as an initial working draft but incorporates additional details that are essential to protect Defendant’s interests. (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 4:9-12.)

           On July 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion, to which Defendant replied on July 26, 2024.

 

II.       LEGAL STANDARD      

“[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.)  

“The Court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The Court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).)  

“When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed . . . may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (a).) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b).) Such protective order may include a direction “[t]hat a trade secret or other confidential research, development, or commercial information not be disclosed, or be only disclosed only to specified persons or only in a specified way.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b)(5).)

The party seeking a protective order has the burden “to show good cause for

whatever order is sought.” (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

 

A.   Meet and Confer Requirement

The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied. Counsel for Defendant, Zakaria H. Amin (“Amin”), declares that the parties met and conferred about the protective order. (Amin Decl., ¶¶ 3-6.)

 

B.    Evidence in Support of the Motion

           Mr. Amin declares the following: Defendant Ford responded to Plaintiff’s first set of discovery, which included 27 form interrogatories, 46 requests for production, and 35 requests for admission. (Amin Decl., ¶ 2.) In response to those requests, Defendant produced 223 pages of documents related to the Subject Vehicle. (Amin Decl., ¶ 2.) In its responses, Defendant agreed to produce certain confidential, proprietary, or trade secret information and materials. (Amin Decl., ¶ 2.) These documents include: (1) Ford’s Warranty Policy & Procedure Manual, which is a document that details the policies and procedures that Ford developed over time for use by its dealers in the United States regarding warranty claim diagnosis, submission, allowance, and reimbursement; (2) Ford’s Customer Relationship Center Policies and Procedures, which are developed and used by and for Ford’s Customer Care Team and Customer Relationship Center, which handles, assesses, documents, categories, and resolves customer concerns that are reported to Ford by consumers; and (3) Ford’s Reacquired Vehicle (“RAV”) Policy and Procedures Manuals, which are documents that set forth guidelines for process vehicle repurchase offers made to a consumer by Ford, and that were developed over time for consideration once a decision has been made to repurchase a specific vehicle. (Amin Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. A.)

           The parties have been discussing a protective order in this case for months and meet and confer attempts regarding the proposed protective order were initiated on January 10, 2022 by Defendant’s counsel. (Amin Decl., ¶ 3.) The parties were ordered to meet and confer substantially about Defendant’s proposed changes to the LASC Model Protective Order and, on October 3, 2022, the parties conducted a video meet and confer. (Amin Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel made clear that they were not going to agree to any modifications of the LASC Model Order and would not be substantively participating in the meet and confer process. (Amin Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel, Phil Thomas, stated that Plaintiff’s plan is to use Defendant’s confidential documents “to prepare for future cases.” (Amin Decl., ¶ 4.) Such reason is why Defendant is seeking protection of its confidential documents. (Amin Decl., ¶ 4.)

           On November 9, 2022, Defendant’s counsel emailed Plaintiff’s counsel a copy of its proposed Modified Protective Order in a final effort to meet and confer. (Amin Decl., ¶ 5; Ex. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel, however, stated that it was not agreeable to the proposed modifications to the LASC Model Order. (Amin Decl., ¶ 5; Ex. B.) Mr. Amin states that Defendant’s proposal does not inhibit Plaintiff’s ability to litigate this matter in any way because it continues to allow for disclosure to counsel for use in this case and is necessary given Defendant’s interest in protecting its confidential business and trade secret information. (Amin Decl., ¶ 9.) A copy of the modified protective order is attached to the declaration of Mr. Amin. (Amin Decl., ¶¶ 7-8; Exs. E and F.)

           Jacob Doss (“Doss”) declares that Ford’s Warranty Policy & Procedure Manuals, Customer Relationship Center Polices and Procedures, and the RAV Policy and Procedures Manual all contain Defendant’s sensitive, confidential, and proprietary information. (Doss Decl., ¶¶ 6-8.)

 

C.   Evidence Opposition to the Motion

In opposition to the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel, Armando Lopez (“Lopez”), states

that over the past year, he has met and conferred with Defendant’s various counsel regarding the terms of a protective order. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 2.) Despite these efforts, the parties have been unable to reach an agreement on a protective order. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 2.) Mr. Lopez states that he is agreeable to execute the LASC Model Protective Order. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 2.) Mr. Lopez declares that over the past few months, his firm and law firms for various automobile manufacturers have agreed to execute the LASC Model Protective Order without modifications. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

D.   Defendant’s Proposed Changes to the LASC Model Protective Order are Unwarranted  

Initially, the Court will address Plaintiff’s contention that the motion should be

denied because Defendant failed to timely move for a protective order. The Court rejects such contention as there is no mandate under CCP § 2031.060 that Defendant must move promptly for a protective order. Additionally, according to the declaration of Mr. Amin, the last meet and confer discussion concerning the protective order occurred in January of 2024, and the instant motion was filed in March of 2024.

           The proposed modifications of Defendant to the LASC Model Protective Order are as follows:

Paragraph 7: “Ford’s revisions clarify subsection (b), confirming that the term ‘affiliated attorneys’ mean attorneys in the same firm. Ford’s revision also provides that Plaintiff’s counsel’s office personnel who have access to Ford’s confidential documents must sign Exhibit A. Ford proposes a revision to sub-section (d) to include videographers and litigation support companies (along with court reporters) who may have access to Ford’s confidential documents by virtue of their retention in a case. Ford removed subsection (f), which permitted mock jurors to access Ford’s confidential documents because Ford has no ability to identify such persons or ensure (or confirm) their compliance. Ford also revised sub-section (g) to include non-attorneys with experts, and to provide that Ford’s confidential documents may not be shown to competitors of Ford.” (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 7:1-11.)

Paragraph 8: “Ford’s revisions provide that the receiving party may not post Ford’s confidential documents to any website or advertise Ford’s documents for sale. These provisions are reasonable and necessary to protect Ford’s documents from improper dissemination.” (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 7:12-15.)

Paragraph 21: “Ford’s revisions clarify the process for Plaintiff’s counsel to return or destroy Ford’s confidential documents at the conclusion of the case and require that all of Ford’s confidential documents be returned or destroyed when the case is over.” (Memo. of Ps and As at p. 7:16-19.)

           Upon review of the declarations of Mr. Amin and Mr. Doss in support of Defendant’s motion for a protective order, the Court finds that Defendant has not established good cause for the proposed modifications to the LASC Model Protective Order. The instant motion is premised on a statement made by Plaintiff’s counsel that Plaintiff planned to use Defendant’s documents in future cases. (Amin Decl., ¶ 4.) However, neither the declaration of Mr. Amin nor Mr. Doss sets forth why the proposed modifications to the LASC Model Protective Order will protect Defendant from “unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b).)

           Additionally, the Court finds that the LASC Model Protective Order sufficiently protects Defendant from any concerns that Plaintiff’s counsel may use such materials to prepare for future cases. The LASC Model Protective Order specifically provides that “[c]onfidential [m]aterials shall only be used by the persons receiving them only for the purposes of preparing for, conducting, participating in the conduct of, and/or prosecuting and/or defending the [p]roceeding, and not for any business or other purpose whatsoever.” (Amin Decl., Ex. E at ¶ 8, emphasis added.) Thus, if the parties enter into the LASC Model Protective Order, Plaintiff’s counsel will be forbidden from using any confidential materials to prepare for future cases and must return all confidential materials and copies thereof, upon request, to Defendant within 30 days after settlement or termination of the proceedings. (Amin Decl., Ex. E at ¶ 21.)

 

IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, Defendant Ford Motor Company’s Motion for Entry of Protective Order to Govern Production of Confidential Materials is DENIED.

The parties, however, may execute the LASC Model Protective Order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING WITHOUT PREJUDICE PLAINTIFF HELEN WOO’S MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION AS ADMITTED AND FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT EON WALK


I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is an action arising from alleged fraudulent actions pertaining to a lease agreement. On August 25, 2023, Plaintiff Helen Woo (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Eon Walk (“Walk”), Sheila Elayne Linly Clark (“Clark”), and DOES 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for: (1) financial and elder abuse, (2) fraud, and (3) breach of contract (rent).

          On November 1, 2023, Defendant Walk filed an answer to the complaint.

          On March 27, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Margaret L. Oldendorf to the Honorable Jared D. Moses sitting in Department P at Pasadena Courthouse effective April 2, 2024.

          On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served the instant Motion for Order that Requests for Admission be Deemed Admitted and for Monetary Sanctions Against Defendant Walk in the Amount of $3,810.00. Pursuant to the motion, Plaintiff moves for an order that the truth of the matters specified in her requests for admission served on Defenant Walk on December 18, 2023 be deemed admitted, and that monetary sanctions of $3,810.00 be imposed against Defendant Walk.

          According to the proof of service attached to the motion, Defendant Walk was electronically served with the motion on June 17, 2024 at the electronic service address of “sheilawalk@att.net” which, according to a November 14, 2023 proof of service filed by Defendant Walk, is the e-mail address for Defendant Walk. (See 11/14/23 Proof of Electronic Service.)

          On July 23, 2024, on the Court’s own motion due to the county wide network outage, the Court continued the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion to deem the RFAs as admitted to August 2, 2024. (07/23/24 Minute Order.)

          As of July 31, 2024, the motion is unopposed. Any opposition to the motion was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD     

After service of requests for admission, a party has 30 days to respond “in writing under oath and separately to each RFA.” (St Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 774.) When a party fails to serve a timely response to requests for admission “[t]he party to whom the request was directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc, § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) “It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction . . . on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated [the] motion.” (Ibid.) A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

 

A.   Evidence in Support of the Motion

Counsel for Plaintiff, James A. Gallo (“Gallo”), declares that Plaintiff’s first set of requests for admissions were served on Defendant Walk via mail on December 18, 2023. (Gallo Decl., ¶ 2; Exs. 1-2.) Mr. Gallo states that, prior to March 21, 2024, he discussed with Defendant Walk that she agreed to accept communications from his office by e-mail. (Gallo Decl., ¶ 6; Ex. 7.) Defendant Walk has not provided any responses to Plaintiff’s requests for admissions. (Gallo Decl., ¶ 6.)  

 

B.    The Improper Service of the Motion and Other Defects

          While the Court acknowledges that Defendant Walk has failed to respond to discovery, the Court finds that the electronic service of the instant motion was improper.

          “Electronic service means service of a document, on a person, by either electronic transmission or electronic notification. Electronic service may be performed directly by a person, including a party, by a person’s agent, including the person’s attorney, or through an electronic filing service provider, and by a court.” (CCP § 1010.6(a)(1)(A).) “If a document is required to be served by certified or registered mail, electronic service of the document is not authorized.” (CCP § 1010.6(a)(2).) “An unrepresented party may consent to receive electronic service.” (CCP § 1010.6(c)(2).) Express consent to electronic service is given by either: (1) serving a notice on all parties and filing the notice with the court; or (2) manifesting affirmative consent through electronic means with the court’s electronic filing service provider, and concurrently providing the party’s electronic address with that consent for the purpose of receiving electronic service. (CCP § 1010.6(c)(3)(i)-(ii).) “The act of electronic filing shall not be construed as express consent.” (CCP § 1010.6(c)(3)(ii).)¿¿ 

          The Court notes that Defendant Walk has always represented herself in pro per in this action. Thus, Defendant Walk is currently self-represented. Here, the relevant proof of service shows that Defendant Walk was served with the motion by electronic service. After reviewing the court file, the Court finds that Defendant Walk has not expressly consented to receive electronic service under CCP § 1010.6. Thus, electronic service of the motion was improper under CCP § 1010.6. As such, the Court cannot grant the motion.¿¿ 

          Additionally, the Court notes that the notice of motion does not sufficiently set forth the location for the hearing on the motion as Plaintiff only articulated the department in which the hearing will occur but did not set forth the courthouse address. Plaintiff was required to set forth the location of the hearing pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1110(b)(1) which states that the notice of motion must set forth “[t]he . . . location, if ascertainable, of any scheduled hearing . . . .” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(b).)  Defendant Walk is entitled to proper notice and the failure of Plaintiff to set forth the courthouse address on the notice of motion does not comport with due process. 

 

IV.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff Helen Woo’s motion to deem RFAs as admitted and for monetary sanctions is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

          Plaintiff Helen Woo to give notice of this order.

Case Number: 24AHCV00017    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT AS MOOT


I.        INTRODUCTION

           On January 3, 2024, Plaintiffs Geoffrey Hanes Wall and Kang Seung Lee filed a complaint against Defendants Maria Manuela Florez and Gustavo Adolfo Florez.

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint, alleging causes of action for Nuisance, Trespass, Violation of Ralph Civil Rights Act, Violation of Tom Bane Civil Rights Act, Assault, Battery, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Negligence. The FAC alleges that Defendant Gustavo, who is Defendant Maria’s son, has a well- documented history of violent and threatening behavior towards his family and neighbors, which long predates Plaintiffs’ move into the neighborhood. He has a reputation for using his numerous large dogs to follow, chase, threaten, and intimidate his neighbors, who have filed multiple police reports and complained to city representatives.

Further, the FAC alleges that Plaintiffs moved into their property and almost immediately observed multiple dogs residing in Defendants’ backyard. Plaintiffs claim that the dogs have excessively and constantly barked, howled, and yelped on a daily basis, non-stop for several hours at a time at all hours of the day.

The FAC further alleges that animal control has visited the Defendants’ property on several occasions and removed dogs from Defendants’ possession, because they were unlicensed or exceeded the pet limitation of four dogs that residents are permitted to own. (L.A. County Code § 10.20.038, subd. (A)). Despite this and Plaintiffs’ notice to Defendants, the noise coming from Defendants’ dogs has not been abated and is ongoing. The FAC also alleges that Defendants threw rocks and other objects at Plaintiff’s property. Moreover, Plaintiffs’ complaint states that they are gay men, and they allege that Defendants yell derogatory epithets at them.

In addition, Plaintiffs claim that on May 26, 2024, Defendants pointed their hose and sprayed Plaintiff with water and yelled “You’re not getting a dime out of me!”

Before the Court is a motion by Defendants to set aside entry of default against them. The motion is denied as moot because Plaintiffs have filed an amended complaint, which opens the default.

II.       LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 473, subdivision (b) provides for two distinct types of relief—commonly differentiated as “discretionary” and “mandatory”—from certain prior actions or proceedings in the trial court.  (Luri v. Greenwald (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 1119, 1124 (Luri).)   

 

The discretionary portion of section 473, subdivision (b) provides: “The court may, upon terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

The mandatory portion of section 473 requires the court to, “whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)  A timely mea culpa declaration by a plaintiff’s attorney establishing that a dismissal was taken against her client as a result of attorney mistake, inadvertence, or neglect deprives the court of the discretion to deny relief.  (Tackett v. City of Huntington Beach (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 60, 65.)   

           III.     DISCUSSION

 

           Defendants move to set aside entry of default against them.

           After the motion was filed, Plaintiffs filed an amended complaint. “A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., § 472.) The amended complaint adds causes of action for assault and battery. After a defendant's default has been entered, if “ ‘a complaint is amended in matter of substance as distinguished from mere matter of form, the amendment opens the default.’” (Ostling v. Loring (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1731, 1743; see Sass v. Cohen (2020) 10 Cal.5th 861, 880-881.) An amended complaint makes material changes when it increases the damages sought or adds or changes a cause of action based on a different factual or legal theory. (See Carrasco, supra, 164 Cal.App.3d at p. 808; Leo v. Dunlap (1968) 260 Cal.App.2d 24, 27-28.) Thus, the amended complaint, which made material changes by adding new causes of action, opened the default against Defendants.

The motion is therefore moot.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

           The motion to set aside default is denied as moot.  Defendants are to answer the complaint within 30 days of this order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION TO TAX COSTS


I.        INTRODUCTION

           This action was filed on April 18, 2016, and arises from a dispute that resulted in the shooting of several people. Plaintiffs Akop Torosian aka Jack Torosian, Robert Torosian, Marina Fermanyan and Elda Madatyan filed a complaint against Defendants Arutyun Fitilchyan, Hrachya Gasparyan, Armen Tashjian, Armen Jermakyan, and Ashon Mkitaryan. The complaint alleges eight causes of action for: 1) assault and battery, 2) conspiracy to commit assault and battery, 3) negligence, 4) loss of consortium, 5) abuse of process, 6) extortion and blackmail, 7) conspiracy to commit extortion and blackmail, and 8) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

On February 8, 2024, the Court granted Defendants Fitilchyan and Gasparyan’s motion for terminating sanctions as to Plaintiffs Akop and Robert’s complaint for failure to obey multiple court orders to respond to discovery.

On March 11, 2024, Defendants Fitilchyan and Gasparyan filed their verified memorandum of costs in the amount of $2,420.

On April 8, 2024, Robert filed a motion to tax costs. On July 24, 2024, Defendants Fitilchyan and Gasparyan filed an opposition. No reply has been filed.

The motion to tax costs is denied as Plaintiff’s motion is untimely and because he failed to meet his burden to present evidence and prove that the claimed costs are not recoverable.

II.       LEGAL STANDARD

 

A prevailing party is entitled to recover costs, as a matter of right, in any action or proceeding absent a statute expressly noting otherwise. (Cal Civ. Proc. Code § 1032, subd. (b).) Unless a statute provides otherwise, a court has no discretion to deny costs to a prevailing party. (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 129).  A prevailing party is (i) a party that receives a net monetary recovery, (ii) a defendant who obtains a dismissal in its favor (iii) a defendant, when neither the plaintiff nor defendant attained any relief and (iv) a defendant, where the plaintiff(s) obtains no recovery from the defendant. (Cal Civ. Proc. Code § 1032, subd. (a)(4).)

 

“If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal. App. 4th 761, 774.) If the items appear to be proper expenses, they are themselves prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses and services were necessarily incurred. If the items appear proper, it is not enough for the losing party to attack submitted costs by arguing that he thinks the costs were not necessary or reasonable. Rather, the losing party has the burden to present evidence and prove that the claimed costs are not recoverable. (Seever v. Copley Press Inc. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1550, 1557.) However, when items claimed as costs do not appear on their face as proper and necessary, and the items are properly challenged by a motion to tax costs, the burden of establishing the necessity of the items is on the party claiming them as costs. (See Id.; See also Whitney v. Whitney (1958) 164 Cal.App.2d 577, 585.) “[I]f the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ladas, 19 Cal.App.4th at 774.)

 

           III.     DISCUSSION

           Plaintiff moves to tax costs.

Defendants are the prevailing parties as their motion for terminating sanctions was granted.

Plaintiff argues that the costs set forth in the memorandum of costs are excessive, unnecessary, and unrecoverable. Specifically, Plaintiff objects to the initial filing fees of $435 for each defendant.

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail or email, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(1).) Here, Defendants served the memorandum of costs by electronic service on March 11, 2024. (Memorandum of Costs POS.) Thus, this motion would have had to be filed 15 days plus two court days from March 11, 2024, which would be March 28, 2024. This motion was filed and served on April 8, 2024, making it untimely.

In any event, as Defendants filed a verified memorandum of costs, the burden of showing that the costs sought were not reasonable or necessary is on Plaintiff. Plaintiff only makes conclusory assertions and offers no declarations or other evidence in support of his assertions. Plaintiff’s failure to provide a declaration offers the Court no evidence from which to consider Plaintiff’s claims. As discussed above, Plaintiff has the burden of proof to show that the item of cost was not reasonably necessary for the conduct of the litigation; mere conclusory assertions are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing costs are proper. (See Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1261, 1266; County of Kern v. Ginn (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 1107, 1113-14.) In sum, the court finds that Plaintiff fails to meet his burden of proof.

  IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The motion to tax costs is denied.            

Defendants are to give notice of this ruling.
