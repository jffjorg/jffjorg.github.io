Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Christina Ramos v. Delta Airlines, Inc., et al. 

21TRCV00215

1.      Delta Airlines, Inc. (“Delta”) and Scott Winters’ Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, for Summary Adjudication

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Delta Airlines, Inc. and Scott Winters’ Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, for Summary Adjudication is denied, in part, and granted, in part.  

 

            The motion for summary judgment is denied. The motion for summary adjudication of the first cause of action is denied, in part, and granted, in part. The motion for summary adjudication of the second cause of action is granted. The motion for summary adjudication of the third cause of action is denied. The motion for summary adjudication of the claim for punitive damages is denied, in part, and granted, in part.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on March 23, 2021. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff was subjected to sexual harassment and discrimination based on sex in her workplace. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Sexual Harassment (Hostile Work Environment) in Violation of FEHA 2. Sex Discrimination in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(a) 3. Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment, or Retaliation in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(k).  

 

Motion for Summary Judgment and/or Adjudication

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”  Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.

 

“A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(p)(1).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.”  (Avivi, 159 Cal.App.4th at 467; CCP § 437c(c).)

 

Defendants move for an order “granting summary judgment in favor of Defendants and against Plaintiff Christina Ramos (“Plaintiff”). Defendants’ Motion is made pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (a)(1) on the grounds that there exists no triable issue of material fact regarding any of Plaintiff’s causes of action, and accordingly, Defendants are entitled to summary judgment as a matter of law. Alternatively, if for any reason summary judgment cannot be granted in its entirety, then Defendants move the Court for an Order granting summary adjudication of issues[.]” (Notice of Motion, page 2, lines 5-11).

 

Defendants move for summary adjudication of 11 purported “issues.” However, the Court notes that the motion for summary adjudication essentially seeks adjudication of the three causes of action and the claim for punitive damages. In the notice of motion, Defendant identified 11 separate “issues” but many of the purported “issues” are not actually proper “issues” for summary adjudication as explained above under Section 437c(f)(1), but instead merely reasons why certain causes of action should or should not be adjudicated. In the ruling below, certain rulings on issues were grouped together.

 

First Cause of Action for Sexual Harassment in Violation of FEHA

 

The motion for summary adjudication of Issue 4 is granted. The motion for summary adjudication of Issue 5 is denied.

 

“The FEHA prohibits the sexual harassment of an employee. (Gov.Code, § 12940, subd. (j)(1).) Sexual harassment consists of any unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, or other verbal or physical conduct of a sexual nature. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 607, 262 Cal.Rptr. 842 (Fisher ).) It usually arises in two contexts. “Quid pro quo” harassment conditions an employee's continued enjoyment of job benefits on submission to the harassment. “Hostile work environment” harassment has the purpose or effect of either interfering with the work performance of an employee, or creating an intimidating workplace.” Rieger v. Arnold (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 451, 459.

 

Gov. Code § 12940(j)(1) states that it is unlawful for an employer or person to harass an employee based on sex.  Whether based on race, gender, age, or any of the other categories set forth in Gov. Code § 12940(j)(1), the elements of a harassment claim are the same.  “The elements of the cause of action are: “(1) [p]laintiff belongs to a protected group; (2) plaintiff was subject to unwelcome [sexual] harassment; (3) the harassment complained of was based on [sexual];  (4) the harassment complained of was sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment; and (5) respondeat superior.  To be sufficiently pervasive harassment, the acts complained of cannot be isolated or trivial.  Rather, there must be a pattern of harassment of a routine or generalized nature.”  Guthrey v. State of California (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1122–1123 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

Defendant has met its burden to show that Plaintiff’s cause of action has no merit by showing that one or more elements of the causes of action cannot be established or that a complete defense exists to the causes of action. CCP § 437c(p)(2).

 

As to Issue 4, Plaintiff has not met her burden to provide specific facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to her cause of action.  CCP § 437c(p)(2). Plaintiff fails to submit evidence to show that any statements, conduct, or comments were made by Defendant Scott Winters that could be considered severe or pervasive. In fact, there does not appear to be any evidence whatsoever with respect to Scott Winters that could be considered conduct that would amount to sexual harassment.

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the first cause of action as to Scott Winters is granted.

 

As to Issue 5, Plaintiff has met her burden to provide specific facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to her cause of action against Defendant Delta.

 

“[A]n employee claiming harassment based upon a hostile work environment must demonstrate that the conduct complaint of was severe enough or sufficiently pervasive to alter the conditions of employment and create a work environment that qualifies as hostile or abusive to employees because of their sex.  . . .[A] workplace may give rise to liability when it “is permeated with ‘discriminatory [sex-based] intimidation, ridicule, and insult,’ [citation], that is ‘sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment.” Lyle v. Warner Brothers Television Productions (2006) 38 Cal.4th 264, 278-79. 

 

Even after eliminating the alleged conduct that Defendant contends falls outside of the statute of limitations and/or which Defendant was not aware of, Plaintiff has set forth sufficient  evidence that would show the existence of a triable issue of fact as to whether the harassing conduct was severe or pervasive. The alleged conduct includes statements made by an Operations Services Manager Scott Williams when he allegedly inquired about Plaintiff’s physical body measurements and wanted to know Plaintiff’s waist size. (Plaintiff’s Additional Separate Statement of Facts, 15.) The alleged conduct includes co-worker Shawn Henderson asking, “Plaintiff for a hug with his arms spread wide and chest protruded, which Plaintiff understood to be a sexual gesture in an attempt to press himself against her breasts.”  Thereafter, Henderson continued to stare at Plaintiff for long periods of time, and, later, treated her in a hostile manner when working together. Defendant allegedly brushed off Plaintiff’s concerns. (Plaintiff’s Additional Separate Statement of Facts, 18-25, 36-37.)  Finally, Plaintiff relays an incident wherein co-worker Clarence Scott touched Plaintiff’s buttocks. (Plaintiff’s Additional Separate Statement of Facts, 38-42.)

 

The evidence is sufficient to show the existence of a triable issue of fact as to whether the harassing conduct was severe or pervasive and created a hostile work environment. Defendant dismisses these complaints by stating that they were investigated by Defendant and that Defendant determined that there was no harassing conduct. However, the incidents, especially when one of the incidents involved an actual touching, are sufficient for purposes of showing the existence of a triable issue of material fact. Again, merely because the Defendant itself investigated and determined that it could not substantiate Plaintiff’s claims is not dispositive for purposes of summary adjudication. If such self-conducted investigations could be deemed dispositive for purposes of a motion for summary adjudication, undoubtedly many similar Plaintiffs would be left with no recourse if the actual accused party could simply rely on its contention that it investigated itself and found no wrongdoing.

 

            The motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action against Delta is denied.

 

Second Cause of Action for Sex Discrimination in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(a)


As to Issues 1 to 3, Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication is granted. Defendant has set forth evidence to meet its burden of showing that an essential element or elements of the second cause of action cannot be established. Plaintiff has not met her burden to provide facts to show that a material issue of fact exists as to the second cause of action. CCP § 437c(p)(2).

 

Gov. Code, § 12940(a) states:

“It is an unlawful employment practice, unless based upon a bona fide occupational qualification, or, except where based upon applicable security regulations established by the United States or the State of California:

(a) For an employer, because of the race, religious creed, color, national origin, ancestry, physical disability, mental disability, reproductive health decisionmaking, medical condition, genetic information, marital status, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, age, sexual orientation, or veteran or military status of any person, to refuse to hire or employ the person or to refuse to select the person for a training program leading to employment, or to bar or to discharge the person from employment or from a training program leading to employment, or to discriminate against the person in compensation or in terms, conditions, or privileges of employment.”

 

“As noted, section 12940(a) prohibits an employer from taking an employment action against a person “because of” the person's race, sex, disability, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristic. The phrase “because of” means there must be a causal link between the employer's consideration of a protected characteristic and the action taken by the employer. The existence of this causation requirement in the statute is undisputed.” Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 215.

 

In employer discrimination actions, California employs the three-part burden shifting test established in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792.  First, Plaintiff must establish a prima facie case of case of discrimination.  “This step is designed to eliminate at the outset the most patently meritless claims, as where the plaintiff is not a member of the protected class or was clearly unqualified, or where the job he sought was withdrawn and never filled.” Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 354.  Second, the employer has the burden “to rebut the presumption by producing admissible evidence, sufficient to raise a genuine issue of fact and to justify a judgment for the [employer], that its action was taken for a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason.”  Id. at 355–56 (internal quotations omitted).  Finally, Plaintiff has “the opportunity to attack the employer's proffered reasons as pretexts for discrimination, or to offer any other evidence of discriminatory motive. The ultimate burden of persuasion on the issue of actual discrimination remains with the plaintiff.” Id. at 356.

 

“Under the three-part test developed in McDonnell Douglas Corp. v. Green, supra, 411 U.S. 792, 93 S.Ct. 1817, 36 L.Ed.2d 668: “(1) The complainant must establish a prima facie case of discrimination; (2) the employer must offer a legitimate reason for his actions; (3) the complainant must prove that this reason was a pretext to mask an illegal motive.” (Clark v. Claremont University Center, supra, 6 Cal.App.4th at p. 662, 8 Cal.Rptr.2d 151.) If the employee establishes a prima facie case, the employer is required to offer a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse employment action. (Horn, supra, 72 Cal.App.4th 798, 806, 85 Cal.Rptr.2d 459.) The employer's burden at this stage is to go forward with additional evidence; it does not take on a burden of persuasion. (Reeves v. Sanderson Plumbing Products, Inc. (2000) 530 U.S. 133, 142, 120 S.Ct. 2097, 2106, 147 L.Ed.2d 105; Clark v. Claremont University Center, supra, 6 Cal.App.4th at p. 663, 8 Cal.Rptr.2d 151.) If the employer produces substantial evidence of a legitimate, nondiscriminatory reason for the adverse employment action, the presumption of discrimination created by the prima facie case “ ‘simply drops out of the picture’ ” (Horn, supra, 72 Cal.App.4th at p. 807, 85 Cal.Rptr.2d 459, quoting St. Mary's Honor Center v. Hicks (1993) 509 U.S. 502, 511, 113 S.Ct. 2742, 125 L.Ed.2d 407) and the burden shifts back to the employee to prove intentional discrimination. (Horn, supra, 72 Cal.App.4th at p. 806, 85 Cal.Rptr.2d 459; Hersant, supra, 57 Cal.App.4th at p. 1003, 67 Cal.Rptr.2d 483; Clark, supra, 6 Cal.App.4th at p. 664, 8 Cal.Rptr.2d 151.) “[T]he plaintiff may establish pretext ‘either directly by persuading the court that a discriminatory reason more likely motivated the employer or indirectly by showing that the employer's proffered explanation is unworthy of credence.’ ” (Godwin, supra, 150 F.3d at p. 1220, quoting Texas Dept. of Community Affairs v. Burdine (1981) 450 U.S. 248, 256, 101 S.Ct. 1089, 67 L.Ed.2d 207.) Circumstantial evidence of “ ‘pretense’ must be ‘specific’ and ‘substantial’ in order to create a triable issue with respect to whether the employer intended to discriminate” on an improper basis. (Godwin, supra, 150 F.3d 1217, 1222; Horn, supra, 72 Cal.App.4th at p. 807, 85 Cal.Rptr.2d 459.)10 With direct evidence of pretext, “ ‘a triable issue as to the actual motivation of the employer is created even if the evidence is not substantial.’ [Citation.] The plaintiff is required to produce ‘very little’ direct evidence of the employer's discriminatory intent to move past summary judgment.” Morgan v. Regents of University of Cal. (2000) 88 Cal.App.4th 52, 68–69.

 

            Defendant has provided competent evidence to show that its management decisions did not amount to an adverse employment action and that even the action that it did eventually undertake with respect to Plaintiff was conducted for non-discriminatory reasons.  Specifically, Defendant submitted evidence that any notations or “coaching” that Plaintiff was provided were simply due to performance and attendance reasons. In addition, Defendant submitted evidence that those notes and “write-ups” were not in any way based on Plaintiff’s sex. (Defendant’s Separate Statement and Supporting Evidence, 1-108.)

 

            Plaintiff failed to submit competent admissible evidence to show that any action taken against Plaintiff was significantly adverse to her employment or was taken for a discriminatory reason. Once an employer articulates a “legitimate non-retaliatory reason” for an employment action, it becomes the plaintiff’s burden to present proof that the reason for the employment action was merely a pretext.  See, Guz, supra, 24 Cal. 4th at p. 378.  The plaintiff must provide “substantial evidence that the articulated reason for her discipline was a false “pretext” designed to hide unlawful motives.  See, Id., at 361. 

 

Here, in opposition, Plaintiff has provided no competent evidence to show that Defendant’s employment action was motivated by discriminatory reasons, based on sex, and that the legitimate reasons for the employment actions provided by Defendant were mere pretext for discrimination based on sex.  Instead, with the opposition, Plaintiff focuses significantly on the sexual harassment allegations, the purported culture within Defendant’s employment structure that fostered such an environment, and Plaintiff’s belief that her speaking out about sexual harassment (both hers and other women) led to these perceived adverse employment actions. However, no competent evidence was provided to show that Plaintiff suffered actual discrimination based on her sex. Perhaps such allegations, if supported with evidence, could have supported a cause of action for Retaliation under FEHA based on her alleged attempts to engage in a “protected activity” (making complaints of sexual harassment) but Plaintiff did not allege a cause of action for Retaliation.

 

Thus, Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the second causes of action is granted.

 

Third Cause of Action for Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment, or Retaliation in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(k)

 

As to Issues 6 and 7, the motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

“Section 12940, subdivision (k) states in part that [i]t is an unlawful employment practice ... : [¶] ... [¶] ...For an employer ... to fail to take all reasonable steps necessary to prevent discrimination and harassment from occurring. It creates a separate actionable tort enforceable upon the establishment of the usual tort elements of duty of care, breach of duty (a negligent act or omission), causation, and damages.” Dickson v. Burke Williams, Inc. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 1307, 1313 (internal quotation omitted).

 

Defendant has met its burden to show that Plaintiff’s cause of action has no merit by showing that one or more elements of the causes of action cannot be established or that a complete defense exists to the causes of action. CCP § 437c(p)(2).

 

As to Issues 6 and 7, Plaintiff has met her burden to provide specific facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to her cause of action. (Plaintiff’s Additional Separate Statement of Facts, 15-58.)  The Court has already set forth the evidence of the alleged conduct that would support the existence of a triable issue of material fact with respect to harassment. Plaintiff has additionally provided evidence of conduct on the part of Defendant, through the course of investigating these allegations, that would support her contention that Defendant did not take all reasonable steps necessary to prevent harassment. Again, Defendant contends that it adequately conducted its own investigation and believes that all of Plaintiff’s accusations are without merit. Plaintiff has provided at a minimum a triable issue of material fact as to whether those investigations were sufficient and whether the conclusions ultimately reached by Defendant were adequate to prevent the harassment.

 

The motion for summary adjudication of the third cause of action is denied.

 

Claim for Punitive Damages

 

As to Issues 8 and 9, the motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

As to Issues 10-11, the motion for summary adjudication of the claim for punitive damages as against Winter is granted.  Since no cause of action now remains against Winter personally, no claim for punitive damages can be maintained.

 

Civ. Code, § 3294 states, in relevant part:

“(a) In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.

(b) An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

 

Issue 8 states as follows: “Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages against Delta fails as a matter of law and must be dismissed because no officer, director, or managing agent of Delta engaged in oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct against Plaintiff.”

 

As to Issue 8, Defendant has not met its burden to show that Plaintiff’s claim for damages has no merit. Thus, the burden does not shift to Plaintiff to provide specific facts to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact. CCP § 437c(p)(2). While Defendant has provided evidence that no officer, director, or managing agent themselves directly engaged in the oppressive or malicious conduct, Defendant has not provided evidence to indicate that an officer, director, or managing agent did not authorize or ratify the malicious or oppressive conduct. In addition, none of the facts set forth in the separate statement of facts attempt to directly establish that no officer, director, or managing agent authorized or ratified the malicious or oppressive conduct. Thus, stated another way, adjudication of the purported “issue” as framed by Defendant would not actually adjudicate the claim for punitive damages because another manner in which Plaintiff can pursue punitive damages against Delta would be through a theory of authorization and/or ratification as opposed to by merely attempting to show that an actual officer, director, or managing agent engaged in the specific conduct complained of by Plaintiff.

 

Issue 9 states as follows: “Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages against Delta fails as a matter of law and must be dismissed because Plaintiff cannot demonstrate that Delta engaged in oppression, fraud, or malice against her.”

 

As to Issue 9, Plaintiff has provided sufficient evidence to show the existence of a triable issue of material fact that agents of Defendant engaged in conduct that would amount to malice or oppression against her based on her allegations and evidence of sexual harassment as noted above in the Court’s ruling to the motion for summary adjudication of the first cause of action.

 

Thus, the motion for summary adjudication of the claim for punitive damages as to Delta is denied.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
