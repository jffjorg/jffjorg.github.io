DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV07692    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: 39

     TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER

19STCV07692

MOTION

Motion to Reopen Discovery

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Guadalupe Encisco

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Avanti Hospitals, LLC

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Guadalupe Encisco (“Plaintiff”) moves to reopen discovery for purposes of discovery on Plaintiff’s recent surgeries.  Defendant Avanti Hospitals, LLC (“Defendant”) opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

In exercising its discretion in determining whether to re-open discovery, the court must consider the necessity and reasons for the additional discovery, the diligence or lack thereof by the party seeking to reopen discovery in attempting to complete discovery prior to the discovery cutoff, and whether permitting the discovery will prevent the case from going forward on the scheduled trial date or will otherwise prejudice any party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (b).)

Plaintiff moves to re-open discovery concerning surgeries she underwent on January 8 and 11, 2024.  (See Declaration of Raymond Ghermezian.)  However, Plaintiff has not submitted a discovery plan, as the court directed Plaintiff to do in its March 1, 2024 order.  Accordingly, the Court continues the matter to July 17, 2024.  Plaintiff is to file and serve a discovery plan no later than July 2, 2024.  Defendant may file a discovery plan in response no later than July 9, 2024.     

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 23STCV30496    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV30496

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Starbucks Corporation

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Ashley Bautista

 

MOTION

              Defendant Starbucks Corporation (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration of the claims Plaintiff Ashley Bautista (“Plaintiff”) asserts against Defendant.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Defendant presents an arbitration agreement Plaintiff does not dispute she signed when Defendant hired her.  That agreement provides Plaintiff and Defendant agree to arbitrate claims “brought under any statute, local ordinance, or common law relating to [Plaintiff]'s employment, including those concerning any element of compensation, harassment, discrimination, retaliation, recovery of bonus or relocation benefits, leaves of absence, accommodations, or termination of employment.”  (Declaration of Kathryn Daly in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit F.)  Defendant has adequately demonstrated the parties entered into an agreement to arbitrate their claims.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the arbitration agreement is unconscionable under Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83 (Armendariz).  The arbitration agreement Plaintiff signed specifies the Federal Arbitration Act governs.  A choice of law provision in an arbitration agreement is enforceable.  (See Coopers & Lybrand v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 524, 538.)  As such, the Federal Arbitration Act applies. 

Under the Federal Arbitration Act, defenses that derive their meaning from the fact an agreement to arbitrate is at issue cannot invalidate agreements to arbitrate.  (AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion (2011) 563 U.S. 333, 339.)  The rules set forth in Armendariz are specific to arbitration agreements.  (Abramson v. Juniper Networks, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 638, 661.)  These rules, therefore, cannot invalidate the arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant.  The Court considers whether the arbitration agreement is unconscionable pursuant to generally applicable principles of contract law.

Civil Code section 1670.5 provides “If the court as a matter of law finds the contract or any clause of the contract to have been unconscionable at the time it was made the court may refuse to enforce the contract, or it may enforce the remainder of the contract without the unconscionable clause, or it may so limit the application of any unconscionable clause as to avoid any unconscionable result.”  (Civ. Code, § 1670.5, subd. (a).)  Unconscionability is defined as “an absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms which are unreasonably favorable to the other party.”  (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486 (A & M Produce).)

Unconscionability thus has a procedural and a substantive element.  (A & M Produce, supra, 135 Cal.App.3d at p. 486.)  The court considers the factors of oppression and surprise in determining whether a contract is procedurally unconscionable.  (Ibid.)  Oppression refers to inequality of bargaining power, which cuts off meaningful negotiation.  (Ibid.)  Surprise refers to the concealment of the agreed-upon terms in a lengthy form drafted by the party seeking to enforce them.  (Ibid.)  Substantive unconscionability refers to the extent to which a contractual term reallocates the risks of a transaction “in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.”  (Id. at 487.)  Both substantive and procedural unconscionability must be present for a contract to be unenforceable, and “the greater the unfair surprise or inequality of bargaining power, the less unreasonable the risk allocation which will be tolerated.”  (Ibid.)

The evidence before the Court shows Defendant prepared the arbitration agreement, and Plaintiff had no opportunity to negotiate or discuss its terms.   It is, therefore, a contract of adhesion.  (Neal v. State Farm Ins. Companies (1961) 188 Cal.App.2d 690, 694.)  This constitutes some evidence of procedural unconscionability.  (Flores v. Transamerica HomeFirst, Inc. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 846, 853.) 

Plaintiff has not demonstrated the arbitration agreement is substantively unconscionable.  Plaintiff argues the agreement is substantively unconscionable because it requires Plaintiff to waive statutory claims with administrative agencies.  The arbitration agreement, however, provides Plaintiff is “giving up the opportunity to recover monetary amounts from” the equal Employment Opportunity Commission, National Labor Relations Board, Department of Labor, or other similar agencies, but may file a “claim or charge” with such agencies.  (Daly Declaration, supra, Exhibit F.)

 In support of her contention, Plaintiff cites OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111 (OTO).  In OTO, an employee filed a complaint with the Labor Commissioner for unpaid wages.  The employer offered to settle the complaint, but the employee rejected the employer's settlement offer and requested a Berman hearing, which is an informal hearing before the Labor Commissioner for an employee with a claim for unpaid wages.  The procedure offers protections for the employee, such as representation by the Labor Commissioner on appeal if the employee cannot afford representation.  The employer filed a petition to compel arbitration and to stay the administrative proceedings, which the hearing officer denied.  The hearing officer proceeded with the hearing without the participation of the employer, and awarded the employee unpaid wages, damages, and penalties.  The employer then sought to vacate the award and compel arbitration.  (Id., at pp. 118-121.) 

In finding the agreement unconscionable and unenforceable, the Court noted “an arbitration agreement is not categorically unconscionable solely because it entails a waiver of the Berman procedure.  An agreement to arbitrate wage disputes can be enforceable so long as it provides an accessible and affordable process for resolving those disputes.” (OTO, supra, 8 Cal.5th at p. 118.)   However, the Court noted the circumstances involved such an unusually high level of procedural unconscionability that the employee’s waiver of the Berman procedure was sufficient to render the agreement substantively unconscionable and unenforceable.  (Ibid.)  The Court found that the arbitration agreement was a contract of adhesion because the employer required the employee to accept it as a condition of employment. (Id. at p. 126.)  The Court noted the employer used a low-level employee to present the agreement, along with other employment-related documents, to the subject employee at his workspace, which gave the employee the impression the employer would not provide any explanation of the significance of the agreement.  (Id. at p. 127.) 

The Court further found the employee did not know he was giving up his Berman rights, and it related, “[t]he agreement is a paragon of prolixity, only slightly more than a page long but written in an extremely small font.  The single dense paragraph covering arbitration requires 51 lines .  . . .  The substance of the agreement is similarly opaque.  The sentences are complex, filled with statutory references and legal jargon.”  (OTO, supra, 8 Cal.5th at p. 128.)  The Court held, “Considering the unusually coercive setting in which this bargain was entered, we conclude it was sufficiently one-sided as to render the agreement unenforceable.”  (Id., at p. 136.)  The Court noted the result would differ if the employer set out the terms of the arbitration agreement in a legible format and understandable language or had given the employee a reasonable opportunity to seek clarification or advice.  (Id., at p. 136.)

In the instant case, the arbitration agreement is set forth in legible text and ordinary language.  Plaintiff agreed to the arbitration agreement after viewing it online at her convenience.  (Daly Declaration, supra,  ¶ 16, Exhibit H.)  As such, Plaintiff had ample opportunity to read the arbitration agreement and seek advice, if necessary.  The circumstances in this instance do not reflect the extraordinary level of procedural unconscionability at issue in OTO.  As such, Plaintiff has not demonstrated the agreement is unconscionable. 

Plaintiff then argues the waiver of Plaintiff’s right to bring class or representative claims is unenforceable.  Plaintiff has the right to bring a representative claim under the Labor Code Private Attorneys’ General Act of 2004 (“PAGA”) regardless of the arbitration agreement.  (See Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104, 1123.)  However, Plaintiff does not assert a representative claim.  Further, under the Federal Arbitration Act, the court must compel arbitration of arbitrable claims, even if some claims are not arbitrable. (KPMG LLP v. Cocchi (2011) 565 U.S. 18, 22.) 

Plaintiff also contends the arbitration agreement attempts to require Plaintiff to waive a jury trial in the event the case were to proceed to trial.  Under the California Arbitration Act, a pre-dispute jury trial waiver is evidence of substantive unconscionability.  (Lange v. Monster Energy Company (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 436, 452.)  However, the Federal Arbitration Act applies here.  Further, because Plaintiff’s claims are arbitrable, this provision is inapplicable.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate her claims is granted.  Accordingly, the court will set an order to show cause why this case should not be dismissed following arbitration.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
