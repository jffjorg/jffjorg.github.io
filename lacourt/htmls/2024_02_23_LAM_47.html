DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCP03518    Hearing Date: February 23, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     February 23, 2024                 TRIAL DATE: April 16, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Myung Hye Kim v. David Okju An et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCP03518           

 

(1) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION (SET ONE); REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

(2) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE); REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1)(2)Plaintiff Myung Hye Kim

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1)(2)No response on eCourt as of 02/20/24

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         09/28/22: Complaint filed.

·         12/22/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         03/03/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Defendant/Cross-Complainant David Okju An as to Plaintiff.

·         03/13/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Defendants/Cross-Complainants Jhon Taehyo Lee and Dong Goon Lim as to Defendant David Okju An.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, and elder abuse arising from a series of agreements with Defendants to perform construction work for Plaintiff

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to requests for production and special interrogatories, and for sanctions.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART as to Requests Nos. 1 and 2 only. Defendant is ordered to provide further verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 20 days of this order.

 

Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions are DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to requests for production propounded to Defendant.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to a demand for inspection when the court finds that: “(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the moving party to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) These facts must also be set forth in a separate statement filed by the moving party. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(c).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Timeliness

 

A motion to compel further responses to requests for production must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

Plaintiff served Requests for Production (Set One) on Defendant on May 11, 2023. (Declaration of John A. S. Baik ISO Mot. ¶ 2; Exh. 1.) After multiple rounds of extensions, responses, and supplemental responses, the most recent round of supplemental responses were served on January 22, 2024. (Id. ¶ 22, Exh. 21.)  This motion was filed the next day, on January 23, 2024. The motion is therefore timely.

 

Meet and Confer

 

A party making a motion to compel further responses must also include a declaration stating facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally the issues presented by the motion before filing the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

 

The Declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel in support of the motion sets forth the extensive history of Plaintiff’s attempts to informally disputes concerning Defendant’s responses to these discovery requests. (See generally Baik Decl.) However, Plaintiff made no effort to meet and confer regarding the sufficiency of this round of responses before filing this motion. In the interest of a final resolution of this dispute, however, the Court will consider the motion on its merits.

 

Good Cause

 

            As a preliminary matter, Plaintiff does not directly address whether good cause exists for any of the discovery propounded here. However, as Plaintiff’s motion is based on a contention that Defendant’s responses failed to comply with the Court’s December 15, 2023 Minute Order pursuant to an agreement by the parties, the Court finds that good cause has been shown by that agreement.

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff contends that Defendant An failed to comply with the Court’s December 15, 2023 order at an informal discovery conference to provide verified, code-compliant responses to this discovery. (See Baik Decl. Exh. 14.) A code-compliant response must be one of the following:

 

(1) A statement that the party will comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling by the date set for the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (c) of Section 2031.030 and any related activities.

 

(2) A representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of a particular item or category of item.

 

(3) An objection to the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210(a).) A representation of inability to comply “shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand” and “shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.) The statement “shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Id.)

 

            Plaintiff contends that none of Defendant’s responses to this discovery are code-compliant because they do not precisely mirror the form described in the Code of Civil Procedure, and because some of the responses are missing documents which should have been produced.

 

1.      Requests Nos. 1-2

 

            Requests Nos. 1 and 2 seek documents identified by Defendant in his responses to the concurrently served form and special interrogatories, respectively. (SS p.2:4-7.) In response, Defendant stated “Responding party has produced responsive documents as he understands the request.” (See Baik Decl. Exh. 21.) Plaintiff contends that this response is inadequate because it does not mirror the Code of Civil Procedure, and because several documents identified in Defendant’s interrogatory responses are still missing. (SS p.2:9-20.) As Defendant has not responded to this motion, the Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to further responses to these requests producing the documents identified in the interrogatories, or otherwise accounting for their whereabouts as required by the code.

 

2.      Requests Nos. 3, 5, 11-16, 18-19, 21-25, 46-48

 

These requests pertain to various documents either described in or referencing matters described in the Complaint and Cross-Complaint. (SS p.2:22-4:5.) Defendant responded to each interrogatory by stating that “Responding party has produced responsive documents as he understands the request.” Plaintiff contends that this response is not code compliant because it does not mirror the Code of Civil Procedure. This argument is not well-taken. Technical defects of the type described here are not indicative of an inadequate or evasive response that requires an order compelling further production.

 

3.      Requests Nos. 4, 26-43, 45

 

These requests pertain to correspondence between Defendant and various individuals concerning the business ventures at the heart of this case. (SS p. 4:14-6:7.) In response, Defendant stated “Responding Party has made a reasonable and diligent search for the documents requested and has found none. Responding Party believes that no such document exists, and as such, is not presently within the care, custody and control of this Responding Party.” (Id. p.6:8-13.) Defendant stated that it is his belief that if such document exists it would be in the possession, custody, or control of specific individuals whom he identifies. (Id.) As above, Plaintiff contends that this response is not code compliant because it does not mirror the Code of Civil Procedure. For the reasons stated above, the Court disagrees.

 

4.      Requests Nos. 6-8, 17, 20

 

These requests pertain to Alaska Bento’s stocks, bylaws, minutes, and financials, and Defendant’s involvement therein. (SS p.7:1-9.) Defendant responded: “Responding Party has made a reasonable and diligent search for the documents requested and has found none. Responding Party does not know if such document exists, and as such, is not presently within the care, custody and control of this Responding Party.” (Id. p.7:10-12.) Defendant stated that it is his belief that if such document exists it would be in the possession, custody, or control of specific individuals whom he identifies. (Id.) As above, Plaintiff contends that this response is not code compliant because it does not mirror the Code of Civil Procedure. For the reasons stated above, the Court disagrees.

 

5.      Requests Nos. 9-10

 

            These requests seek Alaska Bento’s Tax returns and K-1s issued between 2019 and 2022. (SS p.7:21-24.) In response, Defendant stated “Responding party has produced tax returns for years 2019 and 2021. Tax returns for years 2020 and 2022 will be produced as soon as they are in the possession of responding party.” Plaintiff contends that this statement is inadequate because it does not follow the form of a statement of inability to comply as set forth in the Code of Civil Procedure. However, this is not a statement of inability to comply, but rather a statement of compliance.

 

6.      Request No. 44

 

This request seeks all written correspondence between Defendant and any third parties for whom he performed construction work from January 1, 2018 to the present. (SS p 8:7-9.) “Responding Party has made a reasonable and diligent search for the documents requested and has found none. Responding Party does not know if such document exists, and as such, is not presently within the care, custody and control of this Responding Party.” (Id. p.8:10-12.) As above, Plaintiff contends that this response is not code compliant because it does not mirror the Code of Civil Procedure. For the reasons stated above, the Court disagrees.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff also requests sanctions against Defendant for the failure to provide code-compliant responses. As Plaintiff has not fully prevailed on this motion, the Court declines to order sanctions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART as to Requests Nos. 1 and 2 only. Defendant is ordered to provide further verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 20 days of this order.

 

Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories [Sanctions Only]

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to special interrogatories, and for sanctions. At a previous hearing on this motion, Plaintiff represented to the Court that supplemental responses had been provided, mooting the merits of this motion and leaving only the issue of sanctions. (February 2, 2024 Minute Order.) The Court therefore scheduled this further hearing on the issue of sanctions only.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions on any attorney engaging in the misuse of the discovery process by requiring that attorney to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300(d) requires the Court to impose sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

            Here, Plaintiff seeks sanctions in the amount of $5,961.67 representing 10.6 hours of attorney time at $395 per hour, plus 4.3 anticipated hours at the same rate, plus $61.65 in filing fees, plus a further $14.52 in anticipated filing fees. (Declaration of John A. S. Baik ISO Mot. ¶¶ 19-22.) This fee request is unreasonably inflated on its face. It should not take 10.6 hours of actual time, plus a further 4.3 hours of anticipated time for an attorney with nearly three decades of experience to bring a motion to compel responses challenging boilerplate objections following a court order to provide code-compliant responses. The Court therefore exercises its discretion to deny this request as unreasonably inflated. (Chavez v. City of Los Angeles (2010) 47 Cal. 4th 970, 989-991.)

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED IN PART as to Requests Nos. 1 and 2 only. Defendant is ordered to provide further verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 20 days of this order.

 

Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions are DENIED.

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     February 23, 2024                 TRIAL DATE: September 17, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Stewart Marroquin v. CS Hotel LLC et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV15517           

 

MOTION TO QUASH THIRD PARTY DEPOSITION SUBPOENA FOR BUSINESS RECORDS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Stewart Marroquin in pro per.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendants CS Hotel LLC and Percy Cifuentes

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an employment discrimination action that was filed on July 5, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that he was harassed on the basis of a physical disability, and that Defendants refused to provide him with reasonable accommodations for his condition before wrongly terminating him on the basis of his disability.

 

Plaintiff moves to quash a deposition subpoena for business records propounded to his former employer, Los Angeles Conservation Corps, Inc., or, in the alternative, for a protective order limiting the scope of the subpoena.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash Third Party Deposition Subpoena for Business Records is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.  Specifically, the Court grants the motion to quash as to document categories 2, 8 (as to disciplinary records), and 9, and limits the temporal scope of production with respect to document categories 1, 4, 5, 7, 8 (as to performance reviews) and 11, based solely on Plaintiff’s relevance and overbreadth objections, and otherwise denies the motion. 

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to quash a deposition subpoena for business records propounded to his former employer, Los Angeles Conservation Corps, Inc., or, in the alternative, for a protective order limiting the scope of the subpoena.

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1 provides:

 

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1(a).) There is no meet and confer requirement in section 1987.1. There is also no requirement for a separate statement when no response has been provided to the request for discovery. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(b)(1).) Furthermore, there is no requirement for a showing of good cause for production of documents in connection with a deposition subpoena. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2020.510(b); Terry v. SLICO (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 352, 358.)

 

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff moves to quash a subpoena propounded to a former employer, Los Angeles Conservation Corps, Inc. Defendants served the subpoena on December 7, 2023. (Declaration of Stewart Marroquin ISO Mot. Exh. 2.) Attachment 3 to the Subpoena sets forth the documents sought from January 1, 2008 to the present relating to Plaintiff as follows:

 

1.      All applications of employment submitted to LOS ANGELES CONSERVATION CORPS, INC. (hereinafter "YOU") by STEWART MARROQUIN;

 

2.      Any employee handbook(s) YOU provided to STEWART MARROQUIN;

 

3.      Any termination or separation letter YOU provided to STEWART MARROQUIN or notice of voluntary resignation from STEWART MARROQUIN to YOU;

 

4.      The job description for any positions STEWART MARROQUIN held while he was employed by YOU;

 

5.      Documents sufficient to reflect STEWART MARROQUIN's date of hire and date of termination;

 

6.      All work restrictions, doctor's notes, and requests for accommodations STEWART MARROQUIN required during his employment with YOU;

 

7.      STEWART MARROQUIN's payroll records reflecting his rate(s) of pay and compensation;

 

8.      STEWART MARROQUIN's performance reviews and disciplinary actions;

 

9.      All complaints of discrimination, harassment, and/or retaliation made to YOU by STEWART MARROQUIN;

 

10.  Documents sufficient to reflect all work-related injuries sustained by STEWART MARROQUIN including, but not limited to worker's compensation claims; and

 

11.  All W-2s and/or 1099s issued by YOU to STEWART MARROQUIN.

 

 (Id. Exh. 2 Attach. 3.) Plaintiff contends that this documents subpoena is overbroad, seeks documents that are irrelevant and/or are protected from disclosure, is unduly burdensome, invades Plaintiff’s privacy, and seeks documents which would not be admissible at trial. Based on these arguments, Plaintiff asks that the Court limit the disclosure of documents to categories 6 and 10, pertaining to work restrictions, doctor’s notes, requests for accommodations, work-related injuries and worker’s compensation claims. 

 

            At the outset, as to Plaintiff’s contentions that certain evidence sought would not be admissible at trial, such an argument is insufficient to demonstrate that the materials sought are not discoverable. Evidence may be discoverable if it is reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence, whether or not it is, itself, admissible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

 

In ruling on a privacy objection in the context of discovery, the party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) The party asserting a privacy right must also establish an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances. (Id.) Further, the party asserting a privacy right must establish a threatened intrusion that is serious. (Id.) The Court need not proceed to the fourth step of balancing competing interests if all three of the above are not satisfied. (Id. at 555.)

 

If the Court reaches the fourth step, the Court must balance these competing considerations: The party seeking information may raise whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure may serve. (Id. at 552.) The party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy. (Id.) Courts may not require the party seeking discovery to demonstrate a “compelling need” simply because discovery of any facially private information is sought. (Id. at 556-557.) When a privacy interest is asserted, the party seeking production must show that the information sought is directly relevant to a cause of action or a defense. (Harris v. Superior Court (Smets) (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 661, 665, citing Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 859-62.)

 

            With respect to Plaintiff’s privacy objections, Plaintiff contends that he has a legally protected interest in his personnel records, relying various cases including those addressing discovery of third-party personnel records. (See, e.g., Harding Lawson Associates v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 7, 10.) These cases hold generally that “personnel records and employment history are within the scope of the protection provided by the state and federal Constitutions.”  (San Diego Trolley, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 87 Cal. App. 4th 1083, 1097, disapproved of on other grounds in Williams, supra, at p. 557, fn. 8.) That said, Plaintiff provides no basis for the conclusion that he has a reasonable expectation of privacy in the specific documents at issue in this case. Further, Plaintiff provides no basis to support a conclusion that the threatened intrusion is serious. Plaintiff has therefore failed to carry his burden to justify his privacy objection.

 

            Finally, the Court turns to Plaintiff’s overbreadth and relevance objections.  Defendant contends that its request for Plaintiff’s complaints of discrimination, etc., the prior employer’s handbook, and disciplinary and termination records are reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence to “prove Plaintiff’s familiarity with employment rules, grievance protocols, and standards of conduct (he failed to complain about any alleged harassment against Defendants)” in support of Defendants’ affirmative defenses of Consent and Avoidable Consequences.  The Court fails to see the connection between Plaintiff’s complaints against a prior employer or its employee handbook, on the one hand, and either of the affirmative defenses advanced.  While it is true that Plaintiff must show that harassment was non-consensual under FEHA, Plaintiff’s knowledge of a prior employer’s policies or his complaints against that prior employer have no bearing whatsoever on whether Plaintiff consented to the alleged harassment he encountered at Defendants’ workplace.  Nor is the Court aware of any case law permitting a “consent” defense to be asserted against statutory claims for discrimination and retaliation or the related common law claims asserted by Plaintiff here.  This is particularly true given that Defendants’ consent defense is apparently grounded on Civil Code § 56, et seq., which has to do with maintaining the confidentiality of medical records, and thus is utterly immaterial to the allegations here. 

 

Nor can it be said that production of these documents is justified by Defendants’ assertion of the affirmative defense of Avoidable Consequences.  To prove this defense, an employer must demonstrate it took reasonable steps to prevent and correct workplace harassment, that plaintiff unreasonably failed to use those preventative and corrective measures, and that reasonable use of those measures would have prevented some or all of the plaintiff’s harm.  (CACI 2526.)  Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s general awareness of a prior employer’s policies is somehow relevant in determining whether he unreasonably failed to use Defendants’ complaint procedures.  This connection is tenuous at best, but it disintegrates entirely in the face of Plaintiff’s admission he made no complaint because of threats of retaliation by Defendant Cifuentes, not because he was unaware in some general way that some employers had remedial procedures in place.  Defendants’ attempt to support their quest to obtain Plaintiff’s prior disciplinary records is even farther afield, and they make no effort to explain how these documents could ever lead to disclosure of admissible evidence about their consent or avoidable consequences defenses.  Based on these considerations, the Court grants the motion to quash as to document categories 2, 8 (as to disciplinary records), and 9. 

 

Defendants argue that employment applications, performance reviews, job descriptions, and termination or separation documents should be disclosed because they might include admissible evidence in support of their affirmative defenses for unsatisfactory job performance and/or for after-acquired evidence.  The Court disagrees that evidence about Plaintiff’s poor job performance for a prior employer could be used to support such a contention as to Plaintiff’s work for Defendant.  The proffer of such evidence would run afoul of Evidence Code section 1101’s bar on the use of character evidence in the form of specific instances of prior conduct to demonstrate later conduct consistent with that evidence.  That said, the Court concurs that at least some of these documents are reasonably calculated to lead to the disclosure of admissible evidence regarding the latter defense, in that they may contain information about plaintiff’s work experience or reasons for leaving his prior job that contradict representations he made to Defendants in his employment application.  Thus, the Court denies the motion to quash as to document categories 1, 4, 5, and 8 (as to performance reviews), but limits the production to any documents revealing Plaintiff’s compensation during the five years before his hiring by Defendants in 2020.  Earlier documents dating back to Plaintiff’s hire in 2008 are so remote as to lack any probative value with respect to the relevant issues.

 

Defendants argue they are entitled to Plaintiff’s payroll records and W-2 and 1099 forms to determine Plaintiff’s employability and assess whether he has failed to mitigate his damages.  In support of this contention, Defendants cite to their Eighth Affirmative Defense (No Damages), which merely asserts that Plaintiff suffered no adverse action, discrimination, retaliation or harassment, and thus is not entitled to any damages. Plaintiff’s tax and payroll records from prior employment are utterly immaterial to this defense.  Defendants also point to their Tenth Affirmative Defense for Failure to Mitigate to justify this document request but fail to connect the elements of the defense to what the sought-after documents are reasonably calculated to reveal.  In an employment action seeking damages from wrongful termination under FEHA or in violation of public policy, an employer advancing a failure to mitigate defense must prove that employment substantially similar to the plaintiff’s former job was available, that the plaintiff failed to make reasonable efforts to seek and retain such employment, and what amount plaintiff could have earned from this employment.  While the tax records Defendant seeks are wholly irrelevant to the first two elements, they may lead to the discovery of evidence about what Plaintiff could have earned if he had secured substantially similar employment.  Accordingly, the Court denies the motion to quash as to document categories 7 and 11 but limits the production to any documents revealing Plaintiff’s compensation during the five years before his hiring by Defendants in 2020.

 

            In sum, the Court grants the motion to quash as described above as to document categories 2, 8 (as to disciplinary records), and 9, and limits the temporal scope of production with respect to document categories 1, 4, 5, 7, 8 (as to performance reviews) and 11, based solely on Plaintiff’s relevance and overbreadth objections, and otherwise denies the motion. 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash Third Party Deposition Subpoena for Business Records is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.  Specifically, the Court grants the motion to quash as described above as to document categories 2, 8 (as to disciplinary records), and 9, and limits the temporal scope of production with respect to document categories 1, 4, 5, 7, 8 (as to performance reviews) and 11, based solely on Plaintiff’s relevance and overbreadth objections, and otherwise denies the motion.

CASE:                         Wilton Holdings & Management LLC v. 8210 Sunset, LLC et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 24STCV00859           

 

(1)   MOTION FOR LEAVE TO INTERVENE

(2)   ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE: PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1) Non-Party Herbalcure Corporation; (2) Plaintiff Wilton Holdings & Management LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1) Plaintiff Wilton Holdings & Management LLC; (2) Defendant 8210 Sunset, LLC

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a breach of contract and wrongful eviction action that was filed on January 12, 2024. Plaintiff alleges it subleased a commercial property from Defendants in order to operate a cannabis business. The property allegedly had serious structural and habitability defects which Defendants neither disclosed nor remedied. Plaintiffs further allege that Defendants subsequently locked Plaintiffs out of the property without formally terminating the lease agreement.

 

Herbalcure Corporation moves to intervene to assert claims against both Plaintiff and Defendants as the subsequent sublessor of the property. The Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application for a temporary restraining order on January 18, 2024, and now rules on an Order to Show Cause Re: Preliminary Injunction.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Herbalcure Corporation’s Motion for Leave to Intervene is GRANTED. Herbalcure is ordered to file a clean stand-alone copy of the Complaint-in-Intervention within 5 days of this order.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction is CONTINUED to March 21, 2023. Plaintiff is ordered to re-file its moving papers and serve them on Herbalcure Corporation no later than Tuesday, February 27, 2024. Herbalcure and Plaintiff may serve and file a supplemental opposition and reply brief per code. No further briefing will be permitted.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for Leave to Intervene

 

Herbalcure Corporation moves to intervene to assert claims against both Plaintiff and Defendants as a subsequent sublessor of the subject property.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Intervention as of right is permitted when “[t]he person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person's ability to protect that interest, unless that person's interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387 (d)(1)(B).) Whether the petitioner has an interest in the matter in litigation is a question of fact that must be determined by the court before leave to file is granted.  (Muller v. Robinson (1959) 174 Cal.App.2d 511, 515; In re Yokohama Special Bank (1948) 86 Cal.App.2d 545.) The burden rests on the one seeking to intervene to show that this is a proper case for intervention. (Id. at 515.)   

 

Analysis

 

            Herbalcure, as proposed intervenor, claims that it entered into a series of month-to-month sublease agreements for the subject property to operate a cannabis business at the premises commencing in October 2023. (Declaration of Peter Tejera ISO Mot. ¶ 3.) Herbalcure also claims that Plaintiff, through its owners and agents, threatened violence against Herbalcure’s personnel to force them off the subject premises. (Tejera Decl. ¶¶ 7-10.) Proposed Intervenor has produced the relevant sublease agreements, which describe Herbalcure as the subtenant and an individual named “David Kermani” as the sublessor. (Tejera Decl. Exhs. B, C.) Plaintiff argues in opposition that the moving papers do not establish what connection David Kermani has with the subject premises, besides a relation to Elan Kermani that might be inferred from the shared surname. Plaintiff thus claims that Herbalcure has not established standing to intervene because it has not shown a valid interest in the subject property via a lease agreement with an agent of the owner or lessor. The Court observes, however, that Defendants’ opposition to the Motion for Preliminary Injunction expressly concedes that David Kermani is a member of Defendant 8210 Sunset, LLC. (See Declaration of Elan Kermani ISO Opp. Prelim. Inj. ¶ 8.)

 

            Plaintiff also argues that Herbalcure has not shown that the month-to-month leases were renewed after January 31, 2024. This contention is not well-taken, since Herbalcure’s proposed Complaint-in-Intervention concerns conduct which allegedly occurred in that same month, including the wrongful eviction alleged against Defendants. Plaintiff’s assertion that Herbalcure’s claims are divorced from Plaintiff’s claims is belied by a plain reading of the proposed pleading, which alleges that Defendants subleased a property where the previous tenant’s right to possession was in dispute, and that Herbalcure has been improperly removed from the premises because of the Court’s order. (See Motion Exh. A. ¶¶ 74-86.)

 

            The Court is thus persuaded that Herbalcure has demonstrated an interest in the property that is the subject of the action and must be permitted to assert its claims in intervention to protect that interest.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Herbalcure Corporation’s Motion for Leave to Intervene is GRANTED.  Herbalcure is ordered to file a clean stand-alone copy of the Complaint-in-Intervention within 5 days of this order.

 

OSC Re: Preliminary Injunction

 

            On January 18, 2024, Plaintiff applied ex parte for a temporary restraining order enjoining Defendants from denying Plaintiff access to the subject property, interfering with Plaintiff’s quiet enjoyment and use of the premises, or changing locks or otherwise obstructing access to the premises. The Court granted the application pending a hearing on a preliminary injunction, and issued an order to show cause why Defendants should not be so enjoined pending resolution of this action. (January 19, 2024 Order to Show Cause.) The Court set a briefing schedule for initial moving papers, which were to be filed on January 25, 2024, with opposition and reply papers to be served and filed per code. (Id.)

 

            Although it appears from Defendants’ opposition papers that the motion was received by Defendants, Plaintiff’s moving papers were never filed with the Court. More critically, the factual nucleus of Herbalcure Corporation’s Complaint-in-Intervention arises from issuance of the Court’s Temporary Restraining Order. Thus, the interests of justice require that Herbalcure be presented with an opportunity to assert any arguments that are relevant to the question of a preliminary injunction.

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Preliminary Injunction is CONTINUED to March 21, 2023. Plaintiff is ordered to re-file its moving papers and serve them on Herbalcure Corporation no later than Tuesday, February 27, 2024.  Herbalcure and Plaintiff may serve and file a supplemental opposition and reply brief per code. No further briefing will be permitted.

By submitting on the tentative you have, in essence, waived your right to be present at the hearing, and you should be aware that the court may not adopt the tentative, and may issue an order which modifies the tentative ruling in whole or in part.
