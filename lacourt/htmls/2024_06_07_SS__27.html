DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV06479    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

Hearing Date: 6/7/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV06479 NAKISA NOWZAR vs VIA MARINA TAHITI HOA, et al. 

Moving Party: Defendant Personal Touch Cleaning 

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION IS DENIED

 

Background

On February 18, 2020, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Via Marina Tahiti HOA, Horizon Management Company, and Personal Touch Cleaning, alleging a slip and fall on a puddle from a leaky pipe in the parking area of a condo located at 310 Tahiti Way, Marina Del Rey. Via Marina Tahiti HOA had contracted Personal Touch Cleaning to provide cleaning services at this property. Plaintiff contends that Personal Touch Cleaning neglected to maintain the garage, including the water puddle that led to Plaintiff's fall. Defendant Personal Touch now moves for summary adjudication on the following issues.

(1) Plaintiff NAKISA NOWZAR cannot establish Defendant PERSONAL TOUCH CLEANING & MAINTENANCE, INC. owed her a duty.

(2) Plaintiff NAKISA NOWZAR cannot establish Defendant PERSONAL TOUCH CLEANING & MAINTENANCE, INC. breached any duty it may have owed.

(3) Plaintiff NAKISA NOWZAR cannot establish Defendant PERSONAL TOUCH CLEANING & MAINTENANCE, INC. had notice of the alleged condition that caused her to allegedly fall.

(4) Plaintiff NAKISA NOWZAR cannot establish causation as to Defendant PERSONAL TOUCH CLEANING & MAINTENANCE, INC.

(5) Plaintiff NAKISA NOWZAR cannot establish Defendant PERSONAL TOUCH CLEANING & MAINTENANCE, INC. caused or created the alleged condition that caused her to allegedly fall.

(Notice of Motion, p.2),

Preliminary Issues

 

Defendant’s Objections No. 8 and 10 are overruled; objection 11 is sustained. The Parties did not make any other objections to evidence that was material to Court’s ruling on the Motion for Summary Judgment. (CCP § 437c(q).)

 

Plaintiff argues that the present motion is not timely as it is heard less than 30 days before the date of trial. (CCP § 437c(a)(3).) This issue was already addressed on March 28, 2023, when the Court, upon finding good cause (CCP § 437c(a)(3)), continued the trial and specifically set the MSJ to be heard on June 7, 2024. The court will hear the motion on its merits.

Legal Standard

 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.)

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

 

Duty

Defendant argues that it owed no duty of care to Plaintiff because it is not the property owner but merely an independent contractor. "The duty question must be decided on a case-by-case basis, but each case must be governed by the general rule that everyone is required to use ordinary care to prevent causing injury to others." (Bloomberg v. Interinsurance Exchange (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 571, 575.) "A defendant who enters upon an affirmative course of conduct affecting the interests of another is regarded as assuming a duty to act, and will be liable for negligent acts or omissions." (Id.) As in Bloomberg, the allegations against Defendant center around a negligent performance of a contractual duty. The parties do not dispute the existence of contractual duties under the maintenance contract between Defendant Personal Touch and Marina Tahiti HOA. However, they disagree on whether this duty extends to the water puddle in question.

In meeting its initial burden to negate the element of duty, Defendant introduced its Janitorial Maintenance Specification Sheet, which, as part of the contract, specifies the services provided in the parking garage. According to the sheet, Personal Touch will "police the garage area and pick up debris," "dust overheads," "sweep the garage using a push broom," and "squeegee out low areas when it rains" (Def's Exhibit C [Personal Touch's Janitorial Maintenance Specification Sheet]). The term "police garage area and pick up debris" refers to Personal Touch’s responsibility to walk through the garage to pick up large pieces of trash and dirt piles (Def’s Exhibit J, Sarah Morales’ Deposition, 36:11 to 37:2; 62:13-25; 63:1-13]; Def’s Exhibit K, Rosie Huerta’s Deposition, 23:2-6). Defendant has shown that under the contract, it is not responsible for cleaning puddles of water created by a leaky pipe, thus meeting its initial burden.

In raising triable issue of fact, Plaintiff introduced portions of the deposition of Defendant's PMK, Sarah Morales, stating that if there is water on the garage floor posing a slip and fall threat, employees were supposed to take pictures of any water on the ground and report that to the property manager, mop the water, and place cones or wet floor signs as temporary solutions (Plf’s Ex. 5, Morales Depo. 37:16-38:4). Plaintiff also introduces testimony from Rosie Huerta, Director of Operations for Defendant, regarding Defendant’s written obligation under the contract to squeegee water when it rains. Huerta testified that if an employee of Defendant is present and observes water from rain or leaky pipes, they will squeegee the water (Plf’s Ex. 4, Huerta Depo. 37:9-17). Huerta confirmed that the reason for squeegeeing water is to prevent someone from slipping and falling (Huerta Decl. 38:5-7). Huerta also agreed that the principle applies equally to water from leaky pipes as it does to rain water. (Huerta Decl. 38:11-39:12). Plaintiff presented evidence showing that Defendant’s employees were required to mop or squeegee water from the garage if they observed it, and place cones around the puddle to warn residents. Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact regarding the scope of Defendant's duty under the maintenance contract, specifically whether it includes cleanup of the water puddle in question. Thus, summary adjudication is DENIED as to the issue of duty.

Notice

It is undisputed that the incident occurred on April 4, 2018, at 4:30 pm. However, Defendant did not introduce any evidence indicating how long the water puddle at issue had been present or how long the water pipe had been leaking. The only information available relevant to this issue is that Plaintiff filed a prior complaint to the HOA about the leaky water pipe. (Def’s Exhibit H, Nakisa Nowsar’s Deposition, 34:19-22.)

In meeting its burden in negating the notice requirement, Defendant presented testimony from Sarah Morales, Defendant's PMK, who stated that she received no reports from her employees about water puddles or leaky pipes. However, Morales later testified that although “she personally, are not aware of any water leaks, pipe issues, water in the garage before this incident but maybe the employees that were actually on the premise working, they may have some knowledge.” (Def’s Exhibit J, Sarah Morales’ Deposition, 16:1-7; 54:19-55:4.)

Defendant failed to provide any declarations or evidence from its onsite employees, Ms. Banuelos and Ms. Gill (Exhibit K, Rosie Huerta Deposition, 43:19-25; Exhibit L), confirming that they visited the garage and did not see any water puddles. Additionally, there is no evidence, such as checklists, sweep sheets, or declarations from the onsite employees showing that they actually performed their duties to police or sweep the garage as required on the date of the incident and during the period leading up to it. The absence of such declarations, documents, or evidence could lead to the inference that Morales did not receive any reports of the puddle either because the onsite employees were aware of the puddle but failed to report it, or they did not visit the garage and failed to observe the puddle. In ruling on a motion for summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence and all reasonable inferences drawn therefrom, and must view such evidence and inferences in the light most favorable to the opposing party (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843). Due to the lack of evidence to counter these inferences, Defendant failed to meet its initial burden. Thus, Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to the issue of notice.

Causation

Defendant’s argument regarding causation hinges on the scope of its duty under the maintenance agreement.  It asserts that it did not breach this duty nor cause the injury, as there was no duty to clean the water puddle.

Defendant provided no evidence detailing the actions of its employees on the date of the accident or in the days leading up to it. There are no declarations from the onsite employees regarding whether they observed any water puddles, whether they were actually at the property, whether they policed the garage, cleaned the puddle, or placed cones during the relevant time period. The only evidence Defendant provided concerns its employees' weekly schedule since 5/12/17, which outlines tasks and routes to police the garage every 1-2 days and clean the garage weekly. However, no evidence confirms that these tasks and schedules were actually carried out by the onsite employees.

Defendant specifically argues that "Plaintiff cannot establish that Personal Touch’s conduct caused the alleged incident. Personal Touch’s duty under the contract was to police the garage for debris and pick it up. (SSUMF No. 15) Plaintiff does not claim that she slipped, tripped, or fell on debris. Therefore, the manner in which Personal Touch performed its contractual duty did not cause the alleged incident." (Motion at p. 12.) However, the court has determined that Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact concerning the scope of duty. It is conceivable that reasonable minds could find that Defendant was required to clean the water puddle caused by the leak, and by the same token, Defendant’s failure to clean the puddle was a substantial factor leading to Plaintiff’s fall.

Defendant's argument against breach centers on two points: it had no duty to repair the leak and it was not notified of the hazard. (Motion at pg. 9-10.) The Court is not persuaded by these arguments because, as discussed in the notice section, Defendant failed to meet its initial burden of proving it had no notice. Furthermore, the focus of Defendant’s duty has never been on its duty to repair the leaky pipes. The central dispute in this action is whether Defendant was required to clean the water puddle in question. Defendant did not present any evidence that Ms. Gills and Ms. Banuelos, its onsite employees, actually swept the garage, policed the area, or were present at the premises on the date of the incident or during the time period leading up to it.

In essence, Defendant has failed to produce any evidence that its onsite employees were actually performing their duties under the maintenance contract. Defendant’s main argument against breach is that it had no duty to clean the puddle, and therefore, no duty was breached. However, the Court finds that the issue of duty is appropriately left for the jury to decide.

Thus, Defendant failed to meet its initial burden and Summary Adjudication is DENIED as to the issue of causation

Defendant Did Not Cause or Create The Condition That Caused Plaintiff To Allegedly Fall

Defendant seeks to have the Court adjudicate the issue of whether it “caused or created” the condition that caused Plaintiff to allegedly fall.  The Court declines to summarily adjudicate this issue because it is presented in a confusing and ambiguous way that does not, as intended for summary adjudication matters, clarify and/or narrow the issues for trial.  Rather, by joining the concepts of cause and creation, Defendant appears to blur the element of causation.  Undoubtedly, Defendant did not create the leak and water puddle at issue here, but Plaintiff is not contending that Defendant did.  Accordingly, the Court finds the issue for which Defendant seeks summary adjudication to be improper for such adjudication. Thus, Summary Adjudication as to this issue is DENIED.

MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES; MOTION TO DEEM THE CONTENTS OF REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS ADMITTED; REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 6/7/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV08781 FRED WAI HUNG YUE vs PC PALACES INC. 

Moving Party: Defendant ALWAYS PRIVATE SECURITY SERVICES, INC

Responding Party: Plaintiff TZU-FEI CHU

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES; MOTION TO DEEM THE CONTENTS OF REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS ADMITTED; REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED.

 

Legal Standard

 

1.  Requests for Production and Interrogatories

 

A defendant may make a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling without leave of court at any time. Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (a).) A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)

 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

 

2.  Request for Admissions

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ CCP § 2033.010.¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”¿ CCP § 2033.250(a).

 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c). Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to deem RFA admitted does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Discussion

On March 7, 2024, Defendant ALWAYS PRIVATE SECURITY SERVICES, INC. served its first set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents, and Requests for Admissions on Plaintiff TZU-FEI CHU, who is appearing pro per. The deadline for Plaintiff's responses was April 8, 2024. After receiving no discovery responses by April 17, 2024, Defendant attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff, setting a new response deadline of April 26, 2024. As of April 29, 2024, the date on which the current motions were filed, Plaintiff has not provided any written responses to Defendant's discovery requests nor requested an extension. Defendant now moves the court to compel initial responses and deem the contents of its RFA, set one, admitted.

It is undisputed that Plaintiff failed to serve the discovery responses at issue prior to the statutory deadline. No oppositions were filed, nor were there any filings indicating that the responses were served prior to the hearing. Consequently, the present motions are GRANTED. The contents of Defendant’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, are deemed admitted. Plaintiff is ORDERED to provide complete and verified discovery responses without objections within 20 days of today.

The court grants the present motions and sanctions are mandatory. The court does not find that Plaintiff acted with substantial justification. Defendant requested sanctions in the amount of $320.00 per motion. The Court finds this amount reasonable. Accordingly, the Court ORDERS Plaintiff to pay sanctions totaling $1280 to Defendant within 20 days of today’s date.

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT

Hearing Date: 6/7/24

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV40804 KAREN ALFARO vs CALIFORNIA DEPARTMENT OF GENERAL SERVICES

Moving Party: Defendant SEAN LAWSON

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM DEFAULT IS DENIED

 

Background

On October 23, 2020, Plaintiff filed the present complaint against Defendant CALIFORNIA DEPARTMENT OF GENERAL SERVICES. On February 24, 2022, Defendant Sean Lawson was added as a defendant in the present action. On September 4, 2022, January 3, 2023, and January 25, 2023, Plaintiff allegedly served Defendant Sean Lawson through personal service. Defendant did not file an answer, and on March 21, 2023, default was entered against Defendant. Defendant now moves the Court for relief from default on the basis that the person allegedly personally served was not him and he has no actual knowledge of the suit.

CCP §473.5

CCP §473.5(a) permits the court to vacate a default judgment “[w]hen service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him.” The phrase “actual notice” means “genuine knowledge of the party litigant.” (Tunis v. Barrow (1986) 184 Cal. App. 3d 1069, 1077.)

The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. Under section 473.5(b), a “notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action . . . shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party’s lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect.”

Under (c), “upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.”

Timeliness

The present motion is timely. Under CCP 473.5, the notice of motion must be served and filed within a reasonable time, but not later than the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. No default judgment has been entered against Plaintiff, nor has Plaintiff been served with written notice that a default has been entered. Plaintiff’s only evidence in support of her argument that the present motion is untimely is Exhibit D. However, Exhibit D indicates that a copy of the Request for Entry of Default was mailed on March 20, 2023, a day before default was entered and it does not show Defendant served Plaintiff with written notice that a default has been entered.

Actual Notice

The Court finds that Defendant had actual notice of the present action. Defendant admits in his declaration that the California Department of General Services informed him in September 2022 that they would no longer represent him in this lawsuit. (Defendant's Declaration to Motion ¶ 6.) Defendant also presented proofs of service for each of the three personal service attempts and a declaration from the process server for each attempt. First, these service attempts all occurred at 11250 Blucher Avenue, Granada Hills, California. Defendant declares he resides at that address with his son. (Defendant's Declaration to Reply ¶ 2.)

Second, the description provided by the process server of the individual served aligns with Defendant's characteristics. The gender, ethnicity, age, and height are all within a reasonable range. There was an inconsistency in the proof of service dated January 25, 2023, where the individual was mistakenly listed as Caucasian; however, the corresponding affidavit correctly identifies the individual as African American, indicating a likely typographical error. The only discrepancy lies in the weight: the process server recorded the individual as weighing between 170-190 lbs, while Defendant states he currently weighs 240 lbs.

Third, Defendant resides with his 26-year-old son at 11250 Blucher Avenue, Granada Hills, California, a single-family home. This setting reduces the possibility that the individual served was someone other than Defendant, particularly given the age of his son.

Fourth, and significantly, the process server possessed a photograph of Defendant when effecting personal service and confirmed that the individual who received service matches the person depicted in the photograph.

Based on the foregoing reasons, the Court finds that Defendant had actual notice of the action and thus no basis exists to set aside the default.

Electronic Service

Defendant argues in the reply that there was no explicit agreement for service by electronic means between Plaintiff and Defendant, who is appearing in pro per, and therefore the Court should disregard Plaintiff’s opposition. The Court is not persuaded by this argument. First, it is noted that Defendant's moving papers were also served electronically on Plaintiff. Consequently, if the Court were to disregard Plaintiff’s opposition on these grounds, it would similarly need to disregard Defendant’s moving papers.

Second, “It is axiomatic that strict compliance with the code's provisions for service of process is not required.” (Ramos v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1434, 1442.) Service should be liberally construed to effectuate service and uphold the jurisdiction of the Court if actual notice has been received by the defendant. The question of service should ultimately be resolved by considering each situation from a practical standpoint. The liberal construction rule, it is anticipated, will eliminate unnecessary, time-consuming, and costly disputes over legal technicalities, without prejudicing the right of defendants to proper notice. (Id.) Defendant's actual notice of the opposition is undisputed as Defendant filed a reply contesting the merits of Plaintiff’s arguments in the opposition.

MOTION FOR SUBSTITUTION

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV16094 WILLIAM DAVID MARTINEZ vs LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOL

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUBSTITUTION IS GRANTED 

 

Legal Standard

 

“A pending action or proceeding does not abate by the death of a party if the cause of action survives.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.21.) “On motion after the death of a person who commenced an action or proceeding, the court shall allow a pending action or proceeding that does not abate to be continued by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.31.)

 

The person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating:

(1) The decedent’s name,

(2) The date and place of the decedent’s death,

(3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate,”

(4) If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor in interest,

(5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof,

A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”

(B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”

(6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding,” and (7) the statements are true, under penalty of perjury. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32.)

A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (c).)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff WILLIAM DAVID MARTINEZ passed away on November 13, 2023, and now Betty Darlen Martinez, Plaintiff’s mother and successor in interest, moves the court to substitute herself in place of her son in the present case.

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Unopposed 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR SUBSTITUTION IS GRANTED 

 

Legal Standard

 

“A pending action or proceeding does not abate by the death of a party if the cause of action survives.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.21.) “On motion after the death of a person who commenced an action or proceeding, the court shall allow a pending action or proceeding that does not abate to be continued by the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, by the decedent’s successor in interest.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.31.)

 

The person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating:

(1) The decedent’s name,

(2) The date and place of the decedent’s death,

(3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate,”

(4) If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor in interest,

(5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof,

A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”

(B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”

(6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding,” and (7) the statements are true, under penalty of perjury. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32.)

A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (c).)

 

Discussion

The court examined the filing and found that the successor in interest has filed a declaration fulfilling all the requirements under CCP § 377.32. A certified copy of decedent’s death certificate is also attached. Thus, the present motion is GRANTED.
