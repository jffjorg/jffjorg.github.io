DEPARTMENT S25 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23LBCV01221    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: S25

Motion: Defendant Miguel Gonzalez’s Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint

Moving Party: Defendant Miguel Gonzalez

Responding Party: Defendants Nicholas Tran and Cuong Tran

TENATIVE RULING: Defendant Miguel Gonzalez’s Motion for Leave to File a Cross-Complaint against Defendants Nicholas Tran and Cuong Tran is GRANTED.

Procedural Background

This action arises out of a motor vehicle accident. The Complaint, filed on June 30, 2023, alleges that Plaintiff Samuel Sanchez (Plaintiff) was injured by the negligent operation of a motor vehicle by Defendants Nicholas Tran, Cuong Tran, Miguel Gonzalez. (Complaint at p. 4.) Defendants Nicholas Tran and Cuong Tran filed a cross-complaint for indemnity and apportionment of fault against Defendant Gonzalez (“Gonzalez”) on August 10, 2023. Gonzalez filed an answer to the Complaint on September 12, 2023, and an answer to the cross-complaint on March 7, 2024.

Gonzalez filed this motion for leave to file a cross-complaint on February 13, 2024. Nicholas and Cuong Tran filed an opposition on April 12, 2024. Gonzalez filed a reply on April 17, 2024.

Analysis

“A party shall file a cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint. Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50(a)–(b).) A cross-complaint filed outside either of the aforementioned times requires leave of court, which may be granted “in the interest of justice at any time during the court of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50(c).)

A party who fails to plead a cause of action subject to the requirements of this article, whether through oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause, may apply to the court for leave to amend his pleading, or to file a cross-complaint, to assert such cause at any time during the course of the action. The court, after notice to the adverse party, shall grant, upon such terms as may be just to the parties, leave to amend the pleading, or to file the cross-complaint, to assert such cause if the party

who failed to plead the cause acted in good faith. This subdivision shall be liberally construed to avoid forfeiture of causes of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50.) Leave to file a cross-complaint may also be granted “in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 428.50, subd. (c).)

Failure to bring claims in a cross-complaint may have consequences. “[I]f a party against whom a complaint has been filed and served fails to allege in a cross-complaint any related cause of action which (at the time of serving his answer to the complaint) he has against the plaintiff, such party may not thereafter in any other action assert against the plaintiff the related cause of action not pleaded.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.30, subd. (a).) “A motion to file a cross-complaint at any time during the course of the action must be granted unless bad faith of the moving party is demonstrated where forfeiture would otherwise result.” (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 99.)

Defendant Gonzalez seeks leave to file a cross-complaint for indemnity, apportionment of fault, and declaratory relief, against Defendants Nicholas Tran and Cuong Tran. The proposed cross-complaint arises out of the same August 31, 2021, motor vehicle accident which forms the basis of Plaintiff’s complaint. As Defendant Gonzalez’s claims arise from the same incident alleged Plaintiff’s complaint, the court must allow for the filing of his cross-complaint absent a showing of bad faith.

Defendant Gonzalez’s counsel states that she failed to file the cross-complaint at the time the answer by “mistake,” and subsequently delayed seeking leave in the belief that the other parties would stipulate to allow such a claim. (Rousier Decl. ¶¶ 2, 4–7.) Further, Defendants Cuong and Nicholas Tran fail to make any showing of bad faith on the part of Gonzalez or his counsel, arguing only that opposing counsel does not adequately explain the tardiness of the proposed cross-complaint. (Opposition at p. 2.) This argument is unpersuasive. Further, as the other parties would not stipulate to the filing of Defendant Gonzalez’s cross-complaint, counsel was required to bring this motion.

As the Court finds there is no evidence of bad faith by the moving party for a short delay in filing of the cross-complaint, Defendant Gonzalez’s leave to file his cross-complaint is GRANTED.

Case Number: 23LBCV01778    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: S25

MOTION: DEFENDANT CHRISTOPHER MICHAEL MOEHL’S MOTION TO COMPEL VERIFIED RESPONSES AND FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

Moving Party: Defendant Christopher Michael Moehl

Opposing Party: Plaintiff Rossylyn Inez Sanchez

Tentative Ruling: Plaintiff is ordered to serve amended responses, without objection, to Form Interrogatories 2.5, 2.6, 6.5 and 17.1, within 10 days of this ruling. Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) Sanctions in the amount of $2,985 are awarded to Defendants. Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.290(c).

Procedural Background

Plaintiff Rossylyn Inez Sanchez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Melton Truck Lines, Inc., Christopher Michael Moehl, and Does 1 to 50, (“Defendants”), for motor vehicle negligence and general negligence for an automobile accident that occurred on October 7, 2021. Plaintiff filed the complaint on September 21, 2023. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants negligently owned, entrusted, and operated a motor vehicle, resulting in injury to the plaintiff. Furthermore, Plaintiff claims that Defendants negligently hired, trained, and supervised their agents or employees, which contributed to the injuries sustained by Plaintiff.

On November 20, 2023, Defendant Moehl service Form Interrogatories (Set One) on Plaintiff. The responses were due on or before December 22, 2023, however, Defendant Moehl granted Plaintiff an extension to January 3, 2024. When no response was received, defense counsel emailed a meet and confer letter requesting a conference within seven days and verified responses without objections on or before January 23, 2024. No response was received by defense counsel.

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiff served unverified responses with multiple objections to the request. Subsequently, on February 13, 2024, defense counsel sent opposing counsel

another meet and confer letter concerning the “deficient” discovery responses. Plaintiff’s counsel requested to respond by March 5, 2024, to reply. Defense counsel allegedly did not respond to the request until after the February 22, 2024, case management conference. On that day, presumably as a trial date of September 16, 2024, was then set, Defense counsel requested further responses be served by February 27, 2024. As Plaintiff’s counsel assumed her prior proposed deadline of March 5, 2024, was previously acceptable to defense, she requested an extension to March 5. However, in light of the Sept 16 trial date, defense counsel requested further responses by February 27 or indicated motions to compel would be filed.

On February 28, 2024, Defendants filed this instance motion. Plaintiff served additional responses on March 8, 2024. On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition this motion. On April 18, 2024, Defendant filed a reply. Notably, Defendants also filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One) but withdrew the motion once further responses were received.

Analysis

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020, subd. (a), 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

FORM INTERROGATORIES 2.5, 2.6 AND 6.5. Defendant requests further responses, without objection, to Form Interrogatory Nos. 2.5, 2.6, 6.5, and 17.1. Defendant does not dispute that request for further responses to FROGS nos. 2.5, 2.6, and 6.5 are moot given Plaintiff’s supplemental responses. The Court, however, agrees that Plaintiff waived objections by failing to timely respond by the January 3, 2024, deadline, which the Court notes was an extension of time to respond. Thus, Plaintiff is ordered to served amended responses, without objection, within 10 days of this Order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

FORM INTERROGATORY 17.1. The Court finds that Plaintiff fails to justify her objections and show the adequacy of the responses. The information sought is relevant to this case and that the provided responses are insufficient because they lack specific details required to

substantiate the claims made. Specifically, Plaintiff has not provided the necessary contact information for herself as a self-employed individual, nor has she specified the medical providers involved by name and address, which are critical for verifying the claims related to medical treatments and their impact on her ability to work. Furthermore, the references to medical records appear to be vague and lack the specificity needed to assess the claims of lost wages and future earning capacity effectively. For cases involving compensation for injuries, the type of information is relevant and important to the evaluation of Plaintiff’s claims. As the Plaintiff failed to timely respond by January 3, the Court finds Plaintiff waived objections pursuant to Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a). Thus, Plaintiff is ordered to amend her response, without objection, within 10 days of this Order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.290(c) provides: “The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

As previously set forth by this Court, defense counsel made multiple attempts to informally resolve these discovery disputes. Although Plaintiff eventually provided additional responses as requested by Defendants in March 2024, several responses remained inadequate, and the Court was required to intervene.

As the prevailing party, Defendant is entitled to sanctions. Defendant counsel’s declaration sets forth the basis of an award of $2,985.00, which the court finds reasonable considering the level of effort made by defense counsel to obtain the required discovery.

Case Number: 24LBUD00684    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: S25

Motion: (1) Demurrer to Complaint for Unlawful Detainer

              (2) Motion to Strike

Moving Party: Defendant Martin Rocha

Responding Party: Plaintiff Teresa Rodriguez

TENTATIVE RULING: Defendant’s Demurrer is OVERRULED and Defendant's Motion to Strike is DENIED.

Procedural Background:

This is an unlawful detainer action. On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff Rodriguez filed a complaint against Defendant Rocha for unlawful detainer. Plaintiff Rodriguez alleges as follows. Starting in January 1997, Rocha rented a residential property in Wilmington as a month-to-month tenancy. On March 14, 2024, Plaintiff Rodriguez served a three-day notice to quit on Defendant Rocha. Plaintiff Rodriguez alleges Defendant Rocha subsequently failed to comply with the notice. On April 3, 2024, Defendant Rocha filed the present demurrer and motion to strike.

Analysis:

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, at p. 747.)

Defendant Rocha contends the Complaint is insufficiently pled for the following reasons: (1) the complaint fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action; (2) the court lacks subject matter jurisdiction pursuant to Civil Code section 430.10(a), (3) the complaint the vague, uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible.

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s complaint adequately alleges a cause of action for unlawful detainer. The complaint is on the approved Judicial Council form and identities the pertinent information to support an unlawful detainer claim. The required supporting documentation in support of Plaintiff’s claim is also attached.

Defendant Rocha suggestion the Court lack subject matter jurisdiction is without support. The property at issue is located within the City of Los Angeles as the City of Wilmington, as indicated on the complaint, is a neighborhood within the County of Los Angeles and City of Los Angeles. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (g). “[f]acts and propositions that are of such common knowledge within the territorial jurisdiction of the court that they cannot reasonably be the subject of dispute.”)

Further, whether Plaintiff Rodriguez failed to serve proper notices of AB1482 exemption is a question of fact to be litigated at a later stage of the case and is not proper grounds for a demurrer or motion to strike.

The Complaint alleges this action is solely for the nonpayment of rent. (Complaint, ¶ 6(f)(2).) However, in the attached notice to quit, Plaintiff states the lease is forfeited due to Defendant Rocha’s allegedly inappropriate conduct and behavior. (Complaint, Ex. 2.) In opposition, Plaintiff admits this cause of action “is not based on non-payment of rent” and concedes the incorrect box was checked on the Complaint. (Opp., p. 2:26-3:5.) Plaintiff argues the supporting exhibits to the complaint, e.g. the notice to quit, provides Defendant Rocha with enough information as to overcome Defendant Rocha’s contention that the complaint is vague.

The Court takes all properly pled facts as true and draws all reasonable inferences in favor of the plaintiff. (Coleman v. Medtronic, Inc. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 413, 422, 167 Cal.Rptr.3d 300; Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Boyle (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1020, 1034, 100 Cal.Rptr.3d 875.) Inconsistent allegations in amended complaints are disregarded unless the inconsistency is explained. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Internat., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, 104 Cal.Rptr.2d 239.) Exhibits attached to the complaint take precedence to the extent they contradict allegations in the complaint. (Ibid.) Bank of New York Mellon v. Citibank, N.A., 8 Cal. App. 5th 935, 943, 214 Cal. Rptr. 3d 504, 510 (2017), as modified (Mar. 1, 2017). This is instance, the inconsistency has been explained as a mistake with the notice to quit providing the Defendant Rocha with sufficient information to respond to Plaintiff’s claim.

Defendant’s Demurrer is OVERRULED and the Motion to Strike is DENIED.

Defendant Rocha to answer within 10 days. Plaintiff to give notice.
