DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01361    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

I.         INTRODUCTION

This action arises from an incident that occurred on April 19, 2023, at an LA Fitness facility in Arcadia, California. Plaintiff, Hee Sung Kwon (“Plaintiff”), alleges he sustained injuries due to a malfunctioning treadmill. On June 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed claims against Defendants, Fitness International, LLC (erroneously sued as “LA Fitness”) and Francesca Levy (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging 1) Negligence and 2) Premise Liability.

 

On November 21, 2023, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend the complaint. Subsequently, on December 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a renewed motion for leave to amend. On January 3, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) provides: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.¿ The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)¿¿¿ ¿ 

Permissible amendments pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) include amendments which add causes of action.¿ A plaintiff that fails to plead a cause of action arising out of the transaction or occurrence that gave rise to the complaint may apply to the court for leave to amend the complaint to assert the cause of action, whether that failure was the result of oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50; see Code Civ. Proc., § 426.10, subd. (c).)¿ The motion to amend may be filed at any time during the course of the action, on notice to the adverse party.¿ (Id., § 426.50.)¿ ¿ 

The court has broad discretion to permit amendments to pleadings, and “the court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.”¿ (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.)¿ “The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend . . ..”¿¿ (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.)¿ Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.”¿ (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿ ¿ 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1).)¿ A motion to amend a pleading must also be supported by a declaration which specifies the following: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)¿

III.      ANALYSIS

Plaintiff has cured the deficiencies identified in the December 19, 2024, Minute Order denying the prior motion to amend. Specifically, the declaration by Plaintiff’s counsel now satisfies the requirements of rule 3.1324(b) by addressing (1) the effect of the amendment, (2) why it is necessary and proper, (3) when the relevant facts were discovered, and (4) why the amendment was not sought earlier. (Roesler Decl., ¶¶ 13-18; Minute Order, pp. 2-3)

Plaintiff’s proposed amendments allege that Defendants failed to properly inspect and maintain the treadmill’s PC boards, demonstrating a “want of even scant care” and “an extreme departure from industry standards.” (Proposed FAC, ¶¶ 17, 24) Plaintiff argues these allegations are critical to their opposition to Defendants' pending motion for summary judgment, which relies on an enforceable waiver defense. Without amendment, Plaintiff contends that their ability to litigate the gross negligence issue is substantially impaired. (Roesler Decl., ¶¶ 15-16)

Defendants, in opposition, argue that the proposed amendments merely refine existing claims related to the maintenance and operation of the treadmill. (Opposition, pp. 2-3). However, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated the proposed allegations add necessary and substantive details to support a claim for gross negligence. Specifically, discovery revealed that Defendants failed to conduct proper inspections of the PC boards, described as the “brains” of the treadmill, and lacked any written policies or manuals regarding repair or maintenance standards. (Roesler Decl., ¶¶ 6-12)

Plaintiff cites newly discovered evidence obtained during depositions, including testimony from Defendant Levy, who admitted she was unaware of any written policies or manuals for servicing the equipment. (Roesler Decl., ¶ 8; Exh. A¿) Additionally, Castro, a Senior Lead Technician, testified that no preventative maintenance was conducted on the treadmill’s PC board and that no manuals or recommended inspection schedules existed for the equipment. (Roesler Decl., ¶ 8; Exh. B¿)

The Court finds that the evidence provided supports Plaintiff’s assertion that Defendants' actions constituted an “extreme departure” from both manufacturer guidelines and industry standards. (Proposed FAC, ¶ 17)

Courts are more inclined to grant motions for leave to amend when the amendment is based on newly discovered facts not previously known to the plaintiff. (Jersey v. John Muir Medical Center (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 814, 820.) Here, Plaintiff discovered during depositions conducted in November 2024 that Defendants did not conduct preventative maintenance on the treadmill’s PC boards, lacked training protocols, and failed to adhere to manufacturer guidelines. (Roesler Decl., ¶¶ 6-12¿) These newly discovered facts justify allowing the amendment and continuing the summary judgment hearing to incorporate the new allegations into the record. (Ibid.) California law also favors a liberal policy of allowing amendments at any stage of litigation, including during trial, provided there is no prejudice to the opposing party. (Eng v. Brown (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 675, 683.) Allowing the amendment ensures that Plaintiff’s claims can be fully litigated on their merits, aligning with this policy.

Defendants contend they will suffer prejudice because their motion for summary judgment is scheduled for January 16, 2025. However, trial is not set until April 8, 2025, providing ample time for additional discovery or adjustments. Moreover, the Court retains discretion to continue the summary judgment hearing to accommodate the new allegations. (See Jersey, supra, 97 Cal.App.4th at p. 820.) The liberal policy favoring amendments outweighs the minimal prejudice to Defendants, particularly when the amendment seeks to address substantive issues relevant to the litigation. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend to file the FAC.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a First Amended Complaint.  Plaintiff is to file his FAC within five days of this order.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

CCP § 430.10(c) provides that a party against whom a complaint is filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the ground that “[t]here is another action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action.”   

This plea in abatement may be made by demurrer or answer when there is another action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action.  (Plant Insulation Co. v. Fibreboard Corp. (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 781, 789.) The later-filed suit is “abated” (stayed) pending resolution of the earlier action which, if concluded on the merits, will be res judicata; if not, the later action can proceed.  (Id. at 788-89.)  The court can take judicial notice of the court files in determining the basis for this ground. (See Bistawros v. Greenberg (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 189, 191-192.)

“In order to sustain the plea of another action pending it is essential that it shall appear: (1) That both suits are predicated upon the same cause of action; (2) that both suits are pending in the same jurisdiction; and (3) that both suits are contested by the same parties. [Citation.] . . . The only relief to which a litigant is entitled upon the plea, whether by demurrer or answer, is that the second action abate.”  (Colvig v. RKO General, Inc. (1965) 232 Cal.App.2d 56, 70-71.)   

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s entire complaint, arguing that the causes of action are duplicative of pending probate proceedings and fail to state facts sufficient to constitute valid claims.

Pending Actions

Defendant argues that the complaint in this action is improper because there are two other actions currently pending between the same parties over the same issues in probate court, making this complaint a duplicative and impermissible attempt to split causes of action. (Demurrer, p.3) Defendant identifies the two pending matters as the trust matter and the estate matter.

As to the trust matter, Defendant asserts that on October 11, 2022, Plaintiff filed a petition to determine the existence of a trust under Probate Code section 17200, seeking an order finding that the original Carl John Lotito Living Trust dated April 14, 2021, is valid. Defendant further notes that on November 21, 2022, Plaintiff filed a petition to determine the validity of a trust, alleging fraud, undue influence, lack of capacity, financial elder abuse, and seeking damages. In this petition, Plaintiff sought an order declaring the original trust valid and the subsequent restated trust invalid. Additionally, Defendant highlights that on January 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed a petition seeking an order determining certain real property as assets of the trust, specifically properties located at 1721 Marengo Street, South Pasadena, and Barstow, California, as intended by the decedent’s pour-over will. 

As to the estate matter, Defendant states that Plaintiff filed a petition to probate the will, while Defendant filed a petition to probate a lost will, contesting Plaintiff’s petition. Defendant argues that these probate matters involve the same parties, facts, and relief sought, as both involve Plaintiff and Defendant disputing their claims to the decedent’s estate and addressing allegations of undue influence and financial elder abuse. (Demurrer, pp. 4-5)

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that the current civil action is not duplicative of the probate matters because the probate court addresses trust administration and the validity of testamentary documents, whereas the civil action seeks tort remedies such as damages for undue influence, financial elder abuse, and intentional interference with an expected inheritance. Plaintiff argues that the relief sought in the civil action is distinct from the equitable relief available in probate court and denies that the claims in this action constitute impermissible claim splitting. (Opposition, pp. 3-5) 

The Court finds that the claims asserted in the present civil action are barred under the principles of abatement set forth in California Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (c). The court must determine whether the civil and probate actions involve the same "cause of action" by analyzing whether they implicate the same primary right of the plaintiff and a corresponding primary duty of the defendant. (Pitts v. City of Sacramento (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 853, 856; Eichman v. Fotomat Corp. (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 1170, 1174-1175.)

A single cause of action arises from the plaintiff’s injury and the facts constituting the defendant's wrong. If two actions involve the same injury to the plaintiff and the same wrong by the defendant, then the same primary right is at stake, even if the plaintiff pleads different theories of recovery, seeks different forms of relief, or adds new facts in the second action. (Eichman v. Fotomat Corp. (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 1170, 1174-1175.) In this case, the Plaintiff alleges harm stemming from the deprivation of inheritance rights and the defendant’s alleged undue influence and elder abuse. These claims are already being litigated in the pending probate actions, where the same facts and injuries are at issue. (See Exhs. 2, 3, 4)

Further, the rule against splitting a cause of action bars the division of a single cause of action into separate suits. A suit on any part of a cause of action precludes a later suit on the remaining claims that could have been asserted. This principle protects defendants from vexatious litigation and preserves judicial efficiency. (Wulfjen v. Ddolton (1944) 24 Cal.2d 891, 894-95; Lekse v. Municipal Court (1982) 138 Cal.App.3d 188, 193.) Although different evidentiary foci or reciprocal duties can sometimes distinguish causes of action. In this instance, the Court finds that these claims arise from the same operative facts as the probate actions, which are already addressing the validity of testamentary documents and the distribution of the decedent’s estate. Therefore, the civil and probate actions involve the same primary right and corresponding primary duty, with no distinct evidentiary focus or legal duties breached to justify maintaining this separate action. (Pitts v. City of Sacramento (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 853, 856.)

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer pending resolution of the probate matter, as a plea in abatement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (c).

Sanctions

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions against Defendant’s attorney in the amounts of $2,450 and $4,314 for reimbursement of attorney’s fees incurred in preparing the opposition to the motion to strike. (Shin Decl., ¶¶ 3-5)

The Court notes Defendant’s reference to attorney’s fees; however, as Defendant did not specifically move for attorney’s fees, the Court need not rule on this issue.

Given that the demurrer is sustained, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.

Motion to strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a)) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b)) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437)

Given that the Court has sustained the demurrer to the Complaint, the Court DENIES the motion to strike as MOOT.
