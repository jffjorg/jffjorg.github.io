DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21SMCV00716    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           SMC Specialty Finance LLC v. Sanping, et al.

CASE NO.:                21SMCV00716

MOTION:                  Motion for Summary Adjudication

HEARING DATE:   10/10/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

A party may move for summary judgment in any action or proceeding if it is contended the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding. (CCP, § 437c(a).) “The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties' pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  

 

“A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in¿Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs.”¿(CCP,¿§ 437c(f)(1).)¿If a party seeks summary adjudication as an alternative to a request for summary judgment, the request must be clearly made in the notice of the motion. (Gonzales v. Superior Court¿(1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1542, 1544.)¿ “[A] party may move for summary adjudication of a legal issue or a claim for damages other than punitive damages that does not completely dispose of a cause of action, affirmative defense, or issue of duty pursuant to” subdivision (t). (CCP,¿§ 437c(t).)¿ 

 

To prevail, the evidence submitted must show there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.¿(CCP, §¿437c(c).)¿The motion cannot succeed unless the evidence leaves no room for conflicting inferences as to material facts; the court has no power to weigh one inference against another or against other evidence. (Murillo v. Rite Stuff Food Inc. (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 833, 841.) In determining whether the facts give rise to a triable issue of material fact, “[a]ll doubts as to whether any material, triable, issues of fact exist are to be resolved in favor of the party opposing summary judgment…” (Gold v. Weissman (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1195, 1198-99.) “In other words, the facts alleged in the evidence of the party opposing summary judgment and the reasonable inferences there from must be accepted as true.” (Jackson v. County of Los Angeles (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 171, 179.) However, if adjudication is otherwise proper the motion “may not be denied on grounds of credibility,” except when¿a material fact is the witness’s¿state of mind and “that fact is sought to be established solely by the [witness’s] affirmation thereof.” (CCP, § 437c(e).)¿ 

 

Once the moving party has met their burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(1).) “[T]here¿is no obligation on the opposing party... to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element... necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”¿(Consumer Cause, Inc. v.¿SmileCare¿(2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿ 

¿ 

“The pleadings play a key role in a summary judgment motion. The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues and to¿frame¿the outer measure of materiality in a summary judgment proceeding.” (Hutton v. Analysis

First Cause of Action for Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations

 

Cross-Defendant argues that the undisputed evidence shows SMC had no actual knowledge of any fraud by Zhang and believed that he had authority to negotiate and sign documents on behalf of Cross-Complainants Zhengfu and Zhumeng.

 

The elements for the tort of intentional interference with the performance of a contract are: “(1) a valid contract between plaintiff and another party; (2) defendant’s knowledge of the contract; (3) defendant’s intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.” (Asahi Kasei Pharma Corporation v. Actelion Ltd. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 945, 958.) “[I]t is not necessary that the defendant’s conduct be wrongful apart from the interference with the contract itself.” (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 55.) As to the element of intent, a plaintiff must establish “that the defendant ‘[knew] that the interference is certain or substantially certain to occur as a result of his action.’” (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1157.)

 

The action arises from two disputed security agreements. Cross-Defendant is an established lender in the motion picture industry and is operated by its principals, Alastair Burlingham and Gary Raskin. (UMF 1.) Many of SMC’s loans are “bridge” loans, short term loans provided to a borrower to pay for project costs until longer-term financing can be secured. (UMF 2.)  In late January 2018, SMC considered a bridge loan for the motion picture Midway, primarily communicating with J.P. Pettinato (“Pettinato”), CFO of Centropolis Entertainment (“Centropolis”). (UMF 3-4.) Pettinato explained to SMC that the finance plan included $22mm of equity financing from Starlight, a Chinese company. (UMF 5-9.) Starlight and Pettinato told SMC that the $22mm would be funded in multiple installments primarily because of the challenges in wiring funds from China to the United States. (Id.) SMC proposed, and Centropolis and Starlight agreed, that SMC would not take any risk on the Starlight funding but until the full $22mm was funded, SMC would only bridge up to the amount of the Starlight funding (which was to be funded to an escrow account and not available to Centropolis until further conditions were satisfied). (Id.) They entered into a term sheet and an escrow agreement. (Id.)

 

In late May/early June 2018, before the bridge loan closed, Pettinato told SMC that Starlight was unable to meet the full $22mm amount but, instead, part of that financing was now coming from another investor in China, Han Sanping. Critically, SMC argues that it was justified and privileged (9th Aff. Def.) in asserting its rights to collateral under the loan agreements which allegedly interfered with the cited agreements. Justification for the interference is an affirmative defense to intentional interference to contractual relations. (Richardson v. La Rancherita, (1979) 98 Cal. App. 3d 73, 80.)

 

In action in tort “will lie for the intentional interference by a third person with a contractual relationship either by unlawful means or by means otherwise lawful when there is a lack of sufficient justification.” [Citations.] The tort is “a [specie] of the broader tort of interference with prospective economic advantage,” involving similarity in elementary makeup and conduct while differing only in the existence of a legally binding contract. [Citation.] Justification for the interference is an affirmative defense and not an element of plaintiff's cause of action. [Citations.]

 

[…]

 

The test of whether there is justification for conduct which induces a breach of contract turns on a balancing of the social and private importance of the objective advanced by the interference against the importance of the interest interfered with, considering all the circumstances including the nature of the actor's conduct and the relationship between the parties. [Citations.]

 

In harmony with the general guidelines of the test for justification is the narrow protection afforded to a party where (1) he has a legally protected interest, (2) in good faith threatens to protect it, and (3) the threat is to protect it by appropriate means. [Citation.] Prosser adds: “Where the defendant acts to further his own advantage, other distinctions have been made. If he has a present, existing economic interest to protect, such as the ownership or condition of property, or a prior contract of his own, or a financial interest in the affairs of the person persuaded, he is privileged to prevent performance of the contract of another which threatens it; and for obvious reasons of policy he is likewise privileged to assert an honest claim, or bring or threaten a suit in good faith.” [Citation.]

(Id. 80-81.) “Something other than sincerity and an honest conviction by a party in his position is required before justification for his conduct on the grounds of ‘good faith’ can be established.” (Id. at 83 [mere reliance on counsel’s advice, by itself, is insufficient to establish good faith].)

 

SMC presents sufficient evidence that establishes its agents either did not know of the allegedly interfered contracts, or that it was justified in asserting its own contractual rights in good faith. SMC presents evidence that entered into the security agreements with Golden Title with Zhang, an agent for Zhengfu and Zhumeng. (UMF 57-76.) SMC provides detailed evidence showing why its principals believed, in good faith, that Zhang was duly authorized to negotiate the loans on behalf of Zhengfu and Zhumeng. This includes SMC’s experience with the Midway loan, what Mr. Pettinato and others in the film industry explained about Zhang, the due diligence documents provided by Kavanaugh and Zhang, and Zhang’s representations to SMC. In fact, this alone did not satisfy SMC, as SMC required the corporate chops on security agreements. Zhang timely provided the chops via a corporate controlled email address. (UMF 34-38, 62-69.) Strictly construing such evidence, the record demonstrates that SMC reasonably believed Zhang’s authority, reasonably believed that they had a perfected security interest in Zhengfu, and Zhumeng’s entitlements from the films. Thus, SMC was entitled to protect it by appropriate means, such as demanding or threatening to sue for their security. Cross-Defendant thereby establishes a defense to the first cause of action. The burden shifts to Cross-Complainants to produce specific and substantial evidence showing a dispute of material fact as to the Justification defense.

 

Cross-Complainants argue that SMC lacked good faith in support of their justification defense because the evidence on the record reasonably leads to an inference that SMC knew about Zhang’s fraud yet still moved forward with the bridge loan, foreclosed on the Greyhound and Midway Rights, and contacted Sony and the Midway Parties to stop them from allocating revenues to Cross-Complainants. (AMF 117.) Cross-Complainants cite various facts, including that: 1) SMC exclusively negotiated the Destroyer Bridge Loan with Zhang (AMF 93); SMC did not attempt to communicate with anyone else at Zhengfu or Zhumeng about Zhang’s pledge of their rights in connection with the Destroyer Bridge Loan (AMF 94); 2) the Destroyer Bridge Loan was overcollateralized (AMF 95); 3) SMC was aware that Zhengfu and Zhumeng invested a total of over $20 million for the Greyhound and Midway rights (AMF 96); 4) Zhang was not listed in any of Zhengfu’s or Zhumeng’s public corporate documents or information and SMC knew that Han Jianu was Zhengfu’s Director (AMF 97); and, 5) Zhang had not signed any of the operative agreements related to Greyhound or Midway on Zhengfu’s or Zhumeng’s behalf (AMF 98). Cross-Complainants contend that SMC had “all of the information” at its fingertips to have “actual” knowledge that Zhang was committing fraud. (AMF 100.) Further, Cross-Complainants cites evidence that SMC normally negotiates bridge loans with a borrower’s executive level representative and lawyer. (AMF 102.) SMC had no communication with Sacker (Zhang’s attorney) during the Destroyer Bridge Loan negotiation. (AMF 103.) SMC paid the bridge loan directly to Zhang, without contacting anyone else involved in the Destroyer production. (AMF 104.) SMC knew that Golden Title and Zhengfu had different leadership, but only communicated with Zhang during the Destroyer Bridge Loan negotiation. (AMF 105.)

 

The cited evidence does not specifically and substantially support this position.  The evidence does not demonstrate that  SMC actually knew Zhang was committing fraud. (AMF 100; Han Decl., ¶¶ 35-36 [re: corporate chop]; Tie Decl., ¶ 23 [Zhang was not involved in the Substitution Agreement]; Ke Decl., ¶¶ 14, 15 [Ke delegated responsibility for the Midway contract to employees such as Tie and Xiaotang]; Bakewell Decl., Vol. 1, Ex. 1 [Raskin Depo.] at 137:11-14; 139:13-140:23, 141:1-17; 144:21-146:15; 166:1-167:3; 188:25-192:19, 193:11-196:1, 201:22-209:7, 241:8-252:19; 269:18-276:6; Ex. 2 [Raskin Depo. Vol. 2] at 297:21-304:9; 305:11- 308:15; 315:2-9; 350:3-20; 355:14-360:13; 362:7-367:3; Ex. 3 [Burlingham Depo. Vol. 1] at 94:19-97:22, 181:6- 182:2; 117:18-119:21, 120:5-20, 121:9-14, 170:8-171:9, 167:14-20, 172:14-175:4, 180:22-181:5, 184:10-185:5; 264:1-11, 266:1-267:4; & Ex. 4 [Burlingham Depo. Vol. 2] at 66:13-68:7, 68:8-12.) Cross-Complainant contends that SMC “admits” that this was not the “norm” and was not how Sherborne conducted the Midway Bridge Loan. (AMF 104.) However, the deposition testimony does not suggest there was anything out of the ordinary with the subject loan agreements. (AMF 104; Bakewell Decl., Vol 1., Ex. 1 at 83:11-24, Ex. 2 at 376:14-379:22, Ex. 4 at 62:15-63:24; Vol. 7, Ex. 50; Vol. 10, Ex. 65.) If anything, the AMFs admit that Defendants do not have any specific or substantial evidence that SMC had actual knowledge of Zhang’s purported fraud to defeat SMC’s good faith justification for their disruption of the Sony and Centropolis contracts. Cross-Complainants’ evidence shows, at best, SMC should have known that Zhang was a fraudster. Cross-Complainants accordingly argue that the court should infer that SMC actually knew that Zhang was a fraudster. Of course, this is a circular argument—SMC had actual knowledge because they should have had actual knowledge, and therefore had actual knowledge. Further, Cross-Complainants present evidence to the contrary: they admit that Zhang was an actual agent of Zhumeng regarding the production of Midway, albeit as a translator. (AMF 21-28.) In light of this evidence, the Court cannot infer that SMC had actual knowledge of Zhang’s fraud, such that their good faith is in dispute.

 

The Court would agree that if the loans were dramatically overcollateralized, a fact finder could fairly infer that SMC participated in Zhang’s fraud. (AMF 95.) However, Cross-Complainants do not explain, as a matter of fact, why or how the loans were overcollateralized. To establish whether or loan was factually overcollateralized, Cross-Complainants would likely need expert testimony which concludes that the value of the contracts were greater than the risk of loss in case of default. The Court observes no such testimony. Cross-Complainants merely cite the contracts as self-evidently overcollateralized. Therefore, Cross-Complainants present no specific or substantial evidence of overcollateralization for the Court to infer that SMC was participating in Zhang’s alleged fraud.

 

In sum, Cross-Complainants fail to cite specific and substantial evidence which could lead a reasonable fact finder to infer that Cross-Defendant had “actual” knowledge of Zhang’s fraud. The record fails to establish any evidence that Burlingham or Raskin knew information which would reasonably lead to an inference that Zhang was not actually an agent of Cross-Complainants. As Cross-Complainants have not presented such evidence, they fail to meet their burden in opposing summary adjudication of this cause of action and defense.

 

Accordingly, the motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED as to issues nos. 1-2.

 

Second Cause of Action for Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage

 

As noted by the above authorities, the elements for the tort of intentional interference with prospective economic advantage are substantially similar to that of contractual interference. That is: “(1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the acts of the defendant.” (Korea Supply Co., supra, 29 Cal.4th at 1153.) The only difference is that a plaintiff must establish that defendants engaged in an “independently wrongful” act rather than interfering with a specific contract. (Id. at 1158.) “[A]n act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Id. at 1159.) As such, the above analysis concerning SMC’s justification defense equally applies here. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to issue nos. 3-4.

 

Fourth Cause of Action for Conversion

 

“‘Conversion is any act of dominion wrongfully exerted over another’s personal property in denial of or inconsistent with his rights therein.’” (Enterprise Leasing Corp. v. Shugart Corp. (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 737, 747.) “‘It is not necessary that there be a manual taking of the property; it is only necessary to show an assumption of control or ownership over the property, or that the alleged converter has applied the property to his own use.’” (Id.) 

 

Courts have traditionally refused to recognize as conversion the unauthorized taking of intangible interests that are not merged with, or reflected in, something tangible. (Thrifty-Tel, Inc. v. Bezenek (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 1559, 1565.) Courts have provided a remedy through conversion for the loss of intangible property interest, but only if those interests are reflected in something tangible that can be physically taken. (Moore v. Regents of the University of California (1990) 51 Cal.3d 120, 134.) “ ‘We recognize that the common law of conversion, which developed initially as a remedy for the dispossession or other loss of chattel [citation], may be inappropriate for some modern intangible personal property, the unauthorized use of which can take many forms. In some circumstances, newer economic torts have developed that may better take into account the nature and uses of intangible property, the interests at stake, and the appropriate measure of damages. On the other hand, if the law of conversion can be adapted to particular types of intangible property and will not displace other, more suitable law, it may be appropriate to do so.’” (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 210-211, quoting Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 124–125.) “In determining whether property that was taken is subject to a conversion claim, courts have recognized that ‘[p]roperty is a broad concept that includes “every intangible benefit and prerogative susceptible of possession or disposition.” ’ ” (Id., at 212, citations omitted.) For example, courts have recognized the following intangible property as subject to conversion claims: misappropriation of personal identifying information; sale of recorded musical performances; fraudulent transmission of credit card, debit card, or PayPal information; checks and customer lists; and passwords. (Id. at 212-213 [collecting cases].) Welco itself regarded misuse of credit card information to obtain money as not materially different in effect of taking other instruments such as checks, bonds, etc. (Id.)

 

“Money cannot be the subject of a cause of action for conversion unless there is a specific, identifiable sum involved, such as where an agent accepts a sum of money to be paid to another and fails to make the payment.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1491.) Likewise, a mere contractual right of payment, without more, will not suffice to state a claim for conversion. (Farmers Ins. Exch. v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 451-452.) 

 

The SACC alleges that SMC wrongfully exercised dominion over the Greyhound Rights and the Midway Rights.  Specifically, Cross-Complainants’ contractual rights to payment under the respective agreement. The SACC defines the Greyhound Rights as Zhengfu’s entitlement “to a portion of revenues earned from Greyhound’s exploitation, distribution rights, copyright ownership, and other rights provided in the Greyhound Co-Financing Agreement and related agreements”. (SACC ¶23, emphasis added.) The SACC does not allege that the “distribution rights,” the “copyright,” or any other rights in the Greyhound Agreement(s) are subject to the conversion cause of action, but only the revenues earned. The SACC defines the Midway rights as Zhumeng’s entitlement to “revenues earned from Midway’s exploitation and other rights provided in the Midway Co-Financing Agreement and related agreements. (SACC ¶ 37.)  The SACC alleges details of Zhumeng’s entitlement to certain Gross Receipts. (¶¶ 38-40.)

 

The SACC alleges that SMC deprived and continues to deprive Zhengfu and Zhumeng of revenue and benefits owed under the Greyhound and Midway Co-Financing Agreements. (SACC ¶ 90.) Third parties such as Sony and the Midway parties refused to pay Cross-Complainants amounts they are owed under the Greyhound and Midway Co-Financing Agreements because of SMC’s claims to rights under those agreements. (¶ 91.) SMC asserted that it owned the Greyhound Rights to Sony and to the Collection Agent in Midway. (¶¶ 92-93.)

 

The SACC alleges that SMC substantially interfered with Zhengfu's Greyhound Rights by knowingly and intentionally preventing Zhengfu from having access to its Greyhound Rights by (a) engaging in, advancing, and, ultimately, completing the SMC-Golden Title Loan Transaction whereby Zhang purportedly pledged the Greyhound Rights to SMC without authorization or authority from Zhengfu; (b) seeking to foreclose on the Greyhound Rights in a purported foreclosure sale that was defective, commercially unreasonable, and violated the California Commercial Code; and (c) demanding that Sony recognize it as the owner of all of the Greyhound Rights and pay SMC amounts owed to Zhengtu. (SAC ¶ 149.) SMC also substantially interfered with Zhumeng’s Midway Rights by knowingly and intentionally preventing Zhumeng from having access to its Midway Rights by (a) engaging in, advancing, and, ultimately, completing the SMC-Golden Title Loan Transaction whereby Zhang purportedly pledged the Midway Rights to SMC without authorization or authority from Zhumeng; (b) seeking to foreclose on the Midway Rights purported foreclosure sale that was defective, commercially unreasonable, and violated the California Commercial Code; and (c) seeking to redirect revenue streams due to Zhumeng under the Midway Co-Financing Agreement and related Midway agreements after SMC’s purported foreclosure sale. (SAC ¶ 150.)

 

Thus, Zhumeng’s conversion claim is based on alleged interference with the “Midway rights”, comprising of Zhumeng’s contractual rights to receive a portion of revenues from the motion picture Midway. (SACC, ¶¶ 3, 148, 150.) Zhengfu’s conversion claim is based on interference with the “Greyhound rights”, comprising Zhengfu’s contractual right to receive a portion of revenues and other interests from Sony. (SACC, ¶¶ 147, 149.) Both claims are facially based on the mere contractual right of payment, rather than for a certain, identifiable sum of money. As such, the claims fail as a matter of law. Cross-Defendant therefore meets its burden to show entitlement to judgment as a matter of law on this claim.

 

Cross-Complainant, in turn, fails to demonstrate a dispute of material fact, or otherwise show a reasonable probability of successful amendment of the claim. SMC presents evidence that the Greyhound Rights include co-ownership of the underlying copyright in the Greyhound motion picture distribution rights in Mainland China for 15 years, and rights to revenue distributions. (AMF 118.) Zhumeng’s Midway Rights include various “approval rights” such as over the distributor of the film in China and terms of that distribution agreement, consultation rights over business and creative matters both during production and postproduction, and a portion of revenues earned by the film’s exploitation. (AMF 119.) Zhengfu and Zhumeng had rights in the Greyhound and Midway revenues as the revenues were earned. (AMF 120.) The Midway revenues even went directly to a third-party Collection Agent to administer, allocate, and distribute according to the Midway Co-Financing Agreement. (AMF 121.)

 

Of course, the court has already held that it lacks jurisdiction to consider the validity of the copyright interest. (28 USC § 1338(a); see 11/1/22 Minute Order.) Further, there is no allegation or evidence that SMC interfered with (or somehow exerted dominion over) the non-revenue contractual rights. SMC did not allegedly assert control over the distribution rights in China. As alleged, Sony controls the rights but decided not to distribute. Even if such rights were interfered with, Cross-Complainants do not allege or contend that such intangible rights are similar to other intangibles recognized by caselaw as subject to a conversion cause of action. (See Welco, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at 210-211 [listing examples].) Unlike the cases involving fiduciaries converting funds, here there is no misappropriation, commingling or misapplication of funds. (Sanowicz v. Bacal, (2015) 234 Cal. App. 4th 1027, 1042 [real estate agent liable for conversion by accepting commissions for himself and a business partner without giving the partner his share]; Fischer v. Machado, (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 1069, 1073-74 [holding sales agent can be liable for conversion by withholding proceeds from a consignment sale from the principal seller].) Therefore, such an interference would not support a conversion claim. All that remains is Cross-Complainants’ general claim for damages caused by SMC’s interference with their contractual rights of payment.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to issue nos. 5-6.

 

Fraud Causes (Sixth, Seventh Causes)

 

SMC moves to adjudicate the sixth cause of action for conspiracy to commit fraud and seventh cause of action for aiding and abetting fraud. 

 

“Conspiracy is not a cause of action, but a legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves, share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration . . . Standing alone, a conspiracy does no harm and engenders no tort liability. It must be activated by the commission of an actual tort. A civil conspiracy, however atrocious, does not per se give rise to a cause of action unless a civil wrong has been committed resulting in damage.” (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 510-11 [internal citations and quotations marks omitted].) To prevail on this theory, a plaintiff must establish: “‘(1) formation and operation of the conspiracy and (2) damage resulting to plaintiff (3) from a wrongful act done in furtherance of the common design.’ [Citation.]” (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1173.) “It is not enough that the [conspirators] knew of an intended wrongful act, they must agree—expressly or tacitly—to achieve it.” (AREI II Cases (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1022.) A conspirator’s agreement to the conspiracy “may be inferred from the character of the acts done, the relations of the parties, the interest of the alleged conspirators, and other circumstances.” (Id. at 1024.)

 

“A defendant is liable for aiding and abetting another in the commission of an intentional tort … if the defendant ‘ “ ‘knows the other's conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act.’ ” ’ [Citation.]” (Nasrawi v. Buck Consultants LLC (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 328, 343.) “ ‘Liability may . . . be imposed on one who aids and abets the commission of an intentional tort if the person (a) knows the other's conduct constitutes a breach of duty and gives substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act or (b) gives substantial assistance to the other in accomplishing a tortious result and the person's own conduct, separately considered, constitutes a breach of duty to the third person.’ [Citations.]” (Fiol v. Doellstedt (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1318, 1325-1326.) Conspiracy is a concept closely allied with aiding and abetting. (Janken v. GM Hughes Electronics (1996) 46 Cal.App.4th 55, 78.) While conspiracy requires an agreement, aiding and abetting only requires assistance. (Id.)

 

The elements of fraud are “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.)

 

Cross-Defendant presents undisputed evidence that it had no knowledge that Zhang lacked authority to bind Zhumeng and Zhengfu or forged signatures/corporate chops. As discussed above, SMC reasonably believed that Zhang had authority to act on behalf of those entities based on the information and documentation that had been provided to them. (UMF 64, 76.) As SMC had no actual knowledge of purported fraudulent acts of Zhang, Cross-Defendant demonstrates that Cross-Complainants cannot establish the formation and operation of the conspiracy, or SMC’s knowledge that Zhang was breaching his duty to Cross-Complainants. Cross-Complainants fail to present any dispute of material fact concerning SMC’s knowledge of Zhang’s alleged fraud. (AMF 93-105.)

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to issue nos. 7-10.

 

Eighth Cause of Action for violation of the UCL (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200)

 

Business & Professions Code § 17200 requires that a plaintiff prove that a defendant engaged in a business practice that was either unlawful (i.e, forbidden by law), unfair (i.e., harm to victim outweighs any benefit), or fraudulent (i.e., is likely to deceive members of the public). (Albillo v. Intermodal Container Services, Inc. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 190, 206.) “An unlawful business practice or act is an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.” (Klein v. Earth Elements, Inc. (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 965, 969.) “A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.” (McKell v. Washington Mut., Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1473.) A business practice is fraudulent if members of the public are likely to be deceived by the practice. (West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 806.)

 

The SACC alleges that SMC engaged in unlawful, unfair, and fraudulent conduct by the following:

 

·       SMC proceeded with and ultimately completed the SMC-Golden Title Loan Transaction even though SMC knew it only negotiated and transacted with Zhang and knew or should have known that: (a) Zhang was not an officer, director, shareholder, employee, legal representative, or agent of Zhengfu or Zhumeng; and (b) Zhang had no authority or authorization to pledge the Greyhound and Midway Rights.

·       SMC proceeded with and ultimately completed the SMC-Golden Title Loan Transaction even though SMC knew or should have known that Zhang: (a) pledged the Greyhound Rights by forging Jianu Han’s signature and Zhengfu’s corporate seal (chop); and (b) pledged the Midway Rights by signing on behalf of Zhumeng when he has no authority or authorization from Zhumeng to do so and by forging Zhumeng’s chop company seal (chop).

·       SMC failed to perform any reasonable inquiries into Zhang’s relationship with Zhengfu and Zhumeng before proceeding with the SMC-Golden Title Loan Transaction. SMC’s only point of connection to and contact at Zhengfu and Zhumeng was Zhang, but he is not an officer, director, shareholder, employee, legal representative, or agent of Zhengfu or Zhumeng. But, SMC transacted and negotiated only with Zhang about Zhengfu’s Greyhound Rights and Zhumeng’s Midway Rights. Moreover, information about a company’s directors, officers, executives, shareholders, and legal representatives are matters of public record in China.

·       SMC registered Zhang’s fraudulent Greyhound Copyright Mortgage and Assignment with the Copyright Office.

·       SMC sought to foreclose the Greyhound and Midway Rights while relying on Zhang’s fraudulent pledges of the Greyhound and Midway Rights on September 21, 2020. SMC’s purported foreclosure sale violated California’s Commercial Code as SMC did not notify Zhengfu or Zhumeng of the purported foreclosure sale until two months after it happened.

·       SMC unjustly benefited from its wrongful retention of the Greyhound Rights (which rightfully belong to Zhengfu) and the Midway Rights (which rightfully belong to Zhumeng). SMC stated that it entered an $8 million credit bid at the foreclosure sale for the Greyhound and Midway Rights, and no counterbids were submitted. SMC knew or should have known that its “credit bid” was far below the value of the Greyhound or Midway Rights. Greyhound's exploitation in China alone could generate revenue in many tens of millions of dollars, and Midway had generated gross receipts of more than $52 million as of June 2020.

·       SMC sought to enforce its purported ownership of the Greyhound Rights by proclaiming ownership to Sony. In addition, SMC sought to redirect revenue streams due to Zhumeng under the Midway Co-Financing Agreement and related Midway agreements after SMC’s purported foreclosure sale.

·       SMC asserted and continues to proclaim to: (a) Sony and other third parties that it is entitled to the Greyhound Rights relying on the Greyhound-SMC Agreements; and (b) third parties that it is entitled to the Midway Rights relying on the Midway-SMC Agreements.

·       SMC, Golden Title, and Zhang intended for their fraud, deceit, and other wrongful conduct to interfere with Zhengfu’s relationship with Sony and Zhumeng’s relationship with the Midway Parties.

 

(SACC ¶ 188, emphasis added.)

 

SMC fails to defeat all the pled bases for unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business practices. SMC contends that its actions were taken based on its belief that Zhang was authorized to act on behalf of Zhumeng and Zhengfu and were therefore not immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous. However, the SACC alleges that SMC did not “reasonably” inquire into Zhang’s relationship with Zhengfu or Zhumeng, and that SMC should have known that Zhang was not so authorized. SMC expressly does not make any contentions regarding what it should have known, but rather focuses only on its actual knowledge. (See, e.g., Mot. at 2, fn. 1.) SMC presents no authority which would hold that their alleged failure to properly investigate Zhang while proceeding with the transactions could not be considered an “unfair” business practice, even if not unlawful or fraudulent. SMC further does not address whether it violated Commercial Code sections 9611 and 9612 by not notifying Zhengfu and Zhumeng of the foreclosure sale. (AMF 111.)  While SMC argues that the court has already decided the Commercial Code issue, a ruling on a preliminary injunction motion is not an adjudication of the ultimate rights in controversy, and merely represents the trial court's discretionary decision whether defendant should be restrained from exercising a claimed right pending trial. (Cohen v. Board of Supervisors (1985) 40 Cal.3d 277, 286.)

 

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED as to issue nos. 11-12.

 

Punitive Damages

 

SMC argues that Cross-Complainants cannot prove malice, fraud or oppression required by Civil Code section 3294(c).

 

Under Civil Code section 3294(c): (1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others; (2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights; and (3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent4 of the corporation. 

 

(Civil Code § 3294(b).)

 

“Despicable conduct” that rises to the level of supporting an award of punitive damages against a defendant is that which is “so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people” and has been described as “conduct…having the character of outrage frequently associated with a crime.” (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.)

 

“Conscious disregard” means “the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and the he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.”  (Hoch v. Allied-Signal, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.) To be liable for punitive damages the defendant must “have actual knowledge of the risk of harm it is creating, and in the face of the knowledge, fail to take steps it knows will reduce or eliminate the risk of harm.” (Ehrhardt v. Brunswick, Inc. (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 734, 742.)

 

Under the clear and convincing evidentiary standard the evidence is “so clear as to leave no substantial doubt” and “sufficiently strong to command the unhesitating assent of every reasonable mind.” (Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 847, 891.) Given the ultimate burden of proof of clear and convincing evidence, this standard must be taking into account when ruling on a motion for summary judgment or adjudication because it will be necessary for the evidence to meet the standard. (American Airlines, Inc. v. Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 1017, 1049.) Even though the trial court must take this standard into account on a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, this mean plaintiff must “prove” the claim for punitive damages. (Id.) Summary judgment or adjudication of punitive damages is only proper “when no reasonable jury could find the plaintiff’s evidence to be clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression.” (Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 762.)

 

Cross-Complainants seek punitive damages as to their first, second, fourth, sixth, and seventh causes. As discussed above, SMC has demonstrated that each of those causes of action fail as a matter of law. As discussed, SMC demonstrates their principals did not intend any injuries, or consciously disregard Cross-Complainants rights. Instead, SMC believed in good faith that Zhang represented Cross-Complainants. This necessarily precludes a finding of malice, oppression or fraud on the part of SMC’s managing agents. Moreover, SMC asked Cross-Complainants in interrogatories for the basis of their claim for punitive damages and the response detailed no false representations by SMC nor any basis for a claim of concealment of material fact. (UMF 101-102.)

 

Cross-Complainants present no evidence to dispute of the material facts concerning fraud, malice or oppression. Cross-Complainants argue that punitive damages are warranted by SMC’s “intentional” act of interference with Cross-Complainants’ rights under the Greyhound and Midway Co-Financing Agreements. Cross-Complainants contend that the evidence shows SMC knew about Zhang’s fraudulent pledge of Cross-Complainants’ rights, yet accepted that pledge, fraudulently foreclosed on those rights, then contacted Cross-Complainant’s business partners to cut off Cross-Complainants’ ability to collect on their rights. (AMF 93-105.)

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to issue no. 13-14.

 

 

Conclusion

 

The motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED as to issues 1-10 and 13-14 and DENIED as to issue nos. 11-12.

CASE NAME:           Adamyan v. O’Gara Coach Company LLC, et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCV02923

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Initial Discovery Responses

HEARING DATE:   10/10/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

 If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.) 

 

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP section 2031.010 inspection demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300.) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion. Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required. 

 

ANALYSIS 

 

Defendant Automobili Lamborghini America, LLC (“ALA”) moves to compel responses from Plaintiff Adamyan Akop as to ALA’s First Set of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Inspection Demand. Defendant also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,680.00, jointly and severally, against Plaintiff and his counsel of record, Mgdesyan Law Firm.

 

On April 24, 2024, ALA served the subject discovery on Plaintiff, including the interrogatories and requests for production of documents. (Kim Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.) The last day to serve responses was June 12, 2024. (Kim Decl. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff failed to serve timely responses to the discovery. (Kim Decl. ¶ 3.) On July 31, 2024, ALA requested that Plaintiff provide responses to the subject discovery without objections by August 9, 2024. (Kim Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. B.) To date, Plaintiff has yet to respond. (Id. at ¶ 5) Defendant thereby demonstrates that Plaintiff failed to serve any responses to the Interrogatories and Document Requests. Therefore, initial responses are required.

 

Accordingly, the motions are GRANTED. The requests for sanctions are granted in the total amount of $1,740.00, inclusive of costs, against Plaintiff and counsel of record. Sanctions are to be paid to Defendant’s counsel of record within 30 days.


Case Number: 23SMCV03967    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           1239 23rd Street Homeowners Association v. Kurtzer

CASE NO.:                23SMCV03967

MOTION:                  Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

HEARING DATE:   10/10/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (CCP § 284(2).) The attorney seeking to withdraw must take “reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the rights of the client, including giving due notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel.” (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, 3-700(A)(2). See, e.g., Vann v. Shilleh (1975) [holding withdrawal prejudicial where attorney withdraw from the representation of defendant on the Friday before trial began the following Monday].) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.) 

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (CRC 3.1362(a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (CRC, 3.1362(d).) When a client is served by mail, the attorney’s declaration must indicate that the client’s address was confirmed within the last 30 days and how it was confirmed. (Id.) If the attorney is unable to confirm the client’s current address, the declaration must state the reasonable efforts made within the last 30 days to obtain the client’s current address. (Id.)

 

Additionally, the declaration “must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship why” a motion is brought instead of filing a substitution of attorney. (CRC, 3.1362(c).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Richard A. Rodgers, Esq., moves to be relieved as counsel of record for Defendant Denise Kurtzer. Counsel submits all the mandatory forms. Counsel states that an irreparable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship has occurred, and that Defendant refuses to retain new counsel or sign a substitution form. Counsel served his client by mail at her last known address, which was confirmed within the past 30 days by email and telephone. The declaration notes the only other hearing on calendar, a motion to deem the matter “moot” and award Plaintiff attorney’s fees as the prevailing party.

 

The Court is concerned that Defendant will be prejudiced by withdrawal while there is a dispositive motion set for hearing on October 23, 2024.  The prejudice, however, is caused by Defendant’s own conduct.  Here, counsel gave Defendant sufficient time to retain new counsel prior to the October 23, 2024 hearing, and prior to any opposition to the attorney’s fees motion had to be filed.  Despite that, Defendant has apparently refused to get new counsel. Accordingly, counsel filed a substantive opposition to the motion.  Since filing that opposition, Defendant has still refused to sign a substitution of counsel form.  By filing the opposition, counsel has placed Defendant in a far better position and mitigated the potential prejudice.  Under the circumstances, counsel cannot be compelled to continue to represent Defendant.  Thus, the motion is GRANTED. The order is effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the order on the client. Until then, counsel remains counsel of record.


Case Number: 24SMCV00099    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Fanfixapp LLC v. Passes Inc., et al.

CASE NO.:                24SMCV00099

MOTION:                  Motion to Seal

HEARING DATE:   10/10/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

The sealing of trial court records is governed by California Rules of Court, (“CRC”) rules 2.550 and 2.551. (Mercury Interactive Corp. v. Klein (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 60, 68.) Pleadings, motions, discovery documents, and other papers may not be filed under seal merely by stipulation of the parties—a prior court order must be obtained. (CRC, Rule 2.551(a); see H.B. Fuller Co. v. Doe (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 879, 888.)

 

To seal a record, the following requirements are imposed: (1) the party must file a motion or application for an order sealing the record, which must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing; (2) the party must serve a copy of the motion on all parties who have appeared in the case; and (3) the party requesting that a record be filed under seal must lodge it with the court when the motion or application is made unless the record has previously been lodged. (CRC, Rule 2.551(b).)  

 

The Court must make the following express factual findings in order to seal records: (1) an overriding interest exists that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the records; (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest. (CRC, Rule 2.550(d).) These findings embody constitutional requirements for a request to seal court records, protecting the First Amendment right of public access to civil trials. (NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1217-1218.)

 

An order sealing the record must specifically state the facts that support the findings and direct the sealing of only those pages and documents or, if reasonably practicable, portions of those documents and pages, that contain the material that needs to be placed under seal, and all other portions must be included in the public file. (CRC, rule 2.550(e).)  Once sealed, a record can only be unsealed by order of court. (CRC, Rule 2.551(h)(1).) So long as it remains under seal, all parties must refrain from filing anything not under seal that would disclose the sealed matter. (Id., Rule 2.551(c).) If a party files a new document referring to sealed matter, it must submit an unredacted version of the document under seal and a redacted one for the public record. (Id., Rule 2.551(b)(5).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Passes, Inc. moves to seal portions of Passes’ Reply in Support of Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatory No. 1 and portions of the Wang Declaration in Support of Passes’ Reply in Support of Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatory No. 1. Specifically, as to the reply, Defendant seeks to seal pages 1:21-22, 3:25-26, 4:1-2, 4:13-15, 4:26-27, 5:17-22, 6:12-15, and Figures 1-4. As to the declaration in support of the reply, Defendant seeks to seal pages 1:10-11 1:13-15 and Exhibits 2-3.

 

California Civil Code section 3426.5 provides that in an action under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act “a court shall preserve the secrecy of an alleged trade secret by reasonable means, which may include . . . sealing the records of the action[.]” (Civ. Code § 3426.5.)  Defendant explains that the cited portions of the reply quote from confidentially marked materials under the Parties’ protective order. Defense counsel provides a declaration that public disclosure of the redacted portions could cause competitive harm to Plaintiffs. (Hurley Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) Defendant argues that the redactions are narrowly tailored, only affecting the portions which quote from the confidentially marked documents.

 

Defendant demonstrates that Plaintiff has an overriding interest which overcomes the right of public access to its trade secret information. There is a substantial probability of prejudice to Plaintiff’s trade secret interests if the record is not sealed. Moreover, the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored, and no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest, because Defendant has only redacted portions of the motion which quote from the confidentially marked trade secret documents. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.


Case Number: 24SMCV01287    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:             807 Huntley LLC v. Altuwaijri

CASE NO.:                   24SMCV01287

MOTION:                  Motion to Strike Cross-Complaint  

HEARING DATE:   10/10/2024

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD 

  

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP § 435(b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP §§ 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].) 

 

“Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.) It is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where there is any reasonable possibility that plaintiff can state a good cause of action. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on plaintiff to show¿in what manner¿plaintiff can amend the complaint, and¿how¿that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading.¿(Id.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant 807 Huntley LLC moves to strike the claim for punitive damages alleged in the Cross-Complainant by Defendant/Cross-Complainant Ahmed Altuwaijri.

 

To recover punitive damages for malice or oppression, plaintiff must plead facts supporting an intent to harm or “despicable conduct” carried about with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code §3294(c).) Despicable is a powerful term used to describe circumstances that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (Coll. Hosp., supra, 9 Cal.4th 704, 726.) The statute “plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, “malice” requires more than a “willful and conscious” disregard of the plaintiffs' interests. The additional component of “despicable conduct” must be found.” (Ibid.) This includes only truly egregious behavior. (See, e.g., Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1221—1222 [vile, base, or contemptible conduct found where doctor engaged in sexual intercourse with minor, plying the minor with drugs and alcohol, and paying the minor to procure illegal substances for him]; Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1280 [finding despicable conduct when a manufacturer “fully understood that asbestos dust endangered workers, but did not issue warnings to customers” until much later and using the products in ways that generated considerable asbestos dust].) 

 

Cross-Complainant alleges that he is the tenant of the real property commonly known as 807 Huntley Drive, #807, West Hollywood, California, 90068, which is owned by Cross-Defendant. (CC ¶¶ 1-2.) Courts have recognized that a tenant may recover punitive damages against a landlord for wrongful eviction, trespass, invasion of privacy, and intentional infliction of emotional distress, among other theories. (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments, (2009) 171 Cal. App. 4th 1004, 1055.) For instance, a landlord’s intentional or malicious failure to repair can support a claim for punitive damages. (Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 920.) In¿Stoiber, the plaintiff complained of numerous uninhabitable conditions that she repeatedly notified the landlord of, that the landlord had actual and full knowledge of the conditions, but the landlord intentionally made no effort to repair the dangerous conditions. (Id. 912-913, 920.) The dangerous conditions included a heavy cockroach infestation, broken interior walls, broken deteriorated flooring on front porch, falling ceiling, fused¿electrical wiring, lack of proper plumbing connection to sewage system in bathroom, sewage under bathroom floor, leaking roof, broken windows, and fire hazard. (Ibid.) Despite the repeated notice, the defendants knowingly, intentionally and willfully failed to correct the defective conditions by not even attempting to repair the defects, except on one occasion where they sent a plumber in response to a complaint regarding the toilet. (Id.¿at 913, 921.) Furthermore, a nuisance cause of action also could support a claim for exemplary damages if “intentional”. (Id., at 920.)

 

The Cross-Complaint fails to allege sufficient facts showing despicable or intentional conduct required to recover punitive damages. The Cross-Complaint only provides generalized allegations that during the tenancy, Cross-Complainant discovered plumbing issues on the Property, including water leaks and clogged pipes, which rendered the Property untenantable and uninhabitable. (CC ¶ 5.) Despite notice of the condition, Cross-Defendants allegedly failed to “promptly” remedy the situation. (Id.) This failure to “promptly” remedy vague plumbing leaks does not show Cross-Defendant’s intended to harm Cross-Complainant, or any “despicable” conduct warranting punitive damages. For example, there are no facts showing the dangerous nature of the plumbing issues, such that the court may conclude that Cross-Defendant has willfully and consciously disregarded the tenant’s rights and safety. Indeed, there are no specific facts in support of the generic allegations of malice (CC ¶ 25) or of tenant harassment (¶ 27). As such, further facts must be pled to support the punitive damages claim.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED with leave to amend.  Cross-Complainant has 20 days to file any amended cross-complaint.

CASE NO.:                24SMCV01602

MOTION:                  Motion to be Relieved

HEARING DATE:   10/10/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (CCP § 284(2).) The attorney seeking to withdraw must take “reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the rights of the client, including giving due notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel.” (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, 3-700(A)(2). See, e.g., Vann v. Shilleh (1975) [holding withdrawal prejudicial where attorney withdraw from the representation of defendant on the Friday before trial began the following Monday].) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.) 

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (CRC 3.1362(a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (CRC, 3.1362(d).) When a client is served by mail, the attorney’s declaration must indicate that the client’s address was confirmed within the last 30 days and how it was confirmed. (Id.) If the attorney is unable to confirm the client’s current address, the declaration must state the reasonable efforts made within the last 30 days to obtain the client’s current address. (Id.)

 

Additionally, the declaration “must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship why” a motion is brought instead of filing a substitution of attorney. (CRC, 3.1362(c).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Chase P. Nielsen moves to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff David McNeary. Counsel submits all the mandatory forms. Counsel states that an irreparable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship has occurred. Counsel served his client by email and text. Counsel has been unable to confirm a more-current address after mailing the motion papers to the client’s last known address with return receipt requested, calling/texting the client’s last known phone number, and conducting online searches. The client will not be prejudiced by the withdrawal since there is no set trial date and no pending dispositive motions. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED. Counsel must include any hearing dates set at the instant CMC on the MC-053 order form. The order is effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the order on the client. Until then, counsel remains counsel of record.
