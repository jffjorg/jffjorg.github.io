Case Number: 20STCV19466    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

KARINA ESPINOZA ALLENDE,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

SHAWN SHAYAN,

 

                                  Defendant.

 

 Case No:  20STCV19466

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 1, 2024

 Calendar Number:  1

 

Defendant Shawn Shayan moves for prevailing party fees and costs according to Civil Code section 1717.5.

 

The Court DENIES the motion.

 

            Defendant relies on Civil Code section 1717.5:

 

“[I]n any action on a contract based on a book account ... , which does not provide for attorney's fees and costs, as provided in Section 1717, the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees, as provided below, in addition to other costs.”

 

(Civ. Code, § 1717.5(a).)

 

Section 1717.5 does not apply here. Plaintiff sued Defendant for breach of contract on May 22, 2020. (Compl., pp. 1-2.) She did not assert a cause of action based on an open book account. (Ibid.) Nor did she state such a claim, whether or not she captioned it as such. “The elements of an open book account cause of action are: ‘1. That [plaintiff] and [defendant] had financial transactions ... ; [¶] 2. That [plaintiff] ... kept [an] account of the debits and credits involved in the transactions; [¶] 3. That [defendant] owes [plaintiff] money on the account; and [¶] 4. The amount of money that [defendant] owes [plaintiff].’ (State Compensation Insurance Fund v. ReadyLink Healthcare, Inc. (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 422, 449.) Plaintiff alleged an agreement wherein Defendant would use her credit card and pay the bill, and she would keep the points; she did not allege she loaned Defendant money in various amounts and kept a ledger, as an open book account claim contemplates.

 

Plaintiff did not claim attorneys’ fees under section 1717.5. (Id., p. 2, ¶ 10(c) [attorneys’ fees according to proof, no basis specified].) No cause of action for open book account was submitted to the jury. (See 06-07-2024 Jury Instructions.) The jury returned a verdict that the parties’ alleged contract was fatally uncertain. (See 06-07-2024 Special Verdict.)

 

There is no basis for an award of fees under section 1717.5.

 


Case Number: 20STCV40677    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: 72

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

 

DEPARTMENT 72

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

SALVADOR BARRIOS, et al.,

 

                                  Plaintiffs,

 

         v.

 

 

HEALTH START-EAST LA, INC.,

 

                                  Defendant.

 

 Case No:  20STCV40677

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 1, 2024

 Calendar Number:  2

 

Defendants move for an order compelling Plaintiffs to permit Defendants to inspect their residence.

 

The Court DENIES the motion.

 

Background

 

            Plaintiffs Salvador and Elena Barrios sued ten (10) defendants on October 22, 2020 asserting five causes of action based on various negligence theories and one for loss of consortium. Plaintiffs filed their operative first amended complaint (“FAC”) on April 8, 2021, asserting the same claims: (1) negligence (motor vehicle collision), (2) negligence (negligent entrustment and vicarious liability, (3) dangerous condition of public property, (4) negligent/reckless acts of public employees, agents, or contractors, (5) general negligence, and (6) loss of consortium.

 

            Plaintiffs subsequently dismissed all defendants except the moving defendants here, the County of Los Angeles (“County”) and City of Commerce (“City”) (together “Defendants”).

 

            The parties’ dispute arises from a car accident that occurred on February 25, 2020. (FAC, ¶¶ 20-21.) On that date, Plaintiffs allege one of the initially-named defendants, Daniel Tercero Neri, struck Salvador Barrios’ Toyota Camry while driving negligently operating a van owned by his employer, Healthy Start-East LA, Inc. (Ibid.) Barrios sustained “serious and life-threatening injuries.” (Id., ¶ 22.) As to Defendants, Plaintiffs allege they negligently designed or maintained the intersection where the accident took place. (Id., ¶¶ 23-28.)

 

            On August 13, 2024, the City moved for an order compelling Plaintiffs to permit an inspection of their home and imposing sanctions for their failure to comply with the City’s inspection demand. The County joined the City’s motion on August 21, 2024.

 

            On September 17, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their opposition. Neither the City nor the County filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

“A party may demand that any other party allow the party making the demand, or someone acting on the demanding party’s behalf, to enter on any land or other property that is in the possession, custody, or control of the party on whom the demand is made, and to inspect and to measure, survey, photograph, test, or sample the land or other property, or any designated object or operation on it.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010(d).)

 

The party to whom a demand for inspection has been directed must respond with (1) a statement that the party will comply with the particular demand, (2) a representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand or (3) an objection to the particular demand.¿ (Id., § 2031.210(a).) If the propounding party contends a response is incomplete or an objection lacks merit, it may move the court for an order compelling a further response. (Id., 2031.310(a).)

 

Evidentiary Matters

           

Plaintiff argues in its opposition papers that “Defendant’s use and production of a privileged and protected life care plan[,] produced in the course of a confidential mediation, was improper.” (Opp., 2:23-24.)      

 

The Court construes this argument as an evidentiary objection and sustains the objection. “No evidence of anything said or any admission made for the purpose of, in the course of, or pursuant to, a mediation or a mediation consultation is admissible or subject to discovery, and disclosure of the evidence shall not be compelled, in any arbitration, administrative adjudication, or other noncriminal proceeding ... .” (Evid. Code, § 1119.)

 

Exhibit A is clearly marked: “DRAFT FOR PURPOSES OF MEDIATION ONLY.” (Mot., Exh. A, p. 1.) The City’s attempt to introduce the mediation materials in its evidentiary showing is entirely improper.

 

           

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants served Plaintiffs with an inspection demand on May 28, 2024. (Ferrante-Alan Decl., ¶ 5 and Exh. B.)

 

Plaintiffs responded to Defendants’ demand with objections as permitted by Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.210(a). (Ferrante-Alan Decl., ¶ 8 and Exh. E.) Defendants argue in their moving papers that “Plaintiff has failed to formally respond” (Mot., 7:9) – this contention is contradicted by the City’s exhibits providing the objections.  Plaintiff replied with objections on June 24, 2024. (See Mot., Exh. E, p. 4 [POS for Plaintiffs’ objections to demand].)

 

Because Plaintiffs timely responded with objections, Defendants have properly filed a motion to compel further response to an inspection demand under section 2031.310. To the extent Defendants characterize their motion as a motion to compel initial response, that characterization is incorrect.

 

Procedurally, the Court relieves Defendant from the separate statement requirement as permitted by section 2031.310(c), and meet and confer requirements in section 2031.310(b)(2) have been satisfied. (See Mot., Exh. C [meet and confer email].)

 

1. Defendant have not established good cause for the inspection.

 

If the party propounding an inspection demand believes a responsive objection is “without merit or too general”, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a)(3).) The motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery demand. (Id., subd. (b)(1).)

 

Defendants contend that because Plaintiffs’ expert made damages estimates based in part on home remediation, there is good cause for Defendants to examine Plaintiffs’ home. As an initial matter, Defendants rely on mediation-privileged material that has been excluded.  Moreover, Plaintiffs’ expert did not inspect Plaintiffs’ home before making her estimate; the estimate is based on any home where Plaintiffs might live, not the specific residence they reside in now. (See Mot., Exh. B, p. 1.) Moreover, Plaintiffs have offered to stipulate that their own expert will not examine the home for purposes of their opinion or trial testimony.  The Court cannot conclude Defendants have made a good cause showing on the facts of this case.

 

 

2. Defendants have not overcome Plaintiffs’ privacy objection.

 

Even if there were good cause for the inspection, the Court agrees with Plaintiffs’ argument that Defendants’ motion should be denied based on privacy concerns. A party asserting a privacy objection must make an initial showing of “a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious”. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) The party seeking disclosure must show a legitimate countervailing interest and the Court must balance any such interest against narrower feasible alternatives. (Id., citing Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 39-40.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs have not satisfied their initial burden under Williams and Hill. Not so. Defendants want to enter and inspect all the areas Plaintiffs occupy in their private residence. There are few intrusions more profound.  There are also other family members who live in the same home.  Defendants have not shown a countervailing interest that outweighs the privacy concerns here.

 

Because the Court denies the motion on the merits, the Court denies the Defendants’ request for sanctions.

Plaintiff moves for an award of reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs as agreed by settlement.

 

Plaintiff’s motion is granted in part, in the total amount of $47,593.96, inclusive of fees and costs.

 

Background

 

            Plaintiff Kenneth Mackenzie filed this lemon law action against defendant General Motors on April 7, 2022. The case has settled. Plaintiff moves for attorneys’ fees. Defendant does not oppose.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is denied in its entirety. Other trial courts’ rulings are neither binding nor necessarily persuasive for this Court.

 

Discussion

 

Where the buyer of a vehicle prevails in an action under the Song-Beverly Act, he “shall be allowed by the court to recover ... a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred ... .” (Civ. Code, § 1794(d).)

 

Defendant was given timely notice of this motion and filed no opposition. (See 08-23-2024 POS.) Defendant does not dispute that the parties have agreed in their settlement that Plaintiff will recover fees and costs.

 

            Plaintiff seeks reasonable fees.

 

“[T]he starting point of every fee award ... must be a calculation of the attorney's services in terms of the time he has expended on the case. Anchoring the analysis to this concept is the only way of approaching the problem that can claim objectivity, a claim which is obviously vital to the prestige of the bar and the courts.”  (In re Vitamin Cases (2003) 110 Cal. App. 4th 1041, 1058, citation omitted.)  “[T]he primary method for establishing the amount of ‘reasonable’ attorney fees is the lodestar method. The lodestar (or touchstone) is produced by multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by counsel by a reasonable hourly rate.” (Thayer v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2001) 92 Cal. App. 4th 819, 833.) Under this approach, a base amount is calculated from a compilation of time reasonably spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48; Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 639.)

 

The determination of reasonable amount of attorney fees is within the sound discretion of trial courts. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095; Akins v. Enterprise Rent-A-Car Co. (2000) 79 Cal. App. 4th 1127, 1134.) The Court “need not simply award the sum requested.” (Id.) “[R]easonableness of attorney fees is ... to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Plaintiff requests an award of $66,866.54. Plaintiff assigns $3,500.00 for reviewing an opposition, filing a reply, and appearing at the hearing on Plaintiff’s motion. The Court strikes this claim. Defendant filed no opposition and Plaintiff no reply, and Plaintiff does not distinguish the hours billable for his hearing appearance.

 

Plaintiff also requests a 35% multiplier. The Court denies the request. There was no extraordinary “delay in payment” here justifying a multiplier, as Plaintiff contends. The case has gone through the ordinary life cycle for lemon law proceedings.

 

Counsel’s declaration otherwise satisfies the Court that Plaintiff claims reasonable fees based on a reasonable hourly rate: 95.3 hours at an average rate of $472.87.

 

Plaintiff also claims reasonable, awardable costs of $2,529.46.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court awards fees of $45,064.50 and costs of $2,259.46, for a total award of $47,593.96.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ROSENDIN ELECTRIC, INC.,

 

                                  Plaintiff,

 

         v.

 

 

CORE/RELATED GRAND AVE. OWNER, LLC, et al.,

 

                                  Defendants.

 

 Case No:  23STCV11836

 

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 1, 2024

 Calendar Number:  5

 

            Defendants Core/Related Grand Ave. Owner, LLC and United States Fire Insurance Company (collectively, “Moving Defendants”) move to bifurcate trial and discovery related to alter ego allegations.

 

            The Court DENIES the motion.

 

Background

 

            Plaintiff Rosendin Electric, Inc. sued defendants Core/Related Grand Ave. Owner, LLC (“Core”); The Related Companies, L.P. (“TRC”); Deutsche Bank AG; U.S. Bank; Hanover Street Capital, LLC; and Deutsche Bank Securities, Inc. on May 25, 2023, for breach of contract, foreclosure of mechanic’s lien, quantum meruit, and violation of the prompt payment act. Plaintiff added defendant United States Fire Insurance Company in its First Amended Complaint filed July 19, 2023.  Plaintiff filed its operative second amended complaint (“SAC”) on December 1, 2023, replacing its claims for foreclosure and quantum meruit with a single cause of action for recovery against a mechanic’s lien release bond.

 

On February 5, 2024, Moving Defendants answered.  Core cross-complained against Plaintiff (“REI”) and a newly named entity, Rosendin Holdings, Inc., (together “the Rosendin Entities”) on September 28, 2023. It filed its operative second amended cross-complaint (“SAXC”) on June 3, 2024. Core asserts nine causes of action: two for breach of contract, one for restitution based on unjust enrichment, four for various species of fraud, one for contractual indemnification, one for negligence, and one for declaratory relief.

 

            The parties’ dispute arises from REI’s work on a high-rise construction project (“Project”) located at 100 S. Grand Avenue in downtown Los Angeles. REI “acted as the design-builder and engineer of the complex electrical and fire alarm systems for the Project ... .” (DMPA, 2:14-15, citing Teitelbaum Decl., ¶ 7.) REI argues it performed on the parties’ contract and Core refused to pay. Core argues it has paid REI all it is owed (more than $50 million), and REI breached the parties’ contract by providing delayed and/or inadequate work.

 

            The SAC and the SAXC both rely on alter ego theories to impose liability on certain defendants. In the SAC, REI argues Core and TRC are alter egos; in the SAXC, Core argues the same about REI and Rosendin Holdings.

 

            On August 12, 2024, Moving Defendants moved for an order “bifurcating [Plaintiff] and Core’s respective alter ego allegations from all other claims at trial as well as all discovery related thereto ... .” (Not. of Mot., 1:11-13.)

 

            Rosendin filed its opposition on September 17, 2024. On September 23, 2024, the Moving Parties replied.

 

Discussion

 

            Upon a properly noticed motion of a party made no later than the close of pretrial conference, or in other cases, no later than 30 days before the trial date, a court may bifurcate a trial into separate issues when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 598.)

 

The court may also, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, order a separate trial of any cause of action, or of any separate issue of any number of causes of action, preserving the right of trial by jury as required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (b).)¿ 

           

            The Court does not agree that bifurcation of the alter ego issues will serve judicial  economy or streamline the litigation.  To the contrary, the Court envisions that such bifurcation would make the litigation more complicated and potentially lengthy and unwieldy.

This litigation involves high dollar amounts and multiple complex issues.  Bifurcating alter ego will not make the case significantly less complicated.  Core’s bifurcation requests seeks to defer any discovery into alter ego issues until after the trial of the liability issues.  This would potentially lengthen the case by years.  It would also raise issues about what discovery is permissible in the current phase and what is not. The Court concludes that deferring certain issues is likely to delay a final resolution of this case, whether by trial or settlement.  This concern outweighs the speculative possibility that the parties could potentially save monies by not conducting discovery on alter ego issues at this time. 

 

The most practical approach to discovery is also the simplest.  While discovery is proceeding apace, it makes sense for the parties to take discovery of all factual issues.

 

            Moving Defendants make certain arguments about the conduct of the trial.  The Court cannot make a decision about how it will handle the alter ego issues at trial until the parties complete discovery and present the issues of trial management to the Court with specificity.  There are many tools available to the Court in terms of staging or managing the trial to make sure that the jury is not confused by any technical alter ego matters. 

 

            Moving Defendants’ arguments about bifurcation are not persuasive.  Moreover, the Court envisions significant complications and delays if it granted bifurcation.

 

The Court therefore denies Moving Defendants’ motion.  The denial is without prejudice to Moving Defendants raising during the pretrial proceedings the issue of how to handle the alter ego issues at trial.

 Calendar Number:  9

 

 

 

Defendants City of Los Angeles (the “City”) and Los Angeles Board of Police Commissioners (the “Board”) (collectively, “Defendants”) demur to the Complaint filed by Plaintiff Burlesque Enterprise, Inc. (“Plaintiff”). Defendants additionally move to strike portions of the Complaint relating to declaratory and injunctive relief.

 

The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to Plaintiff’s first claim for denial of due process on the grounds that the claim must be stayed and abated pending the outcome of Case No. 21STCV40709 (the “2021 Lawsuit”).  The first claim is not dismissed.  It is stayed pending the outcome of the 2021 Lawsuit.

 

The SUSTAINS the demurrer to Plaintiff’s second and third claims for freedom of expression and equal protection WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

Background

 

This is a land use case. The following facts are taken from the allegations of the Complaint, which the Court accepts as true for the purposes of the demurrer.

 

Plaintiff operates an adult entertainment business known as “Burlesque” at 13324 Sherman Way, North Hollywood, CA 91605. Burlesque has been in operation since around 1984, through multiple owners.

 

On April 30, 1987, Defendants issued Conditional Use Permit ZA 86-1011 (A) (B) (the “CUP”), approving Plaintiff’s adult entertainment presentations. The CUP allegedly permitted Plaintiff’s use of the property for the existing adult entertainment business in perpetuity. Plaintiff’s use was annually licensed by Defendants’ issuance of Los Angeles Police Commission Café Entertainment and Shows/Adult Permits (the “Police Permit”).

 

In the 1980’s, the City enacted Los Angeles Municipal Code, section 12.70 (C), which prohibits the establishment or operation of an adult entertainment business within 500 feet of any lot in an “A” or “R” zone, inter alia.

 

Plaintiff contends that it has a constitutionally protected vested fundamental right to continue adult operations under the United States and California Constitutions.

 

On November 11, 2021, Plaintiff filed Case No. 21STCV40709 (defined above as the 2021 Lawsuit) in the Los Angeles Superior Court against the City only, raising claims for (1) denial of equal protection; (2) denial of due process; (3) chilling effect on exercise of freedom of expression; (4) statutory inapplicability; and (5) imposition of cruel and unusual punishment. The operative complaint in the 2021 Lawsuit is now the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”), which raised one claim for denial of equal protection.

 

The SAC in the 2021 Lawsuit alleges that on February 6, 2020, defendants revoked Plaintiff’s Police Permit to operate Burlesque without an administrative hearing, violating Plaintiff’s due process rights. In the 2021 Lawsuit, Plaintiff seeks declaratory relief that Plaintiff’s business is not subject to Los Angeles Municipal Code, section 12.70 (C). Plaintiff also seeks an injunction against Defendants’ enforcement of that ordinance as to Plaintiff.

 

On May 10, 2023, Defendants granted Plaintiff a Zoning and Use Clearance Form which again confirmed that Plaintiff’s premises were permitted for adult entertainment.

 

On January 10, 2023, Plaintiff asked the Board to renew its Police Permit. On June 1, 2023, the Board refused to process Plaintiff’s request for renewal.

 

On August 16, 2022, the Court sustained the City’s demurrer to the SAC in the 2021 Lawsuit without leave to amend. Judgment was entered for the City on September 26, 2022. Plaintiff appealed.

 

Plaintiff filed this action on June 16, 2023, raising claims for (1) denial of due process; (2) denial of freedom of expression; (3) denial of equal protection; and (4) damages.

 

Defendants filed the demurrer and motion to strike on August 31, 2023. Plaintiff filed an opposition to each, and Defendants filed a reply to each opposition.

 

On October 11, 2023, the Court found that this case was related to the 2021 Lawsuit.

 

On November 9, 2023, the Court took notice of the judgment and appeal in the 2021 Lawsuit. Because Defendants contended in this case that the 2021 Lawsuit would have a preclusive effect on this case, the Court stayed this case pending the outcome of the appeal.

 

A notice of decision and remittitur was filed in this case on March 11, 2024 as to the appeal in the 2021 Lawsuit.

 

On April 16, 2024, the Court ordered that the parties file additional information on the issues of (1) whether this action should be stayed in light of the Court of Appeal’s decision in the 2021 Lawsuit; and (2) any preclusive effect that the Court of Appeal’s decision in the 2021 Lawsuit has on Plaintiff’s claims in this case. Both Plaintiff and Defendants filed an additional brief.

 

The Court held a further hearing on August 24, 2024 and allowed further briefing by the parties on certain preclusion issues.

 

Legal Standard

 

Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) The court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

 

Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

Discussion

 

The Court of Appeal’s Opinion

 

In the 2021 Lawsuit, the Court of Appeal ordered the Court to overrule the demurrer to the cause of action for violation of procedural due process in the First Amended Complaint and to sustain without leave to amend the demurrers to the remaining causes of action in the First Amended Complaint and SAC. (Remittitur at p. 27.) Thus, Plaintiff’s only remaining claim in the 2021 Lawsuit is the claim for violation of procedural due process.

 

On the procedural due process claim, the Court of Appeal found that Plaintiff had adequately alleged a property interest in the Police Permit. (Remittitur at p. 24.) The Court found that Plaintiff had alleged a procedural due process right to notice and an opportunity to be heard before the City revoked the Police Permit. (Remittitur at pp. 25-26.) The Court of Appeal noted that the City’s arguments that it had grounds to revoke the Police Permit did not go to the issue of whether Plaintiff had been denied procedural due process. (Remittitur at pp. 25-26.)

 

On the equal protection claim, the Court of Appeal found that Plaintiff had not alleged that it was treated differently from similarly situated persons or entities. (Remittitur at p. 15.) The Court of Appeal explained that Plaintiff had failed to allege that it was “similarly situated to the proposed comparator in all material respects.” (SmileDirectClub, LLC v. Tippins (9th Cir. 2022) 31 F.4th 1110, 1123.) The Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he ‘similarly situated’ requirement must be enforced with particular rigor in the land-use context because zoning decisions ‘will often, perhaps almost always, treat one landowner differently from another.’ ” (Cordi-Allen v. Conlon (1st Cir. 2007) 494 F.3d 245, 251.)

 

The Court of Appeal explained that, unlike Plaintiff, Plaintiff’s comparators had participated in the litigation Topanga Press, Inc, v. City of Los Angeles (February 22, 1995), Case No. CV-88-7838, United States District Court for the Central District of California (“Topanga Press”), which resulted in a stipulated order for a permanent injunction preventing the City from enforcing Los Angeles Municipal Code, section 12.70 (C) against the participating parties in that case. (Remittitur at pp. 16-17; SAC, 2021 Lawsuit ¶¶ 5, 18.) “This difference is an obvious reason the City would enforce section 12.70 against Burlesque but not against the other adult entertainment businesses Burlesque tried to compare itself to. Had the City attempted to enforce section 12.70 against the parties in the Topanga Press litigation, it might have violated a federal court order. No such limitation precluded it from enforcing section 12.70 against Burlesque.” (Remittitur at p. 17.)

 

Preclusion

 

“As generally understood, [t]he doctrine of res judicata gives certain conclusive effect to a former judgment in subsequent litigation involving the same controversy.’ The doctrine has a double aspect. In its primary aspect, commonly known as claim preclusion, it operates as a bar to the maintenance of a second suit between the same parties on the same cause of action. In its secondary aspect, commonly known as collateral estoppel, [t]he prior judgment ... operates in a second suit ... based on a different cause of action ... as an estoppel or conclusive adjudication as to such issues in the second action as were actually litigated and determined in the first action.” (Boeken v. Philip Morris USA, Inc. (2010) 48 Cal.4th 788, 797 [internal citations and quotation marks omitted.)

 

“Claim preclusion, the primary aspect of res judicata, acts to bar claims that were, or should have been, advanced in a previous suit involving the same parties.” (DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber (2015) 61 Cal.4th 813, 824 [internal quotations and citations omitted].) “Claim preclusion arises if a second suit involves: (1) the same cause of action (2) between the same parties (3) after a final judgment on the merits in the first suit.” (Ibid.) “To determine whether two proceedings involve identical causes of action for purposes of claim preclusion, California courts have consistently applied the ‘primary rights’ theory.” (Boeken v. Philip Morris USA, Inc. (2010) 48 Cal.4th 788, 797 [internal quotations and citations omitted].) “When two actions involving the same parties seek compensation for the same harm, they generally involve the same primary right.” (Id. at 798.) A dismissal ordered by a court constitutes a final judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 581d.)

 

“Issue preclusion differs from claim preclusion in two ways. First, issue preclusion does not bar entire causes of action. Instead, it prevents relitigation of previously decided issues. Second, unlike claim preclusion, issue preclusion can be raised by one who was not a party or privy in the first suit.” (DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber, supra, 61 Cal.4th at p. 824.) “In summary, issue preclusion applies: (1) after final adjudication (2) of an identical issue (3) actually litigated and necessarily decided in the first suit and (4) asserted against one who was a party in the first suit or one in privity with that party.” (Id. at p. 825.)

 

Because the order sustaining the demurrer to the procedural due process claim was reversed, the 2021 Lawsuit has not reached a final judgment. The City has filed an answer in the 2021 Lawsuit and litigation appears to be ongoing. Thus, none of the claims addressed in the 2021 Lawsuit are ripe for collateral estoppel. 

 

Notwithstanding this formal analysis of collateral estoppel, the Court of Appeal opinion and its reasoning are for practical purposes entitled to significant deference.

 

Denial of Due Process – First Claim

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action, for Denial of Due Process, arguing among other things that the cause of action must be abated and stayed pending resolution of the 2021 Lawsuit.  The Court agrees, sustains the demurrer on this ground and stays the current lawsuit until the 2021 Lawsuit is resolved.

 

“A plea in abatement pursuant to section 430.10, subdivision (c), may be made by demurrer or answer when there is another action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action.” (Plant Insulation Co. v. Fibreboard Corp. (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 781, 787 (“Plant Insulation”).) “Abatement . . . is recognized to prevent a multiplicity of suits and unnecessary vexatious litigation. (Hamm v. San Joaquin & Kings River Canal Co. (1941) 44 Cal.App.2d 47, 56.)

 

Plaintiff argues the 2021 Lawsuit does not warrant abatement because it is not between the same parties. Whereas Plaintiff sued only the City of Los Angeles in the 2021 Lawsuit, here it names both the City and the Los Angeles Board of Police Commissioners.

 

The City and the Board are not separate entities. “[G]enerally a political subdivision and the officers, boards, commissions and representatives thereof form but a single entity.” (Servente v. Murray (1935) 10 Cal.App.2d 355, 361; see also French v. Rishell (1953) 40 Cal.2d 477 [for preclusion, “immaterial” if City was a party where respondent pension board was party to prior administrative proceeding]; Pathe v. City of Bakersfield (1967) 255 Cal.App.2d 409, 415-416 [applying French].)  Plaintiff does not establish why any exception to the general rule should apply. The City and the Board are one entity for purposes of abatement.

 

Plaintiff also argues that the claims are different.  The Court disagrees. They are both claims for denial of due process in connection with the regulatory process for Plaintiff’s business.  Indeed, Plaintiff has emphasized in opposing other aspects of this demurrer that the claim at issue in the current lawsuit is integrally related to the due process claim in the 2021 Lawsuit. 

 

Moreover, “ ‘ “[A] ‘cause of action’ is comprised of a ‘primary right’ of the plaintiff, a corresponding ‘primary duty’ of the defendant, and a wrongful act by the defendant constituting a breach of that duty. [Citation.] ... .” [¶] “As far as its content is concerned, the primary right is simply the plaintiff’s right to be free from the particular injury suffered.” ’ ” ’ ” (Colebrook v. CIT Bank, N.A. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 259, 263, citations omitted.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached a primary duty to provide him due process.  The factual instances when this purportedly took place simply elaborate on his single cause of action.

 

The Court sustains the demurrer to the first cause of action on the grounds that it must be stayed and abated pending resolution of the 2021 Action.  As a result, the Court does not reach the Defendants’ other arguments as to the first cause of action.

 

Freedom of Expression – Second Claim

 

The First Amendment states that government “shall make no law... abridging the freedom of speech”. (U.S. Const., First Amend.) The Liberty and Speech Clause of the California Constitution, Article I, Section 2(a), states that, “Every person may freely speak, write and publish his or her sentiments on all subjects, being responsible for the abuse of that right. A law may not restrain or abridge liberty of speech or press.” (Cal. Const., Art. I, § 2(a).)

 

Defendants argue that zoning of adult businesses does not violate free speech and locations need not be the plaintiff’s first choice, unless sites are not otherwise ‘reasonably available’ for such speech. Where land zoned for an adult entertainment business’s usage is reasonably available, and the plaintiff need only compete on the real estate market on an equal footing with other prospective buyers and lessees, there is not a First Amendment violation. (City of Renton v. Playtime Theatres, Inc. (1986) 475 U.S. 41, 54.) Plaintiff has not alleged that other land is not reasonable available for its use.

 

The Court of Appeal decision indicates that the demurrer to this claim ought to be dismissed without leave to amend on this ground.  Plaintiff conceded at the hearing on August 15, 2024 that this was the case. 

 

The Court therefore sustains the demurrer to this claim without leave to amend.

 

Equal Protection – Third Claim

 

As explained by the Court of Appeal, a plaintiff raising an equal protection claim must show that it is “similarly situated to the proposed comparator in all material respects.” (SmileDirectClub, LLC v. Tippins, supra, 31 F.4th at p. 1123.) The Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he ‘similarly situated’ requirement must be enforced with particular rigor in the land-use context because zoning decisions ‘will often, perhaps almost always, treat one landowner differently from another.’ ” (Cordi-Allen v. Conlon, supra, (1st Cir.) 494 F.3d at p. 251.)

 

As explained by the Court of Appeal, Plaintiff is not similarly situated to the plaintiffs in the Topanga Press litigation because it did not obtain a stipulated injunction preventing the City from enforcing section 12.70 (C) against it. Plaintiff is the only entity other than the Topanga Press plaintiffs against which Plaintiff alleges that the City did not enforce the ordinance. Plaintiff therefore has not alleged a similarly situated person or entity.

 

The Court of Appeal decision indicates that the demurrer to this claim ought to be dismissed without leave to amend.  Plaintiff conceded at the hearing on August 15, 2024 that this was the case. 

 

The Court therefore sustains the demurrer without leave to amend. 

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s requests for declaratory and injunctive relief should be stricken. Defendants’ basis for this argument is that the Café Entertainment and Shows Permit (“CES Permit”), which is the permit Plaintiff applied for, does not authorize a business to operate in violation of any zoning laws.

 

This argument is not meritorious at the pleading stage. Plaintiff alleges that it had a permit that allowed it to operate its business notwithstanding Los Angeles Municipal Code, section 12.70 (C). Furthermore, the form that Plaintiff submitted contains a field in which to indicate that the applicant has a CUP permitting their usage. (Complaint, Ex. C.) Plaintiff filled out that section, indicating the number of its CUP relating to its use. Thus, reading the CES Permit application together with Plaintiff’s allegations of the CUP, Plaintiff has alleged a basis on which it has a right to continue operating its business.

 

The Court therefore denies the motion to strike.

            This is an employment suit. Plaintiff George Rahme sued defendants The Bicycle Casino, L.P. and Hashem Minay on July 10, 2023. He now moves to file an operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). Defendants do not oppose.

 

 A party may file an amended pleading once without leave of the court at any time before a demurrer or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed as long as it is served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 472(a), 430.40(a).) Outside that timeframe, a party must obtain leave of the court to amend its pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)

 

The court may grant leave to amend a pleading, in its discretion, “upon any terms as may be just,” if it finds that permitting the amendment would be “in furtherance of justice,” and that the adverse party was noticed. (Ibid.) The court may grant leave to amend at any stage of the proceeding, even “after commencement of trial.” (Id., § 576; see Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489.)

 

Pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, a motion to amend a pleading must:

 

(1)  Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

 

(2)  State what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

 

(3)  State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)

 

A separate declaration must also accompany the motion and must specify:

 

(1)  The effect of the amendment;

 

(2)  Why the amendment is necessary and proper;

 

(3) When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

 

(4)  The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

 

(Id., subd. (b).)

 

            As an initial matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff served the motion on all interested parties electronically, through their counsel, on August 23, 2024. Notice and service comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 and 1010.6. Defendants were given proper notice and filed no opposition.

 

            As for the applicable law: the exhibits to Plaintiff’s motion satisfy Rule 3.1324(a). (Yenoki Decl., Exh. 1 [redline], Exh. 2 [clean proposed FAC].) The motion and counsel’s accompanying declaration satisfy Rule 3.1324(b). The minimal proposed amendments account for “multiple changes with [sic] ownership of the casino and the companies who managed operations over the course of plaintiff’s 37 year[] [employment]”; those changes are still being explored during discovery. (See Yenoki Decl., ¶ 2.)

 

            Plaintiff’s unopposed motion is granted. Plaintiff ordered to electronically file a clean FAC within ten (10) days of this order.

AIDS HEALTHCARE FOUNDATION,

 Hearing Date:  October 1, 2024

 Calendar Number:  8

 

            Defendant Aids Healthcare Foundation moves to strike certain allegations and prayers from relief from Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

            The Court DENIES Defendant’s motion to strike.

 

Background

 

            Plaintiff George Handy sued defendant Aids Healthcare Foundation (“AHF”) on April 15, 2024. He filed his operative first amended complaint (“FAC”) on July 24, 2024. In his FAC, Plaintiff asserts eight causes of action arising from Plaintiff’s tenancy at the residential property located at 501 S. Los Angeles St., Los Angeles, CA 90013 (“the Property”).

 

            As alleged in the complaint and accepted as true for purposes of Defendant’s motion to strike (paragraph citations to the FAC): Plaintiff lived in two different units on the Property pursuant to residential leases executed in 2019 and 2023, respectively. (¶ 9.) Defendant owned, managed, or leased the Property at all relevant times. (¶ 2.)

 

            Defendant maintained both of Plaintiff’s units in untenantable condition. The conditions Plaintiff endured during his tenancy include, but are not limited to, (1) leaking so severe that it caused holes in Plaintiff’s ceiling (¶ 14); (2) bedbug infestation so severe that the insects “infested the entire unit, including the carpet, and their carcasses could be found lying around” (¶ 17); and (3) numerous code violations, including fire safety violations, that went unaddressed despite citations from City agencies (¶ 20).

 

            Plaintiff alleges, in short, “that Defendants are slum lords whose goal is to maximize their income by preying on persons who do not have the power or ability to protect their rights.” (¶ 66.)

 

            On August 23, 2024, Defendant moved to strike two paragraphs from Plaintiff’s complaint and a portion of his prayer for relief. Plaintiff filed his opposition on September 17, 2024. On September 23, 2024, Defendant replied.

           

Legal Standard

 

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer, such as words, phrases, and prayers for damages. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436, and 437.) The proper procedure to attack false allegations in a pleading is a motion to strike. (Id. § 436 (a).)

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435 [notice of motion to strike whole or part of complaint], or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Id. § 436 (a).) Irrelevant matters include immaterial allegations that are not essential to the claim or those not pertinent to or supported by an otherwise sufficient claim. (Id., § 431.10.) The court may also “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id. § 436 (b).)

 

Discussion

 

            Meeting and Conference

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 requires the party filing a motion to strike to meet and confer with its opponent before filing. Defendant’s counsel filed a declaration that satisfied section 435.5. (Dahl Decl., ¶¶ 2-4.)

 

            Punitive Damages

           

Defendant’s motion is directed to Plaintiff’s allegations regarding Defendant’s liability for punitive damages (¶¶ 48, 62) and to Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages (Compl., 21:17).

 

A motion to strike is the proper procedure to attack a purportedly improper remedy such as unjustified punitive damages. (Venice Town Council, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1547, 1561-1562.) A complaint including a request for punitive damages must also include allegations showing that the plaintiff is entitled to such an award. (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

 

California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where … the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice … .” (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)

 

For a corporate defendant, the oppression, fraud or malice “must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (b).)  That requirement can be satisfied “ ‘if the evidence permits a clear and convincing inference that within the corporate hierarchy authorized persons acted despicably in “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” ’  [Citation.]”  (Morgan v. J-M Manufacturing Company, Inc. (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 1078, 1090.) 

 

Plaintiffs’ FAC contains allegations sufficient to ascribe oppression to Defendant’s officers. Oppression requires no evil motive to inflict a specific harm; it requires only a knowledge that the victim’s rights are being violated, and continuation of the violative behavior. (Cf. Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1286-1287 [“[p]unitive damages are appropriate if the defendant’s acts are … in blatant violation of law or policy].) A landlord that abuses its power over a residential tenant may be liable for punitive damages. (See Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1055-1056; cf. Yurick v. Superior Court (1989) 209 Cal. App. 3d 1116, 1129 [power disparity contributes to oppression in employment context] Agarwal v. Johnson (1979) 25 Cal. 3d 932, 946-947 [same].)

 

Here, the FAC characterizes the substandard conditions at the Property as part of an intentional business practice that Defendant uses to maximize its profits at the expense of vulnerable tenants. (¶ 66.) Although Plaintiff does not identify a specific managing agent and ascribe them a specific state of mind, Plaintiff alleges enough facts which, if true, support that Defendant engaged in a widespread practice known to its corporate decisionmakers. This is sufficient, for pleading purposes, to allege Defendant’s officers’ conscious disregard for its tenants’ rights.

 

            The motion is denied. Defendant to give notice.

            Defendant Nancy M. Chein demurs to and moves to strike portions of plaintiff Express Restoration Corp.’s complaint.

 

            The Court OVERRULES the demurrer. The Court DENIES the motion to strike.

 

Background

 

            Plaintiff Express Restoration Corp. sued defendant Nancy M. Chien on April 18, 2024 for (1) breach of contract, (2) work, labor, and materials furnished, and (3) unjust enrichment.

 

            Plaintiff alleges it performed construction work at seven (7) properties owned by Defendant. (Compl., ¶ 7; see also id., Exhs. A-H [copies of contracts].) Defendant allegedly did not pay Plaintiff for its services, and Plaintiff seeks to recover approximately $250,000 in damages.

 

            On August 12, 2024, Defendant demurred to and moved to strike certain portions of Plaintiff’s complaint. On September 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed its oppositions. On September 23, 2024, Defendant replied.

 

/ / /

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendant requests judicial notice of Exhibit 1 to its demurrer, which purports to be Plaintiff’s current license record printed from the Contractors State License Board website. The Court denies the request, as it is not based on a certified government record. Even if the Court granted Defendant’s request, it could not take notice of the facts within the document, which remain inadmissible hearsay. And even if the Court could take notice of the facts within the document, the fact that Plaintiff was licensed between 2021 and 2025 does not establish it was otherwise unlicensed when it performed work for Defendant.

             

            The Court denies the request.

 

Discussion

 

            Meet and Confer

 

            Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.41 and 435.5 require a moving party to meet and confer with its opponent before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, respectively. Counsel submitted a form declaration satisfying the meet and confer requirements. (08-12-2024 Zuetel Decl., ¶ 2(a) [Form CIV-140].)

 

            Demurrer

 

            Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) When considering a demurrer, a court reads the allegations stated in the challenged pleading liberally and in context, and “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

            Defendant demurs to all three of Plaintiff’s causes of action, arguing for each that Plaintiff fails to state a claim or has not alleged its cause of action with sufficient certainty.

 

            Defendant relies on the assertion that Plaintiff did not have a contractor’s license when it performed work on her properties. The Court has declined to take notice of the relevant records.  The matter falls outside the four corners of the complaint and noticeable materials.  Accordingly, the demurrer lacks merit.

 

            Motion to Strike

 

            The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) As with a demurrer, the grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

            The Court denies Defendant’s motion to strike for the same reason the Court overruled the demurrer. Defendant seeks to “strike all paragraphs for which Plaintiff is barred [from recovering] by the Contractors’ State License Law”. (Mot., 3:18-19.) As discussed above, Defendant has not established that any such bar appears on the face of the complaint or in judicially noticeable materials.
