[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff 1525 Colorado, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this quiet title action against Maniben Corp., et al. regarding the property commonly known as 1525 Colorado Boulevard, Los Angeles, CA 90041. The verified complaint sets forth five causes of action for (1) To Quiet Title To Express Easement; (2) To Quiet Title To Equitable Easement; (3) For Enforcement Of Easement Pursuant To CC § 809; (4) Nuisance; and (5) Declaratory Relief. 

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed motions to compel defendants Maniben Corp. (“Maniben”) and Girish R. Patel (“Patel”) (collectively, “Defendants”) to provide further responses to Request for Production of Documents (“RPDs”), Set One, in addition to seeking monetary sanctions. 

On September 23, 2024, Defendants filed an omnibus opposition. 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply. 

 

II. PROCEDURAL 

  1. Meet and Confer Requirement¿ 

A motion¿to compel further responses “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration.”¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(2).)¿ The declaration must state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented in the motion.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.) 

The Court finds that Plaintiff met the requirements of Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(2). 

  1. Separate Statement¿ 

A motion to compel further responses requires a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a).)¿¿ 

The Court finds that Plaintiff properly filed a separate statement for each motion to compel further responses. 

  1. Informal Discovery Conference 

Pursuant to this Court’s department rules, the parties are expected and encouraged to informally resolve disputes. If an informal resolution is not reached after meeting and conferring, then either party may request that the court conduct an Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) for the purpose of discussing discovery matters in dispute between the parties. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.080.) 

On August 23, 2024, the parties participated in the IDC, wherein the following were established: (1) the Court’s ruling will be limited to RFAs 12-16, and (2) Defendants to provide full and complete responses to all RFAs (except 12-16) and to all RFPs (except 19-25) by September 9, 2024. (Min. Order, Aug. 23, 2024.) 

 

III. DISCUSSION 

CCP § 2031.310(a) states, “On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:¿(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.¿(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” 

CCP § 2031.310(a) states, “On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” 

Here, Defendants were to provide full and complete responses to all RFPs except for Nos. 19-25. There being no arguments concerning non-compliance, the Court presumes that any disputes unrelated to Nos. 19-25 are now moot. The Court therefore limits its review to Nos. 19-25 only. 

The call of the requests and Defendants’ responses are identical. The Court will therefore address them collectively. 

Request No. 19: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO any hearings the East Los Angeles Planning Commission held that RELATE TO the PROJECT.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 19: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Request No. 20: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO the Letter of Determination, RELATING TO the PROJECT, issued by the East Los Angeles Planning Commission on or about August 27, 2019.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 20: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Request No. 21: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO the Los Angeles City Council that RELATE TO the PROJECT.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 21: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Request No. 22: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO any prior California Superior Court action that RELATE TO the PROJECT.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 22: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Request No. 23: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO any prior writ of mandate that RELATE TO the PROJECT.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 23: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Request No. 24: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO any proceeding in the Courts of Appeal of the State of California that RELATE TO the PROJECT.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 24: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Request No. 25: “Any and all DOCUMENTS RELATING TO any City of Los Angeles – Department of Building and Safety “Application for Building Permit and Certificate of Occupancy” that RELATE TO the PROJECT.” 

Defendants’ Response to No. 25: “Objection: Relevance; the subject matter of the request is neither relevant not likely to lead to any relevant evidence and therefore is not the subject of discovery. Defendant will produce any non-privileged responsive documents that exist and are in its possession.” 

Plaintiff avers that despite Defendants having agreed to produce documents related to these requests, Defendants have not produced any of those documents. Defendants argue that the requests are not relevant, and that the City’s decision has no impact on an easement dispute between landowners. 

Here, in terms of Defendants’ objections on the grounds of relevance, the Court has reviewed the complaint in this action and cannot say that the requests are wholly irrelevant. “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action,” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) Defendants, according to their responses, had agreed to produce any responsive non-privileged documents that exist and are in their possession and should therefore do so, if the documents have still yet to be produced. 

   

IV. CONCLUSION 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendants to provide further responses to RPDs, Set One, as to Nos. 19-25 are GRANTED. Defendants are ordered to provide further responses to these requests within twenty (20) days. 

Sanctions are mandatory against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (CCP § 2031.310(h).)¿Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions, but fails to provide his rate, support for the rate, or any calculation for the Court to determine whether the request for monetary sanctions is reasonable. Simply stating the time spent and the total amount sought is insufficient; the Court declines to award any uncalculated or unsupported requests for sanctions. 

 

Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

Plaintiff Marta Elvira Rodriguez Capacho (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Nuevo San Miguel Restaurant Group Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 20 for damages arising from her employment with Defendant as a waitress. Plaintiff sets forth eight causes of action for (1) failure to pay wages, (2) failure to pay minimum wages, (3) failure to pay overtime compensation, (4) failure to pay meal and rest periods, (5) failure to pay itemized wage and hour statements, (6) waiting time penalties, (7) failure to permit inspection of personnel and payroll records, and (8) unfair competition. 

 

Plaintiff seeks default judgment against Defendant, and she has properly dismissed all unnamed Doe defendants. (Req. for Dismissal, June 21, 2024.) 

 

This matter was previously heard on July 18, 2024, wherein the Court would conditionally grant default judgment upon Plaintiff submitting a new CIV-100 which completed mandatory Item 8. On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed a new CIV-100 curing the defect. 

 

Additionally, on September 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed supplemental briefing to support the interest calculation. The Court grants the application for court judgment as follows: 

 

  1. Principal Damages 

 

Plaintiff seeks $44,886.12 in principal damages, as pled in the complaint, for the following: Unpaid minimum wage compensation ($8,196.00), unpaid overtime compensation ($4,180.96), payment of meal and rest period compensation ($23,187.76), damages pursuant to Labor Code §226 ($4,000.00), waiting time penalties pursuant to Labor Code §§201-203 ($4,571.40), and statutory penalties pursuant to Labor Code §1198.5 ($750.00). 

 

Plaintiff sufficiently supports the $44,886.12 in principal damages requested, and it is therefore granted. 

 

  1. Interest 

 

Plaintiff seeks prejudgment interest in the amount of $2,250.90 based on 10% per annum of the principal damages. The application for default judgment was initially heard on August 23, 2024, and Plaintiff’s only objection was to this Court’s interest calculation. Plaintiff was given leave to submit further briefing to demonstrate that she is entitled to prejudgment interest on the entirety of the principal amount. On September 3, 2024, Plaintiff provided supplemental briefing on the interest calculation. 

 

The Court amends its prior tentative, and grants Plaintiff $2,250.90 sought in interest. 

 

  1. Attorney Fees 

 

Plaintiff seeks $8,531.50 in attorney fees. However, Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees is greater than that listed in the Los Angeles Superior Court, Local Rule 3.214 fee schedule. “An application for a fee greater than listed in the foregoing schedule because of extraordinary services must include an itemized statement of the services rendered or to be rendered.”¿(Local Rule 3.214 (d).) Although Plaintiff’s counsel provides an itemized statement of services, Plaintiff’s counsel has not demonstrated that the work performed on this case was extraordinary within the meaning intended by Local Rule 3.214(d). [TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING UNOPPOSED MOTION TO STRIKE WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND 

 

I. BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Isaiah Mcgrath, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Gloria Johnson, (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Los Angeles Unified School District, New Designs Charter School, and Does 1 to 50 for damages arising from a slip and fall at Plaintiff’s school located at 1342 W Adams Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90007. The complaint sets forth two causes of action for general negligence and premises liability. Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District has been dismissed from the action. 

Defendant New Designs Charter School (“Defendant”) now moves to strike the prayer for “Other damage: Incidental damages. Other damages are unknown at this time which Plaintiff will prove at trial. Loss of society, love, protection, companionship, consortium, and other related injuries and damages.” 

Any opposition was due on or before September 23, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date. 

 

II. PROCEDURAL REQUIREMENT 

Before filing a motion to strike, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the motion to strike and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5(a).)¿ 

Counsel declares she spoke with Plaintiff’s counsel on June 20, 2024 on the issue. (Harada-Orosz Decl. ¶ 7.) As of the filing of the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel has not agreed to amend the complaint or dismiss the incidental and consortium damages. (Id. ¶ 10.)  

The Court finds the Defendant has fulfilled this requirement prior to filing the motion.¿¿ 

 

III. DISCUSSION 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿ The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) 

Defendant contends incidental damages are not supported for this slip and fall case, because incidental damages are damages resulting from the seller’s breach. Additionally, Defendant argues the loss of consortium damage is improper, because Plaintiff is a minor and there was injury to no other person except for minor Plaintiff. 

Indeed, this slip and fall case does not involve a breach of contract for incidental damages to be applicable. Further, Plaintiff is the only plaintiff to this action. There is no other plaintiff alleging to have a valid and lawful marriage with minor Plaintiff at the time of injury for there to be a derivative claim for loss of consortium. (LeFiell Mfg. Co. v. Superior Court (2012) 55 Cal.4th 275, 284-285.) Furthermore, Plaintiff did not oppose the motion. A failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion. (CRC, rule 8.54(c).) 

 

IV. CONCLUSION 

Because Plaintiff did not oppose the motion, Plaintiff necessarily fails to meet his burden to show in what manner the complaint can be amended to change the legal effect of the pleading due to the lack of opposition. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349; Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.) 

Therefore, Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s complaint is GRANTED without leave to amend.
