DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV24483    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

October 31, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV24483

MOTION

Motion for Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTIES

Plaintiff Aggreko LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant the Little Cottage Caregivers LLC

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Aggreko LLC (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant the Little Cottage Caregivers LLC (“Defendant”) for breach of contract.  Plaintiff alleges it leased equipment to Defendant and Defendant has failed to pay $63,740.40 which is the amount outstanding on the lease agreement.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 8-11.)  Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication on the first cause of action for breach of contract in the complaint, which contains four causes of action.  Defendant opposes the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. . . . There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (Aguilar).)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

Where the plaintiff seeks summary adjudication, the plaintiff bears the burden to produce admissible evidence on each element of every cause of action at issue.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) 

EVIDENCE

            Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to paragraphs 3, 4, and 6 in the declaration of Shlomo Meiri are sustained.  Plaintiff’s other evidentiary objections are overruled.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff propounded written discovery on Defendant, in which Plaintiff asked Defendant to state all facts on which Defendant bases its claim Defendant does not owe Plaintiff the principal sum of $63,740.40.  In response, Defendant stated it lacked responsive information.  (Declaration of Timothy Carl Aires, Exhibits H-J.)  Defendant’s factually devoid discovery responses shift the burden to Defendant to raise triable issues of material fact as to whether Defendant owes the principal sum Plaintiff demands.  (See Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.)

In opposition, Defendant advances the declaration of Sholmo Meiri (“Meiri”), who owns a 50-percent stake in Defendant.  Meiri presents an email he sent that memorializes an agreement between Meiri and Robert Jones, a salesperson for Plaintiff.  In that email, Meiri states Plaintiff agreed Defendant owed $41,129 in total, less $22,536 that had already been paid, for an outstanding balance of $18,593.  (Declaration of Shlomo Meiri, Exhibit C.)  This agreement raises triable issues of material fact as to the amount Defendant owes Plaintiff. 

            In reply, Plaintiff argues “there is nothing left to try” because Defendant admits Plaintiff leased equipment to Defendant and Defendant only partially paid for the lease.  (See Reply, p. 1.)  Plaintiff is incorrect.  The amount of damages Defendant owes to Plaintiff is a triable issue of material fact, which precludes summary adjudication.  The motion for summary adjudication is, therefore, denied.

Plaintiff also argues Meiri’s declaration is vague and Meiri fails to show any representative of Plaintiff responded to the email Meiri sent.  These arguments go to the weight, not the admissibility, of Defendant’s evidence.  The Court cannot weigh evidence on summary adjudication.  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 856.)

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication is denied.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

23STCV26831

MOTION

Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Feria Tjhai

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant Topocean Consolidation Service (Los Angeles) Inc.

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Feria Tjhai (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Defendant Topocean Consolidation Service (Los Angeles) Inc. (“Defendant”) to Requests for Production of Documents, set one (“RPD”), Form Interrogatories—General, set one and Form Interrogatories—Employment, set one (“FROG”), and Special Interrogatories, set two (“SROG”).  Defendant opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories or requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, 2031.310.)

After Plaintiff filed these motions on June 21, 2024, on October 11, 2024 Defendant supplemented its responses to all the requests at issue.  (See Defendant’s Opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Document Production , p. 3; Defendant’s Opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories, p.3.)  Accordingly, the motions are moot except with respect to sanctions. 

The Court finds Defendant’s failure to supplement its discovery responses until after Plaintiff filed these motions, despite multiple extensions of time to do so, constitutes an abuse of the discovery process, and assess sanctions against Defendant. 

Therefore, the Court imposes sanctions against Defendant and its counsel of record, Ballard Rosenberg Golper(See  & Savitt, LLP, jointly and severally, in the amount of $3,045, which represents five hours of attorney time to prepare the motions and attend the hearing at $585 per hour, plus the $60 filing fees for the motions.  Defendant and its counsel of record, Ballard Rosenberg Golper & Savitt, LLP, are to pay sanctions in the amount of $3,045 to Plaintiff, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 24STCV08647    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

October 31, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

24STCV08647

MOTION: 

Demurrer to Complaint

MOVING PARTY:

Defendants Tishbees LLC and Eliot Tishbi

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Zachary Selvin (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Tishbees LLC and Eliot Tishbi (“Defendants”) regarding a commercial lease dispute.  Plaintiff leased commercial premises from Defendants and alleges Defendants obtained an unlawful detainer judgment against Plaintiff’s business (The Living Earth Wellness Center) but agreed to allow Plaintiff to maintain possession of the premises; The Living Earth Wellness Center then entered into a lease of the premises with a third party, to which Defendants consented provided monthly rent was paid; but Defendants thereafter proceeded to enforce the unlawful detainer judgment obtained against The Living Earth Wellness Center and locked out Plaintiff and the third party.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 6-10.)  Plaintiff further alleges the sublessee obtained a temporary restraining order, which prohibited Defendants from locking the third party and Plaintiff, but Defendant ignored this order and changed the locks. (Id., ¶¶ 16-17.) 

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s complaint.  Plaintiff has not filed an opposition to the demurrer.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

First Cause of Action: Conversion

To state a claim for conversion, Plaintiff must allege he owned personal property, and Defendants interfered with Plaintiff’s rights to that personal property in a manner that caused damages.  (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119.)  In this regard, Plaintiff alleges Defendants took “all of Plaintiff’s personal property and load[ed] it into a big white truck and drove it away.  Millions of dollars of inventory were stolen, and not returned, thousands of dollars in the cash registers were stolen and never returned to Plaintiff.”  (Complaint, ¶ 22.)  These allegations state a claim for conversion.  Therefore, the demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

Second Cause of Action: Negligence

In support of his negligence claim, Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ “legal duties were to comply to not steal, takeaway, or mishandle any personal property belonging to the tenant . . . .”  (Complaint, ¶ 26.)  These are not, by themselves and without explanation, legal duties to use reasonable care “so as not to create an unreasonable risk of injury to others,” which could support a claim for negligence.  (McGarry v. Sax (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 983, 994.)  As such, Plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for negligence.  Thus, the Court sustains the demurrer to the second cause of action with leave to amend.

Third and Fourth Causes of Action: Violations of Business and Professions Code Sections 17200 and 17204

 

Plaintiff must allege “with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation” of these provisions.  (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)  For example, Plaintiff neither identifies the particular portions of the statutes which were violated nor describe the manner in which Defendants’ acts were unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent.  (Ibid.)  In this regard, Plaintiff fails to specify the particular acts by Defendants which Plaintiff contends support Plaintiff’s causes of action under Business and Professions Code sections 17200 and 17204.  As such, Plaintiff fails to allege these causes of action with particularity.  Therefore, the Court sustains the demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ demurrer to the second, third, and fourth causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint is sustained with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 10 days of notice of this order.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
