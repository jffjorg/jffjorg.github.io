DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV01955    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Claudia B. Cruz and Alejandro Cruz,      

Plaintiffs,

 

   v.

 

Hyundai Motor America, et al.

          Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

24VECV01955         107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendant Hyundai Motor America (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration of the claims brought by Plaintiffs Claudia B. Cruz and Alejandro Cruz (“Plaintiffs”).  This court denies the motion.

 

I. JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendant requests the court take judicial notice of Plaintiff’s complaint. The court need not take judicial notice of a complaint that is part of the case file.

Thus, Defendant’s request for judicial notice is denied.

II. BACKGROUND

On September 25, 2019, Plaintiffs acquired a 2019 Hyundai Santa Fe (the “Vehicle”) from a dealership, Keyes Hyundai. (Complaint ¶ 7; Ex. 2.) Later, the Vehicle developed various “defects, malfunctions and nonconformities.” (Complaint ¶ 15.) Plaintiffs took the Vehicle to Defendant’s authorized repair facilities, but Defendant failed to repair or replace the Vehicle, or make restitution to Plaintiffs. (Complaint ¶¶ 14-15.)

 

On April 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants Hyundai Motor America, Autonation Hyundai Carlsbad dba HYG Country Drive Imports, Inc. and Does 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act Breach of Express Warranty, Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act Breach of Implied Warranty, Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200, and Negligent Repair.

 

On June 4, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel arbitration. Plaintiff filed an opposition on August 27, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on September 25, 2024.

 

IV. DISCUSSION

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) “[T]he right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.” (Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Inv. Brokerage Co. v. Hock Inv. Co. (1998) 68 Cal. App.4th 83, 88.)

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, the party opposing the petition then bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to demonstrate that there should be no enforcement of the agreement, and the trial court sits as a trier of fact to reach a final determination on the issue. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413; Provencio v. WMA Securities, Inc., 125 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1031.) The court is empowered by California Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.)

   

“California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act, including a presumption in favor of arbitrability.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971–72.) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.” (Coast Plaza Doctors Hospital v. Blue Cross of California (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 677, 686.) Notwithstanding that strong policy, the United States Supreme Court has held, “[A]rbitration is a matter of contract and a party cannot be required to submit to arbitration any dispute which he has not agreed so to submit.” (AT & T Technologies, Inc. v. Communications Workers of America (1986) 475 U.S. 643, 648.)

 

The hearing and determination of a petition to compel arbitration “in the manner and upon the notice provided by law for the making and hearing of motions” generally means that “the facts are to be proven by affidavit or declaration and documentary evidence with oral testimony taken only in the court's discretion.” (Sonic-Calabasas A, Inc. v. Moreno (2013) 57 Cal.4th 1109, 1157.)

 

  1. Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

 

The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence. (Rosenthal v. Great Western Financial Securities Corp., supra, 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff must arbitrate their dispute because the retail installment sales contract (“RISC”) contains an arbitration provision. (Motion p. 3.)

 

On September 25, 2019, Plaintiffs purchased the Vehicle from Keyes Hyundai. (Complaint ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs’ transaction was subject to the RISC, executed by Plaintiff and Keyes Hyundai. (Ex. 2; Ameripour Declaration ¶ 4.) The RISC contains an arbitration provision, which states, in pertinent part:

 

Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors, or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract, or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by court action. (Ex. 2 p. 5.)

 

This provision indicates that disputes arising from Plaintiffs’ purchase or condition of the Vehicle are covered by the RISC’s arbitration provision. However, Keyes Hyundai is not a defendant in this case and Defendant was not a party to the RISC. Accordingly, the court must determine whether Defendant may compel Plaintiff to submit to arbitration.

 

  1. Non-Signatory

 

“As a general rule, only a party to an arbitration agreement may enforce the agreement.” (Felisilda v. FCA USA LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486, 495.) “However, there are several exceptions that allow a nonsignatory to invoke an agreement to arbitration.” (Id.)

 

                 i.          Equitable Estoppel

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs are equitably estopped from refusing to arbitrate their claims. (Motion pp. 6-7.)

 

“Under the doctrine of equitable estoppel, ‘as applied in both federal and California decisional authority, a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are ‘intimately founded in and intertwined with the underlying contract obligations.’” (Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486) “By relying on contract terms in a claim against a nonsignatory defendant, even if not exclusively, a plaintiff may be equitably estopped from repudiating the arbitration clause contained in that agreement.” (Id. at p. 496) “Where the equitable estoppel doctrine applies, the nonsignatory has a right to enforce the arbitration agreement.” (Id.) To determine whether plaintiffs’ claims are founded on or intimately connected with the sales contract, the court examines the “facts of the operative complaint.” (Id.)

 

The parties here dispute which cases this court should follow. Plaintiffs argue this court should follow Ford Motor Warranty Cases v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, review granted Sept. 4, 2024, S279969 (“Ochoa”) and Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 958, 973, review granted Sept. 30, 2023, S279969.  Defendant argues that because the Ochoa line of cases are under review by the California Supreme Court, “Plaintiffs reliance on these cases is improper” and this court should follow Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486.

 

“Pending review and filing of the Supreme Court’s opinion, unless otherwise ordered by the Supreme Court . . . a published opinion of a Court of Appeal in the matter has no binding or precedential effect, and may be cited for potentially persuasive value only.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(e)(1).  In granting review of Ochoa, the Supreme Court’s order noted that “Pending review, the opinion of the Court of Appeal, which is currently published at 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, 306 Cal.Rptr.3d 611, may be cited, not only for its persuasive value, but also for the limited purpose of establishing the existence of a conflict in authority that would in turn allow trial courts to exercise discretion under Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 456 . . . to choose between sides of any such conflict.”  (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(e)(3).)

 

In Felisilda, the plaintiffs purchased a vehicle and later filed a complaint against the dealership and manufacturer. (Felisilda v. FCA US LLC, supra, 53 Cal.App.5th at p. 489.) The plaintiffs had signed a retail installment sales agreement that included an arbitration provision. (Id.) The dealership filed a motion to compel arbitration and the superior court ordered plaintiffs to arbitrate their claims against the dealership and manufacturer. (Id.) Plaintiffs dismissed the dealership and later appealed, arguing the superior court erred by ordering plaintiffs to arbitrate their claim against the manufacturer because the manufacturer was a nonsignatory to the sales contract. (Id.) On appeal, the Third District of the California Court of Appeal upheld the order submitting the matter to arbitration. (Id.) The court noted that “a nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are ‘intimately founded in and intertwined’ with the underlying contract obligations.” (Id. at p. 495.) The arbitration agreement provision stated that “[a]ny claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise ... between you and us ... which arises out of or relates to ... [the] condition of this vehicle ... shall ... be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action.” (Id. at p. 496.) The court then held that the plaintiffs’ claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act “directly relates to the condition of the vehicle that they allege to have violated warranties they received as a consequence of the sales contract” and “[b]ecause the [Plaintiffs] agreed to arbitrate claims arising out of the condition of the vehicle – even against third party nonsignatories to the sales contract – they are estopped from refusing to arbitrate their claim.” (Id. at p. 497)

 

In Ochoa, the plaintiffs purchased vehicles from a dealership. (See Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at p. 1329.) The plaintiffs signed retail installment sale contracts with the dealership that stated:

 

EITHER YOU OR WE MAY CHOOSE TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN US DECIDED BY ARBITRATION AND NOT IN COURT OR BY JURY TRIAL. [and] [a]ny claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claims or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase, or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who did not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action.” (Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 1330.)

 

The manufacturer was not a party to the contracts. (Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 1329.) The plaintiffs filed complaints against the manufacturer alleging various causes of action including violations of implied warranties under the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. (Id. at p. 1330.) The court disagreed with Felisilda and held that equitable estoppel does not apply when the “manufacturer vehicle warranties that accompany the sale of motor vehicles without regard to the terms of the sale contract between the purchaser and the dealer are independent of the sale contract.” (Id. at p. 1334.) The court noted that the sales contracts contained “no warranty, nor any assurance regarding the quality of the vehicle sold, nor any promise of repairs or other remedies in the event problems arise. To the contrary, the sale contracts disclaim any warranty on the part of the dealers, while acknowledging no effect on ‘any warranties covering the vehicle that the vehicle manufacturer may provide.’ In short, the substantive terms of the sale contracts relate to sale and financing and nothing more.” (Id. at p. 1335) The court stated that “California law does not treat manufacturer warranties imposed outside the four corners of a retail sale contract as part of the sale contract” and concluded that equitable estoppel did not apply because “Plaintiffs’ claims in no way rely on the sale contracts.” (Id.)

 

Similarly, in Montemayor, the plaintiffs purchased a vehicle from a dealership and executed a retail installment sales contract. (Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co., supra, 92 Cal.App.5th at p. 962.) Later. Plaintiffs filed a complaint alleging six causes of action against the manufacturer and one cause of action against the manufacturer and dealership. (Id. at pp. 962-963.)  The manufacturer and dealership jointly filed a motion to compel arbitration. (Id. at p. 964.) The plaintiffs later dismissed the dealership. (Id. at p. 965.) On appeal, the court noted that “the fact the [plaintiffs] purchased the defective vehicle from [the dealership] pursuant to the sales contract, and as a result of their purchase they received separate express warranties from [the manufacturer], does not mean their causes of action against [the manufacturer] based on those express warranties are founded in the sales contract.” (Id. at p. 971.) The court disagreed with Felisilda that language referencing third parties “provides a basis for nonsignatory manufacturers to compel arbitration of claims brought by vehicle purchasers” because the language referencing third parties “refers to the subject matter of covered claims, not the scope of who may enforce the arbitration provision.” (Id.) Finally, the court noted that although the plaintiffs in Ochoa only sued the dealership, the plaintiffs in Montemayor sued the dealership and manufacturer. (Id. at p. 972.) But the court stated that “whether vehicle purchasers file suit against only the manufacturer, or the manufacturer and the car dealer, does not affect the analysis of whether a cause of action against the manufacturer may be compelled to arbitration.” (Id.)

 

Recent decisions from the First, Third, and Fourth District Courts of Appeal agree with Ochoa and reject Felisilda’s holding that equitable estoppel applies to enable a non-signatory manufacturer to enforce an arbitration provision in a vehicle sale contract.¿ See Kielar v. Superior Court (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, 619-620, review granted Oct 25, 2023, S279969; Yeh v. Superior Court (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 264, 272, review granted Nov. 15, 2023, S279969; Davis v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 825, 837, review granted May 29, 2024, S279969.

 

Because the majority of recent decisions from the Courts of Appeal have declined to follow Felisilda and instead adopted Ochoa, this court will follow the Ochoa reasoning.

 

As applied here, the arbitration provision in the RISC is identical to the arbitration provision in Ochoa. (Ex. 2 p. 5) (See Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at p. 1329.) Further, like the sales contracts in Ochoa the RISC here stated that “the Seller makes no warranties, express or implied, on the vehicle, and there will be no implied warranties of merchantability or of fitness for a particular purpose. This provision does not affect any warranties covering the vehicle that the vehicle manufacturer may provide.” (Ex. 2 p. 4.) Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ causes of action do not rely on the RISC.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs are equitably estopped from refusing to compel arbitration because “there would be no claims under Song-Beverly but for the existence of the sale transaction which was created by and through the RISC.” (Motion p. 10.) But the court in Montemayor rejected the “but for” argument stating that, “[t]o be sure, the [Plaintiffs] would not have sued [Defendant] for the defective condition of the vehicle but for the sale of the vehicle . . . pursuant to the sales contract . . . [b]ut that does not mean [Defendant’s] obligation to provide a non-defective vehicle under its separate express warranty is in any way founded on an obligation imposed by the sales contract or intertwined with those obligations.” (Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co., supra, 92 Cal.App.5th at p. 970.) Similarly, this court rejects Defendant’s argument.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs are not estopped from refusing to arbitrate their claims against Defendant.

 

               ii.          Third-Party Beneficiary

 

Defendant further argues it can enforce the arbitration provision in the RISC as a third-party beneficiary. (Motion p. 5.)

 

The elements necessary to establish a third-party beneficiary relationship are (1) the third party “is likely to benefit from the contract;” (2) “a motivating purpose of the contracting parties [was] to provide a benefit to the third party;” and (3) permitting the third party to enforce the contract is consistent with the objectives of the contract and the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties.¿ (Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 830.)¿ “Whether the third party is an intended beneficiary or merely an incidental beneficiary involves construction of the intention of the parties, gathered from reading the contract as a whole in light of the circumstances under which it was entered.”¿ (Cione v. Foresters Equity Servs. (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 625, 636, citation omitted.)

 

Here, the RISC shows that Defendant stood to benefit from the RISC because it would receive indirect profits from the dealership’s sale of the vehicle.

 

But the RISC does not show that the parties had a motivating purpose to directly benefit Defendant. The RISC’s arbitration provision is limited to Plaintiffs and Keyes Hyundai and its “employees, agents, successors, or assigns.” (Ex. 2 p. 5.) Defendant argues that the arbitration provision’s reference to any “relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract” should be interpreted to include Defendant because Plaintiffs’ claims “arise out of and relate to the condition of the Vehicle and the ‘resulting’ warranty relationship that arose out of the execution of the RISC.” (Motion p. 9.) But, like Montemayor, where the court concluded the manufacturer was not a third-party beneficiary to the sales contract, the RISC here does not reference Defendant and nothing in the contract indicates Defendant had an interest in the RISC. (See Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co., supra, 92 Cal.App.5th at p. 973.)

 

Further, permitting Defendant to enforce the contract is not consistent with the contract’s objectives and the contracting parties’ reasonable expectations. The RISC shows that the Plaintiff and Keyes Hyundai intended to purchase and sell a vehicle and resolve disputes between Plaintiff, Keyes Hyundai, and Keyes Hyundai’s “employees, agents, successors, or assigns.” (Ex. 2. p. 5.) (See Ochoa v. Ford Motor Co., supra, 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, 1338-1339 (finding “no indication that a benefit to [Defendant] was the signatories’ ‘motivating purpose’ [citation] in contracting for the sale and purchase of a . . . vehicle” because “[t]he manifest intent of the parties was to buy, sell and finance a car, and to allow either the purchaser or the dealer to compel arbitration of the specified categories of disputes between them, or between the purchaser and any of the dealer’s ‘employees, agents, successors, or assigns.’”) The agreement here does not reference Defendant, and nothing suggests permitting Defendant to enforce the contract is consistent with Plaintiffs’ or Keyes Hyundai’s objectives and their reasonable expectations when signing the RISC.

 

Accordingly, this court will not enforce the arbitration agreement under the third-party beneficiary theory.

 

Because there is no agreement to arbitrate, the court need not consider the parties’ unconscionability arguments. However, even if the court considered those arguments, the court finds that although the purported arbitration agreement contains a small degree of procedural unconscionability, it is not substantively unconscionable. That analysis is set forth below.

 

 

  1. Unconscionability

 

The doctrine of unconscionability applies to arbitration agreements. (Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.) Civil Code section 1670.5, subdivision (a) provides, “If the court as a matter of law finds the contract or any clause of the contract to have been unconscionable at the time it was made the court may refuse to enforce the contract.”

 

“Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element, the former focusing on ‘oppression’ or ‘surprise’ due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on ‘overly harsh’ or ‘one-sided’ results. The prevailing view is that procedural and substantive unconscionability must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability. But they need not be present in the same degree. The more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” (Peng v. First Republic Bank, supra, 219 Cal.App.4th at p. 1469, internal citations omitted, emphasis original.) “Ordinary contracts of adhesion, although they are indispensable facts of modern life that are generally enforced, contain a degree of procedural unconscionability.” (Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1244.)

 

i.       Procedural Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the agreement is procedurally unconscionable because it was presented on a “take it or leave it basis.” (Opposition p. 3.)

 

“A procedural unconscionability analysis ‘begins with an inquiry into whether the contract is one of adhesion.” (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho, (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 116.) “An adhesive contract is standardized, generally on a preprinted form, and offered by the party with superior bargaining power ‘on a take-it-or-leave-it basis.” (Id.) Procedural unconscionability also “focus[es] on oppression and surprise due to unequal bargaining power.” (Swain v. LaserAway Medical Group, Inc. (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 59, 69.) “[O]pression occurs where a contract involves lack of negotiation and meaningful choice, surprise where the allegedly unconscionable provision is hidden within a prolix printed form.” (Id.)

 

Here, the agreement was part of a standardized, pre-printed form that Plaintiffs accepted while purchasing the Vehicle. (Ex. 2.) Accordingly, the agreement is an adhesion contract. Further, it appears that the dealership had superior bargaining power because, without signing the RISC, Plaintiff would not have been able to purchase the Vehicle. However, the arbitration agreement is within a five-page documents, and it is relatively short and written in plain language. (Ex. 2 p. 5.)

 

Accordingly, the RISC arbitration agreement contains a small degree of procedural unconscionability.

 

ii.     Substantive Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the RISC’s arbitration agreement are substantively unconscionable because: (1) it deprives Plaintiffs the choice of arbitrator; (2) it improperly shifts fees and costs of arbitration onto Plaintiffs; and (3) it deprives Plaintiffs the right to a jury trial. (Opposition p. 4.)

 

“Substantive unconscionability examines the fairness of a contract’s terms.” (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho, supra, 8 Cal.5th at p. 129.)  “This may include fine-print terms, unreasonably or unexpectedly harsh terms regarding price or other central aspects of the transaction, and terms that undermine the nondrafting party’s reasonable expectations.” (Id.)  

 

The arbitration agreement states that “[a]rbitrators shall be attorneys or retired judges and shall be selected pursuant to the applicable rules.” (Ex. 2 p. 5.) Plaintiffs argue this is unconscionable because it “deprives Plaintiffs the choice of arbitrator” but Plaintiffs do not explain how this provision makes the arbitration agreement substantively unconscionable. (Opposition p. 4.)

 

The arbitration agreement also states that Defendant “shall pay [Plaintiffs’] filing, administration, service or case management fee and [Plaintiffs’] arbitrator or hearing fee all up to a maximum of $5000, unless the law or the rules of the chosen arbitration organization require [Defendant] to pay more. The amount [Defendant] pay[s] will be reimbursed in whole or part by decision of the arbitrator if the arbitrator finds that any of [Plaintiffs’] claims [are] frivolous under applicable law.” (Ex. 2 p. 5.)  In Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 908, the arbitration provision stated “We will advance your filing, administration, service or case management fee all up to a maximum of $2500, which may be reimbursed by decision of the arbitrator at the arbitrator’s discretion,” [e]ach party shall be responsible for its own attorney, expert and other fees, unless awarded by the arbitrator under applicable law” and “[t]he appealing part requesting new arbitration shall be responsible for the filing fee and other arbitration costs subject to a final determination by the arbitrators of a fair apportionment of costs.”  The Court concluded the arbitration agreement was not unconscionable.  (Id. at p. 921.) Here, Defendants have agreed to pay fees up to $5,000, unless the arbitrator finds Plaintiffs’ claims are frivolous.  Accordingly, this court finds that the fee provisions here are less unconscionable than the provisions in Sanchez.

 

Finally, Plaintiffs’ argument that the arbitration agreement is unconscionable because it deprives Plaintiffs’ of a right to a jury trial is meritless because “arbitration involves a waiver of the right to a jury trial.” (Prima Donna Development Corp. v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 174, 186.)

 

Accordingly, although the arbitration agreement contains a small degree of procedural unconscionability, it is not substantively unconscionable.

 

  1. Conclusion

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration.

 

Further, this court DENIES Defendant’s motion to stay the matter until the California Supreme Court issues its rulings on the Ford Motor Warranty Cases. (Reply p. 9.)

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On or about August 27, 2021, Plaintiffs Nvard Oganesyan and Satenik Kazaryan (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) leased a 2021 Jeep Wrangler (the “Vehicle”). (Complaint ¶¶ 8-9.) In connection with the lease, Plaintiffs received written warranties and other express and implied warranties. (Complaint ¶ 11.) The Vehicle developed several defects. (Complaint ¶ 14.) Plaintiffs took the Vehicle to the Manufacturer’s repair facilities, but the defects continued to exist. (Complaint ¶¶ 15-16.)

 

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint against Defendants alleging four causes of action under the Song-Beverly Warranty Act.

 

  1. Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted

 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff Nvard Oganesyan (“Plaintiff”) served her first set of Requests for Admission on Defendant FCA US LLC (“Defendant”). (Markarian Declaration, RFAs ¶ 3.) Defendant’s responses were due July 26, 2024. (Markarian Declaration, RFAs ¶ 4.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, RFAs ¶ 5.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant additional time to provide responses. (Markarian Declaration, RFAs ¶ 6; Ex. B.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, RFAs ¶ 7.)

 

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to deem RFAs admitted. No opposition has been filed.

 

  1. Motion to Compel Responses to Interrogatories

 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff served her first set of Form Interrogatories – General and Special Interrogatories on Defendant. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶¶ 4-5.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 5.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant additional time to provide responses. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 5; Ex. C.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories, ¶¶ 6-7.)

 

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for order compelling response to interrogatories.  No opposition has been filed.

 

  1. Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents

 

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff served her first set of Request for Production of Documents on Defendant. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 3.) Defendant’s responses were due by July 26, 2024. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 4.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 5.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant additional time to provide responses. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 6.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 7.)

 

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for order compelling response to request for production of documents. No opposition has been filed.

 

II.             DISCUSSION

 

a.     Meet and Confer

 

There is no requirement parties meet and confer before bringing a deemed admitted motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280. (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 777.) Further, “[u]nlike a¿motion¿to¿compel¿further¿responses, a¿motion¿to¿compel¿responses is not subject to a 45–day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a ‘meet¿and¿confer’ requirement.” (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)

 

Because no response has been provided to the instant discovery requests and Plaintiff files motions to compel, not motions to compel further, there is no requirement for the parties to have met and conferred prior to the filing of the motions.¿ Regardless, Plaintiff submitted a declaration by Plaintiff’s counsel showing counsel emailed Defendant and offered Defendant additional time to file responses before filing these motions. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 6; Markarian Declaration, RFAs ¶ 6; Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 5.)

b.     Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted

 

i.       Deem Admitted¿ 

 

Plaintiff argues the court should grant the motion because “Defendant has to date not provided any responses to requests for production.” (Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted p. 3.)

 

When a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the requests, unless the court grants them relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a)(1)-(2).)¿  

 

The court shall grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)¿

 

Here, Plaintiff served RFAs, Set One, on Defendant on June 24, 2024. (Markarian Declaration ¶ 3; Ex. A.) Defendant’s responses were due by July 26, 2024, but Defendant did not respond to Plaintiff’s RFAs, Set One. (Markarian Declaration ¶¶ 4-5.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant until August 9, 2024, to provide responses. (Markarian Declaration ¶ 6; Ex. B.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming Plaintiff’s RFAs, Set One, admitted.

             

ii.               Monetary Sanctions

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks $6,097.50 in monetary sanctions. (Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted at p. 6; Markarian Declaration ¶ 8.) This includes four and a half hours preparing the motion and its supporting documents, two hours reviewing the opposition, two hours preparing the reply, and two hours commuting to and from attending the hearing, and the $60.00 motion filing fee. (Markarian Declaration ¶ 8.)

  

The court finds Plaintiff’s failure to respond to Defendants’ discovery requests a misuse of the discovery process.  The court is also required to impose a monetary sanction on Plaintiff for failure to respond to the Requests for Admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c).¿ However, the amount sought is excessive given the simplicity of this Motion and the lack of opposition and reply. Thus, the Court finds a reduction of time spent preparing the motion is warranted and reduces the four and a half hours preparing the motion to two hours. The court also finds the time spent reviewing Defendant’s opposition brief and preparing Plaintiff’s reply is not warranted because Defendant filed no opposition and Plaintiff filed no reply. The court also finds two hours commuting to and from attending the hearing is excessive.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant is granted in the reduced total amount of $1,785.00, reflecting the $60 filing fee, two hours for preparing the motion, and one hour for the hearing at $575.00 per hour.

 

c.      Motion to Compel Responses to Interrogatories

 

i.       Failure to Respond 

 

Plaintiff argues the court should grant is motion because “Defendant has to date not provided any responses to interrogatories.” (Motion for Order Compelling Response to Interrogatories p. 3.)

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [that party] waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.290.) “The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.290(b).) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.290(a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories – General and Special Interrogatories on Defendant on June 24, 2024. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶¶ 3-4.) Defendant did not respond. (Markarian declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 5.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant until August 9, 2024, to respond. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 5; Ex. C.) Defendant has provided no response to the interrogatories. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 6.)

 

Accordingly, the court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses.

 

ii.               Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).)

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks $6,097.50 in monetary sanctions. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 8; Motion for Oder Compelling Response to Interrogatories pp. 5-6.) This includes four and a half hours preparing the motion and its supporting documents, two hours reviewing the opposition, two hours preparing the reply, two hours to commute to and from and to attend the hearing, and the $60.00 motion filing fee. (Markarian Declaration, Interrogatories ¶ 8.)

 

The court finds Defendant did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.  The court also finds Defendant’s failure to respond to Plaintiff’s discovery requests a misuse of the discovery process.  The court also finds a reduction of time spent preparing the motion is warranted and reduces the four and a half hours requested to two hours. The court also finds the time spent reviewing Defendant’s opposition brief and preparing Plaintiff’s reply is not warranted because Defendant filed no opposition and Plaintiff filed no reply. Also, this motion will be heard at the same hearing as Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted, so the court finds that granting Plaintiff attorney’s fees for time spent commuting to and from the hearing and attending the hearing would be duplicative.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanction is granted in the reduced about of $1,210.00, reflecting the $60.00 filing fee and two hours for preparing the motion at $575.00 per hour.

 

d.     Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents

 

i.       Failure to Respond

 

Plaintiff argues the court should grant the motion because “Defendant has to date not provided any responses to requests for production.” (Motion for Order Compelling Response to Request for Production of Documents p. 3.)

 

“I]f a party to whom¿a¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]…[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the¿demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.300.) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that . . . (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff served Defendant with her first set of Request for Production of Documents on June 24, 2024. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 3.) Defendant’s responses were due July 26, 2024, but Defendant did not respond. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶¶ 4-5.) On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel emailed Defendant’s counsel and offered Defendant until August 9, 2024, to respond. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 6; Ex. B.) Defendant has provided no response to Plaintiff’s request. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 7.)

 

Accordingly, the court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses.

 

A.    Sanctions

 

“[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(c).)

 

Plaintiff seeks $6,097.50 in monetary sanctions. (Motion for Order Compelling Response to Request for Production of Documents pp. 5-6; Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 8.) This includes four and a half hours working on preparing the motion and its supporting documents, two hours reviewing the opposition, two hours preparing the reply, two hours to commute to and from attending the hearing, and the $60.00 motion filing fee. (Markarian Declaration, Production ¶ 8.)

 

The court finds Defendant did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.  The court also finds Defendant’s failure to respond to Plaintiff’s discovery requests a misuse of the discovery process.  The court also finds a reduction of time spent preparing the motion is warranted and reduces the four and a half hours requested to two hours. The court also finds the time spent reviewing Defendant’s opposition brief and preparing Plaintiff’s reply is not warranted because Defendant filed no opposition and Plaintiff filed no reply. Also, this motion will be heard at the same hearing as Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted, so the court finds that granting Plaintiff attorney’s fees for time spent commuting to and from the hearing and attending the hearing would be duplicative.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanction is granted in the reduced about of $1,210.00, reflecting the $60.00 filing fee and two hours for preparing the motion at $575.00 per hour.

 

III.           CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted as to Defendant is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Response to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s request for sanctions as to Defendant is also granted in the amount of $4,205.00 to be paid by Defendant’s counsel to Plaintiff’s counsel within thirty (30) days of notice of this order.
