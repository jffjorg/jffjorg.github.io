DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV03726    Hearing Date: August 8, 2024    Dept: 73

8/08/2024 

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

YVONNE E. HALLADA v. NISSAN NORTH AMERICA, INC. (22STCV03726)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Tionna Carvalho (Strategic Legal Practices, APC)

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party: Amir Nassihi (Shook, Hardy & Bacon LLP)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION (filed 02/28/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court reconsiders the January 5, 2023 ruling. Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On January 31, 2022, Plaintiff Younghyeon Lee filed this action against Defendant Nissan North America, Inc. alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(d); 

2.      Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(b); 

3.      Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(a)(3); 

4.      Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability; and

5.      Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On May 7, 2021, Plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with Defendant regarding a 2020 Nissan Pathfinder vehicle identification number 5N1DR2BN4LC636334 (“Vehicle”) which was manufactured and or distributed by Defendant. (Complaint ¶ 9.)  The Subject Vehicle has an express written warranty and had nonconformities that arose during the warranty period.  (Id. ¶¶ 10-14, Ex. A.)  These defects “substantially impair the use, value or safety of the [Subject] Vehicle.”  (Id. ¶ 15.)  Defendant was unable to conform the Subject Vehicle to the applicable express warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts.  (Id. ¶ 17.) In addition, the sale of the Subject Vehicle was accompanied by an implied warranty of merchantability. (Id. ¶ 39.)  However, these warranties were breached due to the defects with the electrical, transmission, and engine.  (Id. ¶ 14.)   

 

On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Reconsideration arguing:

·        Ochoa constitutes a change in law warranting reconsideration of the Court’s January 5, 2023 order granting Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration.

·       The Court’s January 5, 2023 order granted arbitration based on applying the Felisilda case. However, the Court’s ruling is counter to the holdings in Kielar, Ochoa, Montemayor, and Yeh.

 

Defendant did not file an opposition.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of the following:

1.      Third District Court of Appeal’s response to a Petition for Writ of Mandate in Ortiz et al. v. Superior Court of Sacramento County (C099135); notice sent to Superior Court of Sacramento County;

2.      Third District Court of Appeal’s response to a Petition for Writ of Mandate in Campos v. Superior Court of Butte County (C098848); notice sent to Superior Court of Butte County;

3.      The Minute Order in Dina C. Felisilda, et al. v. FCA US, LLC, et al. (Sacramento County Superior Court case 34-2015-00183668-CU-BC-GDS) on signatory dealership Elk Grove Auto Group’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Action.

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)¿¿ 

 

Plaintiff’s unopposed Requests for Judicial Notice are GRANTED.  

 

B.      Review by a Different Judge

 

As an initial matter, the Court addresses whether a judge who did not make the initial ruling can consider this motion.

 

Generally, a motion for reconsideration must be made to the same judge.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a); Marriage of Oliverez (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1248.)  “However, there are narrow exceptions to this general rule.”  (Marriage of Oliverez, supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at 1248.)  “‘[W]here the judge who made the initial ruling is unavailable to reconsider the motion, a different judge may entertain the reconsideration motion.’”  (Id. (quoting Ziller Electronics Lab GmbH v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1222, 1232) (alteration in original).)  “Another exception is when the facts have changed or when the judge has considered further evidence and law.”  (Id.)  “Additionally, a second judge may reverse a prior ruling of another judge if the record shows that it was based on inadvertence, mistake, or fraud.”  (Id. at 1248-49.)  “Mere disagreement . . . with the prior trial judge’s ruling, however, is not enough to overturn that ruling.”  (Id. at 1249.)   

 

Here, Plaintiff moves for reconsideration of the January 5, 2023 order made in this Department, Department 73, by Judge Timothy Dillon. However, Judge Dillon is unavailable to hear the instant Motion due to his temporary reassignment to another Department. Therefore, the Court finds that the assigned judge to Department 73, Judge Rolf Treu, may consider this motion.

 

C.      Legal Standard for Motion for Reconsideration

 

Code Civ. Proc., section 1008 states: 

a.                   When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.¿The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.¿ 

… 

c.                   If a court at any time determines that there has been a change of law that warrants it to reconsider a prior order it entered, it may do so on its own motion and enter a different order. 

 

A court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon “new or different facts, circumstances or law.”¿(Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1499.) Motions for reconsideration are restricted to circumstances where a party offers the Court some fact or circumstance not previously considered, and some valid reason for not offering it earlier.¿(Id.)¿¿¿ 

 

An appellate decision published during an action’s pendency may be a change of law under section 1008, subdivision (c), and requires a trial court to reconsider its earlier ruling if the decision materially changed the law. (Valdez v. Himmelfarb (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1261, 1276.) A change in the law is always an appropriate basis, up until final judgment is entered, for changing an interim order. (State of California v. Superior Court (Flynn) (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 94, 101.) 

 

A trial court may exercise its inherent jurisdiction to reconsider an interim ruling.¿(Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1096-1097.)¿ The trial court is specifically authorized to exercise such inherent jurisdiction to reconsider orders compelling arbitration.¿(Malek v. Blue Cross of California (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 44, 59–60.) 

 

D.     Timeliness

 

Plaintiff moves for reconsideration of the Court’s January 5, 2023 order granting Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration and stay action on the grounds that there is a change of law that warrants reconsideration. 

 

On January 5, 2023, the Court relied on Felisilda¿v. FCA US LLC¿(2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486 to determine that Defendant was a third-party beneficiary to the sale agreement between Plaintiff and the Dealership. On April 4, 2023, the Second Appellate District of the California Court of Appeal reached a decision contrary to Felisilda in Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company (Ford Motor Warranty Cases) (2023) 89 Cal. App. 5th 1324, review granted July 19, 2023, S279969 (“Ochoa”). Moreover, several other appellate decisions on this issue have followed Ochoa, including Montemayor v. Ford Motor Co. (2023) 92 Cal. App. 5th 958, review granted September 20, 2023, S281237; Kielar v. Superior Court of Placer County (2023) 94 Cal. App. 5th 614, review granted October 25, 2023, S281937; and Yeh v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2023) 95 Cal. App. 5th 264, review granted November 15, 2023, S282228. Plaintiff argues the law found in Ochoa and these new cases, which specifically addresses the reasoning in Felisilda, justifies reconsideration of the motion.  

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 subdivision (a) limits the time in which a party may file a motion for reconsideration. Subdivision (a) of Section 1008 does not give Plaintiff the authority to move for reconsideration more than ten days after the order where there is a change of law. (See Advanced Building Maintenance v. State Comp Ins. Fund (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1388, 1392 [finding the trial court’s denial of the motion for reconsideration correct where the motion was served more than 10 days after service of written notice of entry of the order].)

 

However, Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 subdivision (c) gives the court authority to sua sponte reconsider a ruling where there is a change in law at any time. A court may consider a number of factors in determining whether to exercise its discretion, including the importance of the change of law, the timing of the motion, and the circumstances of the case. (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 96, 107.) Here, the parties have not completed arbitration, a final judgment has not been entered, and the Court finds the change in law warrants reconsideration of its prior order.  

 

The Court notes that Ochoa did not overrule Felisilda, but created a split of authority. Under the doctrine of stare decisis, superior courts are bound by all published decisions of the Court of Appeal, but where there is a split in authority, the superior court may choose which appellate decision to follow. (Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57 Cal.2d 450, 456.)  In the cases under review, the California Supreme Court has ruled that the cases can be cited to establish “the existence of a conflict in authority” and that trial courts can “choose between sides of any such conflict.”  As explained further below, the Court follows the holdings in Ochoa, Montemayor, Kielar and Yeh.

 

Accordingly, the Court reconsiders its prior ruling on the motion to compel arbitration pursuant to its own authority.  

 

E.      Compel Arbitration

 

Plaintiff urges the Court to follow Ochoa and reverse the January 5, 2023 order compelling arbitration. Both Felisilda and Ochoa discuss a nonsignatory vehicle manufacturer’s right to invoke the arbitration provision of a sales contract entered into between a plaintiff buyer and a nonparty dealership.  

 

In Felisilda, the Court of Appeal held that a vehicle buyer who signed an arbitration provision as part of her vehicle sales contract with the dealer may be compelled to arbitrate her claim against an automobile manufacturer, even though the manufacturer was a non-signatory to the sales contract containing the relevant arbitration provision. (Felisilda, supra, 53 Cal.App.5th at pp. 496-497.) The Felisilda Court reasoned that where the buyer expressly agreed to arbitrate claims arising out of the condition of the vehicle, even against third party non-signatories to the sales contract, and where the buyer’s claim against the manufacturer relates to the condition of the vehicle that allegedly violated the contract’s warranty, the buyer is estopped from refusing to arbitrate. (Ibid.)¿ 

 

However, on April 4, 2023, the Second Appellate District of the California Court of Appeal reached a decision contrary to Felisilda in Ochoa. In Ochoa, the Court of Appeal expressly declined to follow Felisilda, finding that equitable estoppel should not apply to compel arbitration in such circumstances because “manufacturer vehicle warranties that accompany the sale of a motor vehicle without regard to the terms of the sale contract between the purchaser and the dealer are independent of the sale contract.” (Ochoa, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at p. 1334.) The Ochoa Court held that because the plaintiffs alleged breaches of warranties in violation of the Song-Beverly Act, rather than violations of the sales contracts’ express terms, the agreement to arbitrate in the sales contract did not estop the plaintiff from refusing to arbitrate breach of warranty claims against the manufacturer, who was not a party to the sales contract.¿ (Id. at p. 1335-1336 (“California law does not treat manufacturer warranties imposed outside the four corners of a retail sale contract as part of the sale contract”).)

Additionally, on June 26, 2023, the Second District Court of Appeal decided the case Montemayor, in which the Court also rejected Felisilda’s holding that equitable estoppel applies to enable a non-signatory manufacturer to enforce an arbitration provision in a sales contract like the contract at issue in this case. (Montemayor, supra, 92 Cal. App. 5th 958.)¿On August 16, 2023, the Third District also joined the Second District’s holdings in Ochoa and Montemayor and concluded in Kielar that equitable estoppel does not apply when the court examined an identical arbitration clause in a financing contract. (Kielar, supra, 94 Cal. App. 5th 614.) Further, on September 6, 2023, the First District Court of Appeal held in Yeh that the plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly Act claims are not founded in a similar sales contract “because . . . they did not allege violations of the lease agreement or RISC or sue on any warranties in the agreements, but instead seek to recover based upon [defendant’s] statutory obligations.” (Yeh, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 278.)

Plaintiff argues the Court should follow the decisions in Ochoa, Montemayor, Kielar and Yeh and reverse the January 5, 2023 order compelling arbitration. The Ochoa court ultimately found that “manufacturer vehicle warranties that accompany the sale of motor vehicles without regard to the terms of the sale contract between the purchaser and the dealer are independent of the sale contract.” (Ochoa, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 1334.) The Ochoa court proceeded to distinguish Felisilda by reading the “third party” language in the arbitration provision “as a further delineation of the subject matter of claims the purchasers and dealers agreed to arbitrate” rather than the parties’ consent to arbitrate claims with nonsignatories to the agreement. (Id. at 1334–1335 [emphasis in original].) Thus, the Ochoa court found that the plaintiffs’ claims did not rely on the sales contracts with the dealership, and therefore equitable estoppel did not apply. (Id. at 1336.) Similarly here, as in Ochoa, “the sale contracts include no warranty, nor any assurance regarding the quality of the vehicle sold, nor any promise of repairs or other remedies in the event problems arise,” and Plaintiff does not otherwise allege any violation of the Sales Contract’s express terms. (Ochoa, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 1335.) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant concerns the express manufacturer warranty issued by Defendant, which is independent of and not “intimately founded in and intertwined with” the Sales Contract. Accordingly, equitable estoppel does not apply to allow Defendant to compel arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims. 

 

Further, the Ochoa court discussed the issue of third-party beneficiary standing, and found that “the sale contracts reflect no intention to benefit a vehicle manufacturer under Goonewardene.” (Ochoa, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 1338.) The court further asserted that “if the signatories had intended to benefit [the defendant], such a purpose would have been easy to articulate,” and the contract’s reference to “third parties” concerns only “what may be arbitrated, not who may arbitrate.” (Id. at 1339 [emphasis in original].) 

 

Here, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that Defendant is not an intended third-party beneficiary of the Sales Contract.¿ Any assertion that Defendant may “incidentally or remotely” benefit from the contract, without a further showing of the parties’ specific motivation to provide Defendant with a benefit under the Sales Contract, does not render Defendant a third-party beneficiary to the contract. (Ochoa, supra, 89 Cal.App.5th at 1336.) 

 

The Court follows the reasoning set forth in Ochoa, and notes that if the parties to the Sales Contract had intended to benefit Defendant, they could have easily articulated as such in the contract. (Id. at 1339.) Here, as in Ochoa, “the parties’ choice of the subject of the disputes they agree to arbitrate does not evince an intention to benefit nonparties so as to affect who is entitled to compel arbitration.” (Ibid.) Here, the contractual language is unambiguous that only Plaintiff or the nonparty dealership may invoke the arbitration agreement. For these foregoing reasons, the Court finds that Defendant is not an intended third-party beneficiary to the Sales Contract, and therefore cannot compel Plaintiff’s claims to arbitration. 

 

The Court notes that Defendant did not file an opposition. “A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion or demurrer is meritorious.”¿ (Id.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.” (Id.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.” (Id.)

 

In sum, the Court finds that equitable estoppel does not apply to allow Defendant to compel arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims and Defendant is not an intended third-party beneficiary to the Sales Contract. ¿ 

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, the Court reconsiders the motion to compel arbitration under its own authority in light of Ochoa v Ford Motor Company (Ford Motor Warranty Cases) (2023) 306 Cal.Rprt.3d 611. (See Code Civ. Proc., section 1008, subd. (c).) Upon reconsidering the Court ruling on Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration, the Court denies the motion to compel arbitration

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating he sent Plaintiff’s counsel correspondence on December 18, 2023 detailing the issues raised by the demurrer and that the parties further discussed these issues through additional correspondence. (Pearse Decl., ¶ 2.) An agreement was not reached between the parties. (Ibid.) Although the moving party did not meet its obligations under CCP § 430.41 as the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, the Court continues on the merits.

D.     Second Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

The elements for a breach of fiduciary duty cause of action are “the existence of a fiduciary relationship, its breach, and damage proximately caused by that breach.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 604.)

 

Fiduciary or confidential relationships give rise to a fiduciary duty, that is, a duty “to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.” (Bacon v. Soule (1912) 19 Cal.App. 428, 434.) “ ‘Technically, a fiduciary relationship is a recognized legal relationship such as guardian and ward, trustee and beneficiary, principal and agent, or attorney and client ... whereas a “confidential relationship” may be founded on a moral, social, domestic, or merely personal relationship as well as on a legal relationship.’” (Richelle L. v. Roman Cath. Archbishop (2003) 106 Cal. App. 4th 257, 271.)

 

A confidential relation may exist where there is no fiduciary relation. (Vai v. Bank of America (1961) 56 Cal.2d 329, 337–338.) The existence of a confidential relationship generating a fiduciary duty is a question of fact. Nonetheless, because of “[t]he vagueness of the common law definition of the confidential relation that gives rise to a fiduciary duty, and the range of the relationships that can potentially be characterized as fiduciary,” the “essential elements” have been distilled as follows:

“ ‘1) The vulnerability of one party to the other which 2) results in the empowerment of the stronger party by the weaker which 3) empowerment has been solicited or accepted by the stronger party and 4) prevents the weaker party from effectively protecting itself.’ ” (Richelle L., supra, 106 Cal.App.4th at p. 272.) A key factor in the existence of a fiduciary relationship lies in control by a person over the property of another. (Apollo Cap. Fund, LLC v. Roth Cap. Partners, LLC (2007) 158 Cal. App. 4th 226, 246.)

 

Defendants argue the SAC does not contain allegations showing a fiduciary or confidential relationship existed. Defendants argue there is no indication that their work was anything beyond providing consulting and accounting services in relation to Plaintiff’s dissolution of marriage.

 

Plaintiff asserts that unlike an attorney-client relationship, an accountant-client relationship may not create fiduciary duties as a matter of law, however, a fiduciary duty can arise depending on what happened in the course of that relationship. Plaintiff argues that here, Defendants owed Plaintiff a fiduciary duty because they acted as his agents, confidants and provided professional services analogous to an attorney, as they created, drafted, and provided numerous documents to Plaintiff for filing in the dissolution case. (SAC, ¶¶ 29-34.) Plaintiff asserts the SAC alleges Defendants received confidential information that they were entrusted to handle, in line with their professed professional expertise, concerning Plaintiff’s dissolution action, creating a confidential relationship. (Ibid.) The SAC alleges “Sweeney was repeatedly provided with specific instructions by Carroll to act on Carroll’s behalf in a specific manner concerning the Fraudulent Expense Reimbursements, subject to Carroll’s control.” (SAC, ¶ 32.) Plaintiff contends that accountants may be fiduciaries as a matter of law, but the authorities Plaintiff relies on are not binding. (Wolf v. Super. Ct., 107 Cal. App. 4th 25, 40 (citing the concurring and dissenting opinion); Kanani v. Frost, Ruttenberg & Rothblatt, P.C., 2005 WL 1661521 at *4; Gemstar Ltd. v. Ernst & Young, 185 Ariz. 493, 505 (1996); Concha v. London, 62 F. 3d 1493, 1501-1502 (1995).)

 

Here, the SAC fails to allege Defendants had control over Plaintiff’s property. Whether a fiduciary relationship exists is generally determined by the ability to control the property of another. (Persson v. Smart Inventions, Inc., (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1161.) Since the SAC is devoid of allegations that Defendants had control over the Plaintiff’s money or property, Plaintiff has not sufficiently pled a fiduciary relationship.

 

In addition, the SAC fails to allege that Plaintiff was in a vulnerable position in his relationship with Defendants. “In short, vulnerability ‘is the necessary predicate of a confidential relation,’ and the law treats it as ‘absolutely essential.’” (Persson, 125 Cal.App.4th at 1161 [citation omitted]). “The vulnerability that is the necessary predicate of a confidential relation ... usually arises from advanced

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

age, youth, lack of education, weakness of mind, grief, sickness, or some other incapacity ... In the absence of evidence of any similar vulnerability or incapacity, we decline to extend the scope of fiduciary obligations.” (Id. at 1162). Plaintiff argues the SAC alleges that Plaintiff was vulnerable in connection with the engagement because he lacks education as to the professional services rendered by Defendants—Plaintiff was relying on Defendants’ professed expertise. However, relying on Defendants’ professional services that Plaintiff purchased, creates a contractual obligation between the parties as opposed to placing Plaintiff in a vulnerable position. As the court noted in Zumbrun v. University of Southern California (1972) 25 Cal.App.3d 1, 13, “[t]he mere placing of a trust in another person does not create a fiduciary relationship” and “an agreement to communicate one's knowledge, exercising his special knowledge and skill in the area of learning concerned, does not create a trust but only a contractual obligation.” Plaintiff fails to show how his relationship with Defendants was more than a contractual obligation for services. Therefore, the Court does not find the SAC has sufficiently pled that Defendants owed Plaintiff a fiduciary duty.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

E.      Third Cause of Action: Fraud - Concealment

 

Defendants argue the SAC’s third cause of action fails because Plaintiff has failed to plead all the elements for fraudulent concealment.

 

“The required elements for fraudulent concealment are: (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.” (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.)  

 

Here, the SAC alleges that: (1) Defendants suppressed and concealed the material fact that they conceded Fraudulent Expense Reimbursements; (2) Defendants were under a duty to disclose all material facts related to their fiduciary relationship with Plaintiff; (3) Defendants must have known they conceded the expense reimbursements, but knowingly failed to tell Plaintiff intending to further their own financial personal interests; (4) Plaintiff justifiably relied on that fraud, reasonably believing that the Fraudulent Expense Reimbursements were not conceded against his instructions because he reasonably expected professionals he hired to act as instructed; and (5) Plaintiff was damaged because he was unable to discover the unauthorized concession until it was too late—in the midst of trial when his ex-wife’s counsel did. (SAC, ¶¶ 39-46.) Plaintiff argues that these allegations are sufficient to plead fraudulent concealment.

 

However, as discussed above, Plaintiff has failed to show that Defendants owed Plaintiff a fiduciary duty. “[T]o establish fraud through nondisclosure or concealment of facts, it is necessary to show the defendant ‘was under a legal duty to disclose them.’” (OCM Principal Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 835, 845.) Thus, the SAC’s allegation that Defendants owed Plaintiff a duty to disclose all material facts because they were Plaintiff’s fiduciaries is insufficient to support Plaintiff’s fraud cause of action.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

F.       Fifth Cause of Action: Quasi-Contract

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s quasi-contract cause of action fails because it is the same as the previous unjust enrichment claim and it cannot be simultaneously pled to a breach of contract claim.

 

As the Court previously found in sustaining Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint, Plaintiff cannot state a cause of action for unjust enrichment because in California, there is no separate cause of action for unjust enrichment. (See Rutherford Holdings LLC v. Plaza Del Rey (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 221, 231; Levine v. Blue Shield of California (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1117, 1138.)

 

Defendants first assert that Plaintiff’s quasi-contract cause of action fails because “Plaintiff simply relabeled [the Complaint’s unjust enrichment claim] as a quasi-contract claim even though such is still synonymous with unjust enrichment.” (Oppos., p. 13.) However, courts have found that quasi-contract actions can be based on unjust enrichment. (California Medical Ass’n, Inc. v. Aetna U.S. Healthcare of California, Inc. (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 151.) Yet, “as a matter of law, a quasi-contract action for unjust enrichment does not lie where, …, express binding agreements exist and define the parties’ rights.” (Id., at 172.) “A plaintiff may not…pursue or recover on a quasi-contract claim if the parties have an enforceable agreement regarding a particular subject matter.” (Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1388.)

 

Here, Plaintiff never alleges the absence of an enforceable agreement, which would entitle plaintiff to pursue the quasi-contractual cause of action. The SAC alleges an express contract between the parties that obligated Defendants to render professional consulting services to Plaintiff in exchange for payment. (SAC, ¶ 52.) Plaintiff’s quasi-contract cause of action is premised on Plaintiff’s payment for Defendant’s professional service. (SAC, ¶ 58.) The SAC alleges that Defendants breached their duties to Plaintiff and Plaintiff is entitled to his payment back. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff argues the express contract did not contain a specific provision related to the return of funds paid to Defendants. However, the Court does not find Plaintiff’s argument persuasive. The express contract covers the same subject matter, Plaintiff’s payment for Defendants’ professional services. Therefore, Defendants’ failure to render professional services and the return of Plaintiff’s payment would be covered by the express contract. Thus, Plaintiff cannot pursue his quasi-contract cause of action because the parties have an express contract regarding the same subject matter.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

G.     The Second, Third, Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action are Duplicative

 

Next, Defendants demur as to Plaintiff’s second, third and fourth causes of action because they are duplicative of Plaintiff’s first cause of action for negligence.

 

“California defines a ‘cause of action’ in accord with Pomeroy's ‘primary right’ theory. [Citation.] A cause of action consists of (1) a primary right possessed by the plaintiff and a corresponding primary duty imposed upon the defendant, and (2) a delict or wrong committed by the defendant which constitutes a breach of such primary right and duty. [Citation.]” (Miranda v. Shell Oil Co. (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 1651, 1658.) The cause of action is based on the injury to the plaintiff, not on the legal theory or theories advanced to characterize it. Thus, if a plaintiff states several purported causes of action which allege an invasion of the same primary right he has actually stated only one cause of action. On the other hand, if a plaintiff alleges that the defendant's single wrongful act invaded two different primary rights, he has stated two causes of action, and this is so even though the two invasions are pleaded in a single count of the complaint. (Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1854–1855; Freidberg v. Cox (1987) 197 Cal.App.3d 381, 388.) A cause of action that “add[s] nothing to the complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery” is a recognized basis for demurrer. (Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries, (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501.)

 

As discussed above, the Court finds that Plaintiff fails to state a claim for his second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, third cause of action for fraud and fifth cause of action for quasi contract. Thus, whether these causes of action are duplicative is moot.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s first cause of action for negligence and fourth cause of action for breach of contract are duplicative because they invoke a single primary right.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff cites to Gilman v. Nemetz (1962) 203 Cal. App. 2d 81, 94 in support of his contention that a plaintiff may plead multiple causes of action and legal theories based on the same or similar set of facts. Yet, whether or not Plaintiff’s causes of action rely on the same set of facts does not appear to be disputed.

 

Here, Defendants instead argue that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for negligence and fourth cause of action for breach of contract are duplicative because they invoke the same primary right. The Court agrees.

 

Plaintiff alleges his negligence cause of action is predicated on his “primary right to receive competent professional services from Defendants, which Defendants violated by conceding the Fraudulent Expense Reimbursements without any basis.” (Oppos., pp. 6-7.) Plaintiff also argues his breach of contract cause of action is premised on “Defendants’ failure to adhere to their promise to provide competent professional services and disclose all conflicts of interest, as well as not render services tainted by such interests.” (Ibid.) Therefore, both causes of action appear to be premised on Plaintiff’s right to receive competent professional services. Here, the right, duty, breach, and injury are the same for both causes of action regardless of title: Plaintiff had the right to receive competent expert services, Defendants had the duty to provide competent expert services, Defendants purportedly breached their duty by conceding fraudulent expenses despite instructions to the contrary, and Plaintiff was injured by paying Defendants and paying his ex-wife for the fraudulent expenses. Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for breach of contract is duplicative of his first cause of action for negligence.

 

Thus, Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

H.     All Causes of Action Against Defendant Sweeney

 

Defendants argue that the first, second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action against Sweeney as an individual should be dismissed. As discussed above, the Court finds that Plaintiff fails to state a claim for his second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, third cause of action for fraud, fourth cause of action for breach of contract, and fifth cause of action for quasi contract. Thus, the Court will only discuss whether the first cause of action for negligence has been adequately pled against Sweeney.

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.”  (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

“Ordinarily, a corporation is regarded as a legal entity, separate and distinct from its stockholders, officers, and directors, with separate and distinct liabilities and obligations.” (Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court, (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538). “Directors and officers are not personally liable on contracts signed by them for and on behalf of the corporation unless they purport to bind themselves individually.” (United States Liab. Ins. Co. v. Haidinger-Hayes, Inc., (1970) 1 Cal.3d 586, 595). “The legal fiction of the corporation as an independent entity is partly intended to insulate corporate officers from personal liability for corporate contracts.” (Michaelis v. Benavides, (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 681, 688). “To maintain a tort claim against [an employee] in his or her personal capacity, a plaintiff must first show that the director specifically authorized, directed, or participated in the allegedly tortious conduct.” (Frances T. Village Green Owners Ass’n, (1986) 42 Cal. 3d 490. 466-467).

 

Plaintiff contends the SAC “alleges that Mr. Sweeney personally committed acts amounting to negligence … while he was acting as Mr. Carroll’s accountant (i.e., his agent and fiduciary).” (Oppos., p. 15.)

 

The SAC alleges that Sweeney is a Certified Public Accountant licensed in California, is Defendant Taylor’s Chief Executive Officer and Co-Director of the Litigation Services Department. (SAC, ¶ 6.) The SAC further alleges: “During the course of rendering services to Mr. Carroll, Defendants conceded certain grossly exaggerated reimbursement claims asserted by Mr. Carroll's former wife, Laura Petrucci, and then concealed that they had done so while allowing the dissolution action to proceed to Carroll’s detriment. Ultimately, these claims were settled without Mr. Carroll’s actual knowledge or consent.” (SAC, ¶ 2.) The SAC alleges: “The Fraudulent Expense Reimbursements could not withstand the most relaxing scrutiny. Indeed, they were easily rebuttable: Petrucci’s own forensic accountant’s data showed their fraudulent or duplicative nature.” (SAC, ¶ 16.) The SAC further alleges: “Sweeney did not indicate that he would not follow this instruction when preparing certain litigation documents in the dissolution action related to the Fraudulent Expense Reimbursement.” (SAC, ¶ 18.) The SAC further alleges: “As a result of Defendants’ unauthorized concession of the Fraudulent Expense Reimbursements, Carroll unknowingly transferred at least $525,707.00 to Petrucci that should not have been transferred to her (the “Funds”).” (SAC, ¶ 21.) The SAC further alleges: “Defendants’ conduct caused Carroll to transfer the Funds to Petrucci for no legitimate reason, and without Carroll’s actual consent or authorization.” (SAC, ¶ 23.)

 

As the Court found in its prior order on March 14, 2024, these allegations adequately set forth a negligence cause of action against both Defendants, including Sweeney. (Court’s 3/14/24 Minute Order.)

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action against Sweeney as an individual is OVERRULED. 

 

I.        Leave to Amend

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff has had multiple chances to state a valid claim for its second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action. Plaintiff did not materially amend its allegations as to these causes of action in the SAC. In the opposition, Plaintiff does not show how these causes of action can be successfully amended to state a valid cause of action. Therefore, the Court does not grant leave to amend.

 

J.        Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendants request the Court to strike all allegations for punitive damages as follows:

1.      “Carroll seeks punitive damages for Defendants’ malicious conduct in subverting his interests in the dissolution action in favor of Defendants’ financial interest to get promptly paid for their work in the dissolution action. Defendants intentionally concealed their unauthorized concession from Carroll, and allowed Carroll and his counsel to proceed to trail without knowledge of it to further their own interest in willful and concisions disregard of Carroll’s interests and rights in the dissolution action.” (SAC ¶ 38).

2.      “Carroll seeks punitive damages for Defendants’ malicious conduct in subverting his interests in the dissolution action in favor of Defendants’ financial interest to get promptly paid for their work in the dissolution action. Defendants intentionally concealed their unauthorized concession from Carroll, and allowed Carroll and his counsel to proceed to trail without knowledge of it to further their own interest in willful and concisions disregard of Carroll’s interests and rights in the dissolution action.” (SAC ¶ 48).

3.      “For punitive damages arising from Defendants’ intentional concealment undertaken maliciously and for the purpose of furthering their own interests to Carroll’s extreme detriment.” (SAC, page 13:15-17).

4.      “For punitive damages arising from Defendants’ intentional concealment undertaken maliciously and for the purpose of furthering their own interests to Carroll’s extreme detriment.” (SAC, page 13:22-24).

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

Here, Plaintiff argues he is entitled to punitive damages based on Defendants’ breach of fiduciary duties and his fraud cause of action. However, as discussed above, the Court finds Plaintiff has failed to state facts sufficient for his breach of fiduciary duty and fraud causes of action. In light of this ruling, the motion to strike punitive damages is moot.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike is MOOT.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the second, third, fourth and fifth causes of action against all Defendants.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action against Defendant Sweeney as an individual is OVERRULED. 

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is MOOT.

PRIMARKET, LLC v. RN WELLNESS, LLC, et al. (23STCV25862)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Valerie Horn (Valerie F. Horn & Associates)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party: Maurice Pessah (Pessah Law Group, PC)

 

(1)   DEFENDANT rn wellness, llc’s motion FOR RELIEF FROM ENTRY OF DEFAULT (filed on 06/13/2024)

(2)   DEFENDANT jacob perler’s motion FOR RELIEF FROM ENTRY OF DEFAULT (filed on 06/13/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendants RN Wellness, LLC and Jacob Perler’s motions for relief from entry of default are DENIED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff Primarket, LLC filed this action against Defendants RN Wellness, LLC (“RN”) and Jacob Perler (“Perler”).

 

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Contract;

2.      False Designation of Origin;

3.      Trafficking of Counterfeit Goods;

4.      Violation of California Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq.;

5.      Intentional Misrepresentation;

6.      Negligent Misrepresentation; and

7.      Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant RN is a merchant and wholesaler of personal protective equipment (“PPE”) and COVID-19 testing kits. Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant Perler is the sole member and/or manager of RN and responsible for RN. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants purchased, promoted, distributed, advertised, sold and/or offered to sell counterfeit COVID-19 testing kits.

 

On June 13, 2024, Defendants RN and Perler each filed Motions for relief from entry of default, arguing:

·       Defendants request that the court set aside the default entered by Plaintiff and grant leave to file a responsive pleading. Defendant attaches an Answer to the instant Motion.

·       Defendant brings this motion for relief from default on the grounds that mistake, inadvertence, surprise, and/or excusable neglect pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) has resulted in a default being entered.

·       Defendants have moved quickly to correct this mistake, after first reaching out to Plaintiff’s counsel who refuses to stipulate to set aside the defaults.

·       The mistake made by Defense counsel was to delay filing the Notice of Removal, after receiving the Notice of Ruling regarding the March 13, 2024, Case Management Conference, mailed by Plaintiff’s counsel to Mr. Perler’s father. (Greene Decl. ¶10). Defendants’ counsel did not anticipate that Plaintiff’s counsel, aware that Defendants were represented by the same counsel since the first correspondence from Plaintiff’s predecessor counsel, would file Request for Entry of Default before contacting their office. (Greene Decl. ¶10.) The other mistake by Defendants’ counsel was to schedule the filing of Notice of Removal for in advance of the April 30, 2024, Case Management Conference, but failing to anticipate that Plaintiff’s counsel would rush to get the Request for Entry of Default on file well in advance of that hearing. (Greene Decl. ¶11.)

·       No prejudice will be suffered by Plaintiff if the defaults are set aside.

 

Plaintiff filed a combined opposition to both Defendants’ motions for relief, arguing:

·       Defendants, and their counsel, had received actual notice of the filing of the first amended complaint in February 2024, and after Defendants were duly served with the amended Summons and first amended complaint (“FAC”), they did nothing to respond to the FAC.

·       Defendants have not met the legal standard for setting aside default pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure 473(b).

·       Defendants have not established that default was entered against it due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

·       If the Court grants Defendants’ motion, it should order Defendants to pay Plaintiff’s attorney’s fees in the amount of $4,000.

 

Defendants filed a reply, maintaining that it is entitled to have the default against it set aside, and to pursue a defense on the merits, based on Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Section 473, subdivision (b) states in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.... Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Italics added.) 

 

Section 473, subdivision (b), provides for two distinct types of relief.  “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,” the court has discretion to allow relief from a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” a party or his or her attorney. Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,” the court shall vacate any “resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  The range of attorney conduct for which relief can be granted in the mandatory provision is broader than that in the discretionary provision, and includes inexcusable neglect.  But the range of adverse litigation results from which relief can be granted is narrower. Mandatory relief only extends to vacating a default which will result in the entry of a default judgment, a default judgment, or an entered dismissal.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615–616.)  ¿¿ 

 

To gain the statutory benefits, a party or legal representative must seek such relief “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); see Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980 (Rappleyea).  If relief is sought after the six-month period, a trial court may still vacate a default on equitable grounds even if statutory relief is unavailable.¿ (Olivera v. Grace (1942) 19 Cal.2d 570, 575-76.)

“It is well settled that appellate courts have always been and are favorably disposed toward such action upon the part of the trial courts as will permit, rather than prevent, the adjudication of legal controversies upon their merits.” (Benjamin v. Dalmo Mfg. Co. (1948) 31 Cal.2d 523, 525.) “[T]he law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, [and] any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.” (Shapell Socal Rental Properties, LLC v. Chico’s FAS, Inc., supra, 85 Cal.App.5th 198, 212.) “An order denying relief is therefore scrutinized more carefully than an order granting relief and permitting trial on the merits.” (Id. at p. 213.) “[T]he provisions of section 473 . . . are to be liberally construed and sound policy favors the determination of actions on their merits.” (Id. at p. 212, citation and internal quotations omitted.)  

B.      Discussion

 

Defendants seek relief based on the attached affidavit of fault (mandatory grounds) or, if for any reason the Court finds that the requirements for mandatory relief have not been met, then on grounds of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect (discretionary grounds).

 

On or about February 21, 2022, Defendants received via email correspondence from Plaintiff’s prior counsel contending that Defendants had breached the contract with Plaintiff two-weeks earlier, and therefore must refund $151,200, within four days of the letter, by February 25, 2022. (Greene Decl., ¶ 1.) Defendants’ counsel responded to the Plaintiff’s Demand Letter on March 1, 2022 but did not receive a response. (Greene Decl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff filed its Complaint on October 23, 2023, however, Defendants assert that the Summons and Complaint were never served on Defendants. (Motion, p. 4.)

 

On February 29, 2024, Plaintiff’s current counsel served both Defendants with the amended summons and First Amended Complaint by substituted service on February 29, 2024, and mailed a copy of the amended summons and First Amended Complaint on March 5, 2024. (Horn Decl., ¶ 7.) Defendants’ responses to the First Amended Complaint were due on April 15, 2024. Defendants dispute that substituted service on Defendant Perler was proper as it was sent to his father’s address and not Perler’s home address. (Motion, p. 4.)

 

Defendants did not timely respond to the First Amended Complaint and on April 22, 2024, the Clerk entered defaults against both Defendants. (Horn Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s counsel mailed a Notice of Ruling, with respect to the March 13, 2024, Status Conference, again to the home of Perler’s father. Upon learning of the recent Case Management Conference and without being aware that the Requests for Entry of Default had been entered, Defendants’ counsel was preparing a Notice of Removal, based upon diversity and Federal question. (Greene Decl., ¶ 4). As part of that effort, Defendants’ counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel telephonically on April 22, 2024, and then again by telephone and email on April 23, 2024. (Exh B to Greene Decl. ¶ 5). Defendants’ counsel offered to accept service on behalf of both Defendants by Notice and Acknowledgement. (Exh. B to Greene Decl. ¶ 5). Plaintiff’s counsel rejected that offer on April 23, 2024, and reported that the Court had already entered defaults against both the Defendants. (Exh. C to Greene Decl. ¶ 6).

 

Under the mandatory relief provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b), the application for relief must be made within six months after default was entered. Here, Defendants’ motions are timely. Default was entered on April 22, 2024. Defendants’ filed their motions for relief on June 13, 2024. Thus, Defendants can seek mandatory relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).

 

Defendants’ counsel provides a declaration attesting that: (1) the “mistake made by Defense counsel was to delay filing the Notice of Removal, after receiving the Notice of Ruling regarding the March 13, 2024, Case Management Conference, mailed by Plaintiff’s counsel to Mr. Perler’s father. I did not anticipate that Plaintiff’s counsel, aware that Defendants were represented by the same counsel since the first correspondence from Plaintiff’s predecessor counsel, would file Request for Entry of Default before contacting our office” and (2) the “other mistake was to schedule the filing of Notice of Removal for in advance of the April 30, 2024, Case Management Conference, failing to anticipate that Plaintiff’s counsel would rush to get the Request for Entry of Default on file before that hearing.” (Greene Decl. ¶¶ 10-11).

 

However, the Court does not find that the entry of default occurred because of these mistakes. As Plaintiff asserts in its opposition, Defendants’ counsel’s mistakes were not the cause of the entry of default because the Notice of Removal had to be filed by March 6, 2024, (30 days from the date of Defendants’ receipt of the initial pleading in the civil action through their counsel) or at the very latest March 10, 2024, (30 days from the date that Plaintiff’s counsel emailed the amended summons and first amended complaint directly to Perler and RN Wellness.) (28 USC 1446 (b).) By the time Defendants’ counsel claims to have received the Notice of Ruling regarding the March 13, 2024, Case Management Conference, the deadline to file Defendants’ Notice of Removal had already passed.

Relief is mandatory when an attorney files the required affidavit attesting to the attorney’s mistake or neglect, unless the trial court finds that the dismissal did not occur because of these reasons.¿ (Graham v. Beers (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 1656, 1660.) Here, Defendants have not shown that entry of default occurred because of Defendants’ counsel’s mistakes. Defendants’ counsel’s declaration does not explain why Defendants did not respond to the First Amended Complaint by April 15, 2024.

 

The Court finds Defendants have also not established a basis to set aside the default on the Court’s discretionary basis either. Defendants’ counsel has not submitted a declaration attesting to a mistake made by Defendants’ counsel that caused the entry of default. Defendants have also not established that the entry of default occurred because of any inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. As such, Defendants’ motion is denied.

 

Since Defendants’ motion is denied, the Court does not grant Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees.  

 

III. DISPOSITION

Accordingly, Defendants’ motions for relief from entry of default are DENIED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ODYSSEY GROWTH GROUP, LLC v. DANIEL PATRICK, INC. (23STCV31799) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Steven Booska (Law Office of Steven Booska) 

Counsel for Defendants: N/A

 

pLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 7/30/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On December 29, 2023, Plaintiff Odyssey Growth Group filed the instant action against Defendant Daniel Patrick, Inc. alleging a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about September 5, 2020, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a Consulting Agreement, which Plaintiff attached to the Complaint. (Complaint, ¶ 5, Ex. A.) Plaintiff alleges that it performed all conditions and promises required by them on their part of the contact. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant breached the contract on or about November 30, 2021 and Plaintiff has been damaged in the sum of $29,847.50. (Complaint, ¶ 7.)

 

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Default Judgment packet. Plaintiff sought judgment against Defendants in the total amount of $38,812.42, consisting of the principal sum of $29,847.50, interest in the amount of $7,040.66, costs in the amount of $638.83, and attorney fees in the amount of $1,285.43.

 

On May 17, 2024, the Court scheduled the Order to Show Cause re Default Judgment and thereafter continued the hearing to August 8, 2024.

 

On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended default judgment form. Plaintiff’s counsel stated in its declaration that on or about April 12, 2024, a Stipulation for Judgment was prepared for both parties to sign so that this action can be disposed of with a filing of the Request for Dismissal. (Booska Decl., ¶ 3.) Defendant subsequently made two installment payments totaling $3,200.00 for May and June of 2024. (Booska Decl., ¶ 4.) No payments have been received thereafter. (Ibid.) Thus, Plaintiff proceeded with preparing its Request for Entry of Default and Default Judgment. Plaintiff amended the total amount it is seeking against Defendants to the total sum of $35,612.42 to account for the two installment payments made by Defendant. Thus, Plaintiff is seeking judgment in the total amount of $35,612.42, consisting of the principal sum of $26,647.50, interest in the amount of $7,040.66, costs in the amount of $638.83, and attorney fees in the amount of $1,285.43.

SMITH, et al. v. FORSHPAN CAPITAL, LLC, et al. (BC642674)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  Ethan Brown (Brown Neri Smith & Khan LLP)

Counsel for Defendants: Norm Rodich (Palmieri, Tyler, Wiener, Wilhelm & Waldron LLP), Michael McCollum (McCollum Counsel)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES AND LITIGATION COSTS (filed on 06/28/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Litigation Costs is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiffs $46,900.85 in attorneys’ fees and $50,557.29 in costs to be paid out of the settlement fund.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 2, 2016, Plaintiffs Steven Smith, Oscar George, Robert Margolis, and Tirso George Jr. (collectively “Plaintiffs”), filed this shareholder derivative action. In their operative complaint, Plaintiffs allege derivative claims for breach of contract, breach of fiduciary duty and an accounting against Defendants Forshpan Capital, LLC and Morton Forshpan, and nominal Defendant Mojave Square LLC.

 

Plaintiffs allege that Forshpan Capital, as managed by Morton Forshpan, caused harm to the company by failing to pay property taxes owed by Mojave Square, failing to prudently manage the property and ensure timely payment of taxes, failing to file necessary corporate documents, failing to consummate a sale of the property to limit any losses, and refusing to maintain and provide books and records as required.

 

On April 19, 2024, the parties executed a settlement agreement for this proceeding and filed a motion for preliminary approval of the settlement with the Court on May 15, 2024.

 

On June 26, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preliminary Approval of Derivative Claims Settlement.

 

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Litigation Costs, arguing:

·       The Settlement Agreement provides for the payment of money back to the LLC to be distributed to the members according to their interests.

·       Since Plaintiffs obtained a benefit for the LLC and its members, Plaintiffs seek court approval of an award of attorneys’ fees and costs.

o   The Supreme Court of the United States has long held that, where a representative party has, through its efforts, created a “common fund” for, or conferred a “substantial benefit” upon, an identifiable class or corporate entity, like Plaintiffs and their counsel did here, that party is entitled to attorneys’ fees for the creation of such benefits. See, e.g., Boeing v. Van Gernert, 444 U.S. 472 (1980); Mills v. ElectricAuto Lite Co., 396 U.S. 375 (1970)

·       Plaintiffs’ requests for attorneys’ fees and costs are also reasonable. Plaintiffs’ attorneys’ fees are a fraction of their regular hourly rate fees.

·       Plaintiffs also incurred all costs in the ordinary course of litigation. These included arbitration fees, deposition costs, mailings, copying fees, and filing fees, among others.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

“As a general rule, the prevailing party may recover certain statutory costs incurred in the litigation up to and including entry of judgment. [Citations.] These costs may include attorney fees, if authorized by contract, statute . . . or law. [Citation.] . . . attorney fees require a separate noticed motion. [Citations.]” (Lucky United Props. Inv., Inc. v. Lee (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 125, 137.) This motion may be brought: (1) after judgment or dismissal, for fees incurred “up to and including the rendition of judgment in the trial court--including attorney’s fees on an appeal before the rendition of judgment…”; and (2) on an interim basis, upon remittitur of appeal, of only fees incurred on appeal. (CRC, Rule 3.1702(b)-(c).)

 

In determining what fees are reasonable, California courts apply the “lodestar” approach. (See, e.g., Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.) This inquiry “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) From there, the “[t]he lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id.) Relevant factors include: “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

a.       Entitlement to Attorneys’ Fees

 

Plaintiffs move for an award of attorneys’ fees to be paid out of the settlement in this case. Plaintiffs contend that they are entitled to attorneys’ fees under the common-fund doctrine.

 

“ ‘Under the American rule, which is embodied in Code of Civil Procedure section 1021 in California, as a general proposition each party to a litigation must pay his or her own attorneys fees. There are statutory exceptions to this rule, and the courts have created several exceptions pursuant to their inherent equitable powers.’ Exceptions to the American rule created under the courts' equitable powers include the ‘common fund’ and ‘substantial benefit’ doctrines.” (Cziraki v. Thunder Cats, Inc. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 552, 557, footnotes omitted.)

 

“ ‘[I]f the litigation has succeeded in creating or preserving a common fund for the benefit of a number of persons, the plaintiff may be awarded attorney fees out of that fund. [Citation.] Likewise, if a judgment confers a substantial benefit on a defendant, such as in a corporate derivative action, the defendant may be required to pay the attorney fees incurred by the plaintiff.’ In Fletcher v. A.J. Industries, a California appellate court concluded a ‘substantial benefit’ warranting an award of attorney fees to a successful plaintiff in a shareholder derivative action exists where ‘the results of the action “maintain the health of the corporation and raise the standards of ‘fiduciary relationships and other economic behavior,’ ” or “prevent [s] [sic] an abuse which would be prejudicial to the rights and interests of the corporation or affect the enjoyment or protection of an essential right to the stockholder's interest.” ’ ” (Cziraki, supra, 111 Cal.App.4th 552, 557-58, footnotes omitted.)

 

“In a derivative action, the corporation represents the class of beneficiary shareholders. When the corporation pays attorney fees to the successful plaintiff, all shareholders indirectly share the cost of the beneficial litigation with the shareholder who brought the action.” (Cziraki, supra, 111 Cal.App.4th 552, 558.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs assert that the common-fund doctrine applies because Plaintiffs successfully prosecuted their derivative claims against Defendants, resulting in a settlement fund to be paid by Defendants to Plaintiffs for the benefit of its shareholders.

 

The Court finds Plaintiffs meet their burden in establishing that the common-fund doctrine applies. Plaintiffs were successful in prosecuting its derivative claims against Defendants, which resulted in a common fund for the benefit of the shareholders. As such, Plaintiffs’ counsel may be awarded its attorneys’ fees out of this fund. Defendants do not oppose this motion and, therefore, do not show why Plaintiffs should not be entitled to attorneys’ fees.

 

b.      Reasonableness of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs

 

Plaintiffs request $46,900.85 in attorneys’ fees and $50,494.74 in litigation costs to be paid out of the settlement fund.

 

The parties settled for a total of $193,750. That payment includes a $35,000 payment for the services that Plaintiffs rendered in this action, with $12,500 to Oscar George, $7,500 to Robert Margolis, $10,000 to Steven Smith, and $5,000 to Tirso George, Jr. The $193,750 payment, minus the payment to Plaintiffs for their services rendered, and minus Plaintiffs’ attorneys’ fees (33%) and costs (as incurred), shall be distributed pro-rata to all members of Mojave Square (excepting Defendants Forshpan Capital and Morton Forshpan) based on their investment percentage as calculated after removing those not entitled to any settlement proceeds. (Court’s 6/26/24 Minute Order, p. 4.)

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel submits its billing invoices which show that counsel spent more than 470 hours on litigation tasks in this case. (Brown Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. A.) Plaintiffs’ counsel, Mr. Brown and Mr. Barnes, were the only two attorneys primarily involved in this case. (Brown Decl. ¶ 7.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rates are typically $675 and $395 per hour. (Brown Decl. ¶ 8.) Plaintiffs’ counsel attest to their experience and that their rates are comparable to rates for attorneys of similar skill. (Brown Decl. ¶¶ 8-9.) Plaintiffs assert that calculating the amount of hours spent by counsel on this case at their typical hourly rate would equal $176,447.50 in attorneys’ fees. However, here, Plaintiffs’ request for attorneys’ fees is only $46,900.85 at the 33% contingency fee.

 

As to the work performed in this matter, Plaintiffs assert the parties litigated the case extensively. (Brown Decl. ¶ 4.) Written discovery was exchanged, more than 2,500 documents were exchanged, all parties and one third-party deposition was taken, and subpoenas for business records were served. (Ibid.) Discovery was extensive given that the LLC was formed in 2005, thus, investigation included facts going back more than a decade after the case was filed. (Ibid.) Once discovery was completed, Defendants moved for summary judgment/adjudication. That motion was partially granted and partially denied. (Ibid.)

 

Regarding the litigation costs, Plaintiffs assert they reasonably and necessarily incurred $50,494.74 in litigation expenses in connection with commencing, prosecuting, and settling the claims against Defendants. Reimbursement of litigation expenses is also appropriate under the Common Fund Doctrine. (See Fletcher v. A.J. Industries, Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 313, 319 and 329 (affirming an order awarding costs and attorney’s fees in a derivative action). Plaintiffs’ counsel submitted their invoices showing the expenses incurred for this case included filing fees, service fees, mailings, copying and printing, parking, arbitration fees, court reporter fees, and mediation fees. (Brown Decl. ¶ 10, Ex. B.) The biggest expense, $25,334.37, in this case was arbitration fees, for the Honorable Richard Stone of Signature Resolution. (Ibid.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the court finds that Plaintiffs’ request for $46,900.85 in attorneys’ fees and $50,494.74 in litigation costs is reasonable. The Court finds Plaintiffs’ counsel establishes that they have performed a substantial amount work litigating Plaintiffs’ claims against Defendants and have generally been successful. The amount of work discussed above well exceeds the $46,900.85 in attorneys’ fees that Plaintiffs request in this motion. Plaintiffs’ counsel submits their billing invoices and establishes the reasonableness of their hourly rates, of the hours spent prosecuting Plaintiffs’ claims against Defendants and of the litigation costs incurred. Neither Defendants nor any other party opposes this motion and, therefore, does not show why it should not be granted.

 

The Court therefore GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees. The Court awards Plaintiffs $46,900.85 in attorneys’ fees and $50,557.29 in costs to be paid out of the settlement fund.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorneys’ Fees and Litigation Costs is GRANTED. The Court awards Plaintiffs $46,900.85 in attorneys’ fees and $50,557.29 in costs to be paid out of the settlement fund.
