Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Plaintiffs,

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

HEALTHCARE INVESTMENTS INC. DBA ROSECRANS CARE CENTER, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          October 6, 2023

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Interface Rehab, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Christopher D. Cornelius, et al.

Demurrer to FAC

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.  The court declines to consider the second filed opposition.

RULING

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the 1st and 2nd causes of action in the FAC.  Moving defendant is ordered to file an answer with 10 days.

BACKGROUND

            On March 20, 2023, plaintiffs Christopher D. Cornelius, Jonathan L. Cornelius, Carlos E. Cornelius, Lamont Cornelius, and Dwayne Cornelius, individually and as successors in interest to Earl Cornelius Jr, filed a complaint against Healthcare Investments, Inc. dba Rosecrans Care Center and Interface Rehab, Inc. for (1) wrongful death, (2) elder abuse/dependent adult civil protection act, and (3) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights.

            On April 11, 2023, plaintiffs filed a FAC.

            On June 30, 2023, the case was transferred from the PI Hub to Dept. M.

            On August 4, 2023, plaintiffs filed an amendment designating LivingRight Healthcare Management & Consultation as Doe 1.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.  Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal. App. 2d 636, 643-44.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Interface Rehab, Inc. demurs to the 1st cause of action for wrongful death and 2nd cause of action for elder abuse in the FAC on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

The FAC alleges that defendant Interface Rehab is engaged in the business of owning, operating, and/or managing nursing homes, including defendant Rosecrans.  FAC, ¶6.  Interface Rehab accepted the responsibility to provide caretaking and custodial services to decedent and had custody of decedent.  Id., ¶8.  Earl Cornelius was an elderly, dependent adult who, during his admissions and residencies at Rosecrans, was dependent on its staff for all activities of daily living, including daily hygiene, bathing, turning and repositioning, pressure relief, eating, nutrition, hydration, care planning, pressure relieving devices, medication administration, wound care, pain relief, monitoring for significant changes in condition, assistance with ambulation, assistance with toileting, supervision, and protection from health and safety hazards.  Id., ¶40.

The FAC further alleges that on February 27, 2011, Cornelius was admitted to Rosecrans.  Prior to his admission, Cornelius was a 70-year elder adult, who had a past medical history significant for dementia.  Cornelius did not have any pressure ulcers on his skin at the time of his admission to Rosecrans.  As a result of his co-morbidities, however, Cornelius was dependent on staff for assistance with his ADLs and entirely dependent on Rosecrans’ staff for pressure relief and re-positioning.  Id., ¶41.  Due to his comorbidities, at the time of Cornelius’ Rosecrans admission, Cornelius was assessed by Rosecrans’ staff as being at high risk for skin breakdown and deterioration.  As such, Rosecrans’ staff had a duty to implement an adequate and proactive care plan to protect him from avoidable skin breakdown and injuries.  Id., ¶42.  Rosecrans failed to do any of the aforementioned interventions and throughout Cornelius’ residency at Rosecrans, he suffered otherwise avoidable infections and skin breakdown to his sacrum.  Id., ¶44.  Despite Rosecrans’ staff’s acknowledgement that Cornelius required more assistance to prevent avoidable injury, Rosecrans’ staff failed to institute any effective interventions after Cornelius’ skin continued to avoidably deteriorate during his residency at Rosecrans.  Id., ¶45.  Rosecrans’ staff repeatedly and deliberately neglected Cornelius and deprived him of necessary medical treatment and services, including timely monitoring, timely pressure relief, timely turning and repositioning, provision of pressure relieving/reducing medical devices, care planning and property handling.  Id., ¶47.  Rosecrans failed to inform Cornelius’ family or physician that he was suffering severe skin breakdown.  Once Cornelius’ family became aware of the skin breakdown, his family still believed Rosecrans could have managed the rehabilitation as they were aware of the pressure ulcers at least since 2015.  Id., ¶48.

The FAC further alleges that on October 31, 2022, Cornelius was transferred to Kindred Hospital South Bay in Gardena after finding Cornelius unresponsive and altered suffering from Covid-19 pneumonia.  Upon admission, he was found to have several unstageable pressure injuries including one that was around five inches in length and width with 95% of necrotic tissue infection.  Id., ¶49.  None of Cornelius’ pressure ulcers healed.  He was discharged from Kindred Hospital South Bay on November 7, 2022, and readmitted into Rosecrans that same day.  On November 11, 2022, four days after being readmitted to Rosecrans, Cornelius suffered an untimely death as a proximate result of the injuries that he sustained while a resident of Rosecrans.  Id., ¶50.  During his Rosecrans residency, Cornelius was also often left unattended because there was insufficient staff to provide his timely assistance with his daily hygiene.  As a result, Cornelius suffered poor hygiene throughout his admission at Rosecrans and was left laying in bodily waste for prolonged periods of time, thereby exacerbating his bedsore and increasing the risk of infection.  On multiple occasions, Cornelius was found by his family with soiled sheets or briefs.  Id., ¶51.  Due to grossly inadequate staffing, staff training, staff management, inadequate supervision, and facility management, Cornelius suffered otherwise avoidable skin breakdown, infections, and poor hygiene.  Id., ¶53.

 

1st cause of action for wrongful death

The elements of a cause of action for wrongful death are a tort, resulting death, and damages of the heirs.  Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal. App. 4th 1256, 1263.  In any action for wrongful death resulting from negligence, the complaint must contain allegations as to all the elements of actionable negligence.  Jacoves v. United Merchandising Corp. (1992) 9 Cal. App. 4th 88, 105.  To state a claim for negligence, plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.”  McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 664, 671. Wrongful death damages include value of society, comfort, protection, and personal services expected to be received from the decedent, but not emotional distress, grief, and sorrow. Bouley v. Long Beach Memorial Medical Center (2005) 127 Cal. App. 4th 601, 610.

The FAC alleges that defendants owed a duty to residents, including Cornelius, to (1) provide adequate and appropriate custodial care and supervision, which a reasonably careful person would provide under similar circumstances; (2) exercise reasonable care in providing care and services in a safe and beneficial manner; (3) hire, train, and supervise employees to deliver care and services in a safe and beneficial manner; (4) use reasonable care in treating plaintiff with the degree of skill and learning ordinarily possessed and used by nursing facilities or physicians in the same or similar locality; and (5) assist in attaining and maintaining the highest level of physical, mental, and psychosocial well-being.  FAC, ¶¶ 60-64.  Defendants breached these duties by failing to exercise reasonable care and by failing to prevent the mistreatment, abuse, and neglect of Cornelius.  Id., ¶65(a)-(i).  Defendants’ violations and breaches of the standards of care, federal and state statutes, and federal and state regulations that govern skilled nursing facilities were substantial factors in causing Cornelius’ injuries and death.  Id., ¶69.  As a result, plaintiffs have suffered loss of love, companionship, society, comfort, and support due to the death of their father.  Id., ¶70.

Defendant asserts that it provided therapy services to certain residents of Rosecrans Care Center under an “independent contractor” written agreement with Rosecrans, and that such agreement ended in 2019, at which time defendant ceased providing any therapy services to residents of Rosecrans, including decedent Cornelius who died three years later in 2022.  Thus, defendant argues, there are no factual allegations of anything defendant did with regard to decedent that may have caused any damages to Cornelius or his death.  Defendant further argues that plaintiffs have not alleged with specificity, which defendant contends is required under the statutory claim. 

            As stated above, a demurrer tests the pleadings alone.  Defendant’s assertion and argument are outside the pleadings and as defendant acknowledges, the court cannot take judicial notice of the purported agreement.  In any event, the allegations are sufficient to meet the elements.

            The demurrer is OVERRULED.

2nd cause of action for elder abuse

To establish a claim for elder abuse and neglect under the statute, plaintiff must allege  (1) defendant had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene, or medical care; (2) defendant knew of conditions that made the elder adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs; (3) defendant denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder adult’s basic needs, either (a) with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder adult or (b) with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury recklessness; and (4) the neglect caused the elder adult to suffer physical harm, pain, or mental suffering. Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407; Welfare & Institutions Code § 15610.07.  

A statutory cause of action such as elder/dependent adult abuse/neglect must be pleaded with particularity.  Covenant Care, Inc. v. Super. Ct. (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790; Intrieri v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal. App.4th 72, 82.  A plaintiff that can prove by clear and convincing evidence that (1) the defendant is liable for physical abuse as defined in Welfare & Institutions Code §15610.63, neglect as defined in Welfare & Institutions Code §15610.57, or abandonment as defined in Welfare & Institutions Code §15610.05 and that (2) the defendant acted with “recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of this abuse” can recover heightened remedies under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (the Act).  See Welfare & Institutions Code §15657; Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 152. 

The Act defines “neglect” as “the negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder or a dependent adult to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise.”  Welfare & Institutions Code §15610.57(a).  “[N]eglect refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the ‘failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.’”  Covenant Care, supra, 32 Cal.4th 771, 783. 

The FAC alleges that defendants owed a duty to use ordinary care in the treatment and care of Cornelius.  In particular, defendants were required to (a) follow, implement, and adhere to all physicians’ orders and read the physicians’ progress notes and act upon those notes; (b) provide custodial care; (c) monitor Cornelius’ condition frequently and assess him, accurately record his condition, and report any meaningful change in condition to his family and his attending physician; (d) establish and implement an individualized resident service plan; (e) maintain care attendant and other staffing at levels required by laws; (f) follow proper bedside personal care procedures; (g) provide enough custodial and caregiving staff; (h) respond promptly to reasonable family inquiries; and (i) monitor Cornelius status as it related to his nutrition, hydration, elimination, and pain and to implement policies and procedures to prevent the types of injuries Cornelius sustained.  FAC, ¶74.  Defendants consciously failed to protect Cornelius from health and safety hazards and abused him by failing to act as reasonable care custodians.  Defendants failed to meet any of the above requirements.  Id., ¶75.  See breaches at para. 76.  Additionally, during Cornelius’ residency at Rosecrans, defendants deprived him of food and water for an extended period of time and therefore engaged in “physical abuse.”  Id., ¶77.  Defendants failed to hire, train, and supervise sufficient numbers of caregiving staff to ensure that residents at Rosecrans, including Cornelius, were protected from health and safety hazards.  Id., ¶78.  Throughout Cornelius’ residence, defendants failed to exercise such skill, prudence, and diligence in the assessment, care and treatment of Cornelius.  As a result of defendants’ neglectful failures and inability to provide proper care and treatment to Cornelius, Cornelius suffered multiple infections, including sepsis secondary to pneumonia; suffered avoidable skin breakdown and pressure ulcers; suffered avoidable malnutrition and dehydration; and suffered an untimely death on November 11, 2022.  Id., ¶82.

Defendant makes the same assertion as under the 1st cause of action that it was an independent contractor providing therapy services which ended in 2019.  Defendant also argues that the allegations lack specificity.   

As stated above, defendant’s argument is outside the allegations of the FAC, and the court cannot take judicial notice of the purported independent contractor agreement.  Further, the court finds that the allegations meet the elements for elder abuse under the heightened pleading requirements.

The demurrer is OVERRULED.

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The truth of the matters in the Request for Admissions, Set One are deemed admitted as against Ghulam Sarajzada and Aisha Sarajzada.

BACKGROUND

On June 16, 2023, plaintiff ROIC California, LLC filed a complaint against Ghulam & Sons, Ghulam Sarajzada, and Aisha Sarajzada for (1) breach of written lease, (2) breach of written guaranty, (3) account stated, and (4) money had and received.

On August 15, 2023, Ghulam Sarajzada and Aisha Sarajzada (self-represented) filed an answer.

On August 16, 2023, a default was entered against Ghulam & Sons.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Pursuant to CCP § 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).  “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions.  Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.”  Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶ 8:1370, citing CCP § 2033.280(b).  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  CCP § 2033.280(c).  

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff ROIC California, LLC requests an order establishing the truth of each matter specified in the Requests for Admission, Set One, served on July 23, 2023 on defendants Ghulam Sarajzada and Aisha Sarajzada.

            Plaintiff asserts that responses were due by August 27, 2023 but that plaintiff’s counsel has not received responses.

            The court finds that plaintiff properly served the discovery requests and that defendants failed to respond.

            The motion is GRANTED.

Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” 

It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction on the party or attorney whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated a motion to deem them admitted.  CCP § 2033.280(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” 

Plaintiff requests $2685 in sanctions against defendants.  The court finds that $660 ($400/hr. x 1.5 hrs., $60 filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed in sanctions against defendants.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Demurrer to First Amended Answer

 

            The court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

The demurrer to the FAA is OVERRULED.

BACKGROUND

On July 10, 2023, plaintiff Diana Hayden filed a complaint against Sofi Stadium, Stadco, and Live Nation for (1) negligence and (2) premises liability.  Plaintiff alleges that on October 17, 2021, she and her daughter attended a Rolling Stones concert at Sofi Stadium.  Complaint, ¶7.  Plaintiff decided to leave the concert early with the hope of avoiding the rush of the crowd.  Id., ¶8.  While walking to her car, plaintiff encountered a large crowd of concert goers who were being directed and forced by defendants through temporary guard rails.  Id., ¶9.  These rails were not sturdy, and the crowd began knocking them over in an effort to get to their vehicles faster because the force and energy of the crowd were also unintentionally knocking the rails over.  Id., ¶10.  This crowd of people slammed into plaintiff and caused her to be violently thrown like a ragdoll into adjacent bushes.  This crowd slamming into plaintiff would not have happened but for defendants negligently forcing the crowd into the rails and the improper placement of the rails, which were not suitable for the crowd or directing the crowd.  Also, defendants failed to take adequate crowd control measures and failed to have adequate security and crowd control personnel on hand to ensure crowd safety as they exited the premises.  Id., ¶11.  The result was substantial injury to plaintiff’s body and mind.  Id., ¶12.

On August 21, 2023, Stadco LA, LLC (erroneously sued and served as Sofi Stadium and Stadco) filed an answer.

On August 25, 2023, Stadco LA, LLC filed a FAA.

On August 30, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint correcting Live Nation’s name to Live Nation Worldwide, Inc.

On August 31, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint designating Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. as Doe 1.

On September 15, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment correcting Stadco’s name to Stadco LA, LLC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Plaintiff may demur to an answer on the ground of insufficient pleading of defenses.  CCP §430.20.  Under CCP §431.30(g), every affirmative defense “shall be separately stated, and the several defenses shall refer to the causes of action which they are intended to answer, in a manner by which they may be intelligibly distinguished.”  “The same pleading of ‘ultimate facts’ rather than ‘evidentiary’ matter or ‘legal conclusions’ is required as in pleading the complaint.”  Weil and Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, 6:459.  “The answer must aver facts ‘as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and which are alleged in the complaint.’”  Id. (quoting FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 384).  “In general, whatever defendant bears the burden of proving at trial is ‘new matter,’ and thus must be specially pleaded in the answer.”  Civ. Proc. Before Trial, 6:431.  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff demurs to defendant Stadco LA, LLC’s First Amended Answer and the 1st through 8th affirmative defenses on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a defense and are uncertain.

            Plaintiff argues that the affirmative defenses fail to set forth any facts whatsoever.

            In opposition, defendant argues that the court should overrule the demurrer based on California’s liberal pleading standard that “only requires the plaintiff be put on notice of the nature of the defenses, and because modern discovery procedures can readily clarify any ambiguities.” 

            The court finds that the allegations are sufficient as to each of defendant’s affirmative defenses in its amended answer as defendant has plead the ultimate facts in support.

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice.
