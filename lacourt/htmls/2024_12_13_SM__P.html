Case Number: 23SMCV00608    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Acevedo v. Herrera, Case no. 23SMCV00608

Hearing date December 13, 2024

Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions - unopposed

Plaintiff Acevedo allege he was struck by defendant’s vehicle while riding a bicycle. Defendant in pro per answered on 4/27/23. Plaintiff served special and form interrogatories, requests for production of documents and requests for admission. Defendant failed to respond. Plaintiff moved to compel responses and deem the requests admitted. The court granted plaintiff’s motion. See Min. Order 10/24/23. Plaintiff requested $811.65 in attorney’s fees, which the court deferred. Plaintiff now moves for terminating sanctions; no opposition was filed.

Where a party willfully disobeys a discovery order, courts have discretion to impose terminating, issue, evidentiary or monetary sanctions. E.g., Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2023.010(g), 2030.290(c); R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495. Terminating sanctions are authorized where the evidence shows less severe sanctions will not be successful in compelling responses. Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280. Courts may require financial reimbursement for expenses incurred as to misuse of the discovery process. Code Civ. Proc., §2023.030; Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 789, superseded by statute on another ground as stated in Guzman v. General Motors Corp. (1984) 154 Cal.App.3d 438, 444; In re Marriage of Niklas (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 28, 37.

Plaintiff served discovery. Min. Order 10/24/23. Defendant did not answer. Plaintiff’s motion to compel was granted. At that time, defendant appeared and represented to the court that he was attempting to get his insurance company to provide a defense. Because of that representation, the court deferred the issue of sanctions.

Now, a year later, defendant has not provided responses, and it appears his insurer is not providing coverage. Defendant has not responded to the discovery, opposed this motion for terminating sanctions or retained independent counsel. At this point, it appears lesser sanctions will not compel defendant to respond.

Plaintiff requests $700 in sanctions for the instant motion at 2 hours x $350/hour. Decl. Vardanyan para. 5. This is reasonable. Motion GRANTED. Plaintiff awarded $700 in sanctions, payable within 30 days.


Case Number: 24SMCV03940    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Churchill Funding I, LLC v. 732 Indiana, LLC, Case no. 24SMCV03940

Hearing date December 13, 2024

Plaintiff’s Demurrer to Defendant Ilan Kenig’s FACC

Plaintiff Churchill Funding I, LLC sues defendants 728 Indiana, LLC, 732 Indiana, LLC, Ilan Kenig, Los Angeles 104 Projects 2016 Limited Partnership and Marom Kislev Projects 2016, LP for judicial foreclosure, breach of guarantee and injunctive relief arising from defaulted real estate development loans guaranteed by real property. Defendant Ilan Kenig, in pro per, cross-complained 9/6/24. Kenig filed his FACC 9/9/24 alleging 12 COAs for breach of contract and violations of the California Civil and Professions Codes. Plaintiff demurs to the FACC and requests judicial notice. Kenig opposes.

Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of (1) the stipulation for entry of temporary restraining order, filed on 8/28/24 in this action, and (2) the TRO entered 8/28/24 in this matter. Cal Evid. Code §452(d)(1) permits the court to take judicial notice of any record of courts of California, another state or the United States. Judicial notice of the court’s own records is proper. GRANTED.

Plaintiff’s Demurrer to the FACC

“The function of a demurrer is to test the sufficiency of the complaint as a matter of law.” Holiday Matinee, Inc. v. Rambus, Inc. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1413, 1420. A complaint “is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts,” Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550, but plaintiff must allege essential facts “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint [the] defendant with the nature, source and extent” of the plaintiff’s claim. Doheny Park Terrace Homeowners Ass’n., Inc. v. Truck Ins. Exchange (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1076, 1099.

Plaintiff generally demurs to all 12 COAs on the grounds that Kenig lacks standing to bring any of the claims asserted. Code Civ. Proc., §430.10(e); see also Blumhorst v. Jewish Family Services of Los Angeles (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 993, 1000 (“A litigant’s standing to sue is a threshold issue to be resolved before the matter can be reached on the merits.”) Plaintiff argues the property at issue is not owned by Kenig, and as such Kenig cannot show damages and has no legal interest on which to base his claims. Kenig does not allege ownership or interest in the property. FACC. Para. 12.

Kenig alleges plaintiff has unlawfully violated the one-action rule under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §726 by pursuing both judicial and non-judicial foreclosure. Kenig argues this allegation of unlawful action gives him standing. This argument is unavailing. In the foreclosure context, an election of remedies only takes place once the beneficiary under a deed of trust takes a judicial foreclosure to judgment or completes a nonjudicial foreclosure by trustee’s sale because the beneficiary effectively elects its remedy, thereby forfeiting the other. Flack v. Boland (1938) 11 Cal.2d 103, 107. Plaintiff has not violated Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §726, and as such Kenig lacks standing.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the first and third COAs for wrongful foreclosure. Kenig fails to allege ownership or interest in the property at issue. FACC para. 12. Kenig has no grounds to bring claims for wrongful foreclosure.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the second COA for unfair business practices. Kenig must have suffered injury in fact and lost money or property. See Bus. & Prof. Code, §17204. Kenig does not allege injuries or loss of money or property. Kenig instead argues plaintiff’s actions have limited defendants’ ability to sell the property at issue. FACC para. 33. Kenig argues as guarantor this constitutes an injury to him. This argument is unavailing. Kenig fails to allege with sufficient particularity any invasion of legally protected interests or concrete injuries. Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310, 322-23.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the fourth COA for breach of contract. Kenig fails to allege the necessary elements for breach of contract with sufficient particularity. Kenig fails to attach a copy of the alleged contract, fails to allege specific terms and conditions of the contract and fails to specify which of plaintiff's actions constitute breach of contract. These are all necessary elements of a breach of contract COA, without which a claim is insufficient. See Bowden v. Robinson (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 705, 718.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the fifth, sixth and seventh COAs for injunction, accounting and cancellation of instrument. Kenig lacks standing to assert the alleged rights of the borrowers, as the FACC is brought in his individual capacity and he is not an owner of the property. FACC para. 12 (“The above referenced properties are owned managed and controlled by 728 Indiana LLC, a California Limited Partnership and by 732 Indiana LLC.”), paras. 63, 65, 70, 81.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the eighth COA for abuse of process. “To establish a cause of action for abuse of process, a plaintiff must plead two essential elements: that the defendant (1) entertained an ulterior motive in using the process and (2) committed a willful act in a wrongful manner.” Coleman v. Gulf Ins. Group (1986) 41 Cal.3d 782, 792. Kenig asserts, on information and belief, that plaintff does not intend on “completing the judicial foreclosure but merely intends to expand the search for a legal remedy such as the appointment of a receiver and then revert to the non-judicial foreclosure to expedite the taking over the property.” FACC para. 88. Speculation is insufficient, and the mere filing of a lawsuit even for an alleged improper purpose is not a basis for an abuse of process COA. See, e.g., Kappel v. Bartlett (1988) 200 Cal.App.3d 1457, 1466.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the ninth COA for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Kenig fails to allege the contract at issue that gives rise to the covenant. So far as Kenig seeks to assert a breach of plaintiff's contract with borrower defendants, Kenig lacks standing as he is not a borrower. FACC para. 92. Kenig fails to allege a basis for the ninth COA.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the tenth and eleventh COAs for fraud on the court. Kenig fails to allege facts sufficient to support the COAs, fails to allege fraud with specificity and complains of conduct protected by the litigation privilege. See Civil Code, § 47; see also Bergstein v. Stroock & Stroock & Lavan LLP (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 793, 814. Further, Kenig asserts the fraud is evidenced by the entry of the 8/28/24 TRO; this is unavailing as the TRO was entered by stipulation from the parties. Further, as Kenig alleges conspiracy to commit fraud between plaintiff and plaintiff’s counsel, Kenig is in violation of Cal. Civ. Code §1714.10 which requires leave from the court to permit such a COA.

Plaintiff specifically demurs to the twelfth COA for breach of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §6068(d) (“It is the duty of an attorney to… never to seek to mislead the judge or any judicial officer by an artifice or false statement of fact or law.”) Kenig does not allege any facts showing that plaintiff’s purported statement is a “false statements of fact or law.” Kenig fails to allege that plaintiff, as an institution and not an attorney, is subject to Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §6068(d). Kenig offers no authority providing for a private COA under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §6068(d).

Plaintiff’s demurrer to Kenig’s FACC is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.
