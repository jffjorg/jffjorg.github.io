DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV03944    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: H

John Roe L.S. v. California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation,
Case No. 23PSCV03944

 ORDER ON DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendant California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend. The Motion to Strike is DENIED as MOOT.


I.        Background 

Plaintiff John Roe L.S. (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

Plaintiff, a homosexual man, was sexually, verbally, and physically abused for years by other inmates and by employees of the California Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation Division (“CDCR”), including his parole officer, while under the CDCR’s custody, care and/or control.

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC” or the “Operative Complaint”), asserting causes of action against CDCR and Does 1-50 for:

1.       Civil Rights Violation (42 U.S.C. § 1983);

2.       Negligence;

3.       Negligent Hiring, Supervision and Retention;

4.       Sexual Battery (Civil Code § 1708.5);

5.       Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress;

6.       Sexual Harassment (Civil Code § 51.9);

7.       Violation of Bane Act (Civil Code § 52.1); and

8.       Gender Violence (Civil Code § 52.4). 

Pending before the Court is CDCR’s demurrer and motion to strike.

II.      Legal Standard 

A.       Demurrer 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

B.       Motion to Strike

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

 

III.     Discussion 

CDCR demurs to the second through seventh causes of action in the Operative Complaint, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(e), on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. CDCR also moves, per Code of Civil Procedure §§ 431.10(b), 435 and 436, to strike out portions of the Operative Complaint.

A.       Procedural Defect 

At the outset, the Court notes that CDCR has improperly combined its demurrer and motion to strike into one motion, instead of filing a separate demurrer and motion to strike as contemplated by the Code. The Court admonishes CDCR in this regard but proceeds to the merits as this defect does not prejudice Plaintiff.

B.       Merits

Turning to the merits, CDCR asserts that Plaintiff’s claims are time-barred by Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(a)(1), which provides that “[i]n any civil action for recovery of damages suffered as a result of sexual assault, where the assault occurred on or after the plaintiff’s 18th birthday, the time for commencement of the action shall be . . . “[w]ithin 10 years from the date of the last act, attempted act, or assault with the intent to commit an act, of sexual assault against the plaintiff.”

CDCR acknowledges that subdivision (e) of Code of Civil Procedure section 340.16(a)(1) revives certain expired claims. (Cal. Civ. Proc. Code § 340.16(e) [“Notwithstanding any other law, any claim seeking to recover damages suffered as a result of a sexual assault that occurred on or after the plaintiff’s 18th birthday that would otherwise be barred before January 1, 2023, solely because the applicable statute of limitations has or had expired, is hereby revived, and a cause of action may proceed if already pending in court on January 1, 2023, or, if not filed by that date, may be commenced between January 1, 2023, and December 31, 2023.”].) Specifically, this subdivision revives claims alleging all of the following elements: “(A) The plaintiff was sexually assaulted; (B) One or more entities are legally responsible for damages arising out of the sexual assault; and (C) The entity or entities, including, but not limited to, their officers, directors, representatives, employees, or agents, engaged in a cover up or attempted a cover-up of a previous instance or allegations of sexual assault by an alleged perpetrator or such abuse.” (Id.)

CDCR argues that subdivision (e) is not applicable here. It first argues that CDCR is not an “entity” as defined by the statute. (Dem., 4:16-17.) The Court disagrees.

“As with any case involving statutory interpretation, [the Court’s] fundamental task is to determine the legislative intent to effectuate the law’s purpose.” (Ibarra v. Chuy & Sons Labor, Inc. (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 874, 882.) In doing so, courts “look first to the statutory language and give the words their usual and ordinary meaning.” (Rhonda S. v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 643, 649.) Courts “construe those words in context, giving them a plain and commonsense meaning unless the statute specifically defines the words to give them a special meaning.” (Turo, Inc. v. Superior Court of City and County of San Francisco (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 517, 521 [quotations and citation omitted].) “Courts appropriately refer to dictionary definitions to ascertain the ordinary, usual meaning of a word.” (Id.)

In giving the statutory language its plain and commonsense meaning, courts “examine that language, not in isolation, but in the context of the statutory framework as a whole to discern its scope and purpose and to harmonize the various parts of the enactment.” (Busker v. Wabtec Corp. (2021) 11 Cal.5th 1147, 1156.) “Ordinarily, where the Legislature uses a different word or phrase in one part of a statute than it does in other sections or in a similar statute concerning a related subject, it must be presumed that the Legislature intended a different meaning.” (Campbell v. Zolin (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 489, 497.)

Section 340.16, subdivision (e)(4)(B) defines an “entity” as “a sole proprietorship, partnership, limited liability company, corporation, association, or other legal entity.” The phrase “legal entity” is not defined, which indicates that the Legislature intentionally did not give it any special meaning.

The Court determines that CDCR is a “legal entity” within the meaning of Section 340.16, subdivision (e)(4)(B). The term “legal entity” encompasses both public and private entities, including state governmental agencies. The term “legal entity” is defined by Merriam-Webster as “an entity (as a corporation or labor union) having under the law rights and responsibilities and especially the capacity to sue and be sued.” (Merriam-Webster, legal entity, Merriam-Webster.com Dictionary, https://www.merriam-webster.com/legal/legal%20entity [as of Sept. 26, 2024].) CDCR is an entity under this definition.

Further, the fact that the Legislature used “public entity” elsewhere in Section 340.16 (specifically, in subdivision (c)(2)(C)) does not, in the Court’s view, “indicat[e] an intent [by the Legislature] to distinguish between public entities, such as CDCR, and the private enterprises listed in subdivision (e)(4)(B)” (Dem., 7:8-11); rather, the converse is true. The Legislature plainly limited subdivision (c)(2)(C) to claims against nonpublic entities. If the Legislature intended to make such a distinction in subdivision (e)(4)(B) it easily could have done so.

CDCR next argues that subdivision (e) is not applicable here, because Plaintiff “has not alleged that CDCR staff engaged in a cover up of any instance of sexual assault that occurred prior to his rape by Mr. Perez.” (Dem., 5:13-14.) The Court agrees. Under Section 340.16, subdivision (e)(4)(A), “cover up” is defined as “a concerted effort to hide evidence relating to a sexual assault that incentivizes individuals to remain silent or prevents information relating to a sexual assault from becoming public or being disclosed to the plaintiff, including, but not limited to, the use of nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements.”

“[N]othing in the language of [Section 340.16] requires that the alleged cover up involve a previous instance of sexual assault by the same individual who later assaulted the plaintiff. The statutory language requires only that the plaintiff allege a cover up of a previous instance or allegations of sexual assault by ‘an alleged perpetrator of such abuse.’ (§ 340.16(e)(2)(C).)” (Jane Doe #21 (S.H.) v. CFR Enterprises, Inc. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1199, 1212.)

Plaintiff contends that he alleged in Paragraphs 50 and 51 that CDCR engaged in a cover up of the previous instances of abuse he suffered while incarcerated at Folsom State Prison. (Opp., 9:25-26.) The Court disagrees. Plaintiff has alleged only that “Mr. Perez made no effort to report” Plaintiff’s allegations of sexual abuse. (Complaint, ¶ 51.)

Accordingly, CDCR’s demurrer is sustained. CDCR’s request to strike out portions of the Operative Complaint is denied as moot, based on the ruling made on the demurrer. The Court will grant Plaintiff 20 days’ leave to amend the Operative Complaint.
