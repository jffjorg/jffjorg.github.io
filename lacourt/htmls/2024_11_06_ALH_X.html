DEPARTMENT X LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 23AHCV00625    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: X


MOTION FOR TERMINATING, ISSUE, EVIDENTIARY, AND/OR MONETARY SANCTIONS 


Defendant’s Motion for Terminating, Issue, and Evidentiary Sanctions against Plaintiff is GRANTED.  Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $690.00.

 

The Court denies the request for terminating sanctions for procedural reasons.  In the motion, the correct hearing date, time, and location is listed, but the body of the notice of motion mentions terminating sanctions without mentioning what type of order Defendant is requesting.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030(d) [striking a pleading, staying proceedings, dismissing the action, or rendering judgment by default].)

 

As there is no evidence presented to the Court that Plaintiff has responded to discovery pursuant to the Court’s prior order, the Court issues an evidentiary sanction preventing Plaintiff from presenting any evidence regarding whether Defendant was negligent or maintained the subject premises in an unsafe manner.

 

Finally, the Court issues a monetary sanction against Plaintiff in the sum of $690.00.  Plaintiff is ordered to pay the monetary sanction to Defendant’s counsel within thirty (30) days of notice of this Court’s Order.

 

Moving Party is to give notice.


Case Number: 23AHCV00745    Hearing Date: November 6, 2024    Dept: X


DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO DISMISS

 

Defendant moves to dismiss the case.

 

Plaintiff filed a complaint on April 4, 2023, alleging one cause of action for violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act, California Civil Code section 51 et seq. (“UCRA”).  Plaintiff claims that Defendant Modern Shaman, LLC failed to design, construct, maintain, and operate its website to be fully accessible to and independently usable by blind and visually impaired people.  Plaintiff alleges she is a visually impaired and legally blind person who requires screen-reading software to read website content while using a computer.

 

Tentative Ruling

Defendant’s motion to dismiss the complaint is DENIED.  The Non-Jury Trial re Plaintiff’s Claim for Injunctive Relief scheduled for 12/09/2024 is advanced to this date and vacated.

 

Analysis

In her complaint, Plaintiff seeks permanent injunctive relief, i.e., to cause a change in Defendant’s corporate policies, practices, and procedures so that Defendant’s website will become and remain accessible to blind and visually-impaired consumers.  (Compl., ¶ 4.)

 

In Defendant’s motion to dismiss, it contends the case is moot and this Court should dismiss the case for two reasons:

 

One, the Modern Shaman website was corrected to meet ADA standards immediately following the alleged incident, and monthly compliance logs (attached hereto as Exhibit A) confirm that the website remains compliant despite its permanent removal from public view.  Two, and even more pertinently, the restaurant’s last day of operation was June 30, it has permanently closed, and the website is no longer in operation.  As a result of both actions by the defendant, there is no possibility of ongoing harm, and as such, there is no basis for injunctive relief.

 

(Motion, p. 3.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff makes several arguments.  First, that this motion is a demurrer that is untimely. Second, that this motion is a defective motion for summary judgment and lacks the required notice for a summary judgment motion, is untimely, lacks a separate statement, and defense counsel failed to meet-and-confer regarding this motion for summary judgment.  Plaintiff also argues that (1) Defendant has not met its burden of demonstrating that it is absolutely clear that its wrongful behavior could not be reasonably expected to recur, and (2) even if injunctive relief is moot, Defendant’s motion does not address the $4,000 monetary damages for violation of the Unruh Act.

 

Notably, Plaintiff’s opposition does not address Defendant’s argument that the complaint is moot due to the impending sale of the restaurant equipment and transfer of the lease of the Greenleaf Avenue location.  “No justiciable controversy is presented where the question sought to be adjudicated has been mooted by developments subsequent to filing of the complaint.”  (M.M. v. Lafayette Sch. Dist., 767 F.3d 842, 857 (9th Cir. 2014) (citing Church of Scientology of Cal. v. United States (1992) 506 U.S. 9, 12).)

 

To demonstrate that a case is moot, the defendant must show that (1) there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged violation will recur, and (2) interim relief or events have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged violation.  (County of Los Angeles v. Davis (1979) 440 U.S. 625, 631.)  “When both conditions are satisfied it may be said that the case is moot because neither party has a legally cognizable interest in the final determination of the underlying questions of fact and law.”  (Ibid.)

 

As the moving party contends, it is generally appropriate to dismiss an action once it has become moot.  “A case is considered moot when ‘the question addressed was at one time a live issue in the case,’ but has been deprived of life ‘because of events occurring after the judicial process was initiated.’ . . . The pivotal question in determining if a case is moot is therefore whether the court can grant the plaintiff any effectual relief. . . . If events have made such relief impracticable, the controversy has become ‘overripe’ and is therefore moot.”  (Wilson & Wilson v. City Counsel of Redwood City (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 1559, 1574.)

 

Based upon the evidence presented, the Court determines Plaintiff’s request for injunctive relief is moot.  First, there is no reasonable expectation that the alleged ADA and Unruh violations will reoccur because the business has not operated since its closure on June 30, 2024.  (Harlan Decl., ¶ 3.)  Defense counsel declares that the sale of restaurant equipment and liquor license, and transfer of the lease, is in escrow and projected to be finalized in late October through early November.  (Supp. Harlan Decl., ¶ 5, Exhs. 1-2.)  The intended purchaser has no connection to Modern Shaman, LLC and is not owned by any members of Modern Shaman, LLC.  (Supp. Harlan Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.)  Defendant has no plans to reopen the Greenleaf location nor at any alternate physical nor virtual location.  (Supp. Harlan Decl., ¶¶ 10.)  Second, the Court finds the intervening events of closing the restaurant and selling the premises “have completely and irrevocably eradicated the effects of the alleged violation.”  (Davis, supra, 440 U.S. at p. 631.) Plaintiff’s claim for injunctive relief against Defendant is moot because the restaurant is closed and not reopening.  (Kohler v. Southland Foods, Inc. (9th Cir. 2011) 459 Fed. Appx. 617, 618 (“Kohler’s claims for prospective injunctive relief [under Title III of the ADA] became moot once the restaurant ceased operation.”)  Because the undisputed facts show that Defendant’s restaurant and website is closed to the public and has ceased operations, there is nothing to enjoin. The Court concludes the injunctive relief claim is MOOT.

 

However, the closure of the restaurant does not moot Plaintiff’s Unruh Act claim for statutory damages arising from barriers encountered during her visits to Defendant’s website, as a claim for statutory damages looks to past harm.  (Arroyo v. Aldabashi (N.D. Cal., Oct. 15, 2018) 2018 WL 4961637, at *5 [“Because a claim for damages under the Unruh Act looks to past harm, a change in ownership does not moot this claim.”]; see also Johnson v. Rai Rocklin Investments, LLC (E.D. Cal., Aug. 9, 2017) 2017 WL 3421848, at *2–3 [declining to dismiss an Unruh Act claim for damages after dismissing an ADA claim for injunctive relief as moot].)

 

Defendant asserts that its website was fixed.  But making a website comply with accessibility standards does not moot a Plaintiff’s claim for statutory damages.  Defendant’s motion has not shown that Plaintiff is not entitled to statutory damages for a violation of the Unruh Act.  It merely argues that since injunctive relief can no longer be provided, the case is moot and should be dismissed.  (Motion, p. 5 [“Because the Defendant made the necessary corrections and ceased all operations, including the operation of the website, there is no longer any meaningful injunctive relief that this Court can provide.  Accordingly, the case is moot and should be dismissed.”[.)

 

As Plaintiff’s claim for statutory damages remains active, Defendant’s motion to dismiss the case is DENIED.
