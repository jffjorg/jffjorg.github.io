Case Number: 23TRCV03440    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

KIG PROPERTIES, LLC,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03440

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

ROSALBA QUIROZ,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 5, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff KIG Properties, LLC

Responding Party:                  None

Motion for Terminating Sanction to Strike Answer and Enter Default

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed. 

RULING

The motion is GRANTED.  The Court orders that the answer filed on November 17, 2023 be STRICKEN and a default entered against defendant Rosalba Quiroz.

BACKGROUND

On October 17, 2023, plaintiff KIG Properties, LLC filed a complaint against Rosalba Quiroz for (1) nuisance, (2) negligence, and (3) preliminary and permanent injunction.

Plaintiff alleges that plaintiff and defendant’s properties are separated by a wall located on the western boundary of the Quiroz property. In 2012, defendant constructed or made alterations to the wall between the properties and thereafter KIG began experiencing water intrusion issues as a result of the wall preventing adequate drainage of rainwater from the building’s foundation. At the time the problem first occurred, KIG paid defendant’s son $15,000 to repair, re-stucco, and waterproof the wall. The repair failed and KIG has continued to experience water intrusion, which has resulted in damage to its property. KIG desired to fix the issue and was willing to do so at its expense. Defendant refused to cooperate with KIG and refused KIG’s requests for access to defendant’s property. In June 2023, communications began between KIG and defendant’s son. In mid-August 2023, they discussed an indemnification agreement, which defendant’s son agreed to sign and to allow access. In September 2023, plaintiff contacted defendant’s son to coordinate and schedule the repair work but he failed to respond and to sign the agreement.

On November 17, 2023, defendant (self-represented) filed an answer.

On August 1, 2024, the Court granted plaintiff’s motions to compel and to deem admissions.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

If a party fails to comply with a court order compelling discovery responses or attendance at a deposition, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions.  CCP § 2025.450(h) (depositions); § 2030.290(c) (interrogatories); § 2031.300(c) (demands for production of documents).  CCP § 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . .  [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . . .”  CCP § 2023.010 provides that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery. . . .”

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.  ‘Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.’ . . . [C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal. App. 4th 967, 992 (citation omitted). 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances:  [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’”  Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390 (citation omitted).  “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’”  Id. (citation omitted).

“A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions . . . :  (1) . . . there must be a failure to comply with a court order, and (2) the failure must be willful.”  Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal. App. 4th 1315, 1327 (citation omitted).  

A trial court’s order to impose terminating sanctions will be reversed only if it “was arbitrary, capricious, or whimsical.”  Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal. App. 5th 690, 702 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff KIG Properties, LLC requests an order for terminating sanctions against defendant Rosalba Quiroz and for monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,602.50.

On August 1, 2024, the Court granted plaintiff’s motions to compel defendant to serve responses without objections to plaintiff’s form and special interrogatories and requests for production of documents within twenty days.  The Court also granted plaintiff’s motion to deem admitted the truth of the matters in plaintiff’s requests for admissions.  The Court further ordered defendant to pay sanctions in the amount of $1,440 by September 2, 2024.  Plaintiff gave notice of the ruling to defendant on August 8, 2024.

The Court notes that defendant did not file an opposition to the discovery motions or appear at the hearing.

Plaintiff asserts that defendant’s responses and documents were due by September 3 and the sanction payment was due by September 12, 2024.  Plaintiff contends that defendant has failed to serve the discovery responses or pay the sanctions.

The Court notes that a court may not issue a terminating sanction for failure to pay a monetary discovery sanction.  Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal. App. 4th 608, 610, 615.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that defendant has engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process because defendant has willfully failed to comply with the Court’s August 1, 2024 order to serve responses without objections to plaintiff’s written discovery requests.  Defendant failed to oppose this motion or to provide an explanation as to the failure to provide the court-ordered responses.  See also Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal. App. 3d 771, 787 (“Lack of diligence may be deemed willful in the sense that the party understood his obligation, had the ability to comply, and failed to comply.”).  The Court notes that it had also imposed monetary sanctions against defendant for the failure to provide initial responses.  Neither the Order itself requiring responses to the discovery nor the imposition of monetary sanctions has been effective to produce defendant’s compliance with the discovery orders.  Further, defendant’s failure to comply is detrimental to plaintiff’s ability to prepare for trial.

The Court is mindful of the obligation to provide the defendant an opportunity to comply with her obligations and to participate in these proceedings.  The Court shall not ignore the preference to allow parties to have cases resolved on the merits.  But, this preference to “go slowly” toward terminating sanctions also recognizes that when the recalcitrant party makes it clear that he/she or it had no intention of complying with their obligations to the court and the other parties, terminating sanctions may be in order.  Such is the case here.

The motion is thus GRANTED.  The Court orders that the defendant’s answer be stricken and a default issued.  The Court denies plaintiff’s request for additional monetary sanctions as the terminating sanctions is sufficient.

 

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.  The Court orders that the answer filed on November 17, 2023 be STRICKEN and a default entered.  No monetary sanctions are awarded in light of the tact that the terminating sanction is granted.  Had the Court ordered a lesser sanction, it would have also awarded monetary sanctions.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED and the action is STAYED.

BACKGROUND

On March 6, 2024, Yingnan Cui filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. for violations under Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc. requests that the Court compel arbitration and to stay the case.

The complaint alleges that on March 7, 2023, plaintiff purchased a 2023 Tesla Model X.  Complaint, ¶5.  After plaintiff took possession of the vehicle and during the warranty period, the vehicle contained defects that substantially impair the use, safety, and/or value of the vehicle.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff provided defendant with sufficient opportunity to service or repair the vehicle.  Id., ¶13.  Defendant was unable and/or failed to service or repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts.  Id., ¶14.

            Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant Tesla presents evidence that on May  26, 2022, upon initially placing the order for the subject vehicle, plaintiff electronically accepted the terms and conditions of the Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”).  Defendant asserts that the MVOA contains an arbitration provision, which states, in part:  “Please carefully read this provision, which applies to any dispute between you and Tesla, Inc. and its affiliates. . . . [Y]ou agree that any dispute arising out of or relating to any aspect of the relationship between you and Tesla will not be decided by a judge or jury but instead by a single arbitrator in an arbitration administered by the [AAA] under its Consumer Arbitration Rules. . . .”  The provision contains an opt out clause and that if “you do not opt out, this agreement to arbitrate overrides any different arbitration agreement between us, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract.”  Raymond Kim decl., Exh. 1.

Defendant also contends that plaintiff took delivery of the subject vehicle on March 7, 2023, at which time plaintiff executed a Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) for the purchase of the subject vehicle on credit.  Defendant asserts that the RISC also contains an arbitration provision.  It states in part:  “1.  Either you or we may choose to have any dispute between us decided by arbitration and not in court or by jury trial. . . . Any claim or dispute, whether in contract, tort, statute or otherwise (including the interpretation and scope of this Arbitration Provision, and the arbitrability of the claim or dispute), between you and us or our employees, agents, successors or assigns, which arises out of or relates to your credit application, purchase or condition of this vehicle, this contract or any resulting transaction or relationship (including any such relationship with third parties who do not sign this contract) shall, at your or our election, be resolved by neutral, binding arbitration and not by a court action. […]”  “We” and “us” are defined as the seller, Tesla Motors, Inc.  See Kim decl., Exh. 2.

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

The Court finds that arbitration agreements exist between plaintiff and defendant Tesla (both the manufacturer and seller of the vehicle) and that plaintiff did not opt-out. 

            Scope of agreement

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims against it fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration provisions as they arise directly out of the relationship between plaintiff as purchaser/consumer and Tesla as the manufacturer/retailer/warrantor/repair facility and relate to the “purchase or condition” of the vehicle.

The Court finds that the scope of the arbitration provisions cover plaintiff’s claims against Tesla as they relate to the condition of the vehicle and arise out of the relationship.

Enforceability

                        Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

Defendant argues that the arbitration provisions are enforceable, citing to California Supreme Court case Sanchez v. Valencia Holding, Co., LLC, which “upheld the entirety of a substantially similar arbitration provision against a wide array of unconscionability challenges.”

Plaintiff did not file an opposition and has made no argument that the arbitration provisions are unconscionable.

The Court finds that even if the arbitration provisions are adhesion contracts, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration provision.  Moreover, as in Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 931, “there is no reason in this case to conclude that plaintiff lacked power to bargain.  In general, nothing prevents purchasers of . . . vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that plaintiff could not bargain in this case.”  The Court further finds little to no substantive unconscionability as the arbitration provisions do not lack mutuality.

The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

ORDER

The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

The action is STAYED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

HYUNDAI MOTOR AMERICA, et al.,

Hearing Date:                          November 5, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Hyundai Motor America

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Carlos Vasquez

Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The action is STAYED.

BACKGROUND

            On May 15, 2024, plaintiff Carlos Vasquez filed a complaint against defendant Hyundai Motor America for Song-Beverly Act violations.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Hyundai Motor America requests an order compelling plaintiff to binding arbitration.

            The complaint alleges that on July 13, 2021, plaintiff leased a 2021 Genesis G80.  These causes of action arise out of warranty and repair obligations of Hyundai in connection with the subject vehicle and for which Hyundai issued a written warranty.  Complaint, ¶8.  Hyundai warranted the subject vehicle and agreed to preserve and maintain the utility or performance of the vehicle or to provide compensation if there was a failure in such utility or performance.  In connection with the purchase, plaintiff received various warranties, including a five-year/60,000 mile express bumper to bumper warranty, a ten-year/100,000 mile powertrain warranty, which covers the engine and transmission.  Id., ¶9.  The subject vehicle was delivered to plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities, including engine, electrical, emission, and structural system defects.  Id., ¶10.  In August 2021, plaintiff first presented the vehicle for repairs and reported the armrest not staying down.  Id., ¶11.  Plaintiff presented the subject vehicle eight more times for concerns, in November 2022, December 2022, February 2023, April 2023, May 3, 2023, May 18, 2023, September 2023, November 2023.  Id., ¶¶12-19.  Plaintiff revokes acceptance of the lease agreement.  Id., ¶20.

Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant asserts that when plaintiff leased the subject vehicle on July 13, 2021, a Warranty accompanied the purchase, which included an agreement to arbitrate on pages 12-14. The section is titled, “BINDING ARBITRATION FOR CALIFORNIA VEHICLES ONLY.” 

It states in part:

“If you purchased or leased your Hyundai vehicle in the State of California, you and we, Hyundai Motor America, each agree that any claim or disputes between us (including between you and any of our affiliated companies) related to or arising out of your vehicle purchase, advertising for the vehicle, use of your vehicle, the performance of the vehicle, any service relating to the vehicle, the vehicle warranty, representations in the warranty, or the duties contemplated under the warranty, including without limitation claims related to false or misleading advertising, unfair competition, breach of contract or warranty, the failure to conform a vehicle to warranty, failure to repurchase or replace your vehicle, or claims for a refund or partial refund of your vehicle's purchase price (excluding personal injury claims), but excluding claims brought under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, shall be resolved by binding arbitration at either your or our election, even if the claim is initially filed in a court of law. If either you or we elect to resolve our dispute via arbitration (as opposed to in a court of law), such binding arbitration shall be administered by and through the American Arbitration Association (AAA) under its Consumer Arbitration Rules.”

 

It further states:

“IF YOU PURCHASED OR LEASED YOUR VEHICLE IN CALIFORNIA, YOUR WARRANTY IS MADE SUBJECT TO THE TERMS OF THIS BINDING ARBITRATION PROVISION. BY USING THE VEHICLE, OR REQUESTING OR ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS. IF YOU DO NOT AGREE WITH THESE TERMS, PLEASE CONTACT US AT OPT-OUT@HMAUSA.COM WITHIN THIRTY (30) DAYS OF YOUR PURCHASE OR LEASE TO OPT-OUT OF THIS ARBITRATION PROVISION.”

Defendant also contends that on July 13, 2021, plaintiff enrolled their vehicle in Hyundai’s Genesis Connected Services.  In order to enroll in Genesis Connected Services, customers must agree to the then-effective Connected Services Agreement (“CSA”).  In order for plaintiff to have enrolled in Hyundai’s Genesis services, they would have had to click a box to acknowledge that they “read and agreed to the Genesis Terms & Conditions” and then click the “Complete” button.  Defendant points out that the CSA provides an arbitration provision, which states:  “Hyundai and you agree to arbitrate any and all disputes and claims between us arising out of or relating to this Agreement, Connected Services, Connected Services Systems, Service Plans, your Vehicle, . . . This agreement to arbitrate is intended to be broadly interpreted and to make all disputes and claims between us subject to arbitration to the fullest extent permitted by law. . . . The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to:  claims based in contract, tort, warranty, statute, fraud, . . . .”  See Vijay Rao decl., Exhs. 1, 2; Ali Ameripour decl., Exh. 3.

Defendant also argues that it has standing to compel arbitration because it has provided the warranty directly to plaintiff in connection with their purchase of the vehicle.  Defendant contends that given that plaintiff has purchased the vehicle in California, is bringing this action pursuant to the Warranty, and has alleged that repairs have been performed under the Warranty, “it is clear that arbitration provision applies.”

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant failed to submit an authenticated copy of the purported Owner’s Handbook and Warranty Information booklet.  Plaintiff further contends that the Federal Trade Commission has expressly decided that binding mandatory arbitration of any warranty claim is unlawful, even if the warranty claims arise under state law.

In reply, defendant argues that it has properly established the existence of the agreements to arbitrate and that plaintiff has “not even remotely attempted to prove the falsity of the purported agreements.”  See Ali Ameripour second decl.

The Court finds that arbitration agreements exist—in the 2021 Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information and under the Connected Services Agreement.  Plaintiff has not shown the falsity of the arbitration provisions.  Although plaintiff did not sign the arbitration provision in the Warranty, that is not dispositive because plaintiff accepted the benefits found within the Warranty.  See Cavalry SPV I, LLC v. Watkins (2019) 36 Cal. App. 4th 1070, 1081 (“[A] party may accept a contract by conduct, as well as by words.”).

 

Scope

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration clauses as they arise directly out of the sale of the vehicle and the vehicle’s warranty.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the CSA’s arbitration provision does not apply to plaintiff’s claims because the “clear language of the CSA indicates that arbitration only applies to claims related to the ‘connected services’ that are the subject of the agreement, not the statutory warranty claims brought by plaintiff.” 

The Court finds that the arbitration provision in the “Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information,” specifically “Section 4 Genesis Warranty Information” covers the scope of plaintiff’s claims as it covers “all disputes and claims between us relating to or arising out of . . . use or performance of your vehicle, or the vehicle warranty.”  As alleged in the complaint, Hyundai issued a written warranty and the claims asserted in the claim “arise out of warranty and repair obligations” of Hyundai.  The arbitration provision in the CSA also appears to encompass plaintiff’s claims (“The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to:  claims based in . . . warranty, statute, . . .”).

 

Enforceability

            Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the arbitration provision in the Owner’s Handbook is procedurally unconscionable because plaintiff was never informed by South Bay Hyundai that any warranty claims against defendant would be subject to binding arbitration pursuant to an arbitration agreement that purportedly existed within the Owner’s Handbook out of which plaintiff would be required to opt.  See plaintiff’s decl.  Plaintiff asserts that he never signed any post-sale “arbitration agreement” because the subsequent “arbitration agreement” does not require a consumer’s express consent or contain an actual signature line.  Further, plaintiff argues, the “arbitration agreements” are pre-printed adhesion contracts drafted by defendant and that the provision in the Warranty’s Booklet was “buried”

Plaintiff also argues that the arbitration provisions are substantively unconscionable because they deprive plaintiff of a right to a jury trial and that he would receive “extremely limited due process” as discovery is limited.  Moreover, plaintiff contends, the Song-Beverly Act’s anti-waiver provision precludes arbitration because it would deprive plaintiff of a fundamental right to an action under Civil Code §1794.

In reply, defendant argues that the arbitration provisions are not unconscionable because plaintiff established mutual assent in their complaint to be bound by the warranty.  Defendant also asserts that plaintiff not only uses the warranty as a foundational basis for this action, but plaintiff concedes to having accepted the benefits under the warranty, and thus agreed to be bound by its terms.  Defendant further argues that plaintiff is estopped from refusing to comply with the arbitration provision that is contained in the warranty because he is attempting to enforce the warranty for plaintiff’s benefit.  Defendant also asserts that the arbitration provision is clearly labeled in the table of contents.  Plaintiff was under no obligation to purchase the vehicle, and he had the opportunity to opt out.  Defendant also points out that AAA’s Consumer Arbitration Rules do allow for discovery and the exchange of information between the parties and that AAA’s Rule 22 allows for additional discovery and states that the arbitrator may determine whether further information is needed to provide for a fundamentally fair process.  See Ramirez v. Charter Communications, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal. 4th 478 (analyzing same AAA rules).

The Court finds that even if the Warranty’s arbitration provision is an adhesion contract, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration clause.  Moreover, as in Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 931, “there is no reason in this case to conclude that plaintiff lacked power to bargain.  In general, nothing prevents purchasers of . . . vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that plaintiff could not bargain in this case.”  Moreover, the arbitration provision is not “buried” as the table of contents clearly identifies where the arbitration provision can be found and the opt-out provision is conspicuous (in bold and all capitals).  

As to substantive unconscionability, the Court finds little to none as the arbitration provisions do not lack mutuality.  The discovery rules apply equally to both parties, for example. 

The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

 ORDER

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.           

            Moving party [or prevailing party] is ordered to give notice of ruling.

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

BOUNTY ISLAND CORPORATION DBA SPARTAN COLLEGE OF AERONAUTICS AND TECHNOLOGY,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 5, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Bounty Island Corporation

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff First Class Vending, Inc.

Demurrer to Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first and second causes of action in the complaint.

BACKGROUND

            On June 27, 2024, First Class Vending, Inc. filed a complaint against Bounty Island Corporation dba Spartan College of Aeronautics and Technology for (1) breach of contract and (2) breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant demurs to the first and second causes of action in the complaint on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

            Under the first cause of action for breach of contract, the complaint alleges the parties entered into a written contract whereby plaintiff agreed to provide vending services to defendant.  Complaint, ¶7.  Defendant agreed that the contract would renew unless canceled via written notice, via registered mail, provided to plaintiff at least ninety days, but not more than 120 days before the expiration of the agreement.  Id., ¶8.  Defendant did not notify plaintiff of its intent to cancel the contract within the time period specified. The contract then renewed for a five-year term on April 19, 2024.  Id., ¶9.  Plaintiff performed all obligations required of it under the parties’ written agreement, except those obligations excused by the action or inaction of defendant.  Id., ¶10.  Defendant has breached the contract by failing to provide timely written notice of cancellation of the contract and stating that it would have its personnel unplug and move plaintiff’s machines so that they cannot be used by customers if the machines were not removed.  Id., ¶11.  On June 21, 2024, under protest, plaintiff removed its machines from the premise of defendant and declared defendant in breach of its contract with plaintiff.  Id., ¶12.  As a result of defendant’s breach of contract, plaintiff has incurred damages of which the exact amount is not known, but which plaintiff believes is more than $100,000.  Id., ¶13.

            Under the second cause of action for breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing, the complaint alleges that defendant breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing by threatening to move plaintiff’s machines so that said machines cannot be used by customers thereby terminating the contract prior to the expiration of the term without good cause.  Id., ¶20.  Defendant took such actions for the unlawful, unfair, and improper purpose of terminating the contract prior to the completion of the contract’s term.  Id., ¶21. 

            Defendant argues that the complaint fails to allege the existence of an enforceable agreement because the alleged agreement was signed by a different entity and that it is not enforceable against a new party.  Defendant asserts that the purported contract identifies “Spartan College of Aviation and Technology” as the “client,” and that defendant is not a signatory to the contract and the complaint does not allege how or why Bounty Island should be involved in this matter.  Defendant further argues that the contract lacks consideration because under the agreement, plaintiff has the exclusive “privilege” to sell its merchandise onside in its vending machines, and Bounty Island gets nothing in exchange for granting plaintiff this “privilege.”

            Defendant also argues that the second cause of action is duplicative of the first cause of action and is deficient as it fails to plead any predicate “agreement” with Bounty Island or damages distinct from a breach of contract claim.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient as plaintiff has alleged the existence of a contract between the parties.  Plaintiff asserts that the parties to the contract are readily identifiable, including defendant’s address and the signing authority for defendant.  Plaintiff also contends that defendant’s former president is “presumed to have read the document and see the error in the name” and that “[g]iven that there was no correction, he viewed the error as harmless and signed the Contract.”  Plaintiff argues that defendant’s “complicated DBA should not relieve it from its obligations under contract.”  Plaintiff also asserts that as to consideration, adequate consideration is a factual issue.  As for the second cause of action, plaintiff argues that the “facts go beyond a simple breach of contract” as defendant “deliberately interfered with the operation of” the vending machines by terminating the contract early, without cause, and interfering with plaintiff’s ability to continue to service customers by forcing plaintiff to pick up its machines far in advance of the expiration of the contract.  Plaintiff also argues that the demurrer is defective as it does not comply with Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1320(a).

            The Court finds that the allegations are insufficient as to the existence of an enforceable contract between the parties.  Both parties acknowledge that the “client” in the contract is a different name from defendant, and whether it is a scrivener’s error or otherwise is not alleged.  As for consideration, “[a] written instrument is presumptive evidence of a consideration.”  Civil Code §1614.  Thus, at the demurrer stage, the allegations meet the element of consideration.

            As for the second cause of action, whether it is duplicative is not a ground for demurrer.  As to whether there is an underlying contract, the allegations are insufficient because the contract attached identifies a different name other than plaintiff.

            As for whether the demurrer failed to comply with Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1320, it substantially complied and provided notice of the grounds.

            The demurrer is thus SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first and second cause of action.

 

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first and second causes of action.

BACKGROUND

On June 28, 2024, plaintiff Diane Ma filed a complaint against Luxe Development, Inc., Oren Levy, Luxe Home Solutions, Inc., Tariq Hassan Hill, and Business Alliance Insurance Company for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) recovery on surety bond, (4) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing (insurance bad faith), (5) intentional misrepresentation, (6), (7) statutory violation of Bus. & Prof. Code 7025-7034 and 7125.2, (8) conversion, and (9) unfair business practices. 

On July 10, 2024, defendant Tariq Hassan Hill filed a notice of stay of proceedings based on his Ch. 7 bankruptcy filing.

On August 15, 2024, a default was entered against Luxe Home Solutions, Inc.

On August 20, 2024, defaults were entered against Luxe Development, Inc. and Oren Levy.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  So, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487. 

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Diane Ma requests that the Court compel defendant Business Alliance Insurance Company to respond to plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

            Plaintiff explains that she served the written discovery requests on defendant on July 23, 2024.  Responses were due by August 26, 2024.  On August 29, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel sent an email to defense counsel that the responses were past due and objections were waived and requested responses by September 4.  Defense counsel claimed that the requests were propounded prematurely.  Plaintiff contends that counsel resolved the dispute regarding the timeliness of the discovery and the parties agreed the response and document production would be due on September 13, 2024.  When no responses were received, on September 16, 17, 25, 30 and October 2, plaintiff’s counsel sent numerous emails to defense counsel and discussed the issue on two or three telephone calls requesting that defendant provide the responses and produce responsive documents.  As of the date of the motion, plaintiff’s counsel had not received responses.

            In opposition, defendant argues that the motion is moot because it has served responses and produced documents.  Defendant also asserts that after receiving the case assignment, defense counsel began settlement discussions with plaintiff’s counsel which, if successful, would have obviated the need to respond to the outstanding discovery requests.  As to the untimely opposition, defendant explains that the day before the opposition was due, defense counsel underwent a “simple medical procedure but resulted” in his being out of the office and unable to work for several days.

            In reply, plaintiff argues that the opposition “completely fails to address Defendant’s refusal and failure to provide timely responses.”  Plaintiff contends that the discussions taking place were not really settlement discussions, and that those discussions “fizzled out in late September and officially terminated on October 1, 2024.”  Plaintiff reiterated that throughout September and as late as October 2, plaintiff’s counsel “made several demands for the discovery responses and documents and warned that a motion to compel would be filed no less than three times, yet Defendant ignored all such requests and warnings.”  Plaintiff disputes that defendant provided substantive responses and produced all responsive documents because defendant responded to seven of the eight requests that no documents exist but plaintiff has several documents in her possession, “which would clearly be kept in the file related to the claim Plaintiff made on the bond.”

            The Court finds that plaintiff properly served written discovery and defendant failed to timely serve responses, and thus has waived objections.  Based on defendant’s representation and plaintiff’s acknowledgment that defendant served responses and documents, the motion is MOOT.  The Court declines to review the sufficiency of defendant’s responses as it is beyond the scope of the motion.  The parties are, however, ordered to meet and confer regarding the assertion of the insufficiency of the late responses.

            Accordingly, the motion is MOOT.

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

            Plaintiff requests $3240 ($795/hr.) in sanctions against defendant Business Alliance Corporation and its counsel of record, David Dorenfeld, Esq.  The Court finds that sanctions are warranted as the responses were served after the due date and after the motion date and defendant has not provided substantial justification for doing so.  The Court finds that $1,560 is a reasonable amount to be imposed against defendant and its attorney of record.

 

ORDER

The motion is MOOT.

The parties are ordered to meet and confer regarding the claimed insufficiency of the late responses.

The Court orders that defendant Business Alliance Corporation and its attorney of record, David K. Dorenfeld (SBN 145056) pay a monetary sanction to plaintiff in the amount of $1,560 within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
