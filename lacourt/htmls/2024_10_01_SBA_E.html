R&LS INVESTMENT Inc., et al.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             October 1, 2024         

 

Moving Parties:          Defendants and Cross-Defendants Richard Schulman, and Michael Lease

Responding Party:      Defendants and Cross-Complainants, Kenneth Chung, Janice Jeong Ah Lee, And Dream Realty Asset Management

HEARING: DEMURRER TO Kenneth Chung, Janice Jeong Ah Lee, And Dream Realty Asset Management’s CROSS-COMPLAINT

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

RULING

            The Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer as to the causes of action for contribution with LEAVE TO AMEND and OVERRULES as to indemnity.

 

BACKGROUND

            On April 3, 2023, Plaintiff Shawn Choi filed a complaint against Defendants R&LS Investment Inc., Richard Schulman, Michael Lease, Andrew Rowe, ADR Investment Group, LLC, Dream Realty Asset Mgt., Kenneth Chung, and Ah Janice Jeong Lee, alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (3) Negligence; and (4) Fraud.

 

            On March 13, 2024, Cross-Complainants Andrew Rowe and ADR Investment Group, LLC, filed a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants R&LS Investment Inc. dba Keller Williams Santa Monica, Richard Schulman, and Michael Lease, alleging (1) indemnity; (2) contribution; and (3) negligence.

 

            On March 20, 2024, Cross-Complainants Dream Realty Asset Management, Kenneth Chung, and Janice Jeong Ah Lee filed a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants R&LS Investment Inc. dba Keller Williams Santa Monica, Richard Schulman, Michael Lease, Andrew Rowe, and ADR Investment Group, LLC, alleging (1) equitable indemnity; (2) contribution; and (3) declaratory relief.

 

            On April 22, 2024, Richard Schulman and Michael Lease filed the demurrer to the cross-complaint filed by Andrew Rowe and ADR Investment Group, LLC.

 

 On September 17, 2024, Cross-Complainants opposed.

 

On September 23, 2024, Cross-Defendants replied.

 

            On May 7, 2024, 2024, Richard Schulman and Michael Lease (Cross-Defendants) filed the instant demurrer to the cross-complaint filed by Dream Realty Asset Management, Kenneth Chung, and Janice Jeong Ah Lee (Cross-Complainants.)

 

On September 17, 2024, Cross-Complainants opposed.

 

On September 23, 2024, Cross-Defendants Dream Realty Asset Management, Kenneth Chung, and Janice Jeong Ah Lee replied.

 

            The two demurrers are set to be heard on October 1, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012)

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41 subd. (a).)

 

Cross-Defendants’ counsel provides a declaration showing the parties’ meet and confer efforts and that they were unable to informally resolve the dispute. (Hernandez Decl. ¶ 3-8.) The Court finds this sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. 

 

B.    Indemnity

 

A cause of action for equitable indemnity requires the following elements: (1) a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is contractually or equitably responsible. (CW Howe Partners v. Mooradian (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 688, 700.)

 

“Although the body of law defining and applying principles of equitable indemnity has not fully gelled but is still evolving, one thing is clear: The doctrine applies only among defendants who are jointly and severally liable to the plaintiff. As plaintiff maintains, joint and several liability in the context of equitable indemnity is fairly expansive. We agree it is not limited to ‘the old common term ‘joint tortfeasor’ … .’ It can apply to acts that are concurrent or successive, joint or several, as long as they create a detriment caused by several actors. One factor is necessary, however. With limited exception, there must be some basis for tort liability against the proposed indemnitor. Generally, it is based on a duty owed to the underlying plaintiff, although vicarious liability and strict liability also may sustain application of equitable indemnity. In addition, implied contractual indemnity between the indemnitor and the indemnitee can provide a basis for equitable indemnity.” (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc. v. Forcum/Mackey Construction, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal. App. 4th 848, 852 (citations omitted).) 

 

Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants have not established that the parties are joint tortfeasors or jointly and severally liable for an indivisible injury. (Demurrer p. 6.) Additionally, Cross-Defendants allege that the Cross-Complaint fails to show any causal connection between Cross-Defendants' alleged actions and the damages suffered by Plaintiff. (Demurrer p. 6.)

 

Furthermore, Cross-Defendants argue that the original complaint against Cross-Defendants was dismissed without prejudice, meaning no current claims exist against them, removing the grounds for Cross-Complainants to seek indemnity. (Demurrer p. 7.) Moreover, any potential negligence claim is barred by the statute of limitations, which has already expired. (Demurrer p. 7; Code. Civ. Proc. § 335.1; Thomas v. Gilliland (2002) 95 Cal. App. 4th 427, 524.) Since the negligence claim was not refiled within the prescribed timeframe, Cross-Complainants cannot pursue indemnity from Cross-Defendants. As a result, Cross-Complainants have not sufficiently pled facts to establish a valid cause of action for indemnity, and the court should sustain the demurrer. (Opposition p. 7.)

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that they have sufficiently pled facts supporting their claim. (Opposition p. 2.) They allege that Cross-Defendants, as real estate agents for the sellers of the property in question, failed to disclose a death on the property, contributing to the plaintiff's damages. Cross-Complaints state that parties do not need to be joint tortfeasors to succeed on a claim of indemnity. (Time for Living v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All Am. Dev. Co. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 30, 40.) The Cross-Complaint asserts that Cross-Defendants’ failure to disclose relevant information about the property contributed to the harm suffered by the plaintiff. (Opposition p. 3.) Further, California law allows Cross-Complainants to seek indemnity from Cross-Defendants, even if there is no pending claim against them, and the statute of limitations for indemnity only begins after a settlement or judgment, which has not occurred. (Opposition p. 3; Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10 subd. (b).)

 

In reply, Cross Defendants reiterate that Cross-Complainants have not shown that the parties are joint tortfeasors because, in the complaint, Cross-Complainants make no mention of themselves. (Reply p. 4.) Furthermore, Cross-defendants argue that the parties are not joint tortfeasors because they had separate duties to Plaintiff and, therefore, did not create the same harm. (Reply p. 3.)

 

A cause of action for equitable indemnity requires the following elements: (1) a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is contractually or equitably responsible. (CW Howe Partners, 43 Cal.App.5th 688, 700.) Paragraph 19 of the Cross Complaint states, “Plaintiff has alleged certain injuries and damages because ROWE and ADR, as well as R&LS and its real estate agents, LEASE and SCHULMAN, failed to make certain disclosures (e.g., death which occurred at the Subject Property), when the Subject Property was up for sale.” (Cross-Complaint ¶ 19.) This allegation includes fault by both parties, failing to make certain disclosure, which led to damages to Cross-Complainants, the lawsuit. Therefore, Cross-Complaints have pleaded sufficient facts to state a cause of action for indemnity. (See, CW Howe Partners, 43 Cal.App.5th 688, 700.)

 

Cross-defendants argue that the parties are not joint tortfeasors, and thus, there is no claim for indemnity. However, the Court has found that for indemnity cases, the term joint tortfeasors is interpreted broadly to apply to “acts that are concurrent or successive, joint or several, as long as they create a detriment caused by several actors.” (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc, 119 Cal. App. 4th 848, 852.) Thus, Cross-Defendants argument fails because Cross-Complainants have pleaded that both parties' acts, either concurrently or subsequently, have led to Plaintiffs' damages. (See, BFGC Architects Planners, Inc, 119 Cal. App. 4th 848, 852; Cross-Complaint ¶ 19.)

 

            Therefore, the court overrules the Demurrer as to the claim of indemnity.

 

C.    Contribution

 

A cause of action for contribution requires the following elements: (1) money judgment; (2) rendered jointly against defendants; (3) in tort action; (4) application of equity principles; (5) payment by tortfeasor of more than pro rata share; and (5) no intentional injury by the tortfeasor. Code of Civ. Proc. Section §875. (See also General Elec. Co. v. State of Cal. ex rel. Dept. Pub. Wks.¿(1973) 32 Cal.App.3d 918, 925.) 

 

            The Cross-Complainants have agreed to dismiss, without prejudice, the cause of action for contribution.

 

            Therefore, the court sustains the demurrer as to the claim of contribution with leave to amend.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012)

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41 subd. (a).)

 

Cross-Defendants’ counsel provides a declaration showing the parties’ meet and confers efforts and that they were unable to informally resolve the dispute. (Hernandez Decl. ¶ 3-8.) The Court finds this sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. 

 

B.    Indemnity

 

A cause of action for equitable indemnity requires the following elements: (1) a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is contractually or equitably responsible. (CW Howe Partners v. Mooradian (2019) 43 Cal.App.5th 688, 700.)

 

“Although the body of law defining and applying principles of equitable indemnity has not fully gelled but is still evolving, one thing is clear: The doctrine applies only among defendants who are jointly and severally liable to the plaintiff. As plaintiff maintains, joint and several liability in the context of equitable indemnity is fairly expansive. We agree it is not limited to ‘the old common term ‘joint tortfeasor’ … .’ It can apply to acts that are concurrent or successive, joint or several, as long as they create a detriment caused by several actors. One factor is necessary, however. With limited exception, there must be some basis for tort liability against the proposed indemnitor. Generally, it is based on a duty owed to the underlying plaintiff, although vicarious liability and strict liability also may sustain application of equitable indemnity. In addition, implied contractual indemnity between the indemnitor and the indemnitee can provide a basis for equitable indemnity.” (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc. v. Forcum/Mackey Construction, Inc. (2004) 119 Cal. App. 4th 848, 852 (citations omitted).) 

 

Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants have failed to state a valid cause of action for indemnity due to several key deficiencies. First, they assert that the Cross-Complaint lacks any specific facts demonstrating fault on their part or indicating that they caused any damages to Cross-Complainants. Instead, the Cross-Complaint merely includes a vague, hypothetical statement that if Cross-Complainants are held liable for damages, those damages were primarily caused by Cross-Defendants. This conclusory assertion, according to Cross-Defendants, is insufficient to establish fault or any actual damages at this stage. (Demurrer p. 10.)

 

Second, Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants have failed to establish that both parties are joint tortfeasors, which is necessary for a claim of equitable indemnity. For indemnity to apply, the parties must be jointly and severally liable for an indivisible injury. (The Travelers Indemnity Co. of Connecticut v. Navigators Specialty Ins. Co. (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 341, 362.)  However, the Cross-Complaint does not demonstrate how Cross-Defendants' actions are causally linked to the same injury for which Cross-Complainants are being sued. Instead, it merely asserts that if Plaintiff recovers damages, Cross-Complainants would be entitled to indemnity, without detailing how Cross-Defendants' actions contributed to the damages. (Demurrer p. 10-11.)

 

Third, Cross-Defendants note that there are no pending claims against them, as Plaintiff dismissed the underlying complaint against Cross-Defendants without prejudice on January 11, 2024. Since no claims are currently pending, Cross-Complainants lack grounds to seek indemnity. Furthermore, even if Plaintiff were to refile the underlying complaint, the statute of limitations for a negligence claim would have already expired—either two years after the sale of the property on December 26, 2019, or two years after March 10, 2022, when Plaintiff allegedly learned of the non-disclosure of deaths at the property. Cross-Defendants argue that the statute of limitations continues to run even after a claim is dismissed without prejudice, and therefore, Cross-Complainants cannot rely on the possibility of a future refiling to seek indemnity. (Demurrer p. 11-12; Thomas v. Gilliland (2002) 95 Cal. App. 4th 427, 524.)

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that their indemnity claim is valid and timely under California law, even though Cross-Defendants were dismissed from the original lawsuit. They assert that a defendant can file a cross-complaint for indemnity “against a concurrent tortfeasor for partial indemnity…even when such concurrent tortfeasor has not been named as a defendant in the original complaint.” (American Motorcycle Ass’n v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1978) 20 Cal. 3d 578, 607; Opposition p. 7.)

 

Cross-Complainants argue that the dismissal of Cross-Defendants does not preclude their indemnity claim, as there has been no court determination of a good faith settlement, which would have otherwise cut off their right to seek indemnity. (Opposition p. 7.) They maintain that the cross-complaint incorporates all relevant facts from Plaintiff’s original complaint, including specific allegations against Cross-Defendants. (Opposition p. 7.) Cross-Complainants also argue that they acted on the advice of Cross-Defendants, disclosing what they were advised to disclose during the real estate transaction. (Opposition p. 7.) Therefore, if they are found liable for any errors or omissions, Cross-Defendants should be responsible for indemnifying them since their advice allegedly contributed to the situation. (Opposition p. 7.)

 

            In reply, Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants' claim for indemnity fails because the Cross-Complaint lacks sufficient factual allegations and fails to allege that Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants are joint tortfeasors. (Reply p. 2.)

 

Cross-Defendants assert that the Cross-Complaint does not establish that Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants are joint tortfeasors, a requirement for both indemnity and contribution claims. While Cross-Complainants incorporate Plaintiff's original complaint into their Cross-Complaint, this is insufficient because the duties owed by Cross-Complainants (as sellers of the property) and Cross-Defendants (as listing agents) are distinct and independent. (Reply p. 2.) They assert that Cross-Complainants had a duty to disclose the deaths of the previous owners, while Cross-Defendants, under California law, had a duty to conduct a competent inspection and disclose any material facts revealed by that inspection. Since these duties are separate, Cross-Complainants have not adequately pled that both parties are joint and severally liable, as required for indemnity. (Reply p. 2.)

 

Second, Cross-Defendants contend that the Cross-Complaint fails to allege an indivisible injury. Although the Cross-Complaint claims that damages were caused by Cross-Defendants’ actions, it does not specify how both Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants are liable for the same indivisible injury to Plaintiff. The duties each owed to Plaintiff are different, and the Cross-Complaint does not clarify how both parties’ conduct contributed to a single, indivisible harm. (Reply p. 3.)

 

            The Court finds that Cross-Complainants have pleaded specific facts that demonstrate fault on the part of Cross-Defendants. Cross-Complainants incorporate the Complaint, which pleads that Cross-Defendants failed to notify Plaintiffs of the violent death that occurred in the house and that the Cross-Defendants were aware of the deaths and their duty to disclose such facts. (Cross Complaint ¶ 10; Complaint ¶ 19, 28, 29.) Cross-Defendants argue that these facts do not prove that Cross-Defendants are joint tortfeasors because Cross-Complainants, as sellers of the house, and Cross-Defendants, as listing agents, do not have the same duties. However, even if this argument is correct, it is immaterial. A claim of indemnity may succeed as long as both parties created the detriment, whether they are joint tortfeasors is inconsequential. (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc., 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852.) Defendants also argue, without citing any authority, that Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants are not liable for an indivisible injury as they have separate duties to Plaintiff. However, all that is required for a finding of indemnity is concurrent actions that cause harm to a third party. (BFGC Architects Planners, Inc., 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852.) Here, Cross-Complainant has pleaded that both Cross-Complainant and Cross-Defendant concurrently failed to disclose the previous deaths in the house, leading to harm to Plaintiff in the form of negligence and fraud. (See, BFGC Architects Planners, Inc., 119 Cal.App.4th 848, 852; Cross Complaint ¶ 10; Complaint ¶ 19, 28, 29,48-58.)

 

            Therefore, the Court overrules the Demurrer as to the claim of indemnity.

 

C.    Contribution

 

A cause of action for contribution requires the following elements: (1) money judgment; (2) rendered jointly against defendants; (3) in tort action; (4) application of equity principles; (5) payment by tortfeasor of more than pro rata share; and (5) no intentional injury by the tortfeasor. Code of Civ. Proc. Section §875. (See also General Elec. Co. v. State of Cal. ex rel. Dept. Pub. Wks.¿(1973) 32 Cal.App.3d 918, 925.) 

 

Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants have failed to state a valid cause of action for contribution. They assert that equitable contribution involves recovering from a co-obligor who shares liability, but the Cross-Complaint only makes a broad, conclusory allegation that Cross-Defendants were partially responsible for Plaintiff’s injuries without providing any specific facts demonstrating shared liability. Cross-Defendants state that this lack of detail is detrimental as it fails to establish that they are co-obligors with Cross-Complainants. (Demurrer p. 12.)

 

Furthermore, Cross-Defendants contend that the claim for contribution is premature. Under California law, a right to contribution arises only after a judgment has been rendered that declares more than one party jointly liable. (Code of Civ. Proc. Section § 875.) Since there has been no such judgment in this case, Cross-Complainants cannot assert a valid claim for contribution at this stage. (Demurrer p. 12.) Cross-Defendants also point out that Cross-Complainants have not alleged that they paid more than their pro-rata share of any judgment, which is required for a contribution claim. (Demurrer p. 12.)

 

            In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that their contribution claim is valid and authorized under California Civil Procedure Code section 875. A contribution claim arises when one defendant has paid more than their pro rata share of a judgment that is jointly rendered against multiple defendants. Cross-Complainants contend that, even though Cross-Defendants were dismissed, this does not bar the contribution claim, as no court has found a good faith settlement that would eliminate their liability for contribution. Civ. Pro. Code §877; (L.C. Rudd & Son, Inc. v. Super. Ct., (1997) 60 Cal. Rptr. 2d 703.)

 

They argue that a contribution claim can be filed against concurrent tortfeasors who were not originally named in the plaintiff’s complaint. (American Motorcycle Ass’n, 20 Cal. 3d 578, 607) Under sections 428.10 and 428.20 of the Code of Civil Procedure, additional parties may be joined in a cross-complaint, and Cross-Complainants assert that all relevant facts were included in their cross-complaint by incorporating Plaintiff’s original complaint, which was attached as an exhibit.

 

Furthermore, Cross-Complainants argue that they relied on the advice of their agents and brokers during the transaction and disclosed what they were advised to disclose. As such, if they are found liable for any errors or omissions, they believe that Cross-Defendants should share in the liability since it was the advice of Cross-Defendants that led to the current situation. Therefore, Cross-Complainants maintain that their contribution claim is valid and that Cross-Defendants should be held jointly responsible if liability is imposed. However, these statements are not pleaded in the Cross-Complaint. (Opposition p. 8.)

 

            In reply, Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants' claim for contribution fails because the Cross-Complaint lacks sufficient factual allegations and fails to allege that Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants are joint tortfeasors. (Reply p. 2.)

 

Cross-Defendants assert that the Cross-Complaint does not establish that Cross-Complainants and Cross-Defendants are joint tortfeasors, a requirement for both indemnity and contribution claims. While Cross-Complainants incorporate Plaintiff's original complaint into their Cross-Complaint, this is insufficient because the duties owed by Cross-Complainants, as sellers of the property, and Cross-Defendants, as listing agents, are distinct and independent. (Reply p. 2.) Cross-Complainants had a duty to disclose the deaths of the previous owners, while Cross-Defendants, under California law, had a duty to conduct a competent inspection and disclose any material facts revealed by that inspection. Since these duties are separate, Cross-Complainants have not adequately pled that both parties are joint and severally liable, as required for indemnity and contribution. (Reply p. 2.)

 

            The Court finds that Cross-Complainants’ claim for contribution is untimely. A right to contribution can exist only after the rendition of a judgment declaring more than one defendant jointly liable to the plaintiff. (Coca-Cola Bottling Co. v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1372, 1378; Code Civ. Proc., § 875, subd. (c).) In the Cross-Complaint, Cross-Complainants state that if Cross-Complainants are held liable, then Cross-Dependents are obligated to contribute to the extent the Court finds them at fault. (Cross-Complaint ¶ 17.) Since a judgment has not been rendered in this matter, no contribution can be sought. (Code Civ. Proc., § 875, subd. (c).) Therefore, Cross-Complainants have not pleaded facts sufficient to sustain a cause of action for contribution.

           

The Demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the cause of action of contribution.

 

D.    Negligence

 

“The elements of a negligence cause of action are duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)¿ “Ordinarily, negligence may be alleged in general terms, without specific facts showing how the injury occurred, but there are limits to the generality with which a plaintiff is permitted to state his cause of action, and the plaintiff must indicate the acts or omissions which are said to have been negligently performed. He may not recover upon the bare statement that the defendant’s negligence has caused him injury.” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527 [Internal quotations and ellipses omitted].) “However, there is no requirement that plaintiff identify and allege the precise moment of the injury or the exact nature of the wrongful act.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 [internal brackets omitted].) 

 

Cross-Defendants argue that Cross-Complainants have failed to properly state a cause of action for negligence. They outline that a valid negligence claim requires showing duty, breach, causation, and damages, but Cross-Complainants have not sufficiently established these elements. While negligence can often be pleaded in general terms, Cross-Defendants emphasize that Cross-Complainants have not identified any specific "common law duties" that Cross-Defendants owed them, nor have they detailed how these duties were breached. (Demurrer p. 14) The allegations are described as vague and conclusory, lacking the necessary specifics to support a claim for negligence. (Demurrer p.14.)

 

Additionally, Cross-Defendants argue that the statute of limitations for bringing a negligence claim has expired. Under California law, negligence claims must be filed within two years of the incident. (Cal. Code. Civ. Proc. § 335.1.)  Cross-Defendants assert that the relevant "incident" occurred either on December 2, 2018, when Cross-Complainants purchased the property with knowledge of the previous owners' deaths, or on December 26, 2019, when Cross-Complainants sold the property to the Plaintiff. (Demurrer p. 14.) Since the Cross-Complaint was not filed until March 12, 2024, Cross-Defendants contend that the two-year statute of limitations had already expired by December 26, 2021, making the negligence claim untimely. (Demurrer p. 14.)

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that the negligence claim should not be dismissed based on the statute of limitations, as the Demurrer does not clearly establish when the statute began to accrue. (Opposition p. 8.) They assert that this lack of clarity is problematic because the defense must be apparent from the face of the complaint. Even if the negligence claim is barred by the two-year statute of limitations under Civil Code Section 2079.4, Cross-Complainants propose an alternative solution: amending the claim to assert a breach of fiduciary duty. (Opposition p. 8.)

 

A breach of fiduciary duty claim is not subject to the two-year statute of limitations under Civil Code Section 2079.4 and is governed by the delayed discovery rule. (Opposition p. 9.) This rule delays the claim's accrual until the plaintiff becomes aware of the injury and its negligent cause. Cross-Complainants argue that they had no reason to suspect a disclosure problem until they were sued by Plaintiff on April 3, 2023. (Opposition p. 9.) They assert that any failure to disclose the deaths at the property resulted from the advice given by Cross-Defendants, and a breach of fiduciary duty would cover this type of error or omission. (Opposition p. 9.)

 

            Here, the Court finds that the negligence claim is untimely. Cross-Complainants argue that Cross-Defendants fail to show that there is a defect on the face of the pleading regarding the statute of limitations as they do not state when it began to run. However, Cross-Defendants show that whether the statute of limitations began to run on Dec 2, 2018, when the house was sold, or on December 26, 2019, is immaterial as the instant cross-complaint was not filed until March 12, 2024, long after the statute has expired for both dates. (Cal. Code. Civ. Proc. § 335.1.) Cross-complaints acknowledge the issue and ask for leave to amend. (Opposition p. 8.)

 

            Therefore, the Demurrer is sustained as to the cause of action of negligence with leave to amend.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Judgment on the Pleadings

 

A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings where the court has no jurisdiction over the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint or the complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438 subd. (c)(1)(B).) A motion for judgment on the pleadings is analogous to a general demurrer. (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.) Like demurrers, motions for judgment on the pleadings challenge the legal sufficiency of the allegations, not their veracity. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿¿“The motion provided for in this section may be made as to either of the following: (A) The entire complaint or cross-complaint or as to any of the causes of action stated therein. (B) The entire answer or one or more of the affirmative defenses outlined in the answer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438 subd. (c)(2).) “The motion provided for in this section may be granted with or without leave to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438 subd. (h)(1).) All facts alleged in the complaint are deemed admitted, and the complaint is given a reasonable interpretation as a whole and all parts in their context. (Lance, 44 Cal.App.4th at p. 198.) The court does not, however, do so for “conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or [judicially noticed] facts.” (Stevenson Real Estate Servs., Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Servs., Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-20.)¿ Any defects must either appear on the face of the pleading, or else be taken by judicial notice. (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-22.) A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be granted when, “under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Southern California Edison Co. v. City of Victorville (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 218, 227.) If a motion for judgment on the pleadings is granted, leave to amend “should be granted if there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action.” (Virginia G. v. ABC Unified School Dist.¿(1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1852.)¿ 

 

Right to Partition

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 872.710, for purposes of determining the right of partition, the court must first determine whether the plaintiff has the right to partition the property. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.710.)  Partition of concurrent interests is generally a right unless there is a valid waiver. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.710.)  For successive estates, partition is allowed if it benefits all parties involved. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.710.)  The court will consider factors such as the financial burdens of taxes or repairs, changes in the property’s character or circumstances, and the original intent behind creating the successive estates, weighing these factors in light of equity and the interests of the current owners. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.710.)

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

The Court may take judicial notice of records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States. (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (d).) The Court may also take judicial notice of “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (h).) 

 

            Here, Plaintiff requests that the court take judicial notice of the Complaint, Verified Answer, Requests for Admission, and the Response to the Request for Admissions. Defendant raised no opposition to the request.

 

            The Court takes judicial notice of the Complaint, Verified Answer, Requests for Admission, and the Response to the Request for Admissions.

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the moving party must meet and confer, in person or by telephone, with the party who filed the pleading subject to the motion, to determine if an agreement can be reached that resolves the claims to be raised in the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439 subd. (a).) The moving party must file a declaration stating either the means by which the moving party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading, and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the claims, or that the party who filed the pleading failed to respond to the meet and confer request or failed to meet and confer in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439 subd. (a)(3).)¿ 

 

Plaintiff’s counsel’ declares that on May 31, 2024, she met and conferred with Defendant’s counsel over the phone, where she identified all the specific allegations that she believed were subject to judgment and identified with legal support the basis of the claim. (Sikavi Decl. ¶ 10.) The parties were unable to informally resolve the disputes. (Id. ¶ 11.) The Court finds this sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. 

 

B.    Judgment on the Pleadings

 

This lawsuit involves a partition action for a single-family home located at 27840 Palos Verdes Drive East, Rancho Palos Verdes, California. Plaintiff seeks an interlocutory order to confirm the ownership interests of the parties, appoint a referee, Darin DeRenzis, to market and sell the property, and issue instructions to the referee. An interlocutory order must address three key issues: (1) the plaintiff’s right to partition, (2) the parties’ interests in the property, and (3) the manner of partition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.720.)

 

After Plaintiff filed a verified Complaint, Defendant filed the verified Answer, admitting the ownership percentages of the property and acknowledging that the property cannot be physically divided. (Answer ¶ 3-4.) In response to Requests for Admissions, Defendant confirmed the joint tenancy and the parties’ ownership interests. (Plaintiff Request for Judicial Notice, Exhibit 4 p. 2.) Plaintiff asserts that this establishes that (1) Plaintiff is an owner with the right to partition, (2) Defendant’s admissions conclusively determine the parties’ interests, and (3) the property cannot be physically partitioned. Therefore, each element of section 872.720 is satisfied, and an interlocutory judgment is appropriate. Plaintiff previously filed a motion for interlocutory judgment in March 2024, which was denied without prejudice for procedural reasons. After further meet-and-confer efforts with Defendant’s counsel, the parties could not reach an agreement, leading to this renewed motion. (Sikavi Decl. ¶ 10.) Plaintiff now requests that the Court approve the sale of the property, with final approval subject to a noticed motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 873.720.

 

On the onset, Plaintiff argues the instant motion is procedurally proper because the prior motion was denied without prejudice, allowing him to refile. “Denial of motion without prejudice impliedly invites the moving party to renew the motion at a later date, when he can correct the deficiency that led to the denial, so Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 is inapplicable.” (Farber v. Bay View Terrace Homeowners Assn’ (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1007, 1015.)

 

As to the merits, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has made several admissions that are conclusive under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.410(a). Specifically, in the verified answer, Defendant stated that both Plaintiff and Defendant each own a 50% undivided joint tenant interest in the property and that Defendant does not oppose the sale. (Answer ¶ 3, 4.) Additionally, Defendant admitted through Requests for Admissions that Plaintiff is a rightful owner (RFA No. 2), that the parties are joint tenants (RFA No. 3), and that Plaintiff owns 50% of the property. (RFA No. 4.) Based on these admissions, Plaintiff claims he has the right to judgment on the pleadings as the parties are co-owners, Plaintiff has the right to partition the property, and the property is not subject to physical partition. Moreover, Plaintiff points out that Defendant failed to plead any affirmative defenses, specifically the waiver defense, which is the only defense against a partition action under Code of Civil Procedure section 872.710. As a result, Plaintiff asserts that Defendant has waived the right to raise any defenses. Plaintiff states he is entitled to an interlocutory judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc, § 872.720 (“If the court finds that the plaintiff is entitled to partition, it shall make an interlocutory judgment that determines the interests of the parties…”).).

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that a motion for judgment on the pleadings is not the appropriate mechanism to obtain an interlocutory judgment in a partition action. Section 872.710(a) provides: “At the trial, the court shall determine whether the plaintiff has the right to partition.” No partition can be had until the interests of all the parties have been ascertained and settled by trial. (Bacon v. Wahrhaftig (1950) 97 Cal.App.2d 599, 603.) It is only after the Court determines a party’s right to partition, that an interlocutory judgment may enter. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 872.720(a).) However, the instant case is distinguishable from Bacon. Unlike in Bacon, where one of the main issues was the misjoinder of parties and whether all parties who had an interest in the property had been ascertained, leading to procedural complications, here there is no question about the right to partition, as both Plaintiff and Defendant have admitted joint tenancy ownership of the property. (Bacon 97 Cal.App.2d, at p. 604; Answer ¶ 3, 4.; RFA No. 2-3.)

Furthermore, Defendant contends that extrinsic evidence is necessary to show that the intent behind the property’s title was not to create a true joint tenancy, as Plaintiff contributed nothing to the purchase price, and Defendant paid for all taxes, insurance, and maintenance. (Opposition p. 4.) Defendant further argues that despite the property being held in joint tenancy, this does not automatically reflect the parties’ actual interests, particularly given the financial contributions made solely by Defendant.  Defendant emphasizes that joint tenancy does not prevent courts from considering differing contributions in determining the true ownership interests. (Milian v. De Leon (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 1185.) Defendant asserts that her intention was for Plaintiff to inherit the property upon her death, not for them to share ownership or responsibilities during her lifetime. (Opposition p. 5.) Lastly, Defendant requests that if the Court grants the motion for judgment on the pleadings, she should be given leave to amend her answer, as it may have been imperfectly pleaded. (Opposition p. 6.)

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that although a joint tenancy deed is not conclusive of the nature of ownership, it does create a rebuttable presumption that the property is held in joint tenancy. (Clark v Carter (1968) 265 Cal.App.2d 291, 294-295.) And that, the presumption created by a deed can only be overcome by showing a common understanding or an agreement that the property was not meant to be held in joint tenancy. (Donovan v. Donovan (1963) 223 Cal.App.2d 691, 698.) Furthermore, the presumption cannot be rebutted merely by showing the source of the funds used to acquire the property. (Beck v. Beck (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 396, 408.)

 

 The Court finds that Defendant’s Answer and responses to Defendant's request for admissions, and Defendant’s failure to plead any affirmative defenses, such as a waiver, waives her right to contest partition, as Code of Civil Procedure section 872.710 grants an absolute right to partition absent a valid defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.710.) Defendant has shown that extrinsic evidence may be used to show that a joint tenancy has not been created or that the joint tenancy does not reflect the true interests in the property. (Milian, 181 Cal.App.3d at p. 1191-1195; Cosier v. Norwood, 97 Cal.App.2d 665, 666.) However, here, unlike in Milian, where there was a dispute over whether a joint tenancy had been created, Defendant has admitted that Plaintiff owns 50% of the property as a joint tenant and, therefore, cannot now argue that a joint tenancy was not created. (See, Milian, 181 Cal.App.3d at p. 1195; Barsegian v. Kessler & Kessler (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 446, 451; RFP’s No. 2-3) Nor can Defendant argue that the joint tenancy does not reflect the true ownership of the parties. (See, Milian, 181 Cal.App.3d at 1195; Barsegian, 215 Cal.App.4th at p. 451; RFP’s No. 2-3) Therefore, based on Defendant’s admissions and the absence of a waiver defense, Plaintiff is entitled to partition as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 872.710. Priddel v. Shankie (1945) 69 Cal.App.2d 319, 325.) (finding that once a party seeking partition is shown to be an owner of the property, the right of partition is absolute.)

 

C.    Leave to Amend

 

            CCP §473(a) permits the Court to grant leave to a party to amend a pleading. The Court’s discretion regarding granting leave to amend is usually exercised liberally to permit amendment of pleadings. (Nestle v. Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.) If a party “has a good cause of action, which by accident or mistake he has failed to set out in his complaint, the court, on motion for judgment on the pleadings, should, on his application so to do, permit him to amend.” (MacIsaac v. Pozzo (1945) 26 Cal.2d 809, 815.)

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for judgment on the pleadings WITH LEAVE TO AMEND within 30 days.
