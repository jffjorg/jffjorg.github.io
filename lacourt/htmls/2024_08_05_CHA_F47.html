DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 23CHCV02468    Hearing Date: August 5, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 8/5/24

Case #23CHCV02468

 

DEMURRER TO THE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Demurrer filed on 6/25/24. 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Ford Motor Company

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Maximilian I Webb

NOTICE: ok

 

Demurrer is to the 5th cause of action:

1.      Violation of Civil Code 1793.2(d)

2.      Violation of Civil Code 1793.2(b)

3.      Violation of Civil Code 1793.2(a)(3)

4.      Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civil Code 1791.1, 1794, 1795.5)

5.      Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment

6.      Negligent Repair (not alleged against Ford Motor Company)

 

RULING: The demurrer to the 5th cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  Answer is due within 20 days.    

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff Maximilian Webb’s (Plaintiff) purchase of a 2018 Ford F-150 (the Vehicle) on 1/14/19.  Plaintiff alleges that “[d]efects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves within the applicable express warranty period, including but not limited to, transmission defects, engine defects, body defects; among other defects and nonconformities.”  (SAC ¶33). 

 

On 8/16/23, Plaintiff Maximilian I Webb (Plaintiff) filed this action against Defendants Ford Motor Company (Ford) and Autonation Ford Valencia (Autonation) for: (1) Violation of Civil Code 1793.2(d); (2) Violation of Civil Code 1793.2(b); (3) Violation of Civil Code 1793.2(a)(3);

(4) Breach of Implied Warranty of Merchantability (Civil Code 1791.1, 1794, 1795.5);

(5) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment and (6) Negligent Repair (not alleged against Ford).

 

On 9/18/23, Ford filed and served a demurer to the 1st – 5th causes of action in the complaint which was set for hearing on 2/2/24.  On 1/22/24, nine court days before the hearing, Plaintiff filed and served a First Amended Complaint which rendered the demurrer moot.  See CCP 472(a); CCP 1005(b).  As such, on 2/2/24, the Court placed the hearing on Ford’s demurrer to the original complaint off calendar.  (See 2/2/24 Minute Order).  The First Amended Complaint alleged the same six causes of action as the original complaint.  On 4/19/24, the Court sustained Ford’s demurrer to the 5th cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment with leave to amend.  (See 4/19/24 Minute Order). 

 

On 5/17/24, Plaintiff filed and served the subject Second Amended Complaint which alleges the same six causes of action as the original Complaint and First Amended Complaint.  After meet and confer efforts failed to resolve the issues Ford had with the 5th cause of action for Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment alleged in the Second Amended Complaint, on 6/25/24, Ford filed and served the instant demurrer to the 5th cause of action.  On 7/24/24, Plaintiff filed and served his opposition to the demurrer and on 7/29/24, Ford filed and served a reply to the opposition. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer may be based on the ground that the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.  CCP 430.10(e).  A demurrer admits the truth of all properly pleaded facts regardless of how unlikely or improbable they may be.  See Aubry (1992) 2 C4th 962, 966-967; Del E. Webb Corp. (1981) 123 CA3d 593, 604.  Allegations in a complaint must be liberally construed with a view toward substantial justice between the parties.  CCP 452. 

 

5th Cause of Action – Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment

 

The elements of a fraudulent concealment cause of action are: (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact, (2) a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) intentional concealment or suppression of the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) that the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he/she did if he/she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiffs sustained damage.  Boschma (2011) 198 CA4th 230, 248.  Additionally, every element of a fraud cause of action must be alleged with factual specificity.  Cooper (1990) 219 CA3d 1252, 1262; Lazar (1996) 12 C4th 631, 645.  Concealment of a material fact is actionable when there is a duty of disclosure, which may arise from a relationship between the parties, such as a buyer-seller relationship.  Dhital (2022) 84 CA5th 828, 843-844, review granted, citing Hoffman (2014) 228 CA4th 1178, 1186-1187.  In such case, the plaintiff must describe the content and where the omitted information should or could have been revealed.  Tapia (S.D. Cal. 2015) 116 F.Supp.3d 1149, 1163.

 

Plaintiff has still failed to allege with the requisite particularity the defect in Plaintiff’s vehicle that Ford allegedly concealed.  The Second Amended Complaint, like the First Amended Complaint, again describes a list of ways in which Ford F-150s transmissions may be defective and hazardous driving conditions relating generally to the purported transmission defect but does not state what the alleged defect is in the Vehicle or otherwise .  (See FAC ¶¶47, 85; SAC ¶¶48, 82).  Such allegations are insufficient to support the fraud-based claim.  Santana (2020) 56 CA5th 334, 345.

 

Plaintiff has also still failed to adequately allege where the omitted information should or could have been revealed by Ford and fails to identify the requisite representative samples of advertisements, offers, or other representations by Ford that consumers relied upon to make their purchase.  Additionally, Plaintiff does not identify by name who made the alleged omissions when he purchased the Vehicle.

 

Even if Plaintiff had pled an identifiable defect in the Vehicle, Plaintiff has not alleged facts showing Ford had a legal duty to disclose those facts.  “There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.’”  LiMandri (1997) 52 CA4th 326, 336.  The latter three circumstances presuppose the existence of some other transactional relationship (i.e., seller and buyer, employer and prospective employee, doctor and patient, another contractual agreement) between a plaintiff and defendant giving rise to a duty to disclose.  Id. at 336-337.  Regardless of the circumstances under which a duty to disclose may arise, Plaintiff must allege facts to show that Ford was aware of a defect at the time of sale.  Wilson (9th Cir. 2012) 668 F.3d 1136, 1145.

 

In Dhital, relied on by Plaintiffs, the plaintiffs alleged that they bought the vehicle at issue from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the vehicle with an express warranty, and that Nissan’s authorized dealerships are its agents for purposes of the sale of the Nissan vehicles to consumers. See Dhital, supra at 844.  While Plaintiff now alleges that he purchased the vehicle from Ford’s “authorized retail dealership Galpin Ford, located at 15505 Roscoe Blvd., North Hills, CA 91343,” the Second Amended Complaint does not specifically allege that Ford’s authorized dealerships are its agents for purposes of the sale of Ford vehicles to consumers.  (See SAC ¶28 and generally).  Additionally, the warranty contract relied on by Plaintiff is actually titled “2018 Model Year Ford Warranty Guide” and is not signed by either party.  (SAC ¶¶27, 29, Ex.A).   Even if the facts alleged are deemed sufficient to impose a duty of disclosure on Ford, the claim fails for other reasons.

 

Plaintiff relies on conclusory allegations regarding Ford’s purported exclusive knowledge of the defects.  (See SAC ¶¶83, 85(b)).  Additionally, the Second Amended Complaint, like the First Amended Complaint, concedes that Ford issued publicly available Technical Service Bulletins (TSBs) regarding the alleged Transmission Defect. (FAC ¶¶49-57; SAC ¶¶51-55).  Further, the issuance of TSBs does not establish Ford’s knowledge of a defect in Plaintiff’s specific vehicle which was concealed from Plaintiff upon purchase.  See American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (2011) 199 CA4th 1367, 1378. 

 

As such, it is still not clear what additional information regarding the transmission in Plaintiff’s vehicle was purportedly concealed from the dealerships and/or Plaintiff with whom it appears Ford had no direct interaction.  The claim also appears to be based, in part, on concealment that allegedly occurred after Plaintiff purchased the Vehicle.  (SAC ¶84).  Information allegedly concealed after the purchase could not have fraudulently induced Plaintiff to purchase the vehicle.  Plaintiff has still failed to plead facts to establish active concealment on the part of Ford or partial representations while suppressing material facts.   

 

Additionally, Plaintiff has still failed to allege sufficient facts to establish that the fraud claim is not barred by the “economic loss rule.” “[T]he economic loss rule allows a plaintiff to recover in strict products liability in tort when a product defect causes damage to ‘other property,’ that is, property other than the product itself. The law of contractual warranty governs damage to the product itself.”  Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. (2004) 34 C4th 979, 989 citing Jimenez (2002) 29 C4th 473, 482-483.  Based on the allegations in the Second Amended Complaint, it still appears that the only duty allegedly breached was that created by the terms of the warranty and the only harm which resulted from Ford’s alleged concealment was that Plaintiff would not have leased/purchased the Vehicle.  (See FAC ¶87; SAC ¶¶92-93 (which are duplicative of one another)).  Plaintiff also does not allege any facts which indicate an intent by Ford not to perform under the contract so as to fraudulently induce Plaintiff into purchasing the Vehicle.  See Food Safety Services (2012) 209 CA4th 1118, 1130-1132.  Rather, the allegations indicate that Ford has attempted to comply with the warranty contract, but, according to Plaintiff, has not successfully repaired the problem.  (SAC ¶8).  

 

 As noted in the Court’s ruling on Ford’s demurrer to the First Amended Complaint, the holding in Robinson Helicopter was narrowly limited to “a defendant’s affirmative misrepresentations on which a plaintiff relies and which expose a plaintiff to liability for personal damages independent of the Plaintiffs’ economic loss.”  Robinson Helicopter, supra at 993; See also In re Ford Motor Company Motor Co. (C.D. Cal. 2020) 483 F.Supp.3d 838, 848, 850.  Here, the fraud claim is based on purported concealment, not affirmative misrepresentations.  Plaintiff’s reliance on Dhital, supra, for the proposition that the fraud claim is not barred by the “economic loss rule” is still unpersuasive.  First, Dhital is still under review by the Supreme Court of California as to the economic loss rule; therefore, it is not citable.  Second, the Court of Appeal has recently held that unless there is a specific assertion of agency in the complaint, there is no showing of agency between Ford and the authorized dealerships.  See Ochoa (2023) 89 CA5th 1324 (review granted 7/9/23).  As noted above, while Plaintiff alleged that he purchased the Vehicle from an authorized Ford dealership, Plaintiff has not made sufficient agency allegations.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer to the 5th cause of action is sustained.  While Plaintiff has requested leave to amend, if the demurrer is sustained, Plaintiff does not give any indication as to how he can cure the defect in the Second Amended Complaint as is his burden. See Blank (1985) 39 C3d 311, 318.  Rather, Plaintiff merely states that his “[c]ounsel would be prepared to discuss any specific deficiencies that the Court might view as correctible at the hearing.”  (See Opposition, p.14:20-21).  Additionally, Plaintiff has already had three opportunities to plead his fraudulent inducement – concealment claim and has failed to adequately do so.  Therefore, leave to amend is denied.  See Titus (2004) 118 CA4th 906, 918; Green (1986) 185 CA3d 544, 556.

 

Answer is due within 20 days.

MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES

 

Motion filed on 4/3/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Allegiance Gold, LLC; Unik Marketing LLC, Mark Naaman; Assadour Ebkarian (erroneously sued as Alex Ebkarian); Fred Palmerino; Octavio Rocha (erroneously sued as Ocatvio Rocha) and Monica Ward

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Shawn Campbell

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order awarding and fixing the amount of attorneys’ fees to which Defendants are entitled as the prevailing parties in this action. 

 

RULING: The motion is granted. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

Plaintiff Shawn Campbell (Plaintiff) is a former employee of Defendant Unik Marketing LLC (Unik).  Plaintiff was terminated by Unik on 9/5/23.  On that same day, Plaintiff signed a Severance Agreement and General Release (Agreement).  The Agreement indicated that in any action brought to enforce rights under the Agreement, the prevailing party would be entitled to its costs, including attorneys’ fees. 

 

On 9/5/23, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants Allegiance Gold, LLC; Unik Marketing LLC, Mark Naaman; Assadour Ebkarian (erroneously sued as Alex Ebkarian); Fred Palmerino; Octavio Rocha (erroneously sued as Ocatvio Rocha) and Monica Ward (collectively, Defendants) seeking to enforce his rights under the Agreement and alleging that each Defendant failed to properly compensate him under the terms of the Agreement.

 

On 1/10/24, this Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint with 20 days leave to amend.  (See 1/10/24 Minute Order).  Plaintiff failed to amend his complaint.  On 2/8/24, pursuant to Defendants’ ex parte application, the Court dismissed Plaintiff’s action, with prejudice.  (See 2/8/24 Minute Order; 2/15/24 Judgment of Dismissal).  The Judgment provides that Defendants are entitled to recover their costs, including attorneys’ fees pursuant to a motion for attorneys’ fees. 

 

On 4/3/24, Defendants filed and served the instant  motion seeking an order awarding and fixing the amount of attorneys’ fees to which Defendants are entitled as the prevailing parties in this action.  Plaintiff has not opposed or otherwise responded to the motion. 

 

ANALYSIS 

 

The Agreement which formed the basis for Plaintiff’s action contains an attorney fee provision which provides:

 

“In any action or other proceeding to enforce rights hereunder, the prevailing party shall receive an award of costs and expenses related to such proceeding, including attorneys’ fees. Venue and personal jurisdiction for purposes of any action arising from Employee’s employment with Company or any action or other proceeding to enforce rights hereunder or between the parties is Los Angeles County, California only.”

 

(See DeCastro Decl., Ex.A ¶26). 

 

Based on the judgment of dismissal, Defendants are the prevailing parties in this action and, thereby, are entitled to recover their costs, including attorneys’ fees under the contract.  See CCP 1032(a)(4), (b); CCP 1033.5(a)(10)(A).

 

Based on the lodestar method of calculation (reasonable number of hours spent multiplied by a reasonable hourly rate), Defendants have established that they incurred $14,550.00 in attorney’s fees defending this action (29.1 hours x $500/hour) plus $3,000.00 related to the instant motion, which is also recoverable.  See Serrano (1982) 32 C3d 621, 639, 642; (DeCastro Decl. and Ex.B thereto).  As such, Defendants are entitled to an award of $17,550.00 in attorney’s fees. 

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted.
