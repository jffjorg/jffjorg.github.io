DEPARTMENT 54 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV13576    Hearing Date: August 9, 2024    Dept: 54

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

Marion Martinez,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23STCV13576

 

vs.

 

 

Tentative Ruling

 

West Hills Hospital, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date: August 9, 2024

Department 54, Judge Maurice Leiter

Motions to Compel Further Responses

Moving Party: Plaintiff Marion Martinez

Responding Party: Defendants West Hills Hospital, et al.

 

T/R:     (1) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FROGS GRANTED.

(2) MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO RPD GRANTED IN PART.

 

SANCTIONS AWARDED TO PLAINTIFF IN THE AMOUNT OF $2,100.

 

PLAINTIFF TO NOTICE.

 

            The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wage and hour and wrongful termination action. Plaintiff Marion Martinez filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on December 18, 2023, alleging violations of Labor Code Sections 1102.5, 6310, and 6311; violation of Health and Safety Code Section 1278.5; wrongful termination in violation of Public Policy; defamation; and Private Attorney General Act against Defendants West Hills Hospital; HCA Healthcare Inc.; HCA Healthcare; CHC Payroll Agent, Inc.; HCA Human Resources, LLC; HCA Human Resources Group; Los Robles Regional Medical Center; Charlene Timms; and Adam Gardner.  

 

 

 

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.        FROGs

 

The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to interrogatories if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

Plaintiff's Form Interrogatory 12.1 requests the names, addresses, and phone numbers of individuals with knowledge of the incident leading to the action. Defendant objects based on invasion of privacy.

 

Plaintiff argues that witness contact information is discoverable under Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010, which allows discovery of any non-privileged matter relevant to the case. This includes information that might assist in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. (Glenfed Development v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117.) Discovery may include the identity and location of persons with knowledge of any discoverable matter, as routine and essential pretrial discovery. (Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal. App. 4th 1242, 1251-52 (2008); Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s responses are insufficient as they only list names without providing contact information, directing Plaintiff to contact witnesses through counsel instead.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that FROG No. 12.1 improperly seeks contact information of represented parties and third parties whose information cannot be disclosed due to privacy concerns. Defendants claim that Plaintiff’s counsel is ethically prohibited from contacting employees whose actions might bind Defendant or establish liability. (Triple A Machine Shop, Inc. v. State of California (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 131, 140.)

 

Plaintiff has demonstrated that the information sought is relevant and necessary for trial preparation. Defendant’s objections based on privacy concerns are not sufficient to outweigh Plaintiff’s need for the information. Basic contact information does not constitute a serious invasion of privacy. Defendant’s failure to provide timely and complete responses, despite repeated assurances and extensions, justifies Plaintiff’s motion to compel. Defendant’s compromise to allow contact through counsel is insufficient, particularly when counsel does not represent all identified witnesses. Plaintiff must be able to secure witness testimony independently to prepare adequately for trial.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to FROG no. 12.

 

B.        RPDs

 

On receipt of a response to a Request for Production of Documents, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete, (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive, or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general. (CCP § 2031.310(a).)  

 

The RPDs involves three categories of documents:

 

1.    Documents regarding Plaintiff’s employment terms, termination, and performance (Requests No. 1-24, 32, 35, 39).

2.    Notes, emails, and text messages about Plaintiff from managers and coworkers (Requests Nos. 4, 6-22).

3.    Documents related to Plaintiff making complaints during employment (Requests Nos. 4, 6-22, 29, 30).

Defendants’ objections largely consist of boilerplate. Notwithstanding these objections, Defendant agrees to produce Plaintiff’s termination document. Defendant objects to producing any additional documents.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ statements of compliance are evasive, failing to identify specific responsive documents and not complying with CCP § 2031.210 and § 2031.280(a). Responses must specify what documents are being produced and identify documents withheld based on privilege. (CCP § 2031.240(b).)

 

In opposition, Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s motion is largely moot because Defendant served supplemental responses to these requests on June 19, 2024, and July 15, 2024, along with additional documents and a production log prior to the Person Most Knowledgeable Deposition. (Lewis Decl., ¶¶ 9, 13, Exs. 3, 5-6.) Defendant asserts that it never agreed to supplement its response to Request No. 42. Defendants assert that Plaintiff falsely claims that Defendants’ responses fail to identify what responsive documents have been produced. Defendant provided a production log identifying the responsive documents on July 15, 2024. (Lewis Decl., ¶ 13, Ex. 6.) Defendant also contends that Plaintiff’s discovery requests are overbroad, unduly burdensome, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

            As to the untimeliness of the opposition, the Court will consider the brief in its discretion; there is no showing of prejudice. Given Defendants’ supplemental responses after the filing of this motion, the Court finds that the motion as to all except RPD no. 42 is moot. RPD 42 is not overbroad. The request specifies items such as “emails, text messages, handwritten notes, and memoranda, personal journals, and calendars.” Defendants’ response is inadequate.

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel further response to RPD no. 42.

 

D.        Sanctions

 

Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $2,550 for the FROGS and $4,250 for the RPD. As the prevailing party, Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions. Counsel claims a rate of $425 per hour and anticipates spending 6 hours for the FROG motion and 10 hours for the RPD motion. The Court reduces the hourly rate to $300. Aside from the statement that counsel was previously awarded the $425 rate, there is no information to ascertain whether this rate is reasonable for Plaintiff’s attorney. As for the hours spent, the Court reduces the hours to 3 for the FROG motion and 4 for the RPD motion.

 

The Court awards sanctions to Plaintiff in the amount of $2,100. Sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel by Defendants and Defendants’ counsel, jointly, within 30 days.
