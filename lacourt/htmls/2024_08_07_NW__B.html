DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV08795    Hearing Date: August 7, 2024    Dept: B

ANNETTE MANOUKIAN V. ADAM MICHAEL KNOX

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:          August 7, 2024                                    Trial Date:       3/10/25

Department:               B                                              Case No.:         21STCV08795

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Annette Manoukian

Responding Party:       Defendant and Cross-Complainant Adam Michael Knox dba Adam’s Bootcamp Boxing  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from Plaintiff Annette Manoukian sustaining injuries during an outdoor kickboxing class. Plaintiff Annette Manoukian alleges that her foot became caught on the edge of a rubber tire, which caused her to fall face first into unprotected parking lot asphalt. (Complaint, ¶ 28.) On March 5, 2021, Plaintiff Annette Manoukian filed a Complaint against Defendants Adam Michael Knox dba Adam’s Bootcamp Boxing and Does 1 through 25, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Premises Liability; and (3) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and/or Retention.

 

On April 6, 2021, Defendant Adam Michael Knox dba Adam’s Bootcamp Boxing filed an Answer to the Complaint.

 

Also, on April 6, 2021, Defendant Adam Michael Knox dba Adam’s Bootcamp Boxing (“Cross-Complainant”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Annette Manoukian (“Cross-Defendant”) alleging a single cause of action for Express Contractual Indemnity. The Cross-Complaint alleges that Cross-Defendant signed a written waiver of release of liability. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 8.)

 

On May 11, 2021, Cross-Defendant filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

 

On May 22, 2023, Cross-Defendant filed and served the instant Motion for Summary Judgment (the “Motion”). The motion for summary judgment is made on the grounds that the contract at the basis of the Cross-Complaint is void as a matter of public policy pursuant to the Health Studio Act because it is undisputed that Cross-Defendant never received a copy of the contract.

 

On June 5, 2024, this action was reassigned from the Honorable Michelle C. Kim to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz sitting in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East effective June 24, 2024.

 

On June 11, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed and served an opposition to the Motion.

 

On June 14, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed and served a Notice of Errata Regarding the Compendium of Evidence in support of the Motion. The Notice of Errata indicates that the deposition transcript of Cross-Complainant was not attached to the Compendium of Evidence in support of the Motion due to clerical oversight. The Notice of Errata attaches the deposition transcript of Cross-Complainant thereto.

 

On June 18, 2024, Cross-Defendant filed a reply brief.

 

On June 25, 2024, after hearing, the Court indicated that it needed more time to review the motion and supporting documents. (06/25/24 Minute Order.) The Court therefore continued the hearing on the Motion to July 23, 2024. (06/25/24 Minute Order.).  The hearing was then continued again due to the Court’s inability to access it’s Case Management System (7/23/24Minute Order).

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

The Court DENIES Cross-Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment (the “Motion”).

 

DISCUSSION

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67.)

“As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the defendant has met its burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th 826, 849.) 

“The summary judgment statute [is] not intended nor can it be used as a substitute for existing methods in the trial of issues of fact.” (Travelers Indemn. Co. v. McIntosh (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 177, 182.) The summary judgment “procedure is drastic and should be used with caution.” (House v. Lala (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d 412, 415.) “Any doubt in granting summary judgment should be exercised against the moving party.” (Ibid.)

Evidentiary Objections

The Court SUSTAINS Cross-Complainant’s evidentiary objections numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 to the declaration of Shant T. Hagopian in support of the Motion on the grounds of hearsay.

The Court SUSTAINS Cross-Complainant’s evidentiary objections numbers 1, 2, 3, and 4 to the declaration of Cross-Defendant Annette Manoukian in support of the Motion on the grounds of hearsay.

The Court OVERRULES Cross-Complainant’s evidentiary objection number 1 to the deposition testimony of Cross-Complainant offered by Cross-Defendant as evidence in support of the Motion. Although such deposition testimony was referenced and relied upon in the moving papers, Cross-Defendant inadvertently omitted such deposition testimony from its compendium of evidence. Cross-Defendant filed a Notice of Errata which included the deposition testimony and Cross-Complainant did not file any evidentiary objections to the Notice of Errata.

The Court OVERRULES Cross-Defendant’s evidentiary objections numbers 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, and 8 to the declaration of Cross-Complainant Adam Knox submitted in support of the opposition to the Motion. Cross-Defendant’s evidentiary objections are procedurally improper. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1354.)

Each written objection to evidence must “[s]tate the exhibit, title, page, and line number of the material objected to.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1354(b)(2).) Here, however, Cross-Defendant failed to set forth the page and line number of the purported objectionable material and has only set forth the objectionable excerpts without citing to the page and line number. (See 06/18/24 Evidentiary Objections of Cross-Defendant.) Had Cross-Defendant presented procedurally proper evidentiary objections, the Court would have sustained all of the objections on the grounds that the objectionable material constituted an improper legal conclusion. (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639.)

Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Cross-Defendant’s request for judicial notice and the Court takes judicial notice of the Cross-Complaint. (Evid. Code § 452(d).)

Cross-Complainant’s Defective Separate Statement

A separate statement in opposition to a motion for summary judgment “must unequivocally state whether that fact is disputed or undisputed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1350, subd. (f)(2).) “An opposing party who contends that a fact is disputed must state, on the right side of the page directly of the fact in dispute, the nature of the dispute and describe the evidence that supports the position that the fact is controverted. Citation to the evidence in support of the position that a fact is controverted must include reference to the exhibit, title, page, and line numbers.” (Ibid.) “The opposition papers shall include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating if the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(b)(3).) “The statement shall also set forth plainly and concisely any another material facts the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. Failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may constitute a sufficient ground, in the court’s discretion, for granting the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(b)(3).) 

 Here, Cross-Defendant presented five purported undisputed material facts in support of the Motion. The Court finds that Cross-Complainant’s separate statement is defective. For instance, in response to UMF Nos. 1, 2, 4, and 5, Cross-Complainant states that such facts are “disputed to the extent that as detailed in Cross-Complainant’s Evidentiary Objections . . . .” (Cross-Complainant’s Separate Statement at pp. 1-3.) Cross-Complainant then references its evidentiary objections in the separate statement. Cross-Complainant, however, was required to cite to evidence supporting the position that the fact is controverted.

The Court will not grant the Motion merely on the defects in the separate statement of Cross-Complainant. The Court will still consider the evidence offered by Cross-Complainant since it is before the Court, even though Cross-Complainant did not properly dispute certain material facts. Cross-Complainant is reminded to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure and of the California Rules of Court.

The Deposition Testimony Offered as Evidence

Cross-Defendant offers the deposition testimony of Cross-Complainant as an exhibit. Such deposition testimony, however, is not “marked in a manner that calls attention to the testimony.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1116(c).) The Court will still consider the deposition testimony of Cross-Complainant, which is offered as evidence by Cross-Defendant.

However, Cross-Defendant is reminded to present procedurally proper exhibits in accordance with the California Rules of Court.

Issue No.1: The Health Studio Act

Cross-Defendant argues that Cross-Complainant’s contract referenced in the Cross-Complaint is subject to the Health Studio Act and Cross-Complainant’s failure to provide her with a copy of the contract renders such contract void. Cross-Complainant contends that the waiver form is not a contract for health studio services and therefore the Motion should be denied.

“The Legislature declares that the purpose of the [Health Studio Act] is to safeguard the public against fraud, deceit, imposition and financial hardship, and to foster and encourage competition, fair dealing, and prosperity in the field of health studio services by prohibiting or restricting false or misleading advertising, onerous contract terms, harmful financial practices, and other unfair, dishonest, deceptive, destructive, unscrupulous, fraudulent, and discriminatory practices by which the public has been injured in connection with contracts for health studio services.” (Civ. Code § 1812.80.)

A contract for health studio service is “a contract for instruction, training or assistance in physical culture, body building, exercising, reducing, figure development, or any other such physical skill, or for the use by an individual patron of the facilities of a health studio, gymnasium or other facility used for any of the above purposes, or membership in any group, club, association or organization formed for any of the above purposes . . . .” (Civ. Code § 1812.81.) “Every contract for health studio services shall be in writing and shall be subject to the provisions of this title. A copy of the written contract shall be physically given to or delivered by email to the customer at the time he or she signs the contract.” (Civ. Code § 1812.82.) “Any contract for health studio services which does not comply with the applicable provisions of this title shall be void and enforceable as contrary to public policy.” (Civ. Code § 1812.91.) A contract is unlawful if it is “[c]ontrary to an express provision of law; [or] [is] [c]ontrary to the policy of express law, through not expressly prohibited.” (Civ. Code § 1667(1)-(2).)

Cross-Defendant’s Evidence

Cross-Defendant declares that on September 28, 2019, she presented to Cross-Complainant’s facility in Burbank, California to participate in a group fitness class incorporating boxing themed exercises. (Manoukian Decl., ¶ 2.) Cross-Defendant paid twenty dollars for the class and, as a precondition to her participation, she was asked to complete and sign a document called “Boxing Club Participation Waiver Form” (the “Contract”). (Id., ¶ 3; Knox Depo. at p. 41:20-42:18.) Cross-Defendant signed the Contract and handed it back to Mr. Knox, but she did not receive a copy of the Contract, signed or otherwise. (Manoukian Decl., ¶ 4.) Cross-Defendant never received a copy of the Contract signed, unsigned, or otherwise in electronic form. (Id., ¶ 5.) Cross-Defendant injured herself during the class when she tripped on a tire and fell to the ground. (Id., ¶ 9.) Cross-Defendant was participating in group training. (Donabedian Decl., Exh. 1 at p. 36:23.)

Cross-Defendant presents evidence that the Cross-Complaint is based exclusively on the Contract. (UMF No. 5; RJN at Exh. 1.)

The Contract is attached as Exhibit 1 to the declaration of Sam A. Donabedian in support of the Motion. (Donabedian Decl., Exh. 1.) The Contract is a waiver of liability form wherein Cross-Defendant agreed to discharge Cross-Complainant from any liability. (Id.) The Contract states that Cross-Defendant agrees to assume all risks in consideration of the acceptance of her application to participate in the boxing club at Cross-Complainant’s location. (Id.) Moreover, the Contract states that it is intended to discharge Cross-Complainant “from any and all liability arising out of or connected in any way with [her] participation organized by [Cross-Complainant] even though that liability may arise out of negligence or carelessness . . . .” (Id.)

The Court finds that Cross-Defendant has met her burden in showing that the Contract is one for health studio services. The burden now shifts to Cross-Complainant to show a triable issue of material fact.

Cross-Complainant’s Evidence

In opposition to the Motion, Cross-Complainant declares that the Contract is not part of any membership agreement or other contract for instruction, training, or assistance in physical culture, body building, exercising, reducing, figure development, or any other such physical skill, or for use by an individual patron of the facilities of a health studio, gymnasium, or other facility used for such purposes, or for membership in any group, club, association, or organization formed for any of the above purposes; rather, the Contract is presented as a separate document for the purpose of release of liability. (Knox Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. A.) The Contract does not provide Cross-Defendant with any vested rights to participate in classes, to access the venue, or to hold membership in any club; rather, it simply provides that should Cross-Defendant do what is necessary to participate in classes or secure access to the venue, and that while doing so she will waive ordinary negligence claims. (Id., ¶ 4; Exh. A.) Cross-Complainant states that he has performed all conditions and obligations under the Contract and is entitled to be indemnified by Cross-Defendant. (Id., ¶¶ 13-14.) The Contract does not reflect an agreement concerning instruction, training, or gym membership. (Id., ¶ 6.) Cross-Defendant participated in a nonprofit birthday Bootcamp Boxing class that Cross-Complainant hosted for one of his clients whereat she alleges she was injured. (Id., ¶ 8.)

Analysis

The Court finds that Cross-Complainant has presented a triable issue of material fact as to whether the Contract is one for health studio services. Cross-Complainant has declared that the Contract was not one for participation in classes, access to Cross-Complainant’s venue, or for membership. Cross-Complainant has presented evidence that the Contract was for the sole purpose of releasing Cross-Complainant from liability.

The Court further finds that Cross-Defendant’s citation to Staples v. Arthur Marry, Inc. (1967) 253 Cal.App.2d 507 is inapposite as such a case concerned a contract for payment for dancing classes. Here, there is no allegation in the Cross-Complaint or raised in the moving papers as to payment for gym classes. Thus, the instant action is factually distinguishable from Staples, supra, 253 Cal. App. 2d 507.

Moreover, the Court finds that Cross-Defendant’s citation to Crannage v. Gold’s Gym (C.D. Cal. 2019) 2019 WL 7856763 is inapposite as the Crannage court found that triable issues of fact existed as to a membership agreement and waiver for use of gym facilities.

 

In sum, a triable issue of fact exists as to whether the Contract is one for health studio services pursuant to Civ. Code § 1812.81. Liberally construing the evidence in opposition to the Motion, the Court finds that a triable issue of material fact exists, and summary judgment is not appropriate.

 

Accordingly, the Motion is DENIED.  

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Motion is DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
