DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22BBCV00340    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING CROSS-COMPLAINANTS’ MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT


      I.          Background Facts

This case arises from several agreements to operate rodeo shows.

Defendant and Cross-Complainant Hansen Dam Equestrian Center, LLC (the “Center”) possesses rights from the City of Los Angeles to operate rodeo shows. Plaintiff Bronco Entertainment, Inc. (“Bronco”) claims a contractual right to operate rodeos at the Center. The contractual right to operate rodeo shows at the Center is also alleged by Cross-Complainant and Defendant Lawrence Langer (“Langer”), Cross-Complainants Golden Bull Entertainment, Inc. (“Golden Bull”), Jose Hernandez (“Hernandez”), and Miguel Sahagun (“Sahagun”). (Motion, p. 3.)

On May 17, 2022, Plaintiff Bronco filed a cause of action alleging breach of contract against Defendant the Center under the Sublicensee Agreement for Facilities Use, dated June 9, 2014, by and between the Center and Bronco (the “2014 Bronco Sublicensee Agreement”). (Compl., Exh. A.).

On May 17, 2022, Plaintiff Bronco filed a complaint alleging intentional tort against Defendant Langer, the authorized agent and managing member of the Center, and DOES 1-10 for allegedly interfering with Bronco’s business property via lockout.

On December 8, 2022, Cross Complainants Langer and the Center filed a cross complaint for breach of contract and tortious interference with contractual relations, seeking indemnity and declaratory relief against Cross-Defendants Jose De Los Santos (doing business as Bronco), Sagahun, Hernandez, Golden Bull, and ROES 1-25.

On March 8, 2023, Cross-Defendant Bronco filed an answer to the Center and Langer’s cross-complaint.

On May 12, 2023, Cross Defendants Golden Bull and Hernandez filed an answer to the Center and Langer’s cross-complaint.

On May 12, 2023, Cross-Complainants Golden Bull and Hernandez filed a cross-complaint alleging the following five causes of action: (1) breach of written contract, (2) unjust enrichment, (3) declaratory relief, (4) tortious interference with contract, and (5) negligent interference with prospective economic advantage against Cross-Defendants Bronco, the Center, Ice General Builder, Inc., Vanessa De Los Santos, Fabian De Los Santos and MOES 1-50 inclusive.

On June 20, 2023, the Center filed an answer to the May 12 cross-complaint.

On June 21, 2023, Bronco filed an answer to the May 12 cross-complaint.

On July 31, 2023, Ice General Builder Inc., filed an answer to the May 12 cross-complaint.

On September 15, 2023, Vanessa De Los Santos, and Jose Fabian De Los Santos (erroneously sued as “Fabian De Los Santos”), filed an answer to the May 12 cross-complaint.

Before the Court is a June 3, 2024, Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”), filed by Cross-Complainants Hernandez and Golden Bull (together “Cross-Complainants”). Cross-Complainants concurrently filed a supporting declaration of Timothy R. Hanigan, Esq. (“Hanigan Decl.”).

Cross-Complainants seek to amend the cross-complaint to include a sixth cause of action for breach of contract against Hansen. (Hanigan Decl. ¶ 3.) Cross-Complainants bring this claim upon learning from Hansen’s counsel, James White, that Hansen “did not intend to honor its agreement with Cross-Complainants, and that Hansen has been seeking to enter a new agreement with Sahagun regarding the operation of rodeos at the facilities.” (Id., ¶ 5.) Attorney Hanigan sought a stipulation from the parties to allow the amendment.  Unfortunately, the parties could not reach an agreement.  (Hanigan Decl., ¶ 6.)

As of July 5, 2024, no opposition has been filed by cross-Defendants.

The hearing for the instant matter is set for July 8, 2024.

    II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A.    Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint

CCP § 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.”  (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.)  Ordinarily, the court will not consider the validity of the proposed amended pleading in ruling on a motion for leave since grounds for a demurrer or motion to strike are premature.  The court, however, does have discretion to deny leave to amend where a proposed amendment fails to state a valid cause of action as a matter of law and the defect cannot be cured by further amendment.  (See California Casualty General Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 274, 281, overruled on other grounds by Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390.)

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, a long, unwarranted, and unexcused delay in presenting it may be a good reason for denial.  In most cases, the factors for timeliness are: (1) lack of diligence in discovering the facts or in offering the amendment after knowledge of them; and (2) the effect of the delay on the adverse party.  If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend.  (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation such as an increased burden of discovery.  (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488.)

Here, per CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a declaration has been filed with the motion, explaining (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Cross-Complainants Golden Bull and Hernandez’s motion is made on good cause to file the FACC pursuant to CCP §§ 473, 576. (Motion, p. 2.)  Cross-Complainants argue that upon the filing of the May 12, 2023, motion, the Center’s counsel assured them that Hansen intended to honor its agreement with Golden Bull above the other agreements with the other parties in this lawsuit. (Id., p. 3.) Cross-Complainants argue that because of this assurance, the original cross-complaint included a cause of action for declaratory relief, per paragraph 19: “GBE and Hansen contend that the Bronco Sublicense Agreement is not enforceable, and that GBE has the exclusive right to operate the Facilities under the GBE Sublicense Agreement.” (Id.) Cross-Complainants allege that on February 22, 2024, the Center’s counsel advised them that the Center and Sagahun were “working together for several months to sign a new agreement that would take precedent over the agreement that the Center has with Cross-Complainants”. (Id.) Cross-Complainants sought to amend their agreement, but an amendment was never reached.

Cross-Complainant’s motion is procedurally sound and unopposed.  Counsel for Complainants specifies the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b)).

Therefore, Cross-Complainants’ motion for leave to amend is granted.  Cross-Complainants are ordered to file a separate copy of their proposed cross-complaint within five (5) days of the date of this Order.

Cross-Complainants are ordered to give notice.

ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL CROSS-DEFENDANT, RETAIL WEST, 6, L.P., RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST SET OF RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, FORM INTERROGATORIES, AND REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS; AND GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED; AND GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS  

 

                          I.          INTRODUCTION

            This case arises from a trip and fall incident on a public sidewalk raised by the growth of a nearby tree.  On April 22, 2022, Plaintiff Houry Sevani (“Plaintiff”) sustained a fractured hand and wrist among other injuries due to this fall.

            Procedural Background

On January 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed suit against Defendant City of Pasadena (“Defendant”) for the following two causes of action: (1) negligence under Gov. Code §§ 815.2, 820), and (2) dangerous condition of public property under Gov. Code § 835.

            On February 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed the first amended complaint removing the first cause of action for negligence.

            On March 27, 2023, Defendant filed a cross-complaint against Street Retail West, 6 L.P., a limited partnership (“Cross-Defendant”), and ROES 1 to 20, inclusive for three causes of action: (1) indemnity, (2) apportionment of fault, and (3) declaratory relief.

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed the four instant motions, moving the Court to compel Cross-Defendant’s discovery responses. Plaintiff also concurrently filed a Request for Monetary Sanctions, the Declaration of Ruben Vardanyan, esq. (the “Vardanyan Decl.”), and notice of the instant motion.

As of July 5, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

On June 10, 2024, the Court advanced the July 30, 2024, hearings on the instant motions to July 8, 2024.

Trial is set for January 28, 2025.

Discovery Requests

On November 13, 2023, Plaintiff served Cross-Defendant by e-Service:

1.     an initial set of written discovery, which included inter alia Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One, (collectively the “Interrogatories”); and

2.     an initial set of written discovery, which included inter alia Requests for Admission Admitted, Set One (“RFAs”)

3.     an initial set of written discovery, which included inter alia Production Requests of Documents (Set One) (the “Production Request”) (collectively, the “Discovery Requests”)

4.     a request to pay sanctions of $900.00 in connection with Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees and costs of filing of this motion.
(Motion to Compel Special Interrogatories, p. 3.)

 

Cross-Defendant’s deadline to respond to all requested discovery was on or before December 18, 2023. (Vardyan Decl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff agreed to Cross-Defendant’s 4-week extension request, setting the new response deadline for January 16, 2024. (Id. ¶ 3.) By February 1, 2024, upon receiving no response, Plaintiff inquired about the status of discovery and received no response. (Id., ¶ 4.) Over the next six months, the parties exchanged correspondence, finally a compliance date of May 23, 2024. (Id., ¶¶ 5-12.; Exhs., 2-4.)

To date, the Court is not aware of any further meet or confer efforts or any further document production on behalf of Defendant. The Court considers the instant motion on the understanding that no further efforts have occurred and no further document production, or responses, have been received.

                        II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A.    Law Governing Interrogatories

Code Civ. Proc. Section 2030.290 provides that when a party to whom interrogatories has been propounded fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection to the interrogatories; it further provides that the propounding party may move for an order compelling a response to the interrogatory. Subdivision (c) provides for monetary sanctions in connection with such motion. (CCP § 2030.290.)

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered. (2) An exercise of the party's option to produce writings. (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.”¿ CCP § 2030.210(a).¿ “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”¿ CCP § 2030.220(c).

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).  “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ CRC 3.1345(a)(2).

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(c).  “[T]he time limitations set forth…[are] mandatory and [when] not met, the trial court's order is in excess of its jurisdiction.”¿ Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)

 

B.    Law Governing Requests for Production

“Any party may obtain discovery by inspecting, copying, testing, or sampling documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically stored information in the possession, custody, or control of any other party to the action.” (CCP § 2031.010.) “If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (CCP § 2025.480(a).)

 

C.    Mandatory Sanctions – Interrogatories & Requests for Production of Documents

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Id.)

Furthermore, regardless of the reason for the delay in responding, monetary sanctions are mandatory against a party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response necessitated the filing of the deemed-admitted motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)

 

D.    Law Governing Requests for Admission

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under CCP § 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants that party relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (a)(1)-(2).)  The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

             

                     III.          ANALYSIS

A.    Interrogatories and Production Request

On November 13, 2023, Cross-Defendant was served with Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One, containing 88 specially prepared interrogatories. (Vardanyan Decl. ¶¶ 2-, 5.). Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories (Set One) focused on Cross-Defendant’s contentions as to issues of liability and causation of the incident, ownership and maintenance of the premises where Plaintiff was injured, and information at Cross-Defendant’s disposal at the time of the incident which could have and would have alerted Cross-Defendant to the defect on its property. (Motion to Compel Form Interrogatory Responses, p. 5.) The scope of discovery requested through Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) was identical to that of Form Interrogatories. (Motion to Compel Production of Document Responses, p. 5.)

Here, Plaintiff moves the Court to issue orders compelling Cross-Defendant to produce responses to Plaintiff’s Interrogatories and Production Requests. As discussed above, Cross-Defendant’s deadline to respond to the Interrogatories and Production Requests expired on December 18, 2023. The parties attempted to meet and confer numerous times and extended this deadline until May 23, 2024. At the filing of the instant motion on June 4, 2024, Cross-Defendant had not yet served any responses. (Vardanyan Decl. ¶ 13.).

 B.   Requests for Monetary Sanctions are Granted.

   Plaintiff moves the Court to impose monetary sanctions against Cross-Defendant in the total amount of $3,600 ($900 x 4 motions). Plaintiff asks the Court to impose sanctions because Plaintiff has "made every possible effort to allow Cross-Defendant to provide even the most basic discovery responses.  They have not." (Vardanyan Decl. p. 9).  Plaintiff's counsel declares that his hourly rate is $300, which the Court finds reasonable.  Counsel indicates that he expended three hours on each motion, which included meet and confer efforts.  Unless informed otherwise, the Court assumes that each meet and confer effort involved all four discovery motions.  If so, including time for meet and confer on all four motions could constitute overbilling.  Therefore, the Court finds that two hours per motion is sufficient and awards Plaintiff sanctions in the amount of $2,400.      

  C.    Requests for Admissions are Deemed Admitted

Plaintiff propounded requests for admission (“RFAs”), Set One, on Cross-Defendant on November 13, 2023. After receiving no response, Plaintiff’s counsel commenced lengthy correspondence with Cross-Defendant, accepting their initial extension request and extending the deadline of responses due until May 23, 2024. To date, Cross-Defendant has not filed any responses. Therefore, Defendant seeks an order deeming RFAs admitted.

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under CCP § 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants that party relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (a)(1)-(2).)  The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

As of July 5, 2024, Cross-Defendant has not filed any opposition to the motion, nor has Cross-Defendant otherwise demonstrated that a response to the RFAs were served in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220 before the hearing. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s RFAs, Set One, is deemed admitted against Cross-Defendant. (CCP §2033.280(b).)   

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court reminds the parties that civil discovery is intended to be a self-executing system with minimal judicial intervention. (See Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Ct. (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 331; Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1291-1292.)

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Cross-Defendant to produce responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One).

 

The COURT GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to deem Requests for Admissions (Set One) admitted.

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions in the amount of $2,400.
