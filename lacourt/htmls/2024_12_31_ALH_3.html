[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANTS MICHAEL HOMAYUN, DDS AND MICHAEL HOMAYUN DENTAL CORPORATION dba COMFORT DENTAL CENTER’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

 

Dept. 3

I.            INTRODUCTION

On March 15, 2021, plaintiff Ricardo Calvario (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Michael Homayun, DDS (“Dr. Homayun”), Michael Homayun Dental Corporation dba Comfort Dental Center (“Comfort Dental”), and Oral Aesthetic Advocacy Group, Inc. dba Cosmetic Dentistry Grants (“Cosmetic Dentistry”).

Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) on September 21, 2021, which includes causes of action for: (1) professional negligence, (2) medical battery, (3) intentional misrepresentation, (4) concealment, (5) false promise, and (6) negligent misrepresentation.

On April 24, 2024, Dr. Homayun and Comfort Dental Center (collectively, “Defendants”) filed this motion for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s claims for Second Cause of Action for medical battery, Third Cause of Action for intentional misrepresentation, and Fourth Cause of Action for concealment. Defendants also moved for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action even though those were only asserted against Cosmetic Dentistry. Therefore, as an initial matter, the summary adjudication motion with respect to the Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action is DENIED. 

Plaintiff filed opposition papers on October 25, 2024.

Defendants filed reply papers on November 1, 2024.

On November 8, 2024, the Court continued the hearing on this motion to allow the parties to submit supplemental briefs summarizing the oral arguments made at the hearing. Both parties submitted supplemental briefs on December 2, 2024.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if that party contends that the cause of action has no merit or that there is no affirmative defense thereto, or that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, or both, or that there is no merit to a claim for damages . . . or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) A motion for summary adjudication shall proceed in all procedural respects as a motion for summary judgment.  (Id., subd. (f)(2).)  Therefore, a defendant moving for summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.) The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

III.        DISCUSSION

A.   Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action: Medical Battery

In 2018, Plaintiff was missing four upper front teeth and desired dental treatment to replace the missing teeth. (FAC, ¶ 10.) Plaintiff applied for grant funding through the program that Cosmetic Dentistry advertised as a grant program. (FAC, ¶ 11.) Thereafter, Mr. Calvario was approved for a “grant” and was referred by Cosmetic Dentistry to Defendants. (Id.) Plaintiff told Dr. Homayun that he wished to have four dental implants where his four missing teeth were. (FAC, ¶ 18.) Dr. Homayun told Calvario that four implants would not successfully hold and instead recommended six implants with individual crowns, which would cost $17,000. (FAC, ¶ 18.) On or about November 27, 2018, Plaintiff had six implant posts placed by Dr. Homayun, and various medical problems followed. Plaintiff claims that the treatment plan recommended by Dr. Homayun was not appropriate and that the implant posts were inappropriately placed.

In support of his medical battery claim, Plaintiff alleges that he “consented to the placement of the implant posts on the condition that [Defendants] confirmed in their professional capacity that [Plaintiff[ was an appropriate candidate for the procedure.” (FAC, ¶ 50.) Plaintiff claims Defendants failed to confirm whether he was an appropriate candidate yet proceeded anyways, causing him physical, mental, and financial harm. (FAC, ¶ 54.)

Defendants move for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s medical battery claim on the grounds that Plaintiff provided “informed consent” and Plaintiff cannot prove that Defendants intentionally deviated from that consent. (Motion, p. 10.) Defendants contend that Plaintiff “was adequately informed and consented to [the] recognized risks and complications [of the procedure].” (Motion, p. 11.) As evidence, Defendants refer to a consent form signed by Plaintiff. The majority of the consent form, attached as Defendants’ Exhibit B, is illegible to the Court. And even if the form were legible, Defendants’ emphasis on informed consent is irrelevant. (Motion, p. 11; Defs.’ Supp. Br., pp. 2-3.) As Defendants acknowledge, medical battery, based on a violation of conditional consent, and professional negligence, based on a lack of informed consent, are separate causes of action. (Defs.’ Supp. Br., p. 3 [citing Saxena v. Goffney (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 316, 324].)

The Court additionally notes that Plaintiff’s argument that his consent was conditioned on the Defendants doing the work properly and completing it is nothing more than a malpractice argument; the Court excludes that aspect of Plaintiff’s conditionality argument from its analysis of the medical battery claim. However, Plaintiff's use of the term “confirmed” in the context of Defendants confirming Plaintiff’s suitability for the procedure to which he consented suggests, in its imprecision, that somehow the Defendants were required to do something after obtaining the Plaintiff's consent to “confirm” his suitability or were required, perhaps, to further engage the Plaintiff on the issue of whether the procedure should go forward after some additional examination to “confirm” his suitability. If either were the case, it is impossible to determine from the illegible consent documentation provided by Defendants or any other evidence before the Court.

On reflection, though, that is needless perplexity. Plaintiff’s claim, in essence, is that before engaging in the dental work to which Plaintiff purportedly had consented (again, the illegible exhibits), the Defendants actually knew that Plaintiff was not a suitable candidate for the procedure, and, that, therefore, the Defendants did not, because they could not, “confirm” that the Plaintiff was a suitable candidate for the procedure. Because this condition was not met, and the Defendants nonetheless intentionally undertook work on the Plaintiff, a medical battery ensued.

Plaintiff does not make this claim particularly clearly (perhaps deliberately because it could raise a high bar at trial) and neither parties’ experts address whether Defendants ever met the alleged condition of confirming that Plaintiff was a suitable candidate for the procedure (despite addressing the negligence issue in detail). (See FAC, ¶ 50.) Defendant’s expert declaration of Peter Moy, DMD (“Dr. Moy”) only recites events recorded in Plaintiff’s medical records before broadly concluding that “Dr. Homayun provided an appropriate plan of care” and “did not deviate from the consent given.” (Defs.’s Ex. A, ¶ 25.) There is no reasoning supporting Dr. Moy’s conclusion and his unsupported opinion is insufficient to show that no triable issue of material fact exists. As the movants for summary adjudication, the initial burden to address the issue of conditionality was on Defendants, and having failed to do so, the burden to show a triable issue of fact did not shift to Plaintiff.

Accordingly, the motion for summary adjudication of the Second Cause of Action is DENIED.

B.   Plaintiff’s Third and Fourth Causes of Action for Intentional Misrepresentation and Concealment

Plaintiff alleges that Defendants misrepresented that Cosmetic Dentistry’s grant would cover the dental services and failed to disclose that their services were “significantly above market price” due to their referral agreement with Cosmetic Dentistry. (FAC, ¶¶ 62, 63, 76.) Plaintiff also alleges that Defendants concealed the fact that Dr. Homayun never intended to place implants without charging Plaintiff for additional subsequent work. (FAC, ¶¶ 77-78.)  

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s fraud claims are related to promises, representations, and information communicated to Plaintiff by Cosmetic Dentistry and not any representations by Defendants. Defendants cite Plaintiff’s deposition testimony and discovery responses as evidence that any representations about receiving grant funding for dental care and reimbursements for the cost of dental care were made by Cosmetic Dentistry. However, the deposition testimony merely shows that Plaintiff first had interactions with Cosmetic Dentistry and that Cosmetic Dentistry approved Plaintiff’s grant application. (UMF No. 38 [citing Defs.’ Ex. C, 55:19-56:13, 64:3-8].) It is not evidence that Defendants never made any of the alleged misrepresentations about the treatment cost or grant program. In fact, when describing an interaction with Dr. Homayun, Plaintiff testified that Dr. Homayun told him that the treatment would cost $17,000 and that he “had to pay it up front.” (Defs.’ Ex. C, 74:2-5.)

The Court additionally notes that even if Defendants shifted the burden of production to Plaintiff, Plaintiff testified that “Rose”, identified as “the girl at the front desk in the – in []Dr. Homayun[‘s office]” told him that “[he] had to pay the money up front before anything starts” and that “the grant was going to reimburse [him] later.” (Plaintiff’s Ex. 1, 75:9-76:4.) This is sufficient to raise a triable issue of material fact with respect to the Third Cause of Action for Intentional Misrepresentation.

Furthermore, Defendants fail to show that no misrepresentations were made regarding the market price of their dental services. Defendants cite Plaintiff’s testimony that he was given an estimate of $26,000 “to do all the upper part” and that he could not afford it.  (Ex. C, 58:19-24.) Defendants do not describe whether the quoted cost was for the same treatment and the fact that Defendants only charged Plaintiff $17,000 for six implants does not show that his services were market price. (Defs.’ Ex. C, 74:2-4, 20-25.)

As Defendants cannot prove that the entirety of the Third and Fourth Causes of Action lack merit, the motion for summary adjudication of the Third and Fourth Causes of Action is DENIED.

VI.    CONCLUSION

          In light of the foregoing, the motion for summary adjudication is DENIED.

On November 4, 2024, defendant Caregiver Services & Homecare, Inc. dba 1Heart Caregiver Services (“Defendant”, erroneously sued as “One Heart Caregivers”) filed this motion for an order compelling nonparty Verizon Wireless Services, LLC (“Verizon”) to produce documents in response to a deposition subpoena. The subpoena seeks Plaintiff’s cell phone records for the month of December 2020 in order to corroborate her testimony that she reported Defendant’s employee, Rene Borromeo (“Borromeo”), for touching her “on the hip.” Plaintiff testified that she contacted both 911 and Defendant on December 9, 2020, to report that Borromeo touched her “on the hip.” Whether Plaintiff in fact reported this incident to Defendant is relevant to whether Defendant had notice of Borromeo’s fitness as an employee because this action is premised on the allegation that Plaintiff was sexually assaulted by Borromeo on December 15, 2020.

The subpoena was issued to Verizon on September 24, 2024. (Avetisove Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff did not object to the subpoena, but Verizon sent an objection on October 17, 2024. (Id., Ex. E.)

Neither Plaintiff nor Verizon opposed this motion and Defendant has established that the records sought are directly relevant to the underlying action. Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED and Verizon is ordered to produce the requested documents within 20 days of the date of this Order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: EX PARTE APPLICATION FOR TEMPORARY RESTRAINING ORDER AND FOR ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE RE: PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

On December 16, 2024, defendants AHMC Healthcare, Inc., AHMC Monterey Park Hospital LP, AHMC Garfield Medical Center LP, AHMC Greater El Monte Community Hospital LP, AHMC Anaheim Regional Medical Center LP, AHMC San Gabriel Valley Medical Center, AHMC Seton Medical Center LLC, and AHMC Whittier Hospital Medical Center LP (collectively, “Defendants”) applied ex parte for a temporary restraining order (“TRO”) and for an order to show cause regarding a preliminary injunction staying the arbitration in Chantall Ramirez v. AHMC Monterey Park Hospital LP, AAA Case No. 01-0003-6368 (“Ramirez Arbitration”), which pertains to the individual claims asserted by plaintiff Chantall Ramirez (“Plaintiff”) for violations of Labor Code sections 226, 226.7, 512, and Sections 11 and 12 of the Industrial Welfare Commission’s Wage Orders 4 and/or 5, and Plaintiff’s individual claim for civil penalties for violations of Labor Code sections 2698, et seq. (Fishman Decl., ¶ 4.)

Plaintiff opposes the ex parte application on the grounds that the arbitrator has sole authority to hear (and decide) the issue of a stay. (Opp., p. 2.) The mutual arbitration agreement, attached as Exhibit 1 to the Declaration of Jeffrey F. Bollinger, states that “any and all claims and disputes that arise out of or are related in any way to [Plaintiff’s] employment or the termination of [her] employment with AHMC” shall be submitted to “final and binding arbitration.” (Bollinger Decl., Ex. 1, p. 119.) “[F]inal and binding arbitration will be the sole and exclusive remedy for any claim or dispute against AHMC Healthcare Inc. or any of its affiliated hospitals, facilities, companies or entities, and any of their employees, officers, directors, Medical Staff, physicians or agents.” (Ibid.) There is no carve-out for seeking provisional remedies in a court of law.

In a supplemental brief, Defendants argue that the right to seek injunctive relief cannot be waived by an arbitration agreement. Defendants cite to McGill v. Citibank, N.A. (2017) 2 Cal.5th 945, but as Plaintiff points out in her supplemental brief, McGill is factually and procedurally inapposite because it addresses the nonwaivable “statutory right to seek public injunctive relief under the UCL, the CLRA, or the false advertising law.” McGill distinguished public injunctive relief from “[r]elief that has the primary purpose or effect of redressing or preventing injury to an individual plaintiff—or to a group of individuals similarly situated to the plaintiff.” (McGill, supra, 2 Cal.5th at p. 955.) Defendant also argues that Roberts v. Packard, Packard & Johnson (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 822, supports the proposition that the Court retains jurisdiction in this specific instance to provide injunctive relief. However, Roberts outlines “several specific instances” where jurisdiction may be exercised, such as when an arbitrator is not granted general law discovery powers, or where there is a need “for judicial assistance in moving the arbitration forward”, or when the party is dissatisfied with the award of the arbitrator. (Roberts, supra, 217 Cal.App.4th at pp. 841-842.)  In fact, in Roberts, the specific issue was the award of attorney fees. Additionally, Defendants’ citation to Labor Code section 2699(e)(1) is unavailing because it only establishes that the Court has the same discretion as the Labor and Workforce Development Agency (“LWDA”) to award injunctive relief without any discussion of the scope of the Court’s jurisdiction to do so while arbitration is pending.

The Court agrees with Plaintiff’s assertion that while Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.8 applies to Defendants’ request for a TRO, Defendants have not shown that provisional relief is warranted. Section 1281.8 states that a party to a pending arbitration proceeding may apply to the court for a provisional remedy “only upon the ground that the award to which the applicant may be entitled may be rendered ineffectual without provisional relief.” Defendants have not shown that such grounds exist. Whether a stay is granted does not render “ineffectual” the relief that Defendants would obtain if they eventually prevail in arbitration. While Defendants would certainly incur costs in proceeding with the arbitration, Defendants do not provide any authority for their proposition that such costs rise to the level of irreparable harm justifying a TRO.   

 The ex parte application for a TRO and OSC re: preliminary injunction is DENIED.
