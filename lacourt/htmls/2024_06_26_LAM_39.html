DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV23804    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 26, 2024

CASE NUMBER

19STCV23804

MOTIONS

Motion to Quash Subpoenas

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Gilbert Bell

OPPOSING PARY

Defendant Kyle Johnson

 

MOTION

              Plaintiff Gilbert Bell (“Plaintiff”) moves to quash a subpoena Defendant Kyle Johnson (“Defendant”) served on “Wendover Investments Dan Patterson “(“Deponent”).  The subpoena seeks financial information about GB Entertainment LLC, of which Plaintiff is the sole member, and about Plaintiff.  Defendant opposes the motion.  Plaintiff did not personally serve the motion on Deponent as required.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1346.)  Plaintiff apparently failed to give any notice to Deponent.  Further, Plaintiff failed to include a separate statement setting forth the particular demands in the subpoena and the factual and legal reasons why the court should not compel production of the records.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(5).)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is denied without prejudice.  The court provides the following analysis to assist the parties.

ANALYSIS

If a subpoena requires the production of documents, the court may quash the subpoena entirely or modify it.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  In ruling on a motion to quash, “the Court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney's fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff has a right to privacy in his finances.  (Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, 656-657.)  Accordingly, the Court cannot order disclosure of financial information regarding Plaintiff unless the records are directly relevant to this litigation.  (See Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.)  Here, in the operative cross-complaint, Defendant alleges he is Nichelle Nichols’s (“Nichols”) conservator.  In a cross-complaint filed in this action, Defendant alleges, among other things, Plaintiff abused his position as Nichols’s manager and misappropriated funds from Nichols.  As such, Plaintiff’s financial records are directly relevant to this action. 

In reply, Plaintiff advances his own declaration, in which he asserts, as relevant to this action, he is destitute, without any assets, and the transactions Defendant references in the cross-complaint can be explained.  (See Declaration of Gilbert Bell in Support of Reply to Motion to Quash, ¶¶ 3, 16, 18, 21.)  Defendant is, nonetheless, entitled to conduct discovery and need not accept Plaintiff’s representations regarding the underlying facts. 

Defendant’s request for sanctions is denied, as the court does not find Plaintiff frivolously brought the instant motion. 

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 22STCV22330    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 26, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV22330

MOTIONS

Motions to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Vernon Goodwin

OPPOSING PARTY

Defendant the Regents of the University of California

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Vernon Goodwin (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Defendant Regents of the University of California (“Defendant”): Form Interrogatories, set one (“FROG”); Special Interrogatories, set one (“SROG”); and Requests for Production, set one (“RPD”).  Defendant opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories or requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300; 2031.310.) 

After Plaintiff filed these motions, Defendant supplemented its responses to RPD Nos. 6, 33, 36, 46, 47, and 64.  The motion to compel further responses to the RPD is, therefore, moot as to those RPD.  Likewise, Defendant supplemented its responses to all SROG at issue.  Accordingly, the motion to compel further responses to the SROG is moot.  While Plaintiff argues in reply the supplemental responses are insufficient, the Court cannot reach that issue.  If Plaintiff is dissatisfied with the supplemental responses, Plaintiff must move to compel further responses specifically with respect to them.

In RPD Nos. 27 and 29, Plaintiff asks Defendant to produce employee directories.  The court will not order Defendant to respond to RPD that seek information about other employees in California without providing the employees notice and an opportunity to opt out of the disclosure of their identities and contact information.  (See Belaire-West Landscape, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 554, 561-562 (Belaire-West Landscape) [where current and former employees were potential percipient witnesses to employer’s employment and wage practices, their identities and locations were properly discoverable unless, following proper notice, they objected to the disclosure].) 

Therefore, the court directs the parties to meet and confer regarding the notice procedure.  Likewise, the court will not order Defendant to respond to RPD No. 35, which would require Defendant to produce documents about classification or compensation reviews Defendant conducted with respect to other employees, absent notice and an opportunity for employees to opt out.  Is an alternative, Defendant may produce responsive documents with the identifying information for its other employees redacted.

In FROG No. 209.2, after the parties agreed through meet and confer sessions, Plaintiff asks Defendant to identify any employee in the past 10 years who filed a civil action against Defendant based on race, ethnic origin, disability, discrimination, harassment, or equal pay claims.  Plaintiff agreed to limit the request to the last ten years.  Defendant argues the FROG is overbroad. “In the context of discovery, evidence is ‘relevant’ if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a settlement. Admissibility is not the test, and it is sufficient if the information sought might reasonably lead to other, admissible evidence.” (Glenfed Development Corp. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117.)  FROG No. 209.2 is an Official Form Interrogatory.  Its relevance is obvious: the propounding party seeks information pertaining to potential witnesses to another party’s employment practices.  The FROG, therefore, is not irrelevant.

Defendant argues the request violates the privacy rights of third parties.  In that Plaintiff seeks the names, addresses, and telephone numbers of each employee who filed civil actions against it in the past 10 years.  However, Defendant does not dispute, through meet and confer discussions, such information is not being sought by Plaintiff.  Therefore, this issue is moot. 

Defendant contends the FROG is unduly burdensome.  If that were the case, Defendant was required to advance evidence of “the quantum of work required . . . [and] the ultimate effect of the burden is incommensurate with the result sought.”  (West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.)  Such evidence would include evidence of the total amount of hours required to accomplish the task. (Ibid.)  Defendant has not presented such evidence.

Defendant argues information responsive to FROG No. 209.2 is equally available to Plaintiff.  Plaintiff could conceivably search civil court filings in every jurisdiction, which are public, to find cases that have been filed against Defendant related to Plaintiff’s claims in this action.  However, it is undoubtedly easier for Defendant to locate such information, as Defendant necessarily has notice of such claims.  Therefore, Defendant must further respond to FROG No, 209.2.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the FROG is granted.  The court also grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RPD Nos. 27, 29, and 35, subject to providing the employees notice and an opportunity to opt out of the disclosure of their identities and contact information, pursuant to Belaire-West Landscape.  Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the SROG is moot.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.


Case Number: 23STCV14949    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

June 26, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV14949

MOTIONS: 

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

Motion to Compel Deposition

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Beatriz Villela

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendant General Motors LLC

 

MOTIONS

            Plaintiff Beatriz Villela (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Defendant General Motors LLC (“Defendant”) to Requests for Production of Documents, set one (“RPD”), Nos. 16-21.  Plaintiff also moves to compel Defendant to produce its person most qualified to appear at deposition and to testify on specified topics.  Defendant opposes the motions.

 ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, that party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310.) 

Plaintiff alleges her 2020 Chevrolet Trax vehicle had defects Defendant was unable to repair within a reasonable number of attempts.  Plaintiff, therefore, contends Defendant is liable under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“Song-Beverly”).  Plaintiff demanded Defendant produce documents about Plaintiff’s vehicle and the same make and model of that vehicle.

Defendant objects to the RPD to the extent Plaintiff seeks documents about vehicles other than Plaintiff’s vehicle.  As Plaintiff timely filed this motion to compel further responses, Defendant has the burden to justify its objections.  (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  Plaintiff is entitled to discovery regarding the alleged defects in other vehicles of the same make, model, and year as Plaintiff’s vehicle.  Likewise, Plaintiff is entitled to evidence of Defendant’s conduct towards other purchasers of defective vehicles, which is pertinent to a finding that the manufacturer willfully violated Song-Beverly in failing to adequately repair a vehicle.  (Johnson v. Ford Motor Co. (2005) 35 Cal. 4th 1191, 1204.)  Furthermore, “whether [Defendant] had a written policy on the requirement to repair or replace” under Song-Beverly is relevant to a finding of willfulness.  (Jensen v. BMW of North America, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112, 136.)  Plaintiff is entitled to discovery on Defendant’s policies and procedures.  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is granted as to an order compelling Defendant to respond with responsive documents regarding other vehicles that are of the same year, make, and model as Plaintiff’s vehicle. 

Defendant contends the information Plaintiff seeks about defects in vehicles of the same make and model as Plaintiff’s vehicle is irrelevant. “In the context of discovery, evidence is ‘relevant’ if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a settlement. Admissibility is not the test, and it is sufficient if the information sought might reasonably lead to other, admissible evidence.”  (Glenfed Development Corp. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117.)  As set forth above, in this case, information about vehicles that are the same make and model as Plaintiff’s vehicle is pertinent.

Defendant claims responsive information is a trade secret.  Defendant has not substantiated that objection.  “Trade secret means information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: (1) derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.”  (Civ. Code, § 3426.1.)  The Court is skeptical that Defendant derives economic value from defects in its vehicles and complaints to Defendant about such defects.  In any event, Defendant has not presented any testimony or documentary evidence in support of this contention.

Defendant also argues responsive information may include personally identifiable information.  Again, Defendant fails to substantiate this objection.  Defendant does not even specify whose personally identifiable information the responsive documents might include.  (See Opposition to Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, p. 7.) 

If a party to the action fails to appear for deposition after service of a deposition notice and the party has not served a valid objection to the deposition notice, the party who noticed the deposition may move for an order to compel the deponent to attend and testify at deposition. (Code Civ. Proc., §2025.450, subd. (a).)  Plaintiff served Defendant with a notice of deposition.

In opposing Plaintiff’s motion to compel, Defendant contends the deposition notice is overbroad and seeks trade secret material.  If Defendant sought to avoid producing a person most qualified or documents on specified topics, Defendant was required to seek a protective order so “[t]hat certain matters not be inquired into.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b)(9).)  Defendant did not do so.  The scope of the requested information is not an “error or irregularity” in the deposition notice to which Defendant could object. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410.)

Defendant also argues Plaintiff failed to file a separate statement in connection with the motion to compel deposition. Plaintiff was not required to do so, as Defendant did not appear for deposition.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(4).)  Because Defendant failed to appear for deposition, Plaintiff could not possibly file a separate statement of the questions Plaintiff asked at the deposition and the answers Defendant gave thereto.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subd. (c)(1)-(2).) 

Likewise, Defendant’s argument Plaintiff failed to adequately attempt to informally resolve this matter is misplaced.  Plaintiff was not required to do so, as Defendant did not appear for the  deposition. Instead, Plaintiff was required to “contact[] [Defendant] to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)  Plaintiff did so.  (Declaration of Noreelie Panhwar in Support of Motion to Compel Deposition, Exhibits 4-5.)

Moreover, Defendant concedes it must produce its person most qualified to appear for deposition.  (See Opposition to Motion to Compel Deposition.)  Accordingly, the court grants the motion to compel Defendant’s deposition. (See Code Civ. Proc., §2025.450, subd. (a).)

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to the RPD is granted, and Defendant is ordered to respond with responsive documents regarding other vehicles that are of the same year, make, and model as Plaintiff’s vehicle.

Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant to produce its person most qualified to testify on specified topics and to produce documents is granted. Defendant is to produce its person most qualified for deposition within 30 days of notice of this order, unless Plaintiff stipulates otherwise. The scope of the deposition is as set forth in Exhibit 2 attached to the Panhwar  Declaration.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
