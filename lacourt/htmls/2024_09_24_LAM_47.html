DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22STCV32478    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 24, 2024               TRIAL DATE: October 8, 2024

           

CASE:                         Ken Houang, et al. v. Rochelle H. Sterling, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV32478           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH THIRD-PARTY DEPOSITION SUBPOENA

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Ken Houang.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 09/19/24.  

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a habitability action that was filed on October 4, 2022. Plaintiffs, who are and were tenants at a residential apartment complex, allege that Defendants permitted the premises to develop extensive uninhabitable conditions and allowed those conditions to persist in violation of statutes and municipal ordinances. Plaintiffs seek monetary and injunctive relief.

 

Plaintiff moves to compel non-parties LA Testing and EMSL Analytical, Inc.  to appear for deposition and produce documents.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Deposition is GRANTED. LA Testing and EMSL Analytical, Inc. are ordered to appear for deposition and produce responsive documents on or before October 1, 2024.

 

This ruling is conditioned on the payment of $60 in filing fees within 5 days of this order.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel non-parties LA Testing and EMSL Analytical, Inc.  to appear for deposition and produce documents.

 

Multiple Motions

 

            Multiple motions should not be combined into a single filing.¿(See¿Govt. Code,¿§ 70617(a)(4) [setting forth the required filing fee for each motion, application, or any other paper or request requiring a hearing];¿see¿also¿Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011)¿[“Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.”].)

 

Here, Plaintiff seeks to compel compliance with two deposition subpoenas in a single motion. Combining multiple motions under the guise of one motion with one hearing reservation manipulates the Court Reservation System and unfairly jumps ahead of other litigants. Moreover, combining motions to avoid payment of separate filing fees deprives the Court of filing fees it is otherwise entitled to collect.

 

            The Court therefore conditions its ruling on this motion on the payment of an additional $60 in filing fees within 5 days of this order.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1(a) states, in relevant part:

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion . . . may make an order . . .  directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1(a).) There is no meet and confer requirement in section 1987.1. There is also no requirement for a separate statement when no response has been provided to the request for discovery. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(b)(1).) Furthermore, there is no requirement for a showing of good cause for production of documents in connection with a deposition subpoena. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2020.510(b); Terry v. SLICO (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 352, 358.)

 

Failure to Appear

 

            Plaintiff personally served both LA Testing and EMSL Analytical, Inc. with deposition subpoenas on August 1, 2024 for deposition on August 26, 2024. (Declaration of Gerald S. Ohn ¶ 3.) Neither entity appeared for deposition. (Id. ¶ 3; Exhs. B-C.) Plaintiff is therefore entitled to compel these entities to appear and produce responsive documents.

Case Number: 23STCV03790    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 24, 2024               TRIAL DATE: November 26, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         John Nguyen v. Shift Operations, LLC et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV03790           

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES AND COSTS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff John Nguyen

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         02/21/23: Complaint filed.

·         07/03/24: Judgment on statutory offer to compromise entered as to JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on February 21, 2023, against the vehicle dealer and its assignee. Plaintiff purchased a 2016 Mercedes-Benz S-Class on October 28, 2022. Plaintiff subsequently discovered the vehicle had severe structural damage and engine leaks from a previous accident which was not disclosed to him before purchasing the vehicle.

 

Plaintiff moves for an award of attorney’s fees and costs following acceptance of a statutory offer to compromise.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED in the amount of $19,762.50 plus $1,027.40 in costs.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves for an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $23,098.50 and costs in the amount of $1,027.40 following acceptance of a statutory offer to compromise.

Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections

 

            Plaintiff objects to portions of the Declaration of Tod Beebe submitted in support of the opposition to this motion. The Court rules on these objections as follows:

 

Objection No. 1:  SUSTAINED as hearsay. This exhibit is an out-of-court statement offered for the truth of its contents.

 

Objection Nos. 2-5: SUSTAINED as irrelevant. The conclusions of opposing counsel are arguments, not evidence, and have no probative value.

 

Entitlement to Fees

 

Plaintiff seeks an award of attorney’s fees pursuant to a settlement agreement reached by the parties. Plaintiff brought claims for violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. (Civ. Code § 1790 et seq.). Civil Code section 1794(d) states:

 

If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.

 

            On May 22, 2024, Defendant JPMorgan Chase Bank accepted Plaintiff’s offer to compromise under Code of Civil Procedure section 998 to pay Plaintiff $20,865.76 in exchange for a return of the vehicle. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 4.) The offer provided for an award of reasonable attorney’s fees to Plaintiff by noticed motion pursuant to Civil Code section 1794(d), with the award to be paid by Defendant. (Id. ¶ 4.) The parties agree that Plaintiff is the prevailing party for the purposes of this motion. (Id.)

 

Reasonableness of Fees

 

            Plaintiff requests a total fee award of $23,098.50, based on a total of $19,762.50 in fees accrued by Plaintiff’s counsel before the instant motion, plus $3,336.00 in anticipated fees to review the opposition, draft a reply brief, and attend the hearing, plus $1,027.40 in costs. (See Notice of Motion.)

 

            Reasonable attorney’s fees are allowable costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc § 1033.5(a)(10), (c)(5)(B).) In actions that are based on a contract, “where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract… shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.” (Civil Code § 1717(a) [emphasis added].) A recovery of attorney’s fees is authorized even in noncontractual or tort actions if the contractual provision for fee recovery is worded broadly enough. (See Code Civ. Proc § 10211; Maynard v. BTI Group, Inc. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 984, 993 [agreement to award fees based on outcome of “any dispute” encompasses all claims, “whether in contract, tort or otherwise]; Lockton v. O'Rourke (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1051, 1076; Lerner v. Ward (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 155, 160.)

Reasonable attorney’s fees shall be fixed by the Court and shall be an element of the costs of suit. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(c)(5)(B).) Reasonable attorney’s fees are ordinarily determined by the Court pursuant to the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096; Margolin v. Regional Planning Com. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004 [“California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys' fee award.”].) “[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award….”  (Ibid.) In setting the hourly rate for a fee award, courts are entitled to consider the “fees customarily charged by that attorney and others in the community for similar work.” (Bihun v. AT&T Info. Sys., Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 976, 997 [affirming rate of $450 per hour], overruled on other grounds by Lakin v. Watkins Associated Indus. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 644, 664.)  The burden is on the party seeking attorney’s fees to prove the reasonableness of the fees. (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 615.) 

The Court has broad discretion in determining the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award, which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.” (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1393-1394.)  The Court need not explain its calculation of the amount of attorney’s fees awarded in detail; identifying the factors considered in arriving at the amount will suffice. (Ventura v. ABM Indus. Inc. (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 274-275.)

Plaintiff has provided an itemized list of the attorney time billed in connection with this case. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 6.) Plaintiffs’ counsel billed a total of 54.3 hours of attorney and paralegal time in connection with this case at hourly rates ranging from $155 to $695 per hour, plus 4.5 anticipated hours at $695 per hour. (Id.) Attorneys Gregory Babbit, Stacia Ratliff, and Joseph Green attest to their own skills, training, and hourly rates. (Declaration of Gregory Babbit ISO Mot; Declaration of Stacia Ratliff ISO Mot.; Declaration of Joseph Green ISO Mot.) Attorney Christopher Barry attests to the skills, training, and hourly rates of the remaining attorneys and paralegals involved in this matter as their supervisor and Managing Partner of the firm, as well as his own. (Declaration of Christopher Barry ISO Mot. ¶¶ 9-20.)  The Court observes that these hours and fees were incurred by seven attorneys and two paralegals across eighteen months of litigation. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 6; see also Barry Decl. ¶ 9.) Close examination of the records shows, however, that the bulk of the hours were incurred by Attorney Laura Gaudet, who billed $280 per hour for 29.4 hours of work. (Id.) Further, the records show that the remaining attorneys incurred few hours performing generally non-duplicative tasks. Moreover, the records show that research and drafting tasks were generally assigned to more junior attorneys charging lower rates, while reserving strategic and editing pursuits and client communication to more senior attorneys. (Id.) The sole major example of a senior attorney performing drafting work is Attorney Barry’s preparation of this fee motion, which, including the 4.8 anticipated hours, accounts for 8.3 of his claimed 9.9 hours incurred. (Id.)  These records demonstrate that Plaintiff’s counsel exercised billing judgment, notwithstanding the number of attorneys involved in the case. That said, the Court is not inclined to award Plaintiff fees which were not actually incurred, and therefore will not award the $3,336 in anticipated fees.

            Defendant, in opposition, argues that the hourly rates of Plaintiff’s counsel are unreasonable and should instead be capped at $495 per hour for partners and $371 for associates, based on the “2023 Real Rate Report.” The Court declines to consider this material, as it is not only hearsay, but does not analyze the state of the legal profession with respect to Song-Beverly cases, and thus is of limited value to this matter. Findings by other trial courts are similarly unpersuasive. Nor is the Court persuaded by Defendant’s argument that the case was overstaffed; despite the number of attorneys involved in the case, the billing records do not demonstrate runaway costs caused by high staffing and duplicative work.

 

Defendant also identifies instances of what it contends are excessive billing records for preparation of template filings, including the instant motion and duplicative billing by various attorneys. However, Defendant offers no evidence that this is so: the arguments of Defendant’s counsel in opposition briefing are not evidence that these entries are improper. Nor is the Court persuaded by the unsupported assertion that it was improper for Plaintiff’s counsel to bill time in increments of one-tenth of an hour for various tasks which Defendant claims, without evidence, took “a few seconds.”

 

The Court is therefore not persuaded that any reduction of the fee award is required beyond the elimination of anticipated hours. The Court will therefore award attorney’s fees in the amount of $19,762.50.

 

Costs

 

            Plaintiffs also seek an award of costs in the amount of $1,027.40.

 

            A prevailing party on a Song-Beverly claim may also seek to recover costs reasonably incurred, as well as attorney’s fees. (Civ. Code § 1794(d).) Once a request for costs is properly challenged, the burden shifts to the propounding party to demonstrate why such costs are recoverable and proper. (Whatley-Miller v. Cooper (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1103, 1113.) Costs are normally sought by way of a memorandum of costs pursuant to California Rule of Court 3.1700(a). However, as Defendant consents to the determination of costs via this motion, the Court will exercise its discretion to do so.

 

Plaintiff included a memorandum of costs as part of the filings on this motion. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 8.) Defendant does not object to the costs claimed. The Court will therefore award the full amount of costs. 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED in the amount of $19,762.50 plus $1,027.40 in costs.
