DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 22STCV34229    Hearing Date: October 31, 2023    Dept: 26

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

ELYSIAN HEALTH LLC; MODERN MANAGEMENT SOLUTIONS, INC.; and ZACHARY AMENT,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

justin white; applied approach services, inc.; gerry van nortwick; deena manion; david bifulco; priya martindale; et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.: 22STCV34229

 

 Hearing Date: October 31, 2023

 

 [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ demurrer to the third amended complaint

 

Procedural Background       

            On October 24, 2022, Plaintiffs Elysian Health LLC (“Elysian”), Modern Management Solutions, Inc. (“MM”) and Zachary Ament (“Ament”) filed the instant breach of fiduciary action regarding the management of Elysian. 

On November 3, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint against Defendants Justin White (“White”), Applied Approach Services, Inc. (“Applied”), Gerry Van Nortwick (“Van Nortwick”), Deena Manion (“Manion”), David Bifulco (“Bifulco”), and Priya Martindale (“Martindale”) (collectively “Defendants”).  On February 28, 2022, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the First Amended Complaint with leave to amend.  (Order 2/28/23.)

On March 20, 2023, Plaintiffs Elysian, MM, and Ament filed a Second Amended Complaint against Defendants.  On July 31, 2023, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint with leave to amend.  (Order 7/31/23.)

On August 21, 2023, Plaintiffs MM and Ament (jointly “Plaintiffs”) filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) against Defendants, nominal defendant Elysian, LAVN Investment QSST U/A DTD 9/1/2009, and Lisa A. Van Nortwick.  The TAC asserts twelve causes of action for (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty by MM against White, Van Nortwick, Manion, Bifulco, and Martindale, (2) Enforcing Member/Manager Rights by MM against White, Elysian, LAVN Investment QSST U/A DTD 9/1/2009, and Lisa A. Van Nortwick, (3) Transfer of Ownership of Company Assets by Elysian against White, LAVN Investment QSST U/A DTD 9/1/2009, and Lisa A. Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal 3d 311, 318.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). (Ion Equipment Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881.)

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dep’t of Water & Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 968, 994.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (SKF Farms v. Superior Ct. (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.) 

 

Meet and Confer Requirement

Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41, subdivision (a) requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer¿in person or by telephone¿with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due and if they are unable to meet the demurring party shall be granted an automatic 30-day extension.  (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).)  The demurring party must also file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts.  (Id.¿at (a)(3).)¿ If an amended pleading is filed, the parties must meet and confer again before a demurrer may be filed to the amended pleading.  (Id.¿at (a).)

Moving Defendants have satisfied the meet and confer requirement.  (Burns Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1.)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff Ament’s Standing

            Moving Defendants assert that Plaintiff Ament lacks standing as to the fourth, and eighth through eleventh causes of action.

            Code of Civil Procedure § 367 provides that “[e]very action must be prosecuted in the name of the real party in interest, except as otherwise provided by statute.”  (CCP § 367.)  “Only the real party in interest has ‘an actual and substantial interest in the subject matter of the action,’ and stands to be ‘benefited or injured’ by a judgment in the action.”  (City of Santa Monica v. Stewart (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 43, 60.)  

            “Shareholders may bring two types of actions, ‘a direct action filed by the shareholder individually (or on behalf of a class of shareholders to which he or she belongs) for injury to his or her interest as a shareholder,’ or a ‘derivative action filed on behalf of the corporation for injury to the corporation for which it has failed or refused to sue.’ [Citation.] ‘The two actions are mutually exclusive: i.e., the right of action and recovery belongs either to the shareholders (direct action) or to the corporation (derivative action).” [Citation.] When the claim is derivative, the ‘shareholder is merely a nominal plaintiff.... Even though the corporation is joined as a nominal defendant ..., it is the real party in interest to which any recovery usually belongs.’ [Citation.]”  (Schuster v. Gardner (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 305, 311–312.)  “Thus, ‘the action is derivative, I.e., in the corporate right, if the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, or to the whole body of its stock or property without any severance or distribution among individual holders, or if it seeks to recover assets for the corporation or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.’” (Jones v. H. F. Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93, 106.) 

            However, “[a] single cause of action by a shareholder can give rise to derivative claims, individual claims, or both.”  (Goles v. Sawhney (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 1014, 1019 Fn.3.)  Further, there is no requirement that the alleged wrong be unique to that plaintiff or that the alleged wrong affects any number of shareholders.  Rather, “[i]f the injury is not incidential [sic] to an injury to the corporation, an individual cause of action exists.”  (Jones, supra, 1 Cal.3d at p.107.)

            “Because a corporation exists as a separate legal entity, the shareholders have no direct cause of action or right of recovery against those who have harmed it. The shareholders may, however, bring a derivative suit to enforce the corporation's rights and redress its injuries when the board of directors fails or refuses to do so.”  (Grosset, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p.1108, [italics added].)  Moreover, a shareholder cannot initiate a derivative suit without first informing the directors about the action and making a reasonable effort to induce them to commence suit themselves or otherwise redress the wrong, unless such efforts would be “useless” or “futile.”  (Corp. Code, § 800(b)(2); Corp. Code, § 2900(d)(2).)  A plaintiff must allege compliance.  (Ibid.) “Failure to comply with the requirements of the statute deprives a litigant of standing.”  (Nelson v. Anderson (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 111, 127.)

            Here, Plaintiff MM is the only plaintiff that is a member of Elysian and alleges that it would be futile to inform the directors and use reasonable efforts to induce the Directors White and Van Nortwick to commence suit against themselves.  (TAC ¶¶ 1, 11.)  However, Plaintiff Ament is merely the principal and a shareholder of Plaintiff MM and serving as a director of Elysian on behalf of Plaintiff MM.  (TAC ¶ 3.)  Thus, Plaintiff, who is not a member of Elysian cannot bring a derivative claim on behalf of Elysian.  (Sirott v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 371, 381–382 [“section 17709.02 requires both ‘contemporaneous’ membership—meaning the party seeking to bring a derivative claim was a member in the LLC at the time of the challenged transaction (or became a member by gaining an interest from a party who was a member at the time of the transaction)—and ‘continuous’ membership—meaning the party was a member throughout the litigation of a derivative claim.”].)  

            The fourth cause of action challenging the election or appointment of a director is based on the allegation that the “acts and omissions of WHITE, …, demonstrate that he has abandoned any duty or obligation to the company, that he manipulates the company for his own personal purposes, and therefore, warrant and mandate his removal as a member.”  (TAC ¶ 76, [italics added].)  This allegation makes clear that the alleged harm is to Elysian.  The fourth cause of action is therefore a derivative claim brought on behalf of Elysian.  As a non-member of Elysian, Plaintiff Ament lacks standing to bring this claim individually as asserted.

            The eighth cause of action seeks an accounting of Elysian’s company books and data.  (TAC ¶ 98.)  “A cause of action for an accounting requires a showing that a relationship exists between the plaintiff and defendant that requires an accounting, and that some balance is due the plaintiff that can only be ascertained by an accounting.”  (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 179.) 

            Here, Plaintiffs allege that an accounting is needed “of the financial transactions pursuant to which defendants have taken money for their personal use, used it to pay their personal expenses, or have used ELYSIAN funds to pay for expenses disguised as work related when in fact they were benefitting only these select defendants. Because Plaintiffs do not have complete access to the underlying financial records and backup information, they cannot determine the exact amounts owed or amounts which need to be equalized to MM without a detailed accounting.”  (TAC ¶ 99, [Italics added].)  As alleged, the balance due is owed to Elysian and to MM derivatively as a member and is therefore a derivative claim.  Thus, Plaintiff Ament – who is not a member of Elysian – does not have standing to bring the eighth cause of action.

            The ninth cause of action seeks declaratory relief claiming that “[a] dispute has ensued between the Plaintiffs and Defendants with respect to the interpretation, enforceability, and the rights, duties, obligations and benefits of each party to the Operating Agreement and the rights of the board members of ELYSIAN.”  (TAC ¶ 103.)  Specifically, Plaintiffs allege that there is a “dispute whether and to what extent Plaintiffs have the right to have two of the four board members appointed by Plaintiffs, whether MM is and remains the manager, the conditions under which the board or members may change the manager of ELYSIAN, and the authority of defendants to issue distributions. There is also a dispute as to whether ELYSIAN can form a business relationship with a new company whose certification is tainted by the prior revocation of certification of ELYSIAN such that all of ELYSIAN’s ongoing business and revenue would be jeopardized.”  (TAC ¶ 104.)  Plaintiffs allege that the right to appoint board members belongs to MM as the manager.  (TAC ¶ 21.)  Similarly, Plaintiff Ament does not dispute whether MM is the manager.  However, as Plaintiff Ament is a board member, “the conditions under which the board or members may change the manager of ELYSIAN” is under dispute by Ament in his individual capacity.  Plaintiff Ament has standing to assert the ninth cause of action.

            The tenth and eleventh causes of action seek reformation of “Action by Written Consent dated March 28, 2020 which defendants, WHITE, LAVN and AMENT executed, which document defies the Operating Agreement was not properly executed by all members and should be cancelled or reformed; and its corresponding Action by Written Consent of March 22, 2022 executed by WHITE, AMENT, LAVN and MM as Manager only; the minutes of the special board meeting on November 26, 2019 allegedly attended by WHITE and VAN NORTWICK as well as Bradley Athens who was affiliated with a former member, which took place days after the passing of Justin Wells, and others whose existence is being ascertained in this case.”  (TAC ¶ 109.)  As alleged, Plaintiff Ament is a party to some of the alleged agreements that Plaintiffs seek reformed or cancelled.  Moreover, “‘[t]he right to reformation of an instrument is not restricted to the original parties to the transaction [citation].’”  (Panterra GP, Inc. v. Superior Court of Kern County (2022) 74 Cal.App.5th 697, 714.)  Rather, “Civil Code section 3399 permits reformation of a contract upon application of any aggrieved party.”  (Ibid.)  Similarly, cancellation of an instrument only requires those to have been injured by it.  (Civ. Code, § 3412.)

            Here, Plaintiffs allege that they have been harmed by the various agreements.  (TAC ¶¶ 111, 115.) As such, Plaintiffs have standing to seek this remedy for the tenth and eleventh causes of action.

            In sum, Plaintiff Ament lacks standing as to the fourth and eighth causes of action but has standing as to the ninth through eleventh causes of action.  Accordingly, Moving Defendants demurrer is SUSTAINED for lack of standing as to Plaintiff Ament as to the fourth and eighth causes of action.

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

            Moving Defendants contend that Plaintiff MM fails to sufficiently allege a breach of fiduciary duty against Defendants Martindale, Manion, and Bifulco.

The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are: (1) the existence of a fiduciary duty; (2) the breach of that duty; and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach.’”  (IIG Wireless, Inc. v. Yi (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 630, 646, [internal citation omitted].)  “‘[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.’” (City of Hope Nat'l Med. Ctr. v. Genentech (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386.)  Facts giving rise to a confidential, fiduciary or trustee relationship must be pled, and a “bare allegation that defendants assumed a fiduciary relationship” is a conclusion. (Zumbrun v. Univ. of So. Cal. (1972) 25 Cal.App.3d 1, 13.)

“‘Corporate officers and directors are not permitted to use their position of trust and confidence to further their private interests. While technically not trustees, they stand in a fiduciary relation to the corporation and its stockholders’ [Citation.]”  (Angelica Textile Services, Inc. v. Park (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 495, 509.) 

Here, the claim for breach of fiduciary duty is brought by Plaintiff MM derivatively on behalf of Elysian.  Manion is the Chief Clinical Officer for Elysian and was promoted to Executive Director for Elysian.  (TAC ¶ 8.)  Bifulco is the Chief Operating Officer for Elysian.  (TAC ¶ 9.)  Martindale is the Director of Operations and Human Resources for Elysian.  (TAC ¶ 10.)  Manion, Bifulco, and Martindale are each corporate officers and/or directors of Elysian and as high-level employees of Elysian owe a fiduciary duty to Plaintiff MM as a member and to the company – Elysian – in their roles.  (Angelica Textile Services, Inc., supra, 220 Cal.App.4th at p.509.)  However, there are little to no substantive allegations regarding any alleged breach.

Rather, the TAC alleges that “Plaintiffs are informed and believe that VAN NORTWICK, MANION, BIFULCO AND MARTINDALE knowingly support and enable WHITE to engage in the self-dealing and wrong-doing WHITE has engaged in, because WHITE ensures that benefits inure to each of their benefit as well, and because WHITE and VAN NORTWICK have plans and designs in conjunction with MANION, BIFULCO AND MARTINDALE to usurp ELYSIAN’s assets and business for their own personal benefit and to the detriment of ELYSIAN in establishing a new company to replace ELYSIAN and take over its assets and business. Plaintiffs are informed and believe that VAN NORTWICK, MANION, BIFULCO AND MARTINDALE have knowingly empowered WHITE to take all the actions harming ELYSIAN and Plaintiffs, and expressly supported WHITE by in improper votes of the Board as well as actions WHITE has taken to marginalize and restrict AMENT and MM’s access to ELYSIAN information, and by assuring WHITE that the financial misdealing will be overlooked and not pursued by the Board or other executives and staff.”  (TAC ¶ 57.)

“Plaintiff is further informed and believes that defendants MANION and BIFULCO have each breached their fiduciary duties to ELYSIAN by diverting business and patients of ELYSIAN and future business prospects of ELYSIAN for the benefit of a new entity being formed by WHITE and VAN NORTWICK, in exchange for being promised and receiving equity and other financial benefits from the new entity. In addition, Plaintiffs are informed and believe thereon alleging that these defendants, with no regard for the ongoing sanctity of ELYSIAN, and with only the intent to divert ELYSIAN’s business for their future entities, would regularly allow false information to be circulated to new and prospective patients of ELYSIAN, would admit such patients under incorrect treatment programs and bill them improperly with insurance companies, all of which seriously jeopardizes ELYSIAN’s ongoing status and ability to provide treatment facilities and further undermines ELYSIAN’s ability to obtain future DHCS certificates, none of which concerned these defendants since they were willing to sacrifice ELYSIAN’s long-term existence in exchange for short term financial gains which monies are not being distributed to ELYSIAN’s equity holders, but rather being used to pay expenses and fund the new entity defendants intend to use going forward.”  (TAC ¶ 58.)

These allegations are insufficient.  As to Manion and Bifulco, the TAC alleges that on information and belief they are diverting business and patients from Elysian for their own personal gain.  (TAC ¶ 58.)  While this would be a breach of fiduciary duty, there are no facts alleged to support Plaintiffs’ belief that Manion and Bifulco are diverting business and patients from Elysian.  “A ‘ “[p]laintiff may allege on information and belief any matters that are not within his personal knowledge, if he has information leading him to believe that the allegations are true ’ ” [Citation.], and thus a pleading made on information and belief is insufficient if it ‘merely assert[s] the facts so alleged without alleging such information that “lead[s] [the plaintiff] to believe that the allegations are true.” ’ [Citation.] ”  (Gomes v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1149, 1158–1159.)  Plaintiffs’ claim that defendants Martindale, Manion, and Bifulco “knowingly supported and enabled” Defendant White is similarly defective.  (TAC ¶ 57.)  There is no allegation as to what actions Martindale, Manion, and Bifulco undertook to support White.  Similarly, there are no facts alleged to support Plaintiffs’ belief that Martindale, Manion, and Bifulco “knowingly supported and enabled” Defendant White in some unspecified way.  In sum, the allegations against Martindale, Manion, and Bifulco fail to allege any facts to support an alleged breach of fiduciary duty.  As to Martindale, the TAC fails to make any substantive allegations.

Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Fourth, Sixth and Seventh Causes of Actions

            Moving Defendants contend that the fourth, sixth, and seventh causes of action fail because they are merely remedies.  A cause of action must exist for a claim for relief to be stated.  (See Ivanoff v. Bank of America, N.A. (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 719, 734.)

            As to the fourth cause of action, Plaintiffs seek the removal of Defendant White as a director and member of Elysian.  No clear statutory authority permits such an action.  The cited authority -- Corporations Code § 709 -- provides for suits to determine the validity of an election or appointment of a director of a corporation -- not a director selected for limited liability company pursuant to the operating agreement.  Moreover, though the Court has a power under equity independent of statute to remove a corporate director, (Brown v. North Ventura Road Development Co. (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 227, 232 [“Since directors hold a position of trust, judicial power to remove them exists independent of statute.”]), such relief would have to be warranted by a substantive claim, such as for breach of fiduciary duty.  Accordingly, the fourth cause of action is merely a prayer for specific equitable relief -- not a substantive claim.
            The sixth cause of action seeks appointment of a provisional director.  Under Corporations Code § 308, an action can be brought to appoint a provisional director to the board of directors for a corporation in the case of a deadlock.  No similar provision applies to limited liability companies.  To the extent that such a request may be made for an LLC, such a request would be a remedy based on the Court’s power in equity.  (See e.g., Jarvis v. Jarvis (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 113, 144.)  Accordingly, as a remedy, such a request is not itself a cause of action.

            Finally, the seventh cause of action -- like the fourth cause of action -- seeks removal of White as a director.  As noted above, such relief would have to be warranted by a substantive claim such as for breach of fiduciary duty.  Accordingly, the seventh cause of action is merely a prayer for specific equitable relief -- not a substantive claim.

            Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth, sixth, and seventh causes of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Fifth Cause of Action for Payment of Dividend

            Moving Defendants contend that the fifth cause of action to compel payment of paid dividends is insufficiently alleged.

            “‘The decision concerning the declaration of a dividend where a legal dividend fund is available rests within the sole discretion of the board of directors. Courts are reluctant to interfere with the exercise of the directors' business judgment unless the withholding is fraudulent, oppressive or totally without merit.’ [Citation.]”  (State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 434, 450.)  However, once a dividend has been lawfully declared, a dividend cannot be revoked or withheld and “[e]ach holder of common stock acquired a vested right to the payment of the dividend, which cannot be defeated by later revocation of the dividend without his consent.”  (Meyers v. El Tejon Oil & Refining Co. (1946) 29 Cal.2d 184, 188.)

            Here, the TAC alleges that “WHITE has taken for himself and paid to VAN NORTWICK funds, assets and property of ELYSIAN which Plaintiffs allege on information and belief that have no proper substantiation other than to constitute distributions, even though the proportionate share has not been provided to other members.”  (TAC ¶ 80.)  Thus, as alleged, White declared dividends but failed to make payment to Plaintiff MM.  While made on information and belief, some facts are alleged to support this allegation – i.e., that White took these assets, and there is no other legitimate basis for it. 

            Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

 

Tenth and Eleventh Causes of Action: Reformation and Cancellation of Contracts

            Moving Defendants assert that the tenth and eleventh causes of action fail because they are insufficiently alleged. 

            “When, through fraud or a mutual mistake of the parties, or a mistake of one party, which the other at the time knew or suspected, a written contract does not truly express the intention of the parties, it may be revised on the application of a party aggrieved, so as to express that intention, so far as it can be done without prejudice to rights acquired by third persons, in good faith and for value.”  (Civ. Code, § 3399.)  “‘To prevail on a claim to cancel an instrument, a plaintiff must prove (1) the instrument is void or voidable due to, for example, fraud; and (2) there is a reasonable apprehension of serious injury including pecuniary loss or the prejudicial alteration of one's position.’ [Citations.]”  (Thompson v. Ioane (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 1180, 1193–1194.)

            Here, the tenth and eleventh causes of action seek reformation of “Action by Written Consent dated March 28, 2020 which defendants, WHITE, LAVN and AMENT executed, which document defies the Operating Agreement was not properly executed by all members and should be cancelled or reformed; and its corresponding Action by Written Consent of March 22, 2022 executed by WHITE, AMENT, LAVN and MM as Manager only; the minutes of the special board meeting on November 26, 2019 allegedly attended by WHITE and VAN NORTWICK as well as Bradley Athens who was affiliated with a former member, which took place days after the passing of Justin Wells, and others whose existence is being ascertained in this case.”  (TAC ¶ 109.)  Plaintiffs claim that these agreements do not reflect the true intent of the parties and that Plaintiff will suffer prejudice and pecuniary loss unless the agreements are reformed.

            While these allegations are not particularly specific, California follows a notice pleading standard, (See e.g., Morris v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 279, 305, Fn. 14), and the pleadings are generally liberally construed.  (CCP § 452, [“In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”].)  Critically, these allegations do identify three specific contracts and the basis for cancellation/reformation – i.e., that these agreements are in violation of Elysium’s operating agreement – and that Plaintiffs will suffer harm if these agreements are not reformed.

            Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ demurrer to the ninth and tenth causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

Defendant Van Norwick’s Joinder to the Demurrer

            Plaintiff contends that Defendant Van Norwick’s notice of joinder is untimely under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.40(a), and violates California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1320. 

            “A person against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may, within 30 days after service of the complaint or cross-complaint, demur to the complaint or cross-complaint.”  (CCP § 430.40(a).)  “[S]ection 430.40 is permissive, not mandatory, because it uses the term ‘may’ rather than ‘must.’”  (Jackson v. Doe (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 742, 750.)  Further, a demurrer can be heard regardless of procedural issues as “ ‘[t]here is no absolute right to have a pleading stricken for lack of timeliness in filing where no question of jurisdiction is involved, and where, as here, the late filing was a mere irregularity [citation]; the granting or denial of the motion is a matter which lies within the discretion of the court.’ [Citation.]”  (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281–282.)

            Here, the claim that any challenges to the pleading have been waived because the notice of joinder was filed and served 31 days after service of the TAC is unavailing.  A pleading can be challenged at any time.  (See e.g., Stoops v. Abbassi (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 644, 650 [“A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made at any time either prior to the trial or at the trial itself.”].)  Further, Plaintiffs cannot claim any prejudice as Plaintiffs never sought to enter default before the notice of joinder was filed.

            As to California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1320, the notice of joinder specifies the correct hearing and reservation date.  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1320(c).)  Otherwise, the notice of joinder incorporates the demurrer filed by Moving Defendants.  “[A] litigant may incorporate previously filed documents and, where practicable, should file them with the motion.  But a litigant is not required to do so absent a rule precluding incorporation by reference.”  (Roth v. Plikaytis (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 283, 291.)  Plaintiffs do not contend that Moving Defendants’ demurrer – which is incorporated by reference – is insufficient.  Regardless, to the extent the notice of joinder is procedurally improper, the Court is nevertheless inclined to consider it to avoid the unnecessary duplicity of repetitive motions brought on the same identical grounds.

            Accordingly, Defendant Van Nortwick’s notice of joinder is granted.

 

Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy, supra, 18 Cal.3d at p.348; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) 

Here, the Court has previously sustained demurrers on similar grounds, and it does not appear that either Plaintiff can successfully amend the complaint as to the fourth cause of action, sixth cause of action, and seventh causes of action, and it does not appear that Plaintiff Ament can successfully amend the eighth cause of action.  Accordingly, leave to amend is denied as to these claims.

As to the first cause of action against Defendants Martindale, Manion, and Bifulco as the Court previously erroneously indicated that these claims may have been sufficiently alleged, the Court finds it proper to provide Plaintiffs an opportunity to amend this cause of action as to those defendants. 

 

CONCLUSIONS AND ORDER

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer by Defendant Justin White, Applied Approach Services, Inc., Gerry Van Nortwick, Deena Manion, David Bifulco, and Priya Martindale to the Third Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the fourth, sixth, and seventh causes of action by both Plaintiffs.  Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the eighth cause of action as brought by Plaintiff Zachary Ament.

Defendants Deena Manion, David Bifulco, and Priya Martindale’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first cause of action.

Defendants’ demurrer is otherwise OVERRULED.

Plaintiffs are to file an amended complaint within twenty (20) days of notice of this order.

Moving Parties are to give notice and file proof of service of such.
