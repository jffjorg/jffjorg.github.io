Case Number: 19AVCV00574    Hearing Date: February 20, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is an insurance subrogation action. Plaintiff Interinsurance Exchange of the Automobile Club, an interinsurance exchange (“Plaintiff”), alleges that on or about March 28, 2018, (1) its insured, Tina Trevino (“Trevino”), was the owner of a motor vehicle which was insured under a policy of casualty insurance issued by Plaintiff and which policy was in full force and effect which included insurance against the risk of loss or damage to said motor vehicle; and (2) said vehicle was being lawfully operated or parked along and upon certain public streets and highways within the County of Los Angeles Central Judicial District when Defendant Susan Diane Blackburn (“Susan”)[1] negligently operated an uninsured vehicle that was owned by Susan and Defendant Guy Paul Blackburn, III (“Guy” and collectively “Defendants”), causing a collision that resulted in damage to Trevino in the amount of $31,260.68. The Court notes that page 5 of the Complaint indicates that the incident occurred on “E Ave T and Pearblossom highway, Palmdale 93552” despite the allegations that the incident occurred within the County of Los Angeles Central Judicial District.

 

On August 08, 2019, Plaintiff filed its Complaint against Defendants for Subrogation Recovery.

 

On November 26, 2019, Defendants were placed in default.

 

On March 03, 2021, the Court entered a joint and several Judgment by default against Defendants.

On December 10, 2021, Plaintiff filed an Affidavit of Identity and Order.

 

On December 22, 2023, Guy filed an Ex Parte Application to Vacate and Set Aside, Quash, Estoppel.

 

On December 22, 2023, the Court held a hearing on the Ex Parte Application. The Court denied the Ex Parte Application and set the matter for a regularly scheduled hearing.

 

On January 05, 2024, Guy filed a Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Default.

 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Opposition.

 

No Reply has been filed. “. . .[A]ll reply papers [shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party] at least five court days before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) “Section 1013, which extends the time within which a right may be exercised or an act may be done, does not apply to a notice of motion, papers opposing a motion, or reply papers governed by this section.” (Ibid.) The hearing is set for February 20, 2024. February 12, 2024 and February 19, 2024 are court holidays. Accordingly, a Reply was due by February 09, 2024. Should a Reply be filed, it is now untimely.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard to Set Aside/Vacate Default and Default Judgment – “ ‘ A motion for relief under section 473 is addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court and an appellate court will not interfere unless there is a clear showing of an abuse.’ ” (Burnete¿v. La Casa Dana Apartments¿(2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1262, 1266.)¿ “Although a trial court has discretion to vacate the entry of a default or subsequent judgment, this discretion may be exercised only after the party seeking relief has shown that there is a proper ground for relief, and that the party has raised that ground in a procedurally proper manner, within any applicable time limits.” (Cruz v.¿Fagor¿America, Inc.¿(2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Additionally, the California Supreme Court has held the following:¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Section 473 is often applied liberally where the party in default moves promptly to seek relief, and the party opposing the motion will not suffer prejudice if relief is granted. [Citations.] In such situations “very slight evidence will be required to justify a court in setting aside the default.” [Citations.] [¶] Moreover, because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default. [Citations.] Therefore, a trial court order denying relief is scrutinized more carefully than an order permitting trial on the merits. [Citations.]¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

(Elston¿v. City of Turlock¿(1985) 38 Cal.3d 227, 233-34.)¿¿¿¿¿

Discussion

 

Application – The Court takes into consideration the moving papers filed for the Ex Parte Application as well as Plaintiff’s new filing as the hearing set for February 20, 2024 resulted in scheduling the Ex Parte Application as a regularly scheduled hearing.

 

The first set of moving papers (i.e., the Ex Parte Application) states that: (1) in January 2015, Guy released liability as the vehicle and debt was not his at the time of the incident; and (2) Guy presents that the California Department of Motor Vehicles (“DMV”) released his license one month after suspending it due to the fact that the car was not his and he had no responsibility. Guy attaches a transmission from the DMV showing that on February 14, 2022, his driving was suspended due to this action. The second page of the attachment, titled the Order of Reinstatement and dated March 03, 2022, states: “The action taken against your driving privilege under section(s) 16370 of the Vehicle Code (V.C.) has been set aside under section 13551 effective February 18, 2022. You may keep your present license. Department of Motor Vehicles” Guy further attaches the Abstract of Judgment – Civil and Small Claims filing; a second copy of the DMV Order of Reinstatement; and correspondence from GCC, a debt collector, regarding the debt resulting from the default judgment.

 

The second set of moving papers (i.e., January 05, 2024 filing) states that the car at issue is either a 2001 or 2007 Chevrolet Tahoe which has not been in Guy’s possession or registered in Guy’s name since December 2014. Guy states that, to the best of his knowledge, it has not been registered in anyone’s name since it was junked in February 2015 and that he has not had financial responsibility for the vehicle in over nine years. Guy argues that the default judgment is inappropriate as Plaintiff did not provide evidence such as photos, police reports, citations, witness statements, proof of liability, etc. Guy requests the Court vacate and set aside this default judgment. Guy attaches a copy of the Order of Reinstatement, the Minute Order from December 22, 2023, the Abstract of Judgment – Civil and Small Claims, and the Order of Suspension.

 

Plaintiff presents that the moving papers lack supporting documentation to show that the vehicle in question was not registered to Guy and highlights that it received a registration reporting indicating that the vehicle in question was registered to Guy. Further, Plaintiff presents that (1) it complied with the statutory requirement by personally serving Guy on September 08, 2019 at his residence; (2) that mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect is not applicable as Guy was aware of the action against him; (3) that this motion is untimely as it has been brought 33 months after the entry of the default judgment; and (4) alternatively, if this motion is brought under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(d), there has been no showing of clerical error.

 

Guy does not provide which statute he is moving under. However, the Court is aware of the legal standard set under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(a) discusses amendment of the pleadings and is inapplicable.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) provides:

 

The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. However, in the case of a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding determining the ownership or right to possession of real or personal property, without extending the six-month period, when a notice in writing is personally served within the State of California both upon the party against whom the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding has been taken, and upon his or her attorney of record, if any, notifying that party and his or her attorney of record, if any, that the order, judgment, dismissal, or other proceeding was taken against him or her and that any rights the party has to apply for relief under the provisions of Section 473 of the Code of Civil Procedure shall expire 90 days after service of the notice, then the application shall be made within 90 days after service of the notice upon the defaulting party or his or her attorney of record, if any, whichever service shall be later. No affidavit or declaration of merits shall be required of the moving party. Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties. However, this section shall not lengthen the time within which an action shall be brought to trial pursuant to Section 583.310.

 

There has been no showing by Guy of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect as required by Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b). Further, the documents filed by Guy do not include a proposed pleading and do not fall within the six-month timeline provided by statute as the judgment by default was entered approximately two years and 11 months ago on March 03, 2021.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 provides for relief in another form in subdivision (d):

 

The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.

 

The judgment by default was not entered due to a clerical error, but rather because Defendants did not answer the Complaint. “ ‘A judgment or order is said to be void on its face when the invalidity is apparent upon an inspection of the judgment-roll.’ ” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441 [internal citation omitted].) While Guy attempts to provide evidence that the judgement is void in the form of the DMV’s Order of Reinstatement, there is no discussion of the vehicle within the order of the reinstatement. Rather, the Order of Reinstatement directs the parties to Cal. Veh. Code § 13551, which provides:

 

(a) Whenever the department revokes or suspends the privilege of any person to operate a motor vehicle, the revocation or suspension shall apply to all driver's licenses held by that person, and, unless previously surrendered to the court, all of those licenses shall be surrendered to the department, or, pursuant to Section 13388, 23612, or 13382, to a peace officer on behalf of the department. Whenever the department cancels a driver's license, the license shall be surrendered to the department. All suspended licenses shall be retained by the department. The department shall return the license to the licensee, or may issue the person a new license upon the expiration of the period of suspension or revocation, if the person is otherwise eligible for a driver's license.

 

(b) The department shall return the license to the licensee, or may issue the person a new license, whenever the department determines that the grounds for suspension, revocation, or cancellation did not exist at the time the action was taken, if the person is otherwise eligible for a driver's license.

 

The DMV has determined that some basis exists for the determination that “the grounds for suspension, revocation, or cancellation did not exist at the time the action was taken, if the person is otherwise eligible for a driver's license.” (Id. at (b).) However, it is not articulated by the DMV. Additionally, the Affidavit of Identity and Order filed by Plaintiff shows that a 2007 Chevrolet, model unknown, body type model utility was registered to Guy as recent as January 10, 2016. This contrasts with Guy’s moving papers which cite a 2015 date as the last date of registration. There has also been no showing that the vehicle, despite being potentially unregistered and junked could not have been driven or involved in the alleged incident.

 

The Court notes for clarity that the judgment by default was supported by affidavits filed by Trevino, the insured, which indicated under the penalty of perjury that Susan identified herself at the scene and provided the registration for the vehicle and the Custodian of Records for Plaintiff which includes the same vehicle registration as presented in the Affidavit of Identity and Order filed by Plaintiff for proof of ownership of the vehicle and the amount requested by the Complaint.

 

Thus, the judgment by default is not void on its face.

 

Alternatively, to the extent that Guy is moving under the equitable powers of the Court, the California Supreme Court has held:

 

·         Equitable relief may be grounds for vacating a default even if statutory relief is unavailable;

·         While there is strong public policy favoring the grating of relief and allowing the requesting party his or her day in court, there is also strong public policy in favoring the finality of judgments and only in exceptional circumstances should relief be granted; and

·         The test for equitable relief is that the party seeking relief must show (1) a meritorious defense; (2) a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense to the original action; and (3) diligence in seeking to set aside the default once it was discovered.

 

(Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 981-82.)

 

Guy has not shown a meritorious defense, a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense to the original action, and diligence in seeking to set aside the default once it was discovered.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Set Aside/Vacate is DENIED.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Further Responses to Requests for Production (“RFPs”) – “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿For discovery purposes, information is regarded as relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof.” (City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 272, 288.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.310(a).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿2031.310(b)(1).) In¿Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court¿(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there¿is¿“a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v.¿Sup. Ct¿(2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ.¿Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party’s subjective intent to create burden, or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to actually respond. (See¿West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County¿(1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face, no such evidence is necessary. (See¿Obregon v. Superior Court¿(1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Trial courts are vested with “wide discretion” to allow or prohibit discovery. (Williams v. Superior Court¿(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 540.)¿¿¿¿

The Court applies the plain meaning of the words objected to by Defendant. Regarding privilege, “[i]n general, when a party asserts the attorney-client privilege, that party has the burden of showing the preliminary facts necessary to support the privilege… After this burden is met, or where there is no dispute concerning the preliminary facts, the burden shifts to the party opposing the privilege to show either the claimed privilege does not apply, an exception exists, or there has been an express or implied waiver. [Citation].” (Venture Law Group v. Superior Court (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 96, 102 [internal citations omitted].).” Defendant has failed to meet its initial burden of demonstrating that the privilege of attorney-client relationship applies. Moreover, the work-product doctrine is divided into two types – absolute privilege and qualified protection – and work-product doctrine designed to protect writings that reflect “an attorney's impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories.” (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2018.030.) Work-product privilege does not appear to be applicable to a request seeking published documents (i.e., Warranty Policy and Procedure Manuals) that have been distributed by Defendant. (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 2018.030.) Likewise, such documents are published to the public and has been distributed, at a minimum, to Defendant’s authorized repair facilities and its workers and does not appear to fall under confidential communication between Defendant and its attorney. (See Cal. Evid. Code § 954.) As to burdensome and oppressive, discovery burden is “undue” only if the inconvenience and expense of responding clearly outweighs the benefits likely to be obtained if the interrogatories are answered. (Weil & Brown, CPG: Civ. Proc. Before Trial (TRG 2021) §8:1475.13, citing Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2031.210(d).) The objecting party is required to provide evidence to support its claim of undue or excessive burden that shows the amount of work required, otherwise, the trial court has nothing “upon which to base a comparative judgment that any responsive burden would be undue or excessive, relative to the likelihood of admissible evidence being discovered.” (Id. [citing Williams v. Sup. Ct. (Marshalls of CA, LLC) (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549-550].) Further, as held in West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, reiterated in Mead Reinsurance Co. v. Superior Court (1986) 188 Cal.App.3d 313, 320-21, and incorporated into CA Pretrial Civil Procedure: The Wagstaffe Group § 42-III, “[oppression] must not be equated with burden [all discovery imposes some burden on the opposition]. . . . to support an objection of oppression there must be some showing . . . that the ultimate effect of the burden is incommensurate with the result sought.” Defendant has not made no such showing. Additionally, the request at hand is distinct from Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216 (“Calcor Space Facility”) The requests in Calcor Space Facility read: “Request No. 1. [P] The Gun Mounts, including but not limited to documents relating to the design, modification, engineering manufacture, testing, rejection, revision, modification, or acceptance of the Gun Mounts or any subassemblies or components manufactured in connection with the Gun Mount Project. [P] Request No. 2. [P] All purchase orders, amendments to purchase orders, engineering change orders, drawings, specifications, invoices, rejection reports, accident reports and Material Review Board ('MRB') authorization reports relating to the Gun Mounts or the Gun Mount Project. [P] Request No. 3. [P] All requests for quotation ('RFQ') or requests for proposal ('RFP') in connection with the Gun Mounts or the Gun Mount Project. [P] Request No. 4. [P] All bid materials, including but not limited to cost estimates, labor estimates, and production time estimates prepared for and submitted by Calcor in connection with the Gun Mount Project.” (Calcor Space Facility, supra, 53 Cal.App.4th at 219-220.) The Calcor Space Facility Court noted each of these 32 "requests" was expanded by 6 pages of definitions and instructions,” and, although facially detailed and particularized, the demand, ordered Calcor to “produce everything in its possession which has anything to do with gun mounts (including the gun mount assemblies themselves).” (Id. at 220.) This, on its face, is distinguished from documents that have been published and disseminated by Defendant. Further, Calcor did not discuss RFPS, but rather subpoenas to non-parties under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2020(d), now Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2020.010 – 2020.030. To the extent that this objection is to protect trade secrets and/or proprietary information, whether information constitutes a trade secret is a question of fact. (Amgen, Inc. v. California Correctional Health Care Services (2020) 47 Cal.App.5th 716, 733.) The party claiming the trade secret privilege has the burden of establishing that the trade secret exists. (Ibid.) It must establish that the information holds independent economic value because it is not generally known and is subject to efforts to maintain its secrecy. (Ibid.) If the party meets this burden, then the party seeking discovery of the trade secret must make a prima facie, particularized showing that the information sought is relevant and necessary to proof of a material element of one or more causes of action presented in the case and that it is reasonable to conclude that the information is essential to a fair resolution of the lawsuit. (Bridgestone/Firestone, Inc. v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1384, 1393.) It is then up to the court to balance the interests of both sides and consider a protective order for the discovery sought. (Ibid.) Defendant has not met its burden to establish that the trade secret exits.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Interrogatories – Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290 provides that a party may bring a motion to compel responses to interrogatories, where the responding party failed to serve a timely response. Unless otherwise agreed, the responding party is required to serve response within 30 days after service of the discovery demand for production documents. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290(b).) If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, the party “waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290(a).) The requesting party need not demonstrate “good cause to compel responses, nor that it satisfied the “meet and confer” requirement. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 143 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) 

 

Responses to interrogatories are due within 30 days after service of interrogatories. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.260.) “The party propounding interrogatories and the responding party may agree to extend the time for service of a response to a set of interrogatories, or to particular interrogatories in a set, to a date beyond that provided in Section 2030.260.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.270(a).) While this agreement may be informal, it must be confirmed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.270(b).)  

 

---- 

 

Standard for Compelling Responses¿to Requests for¿Production¿(“RFPs”)¿–¿Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 provides that a party may bring a motion to compel responses to a request for production of documents, where the responding party failed to serve a timely response. Unless otherwise agreed, the responding party is required to serve response within 30 days after service of the discovery demand for production documents. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).) If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, the party “waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Defendant propounded Plaintiff with its Request for Responses to Form Interrogatories, (Set One). (See id. at ¶ 4 and Exh. B.) As such, Plaintiff’s new deadline was April 27, 2023. Thus, the agreed upon deadline was June 06, 2023. Despite this, the office representing Plaintiff requested further extensions which Defendant did not agree to. (See id. at ¶¶ 8-9 and Exhs. D and E.) (See id. at ¶ 12 and Exh. H.) However, no supplemental responses were received by the deadline and, to date, Defendant has not received any supplemental discovery. (See id. at ¶¶ 12-15.)

 

It appears what Defendant seeks is not a motion to compel, but rather a motion to compel further as Defendant has resented (1) responses were received; and (2) the objections given by Plaintiff in his responses are without merit. “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300.) The responses contain only objections and are signed by Plaintiff’s then counsel, Jeffrey M. Schwartz (“Schwartz”). (Motion to Compel Form Interrogatory Responses, Decl. of Jessie A. Nudelman, ¶ 10 and Exh. F.) The Proof of Service of Plaintiff’s responses indicates the responses were served on July 05, 2023. (Ibid.) Defendant did not move to compel further until September 18, 2023, over two months since Plaintiff’s responses were received (75 days). (See 09/28/2023 Notice of IDC.) Taking into consideration the agreement between the parties in which supplemental responses were due on July 14, 2023, the date of the Notice of the IDC, September 18, 2023, is still over two months (66 days) from the date decided upon by counsels. Defendant has not provided argument for why the 45-day statutory deadline should not apply. Defendant has waived its right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.

 

The Court notes that Defendant has also failed to adhere to the separate statement required for Motions to Compel Further. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345; see also Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(a)(2) [discussing separate statement requirement].)

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c) provides: “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.” As with the interrogatories, (1) the response is verified as it contains only objections and then-attorney Schwartz signed the responses (see Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Demand for Inspection and Production of Documents (Set One), Decl. Defendant has not provided argument for why the 45-day statutory deadline should not apply. Defendant has waived its right to compel a further response to RFPs.

 

The Court notes that Defendant has also failed to adhere to the separate statement required for Motions to Compel Further. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345; see also Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(3) [discussing separate statement requirement].)

Regarding RFPs, Cal. Code Civ. Defendant may have brought the motions from a place of good will; however, Defendant has presented the motions as motions to compel when they are motions to compel further. Standard for Approving Minor’s Compromises – Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim or that of a person lacking the capacity to make decisions.  (Prob. An order for deposit of funds of a minor or person lacking decision-making capacity and a petition for the withdrawal of such funds must comply with California Rules of Court Rules 7.953 and 7.954.  (Cal. Discussion

 

Judicial Notice – Plaintiff requests judicial notice only of certain parts of a whole document (i.e. redaction, certain numbers, location of space, etc.). The Court will take judicial notice of the documents in which the requests are located as they are part of this Court’s records. (Cal. Evid. Code § 452.) The Court will not view the documents piece meal. This statement applies to Plaintiff’s requests regarding Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice Exhibits 1, 3, 14, 15, 16, and 17; Plaintiff’s document in related case 23AVUD0441.

 

As to the request for judicial notice regarding Fontenot v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 256, (“Fontenot”) the request is DENIED. This is case law and not judicially noticeable under Cal. Evid. Code § 452. Further, Fontenot has been overruled in part by Yvanova v. New Century Mortgage Corp. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 919 [questioned by Watson v. Bank of Am., N.A. (S.D.Cal. Nov. 7, 2016, No. 16cv513-GPC(MDD)) 2016 U.S.Dist.LEXIS 154375 as to application to pre-foreclosure sales].)  

 

As to the request for the Sallie Mae Selling Guide regarding MERS, judicial notice is GRANTED  under Cal. Evid. Code § 452(h).

 

Application – The Court must address several initial matters.

 

First, Plaintiff states he seeks an order setting aside and vacating the December 12, 2023 judgment. (Motion, 3:3-9.) The Court’s records reflect that no judgment was entered on December 12, 2023. Rather, there is (1) an order on December 12, 2023 sustaining Newrez and BNY Mellon’s Demurrer, and (2) a judgment entered on January 02, 2024. As Plaintiff has provided the date of the order and described the judgment as one sustaining Newrez and BNY Mellon’s Demurrer, the Court will read this motion as directed to the December 12, 2023 order.

 

Next, Plaintiff states that he is moving under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(d) and the Court’s inherent powers. Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(d) reads: “The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” The order sustaining Newrez and BNY Mellon’s Demurrer was not made as a clerical error. “ ‘A judgment or order is said to be void on its face when the invalidity is apparent upon an inspection of the judgment-roll.’ ” (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441 [internal citation omitted].)

 

As to any issue with notice stemming from these errors, Newrez and BNY Mellon have filed their Opposition. An opposition on the merits is a waiver of any defects or irregularities in the notice of the motion. (See Tate v. Superior Court (1975) 45 Cal. App. 3d 925, 930; Eliceche v. Federal Land Bank Assn. (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 1349, 1375; Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1288-89.)

 

Plaintiff relies on the documents by Newrez and BNY Mellon provided in the Demurrer to argue manifest fraud due to redactions. Plaintiff cites to Ross v. Murphy (1952) 113 Cal.App.2d 453 (“Ross”) to provide a partial quote of Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473. Plaintiff argues that fraud exists because the serialized loan numbers stemming from Selling Guide regarding MERS were redacted and, thus, false. Plaintiff argues that the loan number on the deed of trust provided is 1470002009 while the one Newrez and BNY Mellon foreclosed on is 0555856819. Plaintiff further argues that MERS did not have a valid assignment, citing Fontenot. Plaintiff presents that (1) the buyer did not cancel and return the note which is evidence that Newrez and BNY Mellon did not foreclose against the note, and (2) res judicata does not apply as the previous cases were filed before the nonjudicial foreclosure.

 

Newrez and BNY Mellon present: (1) there is no grounds to vacate under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(d) as Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §473(d) does not provide authority to set aside a judgment that is void on its face as Plaintiff claims; (2) there are no grounds to set aside the order on the demurrer as a party moving to set aside fraud must establish the elements of fraud as well as establish three elements – (1) a meritorious defense; (2) a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense in the first place; and (3) diligence in seeking to set aside the default judgment once discovered – and Plaintiff has not done so. Newrez and BNY Mellon further present that Rodriguez v. Cho (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 742 (“Rodriguez”) holds that (1) a party seeking equitable relief on the grounds of extrinsic fraud or mistake must show a meritorious defense, a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense in the first place, and diligence in seeking to set aside the default judgment once discovered; and (2) fraud is "intrinsic," and not a valid ground for setting aside an order, when the moving party was given notice of the action and had an opportunity to present its case and to protect itself from any mistake or fraud by the opposing party, but unreasonably neglected to do so.

 

Plaintiff’s citation to Ross is inapposite. Ross cites to Provencher v. City of Los Angeles (1935) 10 Cal.App.2d 730, 733 where the plaintiffs had dismissed the action themselves and the trial court was without power to render a judgment, making the judgment entered after dismissal void on its face pursuant to Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(d). Ross used this citation to answer whether a judge of one superior court has the power to vacate a void order of another judge of the superior court. (Ross, supra, 113 Cal.App.2d at 455.) Ross and the source of its citation are distinguished from the action at hand. Plaintiff has not presented any argument for invalidity apparent upon an inspection the judgment-roll in his moving papers. The Court notes that Plaintiff’s Reply attempts to argue that the judgement in this case is void on its face; however, Plaintiff’s argument simply (1) cites to new cases (i.e., cases not presented in the moving or opposing papers) discussing that a judgment is void on its face when invalidity appears upon inspection of the judgment-roll; (2) a bullet pointed list of what a judgment roll consists of under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 670(a); and (3) a statement that reads: “Here, inspection of the judgment-roll without question there is grounds for relief and the Court has the power to grant the relief requested.” (Reply 3:1-19.) Plaintiff does not present what in the judgment-roll would make the judgment void on its face in his Reply. The order issued for the Demurrer is not void on its face as it was based upon and supported by arguments from Newrez, BNY Mellon, and Plaintiff and their corresponding filings. Therefore, Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(d) does not apply.

 

As to equitable powers of the court, Newrez and BNY Mellon’s cited case, Rodriguez, is binding on this Court. Rodriguez provides, in relevant part:

 

‘The court may grant relief under its inherent equity power if, because of the fraud of his opponent, the aggrieved party was prevented from presenting his claim or defense to the court. [Citations.] “Two essential conditions are found in a classic case in equity which seeks to set aside a judgment: first, the judgment is one entered against a party by default under circumstances which prevented him from presenting his case; second, these circumstances result from extrinsic fraud practiced by the other party or his attorney.”’” (Id. at p. 314.) “‘Fraud is intrinsic and not a valid ground for setting aside a judgment when the party has been given notice of the action and has had an opportunity to present his case and to protect himself from any mistake or fraud of his adversary, but has unreasonably neglected to do so.’” (Id. at p. 315.)

 

Additionally, the party seeking equitable relief on the grounds of extrinsic fraud or mistake must show three elements: (1) a meritorious defense; (2) a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense in the first place; and (3) diligence in seeking to set aside the default judgment once discovered. (Gibble v. Car-Lene Research, Inc., supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at p. 315.)

 

[. . .]

 

Moreover, even if evidence of extrinsic fraud existed, Cho did not establish he had a meritorious defense—a necessary element for equitable relief. (Gibble v. Car-Lene Research, Inc., supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at p. 315.) He attached a proposed answer to his motion reciting a kitchen sink full of affirmative defenses, but he did not attempt to demonstrate any of these defenses had merit by, for example, explaining the underlying facts and applying the law to them. On appeal, he contends for the first time that he has a meritorious defense because Rodriguez had no evidence that he, as opposed to Reliable, was her employer. This contention is not well taken. Rodriguez's prove-up package contained court documents from a small claims court action Cho filed against her in which he identified himself as “Nam Min Cho DBA Reliable Building Maintenance Company,” and he referred to Reliable as “plaintiffs [sic] company.” She further presented evidence of a payroll document and other correspondence from Reliable showing she was an employee, besides her own sworn declaration. Cho had the burden of proving he had a meritorious defense, and he did not carry that burden. (Sakaguchi v. Sakaguchi (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 852, 862 [92 Cal. Rptr. 3d 717].)

 

(Rodriguez, supra, 236 Cal.App.4th at 749-52.)

 

Though slightly different (i.e., this action does not concern default), the basic premise of Rodriguez applies. Plaintiff was not prevented from presenting his claim or defense to the Court. Rather, Plaintiff could have brought up the arguments that he brings for this motion in either his Opposition to the Demurrer or during oral argument at the hearing. Plaintiff did not. Plaintiff has not a provided a satisfactory excuse for not presenting a defense in the first place.

 

Further, as to res judicata, “ ‘Res judicata’ describes the preclusive effect of a final judgment on the merits. Res judicata, or claim preclusion, prevents relitigation of the same cause of action in a second suit between the same parties or parties in privity with them. Collateral estoppel, or issue preclusion, ‘precludes relitigation of issues argued and decided in prior proceedings.’ (Lucido v. Superior Court (1990) 51 Cal.3d 335, 341 [272 Cal. Rptr. 767, 795 P.2d 1223].) Under the doctrine of res judicata, if a plaintiff prevails in an action, the cause is merged into the judgment and may not be asserted in a subsequent lawsuit; a judgment for the defendant serves as a bar to further litigation of the same cause of action.” (Mycogen Corp. v. Monsanto Co. (2002) 28 Cal.4th 888, 896-97.) The Court takes judicial notice of its Order After Hearing/Statement of Decision, filed on December 12, 2023 under Cal. Evid. Code § 452 as this filing is part of the Court’s record. The Court provides this tentative to afford each party reasonable opportunity to meet such information before judicial notice of the matter may be taken. (See Cal. Evid. Code § 455.) The Court determined that as (1) a final decision was rendered (2) upon the same primary rights/claims (3) with parties BNY Mellon, sued directly in previous cases, and Newrez, as the party in privity with then-servicer Bank of American, N.A. (See 12/12/2023 Order After Hearing/Statement of Decision.) Case law holds the doctrine of res judicata applies to a new theory of wrongful foreclosure premised on the same primary right as past litigation that had resulted in final judgments allowing foreclosure proceedings to go forward. (Gilles v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 907 [generally].) The timing of the filing does not bar the application of res judicata.

 

With regards to the Court’s inherent powers, Plaintiff’s Reply contains arguments that: (1) courts have inherent power at law to set aside a void judgment on the motion of a party, whether it is void on its face; and (2) a finding that the fraud worked upon a court was intrinsic rather than extrinsic is immaterial upon a motion to vacate made within six months. These are arguments do not change the Court’s opinion as the item at issue is an order, not a judgment; the order on the Demurrer is not void as discussed ante; and Plaintiff has not provided argument that overcomes the bar due to res judicata.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate/Set Aside is DENIED.

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Rodney Rouzan’s Motion to Vacate/Set Aside Judgment is DENIED.

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿While under California law¿leave¿to amend¿is liberally granted, “leave¿to amend¿should not be granted¿where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.”¿(Vaillette¿v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co.¿(1993),¿18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿“A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿¿¿

 

The law favors substance over form. (See Cal. Civ. Code § 3528.)

 

While Plaintiff has titled his FAC a complaint “for violation of civil rights,” the FAC includes allegations of violations of California law. Due to the sham pleading doctrine and Plaintiff’s current allegations, there is no viable way to amend the FAC with regards to the discrimination allegation as all persons could not use the restroom at the Arco AM/PM; however, there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment with regards to the alleged violation of California law.

 

Accordingly, the Court allows leave to amend.

Discussion –

 

a.      First Cause of Action (Medical Negligence)

 

AVHD argues that it did not have a duty to prevent Decedent’s suicide as (1) a hospital’s duty to depends on whether a hospital has accepted the responsibility to care for and attend to the needs of the suicidal patient, and the physicians must be aware of the patient’s unstable condition; and (2) the SAC is devoid of allegations that Decedent was admitted into AVHD’s hospital, that a hospital-patient relationship existed, that care had been transferred to AVHD, and that Decedent’s suicide was foreseeable. AVHD presents that, assuming duty was adequately alleged, the SAC lacks allegations of conduct that would fall below the standard of care and that AVHD had some indication that Decedent would harm himself. AVHD also notes that the Complaint is uncertain because it is unclear as to which allegations pertain to which defendant. Finally, AVHD presents that it has statutory immunity under Cal. Gov. Code § 855.6.

 

Plaintiff argues that the scope of medical negligence is more general that AVHD’s characterization. Plaintiff presents California case precedent holds that hospitals have a duty to: (1) use reasonable care and diligence not only in operating upon and treating but also in safeguarding him, and such care and diligence is measured by the capacity of the patient to care for himself (citing to Valentin v. La Societe Francaise de Bienfaisance Mutuelle (1946) 76 Cal.App.2d 1, 4) and (2) to observe and know the condition of a patient as a hospital’s business is caring for patients and a hospital’s conduct must be in accordance with that of a person of ordinary prudence under the circumstances, a vital part of those circumstances being the illness of the patient and incidents thereof (citing to Rice v. California Lutheran Hospital (l945) 27 Cal.2d 296, 302.) Plaintiff argues that a liberal construction of the SAC sufficiently pleads that Decedent was transported to AVHD’s hospital and was being admitted as Hall Ambulance’s employees were completing the intake process. Plaintiff further argues that (1) the demurrer raises factual contentions that should go to the jury; (2) Cal. Gov. Code §855.6 does not apply as commentary states it is for public health examinations; (3) Cal. Gov. Code § 955.8 has exceptions and this action falls under an exception.

 

AVHD, in its Reply, argues that the SAC does not contain any allegations that AVHD undertook treatment of Decedent, there is no support for the duty cited by Plaintiff within the SAC, Plaintiff downplays the distinction between intake and admission, and Plaintiff concedes that only intake was occurring at page 5 lines 9 through 12 of the Opposition. Thus, the SAC fails to adequately allege that AVHD had a duty to Decedent. AVHD reiterates that it has governmental immunity under Cal. Gov. Code §§855.6 and 855.8.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.” (Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) Whether a duty of care exists is a question of law for the court. (Jones v. Grewe (1987), 189 Cal.App.3d 950, 954 [citing Wilson v. All Service Ins. Corp. (1979) 91 Cal.App.3d 793, 796 and Raymond v. Paradise Unified School Dist. (1963), 218 Cal. App. 2d 1, 8-9].)  

 

The SAC clearly outlines what alleged actions AVHD undertook that Plaintiff believes constitutes a breach of duty in the Factual Allegations section. However, in the First Cause of Action, it appears that all allegations are directed to both AVHD and Hall Ambulance. It is unclear which alleged actions, or lack thereof, pertain to which defendant. The two forms of duty that Plaintiff cites are not integrated within the SAC. The issue of intake versus admission, which establishes a hospital-patient relationship is a question of fact and, as such, would be in the purview of the jury. However, this a demurrer and the determination of triable issues of material fact are not at issue. The Court directs the parties to its legal standard, ante, and highlights that a demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. Assuming duty was adequately pled and a liberal construction of Paragraph 34 would encompass admission, statutory immunity remains a barrier to Plaintiff’s claim. Cal. Gov. Code § 855.6 provides:

 

Except for an examination or diagnosis for the purpose of treatment, neither a public entity nor a public employee acting within the scope of his employment is liable for injury caused by the failure to make a physical or mental examination, or to make an adequate physical or mental examination, of any person for the purpose of determining whether such person has a disease or physical or mental condition that would constitute a hazard to the health or safety of himself or others.

 

The SAC alleges that the lack of examination occurred, lack of assessment of Decedent’s psychological state, and a failure to search Decedent during the intake and admission process, not for determining a course of treatment. (See SAC ¶¶ 34, 48).

 

Plaintiff focuses on only one segment of the Law Revision Commission Comments in her argument; however; the full commentary reads:

 

This section declares an immunity that has been recognized by the New York courts in the absence of statute. It grants an immunity for failure to perform adequately public health examinations, such as public tuberculosis examinations, physical examinations to determine the qualifications of boxers and other athletes, and eye examinations for vehicle operator applicants. It does not apply to examinations for the purpose of treatment such as are made in doctors’ offices and public hospitals. In those situations, the ordinary rules of liability would apply.

 

The immunity provided by this section relates only to failure to make any examination or, if an examination is made, to the “adequacy” of the examination; the section does not provide immunity, for example, where a public employee negligently injures a person while making an examination.

 

(Cal. Gov. Code § 855.6 (Deering, Lexis Advance through the 2023 Extra Session Ch 1, 2023 Regular Session Ch. 890) [emphasis added].)

 

Further, binding case precedent has discussed the legislative intent behind the statute. Kravitz v. State of California (1970) 8 Cal.App.3d 301, 306-07 (“Kravitz”) holds that Cal. Gov Code § 855.6 was designed to cover a situation in which, as a result of a faulty diagnosis, a mentally-defective person was either not placed in custody or was released therefrom and thereafter causes harm to another or to himself. (Kravitz, supra, 8 Cal.App.3d at 306-07.) Thus, in Kravitz, the statute created immunity for the state and members of the medical staffs of two state hospitals named as defendants in a wrongful death action brought by the parents of the victim of a deadly assault by a man released by court order under former Cal. Pen. Code § 1026a, now Cal. Pen. Code § 1026.2 (which governed the release of criminals committed for insanity), on the basis of defendants’ medical opinions that he had recovered his sanity. (Id. [generally].) Accordingly, the immunity would apply to this action as the SAC alleges that “[AVHD] failed to conduct an assessment of Mr. Bardin’s mental state. . .As a result of the negligence of DEFENDANTS, Mr. Bardin drew a pistol from his backpack and shot himself in the chest, resulting in his death.” (SAC ¶¶ 35-36, 52.)

 

Cal. Gov. Code § 855.8 provides:

 

(a) Neither a public entity nor a public employee acting within the scope of his employment is liable for injury resulting from diagnosing or failing to diagnose that a person is afflicted with mental illness or addiction or from failing to prescribe for mental illness or addiction.

(b) A public employee acting within the scope of his employment is not liable for administering with due care the treatment prescribed for mental illness or addiction.

(c) Nothing in this section exonerates a public employee who has undertaken to prescribe for mental illness or addiction from liability for injury proximately caused by his negligence or by his wrongful act in so prescribing.

(d) Nothing in this section exonerates a public employee from liability for injury proximately caused by his negligent or wrongful act or omission in administering any treatment prescribed for mental illness or addiction.

 

Alternatively, had the intake and admission encompassed diagnosis Decedent, Cal. Gov. Code § 855.8 applies. The SAC makes clear that (1) no public employee had undertaken to prescribe for mental illness, and (2) no public employee had administered any treatment prescribed for mental illness as the events unfolded during the intake and admission process (i.e., when Decedent was being transferred from Hall Ambulance’s care to AVHD’s hospital).

 

Accordingly, AVHD’s Demurrer to the First Cause of Action (Medical Negligence) is SUSTAINED.

Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81 (“Sababin”) a jury could find that AVHD’s conduct, ignoring prior statements of Decedent’s derangement and failing to implement safety measures for a person suffering a psychotic episode, shows deliberate disregard of the high degree of probability that Decedent would suffer injury and/or was reckless.

 

In its Reply, AVHD argues that the SAC does not contain any allegations that AVHD undertook treatment of Decedent, there is no support for the duty cited by Plaintiff within the SAC, Plaintiff downplays the distinction between intake and admission, and Plaintiff concedes that only intake was occurring at page 5 lines 9 through 12 of the Opposition. AVHD further argues that Sababin is readily distinguishable as the decedent in Sababin was admitted to Covina Care Center; Covina Care Cetner developed a care plan; and, regarding a motion for summary adjudication, the appellate court held that a trier of fact must determine whether there is a significant pattern of withholding portions or types of care. AVHD emphasizes that there cannot be a significant pattern of withholding care as Decedent was not admitted to AVHD at the time he shot himself.

 

Statutory interpretation is a matter of law for the Court to decide. (See Christensen v. Lightbourne (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1251 [“'On questions of law arising in mandate proceedings, [the court] exercise[s] independent judgment.' …. Interpretation of a statute or regulation is a question of law.”]; In re J. W. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 200, 209 [“If there is no ambiguity in the language of the statute, ‘then the Legislature is presumed to have meant what it said, and the plain meaning of the language governs.’ [Citation.] ‘Where the statute is clear, courts will not “interpret away clear language in favor of an ambiguity that does not exist.” [Citation.]’ [Citation.]”]; Hernandez v. Modesto Portuguese Pentecost Assn. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1274, 1280 [“Because liability in this case turns upon a question of statutory interpretation, it is a question of law for the court to decide in a summary judgment motion.”].)

 

Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23 provides:

 

(a) “Dependent adult” means a person, regardless of whether the person lives independently, between the ages of 18 and 64 years who resides in this state and who has physical or mental limitations that restrict his or her ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his or her rights, including, but not limited to, persons who have physical or developmental disabilities, or whose physical or mental abilities have diminished because of age.

 

(b) “Dependent adult” includes any person between the ages of 18 and 64 years who is admitted as an inpatient to a 24-hour health facility, as defined in Sections 1250, 1250.2, and 1250.3 of the Health and Safety Code.

 

The Complaint alleges that Decedent’s symptoms were connected to fentanyl use, not a physical or mental limitation that restricted his ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his rights. (See SAC ¶¶ 31.) As such, Decedent does not fall under Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(a). The following definitions are applicable to Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(b) and this action:

 

·         “ ‘General acute care hospital’ means a health facility having a duly constituted governing body with overall administrative and professional responsibility and an organized medical staff that provides 24-hour inpatient care, including the following basic services: medical, nursing, surgical, anesthesia, laboratory, radiology, pharmacy, and dietary services. A general acute care hospital may include more than one physical plant maintained and operated on separate premises as provided in Section 1250.8. A general acute care hospital that exclusively provides acute medical rehabilitation center services, including at least physical therapy, occupational therapy, and speech therapy, may provide for the required surgical and anesthesia services through a contract with another acute care hospital. In addition, a general acute care hospital that, on July 1, 1983, provided required surgical and anesthesia services through a contract or agreement with another acute care hospital may continue to provide these surgical and anesthesia services through a contract or agreement with an acute care hospital. The general acute care hospital operated by the State Department of Developmental Services at Agnews Developmental Center may, until June 30, 2007, provide surgery and anesthesia services through a contract or agreement with another acute care hospital. Notwithstanding the requirements of this subdivision, a general acute care hospital operated by the Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation or the Department of Veterans Affairs may provide surgery and anesthesia services during normal weekday working hours, and not provide these services during other hours of the weekday or on weekends or holidays, if the general acute care hospital otherwise meets the requirements of this section. . .” (Cal. Health & Saf. Code § 1250(a).)

·         "Inpatient" means a person who has been admitted to a hospital, skilled nursing facility, or intermediate care facility for bed occupancy for purposes of receiving inpatient services. A person is considered an inpatient when he is formally admitted as an inpatient with the expectation that he will remain at least overnight and occupy a bed, even though it later develops that he can be discharged or that he is transferred to another facility and does not actually use a bed overnight. (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 22, § 51108 (Lexis Advance through Register 2024, No. 3, January 19, 2024).)

 

Assuming a liberal construction of Paragraph 34 would encompass admission, the SAC does not allow for a reasonable inference that Decedent was admitted “with the expectation that he will remain at least overnight and occupy a bed, even though it later develops that he can be discharged or that he is transferred to another facility and does not actually use a bed overnight.” (Id.)

 

Under the facts alleged, Decedent does not qualify as a dependent adult under Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23.

 

Accordingly, AVHD’s Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse) is SUSTAINED.

 

c.       Fourth Cause of Action (Survivor Action)

 

AVHD presents that the survival statutes do not create a cause of action, but rather prevent the abatement of a decedent’s cause of action and provide for the enforcement of a cause

of action by the decedent’s personal representative or successor in interest, citing San Diego Gas & Elec. Co. v. Superior Court (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1545, 1553. AVHD argues that the SAC fails as a whole and Plaintiff has not filed the requisite affidavit or declaration under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 377.32.

Plaintiff argues that she has provided such a declaration and that her causes of action are sufficiently pled.

 

The Reply does not appear to have a direct argument tied to the Fourth Cause of Action (Survivor Action), but rather addresses the SAC as a whole.

 

In a survival action, the damages recoverable by a decedent's representative or successor in interest on the decedent's Cause of Action are limited to the loss or damage that the decedent sustained or incurred before death. Code of Civil Procedure Section 377.34. “The survival statutes do not create a cause of action. Rather, ‘[t]hey merely prevent the abatement of the cause of action of the injured person, and provide for its enforcement by or against the personal representative of the deceased.’ (Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1264.)

 

The Court directs the parties to the Request for Judicial Notice section, ante. Plaintiff’s filed declaration does not adhere to the requirements set out in Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 377.32. Further, this cause of action is derivative of the Medical Negligence, Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse, and Wrongful Death actions which fell to demurrer. (See analysis of First and Third Causes of Action, ante, and analysis of Fifth Cause of Action, infra.)

 

Accordingly, AVHD’s Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED.

 

d.      Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death)

 

AVHD presents that a wrongful death action based on negligence must contain allegations as to all elements of actionable negligence and that the alleged wrongful act was a contributing factor to a decedent’s death. AVHD argues that the Complaint: (1) does not state any facts that suggest a duty of care was owed to Decedent by AVHD, and (2) that any act or admission directly attributable to AVHD would constitute a wrongful act.

 

Plaintiff applies her argument for the Survivor Claim of Action as to the Wrongful Death action.

 

The Reply does not appear to have a direct argument tied to the Fourth Cause of Action (Survivor Action), but rather addresses the SAC as a whole.

 

The elements of the cause of action for wrongful death are the tort (negligence or other wrongful act), the resulting death, and the damages, consisting of the pecuniary loss suffered by the heirs.” (Lattimore v. Dickey, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) “In any action for wrongful death resulting from negligence, the complaint must contain allegations as to all the elements of actionable negligence.” (Novak v. Continental Tire North America (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 189, 195.)

 

The SAC bases the Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death) on the negligence claim. (See SAC ¶ 83.) The SAC fails to contain allegations as to all the elements of actionable negligence. (See ante.)

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death) is SUSTAINED.

 

e.       Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿While under California law¿leave¿to amend¿is liberally granted, “leave¿to amend¿should not be granted¿where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.”¿(Vaillette¿v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co.¿(1993),¿18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿“A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿¿¿

 

The First Cause of Action (Medical Negligence) cannot be amended with a reasonable possibility of successful amendment as the statute provides AVHD with immunity.

 

It is unclear whether Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse) may be rectified. The Court has held that, the SAC, currently, does not allow for a reasonable inference that Decedent was admitted as an inpatient. (See ante.) To err on the side of caution, the Court allows amendment.

 

As to the Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death), it is currently based on the Negligence claim; however, a wrongful death cause of action may be based on a wrongful act. Due to the ability to amend the Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse), the Court allows leave to amend for the Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death).

 

The Fourth Cause of Action (Survival Action) is a derivative claim. As two causes of action may be amended, so too may the derivative claim be amended.

 

Accordingly, leave to amend is given as to the Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse), Fourth Cause of Action (Survival Action), and Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death).

 

ii.      Hall Ambulance’s Demurrer

 

a.      First Cause of Action (Medical Negligence)

 

Hall Ambulance presents that the existence of duty is to be determined by the Court and one of the Rowland [v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108] factors is foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff and, here, it was entirely unforeseeable that Decedent would pull a gun out of his backpack and shot himself. Hall Ambulance contends that the SAC’s use of alleging that Decedent was deranged and that Decedent’s friend claimed Decedent had recently taken fentanyl which messed with his head is not enough to make it foreseeable to Hall Ambulance that Decedent had a weapon or that he was inclined to harm himself or others with it. Hall ambulance presents that a duty cannot be imposed on every paramedic and emergency medical technician (“EMT”) in light of the fact that paramedics and EMTs are tasked with responding promptly to medical emergencies and to search the contents of an individual’s personal belongings would violate privacy rights. Hall Ambulance concedes that the SAC contains a statement regarding duty in Paragraph 37, but argues that this statement is conclusory as Plaintiff has not alleged any facts to support the contention that Hall Ambulance reasonably anticipated that Decedent had a firearm. Hall Ambulance further argues that there is an issue with causation as Decedent’s actions are considered an unforeseeable intervening act that severs the chain of causation between any alleged negligence on behalf of Hall Ambulance and Decedent’s self-inflicted injuries and subsequent death.

 

Plaintiff emphasizes that she is only required to set forth essential facts of her case with particularity sufficient to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source, and extent of a cause of action. Plaintiff argues the Hall Ambulance erroneously misconstrues her burden, that she has supported her allegation of duty with the allegations regarding Decedent’s psychotic episode and recent drug use, and whether the incident was foreseeable is a question of fact that must be left to the jury to decide. Plaintiff further emphasizes that Hall Ambulance failed to assess Decedent’s mental state after being notified that he was “deranged” and had possible homicidal or suicidal thoughts. Plaintiff presents that Hall Ambulance should have conducted primary and secondary assessments of Decedent and/or placed him on a 51/50 hold and whether Decedent followed the applicable standard of care and whether Decedent was experiencing homicidal or suicidal thoughts are questions for the jury.

 

The Court does not consider the arguments regarding whether there are triable issues of fact that should be left to the jury and Plaintiff believes Hall Ambulance should have done as it they are not within the scope of this Demurrer. Likewise, the application of factors from Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 and a determination of foreseeability is inappropriate as the issue on demurrer is whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿The Court directs the parties to applicable legal standard, ante.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.” (Lattimore, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) Whether a duty of care exists is a question of law for the court. (Jones v. Grewe (1987), 189 Cal.App.3d 950, 954 [citing Wilson v. All Service Ins. Corp. (1979) 91 Cal.App.3d 793, 796 and Raymond v. Paradise Unified School Dist. (1963), 218 Cal. App. 2d 1, 8-9].)  

 

In Canister v. Emergency Ambulance Service (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 388 (“Canister”), the Court of Appeal held: “[a]s previously noted, courts have broadly construed 'professional negligence' to mean negligence occurring during the rendering of services for which the health care provider is licensed.” (Canister, supra, 160 Cal.App.4th at 406 [questioned by Second District Court of Appeals regarding duty as applied to the EMT driving an ambulance in Aldana v. Stillwagon (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 1 in light of Flores v. Presbyterian Intercommunity Hospital (2016) 63 Cal.4th 75 holding].) Canister held:

 

·         “An EMT's operation of an ambulance qualifies as professional negligence when the EMT is rendering services for which he or she is licensed or when a claim for damages is directly related to the provision of ambulance services by the EMT.” (Id. at 407.)

·         “An EMT similarly performs a number of tasks in transporting a patient to a hospital, any one of which might result in a claim of negligence.” (Id.)

 

Here, the action is slightly distinguished. At hand is not whether driving of the ambulance qualifies as professional negligence, but rather failing to (1) assess Decedent’s mental status, and (2) search Decedent’s belongings for weapons constitutes professional negligence (labeled medical negligence in the SAC). The first is clear – assessment of Decedent’s mental status, as alleged in the SAC, is directly related to the provision of ambulance services rendered by EMTs. This duty is also supported by the allegations in the SAC that Decedent was noted as deranged because he was talking to himself and Hall Ambulance agents were informed that Decedent had taken fentanyl. (See SAC ¶¶ 30, 31.) Thus, the allegations support a reasonable inference that the duty to assess Decedent’s mental state, as alleged in Paragraph 48, existed. The second, search of Decedent’s personal belongings, is less clear. (See SAC ¶¶ 33, 48.) However, on its face, this duty appears supported by the following allegations:

 

·         “DEFENDANT HALL AMBULANCE placed MR. BARDIN onto a gurney and secured him with straps, provided supportive care, and set out for transport to DEFENDANT ANTELOPE VALLEY’S hospital, located at 1600 W. Avenue J, Lancaster, CA 93534.” (SAC ¶ 32.)

·         “While in transport, DEFENDANT HALL AMBULANCE, through its EMTs allegedly asked MR. BARDIN if he had any weapons on him but DEFENDANT HALL AMBULANCE failed to vet his response and/or search MR. BARDIN or his effects for weapons before, during, and/or after transportation to ANTELOPE VALLEY in the ambulance bay.” (SAC ¶ 33.)

 

A reasonable inference of the allegations supports that Hall Ambulance’s EMTs believed Decedent to pose some level of danger to himself and others as (1) they strapped Decedent onto a gurney when Decedent was able to walk on his own, and (2) they inquired as to whether Decedent had a weapon. Thus, the allegations support some sort of duty with regards to weapons as to Hall Ambulance’s EMTs. As pled, Plaintiff has alleged a duty to check Decedent and his belongings for weapons and has supported the allegation with alleged facts that show that Hall Ambulance’s EMTs were concerned about Decedent’s safety to himself and others.

 

Regarding causation, Hall Ambulance’s arguments are, once again, outside the scope of this Demurrer. Whether Decedent’s actions broke the chain of causation is more suited to a motion for summary judgment as it attempts to argue the allegations. No matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co., supra, 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿The allegations of causation are not unsupported conclusions. Rather they are supported by the allegations discussed ante. Thus, Plaintiff has pled (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the EMT/paramedic profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty (lack of mental assessment and lack of searching for weapons); (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury (breach of duty caused injury); and (4) resulting loss or damage (Decedent’s death).

 

Accordingly, Hall Ambulance’s Demurrer to the First Cause of Action (Medical Negligence) is OVERRULED.

 

b.      Second Cause of Action (Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision)

 

Hall Ambulance presents that its employees were not negligent and, assuming they were, that Plaintiff has not established that such negligence caused Decedent’s death, reiterating that the causal chain is broken by Decedent’s suicide.

 

Plaintiff presents the same argument as in the First Cause of Action. Plaintiff further highlights the legal standard of a demurrer and presents that she has adequately pled the Second Cause of Action.

 

As the First Cause of Action did not fall to demurrer, Hall Ambulance’s first argument fails. The second argument concerns causation. The Court reiterates that whether Decedent’s actions broke the chain of causation is outside of the scope of this Demurrer and is more suited to a motion for summary judgment as it attempts to argue the allegations of the SAC.

 

Accordingly, Hall Ambulance’s Demurrer to the Second Cause of Action (Negligent Hiring, Retention, Supervision) is OVERRULED.

 

c.       Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse)

 

Hall Ambulance argues that it does not have the requisite custodial relationship with Decedent to support a claim for dependent adult abuse; that Plaintiff has failed to establish that Decedent qualified as a dependent adult under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (the “Act”); that Plaintiff must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that a defendant is guilty of something more than negligence (i.e., reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct), and the alleged actions do not rise to the level of recklessness as required by the Act.

 

Plaintiff argues that the SAC has sufficiently pled dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse as: (1) the statute defines neglect as failure to provide medical care for physical and mental health needs, failure to protect from health and safety hazards, and failure of an elder or dependent adult to satisfy any of the needs specified in paragraphs (1) to (5) of Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.57, inclusive, for themselves as a result of poor cognitive functioning, mental limitation, substance abuse, or chronic poor health; (2) the SAC pleads that Decedent was in Hall Ambulance’s custodial care; (3) Hall Ambulance assumed a significant measure of responsibility in order to tend to Decedent’s basic needs, and Decedent depended on Defendant to meet those needs; (4) Decedent’s classification is an issue of fact that must be left to a jury; and (5) under Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81 (“Sababin”) a jury could find that Hall Ambulance’s conduct, ignoring prior statements of Decedent’s derangement and failing to implement safety measures for a person suffering a psychotic episode, shows deliberate disregard of the high degree of probability that Decedent would suffer injury and/or was reckless.

 

Statutory interpretation is a matter of law for the Court to decide. (See Christensen v. Lightbourne (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1251 [“'On questions of law arising in mandate proceedings, [the court] exercise[s] independent judgment.' …. Interpretation of a statute or regulation is a question of law.”]; In re J. W. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 200, 209 [“If there is no ambiguity in the language of the statute, ‘then the Legislature is presumed to have meant what it said, and the plain meaning of the language governs.’ [Citation.] ‘Where the statute is clear, courts will not “interpret away clear language in favor of an ambiguity that does not exist.” [Citation.]’ [Citation.]”]; Hernandez v. Modesto Portuguese Pentecost Assn. (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1274, 1280 [“Because liability in this case turns upon a question of statutory interpretation, it is a question of law for the court to decide in a summary judgment motion.”].)

 

Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23 provides:

 

(a) “Dependent adult” means a person, regardless of whether the person lives independently, between the ages of 18 and 64 years who resides in this state and who has physical or mental limitations that restrict his or her ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his or her rights, including, but not limited to, persons who have physical or developmental disabilities, or whose physical or mental abilities have diminished because of age.

 

(b) “Dependent adult” includes any person between the ages of 18 and 64 years who is admitted as an inpatient to a 24-hour health facility, as defined in Sections 1250, 1250.2, and 1250.3 of the Health and Safety Code.

 

The Complaint alleges that Decedent’s symptoms were connected to fentanyl use, not a physical or mental limitation that restricted his ability to carry out normal activities or to protect his rights. (See SAC ¶¶ 31.) As such, Decedent does not fall under Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(a). Regarding Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(b), "inpatient" means a person who has been admitted to a hospital, skilled nursing facility, or intermediate care facility for bed occupancy for purposes of receiving inpatient services. A person is considered an inpatient when he is formally admitted as an inpatient with the expectation that he will remain at least overnight and occupy a bed, even though it later develops that he can be discharged or that he is transferred to another facility and does not actually use a bed overnight. (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 22, § 51108 (Lexis Advance through Register 2024, No. 3, January 19, 2024).) Decedent is not an inpatient as to his time in Hall Ambulance’s care. Further, Hall Ambulance does not qualify as a 24-hour health facility, as defined in Sections 1250, 1250.2, and 1250.3 of the Health and Safety Code as it is not a general acute care hospital (Cal. Health & Saf. Code § 1250(a)), an acute psychiatric hospital (id. at § 1250(b)), a skilled nursing facility (id. at § 1250(c)), an intermediate care facility (id. at § 1250(d)), an intermediate care facility/developmentally disabled habilitative (id. at § 1250(e)), a special hospital (id. at § 1250(f)), an intermediate care facility/developmentally disabled (id. at § 1250(g)), an intermediate care facility/developmentally disabled nursing (id. at § 1250(f)), a congregate living health facility (id. at § 1250(i)), a correctional treatment center (id. at § 1250(j)), a nursing facility (id. at § 1250(k)), a intermediate care facility/developmentally disabled-continuous nursing (ICF/DD-CN) (id. at § 1250(m)), a hospice facility (id. at § 1250(n)), a psychiatric health facility (id. at § 1250.2(a)), or a chemical dependency recovery hospital (id. at § 1250.3(a)).

 

Decedent does not qualify as a dependent adult in light of Hall Ambulance’s position as an ambulance service.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse) is SUSTAINED.

 

d.      Fourth Cause of Action (Survivor Action) and Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death)

 

Hall Ambulance presents that these causes of action must fail as the other causes of action fell to demurrer.

Plaintiff provides that she has standing, directing the parties to the Declaration of Angelina Herrington.[1] Plaintiff argues that she had pled sufficient facts as to each cause of action.

 

In a survival action, the damages recoverable by a decedent's representative or successor in interest on the decedent's Cause of Action are limited to the loss or damage that the decedent sustained or incurred before death. Code of Civil Procedure Section 377.34. “The survival statutes do not create a cause of action. Rather, ‘[t]hey merely prevent the abatement of the cause of action of the injured person, and provide for its enforcement by or against the personal representative of the deceased.’ (Quiroz v. Seventh Ave. Center (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1264.)

 

The elements of the cause of action for wrongful death are the tort (negligence or other wrongful act), the resulting death, and the damages, consisting of the pecuniary loss suffered by the heirs.” (Lattimore v. Dickey, supra, 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) “In any action for wrongful death resulting from negligence, the complaint must contain allegations as to all the elements of actionable negligence.” (Novak v. Continental Tire North America (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 189, 195.) The SAC bases the Fifth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death) on the negligence claim. (See SAC ¶ 83.)

 

As the Court stated, ante, Plaintiff’s filed declaration does not adhere to the requirements set out in Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 377.32. (See Judicial Notice section.) However, the Survivor Cause of Action was not demurred by Hall Ambulance on that basis. As is, the First Cause of Action (Negligence) has not fallen to demurrer and, as it is properly pled, so too are the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action.

 

Accordingly, Hall Ambulance’s Demurrer to the Fourth (Survivor Action) and Fifth (Wrongful Death) Causes of Action is OVERRULED.

 

e.       Leave to Amend

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿While under California law¿leave¿to amend¿is liberally granted, “leave¿to amend¿should not be granted¿where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.”¿(Vaillette¿v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co.¿(1993),¿18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿“A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.)¿¿¿

 

As to Hall Ambulance’s Demurrer, the only cause of action at issue for leave to amend is the Third Cause of Action (Dependent Adult Neglect/Abuse). This cause of action cannot be rectified by amendment as (1) Decedent does not qualify as a dependent adult under Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(a), and (2) Decedent does not qualify as a dependent adult under Cal. Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.23(b) as he is not an inpatient and Hall Ambulance is not a 24-hour health facility, as defined in Sections 1250, 1250.2, and 1250.3 of the Health and Safety Code.

 

Accordingly, leave to amend is not given.

 

iii.    Hall Ambulance’s Motion to Strike

 

Hall Ambulance seeks to strike the following:

 

·         “reckless” from Paragraph 69 of the SAC; and

·         Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages;

 

Hall Ambulance argues that punitive damages are governed by Cal. Civ. Code § 3294, which requires a showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, and Plaintiff has pled no facts that support such a finding.
