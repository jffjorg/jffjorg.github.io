DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV13910    Hearing Date: October 3, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE AND MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

Hearing Date: 10/3/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV13910 DORIS FAY WILLIAMS, AN INDIVIDUAL vs DILIP BHAKTA

Moving Party: Defendant DILIP BHAKTA

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO CONSOLIDATE AND MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL ARE DENIED.


Legal Standard 

 

California Rule of Court 3.350, subdivision (a) states in relevant part: 

¿¿ 

(1) A notice of motion to consolidate must: 

(A) List all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record; 

(B) Contain the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest-numbered case shown first; and 

(C) Be filed in each case sought to be consolidated. 

¿¿ 

(2) The motion to consolidate: 

(A) Is deemed a single motion for the purpose of determining the appropriate filing fee, but memorandums, declarations, and other supporting papers must be filed only in the lowest-numbered case;¿¿ 

(B) Must be served on all attorneys of record and all non-represented parties in all of the cases sought to be consolidated; and 

(C) Must have a proof of service filed as part of the motion.¿ 

¿¿ 

(Cal. Rule of Court 3.350, subd. (a)).

Also, the consolidation statute, Code of Civil Procedure section 1048, states in relevant part: 

 

(a)¿ When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay. 

¿¿ 

(b)¿ The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1048, subd. (a).)    

 

The granting or denial of the motion to consolidate rests in the sound discretion of the trial court and will not be reversed except upon a clear showing of abuse of discretion. (See Fellner v. Steinbaum (1955) 132 Cal.App.2d 509, 511.)

 

The present motion failed to comply with the standards set forth under Rule 3.350. First, the notice of motion did not list all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, or the names of their respective attorneys of record. Second, it failed to include the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest-numbered case listed first. Third, the notice was not filed in each case sought to be consolidated. Without this information, the Court cannot determine whether all parties in every case have been properly served. Thus, the motion to consolidate is DENIED.

As for the trial continuance, the trial in the instant action is currently scheduled for November 25, 2024. Defendant seeks a continuance on the basis that written discovery and depositions have not been completed in the related actions. The two related cases were both filed in 2022, but Defendant has not specified which related case has outstanding discovery, nor has Defendant provided an explanation as to why discovery has not been completed in cases that have been pending since 2022. Furthermore, Defendant has failed to articulate how, if at all, the incomplete discovery in the related cases would impact the lead case. Additionally, Defendant has not addressed the various factors for a trial continuance under California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1332(c) and (d).

Defendant has provided minimal information for the Court to find good cause for granting the continuance. Therefore, the request for continuance is DENIED.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect.” Code Civ. Proc., § 473(a)(1). In addition, “any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.” Code Civ. Proc., § 576.   

“[T]he court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.”  Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.  “Where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party, the liberal rule of allowance prevails.” Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564; cf. Arthur L. Sachs, Inc. v. City of Oceanside (1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 315, 324 (denial of leave to amend on the eve of trial was error absent a showing a prejudice); Higgins, supra, 123 Cal.App.3d at pp. 565–566 (same). 

“A motion to amend a pleading before trial must: 

1.   Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; 

2.   State what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and 

3.   State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.” 

Cal. Rule of Court, rule 3.1324(a). 

 

“A separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify:  

1.   The effect of the amendment; 

2.   Why the amendment is necessary and proper;  

3.   When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and 

4.   The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.” 

Cal. Rule of Court, rule 3.1324(b). 

 

DISCUSSION

Cross-Complainant Robert Health Logistics moves for leave to amend its cross-complaint and file the First Amended Cross-Complaint. The motion will be granted. 

  1. Cross-Complainant’s motion satisfies the statutory precepts under California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324.¿ 

With its motion for leave to file the FAC, Cross-Complainant has included a “redlined” copy of the proposed FAC.¿ (Motion, Exh. 1.)¿ Cross-Complainant’s motion indicates that the FAC contains new causes of action for Express Indemnity and Declaratory Relief. (Ibid.) Cross-Complainant’s motion indicates that the FAC names two new parties, On Time Express, LLC, and Aziz Trucking, Inc. (Ibid.) Thus, the motion states what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. (Mot., Turner Declaration.)¿

Further, Cross-Complainant’s motion is accompanied by a separate declaration which states the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Mot., Turner Decl.)¿

Therefore, Cross-Complainant’s motion meets the minimum requirements under Rule 3.1324. Its motion will be granted absent a showing of prejudice to Plaintiff, the only party who filed an Opposition.¿See Higgins, supra, 123 Cal.App.3d at p. 564.¿ 

2.   Plaintiff’s Opposition

Plaintiff argues that the Court should deny the motion on the following grounds: a) the proposed amendments are futile under the Worker’s Compensation Exclusivity Rule; b) the proposed amendments are barred by the Statute of Limitations; c) Cross-Complainant’s delay in proposing its amendments in unexcused; and d) undue prejudice will result from the Court granting Cross-Complainant’s motion. No other parties filed an opposition.

  1. Plaintiff fails to establish Futility under the Worker’s Compensation Exclusivity Rule.

Plaintiff argues that the Court should deny the motion because the proposed amendments are futile under the Worker’s Compensation Exclusivity Rule. Plaintiff references “the Worker’s Compensation Exclusivity Rule” but fails to state the language of the rule or cite to a code section or any case law supporting the existence of this rule. Thus, Plaintiff fails to justify why Cross-Complainant’s motion should be denied on this ground.

  1. Cross-Complainant’s claims are not barred by the Statute of Limitations.

Plaintiff argues that the Court should deny the motion because Cross-Complainant’s claims are barred by the statute of limitations.

First, the Court notes that an amended complaint is not barred by the applicable statute of limitations under the relation back doctrine where the proposed amendment: (1) rests on the same general set of facts as the original complaint; and (2) refers to the same incident and same injuries as the original complaint.  Barrington v. A.H Robins Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 146, 151 (Barrington). 

Here, Cross-Complainant apparently seeks to add causes of action arising from the same transaction as their original claims. Cross-Complainant may reasonably succeed at arguing that these claims for Express Indemnity and Declaratory Relief are allowed under the relation back doctrine.  See Barrington, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 151.  If Plaintiff believes that Cross-Complainant’s claims are time-barred because they do not fall under the relation back doctrine, the proper avenue by which Plaintiff might raise such a statute of limitations defense could be a demurrer, a motion for summary adjudication, or a motion for judgment on the pleadings, not in its brief opposing Cross-Complainant’s motion for leave to amend the Cross-Complaint. Thus, Plaintiff fails to justify why Cross-Complainant’s motion should be denied on this ground.

C.   Cross-Complainant has not demonstrated unwarranted delay in bringing this motion.

Plaintiff argues that Cross-Complainant has demonstrated unwarranted delay in bringing its motion for leave to amend. See Huff v. Wilkins (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 732, 746 (unwarranted delay may be sufficient for trial court to deny motion for leave to amend). 

However, Cross-Complainant reasonably argues that the proposed amendment is based on new facts that were first discovered during deposition testimony on February 23 and 28, 2024. (See Mot., p. 8:19-25.) On April 1, 2024, Cross-Complainant’s counsel emailed all parties requesting that they stipulate to the filing of their First Amended Cross-Complaint in light of the deposition testimony. (Mot., p. 8:26-28.) On April 2, 2024, Cross-Complainant filed its First Amended Cross-Complaint and summons. (Mot., p. 9:1-2.) On April 4, 2024, Cross-Defendants On Time Express and Logistics Academy each filed a demurrer and motions to strike the First Amended Cross-Complaint. (Mot., p. 9:3-6.)

On May 17, 2024, the parties stipulated to Cross-Complainant withdrawing its First Amended Cross-Complaint without prejudice and the Cross-Defendants withdrawing their demurrers and motions to strike with the understanding from all parties that Cross-Complainant would seek leave of the Court to file the instant motion. (Mot., p. 9:7-10.) On June 3, 2024, Cross-Complainant’s counsel requested via email that the parties agree that Cross-Complainant would file its First-Amended Cross-Complaint adding causes of action for Express Indemnity and Declaratory Relief. (Mot., p. 9:11-13.) On June 13, 2024, Cross-Complainant’s counsel circulated the proposed red-lined First Amended Cross-Complaint and Cross-Defendant Logistics Academy requested a joint stipulation regarding the same, which was signed by both Defendant Bimbo and Cross-Defendant Logistics Academy on July 1 and 15, 2024, respectively. (Mot., p. 9:13-17.) On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel informed Cross-Complainant that Plaintiff would not stipulate and suggested that Cross-Complainant proceed with the instant motion. (Mot., p. 9:17-18.)

Cross-Complainant filed this motion for leave to amend their cross-complaint and file the first amended cross-complaint on August 27, 2024. The Court finds that under the circumstances in this case, Cross-Complainant has not demonstrated unreasonable delay in bringing the instant motion. Cross-Complainant’s filed their First Amended Cross-Complaint shortly after learning of the new information in Plaintiff’s deposition, but Cross-Defendants’ demurrers lead to a stipulation between all parties regarding Cross-Complainants withdrawal of the initial first amended cross-complaint with an understanding from all parties that Cross-Complainant would seek leave of Court to file. Less than one month passed between the parties’ stipulation for withdrawal of the First Amended Cross-Complaint and Cross-Complainant’s request to stipulate as to the filing of the new First Amended Cross-Complaint, which Cross-Complainant circulated a red-lined version of.

Plaintiff made no indication that he would oppose this motion until August 15, 2024, after being nonresponsive to the request for stipulation for filing and multiple phone calls by Cross-Complainant regarding the same. (Reply, p. 5:3-10.) Shortly after learning of Plaintiff’s unwillingness to stipulate, Cross-Complainant filed the instant motion. Based on the foregoing, Cross-Complainant did not “wait nearly seven months to bring this motion” and did not have “plenty of opportunity to bring its motion immediately after February 2024” as Plaintiff argues. (Opposition, p. 6:20-21.) Thus, Cross-Complainant has not demonstrated unreasonable delay in bringing the instant motion. 

D.  Plaintiff fails to show any undue prejudice will result from the Court granting Cross-Complainant’s motion.

Finally, Plaintiff argues that he will be unduly prejudiced if the Court grants Cross-Complainant’s motion for leave to amend its complaint because Cross-Complainant’s added parties will require Plaintiff to “undergo the discovery process all over again with new parties, and likely undergo another deposition.” (Opp., p. 8:23-24.)  

Having to engage in discovery practice is a basic feature of litigation, not proof of actual prejudice. Furthermore, per Plaintiff’s own application, the Court has already continued the trial date in this case from June 24, 2024, to February 19, 2025. (Opp., p. 8:7-9.) This motion is not made on the eve of trial as Plaintiff argues, but even if this motion were made on the eve of trial, the case law directs the Court to allow parties to amend their pleadings so long as there is no showing of actual prejudice. Negative consequences for the opposing party can be mitigated by continuing the trial. See Thompson Pacific Construction, Inc. v. City of Sunnyvale (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 525, 545. 

Here, the facts are such that allowing Cross-Complainant to amend its cross-complaint will not improperly prejudice Plaintiff. Plaintiff also argues that granting the instant motion will result in the loss of critical evidence because Defendant Bimbo will not agree to produce its policies and procedures without the signatures of the proposed new Cross-Defendants, On Time Express and Aziz Trucking, because they would now be considered parties to the case based on the Proposed Cross-Complaint. However, Plaintiff provides no authority to support the argument that another party’s refusal to produce evidence in discovery justifies a denial of a Cross-Complainant’s motion for leave to amend. The court’s discretion to liberally permit amendment of the pleadings is not defeated by another party’s choice to withhold discovery on the basis of the Cross-Complaint, which is an issue independent of whether the proposed amendments to the Cross-Complaint are proper to begin with. Plaintiff asserts that he has filed Motions to Compel relating to these discovery issues, which is the proper avenue by which they might raise such an argument.

Further, the Cross-Complaint does not name Plaintiff, such that Plaintiff does not need to mount any defense to the causes of action therein. However, if Plaintiff believes that he neede additional time to conduct discovery, the Court is open to providing another continuance of the trial date. If necessary, the parties are encouraged to meet and confer to stipulate to an approximate new trial date. 

Because Plaintiff has not shown that he will suffer undue prejudice upon the Court granting Cross-Complainant’s motion, the motion will be granted.¿ See Honig, supra, 6 Cal.App.4th at p. 965.¿ 

III.        CONCLUSION

The Court GRANTS the Motion for Leave to file First Amended Cross-Complaint.

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DISMISSAL 

Hearing Date: 10/3/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV19369 MANUEL LOPEZ, et al. vs SUSANA CASARRUBIAS HERRERA

Moving Party: Plaintiffs 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO SET ASIDE DISMISSAL IS GRANTED

 

Section 473, subdivision (b) states in pertinent part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.... Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Italics added.) 

        Section 473, subdivision (b), provides for two distinct types of relief.  “Under the discretionary relief provision, on a showing of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect,” the court has discretion to allow relief from a “judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against” a party or his or her attorney. Under the mandatory relief provision, on the other hand, upon a showing by attorney declaration of “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect,” the court shall vacate any “resulting default judgment or dismissal entered.”  The range of attorney conduct for which relief can be granted in the mandatory provision is broader than that in the discretionary provision, and includes inexcusable neglect.  But the range of adverse litigation results from which relief can be granted is narrower. Mandatory relief only extends to vacating a default which will result in the entry of a default judgment, a default judgment, or an entered dismissal.”  (Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 615–616.)  ¿¿ 

To gain the statutory benefits, a party or legal representative must seek such relief “within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); see Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980 (Rappleyea).   

Here, the case was dismissed on December 12, 2023, and the present motion to set aside was filed on June 12, 2024, within the six-month deadline. Further, Plaintiffs’ prior attorney submitted a declaration detailing his mistake or neglect, which resulted in the law firm not attending trial on December 12, 2023. Counsel’s mistake led to the dismissal as the minute order indicate that the case was dismissed under Code of Civil Procedure § 581(b)(3) for failing to appear at trial. Finally, dismissal is a type of court order that falls within the scope of mandatory relief under CCP § 473(b). All requirements for mandatory relief have been met and therefore Plaintiffs’ motion is granted.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

 

Meet and Confer¿

Before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the demurring and moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading sought to be stricken or demurred to, in person or telephonically, for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer and motion to strike.¿ (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.)¿

 

Demurrer¿

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)¿ When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿ (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿ “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)¿ “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿ (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿ The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿ (Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)¿

 

Motion to Strike¿

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)¿ The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)¿

In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.¿ (College. Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)¿ These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice.¿ (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)¿¿¿

“Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”¿ (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725 [examining Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1)].)¿ “A conscious disregard of the safety of others may constitute malice within the meaning of section 3294 of the Civil Code. In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.”¿ (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895-896.)¿

“As amended to include [despicable], the statute plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests.¿ The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.”¿ (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)¿ The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.¿ Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’ ”¿ (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287.). Factors in assessing the degree of reprehensibility of a defendant’s conduct are (1) whether the harm caused was physical or merely economic; (2) whether the conduct demonstrated indifference or reckless disregard for the health or safety of others; (3) the financial vulnerability of the target; (4) whether the conduct occurred repeatedly or only once; and (5) whether the harm was the result of intentional malice, trickery, or deceit, or was a mere accident. (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1311, fn 12.)

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿ [Citations.]¿ In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.¿ [Citations.]¿ In ruling on a motion to strike, courts do not read allegations in isolation.¿ [Citation.]”¿ (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)¿ “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.¿ [Citation.]¿ Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.¿ [Citation.]”¿ (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.)¿

“[T]he imposition of punitive damages upon a corporation is based upon its own fault.¿ It is not imposed vicariously by virtue of the fault of others.”¿ (City Products Corp. v. Globe Indemnity Co. (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 31, 36.)¿ “Corporations are legal entities which do not have minds capable of recklessness, wickedness, or intent to injure or deceive.¿ An award of punitive damages against a corporation therefore must rest on the malice of the corporation’s employees.¿ But the law does not impute every employee’s malice to the corporation.¿ Instead, the punitive damages statute requires proof of malice among corporate leaders:¿ the ‘officer[s], director[s], or managing agent[s].’ ”¿ (Cruz v. Home Base (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 167 [citation omitted].)¿ As to ratification, “[a] corporation cannot confirm and accept that which it does not actually know about.”¿ (Id., at p. 168.) For ratification sufficient to justify punitive damages against corporation, there must be proof that officers, directors, or managing agents had actual knowledge of the malicious conduct and its outrageous character. (College Hospital, Inc., supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 726.)¿

 

III.    DISCUSSION  

 

Meet and Confer¿ 

The Court finds Defendant has filed code-compliant meet and confer declarations for both the demurrer and the motion to strike.¿ (See Kahn Decl. ISO MTS, ¶¶ 5-6; Kahn Decl. ISO Demurrer ¶¶ 5-7.) 

 

Demurrer

Defendant has demurred to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for battery, eighth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1941.3, ninth cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1942.4, and tenth cause of action for negligent violation of statutory duty to Plaintiff.

 

 

          Demurrer – First Cause of Action – Battery

The elements of a civil battery are: (1) the defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, (2) the act resulted in a harmful or offensive contact with the plaintiff’s person, (3) the plaintiff did not consent to the contact, and (4) a reasonable person in the plaintiff’s position would have been harmed or offended by the contact. (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 669.)

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not stated a claim for civil battery. The complaint includes a statement that Defendant’s “intentional, deliberate, and reckless actions… cause Plaintiff to be touched by bedbug infestation and other uninhabitable conditions.” (Complaint, ¶ 44.) However, this is a conclusory allegation that the Court need not credit. The complaint further alleges that Defendant’s intent is evidenced by its actions in failing to properly and thoroughly inspect the apartment to ensure that the apartment was free from bedbugs, thereby demonstrating wanton and reckless misconduct (Complaint, ¶ 45; Opp. to Demurrer, p. 7.) However, reckless conduct alone does not constitute a sufficient basis for battery, unless an act “inherently dangerous to others is committed with a conscious disregard of human life and safety” such that the act transcends recklessness. (People v. Martinez (1977) 75 Cal.App.3d 859, 863.) Plaintiff has not alleged that Defendant acted with a conscious disregard of human life and safety. Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to allege the intent element of battery and has failed to state a claim for civil battery.

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first cause of action is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

 

Demurrer – Eighth Cause of Action – Violation of Civil Code Section 1941.3

          Plaintiff has stated in her opposition that, due to attorney error, she mistakenly alleged Civil Code section 1941.3 as a cause of action when she intended to allege Civil Code section 1942.3.

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

 

Demurrer – Ninth Cause of Action – Violation of Civil Code Section 1942.3

Civil Code section 1942.3 states that a landlord of a dwelling “may not demand rent, collect rent, issue a notice of a rent increase, or issue a three-day notice to pay rent or quit if all of the following conditions are met:

(1) The dwelling substantially lacks any of the affirmative standard characteristics listed in Section 1941.1 or violates Section 17920.10 of the Health and Safety Code, or is deemed and declared substandard as set forth in Section 17920.3 of the Health and Safety Code...

(2) A public officer or employee who is responsible for the enforcement of any housing law, after inspecting the premises, has notified the landlord or the landlord's agent in writing of his or her obligations to abate the nuisance or repair the substandard conditions.

(3) The conditions have existed and have not been abated 35 days beyond the date of service of the notice specified in paragraph (2) and the delay is without good cause…

(4) The conditions were not caused by an act or omission of the tenant or lessee in violation of Section 1929 or 1941.2.”

The complaint alleges that the apartment lacked at least one of the affirmative standard characteristics listed in Civil Code section 1941.1 and Defendant continued to collect rent even after being placed on notice of this condition, and the condition was not cause by an act or omission of Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶¶ 146, 148, 151.) However, Plaintiff does not allege that the condition existed and was not abated 35 days beyond service of the notice without good cause, nor does Plaintiff allege that a public officer or employee responsible for the enforcement of any housing law inspected the premises and notified Defendant in writing of its obligations to abate the condition. Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 1942.3.

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the ninth cause of action, with leave to amend.

         

 

Demurrer – Tenth Cause of Action – Negligent Violation of Statutory Duty to Maintain Habitable Conditions

Plaintiff has not alleged which statute Defendant has violated for this cause of action. Plaintiff has therefore failed to state a cause of action for negligent violation of a statutory duty to maintain habitable conditions.

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the tenth cause of action, with leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

Defendant seeks to strike paragraphs 52, 62, 71, 78, 100, 101, 116, 117, and 136 of the complaint, as well as paragraphs 9 and 13 of the prayer for relief. The majority of these are punitive damage allegations, while paragraph 62 of the complaint is an allegation concerning section 40 of California State Housing Law and a paragraph 13 of the prayer for relief requests attorneys’ fees.

Plaintiff has not opposed the motion to strike as it pertains to paragraph 62 of the complaint or paragraph 13 of the prayer for relief. Therefore, these paragraphs are stricken.

          Punitive Damage Allegations

The Court finds that Plaintiff has adequately stated a prima facie claim for punitive damages. Plaintiff has adequately alleged malice as required by Civil Code section 2394 because Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant deliberately and recklessly failed to inspect Plaintiff’s apartment or provide notice to Plaintiff of bedbug infestation despite having knowledge of existing infestations. (Complaint, ¶ 38.) Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant has fulfilled two of the five factors indicating despicable conduct by alleging physical harm in the form of bedbug bites and recklessness to the health or safety of others despite prior notice of a defective condition at the apartment. (Complaint, ¶ 38.)

Plaintiff has further alleged that Defendant was aware of the bedbug infestations in the apartment complex because of reports from other residents. (Complaint, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant, a corporation, authorized or ratified the conduct of their employees by permitting and tolerating a common practice and culture of extreme indifference by employees and failing to implement adequate policies and procedures to prevent bedbug infestations. (Complaint, ¶ 38.)

This is sufficient to survive a motion to strike. Therefore, Defendant’s motion to strike 52, 71, 78, 100, 101, 116, 117, and 136 of the complaint, as well as paragraph 9 of the prayer for relief, is DENIED.

IV.     CONCLUSION

         Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer with leave to amend and GRANTS in part and DENIES in part the Motion to Strike.

          On November 22, 2022, plaintiff Marco Nicolas (Plaintiff), filed this action against defendants Gerardo Zamora; Uber Technologies, Inc.; Raiser-CA, LLC; Raiser, LLC; and Does 1 through 100 for injuries arising from a vehicle collision.

On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff served defendants Uber Technologies, Inc; Raiser-CA, LLC; and Raiser, LLC (collectively, Defendants) with Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories. On June 5, 2024, Defendants provided their objection-only responses to the foregoing discovery. The responses were not verified by defense counsel. On July 15, 2024, Defendants provided further responses to the foregoing discovery. These responses also were not verified by defense counsel.

On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel Defendants to serve further, verified responses to Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories.

On September 16, 2024, Defendants served Plaintiff with signed verifications for both the June 5 objection-only responses and the July 15 substantive responses.

On September 19, 2024, Defendants filed oppositions to the motions to compel further responses. Defendants stated in their oppositions and included supporting exhibits that they have agreed to provide further responses in accordance with Plaintiff’s separate statements.

On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed replies to the oppositions.

 

Discussion

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) If a court determines the scope of discovery imposes a burden, causes great expense or is intrusive and such outweighs the likelihood that the discoverable information sought will garner admissible evidence, the court shall limit the scope of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.020.)  

When responses to interrogatories are deficient, the propounding party may file a motion to compel further responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300.) A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought when the responses contain: (1) “a statement of compliance when the demand is incomplete;” (2) “answers that are inadequate, incomplete or evasive;” and (3) “an objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (Ibid.)  

The court finds that Plaintiff’s motions are moot. Here, Defendants have served verification on September 16, 2024, before the hearing, for their prior responses, and have agreed to provide further responses in accordance with Plaintiff’s separate statement. (Opp. Mot. Compel Further Resp. to FROGS, p. 4; Fuentes Decl. Opp. Mot. Compel Further Resp. to FROGS, Ex. 6-7.)

Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides that a court may impose a monetary sanction for misuse of the discovery process unless a court finds that the one subject to sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivision (b) and (d) provides that misuse of the discovery process includes using a discovery method in a manner that does not comply with its specified procedures and failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.

The court finds that sanctions are warranted. Defendants failed to submit verified responses to Plaintiff’s form interrogatories or special interrogatories until after Plaintiff filed motions to compel further responses.
         

Moreover, Defendant contends the responses were timely. It contends the responses were due on June 5, based on the fact that, without considering the 2-day extension for electronic service, the responses were due on a Saturday and, pursuant to CCP 12 sections 12a and 1010.6(a)(2)(B), the due date then became Monday. Then, adding the two days for service, Defendant calculated that the final deadline was June 5, 2024.

Defendant’s calculations are a morass of mistakes.  First, Defendant contends the initial calculation had the responses due on Saturday, June 2.  But, June 2 was a Sunday.  As a result, CCP 12a, which applies to holidays and Saturdays, does not apply here.  Defendant is correct that Section 1010.6(a)(2)(B) does apply, but using June 2 as the due date, extends the due date to June 4, not June 5.  So, while in its opposition, Defendant recognizes it made some scheduling mistake, it fails to recognize the mistake it actually made.  Plaintiff was correct – the responses were due on June 4.

Based on the discussion that the Court had with the parties at the IDC, the issue here becomes centered on waiver and sanctions because Defendant did provide further responses.  Technically, Defendant waived its objections because its responses were late (whether or not the Court would provide it with relief from this waiver is not before the Court).  Ultimately, Plaintiff had to bring motions that likely could have been avoided if Defendant had simply recognized its due date calculation error.  But, that did not happen.  Accordingly, the Court awards Plaintiff sanctions against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally in the reasonable amount of $3000, to be paid within 20 days.  (The Court denies Defendant’s request for sanctions.)  

Plaintiff has requested sanctions in the amount of $4,355, calculated based on his attorney’s hourly fee of $450 for 9.5 hours and an $80 filing fee. Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendants and their counsel is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $3,000, which the Court deems reasonable.  Sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiffs’ counsel within 20 days.

 

Conclusion

          In light of the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One and to Special Interrogatories, Set One are MOOT.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions against Defendants and their counsel in the amount of $3,000. Sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within 20 days.

DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

Hearing Date: 10/3/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV13640 CHRISTIAN HAYWOOD vs CEDAR SINAI

Moving Party: Defendant Cedars Sinai 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

 

Ruling: DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE ARE GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

 

Background

On June 14, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed a two-page Complaint. The first page contains the case caption and lists the defendant as “Cedar Sinai.” The second page is handwritten and almost illegible, but it appears to allege the following:

·        Plaintiff had a stroke three years ago and was brain dead for seven days.  

·        “On May 21,” Plaintiff went to “Cedar Sinai” with “head swelling” and “a hard time breathing.”

·        Plaintiff was never called to the back and was then discharged.

Defendant now demurs and moves to strike the pleading on the grounds of uncertainty and the fact that the pleading is not signed. Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

Uncertainty 

CCP section 430.10(f) provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous and unintelligible. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.” (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.) “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)  

Discussion

Here, the complaint contains no jurisdictional allegations. Plaintiff does not state whether the events occurred in California or elsewhere, leaving the Court uncertain if it has jurisdiction to hear this case.

A significant portion of the handwritten complaint is nearly illegible, which further exacerbates the uncertainty of the pleading. Plaintiff alleges that he was "not called to the back and then discharged." At another point, Plaintiff alleges that “they stole my belt buckle” and “[p]rice is well over the limit.”  Ultimately, it is unclear what cause of action Plaintiff is alleging: medical malpractice, negligence, or another cause of action. This lack of clarity makes it impossible for Defendant to prepare a defense or even understand what claims are being made against it.

Additionally, the complaint does not allege any specific damages (except the stolen belt buckle) or explain how Defendant's conduct caused harm. Under California law, a complaint must include not only the facts supporting the cause of action but also a demand for judgment for the relief to which the pleader claims to be entitled. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10(a).) The foregoing absences adds to the uncertainty of the pleading.

Furthermore, under California law, every pleading, including a complaint, must be signed by the party (if they are representing themselves) or by the attorney representing the party. (California Code of Civil Procedure § 128.7(a).) The present complaint does not include Plaintiff's signature and therefore should be stricken.

The Complaint is uncertain and lacks Plaintiff’s signature, but since this is Plaintiff’s first complaint, Plaintiff will be given an opportunity to amend. Thus, the demurrer is sustained with leave to amend within 20 days.
