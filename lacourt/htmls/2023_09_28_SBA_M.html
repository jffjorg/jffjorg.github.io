ALERT

Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

 

Department M strongly encourages the use of  LA CourtConnect* for ALL hearings, without need for prior approval, unless live testimony by a witness is required.

 https://lacourt.portalscloud.com/VCourt/

 

 

*Parties with a fee waiver on file may be eligible to appear at no/reduced cost


Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.



Case Number: 23TRCV01567    Hearing Date: September 28, 2023    Dept: M

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. M

 

JOHN YELUSICH, et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV01567

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

DRIFTWOOD HEALTHCARE CENTER, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                         September 28, 2023

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center-Torrance, Providence Health System-Southern California, and Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center-San Pedro

Responding Party:                  None

(1)   Demurrer to Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

 

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            Defendants Providence’s demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd cause of action for elder abuse.  The motion to strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

BACKGROUND

            On May 17, 2023, plaintiffs John Yelusich and Donna Yelusich filed a complaint against Driftwood Healthcare Center, Driftwood Healthcare & Wellness Center, LLC, Citrus Wellness Center, LLC, Torrance Memorial Medical Center, Torrance Health Association, Inc., Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center – Torrance, Providence Health System – Southern California, and Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center – San Pedro for (1) negligence/professional medical negligence, (2) elder abuse/willful misconduct, and (3) loss of consortium.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.  Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.” Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal. App. 2d 636, 643-44.

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  CCP § 436(a).  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  CCP §436(b).  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  CCP §436.  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

Demurrer

Defendants Providence demur to the 2nd cause of action for elder abuse in the complaint on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

            The complaint alleges that on March 10, 2022, plaintiff John Yelusich was taken by ambulance to the ER of defendant Providence Little Company of Mary Medical Center – San Pedro, after passing out at home.  Complaint, ¶20.  At Providence, he was diagnosed with hemiparesis of his left side.  An x-ray was taken and defendant’s staff misdiagnosed and/or failed to ascertain that plaintiff had suffered a T-10 compression fracture.  Plaintiff was given a shot of morphine and just three pain pills (Vicodin).  After his discharge from Providence, he continued to suffer excruciating pain.  Id., ¶21.

            Plaintiffs allege that on March 24, 2022, plaintiff who had been in extreme pain since his discharge from Providence, suffered a stroke and was taken by paramedics to defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center.  Plaintiff had suffered a hemorrhagic bleed stroke and became partially paralyzed.  Plaintiff suffered from facial droop, speech issues, tongue maneuvering issues, paralysis in the left arm and hand, left leg and drop toe.  He was left incontinent of bowel and bladder.  Id., ¶22.  Plaintiff continued to complain of back pain.  An x-ray of the thoracic spine revealed a compression fracture at T-10.  Id., ¶23.

            Plaintiffs further allege that on April 15, 2022, plaintiff was discharged to defendant Driftwood Healthcare Center for rehabilitation.  Upon admission, plaintiff was still paralyzed and weak on the left side.  He continued to suffer from incontinence.  Id., ¶24.  Plaintiff developed a pressure injury while at Driftwood.  Plaintiff was unable to swallow food properly (which Driftwood staff failed to diagnose and/or timely diagnose), and he lost weight and became malnourished and/or dehydrated.  Plaintiff would need to swallow “10 times” in order to swallow a bite of food and get something into his stomach.  Plaintiff’s weight dropped to 165 lbs.  Id., ¶25.  On July 1, 2022, plaintiff was discharged home.  Id., ¶26.

            Plaintiff further alleges that he had always had a big appetite and his wife could not understand why he was eating so little.  She realized he had swallowing issues and began feeding him small amounts of pureed food and protein drinks.  Id., ¶27.

            Plaintiff also alleges that on August 3, 2022, plaintiff was admitted to Providence because he was borderline septic, he had a suspected UTI, he was not responding well, and he was suffering from confusion.  Plaintiff was malnourished no matter what his wife could get him to eat and he had lost approximately 65 lbs. from March 10, 2022.  Id., ¶28.  At Providence, a swallowing evaluation was conducted and plaintiff’s ability was noted as dysfunctional.  Plaintiff was given hydration, antibiotics, and a new Foley catheter.  Id., ¶29.  On August 9, 2022, plaintiff was discharged home with home care.  Id., ¶30.

            2nd cause of action for elder abuse

In order to state a cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act, “a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.” Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 789. 

 “Neglect” is defined in section 15610.57 as “(a). . . . (1) The negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder . . . to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise. . . .(b) Neglect includes, but is not limited to, all of the following: . . . (2) Failure to provide medical care for physical and mental health needs.”

“It is true that statutory elder abuse includes 'neglect as defined in Section 15610.57,' which in turn includes negligent failure of an elder custodian 'to provide medical care for [the elder's] physical and mental health needs' (§ 15610.57(b)(2)). . . . '[N]eglect' within the meaning of [section 15610.57] covers an area of misconduct distinct from 'professional negligence.'  As used in the Act, neglect refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the 'failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations.'  Thus, the statutory definition of 'neglect' speaks not of the undertaking of medical services, but of the failure to provide medical care.  Notably, the other forms of abuse, as defined in the Act--physical abuse and fiduciary abuse [§15657]--are forms of intentional wrongdoing also distinct from 'professional negligence.'"  Covenant Care, Inc., supra, at 783.

“[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.”  Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407 (citing Covenant Care).  “Allegations of misconduct directed against one defendant . . . do not state a cause of action against another defendant against whom the allegations of misconduct are not directed.”  Id. at 410 (citation omitted).  “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence.”  Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal. App. 4th 336, 347 (citation omitted).  Citing to the content of the definition of “neglect” in Welf. and Inst. Code 15610.57(b), the Supreme Court stated, “[w]hat they each seem to contemplate is the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder’s basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.”  Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148, 58.  “What seems beyond doubt is that the Legislature enacted a scheme distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together – claims of professional negligence and neglect.”  Id. at 159.

            Plaintiffs allege that defendants were responsible for the care or custody of plaintiff.  Complaint, ¶61.  Plaintiff was in a caretaking or custodial relationship with defendants.  Id., ¶62.  Defendants had a substantial caretaking or custodial relationship, involving ongoing responsibility for one or more basic needs of plaintiff such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene, and/or medical care.  Id., ¶63.  Defendants engaged in conduct which resulted in plaintiff being subjected to “physical abuse,” “neglect,” “financial abuse,” “abandonment,” “isolation,” and “abduction” as defined under Elder Abuse Act, Welf. & Inst. Code §15600, et seq.  Id., ¶69.  Defendants neglected plaintiff by, in part, failing to assist in personal hygiene and/or in the provision of food, clothing or shelter, failing to provide medical care for plaintiff’s physical and mental health needs, failing to protect from health and safety hazards, failing to prevent malnutrition and/or dehydration, and/or failing to ensure adequate supervision of plaintiff who was left unsupervised, unmonitored, and unassisted on many occasions and who suffered the development and/or worsening of pressure injuries.  Id., ¶70.  Defendants breached their duties to plaintiff under the Elder Abuse Act and the duties owed under state and federal nursing home regulations and failed to require others under their supervision and control to do so, even though said laws/regulations are minimum standards of care which were established to protect elderly patients in nursing homes from unnecessary exposure to health and safety hazards.  Id., ¶72.  Defendants’ staff knew that plaintiff’s pressure ulcers were getting worse, but defendants continually failed to follow the patient care plan or reassess his condition and implement a new care plan based on his worsening condition.  Defendants failed to transfer plaintiff to an appropriate medical facility trained to properly care for plaintiff’s condition.  Id., ¶76. 

            Defendants Providence argue that the complaint raises issues pertaining to medical services (subject to theory of negligence), not custodial caretaking (subject to theory of abuse).  Citing to Winn, supra, defendants argue that a theory of elder abuse is not available for medical services that an able bodied person would not provide for themselves.  Defendants assert that plaintiff’s allegations show that he relied on Providence the way a person would rely on a hospital as a medical provider, as a person requiring hospitalization, and not as a custodial caregiver attending to basic needs that an able-bodied adult could manage without assistance.  See also Kruthanooch v. Glendale Adventist Medical Center (2022) 83 Cal. App. 5th 1109, 1131 (“We are not persuaded that a hospital necessarily assumes a robust caretaking or custodial relationship and ongoing responsibility for the basic needs of every person admitted.”) (citations omitted).  Defendants further assert that the allegations as to Providence-San Pedro do not suggest a custodial caretaker relationship as plaintiff’s first encounter was on March 10, 2022 at the ER and from August 3-9, 2022 when he was malnourished and where he received a swallowing evaluation, hydration, antibiotics, and a new Foley catheter.  Also, plaintiff alleges that the pressure injury occurred at Driftwood.  Defendants further contend that plaintiff’s allegations refer to Providence hospitals’ acts or omissions as a hospital providing professional medical services, “the sort of conduct triggering more conventional tort liability for medical negligence.”  Also, defendants assert, plaintiff allegations are improperly alleged generally against all the defendants.  Defendants also point out that there are no allegations as to Providence-Torrance.  Moreover, defendants argue, there are no allegations of willful misconduct.

            There is no opposition.

            The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action for elder abuse against the Providence defendants for the reasons argued by defendants.  The allegations are insufficient that Providence defendants were custodial caretakers of plaintiff.  Rather, plaintiff alleges that Providence negligently provided medical services.  There are also no allegations of willful misconduct as to the Providence defendants.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd cause of action.

            Motion to Strike

            The Providence defendants request that the court strike the 2nd cause of action for elder abuse/willful misconduct (paras. 59-135), allegations of punitive damages and attorney fees in the 2nd cause of action (paras. 94, 113, 121-122, 134), and prayer for relief at 1. For attorney’s fees and 4. For punitive and exemplary damages.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).  In order to be entitled to the heightened remedies set forth in the Elder Abuse Act, Welfare & Institutions Code §15657, the plaintiff must establish “recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of this abuse” by “clear and convincing evidence.”

Defendants argue that the 2nd cause of action fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for elder abuse against them.  Defendants further argue that plaintiff has not alleged facts showing oppression, fraud, or malice.  Defendants also contend that because the allegations arise from alleged professional negligence, a claim for punitive damages cannot be asserted without prior permission from the court pursuant to CCP §425.13.  Defendants further assert that because plaintiff has not alleged sufficiently a cause of action for elder abuse, plaintiffs are not entitled to attorney’s fees.

In light of the ruling on the demurrer as to the 2nd cause of action, the motion is GRANTED.

Moving defendants are ordered to give notice of ruling.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. Williams (1961) 193 Cal. App. 2d 636, 643-44.
