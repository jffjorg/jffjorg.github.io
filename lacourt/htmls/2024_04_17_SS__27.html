Hearing Date:                4/17/2024 at 1:30 p.m. 

Case No./Name.:         18STCV00734 ADI AKE LOPEZ, et al. vs THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA., et al. 

Motion:                              MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

Moving Party:                 Plaintiff’s Counsel Martinian & Associates

Responding Party:      Unopposed

Notice:                                Sufficient

 

Ruling:                               MOTION TO BE RELIEVED IS GRANTED.

 

Background

 

Martinian & Associates, Inc (Martinian) currently represents Plaintiff Lucila Ake Iuit. Martinian moves to be relieved as counsel, citing a breakdown in the attorney-client communication.

 

Legal Standard

 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.)

 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).)

¿ 

Analysis and Conclusion

 

Martinian has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Martinian seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client communication. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).) Although Martinian has not confirmed that Plaintiff's mailing address is current, counsel has made reasonable efforts to do so. Specifically, Plaintiff's counsel has attempted to contact Plaintiff using the phone number and address Plaintiff provided. For several months, Plaintiff's counsel has made numerous attempts to locate Plaintiff, including online searches related to social media profiles and other online identification resources. Additionally, counsel was unable to reach any emergency contacts provided. Plaintiff's counsel even employed an investigator to locate the client, but these efforts were unsuccessful.

 

The Court notes that the next hearing is an FSC set for 5/21/24, which should be sufficient time for Plaintiff to retain new counsel. Alternatively, if Plaintiff or new counsel need additional time to prepare for trial, sufficient time exists for Plaintiff to take action to seek a continuance of the trial. Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the Motion to be Relieved, effective upon Notice on Plaintiff of this order at his last known address.

Ruling:                    MOTION FOR LEAVE TO OBTAIN ADDITIONAL PHYSICAL EXAMINATION OF PLAINTIFF MIREYA LACAYOAND DAVID LACAYO IS GRANTED.

 

Legal standard

In any case in which a plaintiff is seeking recovery for personal injuries, any defendant may demand one physical examination of the plaintiff where: (1) the examination does not include any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive; and (2) the examination is conducted at a location within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220, subd. (a).)¿ A defendant may make a demand for physical examination without leave of the court after that defendant has been served or has appeared (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220, subd. (b)), and the physical examination demanded shall be scheduled for a date at least 30 days after service (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.220, subd. (d)).

 

¿If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with¿Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).)¿ The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under¿Section 2032.310¿only for good cause shown.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).)

 

“(a) The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under Section 2032.310 only for good cause shown.... (d) An order granting a physical or mental examination shall specify the person or persons who may perform the examination, as well as the time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.” (Id., § 2032.430, subds. (a), (d).)  Generally, to find “good cause,” the court requires a showing of both: (1) relevancy to the subject matter of the litigation; and (2) specific facts justifying the discovery, e.g., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining the information elsewhere. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal. 3d 833, 840.)

Analysis

On March 20, 2020, Plaintiffs David Lacayo and Mireya Macayo filed the present complaint, alleging various injuries, including spinal injuries, as a result of a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs contend that they continue to experience ongoing pain in their neck, back, shoulder, arm, and chest due to the accident. In June 2021, Defendant conducted the first independent medical examination (IME) on Plaintiffs. Recently, Defendant learned that Plaintiffs underwent spinal surgeries in February and March of 2024. In light of this new information, Defendant now moves the Court for leave to conduct a second IME on Plaintiffs.

Plaintiffs have attributed various injuries to their spines resulting from the incident [See Plaintiffs’ Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set No. One), Nos. 6.1-6.2.] Plaintiffs do not dispute that Plaintiffs’ spinal injuries, or their spinal surgeries are at issue. Relevancy to the subject matter of the litigation has been established.

Plaintiffs contend that having undergone one prior independent medical examination (IME) in June 2021, Defendant is now barred from conducting a second IME. However, under 2032.310(a), a second IME may be held if good cause is shown.

Plaintiffs do not dispute that they each recently underwent major cervical spine surgery for their claimed continuing injuries in February and March 2024. Each Plaintiff received artificial disc replacements at two levels of their cervical spine. Furthermore, Plaintiffs do not contest the substantial costs associated with these surgeries. Mrs. Lacayo has identified at least $281,596.63 in costs for her surgery, and Mr. Lacayo has estimated his surgery costs at $275,000.00. The Court finds that an examination to assess the success of the treatments is crucial to verify the current state of Plaintiffs’ health and recovery progress, determine the direct impact of the surgeries on the injuries claimed to be caused by the incident, and to assess what. If any, future surgeries or treatments are required. These are all information Defendant needs for his defense regarding Plaintiffs’ claims for damages.

In the Opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant is using the term "Independent Medical Examination" while bringing forth a motion under CCP 2032; however, the terminology used, whether it is "independent medical examination," "defense medical examination," or "physical examination," has no legal bearing on whether or not to grant the medical examination. Plaintiff's sole argument against the existence of good cause is that compelling Plaintiffs to submit to further examinations based solely on their decision to undergo surgery would unfairly penalize them for prioritizing their health and well-being. Plaintiffs’ argument does not challenge Defendant's contention that the information sought is necessary, which is what the Court considers when determining good cause. Furthermore, Plaintiffs fail to articulate how requiring them to undergo a non-invasive examination unfairly punishes them. Furthermore, Plaintiffs do not dispute the scope or any other aspects of the IME request.

Accordingly, the present motion is GRANTED.

Ruling:                               PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR TRIAL PREFERENCE IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

A party who is over 70 years old may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings: (1) the party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole; and (2) the health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (a).)¿ An affidavit submitted in support of a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36 may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 36.5.)

 

“Upon the granting of such a motion for preference, the court shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date and there shall be no continuance beyond 120 days from the granting of the motion for preference except for physical disability of a party or a party’s attorney, or upon a showing of good cause stated in the record.¿ Any continuance shall be for no more than 15 days and no more than one continuance for physical disability may be granted to any party.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 36, subd. (f).)

 

“The issue under subdivision (a) is not whether an elderly litigant might die before trial or become so disabled that she might as well be absent when trial is called.¿ Provided there is evidence that the party involved is over 70, all subdivision (a) requires is a showing that the party’s “health . . . is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing [his/her] interest in the litigation.¿ (Italics added.)”¿ (Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534.)¿ The absence of specifics regarding plaintiff’s prognosis is insufficient reason to deny a request for calendar preference.¿ “Subdivision (a) requires the granting of calendar preference to ‘prevent’ prejudice to a stricken litigant’s interests.¿ (Italics added.)¿ The idea that [plaintiff] should be made to wait to file a preference motion until she is clearly in her final days—when attendance at a trial is hardly what she should be doing—makes no sense at all.”¿ (Id. at p. 536.)

 

“Where a party meets the requisite standard for calendar preference under subdivision (a), preference must be granted.¿ No weighing of interests is involved.”¿ (Fox, supra, 21 Cal.App.5th at p. 535; see also Koch-Ash v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 689, 692 [“section 36, subdivision (a) . . . is mandatory and absolute in its application and does not allow a trial court to exercise the inherent or statutory general administrative authority it would otherwise have”].)¿ Any inconvenience to the court or to other litigants is irrelevant and “[f]ailure to complete discovery or other pretrial matters does not affect the absolute substantive right to trial preference for those litigants who qualify for preference.”¿ (Swaithes v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1082, 1085.)

 

Analysis and Conclusion

 

On July 1, 2020, Plaintiff filed the current case, alleging that she fell from a portable ramp at the entrance of her home. The trial is scheduled for August 20, 2024. Defendant has now moved the court for trial preference, requesting a trial date within 120 days from today. Plaintiff, aged 73, states in her declaration that she has been suffering from 35 health conditions since 2012. She requires constant oxygen support and finds it extremely difficult to speak for extended periods without needing to rest, cough, or struggle for breath. Her health conditions has continued to steadily decline. While currently capable of participating in her lawsuit, she expresses concern that her deteriorating health may soon prevent her from doing so.

Plaintiff meets all three criteria for trial preference under Section 36(a): first, she is over the age of 70; second, she holds a substantial interest in the action as the Plaintiff; third, considering her advanced age and worsening health conditions that impact her ability to breathe and speak, a trial preference is necessary to prevent prejudice to her interests before her condition deteriorates further.

Defendant National Seating & Mobility filed an opposition arguing that Plaintiff should have filed the motion for trial preference when she turned 70 in June of 2020 and the present motion should be denied due to Plaintiff's delay in filing. The Court is not persuaded as Plaintiff’s declaration points out, she did not file the motion until now because she had been healthy enough to participate in her lawsuit. Although she is currently able to participate, her steadily declining health has raised concerns that she may soon be unable to do so. Additionally, the Defendant argues that Plaintiff has had these health conditions since 2012, which was 12 years ago and presents no evidence that she is currently suffering from these conditions. However, the Court notes that a majority of these conditions are chronic illnesses that are not curable but rather worsen over time, such as diabetes and chronic asthma. Defendant also argues that the current trial date set for August 20, 2024 is already near the requested trial preference date and that granting the motion would only advance the trial date by several days. Nevertheless, the court recognizes that trial dates are often postponed by the court or the parties, and the motion for trial preference is intended to ensure that the trial date is firmly set.

Defendant Kaiser filed a limited opposition requesting that the trial date be set after its motion for summary judgment, scheduled for July 18, 2024.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has fulfilled all the requirements of CCP 36(a) and thus GRANTS the present motion. The trial date is maintained for August 20, 2024, with preference.

Legal Standard

 

Continuances of trials are disfavored, but each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).)¿ The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1332(c).) The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial.¿ (See generally CRC Rule 3.1332(d)(1)-(11).)¿ Additional factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1332(c), (d).)

 

Factual Background and Analysis

 

On June 13, 2022, Plaintiff filed the present trip and fall case. On June 20, 2023, Defendants Douglas Westhoff and Pamela Westhoff filed their answer. On October 5, 2023, Defendant Kerry Welch filed her answer. The current trial date is set for June 10, 2024. Defendants now move the Court to continue the trial date by three months, arguing that they need more time to conduct discovery. Plaintiff did not file an opposition alleging prejudice.

 

Given that Defendants filed their answer on June 20, 2023, and October 5, 2023, their request for additional time to conduct discovery appears reasonable. Considering the age of the case, the absence of multiple prior continuances, the lack of opposition alleging prejudice, and the request for a short continuance of only three months, the Court grants Defendants' motion to continue the trial date.

Moving Party:                 Non-Party Geraldine McIntee and Defendant Federal Insurance Company

Responding Party:      Plaintiff

¿ 

Ruling:                               MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA, MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER, AND REQQUEST FOR SANCTIONS ARE DENIED.

 

Legal Standard

 

1.  Motion to Quash

 

CCP section 1987.1 states that “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1(a).) “In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (Id.) Subdivision (b) provides that a party may make a motion pursuant to subdivision (a). (Id., § 1987.1(b).)

 

As a general rule, all unprivileged information that is relevant to the subject matter of the action is discoverable if it would itself be admissible evidence at trial or if it appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010; Schnabel v. Superior Court. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 711.) When the information sought to be discovered impacts a person’s constitutional right to privacy, limited protections come into play for that person. (Shaffer v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 993, 999.) The protections cover both a person’s personal and financial matters. (Id.) The court must balance competing rights — the right of a litigant to discover relevant facts and the right of an individual to maintain reasonable privacy — in determining whether the information is discoverable. (Id.)

 

For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement. (Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)

 

When a subpoena has been issued requiring the attendance of a witness or the production of documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion “reasonably made” by the party, the witness, or any consumer whose personal records are sought, or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms and conditions as the court may specify. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1; Southern Pac. Co. v. Superior Court (1940) 15 Cal. 2d 206.)

 

2.  Protective Order

“In accordance with the liberal policies underlying the discovery procedures, California courts have been broad-minded in determining whether discovery is reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 172; Pettie v. Superior Court (1960) 178 Cal.App.2d 680, 687.) As a practical matter, it is difficult to define at the discovery stage what evidence will be relevant at trial. Therefore, the party seeking discovery is entitled to substantial leeway. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co., supra, 2 Cal.3d at p. 172.) Furthermore, California’s liberal approach to permissible discovery generally has led the courts to resolve any doubt in favor of permitting discovery. (Id. at p. 173.) In doing so, the courts have taken the view that if an error is made in ruling on a discovery motion, it is better that it be made in favor of granting discovery of the nondiscoverable rather than denying discovery of information vital to preparation or presentation of the party's case or to efficacious settlement of the dispute.” (Norton v. Superior Court (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1750, 1761.)

 

Where good cause is shown, courts may enter protective orders limiting depositions. “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420(b).) Courts have considerable discretion in granting and crafting protective orders. (Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 588.) A court may quash a subpoena entirely or partially, and issue an order to protect parties, witnesses or consumers from unreasonable or oppressive demands including violations of privacy. (Code Civ. Proc., §1987.1; Weil & Brown, Civ. Pro. Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2008) ¶ 8:598. See also generally Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Trials & Ev. (The Rutter Group 2008) ¶1:91.)

 

A court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.090, subd. (b).) The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks. (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

3.  Procedural Requirements for Consumer Records 

 

Subpoenas which seek consumer records, including personal financial information, must adhere to additional procedural requirements laid out in CCP § 1985.3. The subpoena must first be served on the consumer at least five days before it is served on the nonparty from whom records are sought (with a five-day extension if served by mail, pursuant to CCP § 1013). (Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3(b)(3).) A subpoena must be served on the custodian of records at least fifteen days before the date upon which records are to be produced. (Id., §§ 1985.3(d), 2020.410(c).) Therefore, as a practical matter, the subpoena must be personally served on the consumer at least twenty days before the date upon which records are to be produced. This is extended by an additional five days if service on the consumer is made by mail. (Id., § 1985.3(b)(3).) 

 

4.  Meet and Confer

 

Both motions for a protective order and motions to quash must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. California Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.410(c) provides that a motion to quash a deposition notice must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. A motion for a protective order “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.090.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)

 

Background

On August 24, 2022, Plaintiff Jane Kim filed the present personal injury case alleging that she was injured at Defendant Papilion, Inc.’s restaurant, Caffe Concerto, where an outdoor propane heater struck Plaintiff in the head. Prior to initiating this lawsuit, Plaintiff's counsel spoke with non-party Geri McIntee ("McIntee"), Defendant’s insurance adjuster, regarding this matter. (Plf’s Ex. 1.) McIntee informed Plaintiff's counsel that the insured possesses the video of the incident, but she had not received a copy. (Id.) She requested the video, but it was not supplied to her. (Id.) Further, she stated that she had no authority to provide a copy of the video as it is her insured’s internal documentation. (Id.) Subsequently, Defendant, in its discovery response, stated that no such video or footage of the incident exists. On March 2, 2024, Plaintiff served McIntee with a deposition subpoena for a deposition scheduled for April 29, 2024, due to the contradictory responses received from McIntee and Defendant’s discovery responses. Defendant now moves the court to quash Plaintiff’s subpoena or seek a protective order.

Subpoena for Document Production

The subpoena served on McIntee requests her to produce the following documents at the deposition:

Any and all documents, notes, memorandum, correspondence (to the extent correspondence contains privileged information, the correspondence may be redacted to conceal any privileged information) regarding any and all photos or videos depicting any area of Caffe Concerto, located at 610 South Serrano Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90005, at any time on November 25, 2021. Any and all photos or videos depicting any area of Caffe Concerto, located at 610 South Serrano A venue, Los Angeles, California 90005, at any time on November 25, 2021.

Meet and Confer

Both the motion to quash and the motion for a protective order require the Defendant to file a declaration demonstrating that a meet and confer attempt was made. The court reviewed the Party's declaration and found that Defendant has met this requirement.

Analysis

The main thrust of Defendant's argument is that employees who have gained knowledge during the course of employment cannot testify about this knowledge. Defendant contends that Plaintiff must either serve notice to the custodian of records for documents or depose Defendant’s person most knowledgeable (PMK); however, Defendant failed to provide any legal basis for this contention. Defendant references C.C.P. §§ 2025.230 and 2020.310(e), but these sections pertain to the deposition of an organization or corporation. Plaintiff did not serve a deposition on a corporation but rather on McIntee in her individual capacity. The burden is on the Defendant, as the moving party, to convince the court why it should either quash the subpoena or issue a protective order. Defendant has not provided any legitimate legal basis for why a party cannot issue a deposition subpoena to an employee to testify based on her personal knowledge acquired during the scope of her employment.

Defendant argues that in verified discovery responses, it has already stated that no video of the incident exists and therefore there is no need to depose McIntee. However, this contradiction between Defendant’s verified responses and correspondence between McIntee and Plaintiff's counsel is what prompted Plaintiff to seek a deposition in the first place. Plaintiff is certainly entitled to question McIntee about the video to obtain information reasonably calculated to lead to discovery of the video of the incident or to obtain information regarding the issue of spoliation of evidence. Plaintiff stated that the Parties considered resolving the issue through a declaration from McIntee instead of conducting her deposition. However, Defendant failed to produce such a declaration and opted instead to file the current motion to quash.

1.  Service

Defendant then argues that the deposition subpoena was not properly served; however, Plaintiff attached a proof of service to her opposition showing that McIntee was served with the subpoena on March 2, 2024 (Plf’s Ex. 5). Defendant's next argument that Plaintiff failed to provide notice to Defendant Papilion, Inc. as required under CCP 1985.3, when seeking a consumer’s personal records from an insurance company, is also unconvincing. Defendant correctly notes that "Personal records" include originals, copies of books, documents, other writings, or electronically stored information pertaining to a consumer and maintained by any "witness," including insurance companies as specified in CCP 1985.3(a)(1); However, the subsequent section clearly defines a "consumer" as any individual, partnership of five or fewer persons, association, or trust that has transacted business with or used the services of the witness. (CCP 1985.3(a)(2).) This definition of a consumer does not include corporations. Therefore, the insured, Defendant Papilion, Inc. does not constitute a consumer, and documents pertaining to Papilion, including video of the incident, do not require a consumer notice under CCP 1985.3.

2.  Subpoena for Production of Documents

Furthermore, the court finds that the requested documents are relevant and not overly broad, as they are narrowly tailored to include only video or footage of the date of the incident and any communications relating to that footage. California’s liberal approach to permissible discovery generally has led the courts to resolve any doubt in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.) Defendant put forward several other objections that the court does not find persuasive. The objection that the materials are Not Within Non-Party McIntee’s Possession, Custody, or Control is not valid; the deponent can state so in her responses. Defendant also objects that the Insurance Information and Privacy Protection Act bars disclosure of the insured’s file; however, Insurance Code § 791.13 expressly allows for disclosure if “in response to a facially valid administrative or judicial order, including a search warrant or subpoena.” However, the court acknowledges some merit in Defendant’s argument that the request for production calls for documents protected by privilege and privacy rights. To the extent that certain documents are withheld on the basis of privilege and privacy rights, Defendant is required to produce a privilege log.

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to quash and for a protective order is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. McIntee is required to sit for her oral deposition on the noticed date. Additionally, McIntee must comply with Plaintiff’s subpoena for the production of documents and produce all non-privileged and non-private responsive documents at the time of the deposition. Should McIntee withhold any documents on the date of her deposition based on claims of privilege or privacy, she is required to produce a privilege log detailing the basis for such withholding. Sanctions are DENIED as Plaintiff acted with substantial justification.

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”¿ Amendment may be allowed at any time before or after commencement of trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) “[T]he court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings. The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428 (internal citations omitted).) “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend . . .”¿ (Morgan v. Sup. Ct. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) The type of prejudice the court is to be concerned with should be something beyond simply having to cope with a potentially successful new legal theory of recovery that has been revealed during discovery. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486.) Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.” (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)

 

DISCUSSION and CONCLUSION

 

On December 28, 2022, Plaintiffs filed the present wrongful death action, alleging that Defendants Susan and Gregory Owen failed to stop at a red light and crashed into Plaintiffs’ vehicle. Plaintiffs recently learned through Defendants’ criminal proceedings that they were working during the road trip and under the scope of employment for OWEN, PATTERSON & OWEN, LLP and GREGORY JAMES OWEN, PLC during the incident. Based on this information, Plaintiffs now move the court to amend their Complaint to include a claim for vicarious liability against the employers.

 

This case is still in its early stages, and Plaintiffs did not act with undue delay in seeking the amendment. During the Defendants' criminal trial, Plaintiffs discovered that Defendants were working during the road trip and potentially within the scope of employment of OWEN, PATTERSON & OWEN, LLP and GREGORY JAMES OWEN, PLC at the time of the incident. Plaintiffs then timely filed the present motion to amend their complaint to include a claim for vicarious liability against the employers. No opposition alleging prejudice has been filed by the Defendants. In light of the state’s longstanding and extremely liberal standard for permitting amendments to pleadings, the present motion is hereby granted.

 

The Court hereby orders that the proposed second amended pleading be filed and served within five court days.

Ruling:                              MOTION TO COMPEL SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND FOR SANCTIONS IS GRANTED.

 

Background

On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present auto collision case. On October 10, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant Boris Navarro with his Special Interrogatories, Set One. On December 12, 2023, Plaintiff sent Defendant's counsel, Hanger, Steinberg, Shapiro & Ash, courtesy copies of the discovery requests. From December 2023 to February 2024, the parties were in communication, and Plaintiff granted Defendant several extensions to provide the discovery responses, even allowing Defendant to preserve his objections. However, Defendant failed to provide the discovery response by the final deadline set for February 19, 2024, and Plaintiff now moves the court to compel Defendant to provide discovery responses to Plaintiff’s SROG, Set One.

 

Legal Standard

 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)

 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.¿¿¿¿ 

¿

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)   A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)  

 

Analysis

 

Motion to Compel

 

It is undisputed that Defendant failed to serve an initial response, even one consisting solely of objections, within the statutory timeline. Defendant filed an opposition but did not show that a response was served prior to the hearing date. Therefore, the present motion is thereby GRANTED.

 

Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve timely discovery responses necessitated Plaintiff to file the current motion. (CCP § 2030.290(c).) Defendant's counsel contends that they have not resolved a representation issue with Defendant's carrier and intend to move to be relieved as counsel. However, this does not excuse Defendant’s failure to provide responses for over six months, during most of which time Plaintiff has also been in communication with defense counsel. Defendant has requested sanctions in the amount of $1,250.00, which the Court finds reasonable. Given that Plaintiff’s counsel and Defendant’s counsel have been in communication since December 12, 2023, with Plaintiff agreeing to several extensions and even consenting to preserve Defendant’s objections, Plaintiff has been more than accommodating to Defendant. Thus, under the circumstances, the Court finds that Defendant did not act with substantial justification and GRANTS Plaintiff's request for sanctions. Defendant and his counsel are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $1,250.00 to Plaintiff within 20 days of today’s date.
