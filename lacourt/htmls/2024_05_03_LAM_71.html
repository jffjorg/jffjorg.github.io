DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


            All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDept71@LACourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    

            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  

Case Number: 23STCP03931    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ABP PARCEL 8, LLC, 

 

         vs.

 

MARINA PACIFICA, LLC.

 Case No.:  23STCP03931

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

Petitioner ABP Parcel 8, LLC’s motion for attorneys’ fees and interest is granted. Petitioner’s motion for attorneys’ fees is granted in the reduced total amount of $242,194.00, and interest from the date of the Arbitration Award on October 10, 2023, to the date of the instant hearing on May 3, 2024, in the amount of $159,688.59, plus $775.19 each day thereafter. 

 

Petitioner ABP Parcel 8, LLC (“ABP”) (“Petitioner”) moves for an order awarding it attorneys’ fees and interest against Respondent Marina Pacifica, LLC (“MP”) (“Respondent”) as the prevailing party in the instant case by way of the Court granting Petitioner’s petition to confirm the arbitration award (“Award”) and denial of Respondent’s cross-petition to vacate (“March 29 Ruling”).  (Notice of Motion, pgs. 1-2; Civ. Code §1717.)  Petitioner moves on the basis (1) there is a contractual prevailing party attorney’s fees provision as declared in the Award and March 29 Ruling; (2) Petitioner was awarded prevailing party attorney’s fees and costs in the Award; and (3) the court ruled that Petitioner may move for attorney’s fees and interest in this case.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.)

 

Background

On October 25, 2023, Petitioner filed its Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award, and later on October 27, 2023, Petitioner filed its Notice of Petition and Hearing on Petition.  On November 3, 2023, Respondent filed its opposition to Petitioner’s petition, as well as its cross-petition to vacate the contractual arbitration award.  On December 15, 2023, Petitioner filed its reply to its petition and opposition to Respondent’s cross-petition.  Respondent filed its reply to its cross-petition on January 26, 2024.

On February 28, 2024, this Court heard argument on parties’ motions and took the matters under submission.  On March 29, 2024, this Court granted Petitioner’s petition and denied Respondent’s cross-petition.  (3/29/24 Minute Order (Ruling on Submitted Matter).)

On April 8, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant motion.   On April 15, 2024, Petitioner filed its Memorandum of Costs.  On April 22, 2024, Respondent filed its opposition.  On April 26, 2024, Petitioner filed its reply.

 

Discussion

Civil Code §1717(a) provides, in part:

In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce the contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.

 

(Civ. Code §1717(a).)

A party who obtains an “unqualified victory” on a contract dispute is considered the prevailing party for purposes of Civil Code §1717 as a matter of law.  (Hsu v. Abbara (1995) 9 Cal.4th 863, 876-877.)  Additionally, the phrase “prevailing party” is understood to include,

[T]he party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant. If any party recovers other than monetary relief and in situations other than as specified, the ‘prevailing party’ shall be as determined by the court, and under those circumstances, the court, in its discretion, may allow costs or not and, if allowed, may apportion costs between the parties on the same or adverse sides pursuant to rules adopted under Section 1034. 

 

(C.C.P. §1032(a)(4), emphasis added.)

Here, the Award provides, “Paragraph 23 of both Ground Leases contain a provision for attorney’s fees to be awarded to the prevailing party. (Exhs. 8, 15) This is confirmed in the written term sheet and release agreement entered into August 31, 2021, and is consistent with JAMS Rule 24(f).”  (10/25/23 Petition, Attach. 8(c) [“Award”] at pg. 9.)  Paragraph 23 declares: “Should either party hereto institute any action or proceeding in court to enforce any provision hereof or for damages by reason of an alleged breach of any provision of this Lease, except as may otherwise be specified in this Lease, the prevailing party shall be entitled to receive from the losing party such amount as the court may adjudge to be reasonable attorneys’ fees . . . in such action or proceeding.”

The arbitration order awarding fees and costs states:

Paragraph 23 of the Ground Leases says: [quoting]. [¶] ABP argues that this language means that fees are available either “to enforce any provision hereof” or “for damages by reason of an alleged breach of any provision of this Lease,” and that “enforcement” of a provision of contract includes declaratory relief. See Cussler v. Crusader Ent., LLC (2012) 212 Cal. App. 4th 356, 365-366 (provision granting either party the right to recover attorneys’ fees in the event of litigation ‘on the contract’ includes declaratory relief).

 

ABP’s reading of Paragraph 23 is reasonable. Its reasonableness is borne out by the fact that MP also prayed for an award of attorney’s fees and costs under the same language. MP further argues that ABP did not include a request for fees in its baseball statement, and, after having successfully urged the Arbitrator to rule that only a single baseball statement could be adopted, ABP cannot now be heard to seek relief it did not include there. ABP counters that this case is not an “amount of money damages” baseball choice under JAMS Rule 33(a), and therefore, under JAMS Rule 33(d), the provisions of JAMS Rule 24 apply. JAMS Rule 24(g) provides that the Arbitrator may allocate attorneys’ fees if provided by the parties’ agreement or allowed by applicable law. ABP’s reading of the JAMS Rules is correct, and, since the parties’ agreement provides for fees, ABP, as the prevailing party, is entitled to fees.

 

(Decl. of Bertzyk ¶4, Exh. 2 at pgs. 2-3.)

The foregoing is determinative and applies equally to awarding fees for this petition case.  Arbitration awards have res judicata and collateral estoppel effect. (See, e.g., JPV I L.P. v. Koetting (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 172, 192 [“[I]t is well settled that an arbitration award may provide the basis for issue preclusion.”].)

Further, under Civil Code §1717, “[i]f any party recovers other than monetary relief and in situations other than as specified, the ‘prevailing party’ shall be as determined by the court.”  (C.C.P. §1032(a)(4).)  Here, the Court determined Petitioner is the prevailing party.  (3/29/24 Minute Order (Ruling on Submitted Matter) at pg. 10.)

Accordingly, ABP is the undisputed prevailing party in this action and is entitled to an award of attorneys’ fees.

 

Reasonable Fees

To calculate a lodestar amount, the Court must first determine the reasonableness of the hourly rates sought by Petitioner’s counsel. The Supreme Court of California has concluded that a reasonable hourly lodestar rate is the prevailing rate for private attorneys “conducting non-contingent litigation of the same type.”  (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133, emphasis added.)

Petitioner’s Counsel declares the following hourly rates for attorneys who worked on this case based on the JAMS Rate approved by Judge Elizabeth A. White (Ret.) during the arbitration: (1) Erika Arambula ($260.00/hour in 2023, $290.00/hour in 2024); (2) Timothy Baumann ($400.00/hour); (3) Yvette Etnire ($275.00/hour); (4) Jules L. Kabat ($800.00/hour); (5) Alexandra Loew ($90.00/hour and $225.00 in 2023, and $100.00/hour in 2024); (6) Nate D. Meyer ($550.00/hour); (7) Don Moody ($125.00/hour in 2023, $135.00/hour in 2024); (8) Matthew Rips ($650.00/hour); and (9) KeiAna Sanderlin ($75.00/hour).  (Decl. of Kabat ¶8, Exh. B.)  Petitioner has sufficiently demonstrated its counsel’s hourly rates are reasonable in their community of practice in their specialized area of law.  (Decl. of Kabat ¶¶4-8, Exh. B.)

Respondent challenges Petitioner’s counsel’s hourly rates.  (Opposition, pgs. 8-9.)  Petitioner’s counsel’s declarations of work, hours, fees, etc. are entitled to a presumption of credibility, including respecting an attorney’s professional judgment as to whether time spent was reasonably appropriate to the matter.  (See, e.g., Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396 [“We think the verified time statements of the attorney as officers of the court are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”].)  Therefore, the Court determines Petitioner’s counsel’s hourly rates are reasonable.

 

Billed Hours

The party seeking fees and costs bears the burden to show “the fees incurred were allowable, were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, and were reasonable in amount.”  (Nightingale v. Hyundai Motor America (1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 99, 104.)

In this case, the declarations and billing records provided by Petitioner’s counsel are sufficient to meet the burden of proving the reasonableness of the claimed fees in terms of amounts and tasks. To satisfy this burden, evidence and descriptions of billable tasks must be presented in sufficient detail, enabling the court to evaluate whether the case was overstaffed, the time attorneys spent on specific claims, and the reasonableness of the hours expended.  (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 486-487.)

Petitioner’s counsel’s fee recovery is based on the 464 hours spent litigating this case.  (Decl. of Kabat ¶8, Exh. B.)  Respondent argues Plaintiffs’ counsel’s billed hours were overbilled.  (Opposition, pgs. 3-8.)  The Court has reviewed Respondent’s objections to Petitioner’s counsel’s billed hours finds them to be unavailing.  A losing party cannot litigate aggressively only to complain too much work addressed its aggressiveness or dispute that concomitant fees should be fully compensatory, including for obtaining the fees award.  (See, e.g., Peak-Las Positas Partners v. Bollag (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 101, 114 [“A defendant cannot litigate tenaciously and then be heard to complain about the time necessarily spent by the plaintiff in response, in disputing the amount of a contractual attorney fee award.”].)

          Respondent objects to Petitioner’s hours billed on three grounds.  First, Respondent’s objection to Petitioner billing $61,308.00 in fees for

“checking boxes and writing less than 10 sentences,” on its form petition to confirm the arbitration award is not supported by legal argument and does not meet the requisite specificity to challenge Petitioner’s fees.  Therefore, Respondent failed to meet its burden to challenge Petitioner’s fees on its motion to confirm the arbitration award.

          Second and Third, Respondent’s objections to Petitioner’s 12/20/23 time entry by Nathan D. Meyer and 12/21/23 time entry by Matthew A. Rips are unavailing.  Petitioner submitted a supplemental declaration clarifying that the times billed were to review Respondent’s notice of related cases.  (Supp.-Decl. of Kabat ¶8.)

Accordingly, Petitioner’s requested fees are granted in the requested amount of $195,235.

 

Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

Petitioner’s counsel requests an additional $46,959.00 in fees, or 73.40 hours, for oral argument at the hearing on the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award and on the instant motion for attorney’s fees: (1) 51.70 hours and $13,925 fees preparing for and conducting oral argument on the petition; and 38.10 hours and $22,115 fees through filing this motion.  (Reply, pg. 11; Supp.-Decl. of Kabat ¶5, Exh, A.)  Petitioner estimates that preparing for, appearing in court and conducting the instant hearing will entail at least another 15 hours by Kabat and Meyer.  (Reply, pg. 11.)  Anticipated fees are not fees reasonably incurred, and accordingly, the Court will not grant the anticipated 15 additional hours, or $20,250, requested.  Accordingly, the Court grants Petitioner the reduced total of $46,959.00 in fees incurred on the instant motion and for oral argument on the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award.

 

Interest

Like judgments, arbitration awards impose res judicata and collateral estoppel certainty: “it is well settled that an arbitration award may provide the basis for issue preclusion.”  (JPV I L.P., 88 Cal.App.5th at pg. 192.)  The judicial council petition to affirm an arbitration award states interest is properly sought at “the statutory rate.”  (See Petition §10(d).)

Respected as having the legal consequence of judgments, arbitration awards should bear C.C.P. §685.010 “statutory rate” 10% interest.  Arbitration awards are not mere sum certain contracts limited to Civil Code §3287 7% interest.  The Award was made on October 10, 2023.  (See Petition §8a, cross-petition to

vacate §8a.)  The Award gave $2,829,433.78 to ABP.  From that date to the date of the instant hearing on May 3, 2024, is 206 days, resulting in interest at the rate of 10% of $159,688.59, plus $775.19 each day thereafter.

          Accordingly, Petitioners are entitled to interest of $159,688.59 to the date of this hearing on May 3, 2024, plus $775.19 each day thereafter.

 

Conclusion

Accordingly, Petitioner’s motion for attorneys’ fees is granted in the reduced total amount of $242,194.00, and interest from the date of the Arbitration Award on October 10, 2023, to the date of the instant hearing on May 3, 2024, in the amount of $159,688.59, plus $775.19 each day thereafter.

Case Number: 23STCV05798    Hearing Date: May 3, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

GLENN WRIGHT, et al., 

 

         vs.

 

EXODUS RECOVERY, INC., et al.

 Case No.:  23STCV05798

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

Defendant William Wirshing, M.D.’s unopposed motion to compel Non-party James P. Hall, D.O. to comply with a deposition subpoena for the production of business records is granted.

 

Defendant William Wirshing, M.D., (“Wirshing”) (“Defendant”) moves unopposed for this Court to compel Non-party James P. Hall, D.O. (“Dr. Hall”) to comply with his Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records on the grounds that the records sought pertain to Decedent Le’Wayne Anthony Wright’s (“Decedent”) mental state which is at issue in this lawsuit.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§1987.1, 2025.480, 56.10, 56.05.)

Wirshing moves in the alternative to compel Plaintiffs Glenn Wright (“Glenn”) Nina Woolfolk (“Nina”) (“Plaintiffs”) as successors-in-interest to Decedent to sign authorization for release of records relative to the mental condition and treatment of Decedent.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.)

 

Background

          This matter arises out of Decedent’s death by suicide and Plaintiffs’ allegation that Defendants should have prevented Decedent’s death.  As part of plaintiff Glenn’s response to special interrogatories, Dr. Hall was identified as a psychiatrist who provided care to Decedent during the ten years prior to death.

(Decl. of Pruett ¶3, Exh. 1.) 

Defendant issued a subpoena to Dr. Hall on or about January 17, 2024, for records to be produced on February 16, 2024.  (Decl. of Pruett ¶4, Exh. 2.)   The subpoena was served to the custodian of records for Dr. Hall at the location of 5816 Corporate Avenue, #170, Cypress, CA 90630.  (Decl. of Pruett ¶4, Exh. 2.)   On February 13, 2024, Dr. Hall objected to the subpoena on the basis the subpoena seeks confidential and privileged psychiatric records which are protected from disclosure absent a Court order or patient authorization.  (Decl. of Pruett ¶5, Exh. 3.)

The subpoena requested the following documents:

Any and all medical records, documents, medical reports, including doctor’s entries, nurses’ charts, progress reports, physical therapy records, pathology reports, all films, original x-ray films, MRI’s, CT scans including any films/images that may be stored digitally/electronically, x-ray reports, lab reports, case history, emergency room records, admitting sheets, special tests, inpatient and outpatient records, laboratory test/results, original color photographs, videos/dvds of all clinical lab procedures, referrals, prescriptions, medications, and any sign-in sheets pertaining to the care and treatment, diagnosis, prognosis, condition, discharge, insurance records, billing records, billing ledgers, and statement of charges rendered, and any payments made, including but not limited to any records/documents/correspondence that may be stored digitally and/or electronically, including all patient orders and patient results and specifically for any discharge orders, all detailed screen shots within any computer system, electronic records, purged records, and all electronic correspondence affecting or relating to Le’Wayne Wright AKA: Le’Wayne A. Wrights, Le’Wayne Anthony Wright, DOB: 4/11/1994, SS#: XXX-XX-0381, including but not limited to those pertaining to psychiatric treatment, psychiatric rehabilitation, psychological treatment, psychological rehabilitation, treatment for alcoholism, drug addition, depression, mental instability, mental disease or emotional instability.

 

(Decl. of Pruett ¶4, Exh. 2.)

          The subject subpoena was properly issued by attorneys for Defendant, as permitted under C.C.P. §2020.210(b): “Instead of a court-issued deposition subpoena, an attorney of record for any party may sign and issue a deposition subpoena.”  Subpoenas may be used to compel a California nonparty’s production of documents.  (C.C.P. §§2020.010(a)(3), 2025.280(b); Terry v. SLICO (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 352, 357.)  A deposition subpoena may require a witness to produce records for copying.  (C.C.P. §2020.510.)

          C.C.P. §56.10 provides: “A provider of health care, a health care service plan, or a contractor shall disclose medical information if the disclosure is compelled by any of the following: [¶] (1) A court order. . .. [¶] (3) A party to a proceeding before a court or administrative agency pursuant to a subpoena, subpoena duces tecum, notice to appear served pursuant to Section 1987 of the Code of Civil Procedure, or any provision authorizing discovery in a proceeding before a court.”  (C.C.P. §56.10.)

C.C.P. §56.05(j) defines “‘Medical Information’ means any individually identifiable information, in electronic or physical form, in possession of or derived from a provider of health care, health care service plan, pharmaceutical company, or contractor regarding a patient’s medical history, mental health application information, reproductive or sexual health application information, mental or physical condition, or treatment. ‘Individually identifiable’ means that the medical information includes or contains any element of personal identifying information sufficient to allow identification of the individual, such as the patient's name, address, electronic mail address, telephone number, or social security number, or other information that, alone or in combination with other publicly available information, reveals the identity of the individual.”  (C.C.P. §56.05(j), emphasis added.)

The subpoenaing party may seek a court order mandating the non-party’s compliance with the subpoena.  (C.C.P. §2025.480; Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 132-133.)  Additionally, C.C.P. §1987.1 provides, in relevant part, “if a subpoena requires the . . . production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court . . . or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b) . . . may make an order . . . directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare.”  (C.C.P. §1987.1.)

Here, Dr. Hall objected to Defendant’s subpoena based on privacy concerns.  Plaintiffs have not asserted any objections to the subject subpoenas. Defendant has exhausted all legal avenues to obtain the requested records from Dr. Hall and the Plaintiffs have failed to cooperate or provide a valid legal basis for withholding the records by not responding to Defendant’s requests for a signed authorization form to authorize Dr. Hall to release the medical and psychiatric-psychological records.

Although medical and mental health information is generally privileged, the privilege gives way when the medical or mental health issues are presented as the issues in litigation, as here.  Defendant’s subpoena seeks records pertaining to the Decedent’s mental state and health attributed to the incident giving rise to the lawsuit. Litigation seeks to ascertain the truth and this discovery is needed to pursue the truth relative to the nature of the decedent’s mental illness that ultimately led to his suicide. Therefore, such records are well within the scope of permissible discovery.  (C.C.P. §2017.010.)

 

Conclusion

Defendant’s unopposed motion to compel Dr. Hall to comply with Defendant’s Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records is granted.

Moving Party is to give notice of this ruling.
