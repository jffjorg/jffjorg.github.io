DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 22STCV14135    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: 56

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

CENTER INT’L INVESTMENTS, INC., a California Corporation,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

CITY OF MONTEREY PARK, a Municipal Corporation, DOES 1-20,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  22STCV14135

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

Date: October 2, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

            The operative Third Amended Complaint asserts the following causes of action: (1) Declaratory Relief; (2) Breach of Contract – Development Agreement; (3) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing – Development Agreement; (4) Breach of Contract – Settlement Agreement; (5) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing – Settlement Agreement; (6) Declaratory Relief Re Ability to Foreclose on Deeds of Trust; (7) Declaratory Relief Re Liens Against the Property.

             

            On August 13, 2024, Sheppard Mullin Richter & Hampton LLP (“SMRH”), counsel for Plaintiff Center Int’l Investments, Inc. (“CII”), filed the instant Motion to be Relieved as Counsel (the “Motion”), seeking to withdraw as counsel for CII.  On September 18, 2024, Defendant City of Monterey Park (“City”) filed a Response to Plaintiff Center Int’l Investment’s Counsel’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel and Request for Dismissal for Failure to Engage in Mediation per Court’s Minute Order (“Response”).  SMRH filed a reply on September 24, 2024.

 

            The case has not been set for trial.

 

DISCUSSION

The court has discretion on whether to allow an attorney to withdraw, and a motion to withdraw will not be granted where withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)  Withdrawal is generally permitted unless there is a compelling reason to continue the representation.  (Heple v. Kluge (1951) 104 Cal.App.2d 461, 462.)

 

The Motion is compliant with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.  In connection with the Motion, SMRH declares that “[a] complete break down in the attorney client relationship has occurred and resulted in the inability for Sheppard Mullin to effectively participate in this case. This forces Sheppard Mullin to seek withdrawal.”

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

SALVADOR LOYA, an individual,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

BAKEMARK USA LLC, a Delaware limited

liability company; DANIEL BARRIOS, an

individual; and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  22STCV28228

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

Date: October 2, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant DANIEL BARRIOS (“Barrios”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff SALVADOR LOYA (“Plaintiff”)

 

            The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

             This case arises from an employer-employee relationship.  Plaintiff alleges that he was hired by Defendant BAKEMARK USA LLC (“Bakemark”) in July 2019 as a worker in its Dry Manufacturing Department.  (Complaint, ¶ 12.)  Plaintiff claims that throughout his employment with Bakemark, he was subjected to severe and pervasive harassment and discrimination from Barrios, his Foreman and Supervisor.  (Id., ¶¶ 13-16.)  Plaintiff further alleges that after reporting and complaining about the discrimination and harassment he faced, Barrios, in retaliation, fabricated reasons to complain about Plaintiff.  (Id., ¶¶ 17-19.)  Plaintiff’s employment was terminated in August 2021.  (Id., ¶ 21.)  Based on these allegations, Plaintiff filed a complaint (“Complaint”) on August 30, 2022, asserting the following causes of action: (1) National Origin Discrimination in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940 et seq.; (2) Age Discrimination in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940 et seq.; (3) Retaliation in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(h); (4) Harassment in Violation of Gov. Code § 129400); (5) Failure to Prevent Discrimination and Harassment in Violation of Gov. Code § 12940(k); (6) Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code § 1102. 5; (7) Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy; (8) Unfair Competition in Violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200 et seq.; and (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.  Only the fourth and ninth causes of action are asserted against Barrios.

 

            On May 7, 2024, Barrios filed the instant Motion for Summary Adjudication (the “Motion”) on Plaintiff’s fourth and ninth causes of action for harassment and intentional infliction of emotional distress.  Barrios filed an opposition on September 18, 2024.

 

DISCUSSION

            The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.”  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.)

 

Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion “shall not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Id.; Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”  (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Harassment

The Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”) prohibits harassment, stating in relevant part: “It is an unlawful employment practice. . . .[f]or an employer, … or any other person, because of race, religious creed, color, national origin, ancestry, physical disability, mental disability, medical condition, genetic information, marital status, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, age, sexual orientation, or military veteran status, to harass an employee ...”  (Govt. Code, § 12940(j)(1).)  To state a claim of harassment under FEHA, Plaintiff must show “(1) [he] is a member of a protected class; (2) [he] was subjected to unwelcome harassment; (3) the harassment was based on [his] protected status; (4) the harassment unreasonably interfered with [his] work performance by creating an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment; and (5) defendants are liable for the harassment.” (Galvan v. Dameron Hosp. Assn. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 549, 563.)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleged in the Complaint that he was repeatedly harassed at work by Barrios on the basis of his Mexican national origin and age.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 13, 15-16.)  In his Motion, Barrios contends that the evidence against Barrios amounts only to: (1) Barrios requiring Plaintiff to use a formula for running a mix, which Plaintiff refused and disagreed with, causing Plaintiff to get a written warning from his supervisors (Defendant’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts [“DUMF”] Nos. 17-21.); and (2) Barrios occasionally using rough language with Plaintiff and other co-workers (DUMF Nos. 6-11.)  More specifically, Barrios contends that he “was gruff with everyone – co-workers and supervisors alike.”  (DUMF No. 6.) Indeed, Barrios acknowledges that he called Plaintiff an “old man” (DUMF No. 9), and made fun of Plaintiff’s Mexican descent (DUMF No. 10).  Barrios contends, however, that Barrios’ conduct of which Plaintiff complains consists entirely of personnel management actions for which Barrios cannot be held personally liable.  Barrios further argues that Plaintiff’s allegations are insufficient to establish a severe and pervasive hostile work environment as to rise to the level of actionable harassment.

 

 In his opposition, Plaintiff contends that his firing was pretextual and that his refusal to start the mix without the full formula was justified and in accordance with Bakemark’s policy.  (Opposition, p. 4); Plaintiff’s Statement of Disputed Material Facts [“PDMF”] No. 20.)  Regarding his allegations of harassment against Barrios, Plaintiff presents his testimony during deposition that Barrios told Plaintiff to “Go back to Mexico, I don’t want you here, you old man.”  (Plaintiff’s Additional Material Facts [“PAMF”] Nos. 5- 6.)  Barrios told Plaintiff that he did not want Mexicans near him.  (PAMF No. 5).  Barrios frequently told Plaintiff “You stupid Mexican. Go back to your country. You’re no good here. You’re too old.” (Id.).  In addition to repeatedly making such offensive comments about Plaintiff, Barrios cursed and yelled at Plaintiff due to his national origin and age.  (PAMF No. 7). Barrios harassed Plaintiff on a daily basis, calling him a ‘stupid imbecile’ and ‘motherfucker.’ (Id.).  Plaintiff contends that Barrios’s repeated conduct of derogatory remarks about Plaintiff’s age and Mexican origin are not personnel management decisions, and that Barrios is individually liable for his actions.

 

Indeed, FEHA provides that individual employees may be liable for their own acts of harassment.  (Govt. Code, § 12940(j)(3) [“An employee of an entity subject to this subdivision is personally liable for any harassment prohibited by this section that is perpetrated by the employee, regardless of whether the employer or covered entity knows or should have known of the conduct and fails to take immediate and appropriate corrective action.”].)  The Janken case relied upon by Barrios held that “FEHA does not impose personal liability on individual supervisory employees for age discrimination in making personnel management decisions that are necessary to job performance. Supervisory employees are, however, subject to individual liability for harassment.”  (Janken v. GM Hughes Elecs. (1996) 46 Cal. App. 4th 55; See also, Fiol v. Doellstedt (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 1318, 1331 [“harassing supervisor is personally liable for money damages and the employer is vicariously and strictly liable for the harassment”]; Page v. Superior Ct. (1995) 31 Cal. App. 4th 1206, 1213 [“the policy of deterring and eliminating harassment and retaliation in employment is served by holding a supervisor liable for his own acts which are violative of FEHA in accordance”].)  There is thus no merit to Barrios’ contention that he cannot be held personally liable for harassment.

 

On the issue of whether Barrios’ conduct was so severe as to create a hostile work environment, the Court of Appeal, in the case of Beltran v. Hard Rock Hotel Licensing, Inc., pointed out the clarification that ““[a] single incident of harassing conduct is sufficient to create a triable issue regarding the existence of a hostile work environment if the harassing conduct ... created an intimidating, hostile, or offensive work environment,” and the instruction that “[h]arassment cases are rarely appropriate for disposition on summary judgment.”  (Beltran v. Hard Rock Hotel Licensing, Inc. (2024) 97 Cal. App. 5th 865, 880).   The Court of Appeal went on to explain that older cases requiring “a concerted pattern of harassment of a repeated, routine or a generalized nature” are no longer good law when it comes to determining what conduct creates a hostile work environment in the context of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication.”  (Id., citing Govt. Code § 12923 and CACI No. 2524.)

 

Thus, the Court concludes that Barrios failed to meet his burden to demonstrate that no actionable harassment occurred.  As noted above, there is ample evidence of Barrios’ conduct involving offensive statements pertaining to Plaintiff’s age and national origin, which Barrios does not deny and instead attempts to characterize as his typical “gruff” behavior to everyone – co-workers and supervisors alike.  The evidence is more than sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact as to whether “a reasonable person who was subjected to the harassing conduct would find that the conduct so altered working conditions as to make it more difficult to do the job.”  (CACI No. 2524.)  

 

Accordingly, summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for harassment

is DENIED.

 

Ninth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

To state a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress, a plaintiff must allege “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress; and (3) the defendant’s extreme and outrageous conduct was the actual and proximate cause of the severe emotional distress.”  (Crouch v. Trinity Christian Center of Santa Ana, Inc., (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 994, 1007). 

 

Conduct is outrageous if it is of “such substantial quantity or enduring quality” that an individual in civilized society should not be expected to endure it.  (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227; Cochran v. Cochran (1998) 65 Cal App.4th 488, 494 [stating that the conduct must be “so outrageous in character, and so extreme in degree, as to go beyond all bounds of decency, and to be regarded as atrocious. . .”].)  The pleading must include “specific facts that establish severe emotional distress resulting from [a] defendant’s conduct.”¿ (Michaelian v. State Comp. Ins. Fund (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1093, 1114; see, e.g., Hailey v. California Physicians’ Service (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 452, 474-77 [finding facts supporting IIED claim where plaintiff plead resulting “depression, anxiety, and physical illness,” which manifested as “vomiting, stomach cramps, and diarrhea”]; see also Alcorn v. Anbro Engineering, Inc. (1970) 2 Cal.3d 493, 498 [finding supervisor’s shouting of racial epithets combined with termination without cause or motivation gave rise to IIED claim].

 

Barrios contends that the conduct complained of was not so egregiously outside the realm of civilized conduct as to give rise to actionable intentional infliction of emotional distress (“IIED”).  Instead, Barrios argues that these are annoyances and trivialities common to the workplace and therefore do not give rise to a viable claim for IIED.  Plaintiff, on the other hand, alleged and testified that Barrios’ behavior prevented him from sleeping at night, that he would cry, and that Barrios’ harassment was humiliating, stressful and caused him to feel destroyed.  (PAMF No. 13).

 

Though reasonable people may differ, it is for the jury, subject to the control of the court, to determine whether, in a particular case, a defendant’s conduct has been sufficiently extreme and outrageous to result in liability to plaintiff for the intentional infliction of emotional distress.  (Alcorn, supra, at 495.)  Similarly here, reasonable persons may differ, and as such, it should be for the trier of fact to determine whether Barrios’ conduct has been sufficiently extreme and outrageous. 

 

Accordingly, summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s ninth cause of action for IIED is DENIED.

RULING

            Based on all the foregoing, the MOTION is DENIED.

COLEEN L. JOHNSON,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

SHELDON PARK CONDOMINIUM OWNERS ASSOCIATION, et al.

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  22STCV40550

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR PRELIMINARY INJUNCTION

 

Date: October 2, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant SHELDON PARK CONDOMINIUM OWNERS ASSOCIATION (the “Association”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: None

 

            The Court has considered the moving papers.  No opposition has been filed.  Any opposition was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

BACKGROUND

            This case involves alleged damage to a condominium unit (“Unit 202”) owned by Plaintiff Coleen L. Johnson (“Johnson”) due to a burst pipe in a connected condominium unit (“Unit 302”) directly above Unit 202, which is owned by Defendant Maria B. Rivera Henriquez (“Henriquez”).  On December 28, 2022, Johnson filed the operative Complaint against the Association and Henriquez alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs); (2) negligence; and (3) nuisance.

 

            On September 28, 2023, the Association filed a Motion for Preliminary Injunction seeking an order for access to Unit 302 in order to effect necessary repairs in it by the Association’s contractor, which this Court granted in its Minute Order dated October 23, 2023. 

 

            On August 30, 2024, the Association filed the instant Motion for Preliminary Injunction (the “Motion”), renewing its request for an order for exclusive access to Unit 302 for a period of up to 15 business days in order for the Association’s contractor to make necessary repairs in Unit 302.  No opposition to the Motion has been filed.  

 

DISCUSSION

             “In deciding whether to issue a preliminary injunction, a court must weigh two ‘interrelated’ factors: (1) the likelihood that the moving party will ultimately prevail on the merits and (2) the relative interim harm to the parties from issuance or nonissuance of the injunction. . . . The trial court's determination must be guided by a ‘mix’ of the potential-merit and interim-harm factors; the greater the plaintiff’s showing on one, the less must be shown on the other to support an injunction.”  (Butt v. State of California (1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 677-78 (citations omitted); Ryland Mews Homeowners Assn. v. Munoz (2015) 234 Cal. App. 4th 705.)

 

Generally, “restrictive covenants are construed strictly against the person seeking to enforce them, and any doubt will be resolved in favor of the free use of land.  But it is also true that the ‘intent of the parties and the object of the deed or restriction should govern, giving the

instrument a just and fair interpretation’.”  (Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006)

143 Cal.App.4th 1360, 1377.)

 

Under the CC&Rs, Sections 3 and 4 of Article 6, the owner of one condominium unit must make repairs to their unit when the lack of repairs is causing damage to other units, and further provides that the Association has the authority to obtain access to the unit and make repairs at the unit owner’s expense.  (Complaint, Exh. A.)

 

In its Motion, the Association states that it made multiple efforts to carry out the necessary repairs per the Court's Order dated October 23, 2023, but without success.  The Association cites the following reasons in support of its Motion, along with references to its supporting evidence (Table of Evidence and Supporting Declarations [“TOE”]):

 

1.              Unit 302 owner (who appears to have significant mental health challenges) has allowed access into the Unit to have pictures taken but has refused to allow any repairs to occur (claiming repairs were not necessary) and has been verbally threatening [TOE, Exhs. 6, 15, 18, 19, 22-25];

2.              The (licensed) contractor hired by the SHELDON PARK CONDOMINIUM OWNERS ASSOCIATION (“ASSOCIATION”) has determined it will need up to 15 business days to complete repairs due to the dire conditions of the bathroom shower stall at the Unit [TOE, Exh. 25];

3.              The contractor hired by the ASSOCIATION is highly concerned about the safety of its workers and is not willing to make the repairs unless the Unit owner and her adult son vacate for the 15 business day period of time to allow repairs to occur [TOE, Exh. 25];

4.              The ASSOCIATION has offered compensation to the Unit owner to vacate the Unit for the 15 business day time period so that the Unit owner and her son will have a place to stay while repairs occur; but the Unit owner has so far refused [TOE, Exh. 24];

5.              The only practicable solution at this point is for the Court to Order that exclusive access be granted to the ASSOCIATION's contractor to effect the long needed repairs (with reasonable compensation for alternative living expenses). [TOE, Exhs. 24-26].

 

(Motion, p. 1.)

 

In an analogous case of Ryland Mews Homeowners Assn. v. Munoz, the Court of Appeal held that the trial court acted within its discretion in weighing the interim harm to homeowner and homeowners’ association (HOA) and the trial court’s implied determination that HOA would ultimately prevail on the merits, in issuing preliminary injunction pending litigation, requiring homeowner to put throw rugs on the floor of his unit and to submit a proposal to bring the unit’s floor covering into compliance with the guidelines established by the HOA, where the downstairs neighbors complained of noise from the hardwood floors.  (Ryland Mews Homeowners Assn. v. Munoz (2015) 234 Cal. App. 4th 705.)

 

            Here, there is a restrictive covenant that permits the Association to have access to Henriquez’s unit (Unit 302) if it has caused damage to other units and Henriquez does not resolve the issue.  The restrictive covenant is recorded, which indicates that both parties intended

to be bound by the terms of the CC&Rs.  There is, therefore, a high probability that Johnson will prevail against Henriquez, who, as the evidence demonstrates is subject to the CC&Rs, failed to have repairs done on her own, owns the unit that has caused damage to another unit, and refuses to grant the Association access to conduct the necessary repairs.  Furthermore, it appears that from the declarations and exhibits provided, if the injunction is not issued, Johnson will continue to suffer damage to her own unit.  Johnson has submitted a declaration in support of this Motion.  Additionally, Henriquez has not filed an opposition to rebut the arguments advanced by the Association.

RULING

Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Motion and issues a Mandatory Preliminary Injunction as requested in the Motion.

                        Defendant.                           

 

      CASE NO.:  24STCV09244

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Date: October 2, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

             On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff American Textile Company (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint for unlawful detainer against Defendant Downtown Natural Caregivers, Inc. based on alleged failure to pay rent.  On August 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint for breach of contract.

 

            On August 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint (the “Motion”).  The Motion is unopposed.

 

DISCUSSION

            CCP §473(a)(1) states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, the courts have held that “there is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.”  (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296-97; see also, Ventura v. ABM Industries, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 268) [“Trial courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial where the adverse party will not be prejudiced.”].)

 

Pursuant to CRC 3.1324(a), a motion to amend must: (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered; and (2) state what allegations are proposed to be deleted from or added to the previous pleading and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, such allegations are located.  CRC 3.1324(b) requires a separate declaration that accompanies the motion, stating: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

 

Here, in support of the Motion, Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration stating that: “When the First Amended Complaint was prepared, through mistake and inadvertence, [he] neglected to name the guarantors to the lease in addition to the sublessee.  There are viable guarantors to the lease agreement who should properly be named.”  (Declaration of Fred M. Szkolnik, ¶ 3.)  The declaration identified and stated the proposed changes as between the First Amended Complaint and the Second Amended Complaint.  (Id.)  The Motion also includes a copy of the proposed amended pleading.  Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff has complied with CRC 3.1324. 

 

RULING

            Accordingly, the Motion is GRANTED.  The Second Amended Complaint is to be filed and served within 15 days from the date of this Order.

BARTOM INVESTMENTS, LLC and

BACKGROUND

             On August 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their complaint asserting the following causes of action: (1) declaratory relief; (2) breach of duty of care; (3)           breach of duty of loyalty; (4) aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duty; (5) unjust enrichment; (6) fraud; (7) breach of contract; (8) breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (9) fraudulent transfer; (10) usury; (11) accounting; and (12) constructive trust. 

 

On August 22, 2024, attorneys Solis and Funk filed their respective applications (the “Applications”) to be admitted pro hac vice to represent Defendants Berlekamp Family Investments LLC, David Berlekamp, Persis Berlekamp and Bronwen O’Wril in this action. 

 

The Court has considered the Applications.  No opposition papers were filed.  Any opposition papers were required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b).

 

DISCUSSION

California Rules of Court (“CRC”) rule 9.40 provides that an attorney in good standing in another jurisdiction may apply to appear pro hac vice in this State by way of written application upon due notice to all interested parties, as well as service on the State Bar in San Francisco with payment of a $50 fee, provided that the attorney (a) is not a California resident, (b) does not work in California, and (c) does not perform regular or substantial business, professional or other activities in California.

 

An application for pro hac vice admission must set forth: (1) the applicant attorney’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant attorney has been admitted and dates of admission; (3) a representation that the attorney applicant is a member in good standing in the courts of admission and is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (4) the title of each court and action in which the applicant attorney has appeared pro hac vice in this State in the prior two years; and (5) the name, address and phone number of the active California State Bar member with whom the applicant is associated.  (CRC, rule 9.40(d).)

 

The Court finds that Solis and Funk each have established that they satisfied the requirements to appear as counsel pro hac vice pursuant to CRC, rule 9.40.

 

The Court, therefore, GRANTS the Applications, and each of them.
