Hearing Date: October 24, 2024

Department 54, Judge Maurice A. Leiter

Motion to Compel Compliance with Court Order

Moving Party: Plaintiff Daniel Meza

Responding Party: Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

 

T/R:      PLAINTIFF’S MOTION IS DENIED.

 

THE REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE DENIED.

 

PLAINTIFF TO NOTICE.

 

If the parties wish to submit on the tentative, please email the courtroom at SMCdept54@lacourt.org with notice to opposing counsel (or self-represented party) before 8:00 am on the day of the hearing.

 

The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

               

This is a lemon law action arising out of the purchase of a 2022 Honda Accord manufactured and distributed by Defendant Honda. Plaintiff brings this action for violations of the Song-Beverly Act and fraudulent concealment.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff moves to compel compliance with the Court’s August 2, 2024 order compelling the deposition of Defendant’s person most knowledgeable. Plaintiff represents that the deposition went forward on August 8, 2024, but the PMK, Jennifer Pacheco, was not qualified to testify on several categories, including: (1) reports, surveys, summaries identifying a defect with the Honda Sensing System; (2) advertisements, marketing, and communications regarding the Honda Sensing System; (3) corrective action to address defects with Honda Sensing System; and (4) estimated costs to remove the Honda Sensing System from the US market. Plaintiff also represents that Pacheco produced no documents at the deposition.

 

In opposition, Defendant asserts that Plaintiff failed to question Pacheco properly by focusing on Pacheco’s personal knowledge instead of asking questions relating to general corporate knowledge of the categories at issue. Defendant also asserts that any gaps in Pacheco’s knowledge can be filled with the deposition transcripts of Scott Hunter and Chris Martin, who were deposed as PMKs in another case involving Plaintiff’s counsel’s firm, which Defendant has since provided.

 

The Court finds that Defendant has complied with the Court’s August 2, 2024 order. Defendant provided a person most knowledgeable regarding corporate knowledge on the categories listed. That Pacheco was not personally involved in, for example, the creation of advertisements does not mean she is not qualified to testify as a PMK. Additionally, Defendant has provided deposition transcripts of other witnesses to supplement Pacheco’s deposition. Plaintiff does not identify any specific documents needed that were not produced.

 

Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED. The Court declines to award sanctions to either party.

BACKGROUND

               

This is a lemon law action arising out of the purchase of a 2022 Honda Accord manufactured and distributed by Defendant Honda. Plaintiff brings this action for violations of the Song-Beverly Act and fraudulent concealment.

 

ANALYSIS

 

“The purpose of the law of summary judgment is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties' pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Trial judges are required “to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) Once the defendant has met that burden, “the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Id.)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce “substantial responsive evidence.” (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

Defendant moves for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s cause of action for fraudulent concealment. “The elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Linear Technology Corp. v. Applied Materials, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 115, 131.)

 

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff has no evidence showing Defendant had previous knowledge of the defect, misrepresented the defect, or had a duty to disclose the defect. Defendant also asserts that the claim is barred by the economic loss rule.

 

Defendant cites no evidence to support its separate statement of material facts, instead pointing only to the allegations in the complaint. In its November 3, 2023 ruling on Defendant’s demurrer, the Court held that Plaintiff’s allegations were sufficient to state a claim for fraudulent concealment. The Court also found that the claim was not barred as a matter of law by the economic loss rule, under Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828.

 

To succeed on a motion for summary adjudication, Defendant must present evidence negating one or more elements of the subject cause of action. (See CCP § 437c(b)(1) [“The motion shall be supported by affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice shall or may be taken.”]) If Defendant seeks to show that Plaintiff has no evidence to support his claim, Defendant must either present evidence showing that the allegations in the complaint are untrue or evidence showing Plaintiff does not possess evidence. The latter may be done, for example, by presenting a plaintiff’s factually devoid discovery responses. (See Andrews v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 96, 102-103; Union Bank v. Sup.Ct. (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573.) Defendant has not provided any such evidence, instead focusing only on the allegations in the complaint. Challenging the pleadings, particularly where the Court already has found the pleadings sufficient, does not shift the evidentiary burden on summary adjudication.

 

Defendant has failed to meet its burden to show it is entitled to summary adjudication of the cause of action for fraudulent concealment. Defendant’s motion is DENIED.

If a party fails to provide a timely response to a request for admission, the party waives any objection to the requests.  (C.C.P. § 2033.280(a).)  Moreover, “[t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction….”  (C.C.P. § 2033.280(b).) 

 

Plaintiff moves to deem RFAs admitted against Defendant on the ground that Defendant failed to serve responses to RFAs. Plaintiff served the RFAs on June 25, 2024. Responses were due on July 30, 2024. In opposition, Defendant represents that responses were served on August 27, 2024.

 

As responses have been served, this motion is MOOT. The Court declines to award sanctions as this discovery dispute could have been resolved without court intervention.
