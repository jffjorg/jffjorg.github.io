DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01156     Hearing Date: June 11, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Jubilio Escalera,

 

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

Sergio Miramontes, et al.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

   22VECV01156                  107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Quash Service of Summons

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

On January 1, 2021, plaintiff Jubilio Escalera (“Plaintiff”) executed a credit application and agreement to convert a garage located at 21025 Costanso Street, Woodland Hills, CA 91364 (“21025 Constanso”), into a new accessory dwelling unit (“Project”). (Compl. ¶ 13.) Plaintiff contends that he supplied all the construction materials required to complete the Project and that as of July 28, 2021, defendants Sergio Miramontes (“Sergio”) and his wife, Rosa Miramontes (“Rosa”) (jointly “Defendants”), have failed to make full payment of $35,000.00 due to Plaintiff for the materials required to complete the Project. (Compl. ¶¶ 14,16.)

 

On May 11, 2022, Plaintiff recorded a mechanics lien (“Mechanics Lien”) against the Project in Los Angeles County, California under Instrument No. 20220514463. (Compl. ¶ 17; Exh. A.) On August 11, 2022, Plaintiff, in pro per, initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) for foreclosure of the Mechanics Lien (“First Cause of Action”) against Defendants. (Compl. p. 5.; Exh. A.)

 

On May 2, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to quash service of summons (the “Motion”) along with a request for judicial notice, both of which were served by first class mail on Plaintiff at 7912 Ventura Canyon Avenue, Panorama City, CA 91402-6311.  Plaintiff has not filed an opposition to the Motion. 

 

The Court notes Plaintiff last appeared at the April 9, 2024 OSC, at which time he informed the Court that his counsel had passed away and, due to Plaintiff’s own health problems, he had been delayed in finding new representation.  Plaintiff failed to appear at the May 9, 2024 OSC and CMC.

Defendants’ 5/2/24 request for judicial notice of the entire Court file in this matter and the Court’s Case Information Sheet is denied.  There is no need for the Court to take judicial notice of the case file because the Court can always review the file for the case at hand. 

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

A motion to quash service of summons may be brought on the grounds that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over the defendant. (Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).) “[W]here a defendant properly moves to quash service of summons the burden is on the plaintiff to prove facts requisite to an effective service.” (Sheard v. Superior Court (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 207, 211.)

 

Personal service may be accomplished by personally delivering a copy of the summons and complaint to the person to be served.¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)¿

 

In determining whether a plaintiff exercised reasonable diligence, “[t]he question is simply whether [the plaintiff] took those steps which a reasonable person who truly desired to give notice would have taken under the circumstances.”  (Donel, Inc v. Badalian, (1978) 87 Cal. App. 3d 327, 333.)  “No single formula nor mode of search can be said to constitute due diligence in every case.”  (Id.)

 

Filing a proof of service by a registered process server creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper.¿ (American Express Centurion Bank v. Zara, (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390; Evid. Code, § 647 [“The return of a process server registered pursuant to . . . the Business and Professions Code upon process or notice establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return”].)¿ However, the presumption only arises if the proof of service complies with the statutory requirements regarding such proofs.¿ (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1441-1442.)¿ The method described as “personal service” means service that is accomplished “by personal delivery of a copy of the summons and of the complaint to the person to be served.” (§ 415.10.) If the complaint and summons were personally delivered to, i.e., handed to, defendant then he could be said to have been “personally served.” (Am. Express Centurion Bank v. Zara, 389.)

 

Although the filing of a proof of service by a registered process server creates a rebuttable presumption that service was proper (see Evid. Code § 647), that presumption may be impeached by contrary evidence submitted by a defendant. (See, e.g., Fernandes v. Singh (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 932, 941 p. 6.) 

 

C.C.P. §418.10(a)(1) provides that, “A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion… To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.”

 

If service of summons is not properly and validly effectuated by one or more of the approved methods, California courts lack personal jurisdiction over the defendants, and any judicial actions taken without such service violates principles of fundamental due process and is therefore void. (Cnty. of San Diego v. Gorham (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1231.)

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s complaint offends due process and in turn fails to authorize personal jurisdiction over them because the Complaint and Summons were never effectively served on them. (Motion, p. 4.) Defendants argue that personal service on them at 21025 Constanso was impossible because the unit is unoccupied, and their residence is actually 21027 Constanso Street, Woodland Hills, CA 91364. (Motion, p. 4; Declaration of Sergio Miramontes (“S. Montes Decl.”) ¶¶ 3-5, Exh. A.)  Specifically, Sergio declares that while he and Rosa were at home on the date of the alleged service, no process server or individual presented them with the Summons and Complaint.  (S. Montes Decl. ¶3.)  The Court notes that only Sergio has submitted a declaration supporting his assertion that he was not served with the Summons and Complaint in this action; however, there is no declaration by Rosa asserting she was not served on the alleged date of service. 

 

To further support their claim, Defendants submit evidence that Susanna Turner (“Turner”), a paralegal of their counsel, called the Court on May 24, 2024, and at that time was informed by a Call Center Clerk that no Summons had been issued in the instant matter, Case No.  22VECV01156. (Declaration of Susanna Turner (“Turner Decl.”) ¶¶ 3-4.)  While the truth of the matter of any assertions the Call Center Clerk made to Turner regarding the issuance of a summons in this matter would amount to hearsay, a review of the Case File demonstrates that no Summons has been filed in this action. 

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff’s process server Richard Williams (“Williams”) is not a registered California process server.  (2/22/23 PoS as to Rosa; 2/22/23 PoS as to Sergio.)  As such, the proofs of service themselves do not create a rebuttable presumption that service was proper.  To the extent Plaintiff contends service was properly made, Plaintiff must submit evidence or a declaration of Williams in support of the Proofs of Service.  However, Plaintiff has not opposed the instant motion.  In addition, as discussed above, Defendants submitted evidence Sergio was not served with the summons and complaint in the manner described in the Proof of Service as to him given the incorrect address and given he did not receive service of the document on the alleged date.  Sergio declares Rosa was likewise not served with the summons and complaint in the action and was not located at the service address, which is the address of their then unoccupied ADU.  Finally, the case file for the instant matter does not appear to include an issued filed summons; however, the Court acknowledges that this does not preclude a summons from being issued to Plaintiff for service on Defendants.

 

Plaintiff did not meet his burden of demonstrating facts justifying the existence of jurisdiction over Sergio given Sergio submitted evidence showing he was not served with the summons and complaint and the Proof of Service was not completed by a registered process server.

 

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to Sergio Miramontes. The motion is DENIED as to Rosa Miramontes.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

 

                        

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

I.         Tentative Ruling

 

The motion is granted with leave to amend.

 

II.        Background

 

Brian Whitaker, a disability access “tester” plaintiff, filed this suit against defendant Marcos Lopez on July 28, 2023, alleging violations of California’s Unruh Civil Rights Act and Disabled Persons Act.

 

The Court accepts Plaintiffs’ facts as true for purposes of a pleading challenge, as follows:

 

Plaintiff is quadriplegic and relies on a wheelchair for mobility. (Compl., ¶ 1.) Marcos Lopez owns a restaurant (the Restaurant) at 16435 Vanowen St. in Van Nuys. (Id., ¶¶ 2-3.) On June 17, 2023, Plaintiff went to the Restaurant “with the intention to avail himself of its goods, services, privileges, or advantages”, and intending to “test” its compliance with access laws (Id., ¶ 8.) He discovered there was insufficient toe and knee clearance under the Restaurant’s outdoor dining surfaces. (Id., ¶ 11.)

 

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiff sued. On October 3, 2023, Defendant answered.

 

On April 11, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for judgment on the pleadings.

 

On May 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition.

 

Defendant did not file a timely reply. The motion now comes before the Court.

 

III.      Analysis

 

A. Legal Standard

 

A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings when the “complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against the defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(b)(1) and (c)(1)(B)(ii).) “A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. Presentation of extrinsic evidence is … not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999, citations omitted.)

 

The standard for ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings is essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer: that under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Schabarum v. California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216.) The court “must accept as true all material facts properly pleaded, but does not consider conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or facts that are judicially noticed.” (Stevenson Real Estate Services, Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Services, Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-1220, citing Shea Homes Limited Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254.)

 

B. Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 439 requires a party to meet and confer with its adversary before it moves for judgment on the pleadings.

 

Defendant submits no evidence that he complied with section 439. Nonetheless, his failure to comply is not grounds to deny his motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a)(4).) The Court admonishes Counsel for failure to comply with the Code. But for the sake of efficiency, the Court considers the motion on its merits.

 

C. Evidentiary Matters

 

1. Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests judicial notice of seven other complaints filed by the same Plaintiff, against other business establishments, on equivalent grounds. The Court denies the requests.

 

“There is ... a precondition to the taking of judicial notice in either its mandatory or permissive form—any matter to be judicially noticed must be relevant to a material issue.”  (People ex rel. Lockyer v. Shamrock Foods Co. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 415, 422 fn. 2.) Evidence is relevant when it has “any tendency in reason to prove or disprove any disputed fact ... .” (Evid. Code, § 210, emphasis added.)

 

Plaintiffs’ litigiousness is not in dispute. (See Compl., ¶¶ 24-25.) The proffered matter is not noticeable because it is irrelevant.

 

            2. Evidence Submitted in Opposition

 

Plaintiff submitted a declaration in support of his opposition. This is improper. The declaration will not be considered. (See Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp., supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at p. 999 [judgment on pleadings depends on only on content of pleadings and noticeable matters].)

 

 

            D. Discussion

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff has not pled his claim specifically enough to satisfy Code of Civil Procedure section 425.50. Section 425.50 imposes heightened pleading standards on “tester” plaintiffs, that is, high-frequency litigants who patronize businesses partly with the motive and intent to sue them for ADA violations. Plaintiff admits he is one such litigant. It is undisputed that section 425.50 applies to his complaint.

 

Section 425.50 reads, in relevant part:

 

(a) An allegation of a construction-related accessibility claim in a complaint ... shall state facts sufficient to allow a reasonable person to identify the basis of the violation or violations supporting the claim, including all of the following:

 

(1) A plain language explanation of the specific access barrier or barriers the individual encountered, or by which the individual alleges he or she was deterred, with sufficient information about the location of the alleged barrier to enable a reasonable person to identify the access barrier.

 

(2) The way in which the barrier denied the individual full and equal use or access, or in which it deterred the individual, on each particular occasion.

 

(3) The date or dates of each particular occasion on which the claimant encountered the specific access barrier, or on which he or she was deterred.

 

(4)

(A) Except in complaints that allege physical injury or damage to property, a complaint filed by or on behalf of a high-frequency litigant shall also state all of the following:

 

(i) Whether the complaint is filed by, or on behalf of, a high-frequency litigant.

 

(ii) In the case of a high-frequency litigant who is a plaintiff, the number of complaints alleging a construction-related accessibility claim that the high-frequency litigant has filed during the 12 months prior to filing the complaint.

 

(iii) In the case of a high-frequency litigant who is a plaintiff, the reason the individual was in the geographic area of the defendant's business.

 

(iv) In the case of a high-frequency litigant who is a plaintiff, the reason why the individual desired to access the defendant's business, including the specific commercial, business, personal, social, leisure, recreational, or other purpose.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 425.50(a).)

 

Plaintiff’s complaint does not satisfy section 425.50. He must allege “[t]he way in which the barrier denied [him] full and equal use or access, or in which it deterred [him], on each particular occasion” he visited the Restaurant. (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.50(a)(2).) He must also allege “the reason the individual was in the geographic area of the defendant's business.” (Id., subd. (a)(4)(iii).) He must also allege “the reason why [he] desired to access the defendant's business, including the specific commercial, business, personal, social, leisure, recreational, or other purpose.” (Id., subd. (a)(4)(iv).) He has not alleged facts establishing any of these.

 

Our Legislature added section 425.50 to the Code of Civil Procedure via Senate Bill 1186, introduced and passed during the 2011-2012 Regular Session (SB-1186). The commentary on the bill demonstrates that this complaint is precisely the sort of social ill the Legislature sought to prevent. According to the Assembly Committee on the Judiciary, the Senate Judiciary Committee, and the Assembly Floor Analysis:

 

“This particularly provision [section 425.50] is designed to deter the use of form demand letters and complaints by so-called ‘mill’ attorneys who assert hundreds of the same or nearly identical claims, often by the same client against different businesses.”

 

(SB-1186 Assem. Comm. on Judiciary Report (Aug. 28, 2021) p. 8; see also Sen. Jud. Committee Report (Aug. 31, 2012) [substantially the same language]; Assem. Floor Analysis (Aug. 30, 2012) [same].)

 

Also, as a general matter, California’s “fact pleading” rules require a pleading to apprise an adversary of the factual basis of the plaintiff’s claim, providing information detailed enough for the adversary to prepare a defense. (See Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 719-721.) Defendant must be able to ascertain from the complaint what services Plaintiff intended to avail himself of, or there is no way for Defendant to defend himself against Plaintiff’s allegations.

 

Plaintiff construes Defendant’s argument as one that an ADA plaintiff cannot have standing if he or she sought an establishment’s services while also intending to “test” the establishment’s compliance. All of Plaintiff’s opposition is directed to this point. If this were what Defendant contended, then Defendant’s motion would fail. (See Thurston v. Omni Hotels Mgmt. Corp. (20212) 69 Cal. App. 5th 299, 309 [bona fide intent to avail may be held concurrently with “tester” motive, but must be factually pled].) But it is not Defendant’s argument. Plaintiff has set up and struck down an elaborate straw man.

 

To clarify: the Court does not suggest that Mr. Whitaker cannot state a claim because he is a high-frequency litigant. The law clearly states the contrary. The Court holds that Mr. Whitaker has not stated a claim because his boilerplate complaint runs afoul of section 425.50.

 

It is not a high burden for Plaintiff to provide the barest factual essentials of why he went to the Restaurant and how he was prevented from using its services. He has not done so. The motion is granted with leave to amend.

 

E. Conclusion

 

The motion is granted with leave to amend.
