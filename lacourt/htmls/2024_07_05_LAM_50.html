DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION TO THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT

           

Background

Plaintiff Ricardo Armando Nunez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on June 19, 2020 against Defendant San Martin De Porres Medical Clinic of South Gate, Inc. (“Defendant”).

Plaintiff filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on April 17, 2023, asserting causes of action for (1) FEHA retaliation, (2) violation of Labor Code sections 510, 1194, and 1198, (3) violation of Labor Code section 2802, (4) violation of Labor Code section 226.7 and 512, (5) violation of Labor Code section 226, (6) violation of Labor Code sections 200-204, and (7) violation of Labor Code section 1194.

Defendant now moves for summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for FEHA retaliation. Plaintiff opposes.

The Court notes that Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s separate statement of undisputed material facts lists evidentiary objections to certain facts submitted by Defendant. Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1354, evidentiary objections are to be served and filed separately from the other papers in support of or in opposition to a motion for summary judgment. Plaintiff did not comply with the format requirements of rule 3.1354. Thus, the Court declines to rule on the evidentiary objections.[1]  

Legal Standard

“[A] motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action within an action, one or more affirmative defenses, one or more claims for damages, or one or more issues of duty, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit, that there is no affirmative defense to the cause of action, that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action, that there is no merit to a claim for damages, as specified in Section 3294 of the Civil Code, or that one or more defendants either owed or did not owe a duty to the plaintiff or plaintiffs. A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) If the moving party carries this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Ibid.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) When a defendant seeks summary judgment or summary adjudication, he/she must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established; or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

Discussion

A.    Allegations of the TAC

In the TAC, Plaintiff alleges that in or about 1994, he became employed with Defendant as a janitor, and he remained in this position until he was terminated on or about March 11, 2020. (TAC, ¶ 9.) Plaintiff alleges that he was denied financial compensation for the work he did for defendants, resulting in Plaintiff receiving less than the minimum wage and unpaid overtime. (TAC, ¶ 14.) Plaintiff also alleges that from the year 1994 to 2015, Plaintiff regularly used his own vehicle to go purchase his own cleaning supplies, but these expenses were never reimbursed by defendants. (TAC, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that during the entirety of his employment, he was denied meal and rest breaks. (TAC, ¶ 16.)

Plaintiff alleges that in or about January 2020, Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca told Plaintiff that “you’re too old to do what you need to do” and “if it were up to me, you would have been fired already because you’re old.” (TAC, ¶ 18.) Ana Maria Rodriguez, another one of Plaintiff’s supervisors, told Plaintiff “you are too old.” (TAC, ¶ 18.) In or about early March 2020, Ms. Rodriguez told Plaintiff, “you are getting old, and shouldn’t even be working anymore” and that “it would be best if you went back to your… country and stopped working.” (TAC, ¶ 19.) In or about early March 2020, Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca Ponce, the son of Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca, told Plaintiff, “You’re old, go back to your home country. You’re so old you can barely even stand; you really should stop working here and go back to your home country…” (TAC, ¶ 20.)

In March of 2020, Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca approached Plaintiff and informed him that he needed to find another company to work for by the end of the month. (TAC, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff responded to Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca by stating that he needed to speak to an attorney. (TAC, ¶ 22.) After Plaintiff expressed his intention to speak to an attorney, Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca immediately terminated Plaintiff’s employment. (TAC, ¶ 22.) Plaintiff alleges that he “is informed and believes that he was terminated for expressing his intention to consult an attorney.” (TAC, ¶ 23.) 

B.    First Cause of Action for FEHA Retaliation

“[I]n order to establish a prima facie case of retaliation under the FEHA, a plaintiff must show (1) he or she engaged in a ‘protected activity,’ (2) the employer subjected the employee to an adverse employment action, and (3) a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer’s action.” (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1042.) “The causal link may be established by an inference derived from circumstantial evidence, ‘such as the employer’s knowledge that the [employee] engaged in protected activities and the proximity in time between the protected action and allegedly retaliatory employment decision.’” (Morgan v. Regents of University of California (2000) 88 Cal.App.4th 52, 69.) Protected activity includes opposing “any practices forbidden” under FEHA or filing a complaint, testifying, or assisting in any proceeding under FEHA. (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (h).)

            In the instant motion, Defendant asserts that “Plaintiff has filed a Third Amended Complaint alleging termination due to expressing an intent to seek legal advice on employment rights. Yet, the Plaintiff’s deposition transcript reveals a critical inconsistency – he admits to being terminated before notifying the Defendant about his intention to seek legal advice.” (Mot. at p. 5:6-9.) Defendant asserts that accordingly, there is no merit to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for FEHA retaliation.

            Defendant cites to the following testimony from Plaintiff’s deposition: “Q[.] When Dr. Torreblanca Jr. told you that he was terminating you because you were no good, you are too old, and the virus was coming, how did you respond to him? What did you tell him in response to that? A[.]…I said, ‘Why am I being fired? I did not make any mistake. I was still working well.’ And he told me, ‘You are no good. No, no, and no.’ ‘Then, sir, I am going to complain to my attorney.’” (Lopez Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 2 (Nunez Depo.) at pp. 135:16-136:4.) Defendant also cites to the following testimony from Plaintiff’s deposition: “MR. LOPEZ: Can you read back the question, please. Q[.] And then can you respond to the question. And we’ll keep your objection on the record. (The record was read as follows: ‘So he had already fired you before you threatened to go talk to an attorney; correct?’) THE WITNESS: Yes.” (Lopez Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. 3 (Nunez Depo.) at p. 137:10-19.)

            Defendant also argues that Plaintiff “was terminated for poor work performance.” (Mot. at p. 13:2.) In his supporting declaration, Jose Manuel Torreblanca, MD states, inter alia, that “[i]n or about March 10, 2020 my wife and I made the decision to terminate plaintiff’s independent contractor janitorial services with the clinic. The decision was solely based on plaintiff’s work performance and behavior and for the fear that the clinic personnel/employees and patients would be at an increased risk of contracting Covid 19. The decision had nothing to do with plaintiff’s desire to speak to an attorney…My wife and I informed our son, Dr. Jose Fernando Torreblanca, and gave him the go ahead to terminate the janitorial services provided by Mr. Nunez to the clinic.” (Torreblanca Decl., ¶ 12.)

            Defendant argues that “Plaintiff’s FEHA retaliation claim falters due to the absence of a crucial element – the alleged causal link. The Plaintiff’s admission in the deposition transcript, where termination predates any communication about seeking legal counsel, forms the linchpin of the Defendant’s argument. This, coupled with the Defendant’s assertion of a legitimate, nondiscriminatory basis for the adverse employment action rooted in poor performance and misconduct, renders the retaliation claim baseless.” (Mot. at p. 5:22-27.)

            In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that “the moving party erroneously asserts there was no protected activity and there is no causal link between the activity and the adverse action.” (Opp’n at p. 5:9-10.) Plaintiff cites to the following testimony from the deposition of Jose Torreblanca, MD (aka ‘Jr.’): “Q[.] I’d like to go back to my question, which was what was your discussion with your parents after you told Ricardo that you were ending the working relationship? Well, yeah, I told them that as soon as I said, I can give you until the end of the month, that he said, oh, this -- you can’t do this. I’m going to have to talk to an attorney. And then I told him that I just said, and we can stop having this relationship and services by you, immediately.” (Zambrano Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. C (Torreblanca Depo.) at p. 66:16-25.)

            Plaintiff also cites to the following testimony from the deposition of Jose Torreblanca, MD (aka ‘Jr.’): “Q[.] Okay. And I just want to get a direct understanding of what happened. So you and your parents discuss this, and you came to the consensus that you’re going to give him until the end of one month to finish his services; is that correct? A[.] That was the original intent, yes. Q[.] And then when you had the discussion with Mr. Nunez, telling him that he had till the end of the month, at that point, you decided to end it at that time? A[.] Then I told him please do not return because of his, you know, threat of seeking attorney services. Q[.] And then you went and told your parents that you decided to end Mr. Nunez’s services that day; is that correct? A[.] No. Then I discussed then what happened, and they agreed with him not returning.” (Zambrano Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. C (Torreblanca Depo.) at p. 91:9-25, emphasis added.) As Plaintiff notes, “an employee engages in protected activity under the FEHA when he threatens to file a charge of employment discrimination.” (Iwekaogwu v. City of L.A. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 803, 815.)

            In light of the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of material fact regarding whether “a causal link existed between the protected activity and the employer’s action.” (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc., supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 1042.)

            Lastly, the Court notes that Defendant’s instant motion appears to contain arguments concerning a discrimination cause of action. For instance, Defendant argues that “Plaintiff has not met its [sic] burden in proving all elements for the Age Discrimination cause of action…” (Mot. at p. 15:6-7.) However, the TAC does not allege any cause of action for discrimination. In addition, the Court notes that on March 4, 2022, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication as to the cause of action for discrimination in the SAC. (See March 4, 2022 Order.) Thus, any purported motion for summary adjudication by Defendant concerning the SAC’s discrimination cause of action is moot.

            Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication.

The Court orders Plaintiff to give notice of this Order.

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 ANTELOPE VALLEY SCHOOL TRANSPORTATION AGENCY, et al.,

                        Defendants.

24STCV02860

July 5, 2024

Hearing Time:    2:00 p.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEMURRER OF DEFENDANTS TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT FOR DAMAGES

 

 

Background

On February 2, 2024, Plaintiff Gail Boucher (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Antelope Valley School Transportation Agency (“AVSTA”) and Morris Fuselier III (“Fuselier”) (jointly, “Defendants”). The Complaint alleges cause of action for (1) retaliation for reporting illegal activities (violation of Gov. Code, § 1102.5), (2) retaliation for reporting safety conditions (violation of Labor Code, § 6310), (3) failure to maintain safe work environment (violation of Labor Code §§ 6400, et seq.), (4) negligent supervision, hiring, and retention (violation of California common law), (5) common law assault, and (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress.[1]

The first, second, third, and fourth causes of action of the Complaint are alleged against AVSTA only. The fifth cause of action is alleged against Fuselier only. The sixth cause of action is alleged against both Defendants.

            Defendants now demur to the first, second, third, fourth, and sixth causes of action of the Complaint. Defendants also demur to Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages. Plaintiff opposes.

Request for Judicial Notice

The Court grants Defendants’ request for judicial notice. With respect to Exhibit 2, Defendants cite to Gong v. City of Rosemead (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 363, 376, where the Court

of Appeal noted that “[i]f a plaintiff alleges compliance with the claims presentation requirement, but the public records do not reflect compliance, the governmental entity can request the court to take judicial notice under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c) that the entity’s records do not show compliance.” The Gong Court further noted that “[t]he court may take judicial notice of the filing and contents of a government claim, but not the truth of the claim.” (Id. at p. 368, fn. 1.)

Discussion

A.    Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (¿¿Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f)¿¿.) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams ¿v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2¿¿.) However, “¿¿[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.¿¿” (¿Khoury ¿v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616¿¿.)

 

B.     Allegations of the Complaint

In the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that since approximately 1986 to March 2022, Plaintiff was employed by AVSTA in the positions of “Bus Driver” and “Dispatch.” (Compl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff alleges that she was “subject to hostile treatment from AVSTA’S Chief Executive Officer, [Fuselier], throughout her employment.” (Compl., ¶ 12.)  

“[O]n or around October 2021, [Plaintiff] and two of her colleagues discovered that AVSTA was bidding away scheduled bus runs. While [Plaintiff] and her two colleagues were on their lunch break, they inquired with [Fuselier] about the sudden change in bus runs. [Fuselier] denied any knowledge of such.” (Compl., ¶ 14.) “[Fuselier] returned to the room shortly thereafter and began screaming at [Plaintiff] while pounding and lifting the table in front of her.” (Compl., ¶ 15.)

“At a Board Meeting on or around October 19, 2021, [Plaintiff] gave AVSTA a letter regarding [Fuselier’s] actions. On or around November 2021, [Plaintiff] also submitted signed declarations to AVSTA regarding [Fuselier] and the hostile work environment he created. These complaints included, but were not limited to, [Fuselier’s] threats to fire people, bullying, and use of profanity.” (Compl., ¶ 18.) “Despite these complaints, AVSTA indicated that they could not speak on the matter at the time, and eventually conveyed to [Plaintiff] that [Fuselier] would not be terminated and nothing could be done.” (Compl., ¶ 19.) Plaintiff alleges that “[d]ue to AVSTA and DOES 1 through 50’s inaction regarding the circumstances, [Plaintiff] was forced to resign on or around March 11, 2023.” (Compl., ¶ 20.)

C.     First, Second, Third, Fourth, and Sixth Causes of Action

Defendants assert that “Plaintiff’s first, second, third, fourth and sixth claims against AVSTA are barred since Plaintiff failed to file a timely tort claim pursuant to the Government Tort Claims Act.” (Demurrer at p. 6:22-25.)

 Defendants cite to State of California v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1234, 1237, where the California Supreme Court noted that “[a]s part of the California Tort Claims Act, Government Code section 900 et seq….establishes certain conditions precedent to the filing of a lawsuit against a public entity. As relevant here, a plaintiff must timely file a claim for money or damages with the public entity. (§ 911.2.) The failure to do so bars the plaintiff from bringing suit against that entity. (§ 945.4.) In this case, we consider whether failure to allege facts demonstrating or excusing compliance with this claim presentation requirement subjects a complaint to a general demurrer. We conclude it does.”

“Under [Government Code] section 911.2, ‘[a] claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property…shall be presented as provided in Article 2 (commencing with Section 915) of this chapter not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.’ [Government Code] [s]ection 945.4 then provides that ‘no suit for money or damages may be brought against a public entity on a cause of action for which a claim is required to be presented in accordance with Chapter 1 (commencing with Section 900) and Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 910) of Part 3 of this division until a written claim therefor has been presented to the public entity and has been acted upon by the board, or has been deemed to have been rejected by the board, in accordance with Chapters 1 and 2 of Part 3 of this division.’…Thus, under these statutes, failure to timely present a claim for money or damages to a public entity bars a plaintiff from filing a lawsuit against that entity.” (State of California v. Superior Court, supra, 32 Cal.4th at p. 1239 [emphasis in original]. The California Supreme Court in State of California v. Superior Court “conclude[d] that a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating or excusing compliance with the claim presentation requirement. Otherwise, his complaint is subject to a general demurrer for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Id. at p. 1243.)

Defendants note that the Complaint in this action alleges that “AVSTA is a California public entity…” (Compl., ¶ 6.) The Complaint also alleges that “[p]ursuant to California Labor Code sections 6310, 6311 as well as the holding in Sheridian v. Touchstone Television Productions, LLC (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 508, [Plaintiff] is not required to exhaust administrative remedies when his or her protected activity regarding employee health and safety is at issue. Nonetheless, [Plaintiff] has sent written notice by certified mail to the California

Division of Occupational Safety and Health, [Plaintiff’s] former employers, and the Labor and Workforce Development Agency (LWDA) including specific provisions of the Labor Code that have been violated and facts and theories to support said violations. The same was sent to [Defendants] via certified mail.” (Compl., ¶ 10.)

            In the demurrer, Defendants assert that Plaintiff failed to file a timely tort claim. As set forth above, pursuant to Government Code section 911.2, subdivision (a), “[a] claim relating to a cause of action for death or for injury to person or to personal property or growing crops shall be presented as provided in Article 2 (commencing with Section 915) not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action. A claim relating to any other cause of action shall be presented as provided in Article 2 (commencing with Section 915) not later than one year after the accrual of the cause of action.” Defendants note that here, Plaintiff alleges that she “was forced to resign on or around March 11, 2023.” (Compl., ¶ 20.) Defendants assert that accordingly, Plaintiff’s “tort claim needed to be presented by September 2023.” (Demurrer at p. 9:12-13.)

            Defendants assert that “Plaintiff did not file a tort claim with AVSTA. Instead, she submitted a labor claim on February 2, 2024. Defendants dispute this is a tort claim. Notwithstanding, what Plaintiff submitted in February 2024 was untimely.” (Demurrer at p. 9:13-15.) “Exhibit 2” to Defendants’ request for judicial notice is a letter dated February 2, 2024, with the heading “Re: PAGA CLAIMS NOTICE…Gail Boucher v. Antelope Valley School Transportation Agency, et al.” (Defendants’ RJN, Ex. 2.) The February 2, 2024 letter states, inter alia, that “AVSTA violated Labor Code sections 1102.5, 6310, and 6400 based on the facts set forth below…” (Ibid.) 

            In the opposition, Plaintiff states that she “will not argue against Defendants’ demurrer on Plaintiff’s first, second, third, fourth and sixth Causes of actions against Defendant AVSTA.” (Opp’n at p. 2:9-10.) Thus, Plaintiff does not appear to dispute that AVSTA’s “public records do not reflect [Plaintiff’s] compliance” with the claims presentation requirement. (Gong v. City of Rosemead, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 376.)

            Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains Defendants’ demurer to the first, second, third, fourth, and sixth causes of action as to AVSTA, with leave to amend. Plaintiff states that she “requests that she be granted leave to amend her Complaint.” (Opp’n at p. 2:6-7.)

 

D.    Sixth Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

As set forth above, the sixth cause of action is also alleged against Fuselier. “A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress exists when there is (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. A defendant’s conduct is outrageous when it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

Defendants argue that here, “Plaintiff claims that defendant Fuselier (on one occasion) screamed at her while pounding and lifting a table such that she reasonably believed she was in danger. This conduct alone is insufficient to support her claim that defendant Fuselier’s conduct was extreme and outrageous as a matter of law.” (Demurrer at p. 11:2-5.) In support of this assertion, Defendants cite to Schneider v. TRW, Inc. (9th Cir. 1991) 938 F.2d 986. However, this is a non-binding federal case. In addition, the Court finds that the allegations of the Complaint sufficiently allege “extreme and outrageous conduct” for purposes of the intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action. As noted by Plaintiff, she alleges that “[Fuselier]…began screaming at [Plaintiff] while pounding and lifting the table in front of her. [Fuselier’s] demeanor led [Plaintiff] to reasonably believe that she was in danger.” (Compl., ¶ 15.)

In light of the foregoing, the Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action as to Fuselier.

E.     Punitive Damages Claim

Lastly, Defendants assert that “Plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts to support her claim for punitive damages against Defendant Fuselier.” (Demurrer at p. 11:8-9.) In the Prayer for Relief of the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that she seeks “punitive damages, pursuant to California Civil Code section 3294, in amounts sufficient to punish FUSELIER for the wrongful conduct alleged herein…” (Compl., Prayer for Relief, ¶ 4.)

As an initial matter, the Court notes that “¿¿a demurrer cannot rightfully be sustained to part of a cause of action or to a particular type of damage or remedy.¿¿” (¿Kong ¿v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redevelopment Agency (2002) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1047¿¿; ¿see also ¿PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682 [“A demurrer does not lie to a portion of a cause of action.”]¿¿.) “¿¿[W]hen a substantive defect is clear¿from the face of a complaint…a defendant may attack that portion of the cause of action by filing a motion to strike.¿¿” (PH II, Inc. v. ¿Superior Ct., supra¿, ¿¿33 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1682-1683¿¿.)¿

In light of the foregoing, the Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the first, second, third, fourth, and sixth causes of action as to AVSTA, with leave to amend.

The Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action as to Fuselier. The Court also overrules Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages.

The Court orders Plaintiff to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of this order. If no amended complaint is filed within 20 days of this Order, Fuselier is ordered to file and serve his answer within 30 days of the date of this Order.¿¿In addition, if no amended complaint is filed within 20 days of this Order, the Court orders AVSTA to file and serve a proposed judgment of dismissal within 30 days of the date of this Order as to AVSTA.[2]

Defendants are ordered to give notice of this Order.¿
