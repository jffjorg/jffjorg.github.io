DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV03674    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: 73

12/03/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

GATTI v. CBS BROADCASTING, INC., et al. (19STCV03674)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Brian Dunn (Cochran Firm)

Counsel for Defendants:  Adam Levin (Mitchell Silberberg & Knupp LLP)

 

PETITION TO CONFIRM ARBITRATION AWARD (filed 09/05/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The petition to confirm arbitration award is GRANTED. 

 

Plaintiff is to lodge a proposed judgment consistent with the arbitration award within 10 days.

 

Moving party to give notice. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 4, 2019, Plaintiff Gwendolyn Gatti filed this action against Defendants CBS Broadcasting, Inc. and Kevin Hall.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Discrimination in Violation of Statute (California Government Code § 12490, et. seq.);

2.      Retaliation in Violation of Statute (California Government Code § 12490, et. seq.);

3.      Failure to Prevent Retaliation in Violation of Statute (California Government Code § 12490, et. seq.);

4.      False Imprisonment;

5.      Violation Civil Code §52.1, et. seq.;

6.      Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress;

7.      Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress;

8.      Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy;

9.      Breach of Implied Covenant Not to Terminate without Good Cause; and

10.  Violation of California Business & Professions Code § 17200, et. seq.

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff was employed by Defendant CBS as "Head of Makeup" for television stations CBS2 and KCAL9. Plaintiff’s employment with Defendant CBS began on or about 1999 and ended on or about September 19, 2018 when she was wrongfully terminated by Defendant CBS. Plaintiff alleges that she was retaliated against for complaining about sexual harassment and discriminatory conduct.

 

On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award.

 

On November 13, 2024, Defendants filed a Notice of Non-Opposition to Petition to Confirm Contractual Arbitration Award.

II. DISCUSSION

 

“Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.)  The petition must set forth (1) the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate, (2) the names of the arbitrators, and (3) the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, or attach a copy.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4.)  If such a petition is filed, the court must confirm the award unless it corrects the award and confirms it as corrected, vacates the award, or dismisses the proceeding.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.) Where the petition is served but no response is served and filed, the allegations in the petition are deemed admitted.  (CCP § 1290; Taheri Law Group, A.P.C. v. Sorokurs (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 956, 962.)    

 

Every presumption is in favor of the arbitration award. (See Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. v. United Rubber Workers of America (1959) 168 Cal.App.2d 444, 449.) CCP section 1286.2 provides that “the court shall vacate the award if the court determines any of the following: (1) [t]he award was procured by corruption, fraud or other undue means.” (CCP § 1286.2(a).) The defendant moving for vacation of an arbitration award due to corruption, fraud, or other undue means must demonstrate a nexus between the award and the alleged undue means used to attain it. (See Pour Le Bebe, Inc. v. Guess? Inc. (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 810, 833-34.) An objection to the granting of a motion to confirm an award is equivalent to a motion to vacate. (See Thriftimart, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 202 Cal.App.2d 421, 425-26.) 

 

Here, the petition complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4.  First, the petition sets forth the arbitration provision of the parties’ agreement to arbitrate, specifically that “Any and all controversies, claims or disputes arising out of or related to this Agreement or the interpretation, performance or breach thereof … or any claims arising out of [Gatti's] future employment with KCBS-TV/KCALTV,shall be resolved in an arbitration to be conducted at the Los Angeles office of JAMS, or its successor…” (Petition, p. 2, ¶ 4.) Second, the petition provides the name of the arbitrator, Honorable Terry Friedman (JAMS). (Petition, p. 2, ¶ 6.) Third, the petition sets forth the terms of the Arbitration Award, stating “Gatti is awarded $100,000 on her false imprisonment claim; Gatti failed to prove her other causes of action; CBS is the prevailing party; neither CBS or Gatti is awarded attorneys' fees or costs.” (Petition, p. 2, ¶ 8.)

 

Defendants filed a Notice of Non-Opposition of the Petition. The Court does not have any reason to correct the award, vacate the award, or dismiss the proceedings.

 

The Court GRANTS the Petition and confirms the arbitration award.

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A notice of pendency of action may be filed when a real property claim is alleged in an action.  (C.C.P., § 405.1, 405.2.)  A real property claim means the causes of action, if proven, would affect either: (a) title to or the right to possession of specific real property, or (b) the use of an easement identified in the pleading.  (C.C.P., § 405.4.)¿ A party seeking to remove the notice has two options, either attack the pleading as failing to state a real property claim¿(C.C.P., § 405.31),¿or attack the real property claim as lacking evidentiary merit¿(C.C.P., § 405.32).¿¿ 

 

In determining whether a real property claim has been asserted, the Court engages in a demurrer-like analysis.  (Park 100 Investment Group II, LLC v. Ryan¿(2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 795, 808.) ¿

 

When questioning the evidentiary merit of a claim, the claimant who filed the lis pendens has the burden of proof.  (C.C.P., § 405.30.)  Thus, that claimant, in opposing the motion to expunge the lis pendens, must demonstrate the following: (1) the action affects title to or right of possession of the real property described in the notice; (2) in so far as the said notice is concerned, the party recording the notice has commenced the action for a proper purpose and in good faith; and (3) the probable validity of the real property claim by a preponderance of the evidence.  (Hunting World, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 67, 70; see also C.C.P., §405.30, et seq.)  Only admissible evidence is permitted on the motion.¿ (Burger v. Superior Court¿(1984) 151 Cal.App.3d 1013, 1019.) 

 

B.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of the following: 

1.      The deed of trust recorded on July 2, 2019, in the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office as document number 20190639197, against the real property located at 329 Magnolia Street, Compton, California 90220.

2.      The deeds of trust recorded on July 2, 2019, in the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office as document numbers 20190639198, against the (a) real property located at 534-536 East 19th Street, Long Beach, California 90806 and (b) the real property located at 902 Nicholson Avenue, Wilmington, California 90744.

3.      The September 16, 2021 bankruptcy court order terminating the automatic stay with respect to RJS.

4.      Notice of Pending Action (“Lis Pendens”) recorded in the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office, instrument number 20211285797, on August 23, 2021.

5.      The November 5, 2021 order disqualifying Nick A. Alden and the Alden Law Firm from this case.

6.      The August 23, 2022 Court of Appeal order affirming the November 5, 2021 order to disqualify.

7.      The February 22, 2024 order granting RJS’ Motion for Summary Adjudication.

8.      The Request for Dismissal With Prejudice filed on April 22, 2024 and entered by the Court on April 23, 2024.

10.  The May 29, 2024 order denying Mr. Alden’s ex parte to intervene.

13.  The September 9, 2024 Court of Appeal order granting RJS’ motion to dismiss Mr. Alden’s appeal.

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).)¿¿ 

 

The Court grants Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice.

 

C.      Procedural Deficiencies with the Opposition

 

Plaintiff argues that the opposition filed by Beverly Hills Real Estate Investment LLC (“BHREI”) should be disregarded because (1) it was filed late and (2) it was never served on counsel.

 

Opposition papers are required to be filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b). 

 

Here, the hearing in this matter is set for December 3, 2024. The opposition was due by November 18, 2024. However, BHREI did not file its opposition until November 20, 2024.

 

Nevertheless, a court may properly consider papers that are not timely filed pursuant to CCP section 1005(b) unless “substantial rights” are affected.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d); Code Civ. Proc., § 475.)  The appearance of a party at the hearing of a motion and his or her opposition to the motion on the merits is a waiver of any procedural defects or irregularities in a motion.  (Carlton v. Quint (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 690, 697.)

 

The Court elects to exercise its discretion to consider the untimely opposition. (CRC 3.1300(d).)

 

Plaintiff also argues the opposition should be disregarded because BHREI failed to serve it on Plaintiff. (Reply, p. 3.)

 

Failure to give notice of a motion is not only a violation of the statutory requirements but of due process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005; Jones v. Otero (1984) 156 Cal.App.3d 754, 757.)

 

However, the Court notes that the opposition’s signed proof of service indicates it was served on Plaintiff’s counsel and Plaintiff does not provide any evidence in its reply in support of its assertion that the opposition was not served.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court is not inclined to disregard the opposition and continues its discussion on the merits of the motion.

 

D.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to expunge the Notice of Pendency of Action recorded on August 23, 2021 in the Official Records, Recorder’s Office, Los Angeles County, as document number 20211285797 (“Lis Pendens”), against the real properties commonly known as 329 Magnolia Street, Compton, California 90220 (the “Magnolia Property”), and 534-536 E. 19th Street, Long Beach, California 90806 (the “19th Street Property,” collectively the “Properties”), and any other Notice of Pendency Action that has been recorded on the Properties.

 

Plaintiff asserts that the Lis Pendens must be expunged since there is no “pending action” as the case has been fully settled and dismissed since April 22, 2024. The Court agrees.

 

On February 22, 2024, the Court heard and granted Plaintiff RJS’ Motion for Summary Adjudication (“MSA”) which established that Potrillos owned the Nicholson property and it was therefore subject to RJS’ deed of trust recorded on July 2, 2019. The MSA resolved all factual disputes of RJS’ claims and resulted in summary judgment. (Warrington Decl. ¶ 13, Ex. F.) On April 15, 2024, the parties attended a mediation with Judge Charles Rubin (Ret.) to settle the remaining issues related to the Cross-Complaint. The parties settled the remaining claims pursuant to a written and binding settlement agreement and, as part of the settlement, Potrillos agreed to dismiss with prejudice the Cross-Complaint against RJS. (Warrington Decl. ¶ 14.) On April 22, 2024, Potrillos filed a Request for Dismissal with Prejudice for the entire Cross-Complaint. The Court entered the dismissal on April 23, 2024. (Warrington Decl. ¶ 15, Ex. G.) Therefore, since the case has been dismissed the Lis Pendens must be expunged.

 

In opposition, BHREI contends that it has standing to oppose Plaintiff’s Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens because it owns an interest in property that Plaintiff seeks to foreclose on. First, the Court notes that BHREI fails to provide any evidence in support of its assertion that it has standing as an intervenor.

 

Next, BHREI argues in its opposition that the Plaintiff’s Motion should be denied because Plaintiff lacks standing. BHREI argues that as shown by the certificate of no record from the California Secretary of State, there is no legal entity named RJS Financial Inc., as a California Corporation. (Oppos., p. 8, Ex. A.)

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues that not only has the case already been adjudicated on its merits and dismissed, but that Plaintiff does have standing. Plaintiff argues that RJS is a California registered corporation and while the registration does not have “Inc.” at the end, a corporation may add “inc.” to its name even if it is not included in the original registration. (Reply, p. 4.)

 

Moreover, the Court finds that BHREI has failed to meet its burden. Opposing a motion to expunge the lis pendens must demonstrate the following: (1) the action affects title to or right of possession of the real property described in the notice; (2) in so far as the said notice is concerned, the party recording the notice has commenced the action for a proper purpose and in good faith; and (3) the probable validity of the real property claim by a preponderance of the evidence.  (Hunting World, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 67, 70; see also C.C.P., §405.30, et seq.) Here, BHREI does not provide any argument or evidence in support of these elements. As such, BHREI has failed to meet its burden of demonstrating the probable validity of the real property claims. Further, since the case has been dismissed, the Lis Pendens must be expunged.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

E.      Sanctions

 

The prevailing party on a motion to expunge is entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs in making or opposing the motion unless the other party acted with substantial justification.  (C.C.P., § 405.38.)¿ 

 

Plaintiff seeks attorney’s fees in the amount of $7,980.00 and costs in the amount of $60. Plaintiff attached the declaration of its counsel, Kristina Ross, who attests to spending 16 hours on the motion and an anticipated 5 hours on the opposition and reply, at a rate of $380 an hour. (Ross Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

As the motion is being granted, Plaintiff is entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees and costs. However, since the Motion appears to be relatively straightforward, the Court is inclined to reduce the award of attorney’s fees and costs to the total amount of $3,860.00 (10 hours of work total and $60 filing cost).

 

Therefore, the Court awards attorney’s fees and costs in the total amount of $3,860.00.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Expunging Notice of Pendency of Action is GRANTED. The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against the Defendants in the reduced amount of $3,860.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of the date of this order.

CROSS-COMPLAINANT MONTEBELLO MALL MANAGEMENT PR, LLC’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE FIRST AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT (filed 8/22/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Cross-Complainant Montebello Mall Management PR, LLC’s Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 13, 2021, Plaintiffs Laurie Encinas and Jesus Encinas filed this action against Defendants Hikari Japanese Restaurant, a.k.a. Sake House by Hikari, a.k.a. Hikari Sushi Bar, an unknown entity; UBS Realty Investors, LLC, a Massachusetts Limited Liability Company and Does 1 to 25.

 

On June 15, 2021, Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC (“MTCI”) was named as Doe 1.

 

On November 18, 2021, Montebello Mall Management PR, LLC (“MMMPR”) was named as Doe 6.

 

On September 7, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Notice of Death of Plaintiff Laurie Encinas.

 

On November 30, 2023, a First Amended Complaint was filed by Plaintiffs Jesus Encinas, Priscilla Pamela Chey, Jesus Encinas, Jr., Crystal Laurie Encinas, Jennifer Rose Encinas, and Jennifer Rose Encinas on behalf of the estate of Laurie Encinas.

 

On October 24, 2024, the Court denied Defendant Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

This is an action for premises liability arising from a trip and fall incident. Plaintiffs allege that on March 7, 2021, Laurie Encinas attempted to enter the outdoor dining area of the “Sake House by Hikari Sushi Bar” (hereinafter “Hikari Restaurant”) when she tripped and fell due to a dangerous condition, an abrupt and exposed height differential of approximately 1-inch-high where the concrete walkway and Hikari Restaurant’s outdoor patio entryway threshold met (“subject entryway”). Plaintiffs further allege that Laurie Encinas fell forward and struck her head on an umbrella stand which caused serious head injuries requiring immediate medical intervention. Plaintiffs assert causes of action for (1) Negligence and (2) Premises Liability.

 

On August 22, 2024, Cross-Complainant MMMPR filed the instant Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint, arguing:

·       At the time of the trip and fall incident, MMMPR was the mall manager pursuant to a contract entered into between MTCI and MMMPR on December 28, 2018.

·       The alleged property defect over which Mrs. Encinas tripped was created during JHOON, INC.’s remodel of the space in 2016-2017, prior to MMMPR becoming the manager of the mall.

·       MMMPR filed its cross-complaint for contractual indemnity, equitable indemnity and declaratory relief on May 4, 2022.

·       MMMPR contends that by the terms of the lease between JHOON, INC. and MTCI as well as the management agreement between MMMPR and MTCI that JHOON, INC. and MTCI were to arrange insurance coverage for MMMPR to protect it in instances such as this lawsuit. However, despite several tenders, the insurer for JHOON, INC. stated that there is no coverage for MMMPR under JHOON, INC.’s policy and the insurer for MTCI has never responded to the several tenders. Accordingly, MMMPR contends that JHOON, INC. and MTCI have breached their respective contracts.

·       At the time that MMMPR filed its cross-complaint on May 4, 2022, the tenders to the insurance carriers for JHOON, INC. and MTCI had not been responded to. It is on that basis that MMMPR bring this motion for leave to file a First Amended Cross-Complaint.

·       MMMPR seeks to file a First Amended Cross-Complaint to include causes of action for breach of contract due to the failure of MTCI and JHOON, INC. to procure insurance that protects MMMPR against this lawsuit. Leave to file the First Amended Cross-Complaint is compulsory because it arises out of the same transaction as Plaintiff’s Complaint under Code of Civil Procedure §426.50 and is a “related cause of action” under Code of Civil Procedure §426.10.

 

Defendant Jhoon, Inc. filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The motion is based upon what is now a false representation to the court

·       The motion is not yet ripe

·       The first amended cross-complaint’s allegations do not support breach of contract; and

·       The motion is procedurally defective because it violates a mandatory Rule of Court.

 

No reply was filed.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(a)(1) provides, in relevant part:  “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer.  The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” 

“This discretion should be exercised liberally in favor of amendments, for judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters in the same lawsuit.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1047.)

B.      Procedural Deficiencies

As an initial matter, Cross-Complainant MMMPR’s Motion fails to comply with California Rule of Court 3.1324.

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading shall (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. 

Under CRC Rule 3.1324(b), a separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

Here, MMMPR attached a copy of the proposed First Amended Cross-Complaint. (Motion, Ex. A.) However, the Motion does not state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, nor the allegations that are proposed to be added to the previous pleading and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. In addition, the attached declaration by MMMPR’s counsel does not specify the effect of the amendment and when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered. Thus, MMMPR’s Motion fails to comply with California Rule of Court 3.1324.

Based on the foregoing procedural deficiencies, Cross-Complainant Montebello Mall Management PR, LLC’s Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Cross-Complainant Montebello Mall Management PR, LLC’s Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint is DENIED.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

 “A party may move for summary adjudication of issues, either by itself or as an alternative to summary judgment. If it appears that the proof supports the granting of the motion for summary adjudication as to some but not all the issues involved in the action, or that one or more of the issues raised by a claim is admitted, or that one or more of the issues raised by a defense is conceded, the court shall, by order, specify that those issues are without substantial controversy. Moreover, upon a motion for summary adjudication, the court shall, by written order or oral order recorded verbatim, specify those issues raised by the motion for summary adjudication as to which there exists a material, triable controversy, and shall specifically refer to the evidence which establishes a triable issue of fact regarding each of those issues ....”  (Cal. Code. Civ. Proc. § 437c(f)).   

 

A plaintiff moving for summary judgment/summary adjudication bears the initial burden of showing that “there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Plaintiff's¿initial¿burden¿of proof in moving for summary judgment, however, d[oes] not include disproving any affirmative defenses asserted by defendants.”  (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co.¿(2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564, citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 853 (“Aguilar”) [“summary judgment law in this state no longer requires a¿plaintiff¿moving for summary judgment to disprove any defense asserted by the defendant as well as prove each element of his own cause of action.... All that the¿plaintiff¿need do is to ‘prove[ ] each element of the cause of action’ ”])  Once Plaintiff meets its initial burden of proving each element of the cause of action, “the burden shifts to the defendant “to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action¿or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

As noted in Aguilar, “the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385 [citation and footnote omitted].) 

 

The California Supreme Court has recently confirmed that the purpose of the 1992 and 1993 amendments to the summary judgment statute was “‘to liberalize the granting of [summary judgment] motions.’” (Perry v. Bakewell Hawthorne, LLC (2017) 2 Cal.5th 536, 542.)  It is no longer called a “disfavored” remedy.  “Summary judgment is now seen as a ‘particularly suitable means to test the sufficiency’ of the plaintiff’s or defendant’s case.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Opposing parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts are rendered immaterial.’”].) 

 

Courts “construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.)  “The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)    "[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of] more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

a.       First and Tenth Causes of Action: Intentional Misrepresentation/Fraud and Negligent Misrepresentation

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his Intentional Misrepresentation and Negligent Misrepresentation causes of action.

 

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

“The elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.”  (Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1166.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

The elements of a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation include “[m]isrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, and with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented; ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation by the party to whom it was directed; and resulting damage.”  (Hydro-Mill Co., Inc. v. Hayward, Tilton & Rolapp Ins. Associates, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1145, 1154, cleaned up.)  The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  To properly allege fraud against a corporation, the plaintiff must plead the names of the persons allegedly making the false representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

“California courts have recognized a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, i.e., a duty to communicate accurate information, in two circumstances.  The first situation arises where providing false information poses a risk of and results in physical harm to person or property.  The second situation arises where information is conveyed in a commercial setting for a business purpose.”  (Friedman v. Merck & Co. (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 454, 477.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that Jimmy falsely represented that Plaintiff had transferred his shares of NBC to Jimmy. Plaintiff further alleges that that the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document that allegedly shows Plaintiff transferred his shares to Jimmy, is fraudulent and forged. The 21/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document appears to be meeting minutes of a meeting that took place on January 1st, 2021 at 11am where all six shareholders of NBC were present and signed and agreed to allow Plaintiff to give his 19.4% shares to Jimmy. (PMF 17.) Plaintiff alleges that the income payout distributions that were supposed to be issued to Plaintiff were fraudulently issued to Jimmy. Plaintiff alleges Jimmy has stolen at least $629,417.45 ($552,900 + $76,517.45 from Plaintiff’s 19.4% shares) by issuing Plaintiff’s share of NBC’s distributions to himself.

 

In support, Plaintiff submits the declarations of Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto. Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto state in their declarations that the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document is fraudulent. Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto state that they never attended a meeting on January 1, 2021, that they never agreed to transfer Plaintiff’s shares to Jimmy, and that their signatures on the document are forged. (Pltf’s Ex. E, Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 27-29, Lin Decl. ¶¶ 12-15, Chen Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Zheng Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Szeto Decl. ¶¶ 12-15.)

 

Further, Plaintiff also submits Jimmy’s deposition testimony in which he states he was not in a meeting regarding NBC on January 1, 2021. (Tani Depo. Testimony Day 1, 23:16-19.)

 

In addition, Plaintiff attaches emails between Jimmy and a representative from U.S. Small Business Administration (“SBA”) relating to the Covid relief loan. In an email dated May 15, 2021, Jimmy stated NBC’s ownership was as follows: Lin (41.6%), Jimmy (22.2%), Plaintiff (19.4%), Chen (5.6%), Zheng (5.6%) and Szeto (5.6%). (Pltf’s Ex. H, pp. 3-4.)

 

Plaintiff also attached copies of the K1 Income Payouts of NBC that indicates checks were issued to Jimmy from NBC in excess of his 22.2% share. (Pltf’s Ex. G, Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶ 17.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently carried his initial burden. The burden shifts to Defendants.

 

In opposition, Defendants dispute that Jimmy had fraudulently transferred Plaintiff’s shares of NBC to himself.

 

In support, Defendants submit Jimmy’s declaration in which he states that at the January 1st, 2021 meeting, Plaintiff had transferred his NBC shares to Jimmy by agreement and by board ratification. (Jimmy Decl., ¶ 12.)

 

The Court does not find Jimmy’s self-serving declaration persuasive. There are certain circumstances in which the Court can disregard evidence. For example, a declaration submitted in support of a motion for summary judgment may be disregarded if it contradicts prior discovery responses (D’Amico v. Board of Med. Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21). Another instance is when an opposition is supported by “uncorroborated and self-serving declarations,” which the court found insufficient to create a triable issue of material fact (King v. United Parcel Service, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 426).

 

However, Defendants also submit the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document. The title of the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document states “Minutes of Special Meeting of Stockholders of Golden Partner Global Investment Inc.” (Def’s Ex. C.) The parties do not dispute that Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc. does business as NBC. (UMF 20.) According to the document, the stockholders of NBC had agreed to transfer Plaintiff’s 19.4% ownership to Jimmy. (Def’s Ex. C.)

 

While Plaintiff asserts that the signatures on the document were forged, Defendants submit the expert Declaration of James A. Black, an examiner of questioned documents and handwriting identification. (Black Decl. ¶ 6.) Black states that he “reviewed exemplars of signatures for Kam Choi Lin, Chang Ho Chen, Kent Tran, Wei Lin Zheng, and Wai Hung Szeto. These exemplars included documents with signatures of the individuals noted above which documents were filed with state government agencies and also provided to accounting professionals working on behalf of Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc.” (Black Decl. ¶ 3.) Black opines that the signatures on the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document were written by the same persons as the exemplars he reviewed for those persons. (Black Decl. ¶ 4.)

 

Thus, the Court finds that Defendants’ evidence from its expert raises a triable issue of material fact as to the authenticity of the signatures on the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document. The Court finds that Defendants have carried their burden.

 

Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s first and tenth causes of action for Intentional Misrepresentation and Negligent Misrepresentation is DENIED.

 

b.      Second and Sixteenth Causes of Action: Conversion and Unjust Enrichment

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his Conversion and Unjust Enrichment causes of action.

 

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.”  (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.)

 

“The elements for a claim of unjust enrichment are receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.  The theory of unjust enrichment requires one who acquires a benefit which may not justly be retained, to return either the thing or its equivalent to the aggrieved party so as not to be unjustly enriched.”  (Lyles v. Sangadeo-Patel (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 759, 769, cleaned up.)

 

Plaintiff bases these claims on the allegations that (1) Jimmy unlawfully transferred Plaintiff’s shares of NBC to Jimmy and (2) that Jimmy had forged a $15,000 check from Plaintiff’s personal checking account to Maie JT & KT Development LLC on September 18, 2020. (PMF 52.)

 

As to the allegation that that Jimmy had forged a $15,000 check from Plaintiff’s personal checking account to Jimmy, Plaintiff submits his declaration in support as well as a copy of the allegedly forged check. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶ 70; Pltf’s Ex. L.) Plaintiff states in his declaration that he had not signed the check to Maie JT & KT Development LLC, which Jimmy claims is solely owned by himself and his wife, and that Jimmy had forged Plaintiff’s signature. (Ibid.)

 

As to the allegation that Jimmy unlawfully transferred Plaintiff’s shares of NBC to Jimmy, Plaintiff relies on the same evidence as discussed above. Specifically, the declarations of Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto, Jimmy’s deposition testimony stating he did not attend an NBC meeting on January 1, 2021, the SBA loan documents and the K1 Income Payouts. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 27-29, Lin Decl. ¶¶ 12-15, Chen Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Zheng Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Szeto Decl. ¶¶ 12-15; Tani Depo. 23:16-19; Pltf’s Exs. E, G, H.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has carried his initial burden.

 

However, Defendants have established a triable issue of material fact. As discussed above, Defendants submit an expert declaration who opines that the signatures on the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document were written by the same persons as the exemplars reviewed for those persons. (Black Decl. ¶ 4.) Defendants’ expert also states that he reviewed the check dated September 18, 2020, payable to "Maie JT & KT Development LLC" in the amount of $15,000.00. (Black Decl. ¶ 5.) He further states “Comparing the signature on the check to exemplars of Kent Tran it is my opinion that the signature of Kent Tran on the check was probably written by the same person as the exemplars I reviewed for Kent Tran.” (Black Decl. ¶ 5.)

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Defendants’ evidence from its expert raises a triable issue of material fact as to the authenticity of the signatures on the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document and the check dated September 18, 2020. The Court finds that Defendants have carried their burden.

 

Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s second and sixteenth causes of action for Conversion and Unjust Enrichment is DENIED.

 

c.       Third Cause of Action: Declaratory Relief

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his Declaratory Relief cause of action.

 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.”  (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, cleaned up.)  “[T]here is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved.”  (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366, cleaned up.)

 

According to the SAC, Plaintiff seeks declaratory relief in determining that Plaintiff is the rightful 50% owner of the companies Goodland, JT & KT, and/or MAIE JT & KT. (SAC ¶ 91.) In Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication, he states that seeks to “confirm that [Plaintiff] does in fact own 19.4% of NBC from 2008 to present; to confirm that [Jimmy’s] 2-Page Summer Rolls, Inc. (the document that serves as [Jimmy’s] only proof) “proof” of acquiring [Plaintiff’s] 19.4% shares of NBC is forged, falsified, and even irrelevant, as the 2-Page document clearly states that it is for “Summer Rolls, Inc.”, which is not NBC. (Motion, p. 20.)

 

For the same reasons as discussed above, Plaintiff carries his initial burden as to his allegation that Jimmy unlawfully transferred Plaintiff’s shares of NBC to Jimmy. Plaintiff relies on the same evidence, specifically, the declarations of Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto, Jimmy’s deposition testimony stating he did not attend an NBC meeting on January 1, 2021, the SBA loan documents and the K1 Income Payouts. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 27-29, Lin Decl. ¶¶ 12-15, Chen Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Zheng Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Szeto Decl. ¶¶ 12-15; Tani Depo. 23:16-19; Pltf’s Exs. E, G, H.)

 

However, also as discussed above, Defendants have established a triable issue of material fact. The Court finds that Defendants’ evidence from its expert raises a triable issue of material fact as to the authenticity of the signatures on the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document. While Plaintiff asserts that the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document is also irrelevant because it references another company “Summer Rolls Inc.”, the title of the document states “Minutes of Special Meeting of Stockholders of Golden Partner Global Investment Inc.” (Def’s Ex. C.) The parties do not dispute that Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc. does business as NBC. (UMF 20.) Thus, the document is relevant as it is regarding Golden Partner Global Investment, Inc./NBC and appears that the stockholders of NBC had agreed to transfer Plaintiff’s 19.4% ownership to Jimmy. (Def’s Ex. C.) The Court finds that Defendants have carried their burden.

 

Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for declaratory relief is DENIED.

(Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.30(a).)

e.      Seventh Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his Breach of Fiduciary Duty cause of action.

 

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

 

Plaintiff argues in his Motion for Summary Adjudication that Jimmy owed Plaintiff a fiduciary duty as CEO and CFO of NBC.  (PMF 22.) Plaintiff also contends that Jimmy and Plaintiff were business partners in JT & KT and Maie JT & KT. (PMF 49-53.) Plaintiff argues that Jimmy breached his fiduciary duty by misleading Plaintiff with inaccurate information that Goodland and NBC required funding through loans to keep them afloat (PMF 60) and that Jimmy persuaded Plaintiff to secure personal loans, sign documents, and sell his home under the false belief that it was necessary to rescue the companies. (PMF 58, 60.)

 

In support, Plaintiff submits his declaration. Plaintiff states that Jimmy misinformed him that the companies were losing money and that Plaintiff secured personal loans and sold his home under this mistaken belief. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 56-58.) Plaintiff also states that Jimmy breached the NBC By-Laws by allegedly acquiring his 19.4% shares without anything in exchange and without giving the other NBC shareholders the opportunity to purchase. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶ 55.) Plaintiff also relies on the same evidence as discussed above, specifically, the declarations of Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto that they never agreed to transfer Plaintiff’s share of NBC, Jimmy’s deposition testimony stating he did not attend an NBC meeting on January 1, 2021, the SBA loan documents and the K1 Income Payouts. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 27-29, Lin Decl. ¶¶ 12-15, Chen Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Zheng Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Szeto Decl. ¶¶ 12-15; Tani Depo. 23:16-19; Pltf’s Exs. E, G, H.) The parties do not dispute that Jimmy was CEO and CFO of NBC until April 2023. (UMF 1.) Plaintiff carries his initial burden.

 

In opposition, Jimmy submits his own declaration disputing Plaintiff’s assertions. Defendants also submit the same evidence disputing that Jimmy had unlawfully transferred Plaintiff’s NBC shares, specifically the declaration by Defendants’ expert. The Court finds that Defendants’ evidence raises a triable issue of material fact. The Court finds that Defendants have carried their burden.

 

Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty is DENIED.

 

f.        Eighth Cause of Action: Conspiracy

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his Conspiracy cause of action.

 

“Civil conspiracy is not an independent tort. Instead, it is a legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves, share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration.”  (City of Industry v. City of Fillmore (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 191, 211-12, cleaned up.)  “The elements of a civil conspiracy are (1) the formation of a group of two or more persons who agreed to a common plan or design to commit a tortious act; (2) a wrongful act committed pursuant to the agreement; and (3) resulting damages.”  (Id. at 212.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Jimmy and his wife Yong Sook Kim (“Kim”) engaged in a conspiracy to deprive Plaintiff of his 19.4% NBC shares. (Motion, p. 23.) Plaintiff asserts that according to Jimmy, Kim is the legal co-manager and member of Maie JT & Kt Development LLC, and 50/50 owner. Thus, Kim receives shares or interest in JT & KT LLC and JT & KT Maie without any authorization from Plaintiff.

 

However, the Court does not find that Plaintiff has carried his initial burden. Plaintiff fails to provide evidence of a conspiracy between Jimmy and Kim. Plaintiff’s declaration filed in support only asserts a conclusory allegation that Jimmy conspired with Kim. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶ 4.) A conspiracy requires evidence that each member of the conspiracy acted in concert and came to a mutual understanding to accomplish a common and unlawful plan, and that one or more of them committed an overt act to further it. (IIG Wireless, Inc. v. Yi (2018) 22 Cal. App. 5th 630, 652.) Here, Plaintiff fails to specifically allege how Kim was involved in the conspiracy or provide evidence in support. Thus, Plaintiff fails to carry his initial burden as to this cause of action.

 

Summary Adjudication as to Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for Conspiracy is DENIED.

 

g.       Ninth, Eighteenth and Eleventh Causes of Action: Fraudulent Transfer/Constructive Fraud and Fraud by Concealment

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his Fraudulent Transfer/Constructive Fraud and Fraud by Concealment causes of action.

 

“A fraudulent conveyance claim is set forth in the Uniform Fraudulent Transfer Act (UFTA), which is codified in Civil Code section 3439 et seq. ‘A fraudulent conveyance is a transfer by the debtor of property to a third person undertaken with the intent to prevent a creditor from reaching that interest to satisfy its claim.’ [Citation] A transfer under the UFTA is defined as ‘every mode, direct or indirect, absolute or conditional, voluntary or involuntary, of disposing of or parting with an asset…, and includes payment of money, release, lease, and creation of a lien or other encumbrance.’ [Citation] ‘A transfer of assets made by a debtor is fraudulent as to a creditor, whether the creditor’s claim arose before or after the transfer, if the debtor made the transfer (1) with an actual intent to hinder, delay or defraud any creditor [actual fraudulent transfer], or (2) without receiving reasonably equivalent value in return [constructive fraudulent transfer], and either (a) was engaged in or about to engage in a business or transaction for which the debtor’s assets were unreasonably small, or (b) intended to, or reasonably believed, or reasonably should have believed, that he or she would incur debts beyond his or her ability to pay as they became due. [Citations.]’ [Citations]” (Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.4th 642, 648.) (See also Civil Code §§3439.01, 3439.04, 3439.05.) A cause of action for constructive fraudulent transfer under Civil Code §3439.05 seeks to set a side a transfer during or resulting in insolvency

Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 748.)¿¿¿ 

 

Plaintiff bases these causes of action on the same allegations as discussed above. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 27-29, Lin Decl. ¶¶ 12-15, Chen Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Zheng Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Szeto Decl. ¶¶ 12-15; Tani Depo. 23:16-19; Pltf’s Exs. E, G, H.)

h.      Nineteenth and Twentieth Causes of Action: CA Penal Code Section §496(A) and Cancellation of Instrument Pursuant to Civ. Code § 3412

 

Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication as to his CA Penal Code Section §496(A) and Cancellation of Instrument Pursuant to Civ. Code § 3412 causes of action.

 

Pursuant to California Civil Code § 3412, a written instrument that may cause serious injury to a person against whom it is void or voidable can be adjudged and ordered to be delivered up or canceled upon the application of that person. This provision allows for the cancellation of documents that are fraudulent, forged, or otherwise improperly created or used.

 

Violation of Penal Code Section 496(a) refers to the crime of receiving stolen property in California. It is illegal to knowingly buy, receive, conceal, sell, or withhold stolen property, with knowledge that it has been stolen. In the civil context, a plaintiff can seek damages three times the amount of actual damages sustained, as well as costs and attorney's fees.

 

Similarly here, Plaintiff bases these causes of action on the same allegations as discussed above. Plaintiff alleges that Jimmy engaged in various fraudulent activities, including unlawfully transferring Plaintiff’s NBC shares to Jimmy, forging a check from Plaintiff’s personal account and persuading Plaintiff to secure personal loans, sign documents, and sell his home under the false belief that the companies needed financial assistance.

 

As discussed above, Plaintiff provides sufficient evidence to carry his initial burden, specifically, the declarations of Plaintiff, Lin, Chen, Zheng and Szeto that they never agreed to transfer Plaintiff’s share of NBC, Jimmy’s deposition testimony stating he did not attend an NBC meeting on January 1, 2021, the SBA loan documents and the K1 Income Payouts. (Plaintiff’s Decl. ¶¶ 27-29, Lin Decl. ¶¶ 12-15, Chen Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Zheng Decl. ¶¶ 12-16, Szeto Decl. ¶¶ 12-15; Tani Depo. 23:16-19; Pltf’s Exs. E, G, H.)

 

However, also as discussed above, Defendants submit evidence disputing that Jimmy had unlawfully transferred Plaintiff’s NBC shares, specifically, the declaration by Defendants’ expert. Defendants’ expert raises a triable issue of material fact as to the authenticity of the signatures on the 1/1/21 Meeting Minutes Document and the check dated September 18, 2020.

 

The Court finds that Defendants have carried their burden. Since Defendants’ evidence raises a triable issue of material fact, summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s Nineteenth and Twentieth causes of action for CA Penal Code Section §496(A) and Cancellation of Instrument Pursuant to Civ. Code § 3412 are DENIED.

 

In sum, Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication of the SAC’s first, second, third, fifth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, eleventh, sixteenth, eighteenth, nineteenth and twentieth causes of action is DENIED.
