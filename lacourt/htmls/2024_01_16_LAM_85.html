Case Number: 23STCP01055    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: 85

Mick Mars v. Mötley Crüe Touring, Inc. et al, 23STCP01055

Tentative decision on petition for a writ of mandate: denied as moot; Mars entitled to attorney fees

           

 

            Petitioner Mick Mars (“Mars”) seeks a writ of mandate compelling Respondents Red, White & Crue, Inc. (“RWC”), Cruefest limited liability company (“LLC”) (“Cruefest”), Mötley Records, LLC (“Records”), and Masters 2008, LLC (“Masters 2008”) to produce records.

            The court has read and considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply, and renders the following tentative decision.

           

            A. Statement of the Case

            1. Petition

            On April 4, 2023, Petitioner Mars filed the Petition against Cruefest, Records, Masters 2008 (collectively “Respondent LLCs”), RWC, Mötley Crüe Touring, Inc. (“MCT”), Mötley Crüe, Inc. (“MCI”), and Masters 2000, Inc. (“Masters 2000”).  Mars alleged a cause of action for traditional mandamus under Corporations Code sections 1603(a) and 17704.10(f).  The verified Petition alleges in pertinent part as follows.

            Mars was a founding member, lead guitar player, and backing vocalist for the band Mötley Crüe.  Pet. at ¶¶ 1, 23-26.  He is a 25% member of each of the Respondent entities, through which the Mötley Crüe does business.  Pet. at ¶¶ 5, 9, 14-16.

Nikki Sixx (“Sixx”) is the only Mötley Crüe member besides Mars who has been a member throughout the band’s existence.  Pet. at ¶25.  Sixx often made band decisions without consent or knowledge of the other members.  Pet. at ¶33.  He would send documents with instructions to “sign immediately” and the false assertion that the other band members had already signed their copies.  Pet. at ¶34. 

 

            a. Mars’ Final Tour

             Throughout his professional career, Mars has suffered from Ankylosing Spondylitis (“AS”), which debilitated his body over time.  Pet. at ¶¶ 35-37.  Sixx “gaslighted” Mars by saying that he had some cognitive dysfunction, his guitar playing was subpar, and he was forgetting chords and playing the wrong songs.  Pet. at ¶38.

            In 2022, Mötley Crüe signed an agreement to do a U.S. stadium tour (“Stadium Tour”), followed by a world tour with other artists (“World Tour”).  Pet. at ¶39.  Mars advised management that, because of complications from his AS and the rigors of the road, the Stadium Tour would be his last tour.  Pet. at ¶40.  He would not retire from the Mötley Crüe and would remain available for recording and other band activities.  Pet. at ¶40.  Management replied that it understood and that fans would be informed with dignity.  Pet. at ¶41.

            The Stadium Tour lasted through September 9, 2022, and included triple the planned number of stadiums.  Pet. at ¶¶ 42, 48.  During this tour, Sixx gaslighted Mars more than ever.  Pet. at ¶43.  He repeatedly told Mars that he was playing the wrong chords and that fans were complaining about his playing.  Pet. at ¶43.  Mars did play the wrong chords, but only because his in-ear monitors malfunctioned and kept him from hearing his chords.  Pet. at ¶46.  Sixx’s comments suggest that he plotted to force Mars out of Mötley Crüe and take his shares in the relevant Respondent entities.  Pet. at ¶43.

            Sixx did not play a single note on his bass during the tour.  Pet. at ¶44.  He fist-pumped with his strumming hand the entire time, with recordings replacing his live playing.  Pet. at ¶44.  Significant portions of the other two band members’ parts also were pre-recorded, and some fans noticed that Tommy Lee’s (“Lee”) drum part began while he was still walking to the drum set.  Pet. at ¶45.  Mars received the least online criticism of all the band members for their performances.  Pet. at ¶47.

 

            b. Post-Stadium Tour MCI Meeting

            B. Standard of Review

            A party may seek to set aside an agency decision by petitioning for either a writ of administrative mandamus (CCP §1094.5) or of traditional mandamus.  CCP §1085.   

            “A writ of mandate may be issued by any court to any inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or person, to compel the performance of an act which the law specially enjoins, as a duty resulting from an office, trust, or station, or to compel the admission of a party to the use and enjoyment of a right or office to which the party is entitled, and from which the party is unlawfully precluded by such inferior tribunal, corporation, board, or person.”  CCP §1085(a).

            A traditional writ of mandate under CCP section 1085 is the method of compelling the performance of a legal, ministerial duty.  Pomona Police Officers’ Assn. v. City of Pomona, (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 578, 583-84.  Generally, mandamus will lie when (1) there is no plain, speedy, and adequate alternative remedy, (2) the respondent has a duty to perform, and (3) the petitioner has a clear and beneficial right to performance.  Id. at 584 (internal citations omitted).  Whether a statute imposes a ministerial duty for which mandamus is available, or a mere obligation to perform a discretionary function, is a question of statutory interpretation.  AIDS Healthcare Foundation v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Public Health, (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 693, 701.

            No administrative record is required for traditional mandamus to compel performance of a ministerial duty or as an abuse of discretion.

 

            C. Governing Law 

            1. Shareholder or Director Inspection of Corporate Records

            A corporation shall keep adequate and correct books and records of account and shall keep minutes of the proceedings of its shareholders, board and committees of the board.  Corporations Code §1500.[1]  It shall also keep at its principal office, or the office of its transfer agent or registrar, a record of the names and addresses of all shareholders and the number and class of shares each one holds.  Id.

            The board shall cause an annual report to be sent to the shareholders not later than 120 days after the close of the fiscal year.  §1501(a)(1).  The report shall contain a balance sheet as of the end of that fiscal year; an income statement and a statement of cashflows for that fiscal year; and any report thereon of independent accountants or, if there is none, the certificate of an authorized officer of the corporation that the statements were prepared without audit from the books and records of the corporation.  Id.

            The accounting books, records, and minutes of proceedings of the shareholders, board, and board committees of any domestic corporation shall be open to inspection at the corporation’s principal office in California upon the written demand on the corporation of any shareholder or holder of a voting trust certificate at any reasonable time during usual business hours, for a purpose reasonably related to the holder’s interests as a shareholder or as the holder of a voting trust certificate.  §1601(a)(1).  The inspection may be in person or by agent or attorney.  §1601(b).  The right to inspect includes the right to copy and make extracts.  Id.  This right may not be limited by the articles or bylaws.  Id.

            Every director shall have the absolute right at any reasonable time to inspect and copy all books, records and documents of every kind and to inspect the physical properties of the corporation and any subsidiaries.  §1602.

            Upon refusal of a lawful demand for inspection from a corporation’s member, the superior court of the proper county may enforce the right of inspection with just and proper conditions or may, for good cause shown, appoint one or more competent inspectors or accountants to audit the books and records kept in this state and investigate the property, funds and affairs of any domestic corporation or any foreign corporation keeping records in this state and of any subsidiary corporation thereof, domestic or foreign, keeping records in this state and to report thereon in such manner as the court may direct.  §1603(a). 

            If the court finds the corporation’s failure to comply with a proper demand for inspection was without justification, the court may award an amount sufficient to reimburse the shareholder or holder of a voting trust certificate for reasonable expenses incurred in the action, including attorney’s fees.  §1604.

 

            2. Member Inspection of LLC Records

            All limited liability companies must maintain the following in writing or a form capable of being converted into clearly legible form:

(1)   A current list of the full name and last known business or residence address of each member and of each transferee set forth in alphabetical order, together with the contribution and the share in profits and losses of each member and transferee.

(2)   If the limited liability company is a manager-managed limited liability company, a current list of the full name and business or residence address of each manager.

(3)   A copy of the articles of organization and all amendments thereto, together with any powers of attorney pursuant to which the articles of organization or any amendments thereto were executed.

(4)   Copies of the limited liability company's federal, state, and local income tax or information returns and reports, if any, for the six most recent fiscal years.

(5)   A copy of the limited liability company's operating agreement, if in writing, and any amendments thereto, together with any powers of attorney pursuant to which any written operating agreement or any amendments thereto were executed.

(6)   Copies of the financial statement of the limited liability company, if any, for the six most recent fiscal years.

(7)   The books and records of the limited liability company as they relate to the internal affairs of the limited liability company for at least the current and past four fiscal years.  §17701.13(d).

            A member of a limited liability company has a right, upon reasonable request and for purposes reasonably related to the interest of that person as a member, to inspect and copy certain information and financial statements of the company.  §17704.10(b).  A member has recourse to the courts if the company does not comply with a lawful demand for inspection of corporate records.  §17704.10(f).  The court may also extend the time for compliance with the demand for good cause shown.  Id.  A court may award reasonable expenses including attorneys’ fees against a company where a member must resort to court action as a result of the company’s unjustified failure to comply with a proper demand.  §17704.10(g).

            E. Analysis

Petitioner Mars seeks a writ of mandate compelling Respondents RWC, Cruefest, Records, and Masters 2008[3] to produce records and for an award of attorney fees.

 

1. Mars’ Claims Are Moot

“Although a case may originally present an existing controversy, if before decision it has, through the acts of the parties or other cause, occurring after commencement of the action, lost that essential character, it becomes a moot case or question which will not be considered by the court.”  Wilson v. Los Angeles County Civil Service Com., (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 450, 453; Colony Cove Props., LLC v. City of Carson, (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1509.  "The pivotal question in determining if a case is moot is [] whether the court can grant the plaintiff any effectual relief."  Giles v. Horn, (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 206, 227 (claim that county failed to make required findings to approve contracts rendered moot by contract extensions which were the operative agreements); Eye Dog Foundation v. State Bd. of Guide Dogs for the Blind, (“Eye Dog Foundation”) (1967) 67 Cal. 2d 536, 541.     

A court should not dismiss a case as moot if a substantial issue remains. Terry v. Civil Serv. Comm’n, (1952) 108 Cal. App. 2d 861.  A case is not moot where, despite the happening of a subsequent event, there remain material questions for the court’s determination that impact a party’s future and contingent legal rights.  Eye Dog Foundation, supra, 67 Cal.3d at 541.  In other words, a case is moot only where the disposition of the case is “a matter of indifference to the parties” -- where disposition of the case will neither benefit the plaintiff nor harm the defendant.  Turner v. Markham, (1909) 156 Cal. 68, 69. 

Respondents argue that the documents sought by Mars have been provided or simply do not exist.  As a result, there is no relief the court can provide, and the Petition should be denied as moot.  Opp. at 12.

Mars replies that the case is not moot because production is not complete.  Ledger reports showing 2023 financial activity are readily accessible and available to Respondents.  Only one ledger report has ever been produced for 2023, and that ledger report for RWC has minimal 2023 entries.  The ledger reports for all seven Respondent Entities for the years 2020-2022 were generated in about an hour on February 23, 2023.  Presumably, Respondents do not want to produce full 2023 general ledger entries in order to conceal what happened to the tens of millions of dollars in touring revenue earned through the World Tour after RWC’s touring agreement with Live Nation was secretly assigned to Nu and then backdated to January 25, 2023.  Reply at 10.

Mars’ argument that 2023 ledger reports are missing from production is not well taken.  His Requests are limited in scope by their plain language, which merely asked for each Respondents “general ledger” in the form in which it is kept and maintained.  Ex. 6.  It is unclear whether the Requests, which were dated February 3, 2023, included ledger material generated after that date. 

Mars clarified the scope of the Requests in his opening brief, which responded to Respondents’ November 6, 2023 assertion that they had produced all responsive documents in their possession, custody, and control.  McPherson Decl., ¶17, Ex. 9.  As part of Mars’ opening brief, his counsel provided “[a] complete chart of the documents that remain to be produced by Respondents”.  McPherson Decl., ¶23, Ex. 14.  The 2023 ledger reports are not listed in the chart of missing documents.  Ex. 14.  Respondents addressed Mars’s chart by either producing each of the listed documents or confirming that they did not have the them, in large part because Cruefest, Records, and Masters 2008 no longer conduct business.  Neach Decl., ¶¶ 17, 18, Ex. 4.  Mars cannot now contend that any more documents are missing.  The Petition’s merits are moot.

Mars argues that, even if Respondents mooted the Petition shortly before hearing, the court has discretion to hear the Petition’s merits if it “presents an issue of substantial and continuing public interest.”  Fowler v. Golden Pacific Bancorp, Inc., (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 205, 216 (section 1602 claim heard despite mootness because there is a substantial public interest in determining when a director's absolute right of inspection may be curtailed).  He contends that there is a substantial and continuing public interest in determining whether a shareholder or member (or director) is entitled to be reimbursed pursuant to section 1604 or 17704.10(g) for attorney fees when a corporation or LLC fails and refuses to comply with requests for production without justification for months at a time, only does so after a mandate petition is filed, rolls out documents that it withheld in a document dump over a period of ten months, and then arrives at the courthouse steps and says “I’ve complied; no harm, no foul.”  Reply at 10-11.

Mars’ argument about the public interest does not concern the Petition’s merits, but rather attorney fees.  The Petition’s merits are moot but that does not mean that Mars cannot seek attorney fees for a mooted case.  See Zuehlsdorf v. Simi Valley Unified Sch. Dist. (2007) 148 Cal. App. 4th 249, 257-258 (upholding award of attorneys’ fees under Govt. Code section 800 where mandamus petition was dismissed as moot, but petitioner nonetheless the prevailing party based on a practical level).  This issue is addressed post.

 

2. Attorney Fees

Although the merits are moot, Respondents argue that Mars could not have prevailed on his claims anyway because he has not satisfied the statutory requirements for shareholder and member relief.  The court must address this issue before considering whether Mars is entitled to an award of attorney fees.

 

a. Whether RWC Refused to Produce Documents and Whether Mars Sought Production Reasonably Related to His Interest as a Member

Respondent RWC is a corporation.  Section 1603(a) provides that, “[u]pon refusal of a lawful demand for inspection, the superior court of the proper county, may enforce the right of inspection with just and proper conditions….”  (emphasis added).  

Respondents Cruefest, Records, and Masters 2008 are LLCs.  Section 17704.10(a) provides:  “Upon the request of a member . . . , for purposes reasonably related to the interest of that person as a member . . . , a manager . . . shall promptly deliver, in writing, to the member . . . a copy of the information required to be maintained by paragraphs (1), (2), and (4) of subdivision (d) of Section 17701.13, and any written operating agreement of the limited liability company.”  (Emphasis added.)  In addition, “[e]ach member . . . has the right, upon reasonable request, for purposes reasonably related to the interest of that person as a member, . . . : [t]o inspect and copy during normal business hours any of the records required to be maintained pursuant to Section 17701.13” and “[t]o obtain in writing from the limited liability company, promptly after becoming available, a copy of the limited liability company’s federal, state, and local income tax returns for each year.”  (emphasis added.)  

Respondents contend that RWC did not refuse to provide documents.  In fact, Mars claims that RWC and the other Responding Entities provided too many documents.  Without a refusal, there would be no basis for the court to issue a writ of mandate.  In addition to being moot, the Petition should be denied with respect to RWC on this ground.  Opp. at 13.

This argument requires interpretation of section 1603(a).  In construing a statute, a court must ascertain the intent of the legislature so as to effectuate the purpose of the law.  Brown v. Kelly Broadcasting Co., (1989) 48 Cal.3d 711, 724.  The statutory language must be harmonized with provisions relating to the same subject matter to the extent possible.  Orange County Employees Assn. v. County of Orange, (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 833, 841.  As Mars argues (Reply at 11), the word “refusal” in section 1603(a) must be interpreted in context with section 1604, which provides that an award of attorney fees is appropriate if the court finds that the corporation failed to comply with a proper demand without justification.  In other words, a corporation refuses to comply with an inspection demand when it fails to comply without justification.  The Oxford dictionary defines “refuse” as “to express or show determination not to do something.”  See Gertridge v. State Capital Co. (1933) 129 Cal.App. 86, 87 (words in statutes are interpreted according to common acceptance).  Thus, the refusal required under section 1603(a) need not be expressly made and may be shown by action or inaction. 

The chronology of pertinent evidence is as follows.  In the Fall of 2022, the parties disagreed on Mars’ status in the band.  At a November 4, 2022 Zoom meeting of MCI shareholders, Sixx, Lee, and Neil voted by proxy to accept Mars’ resignation from the band, terminate him as an MCI officer and director, and to purchase his shares in MCI for book value.  Mars Decl., ¶14. 

On February 8, 2023, Mars sent the Requests to each Respondent under sections 1600-02 and to all Respondent LLCs under sections 17704.10 et. seq.  McPherson Decl., ¶4, Ex. 6.  He wanted basic financial, organizational, and internal governance information about all the Respondent entities.  McPherson Decl., ¶4. 

A week later, on February 15, 2023, MCI filed a Demand for Arbitration seeking to establish that Mars was terminated as an MCI officer and director and is required to sell his shares.  McPherson Decl., ¶5, Ex. 7. 

The next day, February 16, 2023, the Respondent entities[4] produced 237 pages of documents in response to the Requests.  McPherson Decl., ¶6.  The production did not include any accounting records or statements.  McPherson Decl., ¶6. Respondents informed Mars via letter that they were still locating records and anticipated further production on a rolling basis.  Neach Decl., ¶4, Ex. 1.  At no point did Respondents refuse to produce any of the requested documents.  Neach Decl., ¶4. 

Mars filed the Petition on April 4, 2023.  On May 2, 2023, four weeks after Mars filed the Petition, the Respondent entities produced another 2223 pages of documents.  McPherson Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.  Among the documents produced were the Accounting Records (Ledger Reports, Statements of Income Expense, and tax returns for each Respondent entity from 2020 and 2021, as well as the draft of each document for 2022).  McPherson Decl., ¶10, Ex. 8.  Almost every Accounting Record had a timestamp showing that it was prepared on February 23, 2023.  McPherson Decl., ¶10, Ex. 8. 

            On June 9, 2023, Respondents produced another 673 pages, consisting mostly of Respondents’ contracts with third parties.  McPherson Decl., ¶14. After this third round of production, Respondents believed they had complied with the Requests; Mars had not sent any communication that would lead them to think otherwise.  Neach Decl., ¶9. 

Respondents filed a motion to compel arbitration on May 31, 2023, asking the court to stay the Petition pending the arbitration’s completion.  McPherson Decl., ¶13.  The motion was not decided until September 7, 2023.  Id.  When the court ruled on the motion, it ordered the remaining parties to meet and confer on the briefing schedule for the Petition.  Neach Decl., ¶10.  The parties set a meet and confer date via Zoom for September 21, 2023.  Neach Decl., ¶11. 

On September 19, 2023, less than two weeks after the motion to compel arbitration was decided, Mars’s attorney sent an email to Respondents’ counsel seeking the production of missing documents.  Neach Decl., ¶11, Ex. 2. Unlike the Requests, the September 2023 email had no date limit on the requested documents.  Neach Decl., ¶12.  Respondents therefore made further inquiries for documents going back further in time.  Neach Decl., ¶12.  This required additional time for searches by accountants, business managers, and attorneys.  Neach Decl., ¶12. 

On November 2, 2023, Respondents produced another 5,565 pages of documents.  McPherson Decl., ¶15.  The production consisted mostly of agreements involving the Respondent entities.  Neach Decl., ¶13.  The production included a set of resolutions adopted on June 22, 2023 terminating Mars’ employment with RWC and authorizing an assignment of RWC’s rights under the Touring Agreement to Nu, as well as the Assignment itself.  McPherson Decl., ¶19, Ex. 10.  The production also included June 22, 2023 resolutions terminating MCI’s oral license with RWC to use its intellectual property and authorizing MCI to enter into the License Agreement with Nu.  McPherson Decl., ¶20, Ex. 11.[5]  

            On November 6, 2023, Respondents asserted that the four productions reflected all documents in their possession, custody, and control that were responsive to the Requests.  McPherson Decl., ¶17, Ex. 9. 

Respondents did not hear from Mars again until he served his opening brief on November 20, 2023.  Neach Decl., ¶14.  The chart accompanying the opening brief was the first time Mars asserted that documents were still missing.  Neach Decl., ¶14. 

            On December 8, 2023, Respondents produced a final 1,372 pages of documents.  Neach Decl., ¶17, Ex. 4.  Respondents confirmed they did not have any other responsive documents listed by Mars in his opening brief.  Neach Decl., ¶18, Ex. 4.  This was in large part because Cruefest, Records, and Masters 2008 no longer conduct business.  Neach Decl., ¶18.  Respondents advised Mars that the production rendered the Petition moot.  Neach Decl., ¶18, Ex. 4.

From these facts, Mars concludes that his Requests triggered statutory duties that Respondents initially violated for eight weeks -- from the February 8, 2023 Requests until after the Petition was filed on April 6, 2023 -- and that violation continued for another seven months. 

 Respondents contend that they never refused to produce the requested documents.  The Requests were extensive, some of the Respondents were no longer operating, and they needed substantial time to collect and gather all the requested documents, review the documents, and address privilege concerns.  Neach Decl., ¶¶ 2, 5.  There were three rounds of production by June 9, 2023.

Mars replies that Respondents’ claim that it took substantial time to collect documents from business managers, accountants, and attorneys and to review for privilege concerns is absurd.  Respondents provide no names of the business managers, accountants, and attorneys from whom documents were gathered except Malek, who appears to be the only business manager for these entities.  Malek Decl. ¶¶ 2.  Mars also is a shareholder and director of each Respondent and there was no privilege issue with respect to agreements with third parties, shareholder resolution, or financial records, including tax returns.  Reply at 7-8.

The court agrees with Mars that the Requests are not extensive and that Respondents provide no detail about the collection effort or why a privilege would apply.  These documents could be easily gathered, and most were gathered by February 2023.  Nonetheless, the delay between the February 8 2023 Requests and the second and third productions on May 2 and June 9, 2023 is not particularly significant.  The delay after June 9, however, is significant.

Respondents argue that Mars went silent after the June 9 production until this court prompted action by ordering the parties to meet and confer, which the parties set for September 21, 2023.  Neach Decl., ¶11.  Given that three months went by after June 9, Respondents had every reason to believe that Mars was satisfied with the documents he received.  Opp. at 15.

Mars correctly argues that he had no need to demand additional documents between June 9 and the court’s September 21 ruling on the motion to compel arbitration.  Reply at 6-7.  It would not make sense to do so while the arbitration issue was outstanding, and Respondents could not reasonably rely on his silence during that period.   More important, on September 19, 2023, less than two weeks after the motion was decided, Mars’s attorney sent an email to Respondents’ counsel seeking the production of missing documents.  Neach Decl., ¶11, Ex. 2.  Yet, Respondents did not produce anything until November 2, 2023. 

Respondents contend that, unlike the Requests, the September 2023 email had no date limit on the requested documents.  Neach Decl., ¶12.  Respondents therefore made further inquiries for documents going further back in time.  Neach Decl., ¶12.  This required additional time for searches by accountants, business managers, and attorneys.  Neach Decl., ¶12.

Mars refutes this claim.  Mars notes that the September 19, 2023 email states that only “contracts, agreements [...] greater than $25,000.00 . . . in effect at any time during the years 2020, 2021, 2022, or 2023” were being sought.  Neach Decl., Ex. 2.  Additionally, Mars could not lawfully expand the Requests in the email and Respondents should have inquired if they thought he was doing so.

Respondents produced some requested documents on November 2, 2023, asserting a few days later that this was all of the responsive documents.  They proved to be wrong, as reflected in the chart attached to Mars’ opening brief.  Ex. 14.  This chart included basic documents such as articles of incorporation, shareholder/director minutes, operating agreements, articles of incorporation, and income tax returns.  Id.  These documents should have been produced without the need for prodding by Mars.  He notes that these documents were not produced until December 8, 2023, ten months after the Requests and after his opening brief had been filed.  See Neach Decl., ¶17.  Whatever Respondents’ position about a rolling production, RWC’s failure to produce the documents earlier than December 8 amounts to a refusal.  Respondent LLCs’ failure to produce these documents also was without justification.

With respect to the inspection rights of a LLC member under sections 17701.13(d) and 17704.10(b), Respondents argue that the phrase “purposes reasonably related to the interest of that person as a member” has received little attention.  One commentator on California corporate law stated: “Presumably, the person desiring to exercise the right of inspection would have to show that there was at least some reason to believe that the corporation had not been making full and accurate reports to the shareholders of the financial condition and results of operation of the corporation.”  Marsh’s Cal. Corp. Law, (2015) §15.09.  While this passage speaks to corporations, there is no reason to believe that the “purposes reasonably related” phrase is any different for a limited liability company.  Mars does not claim that any of the Respondents did not provide “full and accurate” reports to him.  For years, Mars received Schedule K-1’s.  Malek Decl., ¶10.  Only after he realized that he could not come to agreement with his former bandmates, and that litigation was imminent, did his attorney serve the Requests.  In short, Respondents contend that the evidence and chronology evince that the Requests serve no purpose reasonably related to Mars’ interest in the limited liability company Respondents.  Opp. at 14.

A petitioner need have only one sufficient basis for demand for inspection of corporate records that is reasonably related to her interest as a shareholder.  See Homestake Mining Co. v. Superior Court of City & County of San Francisco, (“Homestake”) (1936) 11 Cal.App.2d 488, 497.  A request to inspect corporate records to ascertain the value of the stock can be reasonably related to an interest as a shareholder.  Schnabel v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 715; Homestake, supra, 11 Cal.App.2d at 496.  The burden of proving a proper purpose is on the shareholder seeking inspection.  Id. at 715-23.  This reasoning should also apply to a limited liability company.

Mars’ motivation in seeking to inspect the limited liability company Respondents’ books and records was to understand the economic interests that he was being asked to give up in consideration for withdrawing from the band.  He was not even aware of the function of all the Respondents from which he was being asked to resign.  This is a sufficient purpose reasonably related to his interest as a member in Respondent LLCs.

 

b. Whether Respondents Failed to Comply Without Justification

For a corporate respondent, in “any action or proceeding under Section 1600 or Section 1601, if the court finds the failure of the corporation to comply with a proper demand thereunder was without justification, the court may award an amount sufficient to reimburse the shareholder . . . for the reasonable expenses incurred by such holder, including attorneys’ fees, in connection with such action or proceeding.” §1604 (emphasis added).

For a limited liability company respondent, “in any action under…Section 17713.07, if the court finds the failure of the limited liability company to comply with the requirements of this section is without justification, the court may award an amount sufficient to reimburse the person bringing the action for the reasonable expenses incurred by that person, including attorney’s fees, in connection with the action or proceeding.” §17704.10(g) (emphasis added).

Under section 1604, a respondent’s action is justified if it is “well grounded in both law and fact.”  Farnum v. Iris Biotech. Inc., (2022) 86 Cal.App. 5th 602, 611.  The court must examine all of the circumstances of a given case to determine whether awarding fees under the statute would be unjust, with the burden of showing such inequity resting on the defendant.  Id.  Some considerations that the court should consider in exercising its discretion include the necessity for the lawsuit and the likelihood that the problem would have been solved by other means.  Id. at 612.  The trial court has considerable discretion on this issue.  See id. at 69.  The same evaluation should apply to the same language in section 17713.07. 

Mars argues that Respondents have offered no justification for failing to produce readily available documents that they were statutorily and contractually obligated to provide prior to this litigation.  Nor have they offered any justification for the delay in production for months after the Petition was filed when all such documents were maintained by the same business manager for over 20 years.  Pet. Op. Br. at 16.

According to Mars, the only reasonable conclusion that can be drawn is that Respondents intentionally caused Petitioner to incur unnecessary legal fees by withholding the vast majority of documents until after the Petition was filed, dragging out their production over nine months, filing a petition to compel arbitration after already having produced the Accounting Records with very few documents left to be delivered, filing a motion to strike important context allegations which was largely denied, and ultimately producing thousands of documents that were never requested.  Respondents intended all along to do everything possible to ensure that Mars spends as much money as possible, so that he will be “starved out” of the fight and give up the fruits of his life’s work.  Pet. Op. Br. at 17-18.

The court need not agree with Mars to conclude that Respondents acted without substantial justification.  The analysis above concerning Respondents’ refusal applies equally to their failure to produce documents without justification.  The Requests were not burdensome.  Yet, Mars was compelled to file suit and it appears plain that production would not have occurred without it.  Mars is entitled to attorney fees.

 

F. Conclusion

The Petition against Respondents is denied as moot on its merits.  However, Mars is entitled to reasonable attorney fees.  He may make a noticed motion for the award of reasonable fees.  Counsel are directed to meet and confer on the amount of reasonable fees before such a motion is filed.

            B. Governing Law

1. Site Requirements

The primary purpose of the SFSP is to provide food service to children from needy areas during periods when area schools are closed for vacation.  7 C.F.R. §225.1.

SFSP sponsors shall not be eligible for meal reimbursements unless they have executed an agreement with the state agency.   7 C.F.R. §225.9(d.  All reimbursements shall be in accordance with the terms of this agreement.  7 C.F.R. §225.9(d).  Reimbursements shall not be paid for meals served at a site before the sponsor has received written notification that the site has been approved for participation in the program.  7 C.F.R. §225.9(d).  

The state agency must conduct pre-approval visits of sponsors and sites to assess the potential for successful SFSP operations and to verify information provided in the application.  7 C.F.R. §225.7(d).  If the sponsor is a school food authority or CACFP institution, and it was reviewed by the state agency under its respective programs during the preceding 12 months and had no significant deficiencies noted in that review, the state agency has discretion to conduct a pre-approval visit.  7 C.F.R. §225.7(d)(2).

Sponsors may be reimbursed for meals served away from the approved site location if (i) the  sponsor notifies the state agency in advance that meals will be served away from the approved site; (ii) the state agency has determined that all program requirements under 7 C.F.R. Part 225 will be met, including applicable state and local health, safety, and sanitation standards; (iii) the meals are served at an approved meal service time, unless the state agency approves a change thereto; and (iv) the sponsor provides advance notice to the community of the site change, if it is impossible for the sponsor to continue operating at the approved location.  7 C.F.R. §225.16(g)(1).

 

2. Appeal Process

Each state agency shall establish a procedure to be followed by an applicant appealing a denial of a sponsor’s claim for reimbursement.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(a).  The state agency shall send written notification of the complete appeal procedures and of actions which are appealable to each potential sponsor and food service management company applying to register in accordance with 7 C.F.R. section 225.6(k).  7 C.F.R. §225.13(c).

At a minimum, the appeal procedure shall provide that the sponsor or food service management company be advised in writing of the grounds upon which the state agency based the action.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(1).  The notice of action shall also state that the sponsor or food service management company has the right to appeal the state’s action.  Id.  The notice is considered to be received by the sponsor or food service management company when it is delivered by certified mail, facsimile, or email.  Id.

The sponsor or food service management company must be advised in writing that the appeal must be made within a specified time.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(2).  The state agency shall establish this period as not less than one week nor more than two weeks from the date on which the notice of action is received.  Id.

The appeal procedure must allow the appellant to refute the charges contained in the notice of action, either in person or by filing written documentation with the review official.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(4).  Such written documentation must be submitted within seven days of the appeal.  Id.

The state agency’s action remains in effect during the appeal process.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(11).

The determination by the state review official on appeal is the final administrative determination to be afforded to the appellant.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(12).

 

C. Demurrers

Demurrers are permitted in administrative mandate proceedings.  CCP §§1108, 1109.  A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleading alone and will be sustained where the pleading is defective on its face. 

            Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer or motion to strike or by motion for judgment on the pleadings.  CCP §430.30(a); Coyne v. Krempels, (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257.  The party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may object by demurrer or answer to the pleading.  CCP §430.10.  A demurrer is timely filed within the 30-day period after service of the complaint.  CCP §430.40; Skrbina v. Fleming Companies, (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1353, 1364. 

            A demurrer may be asserted on any one or more of the following grounds: (a) The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading; (b) The person who filed the pleading does not have legal capacity to sue; (c) There is another action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action; (d) There is a defect or misjoinder of parties; (e) The pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action; (f) The pleading is uncertain; (g) In an action founded on a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, is oral, or is implied by conduct; (h) No certificate was filed as required by CCP sections 411.35 or 411.36.  CCP §430.10. 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.  CCP §430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan, (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.  The face of the pleading includes attachments and incorporations by reference (Frantz v. Blackwell, (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94), but it does not include inadmissible hearsay.  Day v. Sharp, (1975) 50 Cal.App.3d 904, 914.   

            The sole issue on demurrer for failure to state a cause of action is whether the facts pleaded, if true, would entitle the plaintiff to relief.  Garcetti v. Superior Court, (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 1533, 1547; Limandri v. Judkins, (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 339.  The question of plaintiff’s ability to prove the allegations of the complaint or the possible difficulty in making such proof does not concern the reviewing court.  Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co., (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 47. 

The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.  Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher, (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403.  This rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.  Vance v. Villa Park Mobilehome Estates, (“Vance”) (1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709. 

            For all demurrers filed after January 1, 2016, the demurring party must meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  CCP §430.31(a).  As part of the meet and confer process, the demurring party must identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and provide legal support for the claimed deficiencies.  CCP §430.31(a)(1).  The party who filed the pleading must in turn provide legal support for its position that the pleading is legally sufficient or, in the alternative, how the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer could be amended to cure any legal insufficiency.  Id.  The demurring party is responsible for filing and serving a declaration that the meet and confer requirement has been met.  CCP §430.31(a)(3).

            “[A] demurrer based on a statute of limitations will not lie where the action may be, but is not necessarily, barred. [Citation.] In order for the bar of the statute of limitations to be raised by demurrer, the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.” State ex rel. Metz v. CCC Information Services, Inc., (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 402, 413.

            If a demurrer is sustained, the court may grant leave to amend the pleading upon any terms as may be just and shall fix the time within which the amendment or amended pleading shall be filed.  CCP §472a(c).  However, in response to a demurrer and prior to the case being at issue, a complaint or cross-complaint shall not be amended more than three times, absent an offer to the trial court as to such additional facts to be pleaded that there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured to state a cause of action.  CCP §430.41(e)(1).

 

            E. Analysis[3]

            Respondent DOE demurs to the Petition on the basis that (1) the court lacks subject matter jurisdiction for the administrative mandate claim because LEAP failed to exhaust its administrative remedies, and (2) the Petition fails to state facts sufficient to state a claim for administrative or traditional mandamus, breach of contract, or declaratory relief.

 

            1. Meet and Confer

            On October 3, 2023 DOE emailed LEAP that the Petition is deficient as a matter of law.  Ko Decl., ¶2.  During a telephone conference on October 6, 2023, DOE reiterated the grounds for its position that the Petition is legally insufficient.  Ko Decl., ¶3.  Despite this explanation, the parties failed to reach any agreement.  Ko Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.  DOE fulfilled the meet-and-confer requirement.

 

            2. First Cause of Action for Administrative Mandamus[4]

 

a. Exhaustion of Administrative Remedies

            DOE demurs on the ground that LEAP’s Petition does not show that it exhausted administrative remedies.  Other than the perfunctory statement on the subject, the Petition fails to address the exhaustion issue.  Dem. at 5.

A writ of mandate will only issue when the petitioner has no plain, speedy, or adequate remedy at law.  CCP §1086.  As a general rule, a court will not issue a writ of mandate unless a petitioner has first exhausted its available administrative remedies.  See, e.g., Alta Loma School Dist. v. San Bernardino County Com. On School Dist. Reorganization, (1981) 124 Cal.App.3d 542, 554.  Under this rule, an administrative remedy is exhausted only upon termination of all available, non-duplicative administrative review procedures.  Coachella Valley Mosquito & Vector Control Dist. v. California Public Employment Relations Bd., (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1072, 1080.  The exhaustion doctrine contemplates that the real issues in controversy be presented to the administrative body, which must be given the opportunity to apply its special expertise to correct any errors and reach a final decision, thereby saving the already overworked courts from intervening into an administrative dispute unless absolutely necessary.  Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court, (1992) 2 Cal.4th 377, 391.

            The exhaustion doctrine has been described as “a jurisdictional prerequisite to resort to the courts.”  Abelleira v. District Court of Appeal, (1941) 17 Cal.2d 280, 293.  The failure to allege exhaustion of administrative remedies or facts excusing the failure to exhaust renders the petition subject to demurrer for failure to state a cause of action.  See, e.g., Stenocord Corp. v. City & County of San Francisco, (1970) 2 Cal.3d 984, 990.  One court has held that a mere allegation that petitioners have exhausted their administrative remedies is conclusory and insufficient to survive demurrer.  Pan Pacific Property v. County of Santa Cruz, (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 244, 251.  However, another court has held that such an allegation is sufficient to survive demurrer.  Wong v. Regents of University of California, (1971) 15 Cal.App.3d 823, 829. 

On March 18, 2015, LEAP entered into an agreement to serve reimbursable meals and snacks through the SFSP.  Pet. ¶¶ 21, 60-61.  Pursuant to the agreement, LEAP agreed to comply with 7 C.F.R. section 225.1 et seq.  SFSP sponsors shall not be eligible for meal reimbursements unless they have executed an agreement with the state agency, and all reimbursements shall be in accordance with the terms of this agreement.  7 C.F.R. §225.9(d).  Reimbursements shall not be paid for meals served at a site before the sponsor has received written notification that the site has been approved for participation in the program.  7 C.F.R. §225.9(d).  

            In the March 2023 NOA, DOE notified LEAP that DOE’s Child Nutrition Consultant observed LEAP serving meals on July 21, 2021 at the Wilbur Address, which was an unapproved site.  LEAP was approved to serve meals at the Etiwanda Address but was serving them at the Wilbur Address.  Pet., Ex. 5, p. 2.  The distance between the two locations is 2.8 miles.  Id.  

LEAP submitted a change of address on July 19, 2021, and DOE approved the Wilbur Address as a meal service location the next day, effective as of July 1, 2021.  Id.  In the March 2023 NOA, DOE disallowed reimbursement claims for meals served at the Wilbur Address for the months of May and June 2021.  Ex. 5, pp. 1-2.  LEAP was required to repay the $133,300 which had been reimbursed to it for those months.  Id., p. 1.

DOE is required by the SFSP to establish a procedure to be followed by an applicant appealing a denial of a sponsor’s claim for reimbursement.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(a).  The appeal procedure is required to be sent to each potential sponsor. 7 C.F.R. §225.13(c).

DOE’s appeal procedure shall provide that the sponsor be advised in writing of the grounds upon which the state agency based the action.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(1).  The NOA shall also state that the sponsor or food service management company has the right to appeal the State’s action.  Id.  DOE must advise the sponsor in writing that the appeal must be made within a specified time, which shall be not less than one week nor more than two weeks from the date on which the notice of action is received.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(2).  The notice of action is considered to be received by the sponsor or food service management company when it is delivered by certified mail, facsimile, or email.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(1).

            The March 2023 NOA was sent to LEAP electronically on March 29, 2023.  Pet. Ex. 5, p. 1.  It informed LEAP of its appeal right and provided a link to DOE’s webpage on the appeal process and LEAP’s rights thereunder.  Pet., ¶40, Ex. 5.  DOE’s appeal procedure required LEAP to make a written request for an appeal postmarked within ten days of receipt of the NOA.  Pet., ¶41.

            On May 9, 2023, LEAP sent DOE the appeal.  Pet., ¶43, Ex. 6.  On May 23, 2023, DOE denied appeal as untimely.  Pet., ¶44, Ex. 7.  The denial indicated that DOE’s system showed that LEAP received the March 2023 NOA on Friday, April 6, 2023 at 8:17 p.m.  Pet., ¶42, Ex. 7.  Because this was after hours on a Friday, DOE treated the date of receipt as April 10, 2023.  Pet., ¶44, Ex. 7.  The ten-day deadline to postmark the appeal was therefore April 20, 2023.  Pet., ¶44, Ex. 7.  DOE stated that LEAP missed this deadline, and the appeal was denied.  Pet., ¶44, Ex. 7.

            LEAP argues that DOE’s denial of its appeal as untimely is sufficient to exhaust administrative remedies and trigger the availability of administrative mandamus.  Opp. at 7-8.  LEAP cites Civil Service Commission v. Velez, (“Velez”) (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 115, 117, where the civil service commission argued that the superior court lacked subject matter jurisdiction to hear a discharged public employee’s petition because review of the timeliness determination was not a final order or decision after the taking of evidence within the meaning of CCP section 1094.5.  Id. at 118.  The court held that the fact that no hearing occurred does not preclude administrative mandamus review.  Id.  If a hearing is required by law, administrative mandate may be appropriate to secure it.  Id.  Velez had a right to an appeal hearing upon timely request.  Id.  The commission’s untimeliness determination exhausted Velez’s administrative remedies.  Id. at 119.  Whether the commission proceeded as required by law when it refused to hear her appeal fell squarely within the scope of review under CCP section 1094.5.  Id.

CCP section 1094.5(a) governs a case which seeks review of a proceeding that required a hearing, the taking of evidence, and discretionary administrative determination of facts.  CCP section 1094.5 applies whether or not a hearing was actually held.  Helene Curtis, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Assessment Appeals Board, (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 29, 37; Velez, supra, 14 Cal.App.4th at 117.  LEAP exhausted its administrative remedies with respect to the timeliness of its appeal and any excuse for its untimeliness.[5]

 

            b. Good Cause

DOE demurs to the first cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim for administrative mandamus is based on the same failure to timely appeal the March 2023 NOA.  Dem. at 6.

LEAP argues that it has shown good cause to excuse its untimely appeal.  LEAP relies on Gibson v. Unemployment Insurance Appeals Board, (“Gibson”) (1973) 9 Cal.3d 494, Faulkner v. Public Employees’ Retirement System, (“Faulkner”) (1975) 47 Cal.App.3d 731, Gonzalez v. State Personnel Board, (“Gonzalez”) (1977) 76 Cal.App.3d 364, and Velez, supra, 14 Cal.App.4th at 115  Opp. at 8-11.

In Gibson, the petitioner applied for unemployment insurance after he was discharged by his employer.  9 Cal.3d at 497.  He received written notice that he was not eligible for unemployment benefits and his right to appeal.  Id.  He took the notice to an attorney, who promised to file an appeal.  Id.  The attorney’s receptionist filed to note the appeal deadline on the attorney’s calendar and the attorney immediately filed an appeal three days late, when the error was discovered.  Id.  three days after the statutory appeal period expired.  9 Cal.3d at 497.  Both the Unemployment Insurance Appeals Board and the superior court held that neither inadvertent clerical error nor press of business could constitute good cause for an untimely appeal under Unemployment Insurance Code (“UIC”) section 1328.  Id. at 497-98. 

            In reversing, the California Supreme Court held that it must liberally construe the UIC and dispense with formality to further the legislative objective of reducing the hardship of unemployment.  Id. at 499.  Applicants without legal training have only ten days to either determine the advisability and method of filing an appeal or retain legal aid to do so.  Id. at 500.  Those circumstances can lead applicants to miss the deadline solely through excusable inadvertence.  Id.  The slightest and most excusable inadvertence should not deprive a worker of his right to an appeal on the merits.  Id.  The case concerned a remedial statute that should be administered informally without resort to technicalities that might deprive the unsophisticated applicant of his right to benefits.  Id. at 500-01.

In Faulkner, a police officer appealed the denial of his disability retirement benefits application.  47 Cal.App.3d at 733.  The Public Employees’ Retirement System denied petitioner’s request for an appeal hearing filed four days after the 30-day period for such appeal had passed.  Id.  The respondent argued that the 30-day deadline was a jurisdictional limitation on the right to appeal.  Id. at 735. 

            The appellate court disagreed.  Id.  A denial of retirement disability benefits affects a fundamental, vested right.  Id. at 734.  The retirement system is to be administered without hardship or prejudice, and strictly enforcing the 30-days deadline would constitute hardship and the exercise of prejudice.  Id. at 735-36.  The absence of a specific provision enabling an applicant for retirement benefits to file a late appeal for good cause does not preclude the filing of such an appeal.  Id. at 736.  The petitioner had offered an excuse for the four-day delay, extraordinary circumstances at the law firm serving as his counsel.  Id. at 737.  The possible impact of the denial of his right to appeal outweighed the minimal delay and the lack of prejudice to the respondent.  Id.

In Gonzalez, supra, 76 Cal.App.3d at 364, the court addressed the refusal of the State Personnel Board (“SPB”) to consider an untimely appeal from the petitioner’s dismissal from his employment as a youth counselor.  Id. at 366.  The state Department of Youth Authority had terminated the petitioner based on his arrest for drug-related offenses.   Id. at pp. 365-66.   The department notified the employee of his dismissal and informed him that he had 20 days to appeal the decision.  Id. at 366.  The employee filed his appeal six days late. Ibid. The SPB refused to consider the employee’s untimely appeal request.  Ibid.

The trial court granted the employee’s mandamus petition requiring the SPB to allow the employee to appeal his termination after determining good cause existed “due to the mistake, inadvertence, and excusable neglect of Petitioner and his attorney, due to a breakdown in communication between them, as well as the fact that they were both laboring under the strain of major criminal proceedings.”  Ibid.

In upholding the trial court’s decision, the appellate court relied on Faulkner and concluded that the statutory scheme should be interpreted liberally in favor of an employee when dealing with a fundamental vested right.  Id.  at 367.  The court stated:

 

“Where the delay in filing is long delayed and the employing unit can show that it has, in fact, replaced the employee, a reason for denying equitable relief may exist. However, in the case at bench, the delay in filing the notice of appeal was only six days and the employer made no attempt to show that a replacement had been hired within that brief period. We conclude that a reasonable reconciliation between the rights of the employee and the employer requires that, where good cause is shown for a brief delay and no prejudice to the employer is shown, relief from the default should be given.”  Id. at 367.

 

In Velez, the court addressed an air pollution control board’s termination of a public employee for incompetent, inefficient, and dishonest acts.  14 Cal.App.4th at 117.  The employee filed an appeal to the civil service commission five days after the ten-day deadline in the county’s civil service rules.  Id. at 117-18.  The employee had spoken to her union representative before the deadline and miscommunicated the date on which she received the termination notice.  Id. at 115.  The commission denied the appeal as untimely.  Ibid.  The union requested that the employee be allowed to file a late appeal, and the commission contended that it lacked jurisdiction to hear late appeals under the civil service rules.  Ibid.  The trial court “implicitly ruled the [commission] may not mechanically reject late appeals but must consider whether the employee showed good cause for tardiness.”  Id. at 119.

On appeal, the commission argued that the deadline to file an appeal pursuant to the civil service rules was jurisdictional and could not be extended.  Id. at 120.  The appellate court held that, although no express provision of the civil service rules permitted a late appeal, good cause existed where there was a short delay caused by inadvertence and the commission asserted no prejudice.  Id. at 122.  

The court held that Velez had a fundamental and vested right of employment protected by due process, and  the civil service rules required the commission to “protect the merit basis of the personnel system” similar to the personnel and retirement contexts in Gibson, Gonzales, and Faulkner, where relief was afforded for good cause based on the shortness of the delay, absence of prejudice, and excusable nature of the error.  Id. at 121-22. Velez’s delay was short, caused by inadvertence, and the Commission asserted no prejudice.  Ibid.  The trial court correctly reconciled Velez’s right to have her appeal on the merits and the commission’s concern for an orderly process in granting relief.  Id. at 122-23.  See also Hall-Villareal v. City of Fresno, (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 24, 34 (due process requires reading good cause exception into city’s municipal code administrative procedures where a fundamental right to continued employment is at issue).

From these cases, LEAP concludes that the court should protect the merits of its appeal by requiring DOE to consider a good cause extension.  Neither 7 C.F.R. section 225 et seq. nor DOE’s appeal procedure expressly afford relief from an untimely appeal, but they also do not preclude such consideration.  The federal regulations provide “contextual clues” that emphasize the remedial purpose of the appeal process.  The primary purpose of the SFSP is to provide food service to needy children when schools are closed for vacation.  7 C.F.R. §225.1.  The legislative objective of this program is dependent on reimbursement of sponsors such as LEAP.  Resorting to technicalities during the process frustrates the remedial purpose of the appeal process to efficiently continue serving community needs.  Even where the late filing of an administrative appeal is not expressly permitted by regulation or statute, discretionary extensions of time for appeal for good cause are required.  Gonzalez, supra, 76 Cal.App.3d at 367.  Opp. at 10.

LEAP adds that protection of the merits for appeal requires genuine consideration of LEAP’s good cause showing of its minimally untimely appeal.  While it is true that the appeal was 19 days late, DOE waited over a year and a half after its July 2021 compliance review until its March 2023 NOA.  DOE offered no rationale for this delay, which prejudiced LEAP.  Nor has DOE shown any prejudice.  In fact, it granted approval for the Wilbur Address within one day of LEAP’s submission of an address change.  Opp. at 10.

LEAP only failed to timely request an appeal because of inadvertent error.  Pet., ¶43.  The late appeal was a product of genuine inadvertence because it merely overlooked the tight ten-day turnaround while trying to sustain operations to meet the persistent demand for meals.  The balance of justice weighs in favor of granting a good cause extension for the appeal.  Opp. at 11.

As a threshold matter, LEAP miscalculates the untimeliness of its appeal as 19 days late.  A NOA is considered to be received by the sponsor when it is delivered by certified mail, facsimile, or email.  7 C.F.R. §225.13(b)(1).  It does not matter when it was actually received and viewed.  The March 2023 NOA was sent to LEAP electronically on March 29, 2023.  Pet. Ex. 5, p. 1.  That  is the date of receipt.[6]  DOE’s appeal procedure required the written request for an appeal to be postmarked within ten days of receipt of the NOA.  Pet., ¶41.  Thus, LEAP’s appeal was required to be filed by April 8, 2021.  The appeal filed on May 9, 2023 was 41 days late. 

LEAP’s miscalculation does not bear on the court’s analysis, however, because good cause is a question of fact that generally cannot be determined on demurrer as a matter of law.  The court can only determine whether DOE is obligated to make a good cause determination for LEAP’s appeal.  That is, the question for demurrer is whether the good cause doctrine applies to a SFSP sponsor’s appeal.

DOE argues that the good cause doctrine does not apply and that it does not have discretion to consider an untimely appeal.  DOE is prohibited by 7 C.F.R. section 225.13(b)(2) from setting an appeal more than two weeks after the NOA. Neither the regulation nor DOE’s appeal procedure provides for any extension.  Reply at 3.

            DOE is correct.  DOE is bound by the federal regulations for the SFSP.  While it is possible to interpret the federal regulations to permit a good cause showing, it is inappropriate to do so if the rights at issue are not fundamental and vested and the statutory scheme is not remedial and liberally construed to carry out a policy of aiding the petitioner. 

Unlike Gibson, Faulkner, Gonzales, Velez, LEAP’s right to meal reimbursement is not fundamental and vested.  A right may be deemed fundamental based on either (1) “the character and quality of its economic aspect; (2) the character and quality of its human aspect.”  Amerco Real Estate Co. v. City of West Sacramento, (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 778, 783 (citation omitted).  This is a case-by-case determination.   “[A]s a general rule, when a case involves or affects purely economic interests, courts are far less likely to find a right to be of the fundamental vested character."  JKH Enterprises, Inc. v. Department of Industrial Relations, (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1046, 1060 (impact of agency’s decision to issue an administrative stop work order and penalty for violation labor relations was purely economic and the substantial evidence was appropriate standard of review).  LEAP’s right to meal reimbursement is not fundamental because it is purely economic in nature.  While LEAP has a contractual right to reimbursement, it has no vested right to reimbursement where it violates federal regulation by providing meal service at an unapproved location. 

            The SFSP regulatory scheme also is not remedial in nature with respect to sponsors.  At issue is LEAP’s right to receive reimbursement for two months of meals claimed and distributed at the Wilbur Address before its approval as an SFSP site.  Pet., ¶¶ 33-34, 39, Ex. 5.  The primary purpose of the SFSP is to protect the health and well-being of children during the summer months, but this remedial purpose does not extend to payment of sponsors.  SFSP sponsors are sophisticated organizations and there is no reason to construe the federal regulations broadly in their favor.  Reply at 3. 

            The good cause doctrine is not available to extend the appeal deadline for a SFSP sponsor’s meal reimbursement.           The demurrer as to the first cause of action is sustained. 

 

            3. Second Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

            DOE demurs to the breach of contract cause of action as (1) barred by failure to exhaust administrative remedies and (2) failure to state a claim for breach of contract.  Dem. at 9.

For failure to exhaust, DOE does not show that exhaustion is required for a contract breach claim.  The exhaustion of administrative remedies requirement applies to mandamus (see, e.g., Alta Loma School Dist. v. San Bernardino County Com. On School Dist. Reorganization, (1981) 124 Cal.App.3d 542, 554) and mandamus is not an appropriate remedy for enforcing a contractual obligation against an agency.  300 DeHaro Street Investors v. Department of Housing & Community Development, (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 1240, 1254.  This is because breach of contract is an adequate remedy at law, and the duty which mandamus enforces is not the contractual duty of the entity, but the official duty of its officer or board.  Ibid.  Moreover, a public agency is as free to breach a contract during performance, with accompanying liability for doing so, as a private contracting person or entity.  Id. at 1254-55.  See also Pomona Police Officers’ Assn. v. City of Pomona, (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 578, 590.

As for substantial compliance, recovery based on a claim for breach of contract requires substantial performance rather than complete performance.  Posner v. Grunwald-Marx, Inc., (1961), 56 Cal.2d 169, 186-187.  What constitutes substantial performance is a question of fact, but it is essential that there be no willful departure from the terms of the contract, and that the defects be such as may be easily remedied or compensated, so that the promisee may get practically what the contract calls for.  Id. at 187.  The promisor must have in good faith performed all of the contract’s substantive terms.  Connell v. Higgins, (1915) 170 Cal. 541, 556.  If so, the promisor will not be held to have forfeited his right to recovery by reason of trivial defects or imperfection in the work performed.  Id.  This rule is particularly true if the promisee has received the benefit of what has been done and is enjoying the fruits of the work.  Id.   See also Brown v. Aguilar, (1925) 202 Cal. 143, 147.  Essentially, this rule is one of the materiality of the promisor’s breach, which is a question of fact.

            As part of the SFSP, LEAP agreed to provide meals in accordance with 7 C.F.R. sections 225 et. seq.  Pet., ¶61.  The Petition asserts that LEAP substantially complied with those obligations.  Pet., ¶62.  It provided meals at approved sites like the Etiwanda Address for LAPD Devonshire PALS and sought timely reimbursement for those meals.  Pet., ¶¶ 30, 62.  Guidance from DOE allowed LEAP to serve meals near approved sites during the COVID-19 pandemic.  Pet., ¶65.  DOE’s disallowance of meals served at the Etiwanda Address from May and June 2021 was a material breach of its contract with LEAP.  Pet., ¶65. 

            LEAP argues that its inadvertent clerical error in failing to update the site location for DOE approval of meal distribution for the SFSP when it had done so for CACFP service is not an adequate ground for denying meal reimbursement at the Wilbur Address.  The error stemmed from DOE’s failure to provide clear instructions about how to serve meals during the COVID-19 pandemic.  Pet., ¶25.  Schar confusingly told LEAP in March 2020 that it could not add new distribution sites, and must use pre-approved sites, but the pandemic had caused most of the approved sites to shut down.  Pet., ¶¶ 22-23, 27.  LEAP did not willfully depart from the contract, make any attempt at fraud, or otherwise materially breach the contract.  Although DOE seizes on accusations of a prior finding that LEAP violated 7 C.F.R. Part 225, the parties’ longstanding relationship supports the inference that any non-compliance did not substantially affect the usefulness of its work under the contract.  Opp. at 12.

            DOE replies that it has no contract with LEAP for meal service at an unapproved site.  DOE never contracted with LEAP to provide meals in violation of 7 C.F.R. section 225.9.  LEAP’s service of meals at an unapproved site is a material departure from the agreement.  Its willfulness is acknowledged by its admission that it received March 16, 2020 correspondence from Schar that “SFSP sponsors could not add new sites as only existing sites that were approved could be used.”   Pet. ¶27.  SFSP regulations do not permit sponsors to serve meals at unapproved sites because a state agency needs to determine if the site meets state and local health, safety, and sanitation standards, the meals are served at an approved service time, and the sponsor has provided notice to the community of the site change.  7 C.F.R. §225.16(g).  DOE could not confirm the Wilbur Address met these requirements until July 21, 2021 and prior service of meals at that address was a material breach of the contract.  Id.  Therefore, the doctrine of substantial compliance does not apply.  Reply at 5-6.

The parties’ arguments are factual issues about the materiality of LEAP’s breach which cannot be resolved on demurrer.  DOE argues that it had no contract with LEAP to provide meal service at an unapproved site.  LEAP’s contract is required by the SFSP regulatory scheme, and all reimbursements shall be in accordance with the terms of this agreement.  7 C.F.R. §225.9(d).  Reimbursements shall not be paid for meals served at a site before the sponsor has received written notification that the site has been approved for participation in the program.  7 C.F.R. §225.9(d).  LEAP’s breach of contract issue is whether site approval is a material term such that LEAP’s failure to comply is a breach.

            As presented to the court, LEAP’s breach of contract claim cannot be resolved on demurrer.  The demurrer as to the second cause of action is overruled.[7]

 

            4. Third Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

In the third cause of action for declaratory relief, LEAP asserts that an actual controversy has arisen as to whether DOE’s final decision to disallow reimbursement is valid.  Pet., ¶68.  As with the other causes of action, the decision infringes on LEAP’s due process rights and was an abuse of DOE’s discretion.  Pet., ¶68. 

Any person who desires a declaration of his or her rights or duties with respect to another, or in respect to, in, over or upon property, may, in cases of actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the respective parties, bring an original action or cross-complaint in the superior court for a declaration of his or her rights and duties.  CCP §1060.  He or she may ask for a declaration of rights or duties, either alone or with other relief.  Id.  The court may make a binding declaration of these rights or duties, whether or not further relief is or could be claimed at the time.  Id.

            A complaint for declaratory relief is legally sufficient if it sets forth facts showing the existence of an actual controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the parties and requests that the rights and duties of the parties be adjudged by the court.  Monterey Coastkeeper v. California Regional Water Quality Control Board, (“Monterey”) (2022) 76 Cal. App. 5th 1, 13, for the requirements of declaratory relief.    If these requirements are met and no basis for declining declaratory relief appears, the court should declare the rights of the parties whether or not the facts alleged establish the plaintiff is entitled to favorable declaration.  Id.  Declaratory relief operates prospectively, to resolve controversies before obligations are repudiated, rights are invaded, or wrongs are committed.  Id.  Its purpose is to declare future rights, not to address past wrongs.  Id.

            A party seeking declaratory relief must show a very significant possibility of future harm.  Id.  A probable future dispute over legal rights between parties is sufficiently ripe to represent an “actual controversy” as opposed to purely hypothetical concerns.  Id.  An actual controversy is one which admits of definitive and conclusive relief by judgment within the field of judicial administration, as distinguished from an advisory opinion upon a particular or hypothetical state of facts.  Id.

            DOE argues that the third cause of action is improper because it seeks to remedy a past wrong.  Dem. at 9. 

LEAP responds that is not the case.  Although LEAP disputes the merits on whether DOE should have disallowed reimbursement, it only seeks a declaratory judgment as to its due process rights and its entitlement to a fair appeal process.  LEAP understands it cannot contest the merits of DOE’s decision until it passes the hurdle of administrative review.  LEAP only asks the court to direct the DOE to undertake the appeal it should have received.  Until DOE does this, it cannot call the denial of LEAP’s appeal or the disallowance of reimbursement a past wrong.  Opp. at 14-15.

            LEAP is incorrect for three reasons.  First, the court has concluded that LEAP cannot obtain a good cause determination by DOE for the untimeliness of its appeal and the good cause issue is the only dispute between the parties about the appeal process.  There is no actual controversy on any other aspect of an appeal.

Second, CCP section 1094.5 is the exclusive remedy for judicial review of a final administrative decision.  Woods v. Superior Court (1981) 28 Cal.3d 668, 675.  "The law is well established that an action for declaratory relief is not appropriate to review an administrative decision."  State of California v. Superior Court (Veta) (1974) 12 Cal.3d 237, 249.  “A party cannot circumvent the exhaustion doctrine by bringing actions other than administrative mandamus such as actions for declaratory relief.”  Walter H. Leimert Co. v. California Coastal Commission, (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 222, 232.  The sole controversy in LEAP’s declaratory relief claim is its right to present the good cause issue and this issue is subsumed within the first cause of action.

            Third, the Petition’s prayer for relief does not support LEAP’s argument.  The prayer seeks a declaration that DOE’s decision requiring payment of $133,300 was wrongly determined and that DOE abused its discretion by disallowing meals that were served for reimbursement.  Pet. Prayer for Relief, ¶3.  This prayer seeks the relief that LEAP now asserts it does not want the court to order. Alternatively, the prayer seeks a declaration that DOE abused its discretion by denying the request for an appeal.  Pet. Prayer for Relief, ¶3.  This prayer concerns only the good cause issue and not any appeal procedure. 

            In sum, the third cause of action for declaratory relief is subsumed within the first cause of action for administrative mandamus.  The demurrer as to the third cause of action is sustained.

 

            D. Conclusion

            The demurrer to the Petition is sustained for the first and third causes of action.  Although LEAP asks for leave to amend, it does not allege facts that could cure the defects.  The demurrer is overruled for the second cause of action.  The court will discuss with the parties whether the case should be transferred to Department 1 for reassignment to an I/C court.
