DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV03290    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: W

ALEXANDER GHATAN v. BMW OF NORTH AMERICA, LLC, et al.

 

DEFENDANTS BMW OF NORTH AMERICA, LLC’S AND BMW OF SHERMAN OAKS’ DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        May 2, 2024                                       Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03290

 

Moving Party:            Defendants BMW NA, LLC and BMW of Sherman Oaks

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Alexander Ghatan  

Meet and Confer:      See footnote[1]

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiff Alexander Ghatan filed a complaint against BMW of North America, LLC and Sherman Oaks-B, Inc. dba BMW of Sherman Oaks for 1. Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; 2. Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; 3. Violation of The Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2; and 4. Violation of The Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.22 – Tanner Consumer Protection Act.  Plaintiff leased a new 2020 BMW X5 xDrive 40i from Defendants. Plaintiff alleges the Subject Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to, a malfunctioning steering intervention, vehicle key detection issues, and electrical system defects.

 

On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint adding two additional causes of action for Negligent Repair and Strict Liability – Design Defect as well as punitive damages as a prayer for relief to this matter.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants BMW NA, LCL and BMW of Sherman Oaks’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants BMW NA, LLC and BMW of Sherman Oaks demur to the first amended complaint on the grounds each of Plaintiff’s six claims fails to state sufficient facts to support any of the claims Plaintiff alleges. Specifically, neither BMW NA nor BMW of Sherman Oaks were involved in the lease transaction which is the heart of Plaintiff’s allegations.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests this court take judicial notice of the lease agreement referenced in Plaintiff’s FAC.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff first argues the court should not consider the Lease Agreement attached to Defendants’ demurrer as Defendants have not provided sufficient information to demonstrate that the proffered copy of the lease agreement is not reasonably subject to dispute and is “capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.”

 

Defendants argue the agreement was incorporated by reference into Plaintiff’s FAC, and it is the definitive and indisputable source of rights and obligations regarding the Subject Vehicle and Plaintiff’s claims.  

 

The court finds judicial notice proper. Courts have taken judicial notice of documents referenced, but not attached to a complaint. (See Ingram v. Flippo (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1280, fn. 1.) Here, Plaintiff seeks to enforce express warranties under the lease agreement and seeks to revoke acceptance of the agreement. Moreover, the complaint alleges Plaintiff is entitled to rescission of the contract pursuant to Civil Code section 1794, et seq. and Commercial Code, section 2711 under each cause of action.

 

First and Second Causes of Action

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action for breach of express warranty and second cause of action for breach of implied warranty on the grounds Plaintiff fails to state sufficient facts to support its breach of express or implied warranty claims against BMW NA or BMW of Sherman Oaks.

 

As to BMW NA, Defendants argue it is impossible to identify what Plaintiff’s allegations are because he does not specify any defect(s) in the subject vehicle. Further, Plaintiff fails to state with reasonable particularity the substance of any alleged warrantable defect(s), on what date(s) he allegedly presented the vehicle, how many times he allegedly presented the vehicle, where the vehicle was presented, or the outcome of the alleged presentations. Defendants also contend BMW NA is a “distributor” under the Act, not a “manufacturer,” and thus cannot be held liable under this cause of action. As to BMW of Sherman Oaks, Defendants argue it did not lease the subject vehicle to Plaintiff, it was merely one of the several dealerships that Plaintiff presented his vehicle to twice. Although it may be a retail seller, it was not the retail seller for purposes of this FAC and it did not carry out any duties as a retail seller. Similarly, Plaintiff does not specify any defect(s) in the subject vehicle.

 

 In opposition, Plaintiff argues the complaint exactly pleads the elements for Breach of Express and Implied Warranty under the Song-Beverly Act.

 

The court disagrees. For statutory violations, “facts in support of each of the requirements of a statute” must be “specifically pled,” and “simply parroting the language” of a statute is insufficient to survive a demurrer. (Hawkins v. TACA Internat. Airlines, S.A. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 466, 477-478.) While the complaint does allege the defects suffered from including a malfunctioning steering intervention, vehicle key detection issues, and electrical system defects, the complaint fails to allege when he allegedly presented the vehicle for repair, how many times, where it was presented, and the outcome and so forth.

 

The court notes that as to the second cause of action, Plaintiff has limited it to merchantability. “Under the implied merchantability warranty, “every sale of consumer¿goods¿that are sold at retail in this state shall be accompanied by the manufacturer's and the retail seller's implied warranty that the goods are merchantable.”  (Civ. Code § 1792.) And while BMW NA states it is a distributor, it does not request judicial notice of such. The complaint alleges BMW NA is a manufacturer and/or distributor. Plaintiff

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first and second cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND to allege that defendant is a manufacturer, if indeed, plaintiff has a factual basis for doing so.

 

Third Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for violation of civil code section 1793.2 on the grounds Plaintiff fails to state sufficient facts.

 

Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(1) requires manufacturers to make repair facilities available to consumers, where vehicles can be repaired to conform to any express warranties provided by the manufacturers.   Civil Code § 1793.2(b) requires repairs to be commenced “within a reasonable time” and completed “within 30 days.”

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff merely recites the requisite elements of the statute but fails to support the allegations with facts. Further, BMW NA is not liable under this statute because it is not a manufacturer as defined by the statute. BMW of Sherman Oaks is similarly not liable because it is not the “manufacturer’s representative” as defined by the statute.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues under Section 1793.2(b), the term “representatives” is used as a collective noun that, in part, refers to agents which likely includes repair or service facilities that have been authorized by a manufacturer of consumer goods to carry out the terms of its express warranties. As such, Plaintiff’s FAC exactly pleads the elements for Section 1793.2 under the Song-Beverly Act including delivering the subject vehicle to an authorized service representative and Defendants failing to conform the subject vehicle within 30 days and/or commence repairs within a reasonable time.

 

The court finds the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim for violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(b). As noted above, the causes of action must be plead with specifically. The complaint does not allege how long the vehicle was in for repair or how long it was in there for repair when Defendant and its repair facilities failed to commence and complete repairs to the vehicle.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fourth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for Violation of the Tanner Consumer Protection Act on the grounds Plaintiff does not plead facts sufficient to state a cause of action against Defendants.

 

The Tanner Consumer Protection Act provides a presumption that a reasonable number of attempts to repair the vehicle have been made (i.e., a presumption that the third element of a Song-Beverly new vehicle express warranty claim is satisfied) when one of three enumerated factual situations is satisfied. (Jiagbogu v. Mercedes-Benz USA (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1235, 1244.)

 

Defendants argue the subject vehicle was not out of service for a cumulative total of 30 calendar days within the first 18 months/18,000 miles and Plaintiff failed to specify any details to the contrary. Moreover, Defendants are not liable because there are no facts which exist to support the allegation that the subject vehicle was out of service for a total of 30 days when it never was during its entire 3 year lease period.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Plaintiff’s FAC exactly pleads the elements for Section 1793.22. Again, the court disagrees. The complaint generally alleges “In the first l8 months/l8,000 miles of Plaintiff having owned the Subject Vehicle, Defendants have been given sufficient repair opportunities under Civil Code section 1793.22 (b)(1), (b)(2), and/or (b)(3), but failed to conform the vehicle to warranty.” (FAC ¶62.) This is insufficient.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fifth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the fifth cause of action for negligent repair on the grounds the cause of action is woefully deficient because Plaintiff has failed to plead any facts sufficient to survive demurrer.

 

In a negligent performance case, a “plaintiff will be entitled to recover economic loss damages without the need to allege and prove personal injury or property damage” based on a consideration of the following factors: “(1) the extent to which the transaction was intended to affect the plaintiff, (2) the foreseeability of harm to the plaintiff, (3) the degree of certainty that the plaintiff suffered injury, (4) the closeness of the connection between the defendant’s conduct and the injury suffered, (5) the moral blame attached to the defendant’s conduct, and (6) the policy of preventing future harm.”¿ (North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 782, 786.)¿ “[T]he foreseeability of the economic harm to the plaintiff from the defendant’s negligent conduct [is] the critical factor.”¿ (Id. at 782.)¿¿ 

 

Defendants argue they do not understand what conduct Plaintiff is alleging was tortious and/or compensable. Even if Defendants did understand Plaintiff’s allegations, he has pled no facts in the First Amended Complaint to support any negligence claim. Defendants further argue the cause of action is improperly brought against BMW NA because it is neither the retail seller, nor the servicing dealership, and did not have the opportunity to commit any of the alleged breaches of duty that Plaintiff would have to establish to prove a claim of negligent repair. Moreover, Defendants contend Plaintiff can plead no facts supporting a claim of negligent repair because BMW of Sherman Oaks correctly diagnosed the Subject vehicle as operating normally virtually every time Plaintiff presented the Subject Vehicle for repair, and correctly performed any necessary diagnoses and repairs to resolve Plaintiff’s complaints within a reasonable number of attempts. Lastly, Defendants contend the cause of action is barred by the economic loss rule.

 

While a negligence claim may be generally pled, the court sustains the demurrer to the fifth cause of action so Plaintiff may allege more facts along with the other causes of action. Moreover, the court finds the cause of action against BMW NA improper. Plaintiff has not alleged BMW NA is the one who repaired the vehicle or how BMW NA took part in the repair. As noted above, cases have found that the economic loss rule does not apply to cases involving the negligent performance of services. (North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 777-781 [holding the economic loss rule does not apply in cases involving the negligent performance of services that results in foreseeable economic loss].)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Sixth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the sixth cause of action for strict liability – design defect on the grounds the claim is deficient.

 

In California, a product is defective in design if ‘the product fails to meet ordinary consumer expectations as to safety’ or ‘the design is not as safe as it should be.’ [Citations.]” (Johnson v. United States Steel Corp. (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 22, 31–32.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s FAC fails to state with any level of specificity what the alleged design defect actually is. (FAC ¶ 80.) Instead, the complaint broadly states the elements of a design defect claim, without providing any case specific facts to support such allegations. Moreover, Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts to substantiate an allegation that Plaintiff was actually harmed by the alleged design defect - there are no facts in the FAC that indicate the actual harm suffered by Plaintiff with any level specificity, instead the claim merely states that “Plaintiff was harmed by the Subject Vehicle.” (FAC ¶ 81.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff has failed to allege facts sufficient for a strict liability claim. Plaintiff has failed to allege any facts to substantiate what the defect is.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.


 


Case Number: 23VECV03952    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: W

KHACHATOUR C. PAPAZIAN, et al. v. GENERAL MOTORS LLC, et al.

 

plaintiff’s MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE AND FOR ISSUE/EVIDENTIARY SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT GENERAL MOTORS

LLC FOR FAILURE TO COMPLY WITH THE FEBRUARY 22, 2024 ORDER AND REQUEST FOR $13,773.30 IN SANCTIONS

 

Date of Hearing:        May 2, 2024                                       Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03952

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Khachatour C. Papazian and Complete Total Care Inc.

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Khachatour C. Papazian filed a complaint against General Motors LLC and Cada De Cadillac for 1. Breach of Express Warranty under the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act; 2. Breach of Implied Warranty under the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act; 3. Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200; and 4. Negligent Repair. On January 28, 2021, Plaintiff purchased from an authorized seller of Defendant General Motors LLC a 2021 GMC Yukon. Plaintiff alleges shortly after taking possession of the vehicle, Plaintiff experienced various defects that substantially impair the use, value and/or safety of the vehicle and continues to experience repeated problems with the subject vehicle.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance and for issue/evidentiary sanctions against Defendant General Motors LLC for Failure to Comply with the February 22, 2024 Order and Request for $13,773.30 in Sanctions is GRANTED, in part.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiffs Khachatour C. Papazian and Complete Total Care Inc.  move the court for an order compelling compliance with the Court's February 22, 2024 discovery order that directed GM to provide further responses and documents production in response to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (SROG No. 12-17, 23, 25-28, 37-42) and Request for Production of Documents (RPD No. 26-31, 34, 36-40).

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 provides that disobeying a court order to provide discovery is a misuse of discovery. (CCP § 2023.010(g).) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128(a)(4) a court has the power to “compel obedience to its judgement, orders, and process, and to the orders of a judge out of court, in an action or proceeding pending therein.”   

 

On February 20, 2024, this court took Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set One) and Request for Production of Documents (Set One) under submission. On February 22, 2024, the court mailed the minute order, which granted Plaintiff’s request for further responses but denied sanctions. (Minute Order February 22, 2024.) Although the minute order did not state when responses were due by, Plaintiff took this to be 30 days. (Yepremian ¶13.) As of the date of this motion, however, Plaintiff has yet to receive any responses. (Yepremian ¶13.) Plaintiff argues this is not an oversight by Defendant but a pattern of gamesmanship. As such, in addition to compliance with the February 22, 2024 Order, Plaintiff is seeking evidentiary and/or issue sanctions. Specifically, it is requested that the Court enter a finding of liability against GM on the issues of breach of the express/implied warranty and a finding of civil penalty exposure and that a prove-up hearing on damages be set at the court's convenience. Alternatively, Plaintiff seeks this court impose evidentiary/issue sanctions with respect to the civil penalty issues. (See Mot. p. 6:11-22.) Plaintiff also seeks sanctions against GM in the amount of $4,861.65 each for the prior Motions to Compel Further Responses and an additional $4,111.65 in preparing said Motion with a grand total of $13,773.30.

The motion is unopposed. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel compliance. However, the court finds issue and terminating sanctions are not warranted at this time. Discovery sanctions must be tailored to the alleged discovery abuse and cannot be used to punish the offending party. (Karlsson v. Ford Motor Co. (20 App.4th 1202, 1217 (“Discovery sanctions must be tailored in order to remedy the offending party's discovery abuse, should not give the aggrieved party more than what it is entitled to, and should not be used to punish the offending party”); Rail Services of America v. State Comp. Ins. Fund (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 323, 332 (“A discovery sanction may not place the party seeking discovery in a better position than it would have been in if the desired discovery had been provided and had been favorable.”) Issuing issue or evidentiary sanctions based upon Defendant’s failure to provide further responses after one motion to compel further would violate such principles.

 

The court, however, grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $2,250.00. The court has already denied Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in bringing the motions to compel further. Moreover, no opposition has been filed.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Compliance and for issue/evidentiary sanctions against Defendant General Motors LLC for Failure to Comply with the February 22, 2024 Order and Request for $13,773.30 in Sanctions is GRANTED, in part.
