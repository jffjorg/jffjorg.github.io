DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24AHCV00438    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL SANCTIONS AND/OR STRIKE OBJECTIONS


I.                INTRODUCTION

            On February 29, 2024, Plaintiff Oscar Gabriel Vargas (“Plaintiff” or “Vargas”) filed an action against Defendants Freedom Forever, LLC (“FF”), Blue Collar Logistics, LLC (“Blue Collar”), Chester Ralph Gonzales (“Gonzales”), and Does 1 through 50, for negligence.  The action arises out of an alleged traffic collision which took place around March 2, 2022, between Plaintiff and Defendant Gonzales.  Gonzales was operating his vehicle in the course of employment and/or with the express or implied authority and permission of Defendants FF and Blue Collar.  As a result of the collision and negligence of Defendants, Plaintiff suffered severe injuries and sustained economic loss and damages to personal property.

            Defendant FF filed an Answer on April 16, 2024, and Defendant Gonzales filed an Answer on June 3, 2024.

            On July 29, 2024, Defendants FF and Gonzales filed the instant Motion for Sanctions and/or to Strike Frivolous Boilerplate Objections (“Motion”).  Plaintiff filed an Opposition on September 3, 2024.  No reply has been filed.

            The Motion is denied.

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

A.    Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).)  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Id. at § 436, subd. (b).)  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  (Id. at § 437.)

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 requires that “[b]efore filing a motion to strike pursuant to this chapter, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).)

B.    Motion to Compel Further Responses

Code of Civil Procedure, section 2030.300 provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that” the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete; (2) an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, subd. (a).)  Notice of the motions must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, subd. (c).)  The motion must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration “showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2016.040, 2030.300, subd. (b).)

Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure, section 2033.290 provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to requests for admission, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that” the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete or (2) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, subd. (a).)  Notice of the motions must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.290, subd. (c).)  The motion must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration “showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2016.040, 2033.290, subd. (b).)

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(2)).

C.    Motion for Sanctions

Monetary sanctions may be imposed “ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct…If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust..”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).)

Furthermore, Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300, subdivision (d), and 2033.290, subdivision (d), provide for the issuance of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the court finds the party who would be subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make sanctions unjust.

III.           ANALYSIS

A.     Defendants’ Motion

Defendants move for an order imposing sanctions on Plaintiff and his counsel for frivolous and boilerplate objections and failure to provide Code-compliant discovery responses.  Sanctions are being sought in the amount of $2,025 for attorney’s fees and $60 for costs.  Defendants also move for an order striking improper boilerplate objections.

Defendants propounded the following discovery requests upon Plaintiff: Special Interrogatories, Form Interrogatories, and Requests for Admission.  (Humphrey Decl. ¶ 2A-2C, Exs. A-C.)  Defendants state that Plaintiff’s initial responses were inappropriate and not Code-compliant, thus, Defendants sent an extensive meet and confer letter requesting further responses.  (Id. at ¶ 2D, Ex. D.)  Plaintiff served further responses which were insufficient and not Code-complaint as each set of discovery was prefaced with “General Response and Objections.”  (Id. at ¶ 2E-2G, Exs. E-G.)  Defendants state that they sent another meet and confer letter on June 25, 2024, attempting to explain to Plaintiff’s counsel that each discovery request requires a separate response and there is no authority that allows a 51-line general objection.  (Id. at ¶ 2H, Ex. H.)  They received a response that Plaintiff would not be providing any further responses, and the objections were presented to preserve the record.  (Id. at ¶ 2H, Ex. I.)

Defendants argue that pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2033.010, 2033.210, subdivisions (a) and (b), and 2033.250, the requesting party may serve requests for admissions and within 30 days, the responding party must serve responses under oath separately addressing each request, either with a substantive response or an objection to the particular request.  Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.210, subdivision (a), and 2030.220, the requesting party may serve interrogatories, and the responding party must serve responses separately addressing each interrogatory, either with an answer, production of writing containing the answer, or an objection to the particular interrogatory.  Here, Plaintiff has provided boilerplate responses, not tailored to each discovery request, and “apparently designed to frustrate discovery and create delays.”  (Mot. pp. 6, 15.)  Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s conduct is “sanctionable discovery abuse” and cite both California and federal cases for this assertion.  (Ibid.)  Defendants also make arguments regarding document production demands; however, these arguments are irrelevant as the instant Motion does not pertain to a document production demand.

Defendants argue that pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivisions (e) and (f), sanctions may be imposed for unmeritorious objections and misuse of discovery, as well as failing to meet and confer in good faith to resolve a discovery dispute.  Here, Defendants have demonstrated that there are no provisions in California law that allow for boilerplate objections to each set of discovery.  Moreover, Plaintiff never responded to the June 13, 2024, meet and confer letter to resolve this discovery dispute.

Defendants also seek an order striking the boilerplate objections and requiring Plaintiff to serve proper, code-compliant responses to each identified discovery request.  Defendants have filed a “Separate Statement” that is nearly identical to the Memorandum of Points and Authorities.

B.     Plaintiff’s Opposition

Plaintiff argues that Defendants have not clearly set forth the relief they are seeking through the Motion, or presented good cause for granting any of the relief requested.

Plaintiff argues that although general objections were included as a preface to each set of discovery responses, Plaintiff also provided full and complete separate answers to each interrogatory and request for admission.  As stated in Civil Code section 3532, “The law neither does nor requires idle acts.”  Here, Defendants are asking the Court to perform an idle act by striking the general objection presented in the preface of the responses, which Defendants admit is ineffective.  Moreover, Plaintiff argues that it is customary for attorneys to present prefatory general objections, which should not be a concern where such objections are followed by full and complete substantive answers.  Defendants themselves admit that many lawyers follow this method of responding to discovery requests.

Plaintiff also argues that the Motion is fraught with procedural errors.  First, the Discovery Act does not give the Court authority to strike portions of a discovery response, but only to compel an initial or further response.  Second, if Defendants are seeking to compel further responses, they have not filed a proper separate statement.  Defendants have submitted a repetitive memorandum of points and authorities in lieu of a separate statement that does not comply with the requirements of Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subdivision (c).  There is no evidence that the Court allowed Defendants to submit a concise outline instead of a separate statement, pursuant to subdivision (b).  Third, Defendants have improperly filed three different motions—motion to strike, motion to compel further responses, and motion for sanctions—as a single motion.  They have also improperly filed a single motion to compel further responses to three different discovery requests—form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for admission.  Fourth, the Notice of Motion is defective as it does not comply with Rules of Court, rule 3.1112, subdivision (d)(2), in naming the party against whom it is directed.

Plaintiff also argues that a motion to strike may only be filed to strike portions of a pleading, including a complaint, cross-complaint, answer, or demurrer, on the basis that it is irrelevant, false, improper, or not drawn in conformity with the law.  Here, Defendants move to strike portions of discovery responses and do not demonstrate that the Discovery Act provides the Court with authority to strike portions of discovery responses.  This motion also fails substantively because Plaintiff has provided separate and complete answers to each interrogatory and request for admission in question.  Furthermore, the time to bring another motion to compel further responses has expired, so moving parties have waived the right to demand further responses.

Finally, Plaintiff argues that monetary sanctions may be imposed when a party or attorney unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel interrogatories pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, subdivision (d), unless the opposing party acted with substantial justification.  Here, moving party’s motion must be denied and so must its request for sanctions.  Moreover, Plaintiff acted with substantial justification as it provided prefatory objections to preserve them in the same manner as other attorneys respond to discovery.  These general objections have not caused any delay, as Plaintiff has provided full and complete substantive answers to each discovery request.

C.    Analysis

Having reviewed the moving papers and opposition, the Court makes the following findings.

a.     Improperly Combined Motions

Defendants are seeking the following forms of relief through the Motion: (1) order striking Plaintiff’s general objections, (2) an order compelling further responses to the discovery requests, and (3) sanctions for discovery abuse.

As noted by Plaintiff, Defendants have improperly combined separate requests into one motion.  Moreover, instead of filing separate motions to compel further responses addressing different sets of discovery requests, Defendants filed a single motion.

Combining motions allows the moving party to avoid paying the requisite filing fees.  Filing fees are jurisdictional and it is mandatory for court clerks to demand and receive them.  (See Duran v. St. Luke's Hospital (2003) 114 Cal. App.4th 457,460; Gov. Code § 70617, subd. (a); see also Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2011) [8:1140.1] (“Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately.”)

Moreover, the fact that each motion is authorized by a different statute and the relief available for each device is substantively different indicates the intent of the legislature to require separate motions for each discovery device.  Regarding motions to compel further responses, each such motion requires a separate statement under Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, thus, the effect is to require each request for further response to provide a separate motion as a matter of course.

b.     Motion to Strike

Defendants move to strike the introductory section containing general objections presented in Plaintiff’s responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for admission.

First, Defendants do not cite legal authority that would allow the Court to strike portions of discovery responses.  A motion to strike is generally based on Code of Civil Procedure sections 435 and 436, which give the Court the authority to strike “any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading” or “all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  Thus, the Court does not have the authority to strike discovery responses pursuant to sections 435 and 436.  Defendants do not provide the Court with any other legal authority, particularly in the Discovery Act, to strike the general objections prefacing Plaintiff’s discovery responses.

For this reason, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request to strike portions of the discovery responses.

c.      Motion to Compel Further Responses

Defendants argue that where general boilerplate objections are made, courts have authority to compel further responses.  (Best Products, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 119 Cal. App.4th 1181, 1189.)

            Defendants’ request to compel further responses is deficient for the following reasons.

First, Defendants do not cite any authority that would specifically allow the Court to compel further responses to interrogatories or requests for admission, including Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300 and 2033.290.

            Second, as discussed previously, Defendants address three sets of discovery requests in a single motion.

            Third, the Motion was filed untimely.  Notice of a motion to compel further responses must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (c), 2033.290, subd. (c).)  The Proofs of Service attached to Plaintiff’s second set of responses demonstrate that Plaintiff served responses to the form and special interrogatories and requests for admission on June 10, 2024, by electronic transmission.  (Mot. pp. 89, 150, 162.)  The instant Motion was filed on July 29, 2024, 49 days after service of the discovery responses, thus, the propounding party already waived its right to compel further responses.

Fourth, the Separate Statement filed along with the Motion does not comply with Rules of Court, rule 3.1345 as it is a nearly identical reiteration of the Memorandum of Points and Authorities.  California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(2)).

            For these reasons, the Court denies Defendants’ request to compel further responses to its discovery requests.

d.     Motion for Sanctions

Monetary sanctions may be imposed “ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct…If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust..”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).)

Specifically, Code of Civil Procedure sections 2030.300, subdivision (d), and 2033.290, subdivision (d), provide for the issuance of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the court finds the party who would be subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make sanctions unjust.

Here, Defendants argue that sanctions are warranted pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivisions (e), (f), and (i), because Plaintiff has made, “without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery,” provided “an evasive response to discovery,” and failed to meet and confer in good faith.

As stated above, Defendants have improperly combined several requests into one motion.

Moreover, the proper response to receiving unmeritorious objections or evasive responses to discovery is to file a motion to compel further responses.  Here, Defendants filed an untimely and deficient motion to compel further responses, which is denied.  Thus, the Court does not find a legal basis to grant monetary sanctions.  Defendants’ motion for monetary sanctions is denied.

IV.           CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants Freedom Forever, LLC’s and Chester Ralph Gonzales’s Motion for Sanctions and/or to Strike Frivolous Boilerplate Objections is DENIED.
