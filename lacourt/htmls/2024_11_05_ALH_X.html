Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling. 



Case Number: 21STCV14537    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: X

MOTION TO COMEPL COMPLIANCE WITH RECORDS SUBPOENA

Plaintiff moves for an order to compel the California Commission on Teacher Credentialing (hereinafter “Commission” or “CCTC”) to produce all records related to Defendant Jeremy Hules (“Hules”) in response to Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”)’s deposition subpoena.

This is a sexual assault case.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Jeremy Hules, during his employment as a teacher of the Los Angeles Unified School District (“District”), committed acts of sexual abuse against Plaintiff and other students when Plaintiff was a minor student of the District.  The complaint asserts the following causes of action: (1) childhood sexual assault, (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (3) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, (4) failure to report suspected child abuse, (5) negligent supervision of a minor, and (6) negligence.

Tentative Ruling

Plaintiff’s motion to compel the Commission’s compliance with her subpoena for business records is GRANTED in part.

Legal Analysis 

Generally speaking, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010; but see Catholic Mutual Relief Society v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 358, 366, fn. 6 [“The permissible scope of discovery in general is not as broad with respect to nonparties as it is with respect to parties.”].)

“If a subpoena requires . . . the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made . . . may make an order . . . directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare[.]”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  “In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”  (Ibid.)

On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff served a deposition subpoena for the production of business records on the Commission.  (Suh Decl., Exh. 1.)  Plaintiff requested 9 categories of documents, including any and all documents regarding the Commission’s investigation and/or hearing regarding Hules, disciplinary action taken by the Commission against Hules, complaints filed against Hules and response to complaints filed by Hules, alleged “improper behavior” by Hules, documents submitted by Hules to the Commission, correspondence between the Commission and Hules, correspondence between the Commission and LAUSD regarding Hules, and the Commission’s “complete file” of Hules.

On July 31, 2024, the Commission produced 54 pages of documents, which included Hules’ credential history, credentials, and documents pertaining to Hules’ credential applications.  The Commission redacted personal information.  Hules’ responses to questions regarding professional conduct (“Personal and Professional Fitness”) were redacted from documents entitled Application for Credential Authorizing Public School Service.  (Suh Decl., Exh. 4.)  The Commission also sent an objection letter along with its production of documents.  The letter stated that, except for the documents identified above, (1) the Subpoena seeks information deemed confidential under the California Education Code; (2) the Subpoena seeks records that are not business records of the Commission and are equally available or in the possession of the subpoenaing party; (3) the Subpoena seeks privileged information; and (4) the Subpoena seeks information subject to statutory privacy protections.  (Suh Decl., Exh. 3.)

The production contains redactions that render several of the documents useless.  For example, all information concerning Hules’ personal and professional character and fitness were redacted, including his responses as to whether he had been the subject of any disciplinary action, criminal charges, and whether he was terminated from a school based on misconduct.  (Suh Decl., Exh. 4, pp. 12, 17-18, 24, 26, 41 and 47.)  Plaintiff submits that no records concerning complaints, suspensions, revocations, or the Commission’s investigatory efforts as to Hules’s credential were produced. 

Plaintiff moves to compel the Commission to comply with the subpoena and (1) produce all withheld records requested in Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena and (2) release complete, unredacted versions of records already produced regarding Hules’ personal and professional character and fitness.  

Generally, the Commission fails to provide any persuasive reasoning or authority that overcomes the relevancy of the sought records.  The records are relevant to determine whether the District was on notice of Hules’ alleged improper conduct and how the District responded to any complaints.  The Commission cites authority that does not prevent this Court from ordering production.  In fact, the cited authority suggests that this Court does have authority to order production.  (See Ed. Code, §§ 44230, 44245.)

The Court agrees that certain third-party privacy interests may require redactions, such as the personal identifying information of third parties, including the dates of birth and social security numbers of other credential holders who are unrelated to the underlying conduct at issue in this case.  But any responses by Hules should be disclosed.

The Commission argues that the subpoena seeks records that are not business records of the Commission and can be obtained from other sources.  For example, some of the documents not produced by the Commission include “Hules’ academic transcripts and examination results (prepared by a third-party), LAUSD incident and investigative documents (prepared by a third-party), and Los Angeles County Superior Court Case No. 21STCV14537 civil case filing documents (primarily prepared by Plaintiff).”  (Opp., p. 5.)

A deposition subpoena for business records is to be “directed to the custodian of those records or another person qualified to certify the records.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2020.410(c).)  “At first blush, a person or entity that maintains records would also be the custodian of those records. Nevertheless, the custodian of records or other qualified witness contemplated by Evidence Code section 1561 must also be able to attest to various attributes of the records relevant to their authenticity and trustworthiness. As such, execution of a section 1561 affidavit is more than simply a clerical task.”  (Cooley v. Superior Court (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 1039, 1044.)  The Commission argues the Court should deny Plaintiff’s motion to the extent it seeks documents prepared and maintained by third parties as it cannot attest to the contents. 

In reply, Plaintiff argues it is far more difficult for Plaintiff to discover what documents remain from an investigation into Hules thirteen years ago.  Plaintiff argues that just because the Commission cannot attest to the records in its possession does not bar the Court from ordering production.  In all cases, “it is not necessary that the witness called to present foundational facts have personal knowledge of every transaction; he need only be familiar with the procedures followed.”  (Conservatorship of S.A. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 438, 449 [citing Jazayeri v. Mao (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 301, 322].)  As such, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that it is improper for the Commission to withhold non-privileged records that it can authenticate.  Such records necessarily include all documents prepared by Commission employees that derive from documents prepared by third parties.  These documents, specifically, are not shielded from disclosure by section 1561 of the Evidence Code.

The Court also declines to find that the Official Informational privilege (Evid. Code, § 1040) applies here.  The official information privilege only applies to “official information,” which is defined as “information acquired in confidence by a public employee in the course of his or her duty and not open, or officially disclosed, to the public prior to the time the claim of privilege is made.”  (Evid. Code, § 1040, subd. (a).)  Said information can only be withheld if:
 
(1)  Disclosure is forbidden by an act of the Congress of the United States or a statute of this state.
 
(2)  Disclosure of the information is against the public interest because there is a necessity for preserving the confidentiality of the information that outweighs the necessity for disclosure in the interest of justice; but no privilege may be claimed under this paragraph if any person authorized to do so has consented that the information be disclosed in the proceeding.  In determining whether disclosure of the information is against the public interest, the interest of the public entity as a party in the outcome of the proceeding may not be considered.
 
(Evid. Code, § 1040, subds. (b)(1)-(2).)
 
Here, even accepting that the withheld documents contain “official information,” the Commission has not shown that the Commission’s interest in confidentiality “clearly outweigh[s]” the interests of an individual litigant seeking information that may assist with her case.  

However, the Court finds that certain records may be protected by the Deliberative Process privilege, as contended by the Commission.  More specifically, Plaintiff is seeking any and all documents related to “statements of decision, findings, summaries, opinions, rulings, and/or minutes regarding any California Commission on Teacher Credentialing investigation and/or hearing regarding JEREMY HULES[.]”  (Suh Decl., Exh. 1, Requested Document No. 1.)  Suspension and revocation decisions are made in closed session, and the deliberations are not subject to public disclosure.  (See Education Code, §§ 44230, 44245, 44248; See Gov. Code, § 6254, subdivision (f); see also Civil Code section 1798.24.)  However, withholding “purely factual, investigative” documents is only proper upon an additional showing that the requests are “actually . . . related to the process by which policies are formulated” or “inextricably intertwined with policy-making processes.”  (See Times Mirror Co v. Superior Court (1992) 53 Cal.3d at pp. 1325, 1342.)  As such, the Commission is only entitled to protect records which are “actually . . . related to the process by which policies are formulated” or “inextricably intertwined with policy-making processes.”  (Ibid.)
 
Taken together, the Court finds that the Commission must comply with the majority of Plaintiff’s subpoena requests.  The Court determines that the Commission need not produce documents which are “actually . . . related to the process by which policies are formulated” or “inextricably intertwined with policy-making processes.”  (Times Mirror Co., supra, 53 Cal.3d at p. 1342.)

Case Number: 24AHCV00216    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: X


MOTION TO QUASH PLAINTIFF’S DEPOSITION SUBPOENA 

Defendants move to quash Plaintiff’s deposition subpoena for production of business records by non-party DB Insurance Co.

Plaintiff filed a complaint against his homeowners association (HOA) and individuals in the HOA, alleging six causes of action arising out of his ownership of a condominium unit in a complex he shared with Defendants.  According to Plaintiff, he observed issues on the property, including a roof that needed to be replaced but was ignored; these property issues caused damage to his unit and the common areas. 

Tentative Ruling

The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to quash and modifies the subpoena request.

Legal Analysis

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of documents, the court may, upon motion reasonably made, make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1(a).)

Defendants argue the subpoena seeks information and documents from a liability insurer’s claim file, which is not discoverable in a third-party context for reasons of work-product privilege, as the file was prepared in anticipation of litigation.  Also, Defendants argue the insurance claim files are subject to a right to privacy.  The motion also argues the subpoena seeks irrelevant information and documents from DB Insurance Co., a non-party insurer, which is not discoverable by a third-party claimant.

The subpoena issued to DB Insurance Co. (Defendant’s liability insurer) describes the records to be produced as follows:

All WRITINGS comprising the November 1, 2023, claim files for Policy No. CBP 2320005 01, Claim No. 23CBP0000404 by insured, Monterey Vista HOA, LLC, including recorded statements, investigations, invoices, receipts, correspondence and any other document constituting evidence that was considered by YOU in the course of the investigating and concluding the claim.

(Gravdal Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.)

The motion argues the subpoena seeks documents protected by statute (Insurance Code, § 791.13).  The motion also argues the information sought is private and irrelevant and thus the subpoena should be quashed. 

The Insurance Code establishes that insurance records are privileged and thus may only be produced in discovery upon a showing that the interests of the propounding party outweigh the interest of the other party in keeping the information private.  (Ins. Code., § 791.3; Griffith v. Allstate Mutual Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 59, 68.)  “However, even though the policy limits are ‘personal,’ the Insurance Code authorizes the release of such information in a number of different situations.  An authorization from the insured enables the carrier to release the information.  (Ins. Code, § 791.13, subd. (a).)  Additionally, disclosure is authorized if it is ‘[o]therwise permitted or required by law.’  (Ins. Code, § 791.13, subd. (g).)”  (Griffith, supra, at p. 68.)

Production of private documents may be ordered where there is an interest in production which justifies infringement on the right of privacy.  (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855.)  There must also be no other, less intrusive means of obtaining the information.  (Id., at pp. 855-856.)  “ ‘When the right to discovery conflicts with a privileged right, the court is required to carefully balance the right of privacy with the need for discovery.’  Discovery may be compelled only upon a showing of a compelling public interest.  In those situations where it is argued that a party waives protection by filing a lawsuit, the court must construe the concept of ‘waiver’ narrowly and a compelling public interest is demonstrated only where the material sought is directly relevant to the litigation.  The party seeking the constitutionally protected information has the burden of establishing that the information sought is directly relevant to the claims.”  (Tylo v. Superior Court (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1387, citations omitted, italics in original.)

However, in Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, the California Supreme Court rejected the need to always show a compelling interest to justify the production of private information.  The Supreme Court clarified that, even where a party has shown a discovery request will intrude on its right to privacy, the burden remains on the party moving to quash the discovery to show the extent and seriousness of the prospective invasion.  (Williams, supra, at p. 557.)  The court must then weigh the countervailing interests identified by the opposing party as required by Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1.  “Only obvious invasions of interests fundamental to personal autonomy must be supported by a compelling interest.  To the extent prior cases require a party seeking discovery of private information to always establish a compelling interest or compelling need, without regard to the other considerations articulated in Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn., they are disapproved.”  (Ibid., citations and footnote omitted.)

Here, Plaintiff seeks production of all writings comprising of the November 1, 2023 claim files by the insured HOA defendant, “including recorded statements, investigations, invoices, receipts, correspondence and any other document constituting evidence that was considered by [DB] in the course of the investigating and concluding the claim.” 

The Court determines that the subpoena requests records which may be relevant to Plaintiff’s claim.  Plaintiff learned in discovery that in approximately November 2023—before this lawsuit was filed in February 2024—the HOA started a property damage insurance claim.  (Opp., p. 2.)  Plaintiff further argues that the request is narrowly tailored as Plaintiff is not seeking any documents that are related to the liability side of the insurance policy related to the defense of his claims against the HOA and individual owners.  (Ibid.)  The request seeks records related to the November 1, 2023 property damage claim.  However, DB’s investigations are privileged and not subject to disclosure, but any communications, documents, invoices, or reports prepare or provided by a third-party and which are not privileged pursuant to the attorney-client privilege or work product doctrine can be produced to Plaintiff.  To the extent that any privilege is attached to the latter documents, Defendant is to provide a privilege log of any documents withheld.  Compliance with the Court’s order is required within 30 days of mailing of a notice of ruling.

The Court declines to award sanctions on behalf of either party. 

Moving party is to give notice.

DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE
 
Defendant demurs to the second cause of action (fraudulent inducement) and moves to strike references to punitive damages in the first amended complaint.

This is a lemon law case arising from Plaintiffs purchase of a used, certified pre-owned 2017 Honda Pilot in 2022. The first amended complaint alleges two causes of action: (1) breach of express warranty (Song-Beverly) and (2) fraudulent inducement – concealment.

Tentative Ruling

Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action in the complaint is OVERRULED.  Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.  Defendant is to answer the operative complaint within 20 days.

Legal Analysis

Demurrer to Second Cause of Action for Fraud
 
Fraudulent inducement requires a showing of (1) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of its falsity; (3) intent to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) a resulting damage.  (Dhital v. Nissan North American, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 838, review granted February 1, 2023.)  In Dhital, the defendant argued that because plaintiffs bought a vehicle from a dealership and not directly from defendant, that plaintiff had not adequately pled the buyer-seller relationship.  (Id., at p. 844.)  Defendant similarly argues here that because Plaintiff purchased from a dealership, they have not adequately pled that Honda is a participant in any potential fraudulent inducement by concealment.  In Dhital, the court noted that plaintiffs had sufficiently alleged defendant’s involvement where the allegations included that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan’s authorized dealerships are its agents for sales purposes.  (Ibid.)  Here, Plaintiff alleges that he purchased the car from a Honda dealership with an express warranty from Honda and that the dealership is Honda’s agent.  (FAC, ¶¶ 9, 114.) 

Additionally, in Dhital, the defendant argued that the allegations were not specific about what should have been disclosed.  That court noted that plaintiffs alleged the specifics of the defects and that defendant was aware of the defects because of their premarket testing and consumer complaints made to defendant, its dealers, and to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (“NHTSA”).  (Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at p.  838.)  Here, Plaintiff alleged the transmission defect and his having experienced the transmission’s (1) sudden unexpected and violent jerking or shaking when attempting to shift into higher gear settings, (2) hesitation when accelerating plus unexpected acceleration, and (3) a hard downshift when decelerating.  (FAC, ¶ 13.)  Plaintiff also alleged that Honda was aware of the defects based on premarket testing and consumer complaints. (FAC, ¶ 16.)
 
Dhital refrained from addressing the issue of whether the complaint sufficiently alleged corporate liability for punitive damages for the motion to strike.  (Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th at fn. 8.)  Generally, fraud actions are required to be pled with particularity.  (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216, superseded by statute on other grounds.)  Allegations of fraud against a corporation require that the plaintiff allege who made the fraudulent representation and that person’s authority to speak.  (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)  However, less specificity is required when the defendant has superior knowledge of the facts.  (Committee on Children’s Television, supra, 35 Cal.3d at p. 217.) 

Here, Plaintiff alleged that Defendant has exclusive knowledge of the transmission defect since at least 2012.  (FAC, ¶ 22.)  The FAC has sufficiently alleged the fraudulent inducement cause of action.

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED. 

Motion to Strike Punitive Damages

Defendant moves to strike the punitive damages from the FAC based on similar grounds as Defendant’s arguments concerning the viability of the second cause of action for fraud.  Defendant further argues that punitive damages should be stricken where Plaintiff has failed to comply with Civil Code section 3294(b).  The Court determines that the allegations, if taken as true, are sufficient to maintain a punitive damages claim.

Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED.
