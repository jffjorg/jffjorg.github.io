DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 19STCV26512    Hearing Date: June 11, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     June 11, 2024             JUDGMENT: December 5, 2023

                                                          

CASE:                         Frontline Medical Associates, Inc. v. Benjamin Gluck, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 19STCV26512           

 

MOTION TO REQUIRE UNDERTAKING

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Bird, Marella, Boxer, Wolpert, Nessim, Drooks, Lincenberg & Rhow, P.C.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 6/6/24.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         07/30/19: Complaint filed.

·         12/16/21: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         10/27/23: Terminating sanctions granted against Plaintiff.

·         12/05/23: Judgment entered.

·         02/01/24: Appeal filed by Plaintiff.

·         03/05/24: Amended Judgment entered.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This was an action for fraud, conversion, and breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff alleged that the lawyer Defendants made misrepresentations to it so that it would pay them $600,000 to provide services to another of their clients, Paul Turley. The Court granted terminating sanctions on October 27, 2023 for repeated and flagrant misconduct by Plaintiff and its former counsel of record and perpetration of a fraud on this Court.

 

Defendant Bird, Marella, Boxer, Wolpert, Nessim, Drooks, Lincenberg & Hrow, P.C. moves tor require Plaintiff to furnish an underaking pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 917.9.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant’s Motion to Require Undertaking is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff is ordered to deposit an undertaking with this Court in the amount of $263,058.42 within 5 days of this order.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant Bird, Marella, Boxer, Wolpert, Nessim, Drooks, Lincenberg & Hrow, P.C. moves to require Plaintiff to furnish an undertaking pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 917.9.

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 916(a) states that, “[e]xcept as provided in Sections 917.1 to 917.9, inclusive, and in Section 116.810, the perfecting of an appeal stays proceedings in the trial court upon the judgment or order appealed from or upon the matters embraced therein or affected thereby, including enforcement of the judgment or order, but the trial court may proceed upon any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc § 916(a).) Ordinarily, under section 917.1, an appeal from an order or judgment for money or for costs not available under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5 does not stay enforcement of the judgment unless an undertaking is given. (Code Civ. Proc. § 917.1(a).) Where the award is solely for costs, however, enforcement of the judgment is automatically stayed and no undertaking is required. (Code Civ. Proc. § 917.1(d).) Notwithstanding that exception, section 917.9 provides, in relevant part:

 

(a) The perfecting of an appeal shall not stay enforcement of the judgment or order in cases not provided for in Sections 917.1 to 917.8, inclusive, if the trial court, in its discretion, requires an undertaking and the undertaking is not given, in any of the following cases:

 

. . .

 

(3) The judgment against appellant is solely for costs awarded to the respondent by the trial court pursuant to Chapter 6 (commencing with Section 1021) of Title 14.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 917.9(a)(3).) Thus, “[i]n a case where the costs judgment is large or the danger of asset dissipation is acute,” the Court may, in its discretion, impose an undertaking requirement and thereby “mitigate any injustices arising from the costs-only judgment rule.” (Quiles v. Parent (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 130, 144-45.) If the Court exercises that discretion, “[t]he undertaking shall be in a sum fixed by the court and shall be in an amount sufficient to cover all damages which the respondent may sustain by reason of the stay in the enforcement of the judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 917.9(b).) That undertaking “shall be conditioned upon the payment of the sums required by the judgment or order appealed from if the judgment or order is affirmed or the appeal is withdrawn or dismissed,” and, in such case, “the appellant will pay all damages which the respondent may sustain by reason of the stay in enforcement of the judgment.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 917.9(c).) This includes an amount sufficient to cover the costs awarded to the responding party. (Code Civ. Proc. § 917.9(d)(2).) 

 

            Defendant requests that the Court exercise its discretionary authority to require Plaintiff to furnish an undertaking for the entire award of costs, totaling $263,058.42. Defendant bases this request on the obfuscated and convoluted status of Plaintiff’s assets and ownership as an ostensibly dissolved corporation (see Declaration of Hilary Potashner ISO Mot. Exh. 2) and its conduct throughout the course of this action. Defendant contends that, if an undertaking is not required, there is an acute danger that Plaintiff will dissolve or distribute its assets to evade the reach of the judgment, which, as Defendant accurately observes, is quite large.

 

            Rather than recapitulate the lengthy procedural history of this case and disentangle the layers of contradictory testimony by Plaintiff and its witnesses concerning Frontline’s ownership and assets, the Court will simply reference its October 27, 2023 ruling granting terminating sanctions in which that information was set forth in copious detail. (See October 27, 2023 Minute Order.) Based on the evidence presented in connection with that motion, the Court is persuaded that there is a substantial risk of Plaintiff disposing of its assets or otherwise evading the judgment. The Court therefore finds good cause to require Plaintiff to furnish an undertaking in an amount equal to the award of costs to Defendant.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Require Undertaking is GRANTED.

 

            Plaintiff is ordered to deposit an undertaking with this Court in the amount of $263,058.42 within 5 days of this order.

CASE:                         Martel D. Walker et al v. Avalon Hollywood GP, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV03253           

 

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Avalonbay Communities, Inc., Avalon Hollywood GP, LLC, and Avalon Hollywood LP.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Martel Walker and Lindsay Gold, in pro per

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         01/27/21: Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of a rental lease contract, breach of the implied warranty of habitability, fraud, and negligence.

 

Defendants move for judgment on the pleadings.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED with leave to amend as to all causes of action except the seventh cause of action. Defendants’ Motion is GRANTED without leave to amend as to the seventh cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs shall have 30 days leave to amend the Complaint with respect to the remaining causes of action.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

            Defendants move for judgment on the pleadings.

 

//

 

Legal Standard

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings is the functional equivalent of a general demurrer.  (Lance Camper Mfg. Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. of Am. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198).  Like demurrers, motions for judgment on the pleadings challenge the legal sufficiency of the allegations, not their veracity.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994). Any defects must either appear on the face of the pleading, or else be taken by judicial notice.  (Bezirdjian v. O'Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-22).  The parties’ ability to prove their respective claims is of no concern.  (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 99.)  Though the Court must accept the allegations of the complaint and answer as true (Gerawan Farming, Inc. v. Lyons (2000) 24 Cal.4th 468, 515), it will not do so for “conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or [judicially noticed] facts…” (Stevenson Real Estate Servs., Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Servs., Inc. (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-20).

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the motion for judgment on the pleadings and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to grant or deny a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 439(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Laura Williams, attorney for Defendants, states that she attempted to contact Plaintiffs via email on March 27, 2024, and via telephone on March 28 and April 1, 2024, to no avail. (Declaration of Laura C. Williams ISO Mot. ¶¶ 4-6.) The Court therefore finds that Defendants have satisfied their statutory meet-and-confer obligation.

 

Timing

 

           A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be brought by a defendant at any time after the time to demur has expired and an answer has been filed. (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(f)). Unless a court orders otherwise, a motion for judgment on the pleadings may not be made after entry of a pre-trial conference order, (Cal. Rules of Court 3.720-3.730) or 30 days before the initial trial date, whichever is later. (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(e). No pre-trial conference order was entered in this case. Further, although the motion was filed well after the original October 31, 2023 trial date, this motion was filed and scheduled by order of the Court. (April 24, 2024 Minute Order.) The motion is therefore timely.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice that Plaintiffs’ current address is 6263 Topanga Canyon Blvd, Apt. 723, Woodland Hills, CA 91367, as set forth in their September 10, 2023 Informal Discovery Conference Statement. (RJN Exh. 1.) The Court does not take judicial notice of this conclusion but does take judicial notice of the existence of the document, including the presence of that address, pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records). Defendant’s request is therefore GRANTED to the extent described here.

 

Plaintiffs’ Untimely and Overlength Opposition

 

            Defendants object to Plaintiffs’ opposition to this motion as untimely and overlength. An opposition to a regularly noticed motion must be filed and served nine court days before the date of the scheduled hearing. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) Further, an opposition memorandum may not exceed fifteen pages, excluding exhibits, declarations, or attachments, and must include a table of contents and authorities if it exceeds ten pages. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1113(d), (f).) A memorandum which exceeds the page limit of the Rules of Court must be considered in the same manner as a late-filed paper. (Id. Rule 3.1113(g).) The Court has discretion to reject late-filed papers. (Rancho Mirage Country Club Homeowners Ass’n v. Hazelbaker (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 252, 262.)

 

            The Court is not inclined to reject Plaintiffs’ opposition as untimely where, first, there was ambiguity as to whether Defendants had properly served the Plaintiffs as self-represented litigants (see May 31, 2024 Minute Order), second, the hearing has been continued to June 11, 2024, thereby rendering the opposition timely, and, third, Defendants’ reply was itself filed only four court days before the hearing, rather than the five required by statute. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) Moreover, the length of Plaintiffs’ response does not appear to have prejudiced Defendants in preparing their reply, which dismisses the opposition as merely regurgitating the allegations in the Complaint that Defendants contend are insufficient. The Court will therefore consider the opposition on its merits.

 

Scope of Pleadings

 

            Plaintiffs’ Complaint, taking advantage of its nature as an electronic filing, relies extensively on hyperlinks to various documents, including photographs (Complaint ¶ 1), emails (¶ 23) and other “explanatory addenda,” including video links (Complaint p.4:16.) It is well-settled that the “face of the Complaint” on which a demurrer or motion for judgment on the pleadings must turn includes exhibits attached to the pleading and incorporated by reference. (E.g., Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) However, the bulk of these authorities predate the proliferation of electronic records in judicial proceedings, and none of them speak to circumstances where the documents are not attached in the same manner as in a physical document. Thus, the propriety of the hyperlinks in Plaintiffs’ Complaint appears to be an issue of first impression.

 

            Defendants argue that the materials provided in the hyperlinks should not be considered because they are unsubstantiated, irrelevant, and, in two instances, lead to active Google Documents (the “Factual Background” and “Critical Explanatory Addendum to Factual Background”) which can be altered at any time without leave of Court. While there is nothing in the record that suggests any such mischief has occurred, the Court shares Defendants’ view that such links, which are perishable or malleable, are fundamentally different in kind from attached hard-copy documents which are integrated into the pleading itself and are therefore part of the “face of the pleading.” The Court therefore considers the hyperlinked materials to be extrinsic evidence which cannot be considered in ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

            Defendants contend that Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for breach of contract fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must plead the contract, the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, Defendant’s breach, and the resulting damage. (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458.) Further, the complaint must indicate whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct. (Code Civ.Proc. § 430.10(g).) General allegations stating that defendants violated a contract are insufficient, and plaintiffs must state facts showing a breach. (Levy v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1, 5-6.) For breach of a written contract, the essential terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written instrument must be attached and incorporated by reference. (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co., supra, 166 Cal.App.3d at 459.) However, a plaintiff may plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language. (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. Tig Specialty Insurance (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-99.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have not clearly set forth the terms of the contract or contracts under which this cause of action is brought and do not clearly allege an injury from the conduct which they claim is wrongful. Defendants state that the Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs entered into two lease agreements for an apartment at the AVA Hollywood; one on July 4, 2020 (Complaint ¶¶ 12, 17-18, 22), and a second lease on July 8, 2020, under which Plaintiffs accepted possession of the unit at issue. (Complaint ¶¶ 13, 104.) The July 4 lease appears to be the destination of one of the hyperlinks included in the Complaint, but the Court does not consider this document for the reasons stated above. Aside from that link, the only portion of either lease agreement set forth in the Complaint is in paragraph 68, which states that “Section 5 of the Residential lease agreement states: “you may move into the apartment on or after the date set forth in the summary.” (Complaint ¶ 68.) While the Complaint alleges putative breaches of the contracts by failing to turn over the apartment keys to Plaintiffs (¶ 22) and refusing to remedy HVAC and moisture control defects in the apartment (¶¶ 137-39), without the terms of the contract, it is not possible to determine whether these actions could constitute a breach. Likewise, the Court cannot assess the sufficiency of the contention that Plaintiffs performed their obligations under the contract. (See Complaint ¶¶ 55-58). This could be remedied, of course, by attaching a pdf of the relevant contract, but that is not part of the current complaint.

 

Defendants also separately argue that Plaintiffs have not properly alleged damages caused by any breach of contract. In response, Plaintiffs cite extrinsic documents, including hyperlinked materials referenced in the Complaint, none of which is relevant at this stage. The allegations within the body of the Complaint which Plaintiffs cite reference a non-specific but serious decline in Plaintiffs’ health and general allegations that Plaintiff’s injuries and damages include medicals expenses incurred, emotional distress, and “certain other incidental and consequential expenses and losses.”  (Complaint ¶¶ 137, 148-150.) While these are broad allegations of damage, Plaintiffs are under no obligation to offer specific descriptions of their medical, emotional, and economic injuries, which are matters that can be fleshed out in discovery.  The Court finds, therefore, that Plaintiffs’ allegations of damages suffered are sufficient to withstand Defendants’ motion. 

 

The Court therefore concurs with Defendants that the Complaint does not adequately set forth the terms of the operative contract nor does it include a copy of the contract. Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the first cause of action.

 

Second Cause of Action: Implied Warranty of Habitability

 

            Defendants contend that Plaintiffs have failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability.

 

The elements of a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability “are the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises' habitability, notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant's discovery of the condition, the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency, and resulting damages.” (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1297.)

 

Defendants argue that the Complaint is insufficiently pled with respect to this cause of action because it does not set forth substantive facts demonstrating the existence of uninhabitable conditions, that Defendants were given the opportunity to correct those conditions, or what damages were incurred as a result. As Plaintiffs argue in opposition, the Complaint references an independent inspection which identified moisture control defects in the HVAC system and in openings under the kitchen sink cabinet on November 2, 2020. (Complaint ¶ 139.) The Complaint also alleges that Plaintiffs sent Defendants a 72-page fax and email three weeks before that report raising concerns about unspecified habitability defects. (Id. ¶ 137.) Construed in the light most favorable to Plaintiffs, these allegations establish that Plaintiffs knew of or suspected the existence of these issues, gave notice of those issues to Defendants on October 14, 2020, and subsequently confirmed the existence of those issues with an independent report. However, the Court concurs with Defendants that the Complaint does not allege facts showing that the landlord had a reasonable time to correct those deficiencies.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the second cause of action.

 

Third Cause of Action: Unfair Business Practices

 

            Defendants challenge the third cause of action for unfair competition under Business & Professions Code section 17200 for failure to state facts sufficient to support a claim.

 

California Business and Professions Code § 17200 prohibits any “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice.” Ultimately, “an ‘unfair’ business practice occurs when that practice ‘offends an established public policy or when the practice is immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous or substantially injurious to consumers.” (Davis v. Ford Motor Credit Co., LLC (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 581, 585.) “Unlawful” practices are business practices forbidden by any law.” (Olszewski v. Scripps Health (2003) 30 Cal.4th 798, 827.) “Unfair” practices must also be tethered to some underlying constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provision. (Scripps Clinic v. Superior Court (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 917, 940.)

 

            Defendants contend that the Complaint does not set forth with specificity any unfair or unlawful practices and does not adequately allege fraud for the reasons discussed infra. In opposition, Plaintiffs cite to a series of conclusory allegations in the Complaint claiming that Defendants’ advertising material is contradictory (Complaint ¶ 25), that Defendants’ agent was dishonest in some unspecified manner (¶¶ 37, 42-43, 48), and that Defendants intentionally misconstrued the meaning of requests and responded with fraudulent misrepresentations (¶ 147.) These allegations are bare conclusions, and do not contain facts sufficient to support a cause of action, especially as regards a statutory claim which must be pled with specificity. (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790.) More detail is required to clarify the specific business practices that are being challenged as unlawful, unfair or fraudulent.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED with respect to the third cause of action.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Fraud

 

            Defendants challenge the fourth cause of action for fraud for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            “The elements of fraud that will lead to a tort action are: (a) misrepresentation; (b) knowledge of falsity; (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant[s] to understand fully the nature of the charge made. (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Ibid.) “[G]eneral and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

            Defendants contend that, although the Complaint describes numerous communications with Defendants’ employees, nothing in the pleadings sets forth any misrepresentations upon which Plaintiffs relied which caused any injury. In opposition, Plaintiffs again extensively cite extrinsic evidence which cannot be considered on this motion, or to allegations which assert that communications were fraudulent without describing which portions of the statements were false (¶¶ 51, 88, 96-102; 133, 142) or do not demonstrate justifiable reliance to Plaintiffs’ detriment (e.g., ¶ 108.) The Court concurs with Defendants that these allegations are not sufficient. The Complaint must identify with specificity the statements that were made, who made those statements, when, where, to whom, and by what means they were made, and must allege which parts of the representations were false and how Plaintiffs relied on them, and how they were injured by that reliance. Plaintiffs have not done so.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the fourth cause of action for fraud.

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Premises Liability

 

            Defendants challenge the fifth cause of action for premises liability for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            To state a claim for premises liability, a plaintiff must plead a duty arising from the defendant’s ownership or control of the premises, a breach of that duty, and resultant injury. (See Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1159.)

 

            Defendants contend that this cause of action is inadequately pled because the Complaint pleads only the bare elements of this claim and a series of conclusions that Defendants were informed of multiple structural and habitability concerns (¶ 135) which would have been discovered by a good-faith inspection and maintenance effort (¶¶ 136-37) and which can be attributed only to poor design construction, installation, or maintenance. (Complaint ¶ 140.) Plaintiffs do not address this argument in opposition beyond a bare assertion that they adequately pled the elements of this claim. The Court is not persuaded. Plaintiffs have not adequately alleged facts which demonstrate a duty arising from Defendants’ ownership, a breach of that duty, and resultant injury. Bare conclusions are not facts.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the fifth cause of action.

 

 

//

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision

 

            Defendants also attack the sixth cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, and supervision for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim.

 

            “Negligence liability will be imposed on an employer if it ‘knew or should have known that hiring the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm materializes.’” (Phillips v. TLC Plumbing, Inc. (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1133, 1139.) Liability for negligent supervision and/or retention of an employee is one of direct liability for negligence, not vicarious liability.” (Delfino v. Agilent Technologies, Inc. (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 790, 815.) “To establish negligent supervision, a plaintiff must show that a person in a supervisorial position over the actor had prior knowledge of the actor’s propensity to do the bad act.” (Z.V. v. County of Riverside (2015) 238Cal.App.4th 889, 902.)

 

            Defendants contend that the Complaint does not set forth any prior propensities or behaviors that would create a risk of which Defendants should have been aware. In response, Plaintiffs merely assert that the sixth cause of action pleads the required elements of the claim. The Court does not share Plaintiffs’ view. The Complaint is devoid of any allegations that could potentially establish a risk or hazard created by hiring, retaining, or supervising an employee. Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the sixth cause of action.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Consumer Legal Remedies Act

 

            Defendants move for judgment on the seventh cause of action for breach of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

            The Consumer Legal Remedies Act protects consumers from specified unfair business practices with respect to goods and services. (Civ. Code § 1770(a).) “Goods” are defined as “tangible chattels bought or leased for use primarily for personal, family, or household purposes,” and “services” includes “work, labor, and services for other than a commercial or business use.” (Civ. Code § 1761(a)-(b).)

 

            Defendants contend that this cause of action fails as a matter of law because the Complaint is premised on Defendants’ alleged bad acts concerning the lease and maintenance of Plaintiffs’ apartment, i.e., real property, not a good or service. The Court concurs. The express language of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act defines its applicable scope to goods and services, where this case is grounded in Plaintiffs’ rights as to real property. (See, e.g., Heckart v. A-1 Self Storage, Inc. (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 525, rev. granted and op. superseded sub nom. and aff'd, Heckart v. A-1 Self Storage (2018) 4 Cal.5th 749.) Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the seventh cause of action.

 

 

Eighth Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

            Defendants challenge the eighth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress for failure to state facts sufficient to support a claim.

 

“The elements of the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are:  ‘“(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress;   (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. …” Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ [Citation.]” (Citation omitted.) “It is not enough that the conduct be intentional and outrageous. It must be conduct directed at the plaintiff, or occur in the presence of a plaintiff of whom the defendant is aware.” 

 

(Catsouras v. Department of California Highway Patrol (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 856, 874-875.) The defendant’s conduct must be “directed primarily” at the plaintiffs, “calculated to cause them severe emotional distress,” or “done with knowledge of their presence and of a substantial certainty that they would suffer severe emotional injury.” (Christensen v. Superior Court (1991) 54 Cal.3d 868, 906.) 

 

            The Complaint alleges that Defendants “refused to at first just have access to the premises” and then refused to inspect, repair, and remediate the damages in the apartment and threatened Plaintiffs when they complained. (Complaint ¶¶ 220-221.) Defendants contend that this cause of action is deficient because the allegations asserted do not establish extreme or outrageous conduct nor the requisite intent or reckless disregard of the risk to Plaintiffs. In response, Plaintiffs again simply recite the portion of the Complaint alleging the bare legal elements of this cause of action. The Court agrees with Defendants that the Complaint, as pled, does not demonstrate extreme and outrageous conduct nor requisite intent because the body of the Complaint does not set forth in sufficient detail the hazards in the apartment or the insufficient responses from Defendants. Although it is not difficult for the Court to conceive of extreme and outrageous conduct with reckless disregard towards causing emotional distress in the context of a habitability action, greater clarity of facts is required to support the contentions asserted in paragraphs 220 and 221.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED as to the eighth cause of action.

 

Uncertainty

 

            Defendants also contend that the entire Complaint is unintelligible and ambiguous and therefore uncertain.

 

            Demurrers¿for uncertainty are disfavored, because discovery can be used for clarification, and they apply only where defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated.¿(Chen v. Berenjian (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822; Khoury v. Maly's of Cal., Inc.¿(1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) If the complaint is sufficiently comprehensible that Defendant can reasonably respond, the complaint is not uncertain. (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Ins. Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848 n.3.)  

 

            As should be apparent from the Court’s analysis of each cause of action, the Complaint, though not artfully pled, is not so vague or unintelligible that the individual claims asserted against Defendants cannot be assessed.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318).  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.” (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.) Accordingly, California law imposes the burden on the plaintiffs to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims against a defendant.  (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Plaintiffs have not demonstrated how the Complaint might be cured by amendment. That said, except for the seventh cause of action, the deficiencies in the Complaint arise principally from the lack of detail and clarity in the allegations set forth in the body of the Complaint. Moreover, much of the materials included in the various hyperlinks are documents and photographs which, had they been attached as exhibits to the Complaint itself in the same manner as a physical document, might have bolstered the allegations in the Complaint and resulted in a different outcome on this motion. The means of correcting the deficiencies is therefore straightforward: Plaintiffs should endeavor to clarify their allegations with greater specificity and factual detail and include any critical documents, such as the lease agreement, as attached exhibits, without overwhelming the pleadings with collateral materials. The Court will therefore grant leave to amend as to these causes of action.

 

            As to the seventh cause of action, leave to amend is not proper because the Consumer Legal Remedies Act is neither applicable nor relevant in the context of a habitability defect action.

 

//

             

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is GRANTED with leave to amend as to all causes of action except the seventh cause of action. Defendants’ Motion is GRANTED without leave to amend as to the seventh cause of action.

 

Plaintiffs shall have 30 days leave to amend the Complaint with respect to the remaining causes of action.

 

Moving Parties to give notice.

CASE:                         Griffin M. Bracci v. Orion Housing, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV06963           

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Orion Housing, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Griffin M. Bracci

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract that was filed on February 23, 2021. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant failed to provide proper notice of construction on the property which Plaintiff was leasing from Defendant, denying Plaintiff the full use of that property.

 

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment or summary adjudication of all causes of action.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED. 

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary judgment.

 

            As Plaintiff has demonstrated entitlement to summary adjudication of both causes of action for the reasons stated below, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

 

//

 

Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication of both causes of action asserted in the Complaint.

 

Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) Once the moving party has met that burden, section 437c shifts the burden to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto. If the opposing party cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted. (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Med. Ctr. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the Court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except evidence to which the Court has sustained an objection, as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment. (Ibid.)   

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of its July 19, 2023 order deeming the truth of the matters stated in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions. Plaintiff’s request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

             “There is an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing in every contract that neither party will do anything which will injure the right of the other to receive the benefits of the agreement.” (Comunale v. Traders & General Ins. Co. (1958) 50 Cal.2d 654, 658 [internal citations omitted].) “The covenant of good faith and fair dealing, implied by law in every contract, exists merely to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made. The covenant thus cannot ‘ “ ‘be endowed with an existence independent of its contractual underpinnings.’ ” ’ It cannot impose substantive duties or limits on the contracting parties beyond those incorporated in the specific terms of their agreement.” (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349-350 [internal citations omitted, emphasis in original].)

 

            Plaintiff frames his allegations as premised on a violation of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment. This covenant is implied in every lease agreement and “insulates the tenant against any act or omission on the part of the landlord, or anyone claiming under him, which interferes with a tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy.” (Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 873, 896.) In the context of a lease, the two covenants overlap to impose similar duties of care. (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 589.) However, as the Complaint specifically alleges a breach of the implied covenant of good faith, which requires a showing that the actions complained of were objectively unreasonable or that the Defendant subjectively lacked belief in the validity of its conduct. (Moore v. Wells Fargo Bank N.A. (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 280, 291-92.)

 

            Plaintiff contends, based on matters deemed admitted by Defendant, that the parties entered into a valid lease agreement on August 1, 2020. (SSUMF No. 3.) Based on the matters admitted, Defendant never notified plaintiff of its intent to demolish a part of the property before beginning construction and rescinded Plaintiff’s ability to use his parking spot, which was a material term of the lease. (SSUMF Nos. 4, 6, 8.) As these are matters which have been deemed admitted by the Court’s July 19, 2023 order, Plaintiff has demonstrated objectively unreasonable conduct which deprived Plaintiff of the benefit of his lease agreement. Plaintiff declares that he suffered $64,484 in damages from Defendant’s conduct. (SSUMF No. 14.) As Defendant has not responded to this motion, Plaintiff is entitled to summary adjudication on this cause of action.

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to the first cause of action.

 

Second Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary adjudication of the second cause of action for breach of contract.

 

            To prevail on a claim for breach of contract, a plaintiff must plead and prove (1) a contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) resultant damage to the plaintiff. (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1388.)

 

            Plaintiff contends that the parties entered into a valid lease agreement in April of 2020 (SSUMF No. 3), Plaintiff paid all rent due under the contract. (SSUMF No. 10.) and Defendant breached a material term of the contract by depriving him of the use of his parking spot. (SSUMF No. 6.) Plaintiff declares that he suffered $64,484 in damages from Defendant’s conduct. (SSUMF No. 14.) As Defendant has not responded to the motion, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that Defendant cannot prevail on this claim, and Defendant has not demonstrated a triable issue of fact as to this cause of action.

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED as to the second cause of action.

 

Attorney’s Fees

 

            Plaintiff also requests attorney’s fees on this action. Plaintiff cites no authority authorizing a request for attorney’s fees as part of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, and the Court declines to consider that issue in connection with this motion. If Plaintiff seeks an award of attorney’s fees, that request should be brought as a separately noticed motion.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.
