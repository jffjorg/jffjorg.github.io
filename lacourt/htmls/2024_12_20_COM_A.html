DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT NATIONSTAR MORTGAGE, LLC’S DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

1st AMENDED COMPLAINT FILED: 07/12/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

 

1.     Background

This is an action arising from an alleged wrongful foreclosure. Plaintiff Lashunda Pruitt as power of attorney for Georgia Pruitt (“Plaintiff”) alleges in the complaint that defendant Nationstar Mortgage LLC dba Mr. Cooper (“Nationstar”) recorded a notice of default on a mortgage taken out by Plaintiff without, within the statutorily required period, contacting Plaintiff to notify her of possible foreclosure or discuss foreclosure alternatives. Plaintiff alleges that defendants NewRez, LLC dba Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing (“Shellpoint”) and US Bank Trust National Association (“US Bank”) subsequently wrongfully foreclosed on the mortgage.

The First Amended Complaint (“complaint”) alleges causes of action for violation of Civil Code section 2923.5 and violation of Civil Code section 2924.9 against Nationstar. The complaint alleges causes of action for wrongful foreclosure and unfair business practices against Shellpoint and US Bank.

2.     Demurrer

Nationstar demurs to the first and second causes of action of the complaint, for violation of Civil Code section 2923.5 and Civil Code section 2924.9, on the grounds that the complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action.[1] Nationstar contends that the cause of action for violation of Civil Code section 2923.5, which requires a mortgage servicer to contact a borrower about options to avoid foreclosure at least thirty days before filing a notice of default, fails because Nationstar filed a California Declaration of Compliance along with the notice of default, which under Civil Code section 2924 creates a rebuttable statutory presumption of compliance with Civil Code section 2923.5.

Nationstar further contends that Plaintiff’s claims against Nationstar are barred by the statute of limitations because the statute of limitations for a violation of the Homeowner’s Bill of Rights is three years and the statute of limitations began to run in 2019, when Nationstar filed the notice of default.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that she was available by phone or mail and never received a phone call or any written communication from Nationstar regarding options to avoid foreclosure or foreclosure alternatives either before or after Nationstar filed the notice of default.

In reply, Nationstar contends that Plaintiff’s statement that she received no mail or messages from Nationstar in 2019 is conclusory and insufficient to overcome the presumption arising from the filing of the declaration of compliance. Nationstar further contends that Plaintiff failed to address the statute of limitations argument, which constitutes an abandonment of that argument.

3.     Demurrer standards

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿(SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿

The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿(Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)

 

4.     Request for Judicial Notice

Nationstar requests that the court take judicial notice of the deed of trust executed by Georgia Pruitt on November 10, 2006 and recorded on December 7, 2006, with the Instrument No. 20062716376, securing the principal amount of $163,000.00, and encumbering the real property located at 715 North Dwight Avenue, Compton, California 90220.  The court GRANTS Nationstar’s request for judicial notice.

5.     Discussion

          a. The complaint states a claim for violation of Civil Code § 2923.5.

Civil Code § 2923.5 mandates that a mortgage servicer contact the borrower at least thirty days in advance of filing a notice of default in order to “assess the borrower's financial situation and explore options for the borrower to avoid foreclosure.” Civil Code § 2924.9 provides that, within five business days of filing a notice of default, a mortgage servicer that offers foreclosure prevention alternative shall send the borrower a written communication containing information about the alternatives and the process to be considered for a foreclosure prevention alternative.  Defendant contends that the filing of the Notice of Default creates a rebuttable presumption that can only be refuted by facts, not conclusory allegations.  However, the authorities relied upon by Defendant do not relate to the sufficiency of the allegations in a pleading to state a cause of action.  Instead, they address issues of evidence and proof.  Moreover, Civil Code § 2924 does not address pleading requirements.

Plaintiff alleges that Nationstar failed to contact her by phone or written communication about options to avoid foreclosure at least thirty days before filing the notice of default. Although Nationstar contends that its filing of a California Declaration of Compliance creates a rebuttable presumption that the required communication was made, Plaintiff’s allegation creates competing accounts as to the possibility of compliance with Civ. Code § 2923.5.  (Mabry v. Superior Court (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 208, 235-236.)  A demurrer is “ “simply not the appropriate procedure for determining the truth of disputed facts.’ ” (Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374; Skov v. U.S. Bank (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 690, 696 [assuming the truth of the plaintiff’s allegations, a disputed issue of compliance with Civil Code § 2923.5 cannot be resolved at the demurrer stage]; see also Barrionuevo v. Chase Bank, N.A. (N.D.Cal.2012) 885 F.Supp.2d 964, 976-977 [borrower’s allegation that bank did not contact them before filing the notice of default was sufficient to state a violation of Civ. Code § 2923.5, despite judicial notice taken of declaration in notice of default that asserted statutory compliance]; Argueta v. J.P. Morgan Chase (E.D. Cal. 2011) 787 F.Supp2d 1099, 1107 [despite judicial notice of Notice of Default including declaration of compliance with Civ. Code § 2325.5, plaintiff’s allegations were sufficient to preclude dismissal where plaintiffs alleged they did not receive phone calls, phone messages, or letters before the Notice of Default was recorded].) 

Plaintiff has therefore alleged facts sufficient to state a claim for violation of Civil Code § 2923.5. 

          b. Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to plead around the statute of

    limitations defense apparent from the allegations in the complaint.

Defendant contends that both of plaintiff’s statutory claims are barred by the statute of limitations.  Code of Civil Procedure section 388 provides that the statute of limitations for a cause of action created by statute is three years. Civil Code sections 2923.5 and 2924.9 were statutorily created as part of the Homeowner’s Bill of Rights (See Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109).  The three-year statute of limitations applicable generally to claims for statutory violations applies to nonjudicial foreclosures allegedly violating a Civil Code provision.  (Engstrom v. Kallins (1997) 49 Cal.App.4th 773, 782 n.8—addressing wrongful foreclosures, and Civ. C. §1799.91(a) requiring creditor notice to co-signors, and observing that the plaintiff, “apparently concedes the applicability of Code of Civil Procedure section 338, subdivision (a), which provides a three-year statute for ‘[a]n action upon a liability created by statute, other than a penalty or forfeiture.’”)

Generally, a cause of action begins to accrue, and the statute of limitations begins to run, when the cause of action is completed with all of its elements. (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 806.) “An exception to the general rule for defining the accrual of a cause of action – indeed, the ‘most important’ one – is the discovery rule. [Citation.]  It may be expressed by the Legislature or implied by the courts.  [Citation.] It postpones accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action.”  (Holman, supra at 195, citing Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 395.) 

The common-law discovery rule is available when the three-year statute of limitations for an action upon a liability created by statute, other than a penalty or forfeiture applies.  (Holman v. County of Butte (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 189, 194 (“Holman”)—dismissal of plaintiff’s suit reversed where trial court erroneously sustained defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend after concluding that plaintiff failed to present authority to support application of the delayed discovery rule to the applicable statute of limitations as set forth in C.C.P. § 338, subdiv. (a).)   

In order to rely on the discovery rule for delayed accrual of a cause of action, a plaintiff whose complaint shows on its face that his claim would be barred without the benefit of the discovery rule must specifically plead facts to show (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence. In assessing the sufficiency of the allegations of delayed discovery, the court places the burden on the plaintiff to show diligence; conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer.  (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal. 4th 797, 808.)

Nationstar filed notice of default on September 4, 2019. Both the alleged violation of Civil Code section 2923.5 and the alleged violation of Civil Code section 2924 took place in 2019, more than three years ago. Though the common-law discovery rule is available to claims governed by Code of Civil Procedure § 338, subdiv. (a), Plaintiff has not alleged any facts indicating when Plaintiff discovered the notice of default had been filed, or that Plaintiff was not aware of her default or Nationstar’s notice of default.

Accordingly, the court will sustain the demurrer on statute of limitations grounds, with leave for Plaintiff to amend her complaint to allege sufficient facts to bring her statutory claims within the delayed discovery rule.

6.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court SUSTAINS Nationstar’s demurrer with leave to amend.  Plaintiff shall file an amended pleading not later than twenty (20) days after this order.

Nationstar is ordered to give notice.
