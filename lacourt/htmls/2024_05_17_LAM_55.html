DEPARTMENT 55 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV12007    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Specially Appearing Stylenquaza LLC’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction. Defendant Caesarstone LTD.’s Motion for Order Allowing Lydecker to Withdraw its Notice of Appearance.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs JAIME BLANCO MACIEL and HELEN ORTIZ-BLANCO (“Plaintiffs”) filed suit against over 50 defendants for injuries sustained by Plaintiff JAIME BLANCO MACIEL by inhaling dust from working with defendants’ products containing crystalline silica.

Specially-Appearing Defendant STYLENQUAZA LLC (“Defendant LLC”) moves to quash service of the Summons and Complaint based on lack of personal jurisdiction. Plaintiffs oppose the motion. The Court previously continued Defendant LLC’s motion to quash so that Plaintiffs could take the deposition of Defendant LLC’s person most qualified. Defendant LLC and Plaintiff then filed supplemental briefs.

Defendant CAESARSTONE LTD (“Defendant LTD”) moves to withdraw counsel’s appearance based on the lack of client authority.  Plaintiffs oppose this motion.

The Court considers both motions in this tentative ruling.

I.                   MOTION TO QUASH

 LEGAL STANDARD

When a defendant files a motion to quash for lack of personal jurisdiction, “the burden of proof is placed upon the plaintiff to establish the facts of jurisdiction by a preponderance of the evidence.”  Aquila, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2007) 148 Cal. App. 4th 556, 568. A plaintiff must demonstrate personal jurisdiction based upon competent evidence, and not on allegations in an unverified complaint.  Jewish Defense Org. v. Sup. Ct. (1999) 72 Cal. App. 4th 1045, 1054-1055.  Personal jurisdiction may be general or specific. Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal. 4th 434, 445.

General jurisdiction exists when the defendant has “substantial, continuous, and systematic contacts with California.” DVI, Inc. v. Superior Ct. (2002) 104 Cal. App. 4th 1080, 1099.

Specific jurisdiction exists when a defendant’s “minimum contacts” with the state shows that the defendant purposefully directed its acts to the state, “that the cause of action [is] related to or arise or result from the acts or contacts in the forum, and that the exercise of personal jurisdiction by the forum would be reasonable.” Muckle v. Superior Ct. (2002) 102 Cal. App. 4th 218, 227–28.

EVIDENTIARY RULINGS

The Court sustains Defendant LLC’s objections to the following paragraphs of the Declaration of Ralphael Metzger: 8, 16, 18, 20-21. The remaining objections are overruled.

 

ANALYSIS

Plaintiffs do not contend that the Court has general jurisdiction over Defendant LLC. The Court thus confines its discussion to specific jurisdiction. To establish the “minimum contacts” necessary to create specific jurisdiction, Plaintiffs must establish that Defendant LLC purposefully directed its acts to California and these acts create a “substantial connection” with the state. Walden v. Fiore (2014) 571 U.S. 277, 284.

Plaintiffs contend that they have satisfied the minimum contacts requirement for specific jurisdiction based on (1) Defendant LLC’s admission of distributing prefabricated tops and 22 stone slabs to California; (2) a website; (3) three distribution agreements and a Vicostone JSC annual report.

Products Shipped to California

Defendant LLC is a Texas corporation with its principal place of business in Dallas, Texas. Nguyen Decl. ¶ 3. No owners of Defendant LLC reside in California. Id. ¶ 4. Defendant LLC does business under the name “Vicostone USA.” Id. ¶ 3. Between 2018 and 2022, Defendant LLC distributed prefabricated tops to California which did not need any further fabrication and supplied six shipments to California of stone slabs. Id. ¶ 8. Plaintiffs claim the prefabricated tops needed further polishing that creates silica dust but they have no admissible evidence to support this contention. Defendant LLC asserts that the slabs it sent to California amount to 0.001% of the product it has ever distributed. Nguyen Decl. ¶ 8. Plaintiffs have no other evidence (even after deposing Defendant LLC’s PMK) of Defendant LLC’s sales of products in California. Defendant LLC’s sales of slabs to Pental are not relevant to the jurisdiction analysis because knowledge that a product might enter the forum state does not, taken alone, show purposeful availment. Bombardier Recreational Prods v. Dow Chemical Canda ULC (2013) 216 Cal. App. 4th 591, 602. In any event, Defendant LLC ultimately learned that only 11 of the slabs ended up in California. Teresi Decl. ISO Reply, Exh. 5 at No. 4.

Defendant LLC’s extremely limited and minimal contact with California through the distribution of prefabricated tops and slabs does not warrant a finding of specific jurisdiction. See, e.g., Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475 (defendant cannot “be haled into a jurisdiction solely as a result of ‘random,’ ‘fortuitous,’ or ‘attenuated’ contacts”).

Website

Plaintiffs contend that Defendant LLC operates a website that directs prospective customers in California to dealers in California where customers can purchase Defendant LLC’s product. Plaintiffs contend that Defendant LLC therefore has directed sales of its product to California.

The evidence does not support Plaintiffs’ contention. The evidence shows that Vicostone JSC owns and operated the website. Teresi Decl. ISO Suppl. Reply, Exh. 9 at 77:5-8, 78:17-21, 93:25-94:9. Defendant LLC is a distributor to several states (not California) for Vicostone JCS. Nguyen Decl. ¶ 5. Whether or not Vicostone JSC-a separate legal entity from Defendant LLC- operates a website directed to customers in California is immaterial to determining Defendant LLC’s minimum contacts with California. Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Ct. (2000) 83 Cal. App. 4th 523, 538 (“[o]rdinarily, a corporation is regarded as a legal entity, separate and distinct from its stockholders, officers and directors, with separate and distinct liabilities and obligations”). The cases relied upon by Plaintiffs concern an entity’s purposeful conduct through a website maintained by that entity. Neogen Corp. v. Neo Gen Screening Inc. (6th Cir. 2002) 282 F.3d 883; Snowney v. Harrah’s Entm’t, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal. 4th 1054. That is not the case here and so the holdings in those cases are inapposite.

Distribution Agreements and Annual Report

Plaintiffs contend that three distribution contracts between Defendant LLC and Vicostone JSC and Vicostone JSC’s annual report establish that Defendant LLC is the direct distributor of Vicostone products in North America. Plaintiffs therefore surmise that as “an affiliate and related entity of Vicostone,” Defendant LLC has purposefully availed its acts to California by way of “brand promotion, vendor identification on its website, and distribution through its warehouses.” Suppl. Opp. at 13:6-8. Plaintiffs also rely on the fact that the annual report does not mention Pental or any other supplier of Vicostone JSC’s products on the west coast to conclude that the sales and marketing of Vicostone JSC’s products in California must be attributed to Defendant LLC.

The evidence does not, however, show that the documents mention California, let alone prove Defendant LLC’s purposeful activities towards California. The evidence shows that pursuant to a distribution agreement with Vicostone JSC, Defendant LLC distributes products in certain territories that do not include California. Nguyen Decl. ¶ 5. The deposition of Defendant LLC’s PMK confirmed that Defendant LLC’s distribution area does not include California. Teresi Decl. ISO Supp. Reply, Exh. 9 at 36:3-19, 79:19-80:9, 91:18-92:9. Thus, Plaintiffs’ conclusion that Defendant LLC must market and sell products in California is wholly speculative unsupported by evidence.

Plaintiffs therefore have not met their burden of proof of establishing that Defendant LLC has minimum contacts with California that create a substantial connection with the state. Because Plaintiffs failed to establish the first requirement for specific jurisdiction, the Court need not address whether Plaintiffs’ claim are related to or arise out of Defendant LLC’s contacts and whether the exercise of jurisdiction would be fair and reasonable.

II.                MOTION TO WITHDRAW APPEARANCE

The Court’s independent research reveals that indeed there are directly applicable opinions specifically supporting withdrawal of appearances, including where counsel has lacked client authority to make an appearance.

For instance: “[A] defendant for whom a general appearance has been entered by unauthorized counsel may withdraw from the action upon a sufficient factual showing to overcome the rebuttable presumption that an attorney has authority to appear for the person for whom he professes to act.” Omega Video Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 470, 476.

Generally, an attorney’s unauthorized disposition of parties’ substantive rights can be either voidable or void. W. Bradley Electric, Inc. v. Mitchell Engineering (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 1, 15. Attorney acts affecting substantive rights, like settlements, are void, absent express client consent. Romadka v. Hoge (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 1231, 1236-1237. For example, an attorney cannot settle a case without express client authority. Blanton v. Womancare, Inc. (1985) 38 Cal. 396, 404.

Further, cases indicate that there may be a due-process violation by setting a preferential trial so early that defendants are deprived of a reasonable opportunity for discovery or pretrial preparation.  E.g., Roe v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 642, 643. 

Defendant LTD. requests an order allowing counsel’s withdrawal of an appearance, on grounds including that it mistakenly had thought that Defendant LTD was a client that authorized counsel’s appearance. Plaintiffs argue that Code of Civil Procedure Section 473 is inapposite and Defendant has not satisfied its requirements, such as the failures to include an answer or a motion to quash service. Further, Plaintiffs claim prejudice based on predictable delay of trial in this case entitled to trial preference.

Based upon Defendant LTD’s evidence, the Court finds that counsel purported to appear for a non-client as to which it was not authorized to appear. This constitutes solid legal grounds to allow Defendant LLC to withdraw the appearance and to allow it a reasonable time to prepare for a preferential trial.

CONCLUSION

The Court grants the motion to quash. The Court grants the motion to withdraw appearance.

 

 


Case Number: 23STCV18257    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Defendants’ Motion for Attorney Fees

BACKGROUND

PATRIZIO MOI (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants DLA PIPER LLP (US); DLA Piper attorneys MILES COOLEY; MICHAEL GARFINKEL; JASON LUEDDEKE; and MICHAEL BROWN (collectively “Moving Defendants”); JABARI’s INC.; JABARI MCDAVID (“McDavid,” Jabari Inc.’s owner); and PHILIP LAWRENCE in connection with a lawsuit (“Lawsuit 1”) filed by Plaintiff and Jabari’s Inc. against Lawrence and Moving Defendants’ representation of Lawrence in that lawsuit.

Moving Defendants previously brought a motion to strike under Code Civ. Proc. section 425.16, which extends protections to strategic lawsuits against public participation (“SLAPP” actions). On 1/31/24, the Court issued an order striking the entire Complaint as to Moving Defendants. Moving Defendants now move for an award of mandatory attorneys’ fees under Code Civ. Proc. section 425.16(c) in the amount of $86,400.50 and costs in the amount of $2,235.00, for a total claimed amount of $88,635.50. Plaintiff opposes the motion.  

LEGAL STANDARD

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(c), a prevailing defendant is entitled to recover attorney’s fees and costs associated with the motion. A defendant may only recover fees and costs related to the motion to strike. Lafayette Morehouse, Inc. v. Chronicle Publishing Co. (1995) 39 Cal. App. 4th 1379, 1383. This includes fees associated with bringing the motion for fees. Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal. 4th 1122, 1141.  

ANALYSIS

Plaintiff argues Moving Defendants are not entitled to the fees sought because the motion contains duplicative or unnecessary billing entries and block-billing entries, contains errors, does not attach the invoices, and does not establish the reasonableness of the fees sought. (Opp. 4:16-28; 5:1-26; 6:1-23; 7:1-27; 8:1-27; 9:9-28;10:1-28; 11:1-28; 12:1-23; 13:1-28; 14:1-5.) The Court disagrees.  The Court has conducted its own review of the time entries submitted in Exhibit E to the moving papers, which are summarized in the Declaration of Leah Plaskin Lorenz. The Court finds that the amounts requested for the anti-SLAPP motion are reasonable. As described in the supplemental declaration filed with the reply, the minor error in calculating the number of hours in one category of work results in Moving Defendants seeking 1.7 hours less in fees than they are entitled to recover, hardly a reason to find the overall fees unreasonable. Plaintiff provides no case or statutory law that requires an attorneys’ fee motion be accompanied by invoices, and indeed, invoices are not required.

The hourly rates for Moving Defendants’ counsel are commensurate with their counsels’ respective experience and skill levels and in the Los Angeles legal community. Plaintiff does not dispute that the hourly rates are reasonable.

This was a complex matter that required counsel for the Moving Defendants to research the facts and legal claims in Lawsuit 1 and draft an anti-SLAPP motion analyzing both steps of the anti-SLAPP statute as they pertained to the claims in this case involving Lawsuit 1. The Court agrees with Moving Defendants that Plaintiff multiplied Moving Defendants’ legal fees by filing unauthorized supplemental filings after Moving Defendants had filed their reply for the anti-SLAPP motion and then attempting to dismiss his case prior to the Court’s ruling on the anti-SLAPP motion. Those actions by Plaintiff necessitated additional research and filings by counsel for moving Defendants. Moving Defendants estimate that their attorneys will spend approximately eight additional hours briefing the fees motion, which the Court finds reasonable.

Finally, Plaintiff argues he is not liable for Moving Defendants’ fees because his Complaint was filed in good faith and was not frivolous. (Opp. 14:6-27; 15:1-21.) But Code of Civil Proc. section 425.16(c) mandates the award of fees and costs to a prevailing defendant on an anti-SLAPP motion. Whether or not Plaintiff filed his case in good faith has no bearing on the fee analysis.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court grants Defendants Miles Michael Cooley, Michael Brian Garfinkel, Jason Taylor Lueddeke, Michael Patrick Brown, and DLA Piper LLP’s motion for attorneys’ fees and costs in the amount of $88,635.50.
