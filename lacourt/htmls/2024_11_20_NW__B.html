Case Number: 19STCV21240    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: B

Plaintiff Tammy Lynn Gardner’s Requests for Default Judgment

Defendants: Laura Rabelo and Marcos Guereca

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Tammy Lynn Gardner’s request for default judgment against Defendants Laura Rabelo and Marcos Guereca is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a May 6, 2019, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiff Tammy Lynn Gardner (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendants Laura Rabelo (“Rabelo”), Marcos Guereca (“Guereca”), and Does 1 to 10 (collectively “Defendants”) negligently operated their motor vehicle, colliding with Plaintiff’s motor scooter and causing serious injuries thereof.

On June 18, 2019, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants alleging motor vehicle negligence and negligent entrustment.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On November 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Defendants.

LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.)

ANALYSIS

Plaintiff seeks damages in the amount of $151,167.26 in general and special damages against Rabelo. Plaintiff also seeks damages in the amount of $15,000.00 against Guereca as he is the registered owner of the vehicle which struck Plaintiff.

Defendants have failed to timely respond or appear. Plaintiff’s default packet meets all statutory requirements under CCP § 585. Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Defendants is GRANTED.

Moving party is ordered to give notice. Court to sign JUD-100 orders.

Case Number: 20STCV33026    Hearing Date: November 20, 2024    Dept: B

Petitioner Mary Tuccio’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

TENTATIVE RULING

Petitioner Mary Tuccio’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a November 22, 2019, incident in which Claimant Anthony Ramirez (“Claimant”) alleges Defendant Norwalk-La Mirada Unified School District (“Defendant”) negligently owned and maintained public property, causing Claimant to sustain injury as a result.

On August 31, 2020, Claimant filed an action against Defendant for four causes of action: (1) dangerous condition of public property; (2) negligence; (3) negligent infliction of emotional distress; and (4) negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of an employee.

On September 1, 2020, Petitioner Mary Tuccio (“Petitioner”)’s application for appointment as Claimant’s guardian ad litem was granted.

On November 4, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability on behalf of Claimant.

ANALYSIS

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $99,000.00 from Defendant.

Legal Standard

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Code Civ. Proc., § 372.) For this

purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Id., at p. 1606.)

Probate Code section 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows:

The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person. (Prob. Code, § 3601, subd. (a).)

In cases pursuant to Probate Code section 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955, subd. (a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (Cal. Rules of Court 7.955, subd. (a)(2).)

Discussion

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $99,000.00. Of the $99,000 settlement, $10,678.19 is to be deducted for medical expenses, $24,750.00 for attorney’s fees, and $29,488.19 for costs. Therefore, Claimant’s proposed net settlement amount is $34,083.62.

The Petition sets forth information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise pursuant to CRC Rule 7.950. (MC-350 Attachment 8 [medical records].) Additionally, attorney’s fees represent 25% of the total settlement agreement. (MC-350 Attachment 13a.) Claimant’s counsel provides their retainer agreement as Attachment 17a. Costs are reasonable considering the extent of litigation.

The Petition requests an additional order, requiring Petitioner to use Claimant’s settlement for Claimant’s private school tuition and “nothing else.” (MC-350 Item 20.) The order would also require Petitioner to inform the Court immediately if Claimant ceases to attend private school. The Court finds this additional order reasonable and in the best interest of Claimant.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the petition to confirm minor’s compromise.

I.       BACKGROUND  

            On May 13, 2022, Plaintiff Celeste Haydee Echeverria DeFranco (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint in the instant action against Defendants County of Los Angeles (“County”), City of Los Angeles (“City”), and Does 1 to 100 alleging one cause of action for dangerous condition on public property pursuant to Cal. Gov. Code § 835, et seq. Plaintiff alleges that, on or about October 7, 2021, broken and uneven pavement at or near the sidewalk located near 5455 Bellingham Ave., Valley Village, CA 91607, caused her to trip, fall, and sustain serious injury. (Complaint ¶ 4.)

On August 18, 2022, the City filed a cross-complaint against Roes 1 to 20 for three causes of action: (1) indemnification; (2) apportionment of fault; and (3) declaratory relief.

On November 17, 2022, the City filed an amendment to their complaint identifying PBM 2 LLC (“PBM”) as Roe 1.

On June 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to her complaint identifying PBM as Doe 1.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On September 11, 2024, PBM filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On November 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition.

On November 15, 2024, PBM filed their reply.

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

            PBM moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff’s claims must fail as a matter of law because the governmental provisions under which Plaintiff asserts liability do not apply to PBM, a private entity. (Motion p. 1:24-26.) PBM also argues that, even if the provisions did apply, Plaintiff’s claim still fails because PBM did not create or have notice of the dangerous condition which caused Plaintiff’s injuries. (Ibid.) Finally, PBM argues that PBM did not owe pedestrians, such as Plaintiff, a duty of care under any relevant state, municipal, or county ordinances. (Ibid. at 27-28.)

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

            In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).)

            A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant must present evidence… showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.)

            Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence.

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)    

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Judicial Notice

Plaintiff’s November 6, 2024, request for judicial notice is denied. Judicial notice of applicable statutes is unnecessary and redundant as the Court can always review relevant statutes.

 

Defendant’s Burden

PBM meets their initial burden.

To succeed on a claim for dangerous condition on public property pursuant to Cal. Gov. Code § 835, plaintiff must show that: (1) the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of injury; (2) the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition; (3) the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which occurred; and (4) that either (a) a negligent or wrongful act or omission of a public entity employee within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition, or (b) the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition with sufficient time to remedy such condition. (Mixon v. State (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 124, 130-131.)

“A public entity had constructive notice of a dangerous condition within the meaning of subdivision (b) of [Cal. Gov. Code § 835] only if the plaintiff establishes that the condition had existed for such a period of time and was of such an obvious nature that the public entity, in the exercise of due care, should have discovered the condition and its dangerous character.” (Cal. Gov. Code § 835.2(b).) “‘Dangerous condition’ means a condition of property that creates a substantial (as distinguished from a minor, trivial or insignificant) risk of injury when such property or adjacent property is used with due care in a manner in which it is reasonably foreseeable that it will be used.” (Cal. Gov. Code § 830(a).)

A public entity could be liable for a dangerous condition of public property even where the immediate cause of a plaintiff’s injury is a third party’s negligent or illegal act if some physical characteristic of the property exposes its users to increased danger from third party negligence or criminality. (Mixon, supra, 207 Cal.App.4th at 131.) Third party conduct by itself that is unrelated to the condition of the property does not constitute a dangerous condition for which a public entity may be held liable. (Ibid.)

 

PBM Is a Private Entity

PBM is a private entity that does not employ or manage public employees. (PBM Separate Statement of Facts “PBM-SSF” Nos. 1 and 2; Racette Decl. ¶ 2.) PBM has never performed work on behalf of a public or government entity. (Ibid. No. 3; Racette Decl. ¶ 2.) PBM did not design, develop, or construct the subject sidewalk. (Ibid. No. 4; Racette Decl. ¶ 4.) PBM owns the apartment building which abuts a public sidewalk, but PBM did not perform work or maintenance to its abutting lawn which would affect, change, or otherwise alter the subject sidewalk. (Racette Decl. ¶ 4.) Therefore, PBM sets forth evidence that it is not a public entity for the purposes of Cal. Gov. Code § 835.

 

PBM Did Not Create or Have Notice of the Dangerous Condition

Even if the governmental provisions did apply, PBM did not create or have notice of the alleged dangerous condition.

To succeed on a claim of premises liability, plaintiff must show that: (1) defendant was the owner, occupant, or lessor of the subject premises; (2) defendant was negligent in the use, maintenance, or management of the subject premises; and (3) defendant’s negligence was the cause of injury, damage, loss or harm to plaintiff. (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619.) “Because the owner [of the premises] is not the insurer of [a] visitor’s personal safety [citation], the owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is a key to establishing its liability.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206.) Landowner liability as to trivial defects is generally a question of fact, but where reasonable minds cannot differ, courts may rule that the landowner has no liability as a matter of law. (Ursino v. Big Boy Rests (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 394, 397-398.)

Here, PBM did not design, develop, or construct the subject sidewalk. (PBM-SSF No. 4; Racette Decl. ¶ 4.) PBM does not own, possess, manage, control, or maintain the subject sidewalk. (Ibid. at No. 5; Racette Decl. ¶ 4.) Prior to the underlying incident, PBM did not perform any work or maintenance to the grassy lawn which abuts the subject sidewalk. (Ibid. at No. 6; Racette Decl. ¶ 6.) PBM made no changes or alterations to the subject sidewalk. (Ibid. at No. 8; Racette Decl. ¶ 6.) PBM was not made aware of any prior trip and falls on the subject sidewalk. (Ibid. at No. 11; Racette Decl. ¶ 9.) PBM did not receive any inspection notices from the City concerning the subject sidewalk, nor does the City have any record of sending notices to PBM regarding the subject sidewalk for five years prior to the underlying incident. (Ibid. at Nos. 12 and 13; Racette Decl. ¶ 9; Leighty Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. E [City’s response to PBM’s Requests for Admissions Nos. 15-16].) Furthermore, the City did not send notices to PBM regarding any compliance or non-compliance with repairs or maintenance of the subject sidewalk for five years prior to the underlying incident. (Ibid. at Nos. 14 and 15; Leighty Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. E [City’s response to PBM’s Requests for Admissions Ns. 17-20].) Therefore, PBM sets forth that it did not own, possess, or maintain the subject sidewalk, nor did they have notice of any alleged dangerous conditions.

 

Plaintiff’s Burden

            The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show a triable issue of fact as to PBM’s status as a private entity, ownership or control of the subject sidewalk, or notice of any alleged dangerous condition.

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that “Plaintiff’s allegations for a premises liability cause of action [can] apply to PBM for its control over the subject premises area.” (Opposition p. 2:5-6.) Plaintiff further argues that her complaint is “artfully alleged to include allegations of general negligence that apply to PBM” which would render summary judgment on the basis of PBM’s status as a private entity inappropriate. (Ibid. at 7-9.) Plaintiff argues that, to this end, PBM’s motion for summary judgment acts as a procedurally defective demurrer. “When any ground for objection to a complaint, cross-complaint, or answer appears on the face thereof, or from any matter of which the court is required to or may take judicial notice, the objection on that ground may be taken by a demurrer to the pleading.” (CCP § 430.30(a).) The Court agrees that, in part, PBM’s motion for summary judgment acts as a procedurally defective demurrer.

“If a complaint states a cause of action under any theory, regardless of the title under which the factual basis for relief is stated, that aspect of the complaint is good against a demurrer.” (Quelimane Co., Inc. et al. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. et al. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.) “[The Court] not only treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but also ‘gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context. [Citation.]” (Ibid.)

Here, Plaintiff’s complaint alleges facts sufficient to sustain a cause of action for premises liability against PBM. Plaintiff alleges that “Plaintiff was walking when, without warning, [she] came in contact with the cracked, broken, misleveled pavement [which caused] her to fall and sustains serious injury.” (Complaint ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s complaint also alleges that “Defendants had a duty of care towards all foreseeable plaintiffs, including present Plaintiff, to keep them free from foreseeable risks of harm, including but not limited to a duty to warn, make safe, adequately, properly, and safely maintain and inspect [the sidewalk at or near 5455 Bellingham Ave, Valley Village, CA 91607].” (Ibid. at ¶ 5.) Therefore, Plaintiff’s complaint, read as a whole, gives reasonable interpretation to support a claim of premises liability against PBM.

The Court now proceeds to consider PBM’s motion for summary judgment under this framework. Accordingly, Plaintiff must set forth evidence showing that PBM controlled or maintained the subject sidewalk, had notice of the alleged dangerous condition, or otherwise owed Plaintiff a duty of care.

            Plaintiff argues that Cal. Civ. Code § 831, Los Angeles Municipal Code § 62.104, and Cal. Streets and Highways Code §§ 5610-5630, et. seq., all confer a duty onto PBM of control and maintenance of the subject sidewalk. (Plaintiff’s Response to PBM-SSF (“PR-PBM-SSF") No. 5.) The Court now addresses each statutory argument in sequence.

 

Cal. Civ. Code § 831

Cal. Civ. Code § 831 states that “[a]n owner of land bounded by a road or street is presumed to own the center of the way, but the contrary may be shown.” It’s undisputed that PBM owns the land bounded by the subject sidewalk. While PBM claims the subject sidewalk is publicly owned, PBM fails to set forth supporting evidence. Therefore, a triable issue of material fact exists as to whether PBM owned the subject sidewalk abutting its property, pursuant to Cal. Civ. Code § 831.

 

Los Angeles Municipal Code § 62.104

Pursuant to L.A. Muni. Code § 62.104(b), “[t]he owner of a Lot shall maintain any Sidewalk, Driveway, Approach, Curb Return or Curb on or fronting on the Lot in such condition that the Sidewalk, Driveway Approach, Curb Return or Curb will not endanger any Person or property passing thereon or violate the Americans with Disabilities Act.” (L.A. Muni. Code § 62.104(b).) However, when read as a whole, § 62.104 does not impute private landowners with a duty of care to any persons who may be injured as a result of improperly maintained sidewalks which may front their lot. Instead, it only imputes private landowners with a duty to repair the sidewalk should the city provide notice. Here, it is undisputed that the City did not send PBM any notices regarding the subject sidewalk. (See PR-PBM-SSF Nos. 12 and 13.) Therefore, PBM did not owe Plaintiff a duty of care under L.A. Muni. Code § 62.104.

 

 

Cal. Streets and Highways Code §§ 5610-5630, et. seq.

Cal. Streets and Highways Code § 5610 provides as follows:

“The owners of lots or portions of lots fronting on any portion of a public street or place   when that street or place is improved or if and when the area between the property line of        the adjacent property and the street line is maintained as a park or parking strip, shall     maintain any sidewalk in such condition that the sidewalk will not endanger persons or   property and maintain it in a condition which will not interfere with the public                                     convenience in the use of those works or areas save and except as to those conditions                         created or maintained in, upon, along, or in connection with such sidewalk by any person other than the owner, under and by virtue of any permit or right granted to him by law or

by the city authorities in charge thereof and such persons shall be under a like duty in

relation thereto.”

(Cal. Streets and Highways Code § 5610.)

            Like L.A. Muni. Code § 62.104, Cal. Streets and Highways Code § 5610, while it addresses duties of maintenance and repair, does not impute liability upon private landowners for injuries arising from abutting public sidewalks. Jordan v. City of Sacramento (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1487 held that §5610 “imposes a duty of repair on the abutting property owners for defects in sidewalk, regardless of who created the defects, but does not of itself create tort liability to injured pedestrians or a duty to indemnify municipalities, except where a property owner created the defect or exercised dominion or control over the abutting sidewalk.” Therefore, PBM does not owe Plaintiff a duty under Cal. Streets and Highways Code § 5610,

 

V.       CONCLUSION

            A triable issue of material fact exists as to PBM’s ownership over the subject sidewalk pursuant to Cal. Civ. Code § 831. Accordingly, PBM’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED.
