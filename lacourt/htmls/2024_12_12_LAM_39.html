DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV10135    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

December 12, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV10135

MOTIONS

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Global Plumbing & Fire Supply

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Global Plumbing & Fire Supply (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Ritecon Plumbing and Patrick Kim (“Kim”) (collectively, “Defendants”) to Form Interrogatories, set one (“FROG”).  Defendants have not opposed the motion.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to interrogatories unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300.) 

In response to FROG Nos. 2.2, 2.3, 2.4, 2.5, 2.6, 2.7, and 2.8, Kim responded “N/A.”  (See Declaration of Catherine R. Finamore, Exhibit A, pp. 3-5.)  Defendants’ response is improper. (See Deyo v. Kilbourne (1984) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 783-784.)  Defendants must further respond. 

In FROG No. 15.1, Plaintiff asks Defendants to state all facts in support of their affirmative defenses.  In response, Kim states, in pertinent part, he “does not have any new information to assert to support those defenses at this time, but reserves its right to assert such defenses at trial.”  (See Finamore Decl., Exhibit A, p. 8.)  Defendants cannot fail to provide responsive information while at the same time stating they have the right to assert these defenses at trial.  If Defendants have no responsive information, Defendants must unequivocally so state. 

For the same reason, Defendants’ response to FROG No. 17.1 is improper.  In that response, Kim states he “does not have enough information at this time . . . ,” but “[d]iscovery and investigation is continuing.”  (See Finamore Decl., Exhibit A, p. 11.)  Kim adds, “To be determined.”  (Ibid.)  Defendants cannot unilaterally extend the time to provide responsive information to Plaintiff.  Defendants must provide any responsive information they have now. 

Finally, Defendants’ responses are unverified.  “Unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.”  (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.)  Accordingly, Defendants must serve verifications to the responses.  Therefore, the motion to compel further responses is granted.

The Court concludes Defendants’ failure to properly respond to the discovery is an abuse of the discovery process and awards sanctions.  Based on the evidence before the Court, the Court concludes Defendants bear responsibility for the failure to serve proper responses.  As such, the Court imposes sanctions against Defendants in the amount of $2,000, which represents four hours of attorney time to prepare the motion and attend the hearing at $485 per hour, plus the filing fee.  Defendants are to pay $2,000 in sanctions to Plaintiff, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

Case Number: 24STCV15768    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

December 12, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24STCV15768

MOTION

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Warren Lotas, LLC, Warren Lotas, and Kalin Stoyanchev

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Michael Hope

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Michael Hope (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Warren Lotas, LLC (“Company”), Warren Lotas (“Lotas”), and Kalin Stoyanchev (“Stoyanchev,” collectively, “Defendants”), alleging Lotas and Stoyanchev started the Company, and Plaintiff agreed to work for it in exchange for a five percent ownership interest.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 7-14.)  Plaintiff further alleges Lotas and Stoyanchev re-formed the Company as a two-member limited liability company to exclude Plaintiff.  (Id., ¶ 30.)  Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s first amended complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the demurrer.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

First Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duties Against Lotas and Stoyanchev

Plaintiff alleges “Lotas and Stoyanchev breached their fiduciary duties to Hope by attempting to wrongfully exclude him as a member of the Company in connection with its merger from a Massachusetts entity to a California entity; refusing to provide records to which Hope was entitled; failing to issue timely distributions or inform Hope of any reason for the Company’s inability to make distributions; and hiding corporate activity from Hope, including the Company’s decision to create and act as sole member of a new entity, Warren Lotas Retail, LLC; and likely other conduct now unknown but to be discovered.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 61.)  Pursuant to Corporations Code section 17704.09, a member’s duty of loyalty to a limited liability company and to the other members is limited to: a duty to account to the company and hold as trustee for the company any property, profit, or benefit the member derives in the conduct of the company’s activity or use of the company’s property; a duty to refrain from dealing with the company in the conduct of the company’s business as or on behalf of a person with an interest adverse to the company; and a duty to refrain from competing with the company in the conduct of the company’s business.  (Corp. Code, § 17704.09, subd. (b).)  A member’s duty of care to a limited liability company and the other members of the company is limited to a duty to refrain from engaging in grossly negligent or reckless conduct, intentional misconduct, or a knowing violation of law.  (Corp. Code, § 17704.09, subd. (c).)  Plaintiff’s allegations are that Lotas and Stoyanchev engaged in intentional misconduct to deprive Plaintiff of his ownership interest in the Company.  As such, Plaintiff’s allegations suffice for pleading purposes.

Defendants argue Plaintiff lacks standing because Plaintiff is not a member of the new company Lotas and Stoyanchev formed, Warren Lotas Retail, LLC.  That is irrelevant.  Plaintiff’s claims are against Lotas and Stoyanchev.  Plaintiff’s claims, therefore, suffice.

Defendants also argue the business judgment rule bars Plaintiff’s claims.  The business judgment rule does not shield actions taken with improper motives or because of a conflict of interest.  (Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Boyle (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1020, 1045.)  Plaintiff alleges Lotas and Stoyanchev acted with the improper motive to deprive Plaintiff of his interest in the Company.  As such, Plaintiff alleges the business judgment rule does not apply.

Second Cause of Action: Fraud Against Lotas and Stoyanchev

Plaintiff must allege fraud with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.)  To state a claim for promissory fraud, Plaintiff must allege specific factual circumstances beyond contract breach, which reflect Defendant’s contemporaneous intent not to perform.  (Hills Transportation Co. v. Southwest Forest Ind., Inc. (1968) 266 Cal.App.2d 702, 707.)  In the instant case, Plaintiff alleges Lotas and Stoyanchev falsely promised Plaintiff an ownership interest in the Company.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 64.)  Plaintiff does not allege specific facts to show Lotas and Stoyanchev did not intend to honor that promise at the time Lotas and Stoyanchev made those promises to Plaintiff.  Therefore, the demurrer as to this claim is sustained, but Plaintiff is given an opportunity to amend.[1]

Third Cause of Action: Fraudulent Concealment Against Lotas and Stoyanchev 

To state a case of action for fraudulent concealment, Plaintiff must allege Lotas and Stoyanchev concealed or suppressed material facts Lotas and Stoyanchev were under a duty to disclose to Plaintiff with the intent to defraud Plaintiff; Plaintiff was unaware of the facts and would not have acted in the same way if Plaintiff knew of the concealed facts; and the concealment caused damages to Plaintiff.  (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 868.)  In support of the third cause of action, Plaintiff alleges, “Lotas and Stoyanchev failed to disclose and/or concealed intentionally from Hope their wrongful change of the Company’s structure from a three-member to a two-member limited liability company when merging the Company from a Massachusetts entity to a California entity . . . .”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 73.)  By simply averring “[h]ad this information not been concealed from Hope, he would have undertaken a different course of action at the time” (id., ¶ 79),  Plaintiff has not alleged specific facts concerning how Plaintiff would have acted differently had Plaintiff known of the change in the Company’s structure.  Thus, the demurrer is sustained as to the third cause of action with leave to amend.

Fourth Cause of Action: Negligent Misrepresentation Against Lotas and Stoyanchev 

Plaintiff alleges Lotas and Stoyanchev represented to Plaintiff he “maintained a 5% interest in the Company for which he would be paid distributions.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 82.)  There is no cause of action for a negligent false promise.  (See Tarmann v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 159 [court “decline[d] to establish a new type of actionable deceit: the negligent false promise”].)  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s claim for negligent misrepresentation fails as a matter of law.

Fifth Cause of Action: Conversion Against Lotas and Stoyanchev 

Plaintiff alleges Lotas and Stoyanchev converted his ownership interest in the Company.  Plaintiff’s intangible interest in the Company is a proper subject for a cause of action for conversion.  (See e.g., Fremont Indem. Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 125-126 [“For purposes of ruling on the demurrer, it is sufficient to conclude as we do that the misappropriation of intangible net operating losses alleged here supports a cause of action for conversion”].)  Plaintiff’s claim suffices for pleading purposes.

Sixth Cause of Action: Breach of Contract Against the Company

 Plaintiff alleges he agreed to provide services in exchange for an ownership interest in the Company, and he was thereafter deprived of that interest.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 97-100.)  For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice.  Plaintiff is entitled to plead breach of contract in general terms.   (Ochs v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 795.) 

For the same reason, Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action for declaratory relief suffices.  “[D]emurrer is usually not an appropriate method for testing the merits of a declaratory relief action, because the plaintiff is entitled to a declaration of rights even if it is adverse to the plaintiff's interest.”  (Qualified Patients Ass'n v. City of Anaheim (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 734, 751, internal quotations and citations omitted.)   Plaintiff is entitled to assert a claim for declaratory relief regarding his ownership interest in the Company.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1060.)

Eighth Cause of Action:  Wrongful Termination Against the Company

            Plaintiff claims he was terminated after requesting records for the Company as permitted by Corporations Code section 17704.10; this amounted to retaliation; and the Company violated public policy.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 107-109.)  But Plaintiff does not allege what public policy the Company violated to substantiate his claim he was wrongfully terminated.  Moreover, Plaintiff does not point to any specific statutory provision or other authority which prohibited his termination for requesting the Company’s records.  (See Stevenson v. Superior Court (1997) 16 Cal.4th 880, 901-902 [for a policy to support a wrongful discharge claim, it must be delineated in either constitutional or statutory provisions, be public in the sense it inures to the benefit of the public rather than serving the interest of an individual, be well established at the time of the discharge, and be substantial and fundamental].)  Plaintiff has not sufficiently pled a wrongful discharge claim.  Therefore, the demurrer is sustained regarding this cause of action with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as to the second, third, and eighth causes of action; sustained without leave to amend as to the fourth cause of action; and is otherwise overruled as to the first, fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action. 

Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 20 days of notice of this order.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


[1]The Court notes Defendants contend certain of Plaintiff’s claims are time-barred.  In the operative complaint, Plaintiff alleges he understood his 5% ownership interest remained fully vested; he was “strung along” by Lotas and Stoyanchev into at least 2022; and at no time did Lotas and Stoyanchev tell him he did not have an equity interest in the Company.  (See e.g., First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 44-51.)  Plaintiff adds that for the first time on May 12, 2023, Lotas and Stoyanchev took the position he was not a lawful member of the Company; Plaintiff’s vested interest had been relinquished; and Plaintiff was not entitled to receive any distribution. (Id,. ¶ 52.)  For pleading purposes, such allegations are sufficient to ward off Defendants’ statute of limitations challenge to the claims.
