Case Number: 20STLC08411    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         July 31, 2024

Case Name:                             State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company v. Maria Reyes, et al.

Case No.:                                20STLC08411

Motion:                                   Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and for Entry of Judgment    

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company   

Responding Party:                   Unopposed  

Notice:                                    IMPROPER

 

Tentative Ruling:       The Court CONTINUES the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and for Entry of Judgment to Wednesday, September 11, 2024 at 10:00 AM in this department, so that Plaintiff can provide proper notice to Defendant Estrada. 

                                               

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 5, 2020, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint for Subrogation against Defendants Maria Reyes, Fernando X Estrada (collectively, “Defendants”), and DOES 1 through 30, inclusive. The complaint arises from Defendants colliding with the vehicle of Plaintiff’s insured.

 

On November 18, 2020, Defendants filed an Answer to the Complaint. Defendant Fernando Estrada (“Estrada”) indicates that he was erroneously sued and served as Fernando X Estrada.

 

On October 20, 2021, Defendant Maria Reyes was dismissed from this action without prejudice pursuant to a request for dismissal filed by Plaintiff.

 

On October 22, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Stipulation for Settlement and Order (the “Stipulation”). The Stipulation provides that Plaintiff has paid its insured and is seeking recovery of the monies paid to its insured in the amount of $20,454.96. (Stipulation, ¶ 1.) The parties agreed to settle this matter in the amount of $14,481.49. (Stipulation, ¶ 3.) Defendant Estrada’s insurance carrier, Infinity Insurance Company, was to pay its policy limit available to Defendant Estrada under the policy of insurance issued by Infinity Insurance Company in the amount of $6,981.59. (Stipulation, ¶ 3(a).)

 

Defendant Estrada was obligated to pay the remaining balance of $7,500.00 in the following manner: “$0.00 upon signing; $100.00 shall be paid no later than February 15, 2021 . . . [Defendant] Estrada shall thereafter make 73 payments of $100.00, with each payment being due, consecutively, on the 15th of each month thereafter . . .  [Defendant] Estrada shall make on final payment on May 16, 2027 in the amount of $100.00.” (Stipulation, ¶ 3(b)-(e).)

 

The Stipulation provides that if Defendant Estrada “fail[s] to make the payments, as agreed, on the 15th of each month, he will be deemed in default of this agreement.” (Stipulation, ¶ 5.) If Defendant Estrada is in default, the attorney for Plaintiff “will provide [Defendant] Estrada with written notice of the default and [Defendant] Estrada will have 10 days within which to remedy the default.” (Stipulation, ¶ 6.) “If [Defendant] Estrada does not remedy the attorney for [Plaintiff] may, by ex parte application, submit an order to the court for entry of judgment against [Defendant] Estrada without further notice. Judgment shall be entered in the amount of $20,454.96 plus interest on that amount, at the legal rate, from 01/23/20, plus costs and attorney’s fees incurred by the plaintiff in enforcing the Stipulation, less any payments that have bene made by or on behalf of the defendant to the plaintiff, as of that date.” (Stipulation, ¶ 6.)

 

The Stipulation indicates that if the Court dismisses this matter, then the Court shall retain jurisdiction to enforce the Stipulation pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. (Stipulation, ¶ 8.) Each party shall bear their own attorneys’ fees and costs except as otherwise stipulated. (Stipulation, ¶ 10.)

 

On October 22, 2021, the Court entered an Order (the “Order”), pursuant to the Stipulation, which dismissed this action with prejudice in its entirety but allowed the Court to retain jurisdiction to enforce the Stipulation pursuant to CCP § 664.6. (10/22/21 Order.)

 

On October 26, 2021, Plaintiff filed and served a Notice of Entry of Order which gave Defendant Estrada notice of the Court’s October 22, 2021 Order.

 

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Proposed Judgment on Stipulation; however, such filing was rejected as it was not accompanied by a declaration setting forth the facts supporting the entry of judgment.

 

On June 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and for Entry of Judgment in the Amount of $16,919.86 (the “Motion”). The Motion was served on Defendant Estrada on June 26, 2024 via U.S. Mail.

 

            The Court notes that Plaintiff did not file the Motion with a proposed order.  The Court also notes that the Notice of Motion does not set forth the courthouse address. As of July 30, 2024, the Motion is unopposed. Any opposition to the Motion was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Plaintiff contends that the parties entered into an enforceable agreement and the Court should enter judgment against Defendant Estrada thereto. Plaintiff asserts that it is entitled to interest, costs, and attorneys’ fees.  

 

OPPOSITION

 

            No opposition brief was filed as of July 30, 2024.

 

 

REPLY

 

            No reply brief was filed as of July 30, 2024.

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Setting Aside the Dismissal and Entering Judgment    

A.                Legal Standard

“Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 provides a summary procedure to enforce a settlement agreement by entering judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” Hines v. Lukes (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182. “[I]f the parties to pending litigation enter into a settlement either in writing signed by the parties or orally before the court, the court, upon a motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” Ibid. “The court retains jurisdiction to enforce a settlement under the statute even after a dismissal, but only if the parties requested such a retention of jurisdiction before the dismissal.” Ibid. “Such a request must be made either in writing signed by the parties or orally before the court.” Ibid.

“A court ruling on a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 must determine whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement.” Ibid. “A settlement is enforceable under section 664.6 only if the parties agreed to all material settlement terms.” Ibid. “The court ruling on the motion may consider the parties’ declarations and other evidence in deciding what terms the parties agreed to.” Ibid. “If the court determines that the parties entered into an enforceable settlement, it should grant the motion and enter a formal judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” Ibid. Code Civ. Proc. § 664.6 “expressly provides for the court to enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” Id. at p. 1183. “It is widely recognized that the courts are not at liberty to revise an agreement under the guise of construing it.” Series AGI Est Linn of Appian Group Investors DE, LLC v. Eves (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 156, 164. A prevailing party is entitled to its costs in any action or proceeding. (CCP § 1032(b).) A prevailing party is a party with a net monetary recovery. (CCP § 1032(a)(4).) “Except as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties.” (CCP § 1021.)

B.        Discussion  

In support of the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel, David B. Pillemer (“Pillemer”), provides a declaration. Mr. Pillemer states the following: on or about December 23, 2020, the parties entered into the Stipulation. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. 1.) Pursuant to the Stipulation and Order, the Court retained jurisdiction to enforce the terms of the Stipulation. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendant Estrada has ceased to make payments pursuant to the Stipulation and has failed and refused, and continues to fail and refuse, to make any additional payments pursuant to the Stipulation. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 5.)  

Plaintiff’s counsel sent correspondence to Defendant Estrada on April 24, 2024, warning him that payment pursuant to Stipulation had not been received, and that if payment was not made within 10 days, Plaintiff would seek to enforce the judgment provision of the Stipulation. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 6; Ex. 2.)

Defendant Estrada has made payments in the amount of $10,251.59. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 7(d).) Interest is in the amount of $6,279.84, which is calculated at 7 percent per annum from January 23, 2020 to June 12, 2024. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 7(b).) The principal amount is $20,454.96. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 7(a).) Plaintiff has incurred $375.00 in attorneys’ fees to enforce the Stipulation and has incurred a filing fee for the Motion of $61.65. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 7(c).) Thus, Plaintiff requests entry of judgment against Defendant Estrada in the total amount of $16,619.86. (Pillemer Decl., ¶ 7.)

The Court finds that it has authority to enforce the Stipulation pursuant to Hines v. Lukes, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th 1174, 1182. Plaintiff and Defendant Estrada signed the Stipulation. The Court also has jurisdiction to enforce the Stipulation as the parties explicitly requested—and furthermore agreed—that the Court would retain jurisdiction to enforce such agreement. The Court furthermore determines that the parties entered into a valid and binding agreement. The declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel sets forth the terms of the Stipulation and indicates that Defendant Estrada has breached the Stipulation.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to interest as well as costs and attorneys’ fees incurred in enforcing the stipulation. (Stipulation, ¶ 7.)

 

However, the Court cannot grant the Motion at this time due to the defective notice of the motion. A notice of motion must set forth “[t]he date, time, and location, if ascertainable, of any scheduled hearing and the name of the hearing judge.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1110(b)(1).) Here, the notice of motion does not set forth the courthouse address and merely states that this matter will be heard in Department 25. Based on the notice of motion, Defendant Estrada would not be able to ascertain whether the hearing on the Motion was at the Spring Street Courthouse or the Stanley Mosk Courthouse.

Tentative Ruling:                    Defendant’s Demurrer to the Second Cause of Action in the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.  

                                               

 

BACKGROUND

 

On January 8, 2024, Plaintiff Luxe Development, Inc. dba Luxe Home Solutions, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Judith Taylor (“Defendant”) and DOES 1 through 10, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of written contract, (2) quantum meruit/common count, (3) unjust enrichment, (4) account stated, and (5) foreclosure of lien.

 

Defendant was served with the summons and complaint on February 21, 2024. (02/27/24 Proof of Service.)

 

On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative first amended complaint against Defendant alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of written contract, (2) quantum meruit, and (3) foreclosure of lien.

 

On May 17, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer to the second cause of action. The Court notes that the demurrer was not filed with an accompanying proof of service. Moreover, the Court notes that Defendant demurs “to the [c]omplaint [f]or [d]amages . . . filed by [p]laintiff . . . .” (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:16-19.) The complaint is no longer the operative pleading due to the filing of the first amended complaint. The Court assumes that Defendant is demurring to the first amended complaint. Plaintiff takes no issue with the error in the notice of motion.

 

On July 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the demurrer, to which Defendant filed a reply brief on July 24, 2024. Defendant’s reply brief was also filed without a proof of service.

 

Defendant is admonished to ensure that that its motions are directed at the operative and proper pleading. Defendant is also admonished to file a proof of service for any motions filed with the Court. The Court will therefore treat the demurrer as targeting the second cause of action in the operative first amended complaint.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Defendant argues that quantum meruit is not a cause of action and such cause of action is not available when there is an express contract pled.   

 

OPPOSITION

 

            On opposition, Plaintiff argues that quantum meruit is a valid cause of action and that Defendant is incorrect that quantum meruit cannot be pled alongside a claim for breach of written contract.

 

REPLY

 

            On reply, Defendant asserts that quantum meruit is not a cause of action when the complaint also makes a claim for breach of written contract.   

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Demurrer

A.                Meet and Confer Requirement  

            Before filing a demurrer, the moving party must meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41.) “Any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)

            The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been met. Defendant’s counsel, James Howard (“Howard”), declares that on or about April 22, 2024, he discussed the demurrer with Plaintiff’s counsel. (Howard Decl., ¶ 2.) Mr. Howard and Plaintiff’s counsel, however, were unable to come to a resolution. (Howard Decl., ¶ 2.)

B.        Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. In assessing a demurrer, the court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318. “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” Hahn v. Mirda, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.  A complaint will be upheld against a demurrer if it pleads facts sufficient to place the defendant on notice of the issues sufficient to enable the defendant to prepare a defense.  Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549-50. “Generally it is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.” Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349. A “[p]laintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” Ibid.

1.         Second Cause of Action for Quantum Meruit       

 

“Quantum meruit refers to the well-established principle that the law implies a promise to pay for services performed under circumstances disclosing that they were not gratuitously rendered. To recover in quantum meruit, a party need not prove the existence of a contract, but it must show the circumstances were such that the services were rendered under some understanding or expectation of both parties that compensation therefor was to be made.” Chodos v. Borman (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 76, 96, citations and quotation marks omitted. “[I]n order to recover under a quantum meruit theory, a plaintiff must establish both that he or she was acting pursuant to either an express or implied request for such services from the defendant and that the services rendered were intended to and did benefit the defendant.” Day v. Alta Bates Medical Center (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 243, 248.

 

“A plaintiff may not . . . pursue or recover on a quasi-contract claim if the parties have an enforceable agreement regarding a particular subject matter.” Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1388. A cause of action for quantum meruit “is a quasi-contract claim for the reasonable value of services rendered.” Jogani v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 901, 909. A plaintiff is prohibited from alleging a quasi-contract cause of action where a plaintiff’s “breach of contract claim [pleads] the existence of an enforceable agreement and their [quantum meruit] claim [does] not deny the existence or enforceability of that agreement.” Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1389-90.

 

The first amended complaint (“FAC”) alleges the following: on January 24, 2023, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a contract (the “Contract”) for renovations to 3645 Cardiff Ave., Unit 110, Los Angeles, CA 90034 (the “Project”) owned by Defendant. (FAC, ¶ 12; Ex. 2.) The Contract contained milestone installment payments for twelve discreet phases of construction totaling $94,090.00. (FAC, ¶ 13.) The Contract did not provide for a completion deadline for the Project. (FAC, ¶ 13.) Construction of the Project began as scheduled and was completed. (FAC, ¶ 14.) In September and October of 2023, Plaintiff requested that Defendant pay all amounts outstanding under the Contract, and Defendant did not pay as agreed, which breached the Contract. (FAC, ¶ 15.) On or about October 11, 2023, Plaintiff recorded a Mechanic’s Lien in the principal amount of $18,618.00 with interest thereon at the rate of 7 percent per annum from October 12, 2023. (FAC, ¶ 16.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant breached the Contract and that it has been damaged due to such breach. (FAC, ¶¶ 29-30.)

 

The second cause of action for quantum meruit incorporates the allegations of the breach of contract cause of action. (FAC, ¶¶ 31-35.) Pursuant to the second cause of action, Plaintiff alleges that it furnished work, labor, and services to Defendant at the request of Defendant, for which Defendant promised to pay the reasonable value of said work, labor, and services. (FAC, ¶ 32.) Defendant expressly understood, expected, agreed, and promised that reasonable compensation was required to be paid to Plaintiff for such work, labor, and services, and that Plaintiff’s performance of such work would give rise to the indebtedness on their part for money owed to Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 33.) The reasonable value of the work, labor, and services performed by Plaintiff for Defendant is $18,618.00. (FAC, ¶ 34.) This money has not been paid and remains due and owing. (FAC, ¶ 34.)

 

In analyzing a demurrer, the Court construes a complaint “liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded.” Rodas v. Spiegel (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 513, 517. Thus, based on the allegations of the FAC, an inference is created that Plaintiff is alleging that Defendant breached the contract that gives rise to the second cause of action. Plaintiff, however, does not deny the validity or existence of the agreement between the parties pursuant to the second cause of action. (FAC, ¶¶ 31-35.) Plaintiff cannot allege a claim for quantum meruit where Plaintiff has failed to deny the existence or enforceability of the agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant. Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1389-90.

 

            The Court therefore SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action in the first amended complaint with leave to amend.

 

II.        Conclusion

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action in the first amended complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

           

On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff Cravon Gillespie (“Gillespie”) filed the operative Complaint against Defendants Dillon M. Stanger (“Stanger”); Sachet M. Gagwani (“Gagwani”); DMS Internet Holdings, LLC; (“DMS”); and G Venture Group, LLC (“Venture”) for the following four causes of action related to a dispute arising out of an agreement to do business: (1) Business and Professions Code (§ 17200 use of Deceptive Practices); (2) Business and Professions Code (§ 17500—Inducing Business via False and Misleading statements); (3) Breach of Contract; and (4) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing. Plaintiff alleges that on June 6, 2022[1], Plaintiff entered into a Service Agreement (“Agreement”) with Defendants whereby Plaintiff paid Defendants $30,000 in exchange for Defendants’ agreement to construct an Ecommerce Webstore to sell curated good Defendants would purchase with Plaintiff’s banking information. Defendants were to set up, operate, manage, support, and grow Plaintiff’s Ecommerce Webstore using Defendants’ alleged proprietary and confidential methods, strategies, procedures, and processes which included marketing, customer support, and promotion. According to Plaintiff, both of Defendants’ attempts to operate Plaintiff’s e-commerce store failed.

 

On June 14, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration (“Mot.”) which also requests the Court order the proceedings dismissed or stayed pending resolution of arbitration, as well as an award of attorney’s fees and costs. Defendants filed this motion along with the Declaration of Dillon Stanger (“Stanger Decl.”) and an attached exhibit of the Service Agreement containing the arbitration provisions therein.[2]

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition which Defendants filed a Reply to on July 24, 2024, but failed to attach their proof of service. On July 25, 2024, Defendants filed a second Reply with the relevant proof of service attached.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Under CCP §1281.2, “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.” In determining whether the arbitration agreement applies to the particular dispute in this case, the court should attempt to give effect to the parties' intentions, in light of the usual and ordinary meaning of contractual language and circumstances under which the agreement was made. (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350.)

In deciding whether to compel arbitration, judges identify the controversy and then decide whether it is within the scope of the arbitration provisions. (Titolo v. Cano (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 310, 316.) “‘Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration. The court should order arbitration unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.’” (California Correctional Peace Officers Ass'n v. State (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205.)

Defendants provide that on June 3, 2022, Defendants G Venture Group, LLC, and DMS Internet Holdings, LLC entered into a Service Agreement whereby certain Ecommerce services were to be provided to Plaintiff. Defendants assert that each Defendant in this case is entitled to an order compelling arbitration of all of Plaintiff’s asserted individual claims consistent with the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) (9 U.S.C., §§ 1-16) and the California Arbitration Act (“CAA”) (CCP §§ 1280 et. seq.) because Plaintiff is a party to a written Service Agreement (“Agreement) containing an enforceable provision to arbitrate the disputes encompassed within the action in New Jersey. (Mot., pg. 2.) Moreover, arbitration is in the interests of judicial economy. (Id.)

 

Defendants provide the following relevant excerpt from the June 3 Service Agreement:

 

“9. GOVERNING LAW, DISPUTES AND ARBITRATION

 

9.1 The Service Agreement is made under the exclusive jurisdiction of the laws of the State of New Jersey, as applied to agreements performed wholly within the State of New Jersey. Notwithstanding the preceding sentence with respect to applicable substantive law, any arbitration conducted pursuant to the terms of this Service Agreement shall be governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C., Secs. 1-16).

 

9.2 Any dispute, claim or controversy arising out of or relating to this Agreement or the breach, termination, enforcement, interpretation or validity thereof, including the determination of the scope or applicability of this agreement to arbitrate, shall be determined by arbitration in Wayne, New Jersey before one arbitrator. The arbitrator shall be an attorney or retired judge with no less than ten (10) years of experience resolving disputes between merchants and service providers offering digital marketplace sales, marketing and distribution services. The arbitration shall be administered by JAMS pursuant to its Comprehensive Arbitration Rules and Procedures and in accordance with the Expedited Procedures in those Rules; provided, however, that the parties shall confirm with JAMS whether their dispute qualifies for JAMS EndisputeTM Online Dispute Resolution services (referenced hereafter as “Endispute” and found on “https://www.jamsadr.com/endispute/”), and if it does, the parties agree to resolve the dispute through Endispute services. The parties shall bear their own costs other than for the costs of the arbitrator and JAMS services, which shall be split evenly. The arbitrator shall issue a written opinion with findings of fact and conclusions of law which shall not exceed ten (10) pages. The arbitrator may award reimbursement for the costs of the arbitration, including reasonable attorneys’ fees, to the prevailing party, in addition to any other award issued by the arbitrator. However, the monetary value of the award shall not exceed the amount the Client paid the Contractor minus the Profit Split paid to the Client. This clause shall not preclude parties from seeking provisional remedies in aid of arbitration from a court of appropriate jurisdiction. Judgment on the award may be entered in any court having jurisdiction.

(Stanger Decl., Ex. A.)

 

On Opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ instant Motion should be denied for two reasons: (1) it violates relevant California case law and (2) Defendants should be permitted to engage in limit discovery pending the resolution of the instant motion.

 

Defendants first argument contends that the Service Agreement is in direct violation of Jack v. Ring LLC, 91 Cal.App.5th 1186, 1205 because the Agreement contains a Class Action Waiver in Section 12. Specifically, Plaintiff states that Defendants “have mandated Plaintiff to arbitrate on an ‘individual basis,’ prohibited private attorney general actions, and limited awards in arbitration to ‘relief on behalf of the named parties.’” (Opp., pg. 2.) Plaintiff cites excerpts from Jack containing parentheticals to other cases, including “[arbitration provision that permits ‘relief only on an individual basis’ means the ‘the arbitrator [is precluded] from awarding public injunctive relief which is relief intended to benefit those other than the individual bringing the claim’]; Rogers v. Lyft, Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2020) 452 F.Supp.3d 904, 917-918, affd. (9th Cir., Feb. 16, 2022, No. 20-15689) 2022 WL 474166 (Rogers) . . .” (Opp., pg. 2.) Plaintiff provides the following section for Class Action Waiver from the Service Agreement:

 

“12. CLASS ACTION WAIVER
Any legal action or arbitration arising in connection with the use of the Ecommerce Webstore, Contractor’s Service, or any of the terms and conditions in this Agreement must be on an individual basis. Neither Party agrees to join or consolidate claims by or against other clients, or litigate in court or arbitrate any claims as a representative or member of a class or in a private Attorney General capacity.” (Opp., pg. 2.)

 

Second, Plaintiff argues that he has a limited right to engage in discovery to determine if the Defendants engaged in intentional fraud to induce unsuspecting clients like Plaintiff to enter into agreement with Defendants in violation of California Business and Professions Code § 17200—Deceptive Practices. (Opp., pgs. 4-5.) Plaintiff’s argument points to the tenth term of the Service Agreement:

 

“10. LIMITATION OF LIABILITY - UNDER NO CIRCUMSTANCES WILL EITHER PARTY BE LIABLE TO THE OTHER UNDER ANY CONTRACT, STRICT LIABILITY, NEGLIGENCE OR OTHER THEORY IN LAW OR EQUITY, FOR ANY INCIDENTAL, INDIRECT, SPECIAL OR CONSEQUENTIAL DAMAGES OR DAMAGES FOR LOST PROFITS, REVENUE OR DATA IN CONNECTION WITH THIS AGREEMENT, EVEN IF THE OTHER PARTY HAS BEEN ADVISED OF THE POSSIBILITY OF SUCH DAMAGES AND EVEN IF SUCH DAMAGES ARE FORESEEABLE. EXCEPT IN THE CASE OF INTENTIONAL FRAUD.”

(Stanger Decl., Ex. A, pg. 15.)

 

 

Plaintiff argues that in Arguelles-Romero v. Superior Court, 184 Cal.App.4th 825, 831-832 the Arguelles court provided that the trial court in that case permitted plaintiffs certain limited written discovery and indicated that if plaintiffs had any doubts regarding the veracity of [defendant’s] responses, they could apply ex parte for follow-up discovery, which might include a deposition. (Opp., pgs. 3-4.)

 

In Reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s argument is unintelligible, and the cited cases are inapposite because Plaintiff has cited to two California cases which are preempted by the FAA. (Reply, pg. 2.) Pursuant to the Supremacy Clause of the U.S. Constitution, Defendants argue that federal law supersedes state law on the instant matter. (Id.) Moreover, Plaintiff’s opposition does not directly address Defendants’ motion and the foregoing cases do not apply to the case at bar. (Id.) Jack v. Ring is distinguished from the instant case because Jack involved a class action whereby defendant Ring attempted to compel arbitration pursuant to their terms of service which arose from a contract of adhesion. (Reply, pg. 3.) On the other hand, the instant action is a private dispute between the parties which is governed by the Service Agreement which was signed and entered into bilaterally between the parties. Moreover, the instant action is entirely unrelated to consumer protection laws and makes no mention of an express carve-out provision permitting either party to seek injunctive or other equitable relief in Los Angeles’ state or federal court. (Id.)

 

In this case, the Court agrees that Plaintiff’s arguments on opposition are uncertain and unintelligible and, moreover, fail to demonstrate any applicability to the instant case. The current case does not concern a consumer protection dispute, nor does Plaintiff attempt to argue so. Similarly, the instant case does not concern a claim under Private Attorney General Act (“PAGA”) and nor does Plaintiff argue so. Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate how the Class Action Waiver contained in section 12 applies to bar Plaintiff from arbitrating on an individual basis. Plaintiff does not explicate an argument as to how the instant motion or relevant arbitration provisions directly violate the public policy provisions encouraging public injunctive relief as articulated in Jack.

 

As to Arguelles, Defendants argue in Reply that Arguelles has no applicability to the instant action because it involved a consumer protection dispute, not a private business dispute. (Reply, pg. 3.) Moreover, Arguelles does not support Plaintiff’s contention that there is a right to limited discovery prior to arbitration. Defendants argue the Court has no authority to circumvent the express terms of the Service Agreement and the FAC, by allowing the Plaintiff to conduct any such discovery outside of arbitration in New Jersey. (Id.)

 

Again, the Court finds the instant argument unintelligible and inapplicable. Plaintiff quotes an excerpt from Arguelles whereby the Arguelles court summarizes the actions of the trial court in permitting certain limited discovery. Though the trial court in Arguelles did permit Plaintiff to engage in limited discovery, such discovery was directed as to the issue of enforceability of the arbitration clause and class action waiver. This was because the Arguelles arbitration provision concerned a contract of adhesion on a “take it or leave it basis” wherein Plaintiff had no meaningful ability to negotiate the terms. Here, Plaintiff fails fatally to demonstrate how the facts of the instant case relate at all to Arguelles. Plaintiff’s relevant arbitration provisions concern what appears to be a bilateral business agreement and Plaintiff does not attempt to argue otherwise. Moreover, Plaintiff does not allege facts for intentional fraud as a basis for seeking limited discovery. Thus, Arguelles is similarly inapposite.

 

Lastly, Defendants request the Court award Defendants attorneys’ fees for the five (5) hours of work billed by Defendants attorney in filing the instant motion at the rate of $500 per hour, along with $1,480 in filing fees. In total, Defendants request $3,980 in attorneys’ fees and filing fees.

 

Thus, the Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED, and the Court orders the instant proceedings STAYED pending completion of arbitration. The Court awards attorneys’ fees and court costs for Defendants in the amount of $3,980, to be paid by Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s attorney, jointly and severally.
