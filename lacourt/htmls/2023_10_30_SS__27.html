DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV38000    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: 27

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Lee S. Arian, Department 27

 

 

HEARING DATE:      October 30, 2023                                         TRIAL DATE:  February 13, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                                Ahmad Ali v. Sylvia Ali

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV38000

 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT SYLVIA ALI

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Ahmad Ali

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant Sylvia Ali

 

 

I.          BACKGROUND

 

            This action arises from an alleged assault and battery between former spouses.  Plaintiff Ahmad Ali alleges that he attempted to get into the vehicle of Defendant Sylvia Ali when she suddenly accelerated.  Plaintiff held onto the passenger door.  Despite telling her to stop the vehicle, Defendant continued driving.  As a result, Plaintiff fell off the vehicle, hit his head on the pavement and sustained additional injuries to his left leg when Defendant ran over it.

 

On July 7, 2022, Defendant’s son Joshua S. Morris filed an application to be appointed as Defendant’s guardian ad litem because Defendant was incapable of assisting in her defense.  In support of the application, Mr. Morris submitted a letter from Dr. Arthur An, Defendant’s physician, which stated that Defendant had been diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease with significant impairment of her memory and cognitive function.  The application was granted on the same day. 

 

On December 22, 2022, Plaintiff noticed Defendant’s deposition.  Defense counsel timely interposed an objection based on Defendant’s lack of competence.

 

On March 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel Defendant to appear for deposition.  Plaintiff requests sanctions against Defendant.  

 

On October 13, 2023, Defendant filed an opposition.  Defendant requests sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel.

 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD TO COMPEL DEPOSITION 

            Any party may obtain discovery by taking in California the oral deposition of any person.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)¿ “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action…without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (a).)¿¿¿ 

      Monetary Sanctions 

            “If a motion under [Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450] subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)¿¿¿ 

            Sanctions against counsel:¿ The court in Kwan Software Engineering, Inc. v. Hennings (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 57, 81 (Hennings) noted that discovery sanctions against an attorney are governed by a different standard than sanctions against a party:¿¿¿ 

By the terms of the statute, a trial court under section 2023.030(a) may not impose monetary sanctions against a party’s attorney unless the court finds that the attorney “advised” the party to engage in the conduct resulting in sanctions. (§ 2023.030(a); Ghanooni v. Super Shuttle (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 256, 261, 24 Cal.Rptr.2d 501.)¿ “Unlike monetary sanctions against a party, which are based on the party’s misuse of the discovery process, monetary sanctions against the party’s attorney require a finding the ‘attorney advis[ed] that conduct.’ ” (Ibid.) “It is not enough that the attorney's actions were in some way improper.” (Corns v. Miller (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 195, 200, 226 Cal.Rptr. 247 (Corns).) Because an attorney's advice to a client is “peculiarly within [his or her] knowledge,” the attorney has the burden of showing that he or she did not counsel discovery abuse. (Ibid.) Accordingly, when a party seeking sanctions against an attorney offers sufficient evidence of a misuse of the discovery process, the burden shifts to the attorney to demonstrate that he or she did not recommend that conduct. (Id. at pp. 200–201, 226 Cal.Rptr. 247; Ghanooni, at p. 262, 24 Cal.Rptr.2d 501.)¿

III.      DISCUSSION

 

The central issue to be decided is whether Defendant can provide testimony.

In California, a party is incompetent if he or she lacks the capacity to understand the nature or consequences of the proceeding, or is unable to assist counsel in the preparation of the case.  (In re Jessica G. (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 1180, 1186.)  Section 372 of the Code of Civil Procedure provides that when a person lacking legal capacity to make decisions is a party, “that person shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., 372, subd. (a)(1).)   Section 373 of the Code of Civil Procedure provides, in relevant part, that when a guardian ad litem is appointed, he or she shall be appointed “if the person lacking legal competence to make decisions is a party to an action or proceeding.”  (Code Civ. Proc., 373, subd. (c).)  Incompetency may exist independently of any judicial determination thereof.  (Sarracino v. Superior Court (1974) 13 Cal.3d 1, 12, citing Olivera v. Grace (1942) 19 Cal.2d 570, 577.)

Here, Defendant’s son Joshua S. Morris was appointed as Defendant’s guardian ad litem due to her inability to participate in her defense.  The application was supported by a letter from Defendant’s physician Dr. Arthur An, who stated that Defendant “had been diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease with significant impairment of her memory and cognitive function.”  (Declaration of Nolan King, February 9, 2022, Letter, Ex. I.)  Defendant also submits the Dr. An’s declaration executed on July 29, 2023, which states that Defendant cannot give a meaningful deposition in this case “due to a total mental decline as a result of the advanced stage dementia.”  (King Decl., July 29, 2023 Declaration, Ex. I.)  Based on this information, it appears that Defendant is not capable of being deposed. 

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant should have moved for a protective order.  Plaintiff is mistaken.  When served with a deposition notice, a party may file a motion for protective order, (CCP section 2025.420), or serve written objections no later than three days prior to the noticed deposition date. (CCP section 2025.410(a), (b)).  Here, Defendant chose to serve written objections, and did so on January 6, 2023—more than three days prior to the noticed deposition date of January 24, 2023.  And as is evident, Plaintiff thereafter filed this motion.  Having filed this motion, Defendant’s properly served objection may be considered in the disposition of Plaintiff’s motion. 

 

Plaintiff next argues that Defendant’s medical records do not show a diagnosis of Alzheimer’s disease or dementia.  Yet, Dr. An’s February 9, 2022 letter and Dr. An’s July 29, 2023 declaration indicate that Plaintiff has dementia.  Plaintiff questions the validity of Dr. An’s declaration, but he fails to provide more than speculative evidence to support this serious accusation.  Further, the 2019 Beverly Hospital medical records that are included in Defendant’s Reply make clear reference to Plaintiff demonstrating signs of dementia.  That was in 2019, and dementia is a disease that progressively worsens. Based on the evidence before the Court, the Court finds, consistent with the already issued guardian ad litem appointment, that Defendant cannot provide meaningful testimony.  Thus, Plaintiff is not entitled to an order compelling Defendant to appear for deposition.

 

Monetary Sanctions

            Both parties request sanctions.  Given that the Court intends to deny this motion, the Court considers imposition of sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel only.  Based on Dr. An’s letter and declaration, it is evident to the Court that Defendant is not capable of providing testimony. As such, the Court finds Plaintiff brought this motion without substantial justification.  Pursuant to Hennings, supra, imposition of sanctions against counsel is also proper unless counsel shows that he or she did not counsel the discovery abuse.¿ (Hennings, 58 Cal.App.5th at p. 81.)¿ Plaintiff’s counsel does not meet their burden.  Accordingly, sanctions are imposed against Plaintiff and his counsel in the amount of $750, representing 2 hours at defense counsel’s hourly rate.

IV.       CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel is denied. 

           

Defendant’s request for sanctions is granted.  Plaintiff and his counsel are ordered to pay, jointly and severally, sanctions in the amount of $750, to be paid to Defendant, by and through her counsel within 30 days of this order.

HEARING DATE:      October 30, 2023                                         TRIAL DATE:  March 5, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                                Ami Lanelle Dorian v. Maamar M. Susini, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV45612

 

 

MOTION FOR AN ORDER NUNC PRO TUNC

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Fernando Dorian Ruiz

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     No opposition

 

 

This is a consolidated action arising from a May 27, 2021 pedestrian bicyclist versus motor vehicle accident.  On May 24, 2023, Plaintiff Fernando Dorian Ruiz filed a complaint against Defendant Maamar M. Susini for Loss of Consortium arising from the May 27, 2021 accident.  Due to a technical defect, Plaintiff’s Complaint was rejected.  On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff successfully filed the Complaint.  

 

Plaintiff now moves for an order nunc pro tunc to correct the Complaint’s filing date from June 5, 2023, to the filing date of May 24, 2023.

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

            The Court has inherent authority to enter retroactive orders.  (See Scalice v. Performance Cleaning Systems¿(1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 221, 238-239.)  A request for a retroactive order “is to be granted or refused as justice may require in view of the circumstances of a particular case.” (Young v. Gardner-Denver Co.¿(1966) 244 Cal.App.2d 915, 919.)  

  

            Here, Plaintiff submits the declaration of Amir Kermani (“Kermani”), who is a legal assistant at Plaintiff’s counsel law firm.  Kermani states he e-filed Plaintiff’s Complaint, Summons, Statement of Location, and Civil Case Coversheet with the Journal Technology Court Portal on May 24, 2023.  (Declaration of Amir Kermani, ¶¿3.)  The filing was rejected on May 26, 2023 because the Civil Case Coversheet did not go through.  (Declaration of Amir Kermani, ¶¶¿3, 5; Exhibit 3.)  After an additional attempt, the filing was finally accepted on June 5, 2023. (Declaration of Amir Kermani, ¶ 7; Exhibit 4.)  Kermani contacted the E-Filing Department Supervising Clerk, Christine Meeks, who, after reviewing the accepted and rejected filings, concluded that the rejection was an error.  (Declaration of Amir Kermani, ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff thus argues correction of the filing date from June 5, 2023, to May 24, 2023, is proper because Plaintiff timely presented his Complaint to the Clerk of the Court for filing during business hours on May 24, 2023.  Consequently, Plaintiff’s action commenced on that date; i.e., within the statute of limitations.  (See Carlson v. Department of Fish & Game (1998) 68 Cal.app.4th 1268, 1270-1274; United Farm Workers of America v. Agricultural Labor Relations Board (1985) 37 Cal.3d 912, 918 [for purposes of the statute of limitations, “filing” means delivery to the clerk during business hours].)  As such, the rejection of a filing for a technical defect is not relevant to the timeliness of the filing.  

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Court grants the unopposed motion.

Case Number: 22STCV01540    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: 27

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Lee S. Arian, Department 27

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 30, 2023                               TRIAL DATE:  March 13, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                                Jenny Iturrios, et al. v. EAN Holdings, LLC, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV01540

 

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AN AMENDED ANSWER

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Sabrina Nicole Hicks

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiffs Jenny Iturrios, et al.

 

 

I.          INTRODUCTION

 

This is a consolidated action arising from a three-vehicle collision.  On January 13, 2022, Plaintiffs Jenny Iturrios and Mayra Perez, filed a complaint against Defendants EAN Holdings, LLC,[1] Sabrina Nicole Hicks (“Hicks”), Rashida Isoke Lenard, and Darlene Moka.  Plaintiffs asserted causes of action for negligence, negligence per se, and negligent entrustment.  Hicks filed an Answer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint on April 6, 2022.

 

On March 30, 2023, Hicks filed a Motion for Leave to File First Amended Answer. 

 

On April 19, 2023, Hicks filed this Amended Motion for Leave to File First Amended Answer. 

 

On October 18, 2023, Plaintiffs filed an Opposition.  On October 30, 2023, Hicks filed a Reply.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The court may, in its discretion and after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading, including adding or striking out the name of any party, or correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).)¿ “Public policy dictates that leave to amend be liberally granted.”¿ (Centex Homes v. St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 23, 32.)¿ “Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial . . . this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party.’¿ [Citation].¿ A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown.¿ [Citation.]” ¿(Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)¿ 

 

            A motion to amend a pleading must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments and must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted or added, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the allegations are located. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)¿ The motion shall also be accompanied by a declaration attesting to the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 1.324, subd. (b).)¿ 

 

            In ruling on a motion for leave to amend a pleading, the court does not consider the merits of the proposed amendment, because “the preferable practice would be to permit the amendment and allow the parties to test its legal sufficiency by demurrer, motion for judgment on the pleadings or other appropriate proceedings.”¿ (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048.)¿ While the court may deny leave to amend where the proposed amendment is insufficient to state a valid cause of action or defense, such denial is most appropriate where the insufficiency cannot be cured by further amendment—i.e., where the statute of limitations has expired or the insufficiency is established by controlling caselaw. (California Casualty Gen. Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 274, 280-281, disapproved on other grounds in Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390.)¿ 

 

III.       DISCUSSION

 

Procedural defects in Hicks’ motion prevents the Court from granting leave to file the proposed amended answer at this time.  However, given the lack of prejudice, the Court is inclined to continue the hearing for the motion to allow Hicks to cure the procedural defects. 

 

Procedural Defects 

 

Hicks’ motion is procedurally defective.  “A motion to amend a pleading must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments and must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted or added, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the allegations are located.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)¿ Here, Hicks does not submit a proposed or amended pleading which is serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings.  In her reply, Hicks contends the proposed amended answer is attached as Exhibit I to the declaration of her counsel, Rory D. Leos.  However, Exhibit I is not attached to the Leos Declaration. 

 

Hicks also has not included a declaration in compliance with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324.  “The motion shall also be accompanied by a declaration attesting to the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)¿ As Plaintiffs point out, the Leos Declaration does not attest to the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier.  Hicks has not satisfied this requirement. 

 

Lack of Prejudice

 

Notwithstanding the procedural defects, the Court is inclined to grant this motion.

 

Generally, leave is liberally granted at any stage of the proceedings unless inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party is shown.¿¿(Magpali, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 487.)

 

Hicks seeks leave to file the proposed First Amended Answer to add an affirmative defense based on breach of contract.  In support, Hicks offers the declaration of her counsel, Rory D. Leos, who explains that Plaintiffs and Hicks reached a settlement agreement prior to the initiation of this action.  However, before the agreement could be signed by all parties, Plaintiffs filed the Complaint.  Hicks cites CSAA Ins. Exch. v. Hodroj (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 27 for the proposition that a settlement is valid and enforceable when the parties agree upon the material terms despite differences in how the terms are written in the formal settlement agreement.  As such, Hicks argues the parties have an enforceable settlement which the Plaintiffs breached by filing the Complaint.  Hicks further argues that leave to add this affirmative defense serves the interests of justice as it is potentially dispositive of Plaintiffs’ claims against Hicks.

 

Plaintiffs contend that granting the motion would be prejudicial to Plaintiffs. Specifically, Hicks’s proposed affirmative defense would require additional discovery that does not relate to the underlying accident.  Plaintiffs may be correct that the proposed amended answer may necessitate additional discovery.  However, trial is more than four months away.  Discovery has not closed.  Plaintiffs do not demonstrate that the additional discovery could not be completed in the remaining time before trial.  Moreover, the Complaint was filed less than two years ago.  If a trial continuance is necessary to complete discovery, the five-year deadline to bring this case to would not yet present an independent basis of prejudice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.310.)  The Court finds the prejudice, if any, that would result from the granting of this motion does not overcome the policy that cases be resolved on their merits.

 

Plaintiffs next contend that Hicks unreasonably delayed in bringing this motion.  Plaintiffs are correct that Hicks does not offer any explanation as to why she did not seek leave to amend any earlier.  Presumably, Hicks should have sought leave to amend in early 2022.  By then, the alleged breach had already occurred and the CSAA decision had already been published.  However, as discussed above, Plaintiffs do not demonstrate prejudice.  “Where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party, the liberal rule of allowance prevails.”  (Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565.)  Absent prejudice, the Court is guided by the public policy to grant leave to amend liberally. 

 

IV.       CONCLUSION

 

            The Court is inclined to grant the Motion for Leave to File the First Amended Answer.  Given the procedural defects noted in this order, the hearing for this motion is CONTINUED to November 13, 2023 at 1:30 AM in Department 27 of Spring Street Courthouse.  Defendant Sabrina Nicole Hicks is to file and serve an amended declaration with the proposed amended answer no later than 5 court days of this order.[2]
