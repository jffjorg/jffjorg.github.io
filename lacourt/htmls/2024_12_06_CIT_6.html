DEPARTMENT 6 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV11677    Hearing Date: December 6, 2024    Dept: 6

CASE NAME:  Nobia Joyce v. Shawn M. Millner, et al. 

Defendants’ Joint Motion to Dismiss Plaintiff’s Entire Action for Fabricating Evidence and for an Order Imposing Monetary Sanctions 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ joint motion to dismiss Plaintiff’s entire action for fabricating evidence and for an order imposing monetary sanctions. 

              Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a quiet title action. On March 20, 2020, plaintiff Nobia Joyce (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants Shawn M. Millner, the Shawn Marie Millner Revocable Trust (collectively, Millner), Laurence Todd (Todd) (Millner and Todd may be collectively referred to as Defendants), and Does 1 through 20, alleging causes of action for quiet title, partition, declaratory relief, and ejectment and damages. 

On November 29, 2022, Defendants filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Stephen R. Golden, Stephen R. Golden & Associates, P.C., and Does 1 through 20. On February 2, 2024, Defendants filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint (SACC) against the same parties, alleging causes of action for breach of the July 5, 2018 Settlement Agreement, breach of the covenant of good faith of the July 5, 2018 Settlement Agreement, fraud (No. 1), breach of the August 2018 Co-signor agreement, breach of the covenant of good faith breach [sic] of the August 2018 Co-tenant [sic] agreement, fraud (No. 2), assault, battery, false imprisonment, negligent infliction of emotional distress, intentional infliction of emotional distress, slander of title, fraud (No. 3), invasion of privacy, unjust enrichment, comparative indemnity, apportionment of fault, breach of the May 2023 Settlement Agreement, and breach of the covenant of good faith of the May 2023 Settlement Agreement. On August 27, 2024, the Court dismissed cross-defendants Stephen R. Golden and Stephen R. Golden & Associates, P.C., from the SACC without prejudice. 

On August 28, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the First and Second Causes of Action for quiet title and partition as to Defendant Todd without prejudice. 

On October 31, 2024, Defendants jointly moved to dismiss Plaintiff’s entire action for fabricating evidence. On November 12, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion. On November 21, 2024, Millner replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

The court is authorized, after notice and an opportunity for hearing, to impose the following sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process: monetary sanctions, issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, terminating sanctions, and contempt. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2023.030, subds. (a)-(e).) A terminating sanction may be imposed by an order dismissing the action of the party that brought the action. (Id., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(3).)   

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination. [Citation.]” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc.¿(2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992 (Doppes); see J.W. v. Watchtower Bible and Tract Society of New York, Inc.¿(2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1142, 1169.) If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted. (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.) “Discovery sanctions ‘should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed what is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.’ [Citation.]” (Id.) “But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Id., quoting Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280; Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar¿(2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702, quoting Doppes and Mileikowsky.)   

The court should consider the totality of the circumstances, including conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful, the determent to the propounding party, and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain discovery. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) Terminating sanctions should not be ordered lightly, but are justified where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and there is evidence that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules. (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.) 

Before any sanctions may be imposed the court must make an express finding that there has been a willful failure of the party to serve the required discovery responses. (Fairfield v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 113, 118.) The party who failed to comply with discovery obligations has the burden of showing that the failure was not willful. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 788; Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250, 252-253.)  

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivisions (d) and (f), provide that a misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery” and “[m]aking an evasive response to discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (d), (f).) 

PRELIMINARY ISSUES 

            The Court notes that Millner’s reply is late. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) The Court will still consider the reply, but admonishes Millner to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure going forward. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (d); Juarez v. Wash Depot Holdings, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1197, 1202.) 

DISCUSSION 

            Summary of Arguments 

Millner moves for terminating sanctions on the grounds that Plaintiff’s counsel submitted fabricated evidence during the bench trial on September 11, 2024. Millner contends the changes made to Plaintiff’s deposition testimony were improper, and that the reading of that testimony into evidence was done to dispose of a fact harmful to Plaintiff, namely that Plaintiff testified no agreement existed between her and Millner. Millner contends Plaintiff’s lawyer violated ethical obligations by having Plaintiff provide false testimony. Millner further contends Plaintiff’s counsel submitted at trial a false subpoena duces tecum that was never issued to Plaintiff’s employer, Parsons Corporation, in an attempt to authenticate banking records that were never received from Parsons Corporation (Parsons). Millner contends no one received any documents from Parsons. Millner also contends Plaintiff has a history of participating in fraudulent schemes aimed at Defendant, such as Stephen R. Golden suing Defendant on behalf of Plaintiff when he was disbarred. Millner contends these actions warrant dismissal of Plaintiff’s complaint and requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $500,000.00. 

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that no evidence was fabricated. Plaintiff contends that the failure to sign and send deposition corrections to the court reporter was inadvertent and that those actions have been corrected. Plaintiff contends the subpoena duces tecum was served on Parsons and that Plaintiff’s counsel did not submit a false declaration for those records. Plaintiff contends Parsons received the subpoena and that Parsons stated it complied with the subpoena by emailing the documents to the Court. Plaintiff then contends the check registers showing the issuance of checks received from Parsons were ultimately not needed because copies of the checks issued by Parsons were admitted into evidence. Plaintiff further contends the lawsuit filed by disbarred attorney Stephen R. Golden was dismissed years ago and is not relevant here. 

Analysis 

The Court finds Millner’s motion unavailing. The Court does not find any nefarious intent in making the changes to Plaintiff’s deposition transcript. The Court read the relevant portion of the deposition transcript and accepts Plaintiff’s counsel’s statement that Plaintiff became tired and disengaged toward the end of the deposition and the parties were supposed to continue the deposition later but failed to do so, thus necessitating the changes to Plaintiff’s deposition transcript. (Pallack Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) The Court also accepts Plaintiff’s counsel’s statement that the failure to have Plaintiff sign the changes to the deposition transcript and serve it on the court reporter was inadvertent. (Pallack Decl., ¶ 4.) Also, the issue was raised during the trial and the Court allowed the statement to be read into the record following Defendant’s belated reading of portions of the deposition transcript into the record. The Court advised the parties that the Court would give the evidence the weight it deemed appropriate. The Court did not rely on this portion of Plaintiff’s evidence in reaching its decision, so the focus on this piece of evidence is ultimately a moot point. 

Moreover, Plaintiff presented sufficient evidence showing that Parsons did in fact receive the subpoena duces tecum and attempted to submit the requested documents to the Court. (Pallack Decl., Exs. 2, 15.) Furthermore, Defendants did not present any evidence supporting their claims  that the subpoena was false and none of the challenged documents were entered into evidence at trial. 

The Court also agrees with Plaintiff that the prior action filed by Stephen R. Golden is irrelevant to the issue of whether Plaintiff and her counsel of record have engaged in conduct warranting terminating sanctions in this action. 

The Court finds that there was no willful violation of any discovery process by Plaintiff or Plaintiff’s counsel. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES the motion. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court DENIES Defendants’ joint motion to dismiss Plaintiff’s entire action for fabricating evidence and for an order imposing monetary sanctions. 

              Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order.

Defendant/Cross-Complainant Shawn M. Millner-Todd’s Motion for New Trial 

TENTATIVE RULING 

The Court summarily DENIES Defendant/Cross-Complainant Shawn M. Millner-Todd’s motion for new trial as premature. 

              Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling within five calendar days of this order. 

BACKGROUND 

This is a quiet title action. On March 20, 2020, plaintiff Nobia Joyce (Plaintiff) filed this action against defendants Shawn M. Millner, the Shawn Marie Millner Revocable Trust (collectively, Millner), Laurence Todd (Todd) (Millner and Todd may be collectively referred to as Defendants), and Does 1 through 20, alleging causes of action for quiet title, partition, declaratory relief, and ejectment and damages. 

On November 29, 2022, Defendants filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Stephen R. Golden, Stephen R. Golden & Associates, P.C., and Does 1 through 20. On February 2, 2024, Defendants filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint (SACC) against the same parties, alleging causes of action for breach of the July 5, 2018 Settlement Agreement, breach of the covenant of good faith of the July 5, 2018 Settlement Agreement, fraud (No. 1), breach of the August 2018 Co-signor agreement, breach of the covenant of good faith breach [sic] of the August 2018 Co-tenant [sic] agreement, fraud (No. 2), assault, battery, false imprisonment, negligent infliction of emotional distress, intentional infliction of emotional distress, slander of title, fraud (No. 3), invasion of privacy, unjust enrichment, comparative indemnity, apportionment of fault, breach of the May 2023 Settlement Agreement, and breach of the covenant of good faith of the May 2023 Settlement Agreement. On August 27, 2024, the Court dismissed cross-defendants Stephen R. Golden and Stephen R. Golden & Associates, P.C., from the SACC without prejudice. 

On August 28, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the First and Second Causes of Action for quiet title and partition as to Defendant Todd without prejudice. 

On October 4, 2024, Millner gave notice of intent to move for a new trial. On October 7, 2024, the Court entered an interlocutory judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Millner on the First and Second Causes of Action for quiet title and partition. On October 14, 2024, Millner moved for a new trial. On October 24, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion. On November 4, 2024, Millner replied. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

The verdict may be vacated and any other decision may be modified or vacated, in whole or in part, and a new or further trial granted on all or part of the issues, on the application of the party aggrieved, for any of the following causes, materially affecting the substantial rights of such party:

1. Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial.

2. Misconduct of the jury; and whenever any one or more of the jurors have been induced to assent to any general or special verdict, or to a finding on any question submitted to them by the court, by a resort to the determination of chance, such misconduct may be proved by the affidavit of any one of the jurors.

3. Accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against.

4. Newly discovered evidence, material for the party making the application, which he could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered and produced at the trial.

5. Excessive or inadequate damages.

6. Insufficiency of the evidence to justify the verdict or other decision, or the verdict or other decision is against law.

7. Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 657.) 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE 

            The Court GRANTS Millner’s request for judicial notice filed on November 14, 2024, but only as to Exhibit A. (West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 803 [court may take judicial notice of recorded deed].) But, the Court takes judicial notice only as to the existence, content, and authenticity of such documents; it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted therein. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal. App. 5th 389, 400.) The Court also admonishes Millner for not providing these documents with the moving papers. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (a).) The Court DENIES the request for judicial notice filed on November 21, 2024, as there are no documents attached to it. 

DISCUSSION

A notice of intention to move for new trial served and filed before the decision has been rendered is premature and void, and the court lacks jurisdiction to consider the motion. (Ruiz v. Ruiz (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 374, 379 (Ruiz.) When some issues are to be tried by the court and others by the jury, a decision is not complete for purposes of a motion for new trial until all issues in the case have been determined. (Cobb v. University of So. California (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 1140, 1143-1144 (Cobb).) If a statement of decision has been waived or has not been requested, there is no decision for purposes of a motion for new trial until the court signs and files the judgment. (Marriage of Hafferkamp (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 789, 793 (Hafferkamp).) 

Millner’s notice of intention to move for new trial is premature. First, not all of the issues in this case have been determined. The non-jury trial held on September 9, 10, and 11, 2024, only determined Plaintiff’s quiet title and partition claims. (Minute Order (9/20/24).) A jury trial on the remaining causes of action is currently scheduled for September 2, 2025. Thus, there is no decision for purposes of a motion for new trial from which Millner can seek a new trial at this point in time. (Cobb, supra, 45 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1143-1144.) 

Second, even if there were no remaining causes of action to be adjudicated, Millner’s motion would still be premature because neither of the parties requested a statement of decision and the Court did not sign and enter the interlocutory judgment until October 7, 2024. (Hafferkamp, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at p. 793; Judgment (10/7/24).) Millner filed and served the notice of intention to move for new trial on October 4, 2024, i.e., three days before entry of the interlocutory judgment. (Notice of Intention to Move for New Trial (10/4/24).) The Court’s September 20, 2024 minute order does not constitute a decision for purposes of a motion for new trial either. (See Hafferkamp, supra, 61 Cal.App.4th at p. 793 [unsigned minute order did not constitute a decision for purposes of a motion for new trial].) 

Accordingly, the Court lacks jurisdiction to consider the motion for new trial. (Ruiz, supra, 104 Cal.App.3d at p. 379.) The Court therefore summarily DENIES the motion as premature. 

However, even if the Court had jurisdiction to consider the motion, the Court finds Millner’s arguments unavailing. First, it is not entirely clear upon which grounds Millner is actually moving for a new trial. While the notice of intent to move for new trial cites six of the seven grounds available in Code of Civil Procedure section 657, nowhere in Millner’s memorandum of points and authorities does she cite the specific statutory grounds upon which her arguments are based. From what the Court can infer, Millner’s arguments are based on either irregularity in the proceedings or insufficiency of the evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 657, subds. (1), (6).) 

Second, the Court does not find that the arguments regarding the changes to the deposition transcript sufficient to support the granting of a new trial. The Court did not give any weight to the portion of Plaintiff’s deposition transcript read into the record or the purported corrections to the transcript in rendering its decision. 

Third, the Court did not need that evidence to support its decision. Millner admitted under oath in open court in a prior case that Plaintiff made the $29,000 downpayment for the subject property, and that the parties agreed Plaintiff could live there as long as it was owned. (Plaintiff’s Trial Exhibits, Ex. 1, 58:15-59:12.) Plaintiff also testified and introduced documentary evidence that she made several improvements to the subject property and paid several utility bills. The Court found that Millner was not credible as she had no reasonable explanation in denying her prior admission and she also denied that Plaintiff paid any money for improvements or utilities at the subject property. This evidence is sufficient to support the Court’s decision and satisfy the clear and convincing evidence standard to rebut the presumption of Evidence Code section 662. (See Evid. Code, § 662.) Additionally, Plaintiff testified as to where the funds came from to make the down payment, and properly laid the foundation for the withdrawal checks from her retirement fund for them to be admitted into evidence. (Bowman Decl., pp. 236-243 of pdf.) 

Fourth, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that Chase Bank and The City of Azusa Development Agency are not indispensable parties. “A person is an indispensable party to litigation if his or her rights must necessarily be affected by the judgment. [Citation.]” (Washington Mut. Bank v. Blechman (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 662, 667, internal quotation marks omitted.) Owners of property are indispensable parties. (See Ranch at the Falls LLC v. O'Neal (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 155, 173.) Adding Plaintiff to the title of the subject property does not affect Chase Bank’s or The City of Azusa Development Agency’s interest as lienholders in the subject property since a lien merely provides them the right to foreclose on the property should the borrower, i.e., Millner, fail to repay the loans secured by their respective deeds of trust. (All. Mortg. Co. v. Rothwell (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1226, 1236.) The Court also agrees with Plaintiff that Millner’s reference to “other potential individuals” as indispensable parties is unavailing given its vagueness and lack of evidentiary support. (See Motion, 17:2-4.) 

Fifth and finally, Millner’s arguments regarding the settlement from LASC Case Number KC067782 are also unavailing. The settlement agreement in that case states that Plaintiff agreed to dismiss that action without prejudice. (Plaintiff’s Trial Exhibits, Ex. 11.) “A dismissal ‘without prejudice’ necessarily means without prejudice to the filing of a new action on the same allegations, so long as it is done within the period of the appropriate statute of limitations. [Citations.]” (Eaton Hydraulics Inc. v. Cont'l Cas. Co. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 966, 974.) The court in LASC Case Number KC067782 denied Millner’s and Todd’s motion to enforce settlement agreement precisely because the parties had agreed only to dismiss the claims without prejudice. (Plaintiff’s Trial Exhibits, Ex. 11.) Thus, the fact that the parties may have entered into a settlement agreement in the prior action did not prevent Plaintiff from asserting these claims again in this action because that settlement agreement only provided for dismissal without prejudice. (Plaintiff’s Trial Exhibits, Ex. 11; Eaton Hydraulics Inc., supra, 132 Cal.App.4th at p. 974.) 

Based on the foregoing, the Court would DENY the motion for new trial on substantive grounds. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court summarily DENIES Defendant/Cross-Complainant Shawn M. Millner-Todd’s motion for new trial as premature.
