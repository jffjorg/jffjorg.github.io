I.            INTRODUCTION

On September 30, 2019, plaintiff Jose R. Rocaleon (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant Harley-Davidson Motor Company Group, LLC (“Harley-Davidson”) (erroneously sued as Harley-Davidson Motor Company, Inc.), TMCHB, Inc. dba Huntington Beach Harley-Davidson (“TMCHB”) (erroneously sued as The Motorcycle Company, LLC), and Precision Performance, LLC dba TNT Cycle Works.

On October 11, 2023, Harley-Davidson and TMCHB (collectively, “Defendants”) filed this motion to seal evidence submitted in support of their motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication. Defendants also filed a redacted version of their motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication.

On January 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a brief opposing this motion to seal.

On January 11, 2024, Defendants filed a reply brief in support of their motion to seal.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

Pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 2.550 the court may seal a record “only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d).)

A “reasoned decision about sealing or unsealing records cannot be made without . . . (1) identifying the specific information claimed to be entitled to such treatment; (2) identifying the nature of the harm threatened by disclosure; and (3) identifying and accounting for countervailing considerations. The burden of presenting information sufficient to accomplish the first two steps is logically placed on the party seeking the sealing of the documents, who is presumptively in the best position to know what disclosures will harm and how.” (H.B. Fuller Co. v. Doe (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 879, 894.)

III.        DISCUSSION

The Court notes at the outset that the documents lodged with the Court were lodged in Department 31, to which this action was previously assigned. Not having received these, this Court is inquiring of Department 31 as to the location of the lodged documents, but, as a matter of practicality, Defendants may want to re-lodge those documents with this Department because without the documents the Court necessarily cannot rule on the motion to seal. That said, the Court has reviewed the motion and has some observations about it below.

Accordingly, the Court will continue the hearing to allow for: (1) the documents to be re-lodged or delivered to this Department, and (2) supplemental briefing to address the issues below.

Defendants state that “[c]ourts should find business information confidential and entitled to protection from public view where the party asserting confidentiality has taken measures to guard the information’s secrecy and where the material contains information valuable to the business or its competitors.” (Motion, p. 4.) However, Defendants’ motion does not contain any evidence that they have taken measure to guard the secrecy of the materials which they seek to have sealed. Instead, defense counsel only states that he has “reviewed documents provided to me by Harley-Davidson related to the design and manufacture of Harley-Davidson motorcycles related to the above captioned case and [has] determined that Harley-Davidson treats such documents as confidential.” That determination is one left to the Court, not counsel. It is also unclear how counsel would have sufficient personal knowledge to attest to his assertion that “the documents are not publicly disclosed and would damage Harley-Davidson if released to their competitors.” In total, the evidence presented to the Court, which solely consists of defense counsel’s declaration, is insufficient for the Court to find an overriding interest which supports sealing.

Accordingly, the hearing is CONTINUED to [February 9, 2024], so that Defendants may file a supplemental brief (not to exceed 5 pages) and additional evidence further substantiating their claims that the information at issue in this motion should be sealed, such as a declaration from someone with personal knowledge of how the purportedly confidential information is actually kept confidential and the prejudicial effect, if any, that disclosure would cause. Defendants are also ORDERED to file another notice of lodging and lodge another copy of the unredacted documents with the Court.

Defendants are also ORDERED to file in the record an amended notice of motion which lists each document or exhibit, page, and, if applicable, the line numbers with the information that Defendants wish to have sealed.

All documents should be lodged/filed no later than 5 days before the hearing date. 

IV.         CONCLUSION

The hearing is continued to [February 9, 2024], at 8:30 a.m. in Department 3 of the Alhambra Courthouse.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEMURRER OF DEFENDANTS AGNES SINCLAIR, AMERICAN FRANCHISE REGIONAL CENTER, LLC, AND AMERICANA HESPERIA RETIREMENT FUNDING LLC

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

January 19, 2024

 

I.       INTRODUCTION

          On July 14, 2023, plaintiff Zixuan An (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Agnes Sinclair aka Agnes Yen (“Yen”), American Franchise Regional Center LLC (“American Franchise”), and Americana Hesperia Retirement Funding LLC (“Americana Hesperia”) (collectively, “Defendants”). Plaintiff also named four other defendants who have not yet been served: Sylvia Young (“Young”), John Deyong Hu (“Hu”), Hu & Associates, LLC (“Hu Associates”), and Universo Holdings, Inc. (“Universo”)

          Plaintiff asserts causes of action for breach of contract, rescission, fraud, intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, conspiracy to commit fraud, conversion, breach of fiduciary duties, constructive fraud, and violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 et seq. (“UCL”) against American Franchise and Americana Hesperia. Plaintiff asserts the same causes of action (except for breach of contract and rescission) against Yen.

          On October 6, 2023, Defendants filed this demurrer on the grounds that Plaintiff’s action was barred by the statute of limitations and Plaintiff failed to state sufficient facts.

II.      LEGAL STANDARDS

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face.  (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.) “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed. [Citation.]” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007; Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

III.     REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Defendants request judicial notice of a complaint filed in another action captioned Weibeing Fei, et al. v. American Franchise Regional Center, LLC, et al. (Case No. 20PSCV00207), which was filed on March 12, 2020, and includes identical allegations regarding Defendants and Hesperia. The request for judicial notice is DENIED as the Complaint is irrelevant. Plaintiff is not included as a party in the attached complaint; the only plaintiffs are Weibig Fei and Wan “Wendy” Tao. 

IV.     DISCUSSION

A.   Factual Allegations

Plaintiff alleges that he invested approximately $500,000 with American Franchise and his funds were to be pooled with other investors to create a senior care facility (the “Project”), built and operated by Americana Hesperia, as part of the EB-5 Regional Center Pilot Program. Plaintiff claims that in or about July 28, 2015, Hu held several seminars in various cities in China about Americana Hesperia to promote the Project to Chinese investors. (Compl., ¶ 11.) Hu represented that the Project had been reviewed and approved by the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services (“USCIS”) and any Chinese investors would be qualified to apply for EB-5 immigration visas. (Compl., ¶ 11.) Hu and Yen represented that the Project would have minimal risk because if the Project failed, the investors could recoup their investment by selling the land, which was purportedly worth $22 million. (Compl., ¶ 12.) Hu and Yen also introduced the supposed mayor of Hesperia, “Mr. Ted”, to make speeches which were translated by a Chinese interpreter. (Compl., ¶ 12.) Based on a Power Point presentation, the Project was supposed to begin on or about April 1, 2017, and be completed by May 30, 2019, with a “grand opening event” by January 1, 2020.

On or around August 8, 2016, Plaintiff signed an agreement with American Franchise for one membership unit of Americana Hesperia and paid $500,000 by check to American Franchise. (Compl., ¶ 18.)

On or about July 28, 2017, Yen sent an email to all EB-5 investors advising them that the Project had been delayed and that further updates would be provided. (Compl., ¶ 20.) However, no updated project timeline was ever provided. (Compl., ¶ 20.)

B.   Statute of Limitations

As an initial ground for their demurrer, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s entire action is barred by the statute of limitations. The Court agrees.

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s contract claims are barred by the statute of limitations because his causes of action began accruing more than four years before the filing of this complaint. Defendants argue that Plaintiff was informed on July 28, 2017, that construction permitting would be delayed, which placed him on notice that the original timeline for construction would not be honored. The statute of limitations for recission of contract and breach of contract is four years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 337.) Therefore, Defendants argue, Plaintiff’s contract-based claims should have been filed by July 28, 2021.

The Court notes that Defendants did not include the tolling period from April 6, 2020, through October 30, 2020, implemented in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. (CRC emergency Rule 9.) But, even after factoring in the extra 207 days this tolling period provides, Plaintiff’s contract-based claims are still barred because they should have been brought by February 20, 2022.

As for Plaintiff’s claims based on misrepresentation, Defendants also point to Plaintiff’s allegations that in or about the end of 2018 and 2019, he discovered Defendants’ misrepresentations in 2016 about the ownership and value of the land. (Compl., ¶ 21.) Defendants additionally refer to Plaintiff’s allegation that the Project was due to be completed by June 2019 and contend that any reasonable person would have investigated and pursued available claims when no work had commenced by the date it was set to be completed. (Compl., ¶ 22.) The statute of limitations for negligent misrepresentation is 2 years and the statute of limitations for fraud is 3 years. Therefore, based on a generous interpretation of Plaintiff’s allegations and the lack of any opposition brief from Plaintiff arguing to the contrary, assuming Plaintiff’s causes of action began accruing no later than June 1, 2019, Plaintiff’s causes of action for negligent misrepresentation and fraud should have been brought no later than December 27, 2021, and December 26, 2022, respectively.

Likewise, the analysis above applies to Plaintiff’s claims for conversion, breach of fiduciary duty, and violation of the UCL. The statute of limitations for conversion and the statute of limitations for a claim under the UCL is 4 years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338; Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17208.) The statute of limitations for breach of fiduciary duty depends on the underlying breach which, here, are the alleged misrepresentations, and thus subject to either a 2-year or 3-year statute of limitations. (Compl., ¶¶ 93-94;

Last, Plaintiff’s accounting claim fails as a remedy because there is no underlying theory of liability. (Glue-Fold, Inc. v. Slautterback Corp. (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023, n.3. An action for an accounting “is barred when it appears that the statute [of limitations] has run against the basic cause of action to which the accounting is a mere incident.” (Phillis v. City of Santa Barbara (1964) 229 Cal.App.2d 45, 68 [citing Jefferson v. J.E. French Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 717, 719].)

IV.     CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ unopposed demurrer to the entire Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On November 8, 2023, plaintiff South Goodyear Investment Corporation (“Plaintiff”) filed this unlawful detainer action against defendant Karen Brandt dba Lulu Brandt (“Defendant”) in connection with the premises located at 1 West California Boulevard, Suite #412 in Pasadena, California. On December 1, 2023, Defendant filed an answer.

On December 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for an order advancing the hearing date on a motion to compel arbitration that Defendant served (but did not file) or, in the alternative, deny the motion. The motion was not on file with the Court, but a hearing was reserved for May 20, 2024.

On December 20, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application and advanced the hearing from May 20, 2024 to January 5, 2024, with any opposition brief to be filed before January 3, 2024. The Court ordered the motion filed forthwith.

Defendant did not file the motion to compel arbitration until January 4, 2024. On the same day, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief to the motion to compel arbitration. Because the motion was filed late, on January 5, 2024, the hearing was continued to January 19, 2024.   

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

In deciding a motion to compel arbitration, trial courts must decide first whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then determine the second gateway issue whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) The party seeking arbitration has the “burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842.) The trial court “sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.” (Id.) General principles of contract law govern whether parties have entered a binding agreement to arbitrate. (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236; see also Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)¿

III.        DISCUSSION

The operative lease includes an arbitration agreement as an addendum, but notably explicitly excludes several disputes from the scope of arbitration, including, “[c]laims relating to (a) [Plaintiff]’s exercise of any unlawful detainer rights pursuant to applicable law or (b) rights or remedies used by [Plaintiff] to gain possession of the Premises or terminate [Defendant]’s right of possession to the Premises, all of which disputes shall be resolved by suit filed in the applicable court of jurisdiction, the decision of which court shall be subject to appeal pursuant to applicable law.” (Lease, p. 17  

Defendant argues that this unlawful detainer action is subject to arbitration because the complaint seeks damages. Defendant argues that possession cannot be at issue because she is not in possession of the property, but it is Brandt & Winston Inc. that is in possession, and, therefore, the claim for damages must be arbitrated.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that this action is specifically excluded from the scope of the arbitration agreement because Plaintiff is exercising its unlawful detainer rights by seeking possession and a monetary judgment for failure to pay rent.

By arguing that possession is not at issue, Defendant is challenging the merits of the unlawful detainer action. However, not only does Defendant fail to submit any evidence to support this assertion, but the merits of the unlawful detainer are irrelevant to this motion, which only examines whether this complaint presents a dispute which is arbitrable. The dispute, regardless of whether it has merit, is an unlawful detainer action. (See Briggs v. Electronic Memories & Magnetics Corp. (1975) 53 Cal.App.3d 900, 906 [an unlawful detainer will not lie against a defendant who is not in possession of the premises at commencement of the lawsuit].) Moreover, Plaintiff’s request for damages does not bring this unlawful detainer action within the scope of the arbitration agreement because the unlawful detainer statute allows for the assessment of damages, including the amount of rent due “if the alleged unlawful detainer be after default in the payment of rent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1174, subd. (b).) Also, any attorneys’ fees incurred with this unlawful detainer action would relate to Plaintiff’s exercise of its unlawful detainer rights and therefore, is not subject to arbitration.

Accordingly, the Court finds that this unlawful detainer action falls outside the scope of the arbitration agreement.  

IV.         CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is DENIED.
