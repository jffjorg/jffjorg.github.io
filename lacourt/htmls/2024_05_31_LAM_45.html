DEPARTMENT 45 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All rulings shown here are TENTATIVE ONLY, and thus oral argument WILL be heard. All Counsel are still required to attend.

Case Number: 21STCV03986    Hearing Date: May 31, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

PEOPLE OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA ex rel. FRANK J. CANNATA,

 

                             Relator,

 

                              vs.

LUCKY’S TWO-WAY RADIOS, INC., a Nevada Corporation; BUDDY CORPORATION, a California Corporation; JAMES A. KAY, JR., an individual; DOES 1 through 20, Inclusive,

 

                              Defendants.
Case No.:  21STCV03986

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed: 02/01/2021

[1st Amended Complaint Filed: 02/09/2023]

Trial Date: 09/03/2024

 

Hearing date:              05/31/2024

Moving Party:             Defendants Lucky’s Two-Way Radios, Inc.; Buddy Corporation;                                        and James A Kay Jr.

Responding Party:      N/A - Unopposed

The Court considered the moving papers. Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED. The Court requests that Defense Counsel provide calculations for the requested sanctions prior to the Court imposing sanctions on Relator.

 

        I.            Background

Factual Background

            This case stems from accusations of insurance fraud. On February 1, 2021, Frank J. Cannata (Relator) filed a Complaint against three defendants: (1) Lucky’s Two-Way Radios, Inc.; (2) Buddy Corporation; (3) and James A. Kay Jr (collectively Defendants). The Complaint alleges that Defendants violated the California Insurance Frauds Prevention Act (IFPA or INS § 1871.7). On February 9, 2023, Relator filed the operative First Amended Complaint (FAC). The violations are grounded in Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendants owned several pieces of defunct electronic equipment. (FAC, pgs. 5-7.) The Defendants allege that this equipment was stolen by Relator, this allegation in and of itself is the basis for a separate case, Lucky’s Two-Way Radios, Inc., et al. vs. Frank J. Cannata, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. LC106864 (hereinafter, Case No. LC106864). (See Defendants’ Request for Judicial Notice, Exh. 1.) Per Plaintiff, after Defendants reported the theft, they claimed that the value of the loss was $881,190.43. Plaintiff alleges that this value is grossly inflated and constitutes fraud upon Defendants’ insurer Hartford Fire Insurance Company, as the fair market value of the missing electronics is less than $20,000.00. (FAC, ¶ 29.) Relator then filed suit.

            The motion before the Court is Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories (the Motion). No opposition has been filed.

    II.            Discussion

Legal Standard

            The propounding party may bring motions to compel further responses to interrogatories or requests for production if it believes (1) the responses received are evasive, or (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or too general. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a).) A respondent has the burden to justify objections in response to a motion filed to compel further responses. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) The Court may impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c).)

Analysis

            Defendants contend that after propounding Form Interrogatories to Relator, the responses received back were evasive and that when supplemental responses were promised by Relator they were never received. The Court agrees and grants the Motion.

            Defendants propounded their initial discovery on December 19, 2023. Responses were received on January 29, 2024 which contained objections and promises that substantive responses would follow. (Declaration of Timothy D. McGonigle, hereinafter, McGonigle Decl., ¶3.) As of the filing of the instant Motion, no substantive responses were received. Attempts to meet and confer by Defendants proved futile. (Id. at ¶¶4-5.)

            There are 15 form interrogatories in dispute. Defendants correctly point out that the Form Interrogatories propounded were authored by the California Judicial Council. Objections to these interrogatories, like all objections, must be substantiated by the objecting party, however, as no opposition has been filed, Relator has failed to do so.           Although the majority of the responses state that “Supplemental responses will be furnished shortly” (See Separate Statement ISO Defendant’s Motion), no responses were furnished.

            The responses that constitute objections are meritless. For example, Relator objected to Form Interrogatory No. 2.5 which requests that the Relator state their present address and addresses over the past five years. The objection is based on privacy rights but has no grounds. This is a routine interrogatory that must be answered. Similarly, the objection to Form Interrogatory No. 2.11 contends that the term “Incident” is overbroad. Relator filed this action and is surely aware of the subject incident of this suit, moreover, this could have been resolved upon Defendants’ attempts to meet and confer. Finally, the objections that claim the Form Interrogatories are overbroad, oppressive, or unintelligible are unpersuasive. As noted above these interrogatories were authored and approved by the California Judicial Council. (See  Cal. Prac. Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial, The Rutter Group 2023.) Accordingly, the Motion is granted and sanctions are warranted.

¶8:933.

Sanctions

            Defense counsel requests $4,500 in attorney’s fees and costs for bringing the instant Motion, however, Defense counsel provides no calculations for how they arrived at this figure.

 

  III.            Conclusion

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED. The Court requests that Defense Counsel provide calculations for the requested sanctions prior to the Court imposing sanctions on Relator.

 

 

            It is so ordered.

KRISTINE ROSE J. ROBLES, et al.;

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed:  09/02/21

Trial Date:  TBD

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             May 31, 2024

Moving Party:             Defendant Derek Keane

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Lafayette Apt. Associates, L.P.

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

 

            The court has considered the moving papers, opposition papers, supplemental opposition, reply papers, and second supplemental opposition papers.

            The court DENIES Defendant Derek Keane’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

Background

Plaintiff Lafayette Apt. Associates, L.P. (hereinafter “Plaintiff” or “Lafayette”) filed this action on September 2, 2021 against Kristine Rose J. Robles, Kristian Apple J. Robles, Roselle Poblete, and Derek Keane (“Keane”) (collectively, “Defendants”), alleging the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract; and (2) account stated. The complaint alleges that the parties entered into a lease agreement in May 2019 for the premises located at 401 S. Lafayette Park Place, Unit 206, Los Angeles, CA 90057. (Compl. at pg. 3; Exh. 1.) Defendants allegedly breached the agreement on May 1, 2020 by failing to pay rent. (Id. at pg. 3, ¶ BC-2.) Defendants allegedly owe to date a total of $39,802.47 in past due rent. (Id. at ¶ BC-4.)

On December 6, 2022, Keane filed a Cross-Complaint on December 6, 2022 against Lafayette and his co-defendants, alleging the following causes of action: (1) Retaliatory Eviction; and (2) Contribution. The Cross-Complaint alleges Lafayette unlawfully evicted Keane, who is alleged to be a third-party beneficiary residential occupant of the subject premises, based on his inability to pay under the then-applicable Civil Code § 1942.5(d). (Cross-Compl. ¶ 9.) Keane alleges the eviction was wrongful since the three-day notice overstated the amount that was actually due. (Id. at ¶ 10.) The eviction was also wrongful because the occupants were protected by Civil Code § 1511, as their ability to pay rent starting in March 2020 was attributable to governmental regulations. (Id. at ¶ 11.) Further, Keane alleges the eviction violated the City and County Covid eviction moratoria, the Tenant Relief Act of 2020 and the Tenant Protection Act of 2019. (Id. at ¶ 12.)

On February 6, 2023, Keane filed the instant motion for summary judgment directed at Plaintiff’s complaint.[1]

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition to the instant motion. Thereafter, on April 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed its untimely supplemental opposition papers.

On April 17, 2024, Keane filed his reply.

On April 25, 2024, the court continued the hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(h) to May 31, 2024 in order to accommodate the taking of Keane’s deposition. The court instructed Plaintiff to file its supplemental opposition papers on or before May 17, 2024. Keane was permitted to file a supplemental reply on or before May 27, 2014.

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed its second supplemental opposition papers.

As of May 28, 2024, no supplemental reply has been received.

 

Legal Standard

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing motions for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent's claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment must be denied where the moving party's evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition (Leyva v. Sup. Ct. (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475) or where the opposition is weak (Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384, 387).

 

Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff objects to various portions of Keane’s declaration. The court rules as follows. Objection No. 1 is sustained in part as to the phrase “left as the additional adult occupant of the premises” on the ground that it is an improper legal conclusion. Objection Nos. 2-3 are sustained on the ground that the cited declaration statements are improper legal argument.

            April 17, 2024, Keane objected to various portions of the evidence submitted by Plaintiff in support of its opposition.  The court overrules these objections in their entirety.

 

Judicial Notice

            Keane requests the court to take judicial notice of the Los Angeles Rent Stabilizations Ordinance of 1979. The request is granted pursuant to Evidence Code § 452(c).

 

Discussion

A.    Procedural Issues

Plaintiff first argues that the instant motion for summary judgment is moot based on the filing of the First Amended Complaint, which had been submitted on December 8, 2023. (See Second Suppl. Opposition at pg. 10, relying on (State Compensation Ins. Fund v. Superior Court (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1124, 1131.) This argument presumes that the First Amended Complaint was properly filed. Rather, it was filed without leave of court. Presently, a motion for leave to amend is set to be heard on June 16, 2024. Until a decision is issued regarding that motion, the initial complaint remains the operative pleadings in this action because it has not been superseded by an amended complaint.

Second, Plaintiff argues that the instant motion should be denied pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(i) because of Keane’s failure to answer questions asked of him during his deposition on May 1, 2024. (See Second Suppl. Opposition at pp. 10-18.)

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(i), where a continuance has been afforded pursuant to Section 437c(h) and it has been determined that the party seeking summary judgment has unreasonably failed to allow the discovery to be conducted, then the court may either continue the motion to allow for the discovery to be conducted or to deny the motion.

Previously, the court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to compel Keane’s second deposition. (See March 19, 2024 Minute Order.) When the instant motion first came for a hearing, it was discovered that Keane has been uncooperative by refusing to participate in his deposition. (Suppl. Opposition filed on April 16, 2024 at pg. 4; Williamson Suppl. Decl. ¶¶ 5-7.) For this reason, the court continued the hearing on the instant motion due to Plaintiff’s continuing need to complete Keane’s deposition. (See April 25, 2024 Minute Order.)

On May 1, 2024, the parties proceeded with Keane’s third deposition, but as represented by Plaintiff, Keane continued to be uncooperative by either giving evasive responses or not responding to questions at all. (Second Suppl. Opposition at pg. 12.) For instance, Keane evaded either answering whether he was a guest at the subject property or providing an explanation for his arrangement with the co-defendant to live in the subject property. (Second Suppl. Opposition at pp. 14-16; Williamson Suppl. Decl. ¶ 15, Exh. 16 at pp. 30:12-33:21, 35:5-41:6.)

Also, throughout the deposition, Keane refused to answer many questions posed by Plaintiff’s counsel due to relevance. This is improper. (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1014 [“irrelevance alone is an insufficient ground to justify preventing a witness from answering a question posed at a deposition.”].) Upon review of the deposition transcript, it did not appear that Keane was asked to answer any questions that implicated a privilege, privacy, or legal contention. (Id. at 1014-15; Rifkind v. Superior Court (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 1255, 1259 [questions of legal contention seek answers that “state all facts, list all witnesses and identify all documents that support or pertain to a particular contention in that party’s pleadings” and are reserved for interrogatories].)

Under these circumstances, the Court finds that Keane has “unreasonably failed to allow the discovery to be conducted,” and based on his repeated refusal to answer deposition questions and evasive answers, a second continuance would be futile without an order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.480. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(i).) Thus, on this ground, the motion is properly denied.

B.     Merits

Even assuming that the motion should not be denied under Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(i), the court finds that there are triable issues of material fact that Keane was a party to the lease agreement.

Here, Keane moves for summary adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of contract and the second cause of action for account stated on the ground that he was not a party to lease and that there were no prior transactions between the parties. (Motion at pp. 2-5.) In support of this argument, Keane relies on the lease agreement and the rental application for the subject property. (Motion, Keane Decl., Exhs. A-B.) It is evident that Keane’s signature is missing from the lease agreement. On this basis, the court finds that Keane has met his burden in showing that there are no triable issues of material fact that he was a tenant of the subject property. Thus, the burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show that there is a triable issue of material fact.

In opposition, Plaintiff relies on the following evidence. Within the deposition of Roselie Poblete, she attests that Keane was only allowed to move into the subject property after a signing a lease contract together with the other co-defendants. (Second Suppl. Opposition, Exh. 4, Poblete Depo. at pp. 11:19-12:6, 15:15-16:11.) This evidence creates the inference that Keane was tenant under the lease agreement. Furthermore, based on the correspondence Keane had sent to Plaintiff’s representative on May 26, 2020, it appears that Keane subleased the subject property from the co-defendants, and he was approved to move in based on his rental application and Defendant Roselie Poblete representation that she rental payments would come from her. (Second Suppl. Opposition at pg. 20, Exh. 9.) This further strengthens the inference that Keane had permission to occupy the subject property. Because Keane has refused to cooperate during his various depositions, there remains a triable issue of material fact as to whether Keane was a tenant or a sublessee during his occupancy of the subject property.  Nevertheless, because this evidence shows that Keane had a right to occupy the subject property but continued to remain in possession without Plaintiff’s consent after May 27. 2020, there is a triable issue of fact as to whether a holdover tenancy was created, which lasted for ten months. (Id. at pp. 22-23, Exhs. 4 Poblete Depo at pp. 28:5-10, 31:16-18; Exhs. 9, 10, 12, 14, 15.)

Accordingly, there are several triable issues of fact regarding the circumstances of Keane’s occupancy of the subject property that preclude summary judgment.

 

Based on the foregoing, the court denies Keane’s motion for summary judgment

 

It is so ordered.

            The court has considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

            The court GRANTS plaintiff CR&R Incorporated’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of defendant Robert O’Neill.

            Defendant Robert O’Neil’s deposition shall take place on _________________________

at _______ am/pm, at ________________________________________________.

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant and his counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $2,187.50. The court orders Defendant and his counsel of record to pay this amount within 20 days of the date of this motion to Plaintiff’s counsel.

 

 

Background

Plaintiff CR&R, Incorporated filed this action on March 2, 2022 against defendant Robert O’Neill, individually and dba Simple Dumpsters, alleging causes of action for (1) Common Count for Account Stated; (2) Common Count for Open Book Account; (3) Common Count for Services Rendered; and (4) Quantum Meruit. The Complaint alleges the following. On February 1, 2019, Plaintiff entered into an Asset Purchase Agreement, acquiring the assets of United Pacific Waste (UPW). (Compl. ¶ 7.) UPW previously entered into a contract to prove waste disposal services to Defendant. (Id.) Under the terms of the Asset Purchase Agreement, Plaintiff to agreed to continue proving waste disposal services to Defendant. (Id. at ¶ 8.) Defendant paid for the services rendered but stopped paying on June 15, 2020, resulting in an unpaid outstanding balance due to Plaintiff. (Id. at ¶¶ 9-12.)

On April 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel Defendant’s deposition.

On May 17, 2024, Defendant filed his opposition papers.

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed its reply.

 

Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a) states: “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”

The motion must be accompanied by a declaration showing a reasonable and good faith attempt to informally resolve each issue presented or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the requested documents or things, the motion must be accompanied by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).)

 

Discussion

            Meet and Confer

            Based on the declaration submitted by Defendant’s counsel, the Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently met and conferred in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(b)(2).) (Beam Decl. ¶¶ 9-11, Exhs. 11-16.)

 

Merit of the Motion

            Here, Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant’s deposition. After extensive back and forth, the parties eventually agreed to schedule Defendant’s deposition for January 11, 2024, but neither Defendant nor his counsel appeared for the scheduled deposition. (Beam Decl. ¶¶ 7-8, Exh. 6-10.) When Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to inquire as to Defendant’s failure to appear, his counsel did not provide a specific reason. (Beam Decl. ¶ 9, Exh. 11.)

            In opposition, Defendant argues that the motion is premature and does not comply with Section 2025.450 because his deposition had been re-noticed on April 23, 2024 for May 21, 2024 and the instant motion was filed on April 30, 2024. (Opposition at pp. 2-3.) These arguments are not persuasive because it overlooks the fact that Defendant failed to appear for his properly scheduled January 11, 2024 deposition hearing. Thus, the instant motion is procedurally proper, notwithstanding the fact that Plaintiff attempted to re-notice the deposition. Therefore, under the circumstances, the court finds that there is ground to compel Defendant’s deposition based on his failure to appear to his January 11, 2024 deposition hearing.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s deposition. Defendant’s deposition shall take place on the date, time, and location set forth above.

 

Request for Monetary Sanctions

            Plaintiff requests $5,015 in monetary sanctions against Defendant and his counsel of record, jointly and severally. Plaintiff’s counsels’ hourly rates are $350 for Counsel Gregory Beam and $300 for Counsel Paul Breen. (Beam Decl. ¶ 12.) Counsel Beam attests having spent 3.1 hours on the instant motion and that Counsel Breen has spent 5.9 hours on the instant motion. (Ibid.) Counsel Beam further anticipates an additional 6 hours in reviewing the opposition, preparing the reply, and attending the hearing. (Ibid.)

            Under the circumstances, the court finds that sanctions are warranted under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(g)(1) because Defendant has not shown that the imposition of sanctions would be unjust or that he acted with substantial justification. While the hourly rates requested are reasonable, the amount in sanctions is not because the instant motion concerns an uncomplicated discovery issue that does not justify the amounts sought. However, the court recognizes the substantial delay and costs Defendant created in failing to cooperate in discovery without sufficient justification. The court finds that reducing Plaintiff’s time spent on the motion in half is reasonable. As for Plaintiff’s anticipated hours, based on the limited scope of the opposition and the likelihood that appearances will be conducted remotely, the court reduces the anticipated hours from six to two. Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant and his attorney of record, jointly and severally, reduced to $2,187.50, inclusive of the $60 filed fee.

            Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant and his counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $2,187.50. The court orders Defendant and his counsel of record to pay this amount within 20 days of the date of this motion to Plaintiff’s counsel.

It is so ordered.
