Case Number: 21STCV18145    Hearing Date: August 6, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 August 6, 2023

¿¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   21STCV18145

¿¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        David Zeil v. DOES 1-100

¿¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff, David Zeil

¿¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Defendant, Southwest Airlines Co.

 

TRIAL DATE:                        September 22, 2025

¿¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for Order Compelling Defendant to Serve Further Responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1

                                                (2) Request for Monetary Sanctions 

                                                (3) Motion for Summary Judgment

                                               

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) As to Responses to FROG No. 15.1, MOOTED by recent 4th supplemental responses served while this motion was pending

                                                (2) Request for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED in the lowered amount of $2,068.

                                                (3) DISCUSS.  The Hearing on MSJ will be CONTINUED, unless Southwest can convince the Court that there is no set of facts covered by pending responses to Plaintiff’s discovery that would require a trial as to the negligence cause of action, or unless Plaintiff can convince the Court that Southwest has failed to carry its initial burden on summary judgment because the witness statements supplied as the moving party’s evidence is hearsay not within any exception.  The Court GRANTS summary adjudication on the product liability cause of action as against Southwest

 

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

On May 14, 2021, Plaintiff, David Zeil (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants DOES 1 through 100. Plaintiff eventually learned of the identity of more Defendants including Southwest Airlines Co, and Ms. Manu. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Products Liability; and (2) General Negligence.

 

i.                 Facts relating to the Motion to Compel Further Responses

 

On August 1, 2022, Plaintiff notes that it served Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, Admission Requests and Inspection Demands on Defendant. On August 31, 2022, Plaintiff notes that Defendant served responses to the discovery, but that they contained multiple improper objections, failed to provide substantive responses to most of the discovery and in general were not good faith responses.

 

On September 14, 2022, Plaintiff notes it sent a detailed meet and confer letter to defense counsel regarding the discovery responses, setting forth why further responses were required including Form Interrogatory No. 15.1. On September 21, 2022, Plaintiff notes defense counsel wrote back stating Defendant would not provide further responses.

 

On April 14, 2023, Plaintiff notes that he and Defendant attended a hearing of Plaintiff’s motion to compel. The Plaintiff contends that this Court granted Plaintiff’s motion, compelling further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1. A status conference was subsequently set for June 6, 2023.

 

On May 10, 2023, Plaintiff notes that Defendant Southwest produced further responses to Plaintiff under the court order. On May 25, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel again sent Defendant a meet and confer letter. On June 15, 2023, Plaintiff notes that Defense counsel responded stating Defendant intended to stand on its response and would not provide further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1 or the other discovery. After a phone call on June 26, 2023, Plaintiff contends that on June 27, 2023, Defendant provided further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1. However, Plaintiff contends that these Reponses provided no new information and on June 30, 2023, his counsel wrote another detailed meet and confer letter. On July 6, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel notes that he spoke with Mr. Kaskiw about Form Interrogatory No. 15.1. Plaintiff suggests that Defense counsel suggested that Plaintiff take depositions, and subsequently agreed to provide follow-up responses that complied with the requirements of the interrogatory as to each of Defendant’s defenses and affirmative defenses.

 

When no progress was made, Plaintiff contends that he filed a motion for terminating sanctions which was heard on December 12, 2023. However, on December 11, 2023, one day prior to that hearing, Plaintiff notes that Southwest provided supplemental responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1, as well as other discovery. Thus, this Court mooted the then-pending motion as further responses were submitted.

 

On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff notes that Defendant wrote a meet and confer letter, which Plaintiff responded to on January 11, 2024. Subsequently, on April 26, 2024, Defendant provided further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1. On April 20, 2024, Plaintiff notes its counsel wrote another meet and confer letter concerning those responses. Since then, Plaintiff notes that he has not received any further responses and Defendant has maintained its responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1 are sufficient. But then Southwest served fourth supplemental responses on  July 3, 2024.

 

 

As such, the discovery motion as to Form Interrogatory 15.1 is mooted, but the sanctions issue is not.

 

ii.               Facts relation to the Motion for Summary Judgment

 

Defendant Southwest moves for summary judgment on the grounds it argues: (1) there is no triable issue of facts as to Plaintiff’s claims because the Federal Aviation Act of 1958 preempts state standards of care regarding aviation safety, and thus Plaintiff cannot show Southwest violated a Federal Aviation Regulation; (2) There is no triable issue of fact as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for products liability because Southwest did not manufacture, assemble, design, or sell the subject product; and (3) There is no triable issue of material fact as to Plaintiff’s second cause of action for general negligence asserted against Southwest as Southwest did not have actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition.

 

B. Procedural¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed its Motions to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1. On July 3, 2024, Southwest filed an opposition brief. On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief.

 

On May 8, 2024, Southwest filed a Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication. On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On July 24, 2024, Southwest filed a reply brief.

 

II. ANALYSIS¿¿ 

 

A.    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1

 

 

The motion is MOOTED by the service of fourth supplemental responses on  July 3, 2024, while this motion to compel was pending.

 

                                      i.          Sanctions

 

Although the substantive motion was MOOTED by the filing of Southwest’s fourth supplemental responses, this does not per se moot the request for monetary sanctions. Plaintiff is requesting monetary sanctions be imposed against Defendants and/or their counsel in the amount of $4,468.40. This total amount is based on Plaintiff’s counsel’s indication that he spent two (2) hours on meet and confer efforts; two (2) hours preparing the separate statement; two (2) hours drafting the motion, and will spend at least two (2) hours reviewing any oppositions, preparing a reply brief, and participating in the hearing. Additionally, counsel notes his hourly rate is $550 and that he spent $68.40 in filing fees. The Court finds this total amount to be excessive, especially because this is not the first time this interrogatory has been at issue in this case and much of the background and history that Plaintiff has presented both on the discovery motion and in the MSJ’s separate statement is duplicative. As such, this Court awards monetary sanctions in the lowered amount of $2,068 to be paid by Defendant Southwest and/or its counsel on or before August 20, 2024.  

 

B.    Southwest’s Motion for Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication

 

                                      i.          Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (h)

 

Prior to ruling on the merits of the motion, the Court is inclined to CONTINUE the motion for numerous reasons.

 

The Court first addresses Plaintiff’s opposition argument that this motion should be continued pursuant to Code of Civil procedure section 437c, subdivision (h), which states: “[i]f it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just. The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (h).) “Generally, power to determine when a continuance should be granted is within the discretion of the court, and there is no right to a continuance as a matter of law.” (American Continental Ins. Co. v. C & Z Timber Co. (1987) 195 Cal.App.3d 1271, 1280)

 

Specifically, Plaintiff’s opposition asserts that it believes facts essential to justify his opposition to the motion exist, but cannot be presented at this time. Plaintiff’s counsel, Neil S. Steiner, prepared a declaration (“Steiner Decl.”), indicating that Plaintiff has been unable to obtain evidence essential to opposing Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment and thus, asks this Court to deny or continue this motion. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 88.) Plaintiff notes that it as been unable to obtain information on Defendant’s defenses and affirmative defenses, including its defense of preemption. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 85.) Plaintiff contends that since October 3, 2022, he has filed no less than twelve (12) discovery motions against Defendant, including two terminating sanctions motions, to attempt to obtain this necessary information. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 85.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel beleives that relevant information exists, but cannot yet be presented to this Court is demonstrated by the reports of the flight crew as attached to the Steiner Declaration as Exhibits “G” and “H”. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 86.) However, Plaintiff argues that these statements are hearsay and not falling within any recognized exception including the exception for business records.   (Steiner Decl., ¶ 86.) Moreover, Plaintiff further notes that Defendant refuses to agree to dates for their depositions or for the PMQ, and will not produce the statements of Ms. Bird. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 86.) Plaintiff indicates that he currently has four additional discovery motions pending. (Steiner Decl., ¶ 73.) Further, this Court notes that after the filing of this motion, Defendant Southwest produced further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 15.1, another subject of today’s hearing. Based on these combined issues, the Court finds good cause to continue the motion.

 

As frustrated as Plaintiff’s counsel may be with the claimed stonewalling and continual delays in providing discovery by Southwest, the Court will be patient to hear both sides’ assertions.  Many a defendant has sought to delay responding to discovery because of a righteous belief that it has an insuperable legal defense to the lawsuit.  But California law allows a plaintiff a right to discovery and to delay the hearing of a dispositive motion upon a representation of counsel that facts exist that might show a triable issue of fact sufficient to require a trial.  The Court is not inclined to schedule a continued hearing on Southwest’s MSJ until the previously-sought depositions of key flight crew members and PMK have been taken.  It is not unique to cases subject to federal regulations that the defendant honestly and reasonably believes the case is subject to a complete defense under those regulations.  But where the evidence submitted to prove that complete defense consists of witness statements or declarations from the defendant’s employees, trial judges usually delay the hearing under Section 437c(h) where the party opposing the MSJ has made timely efforts to seek to depose those witnesses but has been spurned by the defense.  It is also not unique to pre-emption defenses that an MSJ hearing must be continued multiple times to enable the opposing party to overcome claimed stonewalling of discovery.  The Court recently granted a Privette Doctrine MSJ, after the hearing was continued multiple times to enable completion of reasonably requested discovery propounded before the motion was filed. 

 

This Court further notes that further evidence was submitted on reply with defense counsel’s reply declaration. Moreover, the parties each take issue with the declarations and/or requests for judicial notice of one another for various reasons. Even where the Court can take judicial notice of various items presented, taking judicial notice does not require the Court to accept the truth of matters asserted in witness statements that were not subject to cross-examination. 

 

The Court believes that as to the product liability cause of action, there is no triable issue of material fact that would require a trial against Southwest, as distinct from the vape pen manufacturer or battery supplier for the vape pen.  Thus, the Court’s tentative would grant MSA on the product liability cause of action against Southwest.  But as to the negligence cause of action, Plaintiff asserts that Southwest has restricted discovery bearing on the existence of a complete federal preemption defense here, and that Southwest refuses to make available for deposition the very witnesses on whose witness statements Southwest seeks to predicate its prima facie showing on summary judgment.  That is problematic to the Court.  Plaintiff asserts that triable issues of fact bear on whether Southwest complied with its federally regulated duty of care.  Much of Plaintiff’s argument appears to be more directed against TSA for its screening of passengers, whether they are non-revenue passengers or others, for the presence of lithium or lithium-ion batteries in carry-on baggage.  The standard of care may have changed since the flight in question in May of 2019, over five years ago.  Further, Plaintiff cannot create a triable issue of fact by asserting different facts in his deposition versus in his declaration opposing summary judgment.  These are things the Court is outlining in advance of oral argument to be discussed at the hearing.

 

The Court will entertain argument from Southwest that there is no set of facts covered by still-pending responses to Plaintiff’s discovery that would require a trial as to the negligence cause of action.  The Court will also entertain oral argument from Plaintiff that Southwest has failed to carry its initial burden on summary judgment because the witness statements supplied as the moving party’s evidence is hearsay not within any exception.  If neither of those arguments is convincing to the Court, Plaintiff should be prepared with a short list of depositions by deponent name and/or request for production by RFP number, and/or interrogatories by set and rog number, that Plaintiff still requires a better or further or amended verified response in order to oppose this motion further.  If there already is a pending discovery motion on calendar for that information, Plaintiff should be prepared to advise the Court the date of the hearing of those motions.  If Southwest intends never to offer the flight captain or flight attendants for deposition, counsel should be prepared to articulate why not.  Then the Court will have sufficient information to decide how far out to re-schedule the hearing on this dispositive motion.

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Kenneth Alexander v. Slater Waterproofing, Inc., et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Defendant, Walker Consultants, Inc. dba Walker Restoration Consultants (DOE 1)  

¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff, Kenneth Alexander

¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        None set.  

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Defendant Walker’s Demurrer 

¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                  (1)  SUSTAINED WITHOUT leave to amend as to the first cause of action, and WITH leave to amend as to the premise liability claim

 

 

 

 

¿¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

On October 5, 2022, Plaintiff, Kenneth Alexander (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants, Slater Waterproofing, Inc., City of Redondo Beach, County of Los Angeles, Sika Corporation, and DOES 1 through 50. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) General Negligence; (2) Premises Liability; and (3) Products Liability.

 

Defendant, Walker Consultants, Inc. dba Walker Restoration Consultants (DOE 1) (“Walker”) now files a demurrer to Plaintiff’s original complaint.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On July 2, 2024, Walker filed its demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint. On July 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On July 5, 2024, Walker filed a reply brief. 

 

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Along with their moving papers, Walker has filed a Request for Judicial Notice seeking this Court judicially notice the following:

 

1.      The Organization of Record Form pertaining to Walker on file with the Board for Professional Engineers, Land Surveyors, and Geologists, California Department of Consumer Affairs, attached as “Exhibit A” to the Declaration of Sultan R. Alshawa, Esq.

 

The Court GRANTS this request and takes judicial notice of the above documents.

 

Further, along with Plaintiff’s opposition brief, Plaintiff requested this Court take judicial notice of the following:

 

1.      Exhibit 1—pertinent portions of the Deposition Transcript of PMQ Lauren Sablan taken on January 24, 2024.

2.      Exhibit 2— conformed copy of the Declaration and Certificate of Plaintiff’s Attorney Of Record, Kevin S. Conlogue, Esq., Pursuant To Code Of Civil Procedure Section 411.35(d) filed and served on April 3, 2024.

 

The Court also GRANTs Plaintiff’s request and takes judicial notice of the above.

 

III. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

A. Legal Standard

¿ 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)¿¿ 

 

B. Discussion

 

             Walker demurs to Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that: (1) Plaintiff’s first cause of action for professional negligence is barred by Code of Civil Procedure sections 335.1 and 411.35 because Plaintiff failed to file a certificate of merit within two (2) years and sixty (60) days of the incident that forms the basis of this suit; (2) Plaintiff’s second cause of action fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible; and (3) Plaintiff’s third cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible.

 

General Negligence - Code of Civil Procedure section 411.35

 

            Here, Plaintiff first argues that Plaintiff’s first cause of action for professional negligence fails and cannot be cured based on Plaintiff’s failure to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 411.35. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 411.35(a), “[i]n every action…arising out of the professional negligence of a person holding a valid architect’s certificate…or of a person holding a valid registration as a professional engineer…or of a person holding a valid land surveyor’s license on or before the date of service of the complaint or cross-complaint on any defense or cross-defendant, the attorney for the plaintiff or cross-complainant shall file and serve the certificate specified by subdivision (b).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 411.35, subd. (a).)

 

            Defendant Walker asserts that Plaintiff did not file a certificate of merit in conformance with Code of Civil Procedure section 411.35 (b)(1), 411.35(d) or the excuse certificate by Plaintiff’s counsel in conformance with section 411.35(b)(2), with Plaintiff’s original pleadings on October 5, 2022. Defendant Walker further asserts that Plaintiff failed to file the required certificate of merit within 60 days after the filing of the original complaint.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that section 411.35 is inapplicable because pursuant to subdivision (d), “[w]here the attorney intends to rely solely on the doctrine of ‘res ipsa loquitur,’ as defined in section 646 of the Evidence Code, or exclusively on a failure to inform of the consequences of a procedure, or both, [Section 411.35] is inapplicable. The attorney shall certify upon filing of the complaint that the attorney is solely relying on the doctrines of ‘res ipsa loquitur” or failure to inform of the consequences of a procedure or both, and for that reason is not filing a certificate required by [Section 411.35].” (Code Civ. Proc., § 411.35, subd. (d).) Plaintiff contends that he was ignorant to the capacity of DOE 1, and was thus, unable to file any such certificate under section 411.35. Plaintiff notes that by filing a certificate pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 411.35, subdivision (d), after DOE 1’s counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel indicating that Walker was a group of engineers, Section 411.35, subdivision (d), made any other certificate requirements under Section 411.35 irrelevant.

 

            This Court notes that on February 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the DOE amendment substituting Defendant, Walker in as DOE 1. Plaintiff confirms that a certificate under 411.35, subdivision (d) was filed within sixty (60) days of naming DOE 1 to the lawsuit. In distinguishing Curtis Eng’g Corp. v. Superior Court (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 542, as relied upon by Defendant Walker, which did not include a DOE Defendant, Plaintiff instead cites to McVeigh v. Doe 1 (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 898. In McVeigh, a plaintiff filed a complaint within the applicable statute of limitations but failed to file a certificate of merit. However, within sixty (60) days of filing the original complaint, that same Plaintiff filed a certificate.

 

            Here, this Court finds that this case is not analogous to that of McVeigh. In McVeigh, again, the certificate was filed within sixty (60) days of the original complaint. Here, Plaintiff did not file a certificate within sixty (60) days from when the original complaint was filed, but within sixty (60) days of when Defendant Walker was amended in as DOE 1. Further, this Court notes that the DOE Defendants did not include any indication, nor did the original complaint, that it was relying on the doctrine of res ipsa loquitur or failure to inform of the consequences of a procedure or both. However, as noted in Plaintiff’s counsel’s attached declaration of certification filed on April 3, 2024, and subsequently judicially noticed as Exhibit 2, Kevin S. Conlogue indicates in paragraph 2 of his declaration that Plaintiff will solely rely on the doctrine of “res ipsa loquitur.”

 

            In its reply brief, Defendant Walker contends that Plaintiff’s allegations, despite claiming to be solely based on res ipsa loquitur, are based on general negligence allegations. The face of the original complaint does indicate that that it is based on general negligence as it alleges duty, breach, causation, and damages versus a presumption of negligence based on injury and no competing negligence on the part of Plaintiff.

 

            Here, this Court notes that neither party presents precedent directly on point, nor does it exist. As such, the Court analyzes this issue based on the plain language of the statute. There, the sixty (60) day allowance of time is as to the filing of the complaint, not a DOE amendment, nor amended complaint. Further, a demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading on its face. Although the declaration was judicially noticed, it does not change the pleadings on the face of the complaint itself, which does not state a cognizable claim for res ipsa loquitur, nor certify that this is the sole doctrine relied upon against DOE 1. Further, the Court is puzzled as to how Plaintiff could legitimately allege a res ipsa loquitur theory when a solo bicyclist is alleged to have fallen from his bicycle while riding on a curve, not a fact pattern where as a matter of law the plaintiff would be free of negligence.  Thus, unless the parties change this Court’s mind in oral argument, the Court’s tentative ruling will be to SUSTAIN the demurrer to the first cause of action without leave to amend.

 

 

Premises Liability

 

The elements to assert a claim for premises liability are the same as those required for a negligence cause of action. The elements of negligence are: (1) a duty to exercise ordinary care; (2) breach of that duty; (3) causation; and (4) damages. (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.) Specifically, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not alleged a cognizable duty owed as Defendant Walker did not own, possess, or control the premises. While those who own, possess, or control property are not insurers of the safety of their patrons, they generally have a duty to exercise reasonable and ordinary care in keeping the premises reasonably safe and in managing the property¿to¿avoid exposing others to an unreasonable risk of harm.  (Ortega v. Kmart Corp.¿(2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205; Annocki¿v. Peterson Enterprises, LLC¿(2014) 232¿Cal.App.4th 32, 37.)  The existence and scope of duty are legal questions for the court.  (Annocki, 232¿Cal.App.4th.¿at¿36.)  “A store owner exercises ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the portions of the premises open to customers, and the care required is commensurate with the risks involved.”¿ (Ibid.)  If a dangerous condition exists, the property owner is “under a duty to exercise ordinary care either to make the condition¿reasonably safe for their¿[customers’]¿use or to give a warning adequate to enable them to avoid the harm.”  (Bridgman v. Safeway Stores, Inc.¿(1960) 53 Cal.2d 443, 446.)  The party asserting the cause of action has the burden to prove that the owner had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition in sufficient time to correct it.  (Ortega, 26 Cal.4th at 1203, 1206.) 

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff alleges that the original complaint clearly alleges that: “Defendants owned, managed, maintained, controlled, used, operated, supervised, inspected, and/or repaired the property involved in this incident, adjacent property, improvements, and/or fixtures thereon, and/or component parts thereof, in a negligent, careless, reckless, unlawful, defective, and/or dangerous manner which were used in a foreseeable manner involving unreasonable damages and/or risk not reasonably apparent, without adequate warning, which caused injuries and damages to Plaintiffs, and each of them, as alleged herein,” and thus, ownership has been sufficiently alleged. This Court disagrees.

 

            First, this allegation comes after the general negligence action and is attached as attachment: GN-1. However, this attachment’s first sentence reads: “Defendants: Slater Waterproofing, Inc., Sika Corporation…” and then proceeds to that allegation. As such, at minimum, this complaint is uncertain as this allegation appears to be referencing only two named defendants and not DOE 1. However, even if it was not uncertain, this Court notes that merely including such a conclusory allegation is not enough to overcome the pleading requirement for premises liability. Based on each of the named and unnamed defendants, an allegation referencing each named and unnamed party as “defendants” and asserting they are the owners, etc., of the subject premises are far to conclusory for this court to overrule on demurrer. As such, the demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED, but the Court will grant leave to amend if Plaintiff represents that it can truthfully amend the allegations to more specifically allege Walker’s role as the owner or operator of property where the claimed injury occurred.

 

Products Liability

 

            Lastly, Defendant Walker demurs to the third cause of action for products liability. As with a products liability action asserted under a strict liability theory, under a negligence theory the plaintiff must prove a defect caused the injury. (Chavez v. Glock, Inc. (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 1283.) However, “[u]nder a negligence theory, a plaintiff must also prove ‘an additional element, namely, that the defect in the product was due to negligence of the defendant.’” (Merril v. Navegar, Inc. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 465, 479.)  Because Plaintiff’s negligence cause of action is barred as discussed above, the Court views this cause of action on the facts presented as a square peg not fitting into a round hole.  A products liability claim is generally unavailable when the alleged injury-causing activity of the defendant is the rendition of a service rather than the sale or distribution of a product.  California follows the Restatement of Torts in precluding products liability claims for services.  (See, e.g., Murphy v. E/R. Squibb & Sons. Inc. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 672, 677.)  The careless rendition of a service by service professionals like engineers may be the subject of a negligence theory, but generally not a product liability or implied warranty theory.  (Allied Properties v. John A. Blume & Associates (1972) 25 Cal.App.3d 848, 855-56.)  Here, the negligence theory against Walker is forecloaed by the absence of a timely certificate or merit and the lack of a proposed amendment purporting to state a res ipsa theory. 

 

 

 

III. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED in part without leave to amend and SUSTAINED in part with leave to amend. Plaintiff is allowed to present argument as to whether this Court should allow twenty (20) days leave to amend.

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A.    Factual¿¿ 

 

On June 15, 2023, Plaintiff, Richard Colburn (“Plaintiff”) filed a product liability complaint against Defendant, BMX Racing Group, Inc., a California corporation, dba BRG, and DOES 1 through 100. The complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Design Defect; (2)Manufacturing Defect; (3) Failure to Warn – Inadequate Warning – Inadequate Instruction; (4) Negligent Design; (5) Negligent Manufacture; (6) Failure to Warn – Inadequate Warning – Inadequate Instruction; (7) Breach of Warranty; and (8) Malfunction Theory – Res Ipsa Loquitur. The complaint is based on the allegations that Defendants designed, engineered, manufactured, sold, distributed, marketed, packaged, labeled, constructed, tested, inspected, warrantied, and/or performed other work in connection with defective Crank Arms with the specific purpose that they be used by riders on their bicycles. (Complaint, ¶ 14.) Specifically, the plaintiff alleges that the crank arm was lauded as a safe product, of merchantable quality, and fit for its intended and reasonably foreseeable uses, but that Defendants failed to disclose that their crank arms were unsafe, and posed a substantial risk that they would break, resulting in injury to the rider of the bicycle. (Complaint, ¶¶ 14, 16.)

 

On September 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Request for Default on Defendant, BMX Racing Group, Inc. (“BMX”). On December 15, 2023, this request was granted and Default Judgment was entered.   Now, BMX has filed a motion to set aside the default judgment.

 

B.     Procedural

 

On June 17, 2024, Defendant BMX filed this Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment. On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On July 30, 2024, Defendant BMX filed a reply brief. 

 

II. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

A.     Legal Standard

¿ 

The Motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5.

 

Courts may set aside a default or default judgment due to lack of actual notice. Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5 states: 

“(a) When service of a summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action and a default or default judgment has been entered against him or her in the action, he or she may serve and file a notice of motion to set aside the default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action. The notice of motion shall be served and filed within a reasonable time, but in no event exceeding the earlier of: (i) two years after entry of a default judgment against him or her; or (ii) 180 days after service on him or her of a written notice that the default or default judgment has been entered. 

(b) A notice of motion to set aside a default or default judgment and for leave to defend the action shall designate as the time for making the motion a date prescribed by subdivision (b) of Section 1005, and it shall be accompanied by an affidavit showing under oath that the party’s lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect. The party shall serve and file with the notice a copy of the answer, motion, or other pleading proposed to be filed in the action. 

(c) Upon a finding by the court that the motion was made within the period permitted by subdivision (a) and that his or her lack of actual notice in time to defend the action was not caused by his or her avoidance of service or inexcusable neglect, it may set aside the default or default judgment on whatever terms as may be just and allow the party to defend the action.” 

Further, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §473(b), both discretionary and mandatory relief is available to parties from a judgment, dismissal, or other proceeding.  Discretionary relief is available under the statute as “the court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §¿473(b).)  Alternatively, mandatory relief is available when “accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.”  (Ibid.)  Under this statute, an application for discretionary or mandatory relief must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) 

 

“‘[W]hen relief under section 473¿is¿available, there is a strong¿public¿policy¿in¿favor¿of granting relief and allowing the requesting party his or her day in court…[Citation.]” (Rappleyea v. Campbell¿(1994) 8 Cal. 4th 975, 981-82.) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Here, Defendant BMX is moving to set aside the default judgment on the grounds that it argues it was not given personal notice through its agent of service for process, but that instead, Plaintiff insufficiently attempted substituted service.

 

As noted by Plaintiff in his opposition, Defendant BMX does not state which portion of the statute it is relying on in bringing this motion. Although this could be grounds to deny the motion, as noted by Plaintiff, in the interest of judicial efficiency, this Court nonetheless rules on this matter. Defendant BMX clarifies in its reply brief that it is moving for relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473 subdivision (b). If this is true, however, this raises an issue as to whether this motion is timely.  While a Section 473.5 motion may be brought within two years, an application for discretionary or mandatory relief must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Here, the clerk’s default was entered on September 1, 2023. If Defendant BMX sought relief from that entry, it would have had to bring this motion by March 1, 2024. Further, Default Judgment was entered in this case on December 15, 2023. Thus, this motion would have been required to be brough on or before June 15, 2024. Nonetheless, the motion was not brought until June 17, 2024, and is thus, untimely. However, as noted by BMX, June 15, 2024 was a Saturday, and therefore, a filing on the next Court date, June 17, 2024, would render the motion timely. (See California Rules of Court, Rule 1.10(a).)

 

Because this motion is timely, this Court must now analyze whether Defendant BMX’s failure to respond was truly due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b). But the arguments made as to 473(b) relief are not made until the reply papers, not the moving papers.  Because new arguments are not to be made on reply, as the opposing party would not be able to respond, this Court will allow a brief continuance to allow Plaintiff to respond to the arguments made in the reply papers.

 

             The parties should be prepared to discuss the timing for Plaintiff to file its sur-reply and for a continued hearing date.
