DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02035    Hearing Date: November 4, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

MARIA GUADALUPE OROZCO,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV02035

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          November 4, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles World Airports

Responding Party:                  None

(1)   Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One

(2)   Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production of Documents

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motions are GRANTED.

Plaintiff Maria Guadalupe Orozco is ordered to respond without objections to defendant City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles World Airports’ Form Interrogatories, Set One, within twenty days.

Plaintiff Maria Guadalupe Orozco is ordered (1) to serve on defendant a verified response without objections to defendant’s Request for Production and Inspection (Set One) and (2) to produce all documents and things in plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to defendant’s request, within twenty days.  

 

 

The Court orders that plaintiff Orozco pay $960 in sanctions to defendant, within thirty days.

BACKGROUND

On June 22, 2023, plaintiff Maria Guadalupe Orozco filed a complaint against City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles World Airports, and Tom Bradley International Terminal Equipment Company for (1) statutory liability – dangerous condition of public property, (2) negligence – premises liability, and (3) negligence based on slip and/or trip and fall on June 23, 2021.

On March 20, 2024, plaintiff filed a FAC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Interrogatories

            If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906. 

            Request for Production of Documents

            Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  So, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487. 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles World Airports requests that the Court compel plaintiff Maria Guadalupe Orozco to respond to the initial form interrogatories and request for production and inspection.

            Defendant asserts that on March 22, 2024, defendant propounded written discovery requests on plaintiff.  Plaintiff’s responses were due on April 23, 2024.  On April 23, plaintiff’s counsel requested an extension and defense counsel provided a two-week extension to provide responses, making the new deadline May 7, 2024.  On May 7, plaintiff’s counsel requested an extension to May 10, 2024, which defense counsel granted.  On May 16, 2024, defense counsel called plaintiff’s counsel and informed him “of the lack of timely discovery responses.”  To date, defense counsel has not received responses. 

            There is no opposition.

            The Court finds that the discovery requests were properly served, and that plaintiff failed to serve responses and thus has waived objections. 

            The motions are thus GRANTED.

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Defendant City requests $840 as to the motion regarding form interrogatories and $1440 for the motion regarding request for production in sanctions against plaintiff.  The Court finds that $960 ($240/hr. x 4 hrs.) is a reasonable amount to be awarded against plaintiff in total for both motions.

 

ORDER

The motions are GRANTED.

Plaintiff Maria Guadalupe Orozco is ordered to respond without objections to defendant City of Los Angeles, Los Angeles World Airports’ Form Interrogatories, Set One, within twenty days.

Plaintiff Maria Guadalupe Orozco is ordered (1) to serve on defendant a verified response without objections to defendant’s Request for Production and Inspection (Set One) and (2) to produce all documents and things in plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to defendant’s request, within twenty days.  

The Court orders that plaintiff Orozco pay $960 in sanctions to defendant, within thirty days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The action is STAYED.

BACKGROUND

            On May 31, 2024, plaintiff Roberto Arroyo Figueroa filed a complaint against defendant Hyundai Motor America for Song-Beverly Act violations.

A Digression

            Defendant has filed a Request for Judicial Notice asking that this Court take judicial notice of the Plaintiff’s Complaint.  It is not necessary to ask the court to judicially notice the complaint filed in the action in which the motion is made.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Hyundai Motor America requests an order compelling plaintiff to binding arbitration.

            The complaint alleges that on January 25, 2023, plaintiff leased a 2023 Hyundai Tucson.  These causes of action arise out of warranty and repair obligations of Hyundai in connection with the subject vehicle and for which Hyundai issued a written warranty.  Complaint, ¶8.  Hyundai warranted the subject vehicle and agreed to preserve and maintain the utility or performance of the vehicle or to provide compensation if there was a failure in such utility or performance.  In connection with the purchase, plaintiff received various warranties, including a five-year/60,000 mile express bumper to bumper warranty, a ten-year/100,000 mile powertrain warranty, which covers the engine and transmission.  Id., ¶9.  The subject vehicle was delivered to plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities, including engine, electrical, emission, and structural system defects.  Id., ¶10.  Plaintiff first presented the vehicle for repairs in May 2023 and reported oil leaking.  Id., ¶11.  In August 2023, plaintiff presented the vehicle again and reported low oil levels.  Id., ¶12.  Plaintiff presented the vehicle in January 2024 and reported the front speakers and rear seat belt malfunctioning.  Additionally, plaintiff reported excessive oil consumption.  Id., ¶13.  In April 2024, plaintiff presented the vehicle for repeated concerns relating to excessive oil consumption.  Id., ¶14.  Plaintiff revokes acceptance of the lease agreement.  Id., ¶15.

 

Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant asserts that when plaintiff purchased the subject vehicle on January 25, 2023, a Warranty accompanied the purchase, which included an agreement to arbitrate on pages 12-14. The section is titled, “BINDING ARBITRATION FOR CALIFORNIA VEHICLES ONLY.” 

It states in part:

“If you purchased or leased your Hyundai vehicle in the State of California, you and we, Hyundai Motor America, each agree that any claim or disputes between us (including between you and any of our affiliated companies) related to or arising out of your vehicle purchase, advertising for the vehicle, use of your vehicle, the performance of the vehicle, any service relating to the vehicle, the vehicle warranty, representations in the warranty, or the duties contemplated under the warranty, including without limitation claims related to false or misleading advertising, unfair competition, breach of contract or warranty, the failure to conform a vehicle to warranty, failure to repurchase or replace your vehicle, or claims for a refund or partial refund of your vehicle's purchase price (excluding personal injury claims), but excluding claims brought under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, shall be resolved by binding arbitration at either your or our election, even if the claim is initially filed in a court of law. If either you or we elect to resolve our dispute via arbitration (as opposed to in a court of law), such binding arbitration shall be administered by and through the American Arbitration Association (AAA) under its Consumer Arbitration Rules.”

 

It further states:

“IF YOU PURCHASED OR LEASED YOUR VEHICLE IN CALIFORNIA, YOUR WARRANTY IS MADE SUBJECT TO THE TERMS OF THIS BINDING ARBITRATION PROVISION. BY USING THE VEHICLE, OR REQUESTING OR ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS. IF YOU DO NOT AGREE WITH THESE TERMS, PLEASE CONTACT US AT OPT-OUT@HMAUSA.COM WITHIN THIRTY (30) DAYS OF YOUR PURCHASE OR LEASE TO OPT-OUT OF THIS ARBITRATION PROVISION.”

Defendant also contends that on January 25, 2023, plaintiff enrolled their vehicle in Hyundai’s Bluelink service through the Dealer-Assisted Enrollment Process.  In order to enroll in Bluelink services, customers must agree to the then-effective Connected Services Agreement (“CSA”).  In order for plaintiff to have enrolled in Hyundai’s Bluelink services, they would have had to click a box to acknowledge that they “read and agreed to the Blue Link Terms & Conditions” and then click the “Complete” button.  Defendant points out that the CSA provides an arbitration provision, which states:  “Hyundai and you agree to arbitrate any and all disputes and claims between us arising out of or relating to this Agreement, Connected Services, Connected Services Systems, Service Plans, the Vehicle . . . . This agreement to arbitrate is intended to be broadly interpreted and to make all disputes and claims between us subject to arbitration to the fullest extent permitted by law. . . . The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to:  claims based in contract, tort, warranty, statute, fraud, . . . .”  See Vijay Rao decl., Exhs. 1, 2; Ali Ameripour decl., Exh. 3.

Defendant also argues that it has standing to compel arbitration because it has provided the warranty directly to plaintiff in connection with their purchase of the vehicle.  Defendant contends that given that plaintiff has purchased the vehicle in California, is bringing this action pursuant to the Warranty, and has alleged that repairs have been performed under the Warranty, “it is clear that arbitration provision applies.”

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant failed to submit admissible evidence of such clause because Ameripour’s declaration does not authenticate the Owner’s Handbook.  Plaintiff further contends that the Federal Trade Commission has expressly decided that binding mandatory arbitration of any warranty claim is unlawful, even if the warranty claims arise under state law.

In reply, defendant argues that it has properly established the existence of the agreements to arbitrate and that plaintiff has “not even remotely attempted to prove the falsity of the purported agreements.”  See Ali Ameripour second decl.

The Court finds that arbitration agreements exist—in the 2023 Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information and under the Connected Services Agreement.  Plaintiff has not shown the falsity of the arbitration provisions.  Although plaintiff did not sign the arbitration provision in the Warranty, that is not dispositive because plaintiff accepted the benefits found within the Warranty.  See Cavalry SPV I, LLC v. Watkins (2019) 36 Cal. App. 4th 1070, 1081 (“[A] party may accept a contract by conduct, as well as by words.”).

 

Scope

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration clauses as they arise directly out of the sale of the vehicle and the vehicle’s warranty.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the CSA’s arbitration provision does not apply to plaintiff’s claims because the “clear language of the CSA indicates that arbitration only applies to claims related to the ‘connected services’ that are the subject of the agreement, not the statutory warranty claims brought by plaintiff.” 

The Court finds that the arbitration provision in the “Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information,” specifically “Section 4 Hyundai Warranty Information” covers the scope of plaintiff’s claims as it covers “all disputes and claims between us relating to or arising out of . . . use or performance of your vehicle, or the vehicle warranty.”  As alleged in the complaint, Hyundai issued a written warranty and the claims asserted in the claim “arise out of warranty and repair obligations” of Hyundai.  The arbitration provision in the CSA also appears to encompass plaintiff’s claims (“The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to:  claims based in . . . warranty, statute, . . .”).

 

Enforceability

            Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the arbitration provision in the Owner’s Handbook is procedurally unconscionable because plaintiff was never informed by South Bay Hyundai that any warranty claims against defendant would be subject to binding arbitration pursuant to an arbitration agreement that purportedly existed within the Owner’s Handbook out of which plaintiff would be required to opt.  See plaintiff’s decl.  Plaintiff asserts that he never signed any post-sale “arbitration agreement” because the subsequent “arbitration agreement” does not require a consumer’s express consent or contain an actual signature line.  Further, plaintiff argues, the “arbitration agreements” are pre-printed adhesion contracts drafted by defendant and that the provision in the Warranty’s Booklet was “buried”

Plaintiff also argues that the arbitration provisions are substantively unconscionable because they deprive plaintiff of a right to a jury trial and that he would receive “extremely limited due process” as discovery is limited.  Moreover, plaintiff contends, the Song-Beverly Act’s anti-waiver provision precludes arbitration because it would deprive plaintiff of a fundamental right to an action under Civil Code §1794.

In reply, defendant argues that the arbitration provisions are not unconscionable because plaintiff established mutual assent in their complaint to be bound by the warranty.  Defendant also asserts that plaintiff not only uses the warranty as a foundational basis for this action, but plaintiff concedes to having accepted the benefits under the warranty, and thus agreed to be bound by its terms.  Defendant further argues that plaintiff is estopped from refusing to comply with the arbitration provision that is contained in the warranty because he is attempting to enforce the warranty for plaintiff’s benefit.  Defendant also asserts that the arbitration provision is on the first page of the “Hyundai Warranty information” section and is clearly labeled in the table of contents.  Plaintiff was under no obligation to purchase the vehicle, and he had the opportunity to opt out.  Defendant also points out that AAA’s Consumer Arbitration Rules do allow for discovery and the exchange of information between the parties and that AAA’s Rule 22 allows for additional discovery and states that the arbitrator may determine whether further information is needed to provide for a fundamentally fair process.  See Ramirez v. Charter Communications, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal. 4th 478 (analyzing same AAA rules).

The Court finds that even if the Warranty’s arbitration provision is an adhesion contract, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration clause.  Moreover, as in Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 931, “there is no reason in this case to conclude that plaintiff lacked power to bargain.  In general, nothing prevents purchasers of . . . vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that plaintiff could not bargain in this case.”  Moreover, the arbitration provision is not “buried” as the table of contents clearly identifies where the arbitration provision can be found and the opt-out provision is conspicuous (in bold and all capitals).  

As to substantive unconscionability, the Court finds little to none as the arbitration provisions do not lack mutuality.  The discovery rules apply equally to both parties, for example. 

The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

 ORDER

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.
