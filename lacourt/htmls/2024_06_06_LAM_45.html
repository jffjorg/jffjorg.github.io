DEPARTMENT 45 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All rulings shown here are TENTATIVE ONLY, and thus oral argument WILL be heard. All Counsel are still required to attend.

Case Number: 21STCV17649    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

FIRE GUARD CORPORATION dba BUILTRIGHT ENGINEERING AND CONSTRUCTION,

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

 

STANFORD AND 12TH STREET, LP, et al.,

 

                              Defendants.

 
Case No.:  21STCV17649

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Complaint Filed: 05/11/21

Cross-Complaint Filed: 07/07/21

Trial Date: N/A

 

 

 

Hearing date:              June 6, 2024

Moving Party:             Cross-Defendant Mayer Separzadeh  

Responding Party:      Unopposed  

 

Motions to Compel Responses

 

The court has considered the moving papers. No opposition papers were filed.

The motions to compel responses are granted. 

Background

            This is an action that arises from the alleged failure to pay monies owed pursuant to a contract for renovating a property located at 901 East 12th Street, Los Angeles, CA 90021 (the “Property”). On May 11, 2021, Plaintiff Fire Guard Corporation dba Builtright Engineering and Construction (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Stanford and 12th Street, LP, Shu C. Kou, Shan C. Kou, and DOES 1 to 100, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract; (2) open book account; (3) account stated; (4) quantum meruit; and (5) foreclosure of mechanic’s lien.

            On July 7, 2021, Defendants Shu C. Kou and Shan C. Kou filed an Answer to the Complaint.

            Also, on July 7, 2021, Defendants Shu C. Kou and Shan C. Kou (collectively “Cross-Complainants”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendants Stanford and 12th Street, LP; Stanford Mart, LP; Mayer Separzadeh; Shahin S. Zackary; Said Bral (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”); and ROES 1 through 25, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) implied contractual indemnity; (2) expressed contractual indemnity; (3) breach of lease; (4) breach of guaranty; and (5) account stated.

            On March 7, 2022, Cross-Defendant Mayer Separzadeh (“Separzadeh”) filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

            On August 15, 2023,  Cross-Defendant Separzadeh filed and served the instant two unopposed discovery motions: (1) a motion to compel Cross-Complainant Shu C. Kou (“Shu”)[1] to serve verified responses to Cross-Defendant Separzadeh’s Form Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Sanctions (the “Shu Motion”); and (2) a motion to compel Cross-Complainant Shan C. Kou’s (“Shan”) responses to Cross-Defendant Separzadeh’s Form Interrogatories, Set One and Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions (the “Shan Motion”) (collectively, the “Motions”). The Motions each request monetary sanctions in the sum of $3,310.00 against Cross-Complainants Shu and Shan, respectively. Given that the Motions are unopposed, there is an inference that the Motions are meritorious. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)  

Initially, the Court notes that the motion as to Cross-Complainant Shan requests to compel responses to both form and special interrogatories. Cross-Defendant Separzadeh should have filed two separate motions as to Cross-Complainant Shan instead of requesting responses to form and special interrogatories in the same motion. Cross-Defendant Separazadeh cannot attempt to avoid the filing fee. However, the Court will still consider the Shan Motion. The Court will address the Motions in this one ruling.

Legal Standard

            “Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.260(a).) If the party to whom interrogatories are propounded does not serve a timely response, “[t]he party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (a).) “The party propounding interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) Unverified discovery response “are tantamount to no responses at all.” (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.)

Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(h) states that a misuse of the discovery process includes “[m]aking or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery.”  A court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) “The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (c).) A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions.  (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771.)

YEVGENY OSTROVSKIY;

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

 

MIKHAIL SIRETSKIY, et al.;

 

                              Defendants.

 
Case No.:  22STCV25707

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed:  08/09/22

Trial Date:  02/03/25

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             June 6, 2024

Moving Party:             Defendant Big Block Realty, Inc.

Responding Party:       Plaintiff Yevgeny Ostrovskiy

 

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint with Motion to Strike

 

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

The court SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action of the Complaint, with 30 days leave to amend.

            The motion to strike is MOOT given that the Court granted leave to amend.

Background

Plaintiff Yevgeny Ostrovskiy filed this action on August 9, 2022 against defendants Mikhail Siretskiy; The Siretskiy Organization, LLC; Siretskiy Real Estate Inc.; and Big Block Realty, Inc., alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Fraud; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (4) Theft. The First Amended Complaint alleges Plaintiff invested $100,000 with Defendants pursuant to their Investment Agreement on August 21, 2018. (FAC ¶ 14.) Defendants allegedly only returned $60,000 and have not repaid $40,000, despite Plaintiff’s demands. (Id. at ¶¶ 14-16.)

Plaintiff filed Amendments to Complaint on October 13, 2022, adding Ronny Santana (Doe 1), Realty Source Incorporated (Doe 2), and eXp Realty of California, Inc. (Doe 3) as doe defendants.

Defendant Big Block Realty, Inc. filed its first demurrer to the Complaint on October 13, 2022. On July 19, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action of the Complaint with 30 days leave to amend. The Court also overruled Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action of the Complaint.

On October 11, 2023, Defendant Big Block Realty, Inc. filed its Demurrer and Motion to Strike the First Amended Complaint. On May 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the opposition. On May 30, 2024, Defendant filed the reply.

 

Legal Standard

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Discussion

Meet and Confer

CCP § 430.41(a) states, in relevant part: “Before filing a demurrer . . . the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.”

Defendant’s counsel attests that multiple meet and confer efforts were made and on October 11, 2023, he spoke with Defendant’s counsel via telephone regarding the Demurrer and Motion to Strike. (Bell Decl. ¶¶ 2-4.) Given the circumstances, the court finds Defendant sufficiently satisfied the meet-and-confer requirements of CCP § 430.41. The court exercises its discretion to proceed with ruling on the merits of the demurrer.

 

Demurrer

Defendant Big Block Realty, Inc. demurs to the first and second causes of action on the ground they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (CCP § 430.10(e).)

 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

Defendant contends that the first cause of action for breach of contract does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because the First Amended Complaint’s allegations fail to establish any actionable agreement between plaintiff Yevgeny Ostrovskiy and defendant Big Block Realty, Inc. Defendant further argues that plaintiff’s Investment Agreement (Exhibit C- First Amended Complaint “FAC” at p. 102.) fails to show that defendant Big Block Realty, Inc. was a party to the contract. Plaintiff alleges that a contract exists which was “executed by Plaintiff and Siretskiy, in his capacity as agent for SRO (the collective of Siretskiy, the Siretskiy Organization, LLC, and Siretskiy Real Estate, Inc.)” (Opposition at 6.) Plaintiff also argues that California Code, Business and Professions Code section 10010.5(b)(2) is sufficiently plead because the FAC sets forth that “BBR, from September 8, 2020, until April 25, 2020, was the responsible broker under California Real Estate statutes for defendant Siretskiy.” (FAC ¶ 6.) In reply, Defendant argues that Defendant BBR was not associated with Siretskiy or Plaintiff at the time the Agreement was executed and could not have supervised and oversaw Siretskiy’s act of entering into the Agreement, regardless of whether such act requires a license pursuant to Bus. & Prof. Code section 10010.5(b)(2).

“To establish a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must plead and prove (1) the existence of the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Maxwell v. Dolezal (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 93, 97-98 [internal citation omitted].) For a written contract, the plaintiff may “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than the price language.” (Ibid.) To “plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms. This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’ ” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.)  

The court finds Defendant’s arguments have merit. The First Amended Complaint alleges that “Siretskiy has been a licensed California real estate agent and is an agent of Big Block Realty, Inc. [(“BBR”)] and subject to its supervision as a responsible broker, and on information and belief at all times was and is acting within the scope of such agency.” (FAC ¶ 2.) The FAC further alleges “Although defendant BBR was not the responsible broker for defendant Siretskiy at the date the investment agreement was executed, defendant Siretskiy continued to renew the Investment Agreement with plaintiff Ostrovskiy and continued to make representations about the Investment Agreement during the period of time that defendant BBR was the responsible broker for defendant Siretskiy and was obligated by law to supervise all of defendant Siretskiy’s activities, including his activities as relate to plaintiff Ostrovskiy.” (FAC ¶ 7.) The FAC further alleges “Defendant Siretskiy, on behalf of himself and on behalf of the other Siretskiy Defendants, promised plaintiff before and on or about August 21, 2018, that his investment in real estate was safe and secure and would be returned to him with the guaranteed investment income within less than one year.” (FAC ¶ 28.)

Nevertheless, these facts are insufficient to state a cause of action for breach of contract because there is no agreement between Defendant Big Block Realty, Inc. For instance, Plaintiff concedes that “defendant BBR was not the responsible broker for defendant Siretskiy at the date the investment agreement was executed” which fails to show that there was an agreement between BBR and Plaintiff. (FAC ¶ 7.)

The court therefore SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurer to the first cause of action because it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (CCP § 403.10(e).) The court grants Plaintiff 30 days leave to amend.

 

Second Cause of Action – Fraud

Defendant argues the second cause of action for fraud does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because (1) it is duplicative of and arises from the same duty as Plaintiff’s breach of contract claim; (2) the fraud allegations are not pled with the requisite specificity.

Plaintiff asserts that the second cause of action is sufficiently pled because the broker is liable for the torts of its real estate salesman under the doctrine of respondeat superior. Plaintiff contends that the FAC’s allegations show the Siretskiy Defendants made false representations to Plaintiff, knew they were false, and never intended to repay Plaintiff.

“A person may not ordinarily recover in tort for the breach of duties that merely restate contractual obligations. Instead, courts will generally enforce the breach of a contractual promise through contract law, except when the actions that constitute the breach violate a social policy that merits the imposition of tort remedies.” (Stop Loss Ins. Brokers, Inc. v. Brown & Toland Medical Group (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1041, internal quotations omitted, quoting Aas v. Superior Court (2000) 24 Cal.4th 627, 643.)

The elements of fraud are: “(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184.) In California, fraud, including negligent misrepresentation, must be pleaded with specificity. (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.) “The particularity demands that a plaintiff plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469, quoting Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645, internal quotations omitted.)

The court finds Defendant’s arguments have merit. The FAC alleges that the Siretskiy Defendants “promised plaintiff before and on or about August 21, 2018, that his investment in real estate was safe and secure and would be returned to him with the guaranteed investment income within less than one year.” (FAC ¶ 28.) This is the exact same duty Plaintiff alleges for his breach of contract claim. (See id. at ¶¶ 21-22.) Plaintiff once again does not address this issue in his opposition.

The court notes that, while Plaintiff maintains the Siretskiy Defendants never intended to repay Plaintiff, this is not specifically alleged in the Complaint. (See Compl., ¶¶ 21-22.) The FAC also alleges “[t]he Siretskiy Defendants and the responsible broker, BBR, knew or should have known that plaintiff Ostrovskiy was a person of limited means and that he could not afford to make the $100,000 investment requested by defendants.” (FAC ¶ 32.)

The FAC further alleges “Defendant Siretskiy made representations to the plaintiff Ostrovskiy which were false and known by Siretskiy to be false when made. The representations made by defendant Siretskiy as agent for BBR were intended to cause reliance on each representations by plaintiff Ostrovskiy. These representations were reasonably relied upon by plaintiff Ostrovskiy to his detriment. These representations were: a. Your money is safe with me and will be returned in one year, guaranteed; b. I shall return your money with a substantial profit; c. I will make you a millionaire; d. I am invested in multimillion dollar real estate projects in the Los Angeles area; e. You should sell your house, rent your residence, and give me the proceeds of the sale of your house so I can make you a fortune; and f. I will return your investment with a substantial profit.” (FAC ¶ 11.) The FAC further alleges “[t]hese representations were made in writing in text messages to plaintiff and are attached to this First Amended Complaint and incorporated in this First Amended Complaint as Exhibit “A,” as well as in e-mails attached and incorporated in this First Amended Complaint as Exhibit “B.”” However, the allegations do not provide dates as to when these statements were made.

The FAC also alleges “[i]n fact, Siretskiy is a crook and a fraud, taking money from unsophisticated people who cannot afford to make the investment demanded of them on the pretense of being part of a massive investment real estate group when in fact he is not. The Siretskiy Defendants take the money of unsuspecting and somewhat naive immigrants to the United States and not only makes false promises of having a great return on income, never fully returns the principal of the transferred funds which constituted the business opportunity.” (FAC ¶ 14.)

However, these alleged facts are insufficient to state a cause of action for fraud because they are conclusory. Accordingly, the court also finds Plaintiff’s allegations are not pled with the requisite specificity for fraud.

The court therefore SUSTAINS Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (CCP § 430.10(e).) The court grants Plaintiff 30 days leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

            Defendant BBR moves to strike improper matters from the FAC filed by Plaintiff on the grounds that Plaintiff has not alleged facts supporting his claim for attorneys’ fees and punitive damages against Defendant.

            The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.) 

 

            The motion to strike is MOOT given that the Court granted leave to amend.

 

It is so ordered.

Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise

 

The court considered the petition. No opposition was received.

The court CONTINUES the petition to confirm minor’s compromise of claimant Jeremy Elijah Chavez Laynez, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Beatriz Laynez.

 

Background

This is a habitability case. Plaintiffs Beatriz Laynez, an individual; Emili Gabriela Chavez Laynez, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem; Beatriz Laynez; Jayleen Arlette Chavez Laynez, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Beatriz Laynez; and Jeremy Elijah Chavez-Laynez, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Beatriz Laynez filed this action against Defendants Alvarado Equities LLC, Robert Staback, as Trustee of The Rudolph and Grace Staback Revocable Inter Vivos Trust, and JNM Realty Inc. alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of Warranty of Habitability; (2) Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (3) Negligence; and (5) Breach of Contract.

Claimant Jeremy Elijah Chavez Laynez, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Beatriz Laynez filed the petition on May 6, 2024.

 

Discussion

            Claimant Jeremy Elijah Chavez Laynez, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Beatriz Laynez, petitions for approval of compromise of pending action.

Court approval is required to establish an enforceable settlement of a minor’s claim. (Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600, et seq.; CCP, § 372.) Indeed, until a petition for such approval is granted by the court, there is no final settlement, and any settlement agreement therefore is voidable. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-05.) Petitions for approval of the compromise of a pending action are governed by Cal. Rules of Court, Rules 7.950–7.955 and Probate Code §§ 2504, et seq. Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.950 provides that a petition “must be verified by the petitioner and must contain a full disclosure of all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise.” Rule 7.950 states that the Petition must be filed on Judicial Council Form MC-350.

In addition to approval of the settlement, attorney fees to be paid for representing the minor or incompetent must be approved by the court. (Fam. Code, § 6602; Prob. Code, § 3601.)  The court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney's fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.955(a)-(b).)

The court reviewed the petition and settlement and finds it fair and reasonable. The petition herein was properly filed on the Judicial Council Forms and is verified. (Petition, p. 10.) Petitioner properly include in sections 10 and 11 of the MC-350 form the total amount settled for all Plaintiffs, $50,000, and the amount paid to claimant, $2,500, by Defendants Alvarado Equities LLC and JNM Realty Inc. Sections 14-17 show that the claimant would receive a net payment of $1,613.82, after total attorney’s fees and expenses (other than medical). The petition indicates no medical expenses. (Id., § 12.)

The petition shows that claimant agreed to settle their claim arising out of Defendant’s alleged improper maintenance of the subject property, resulting in insect infestations, unsanitary conditions, damaged, and general dilapidation/improper maintenance. (Petition, § 5.) Claimant’s injuries include damage to personal property, personal injury, emotional distress, pain, anxiety, discomfort, annoyance, inconvenience, diminishment in the value of their leasehold, rent abatement, restitution of all rents paid. (Id., § 6.) Claimant did not receive any care and treatment for the injuries. (Id., § 7.) The claimant has recovered completely from the effects of the injuries, with no permanent injuries. (Id., § 8(a).)

The amount of attorney’s fees requested for claimant is $625. (Petition, § 13(a).) Hence, the amount of attorney’s fees is approximately 25 percent of the recovery for claimant. The petitioner is requesting costs of $261.18 for claimant. (Id., § 13(b).) Counsel has provided a declaration and the retainer fee agreement. The Court finds the attorney’s fees and costs reasonable.

However, it is unclear why Defendants Alvarado Equities LLC and JNM Realty Inc. are paying $18,250 in attorney fees to Plaintiffs’ counsel and how that is appropriate. (Petition, § 17(f).)

Based on the foregoing, the court CONTINUES the petition for counsel to explain the $18,250 in anticipated fees.

 

It is so ordered.
