DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 19STCV09659    Hearing Date: September 26, 2023    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

MEHDI YAHYANEJAD,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

1812 INVESTMENTS, LLC, a California Limited Liability Company; JOSEPH HARRISON SOARIS aka JOSEPH SOARIS, an Individual, GODSTIME EGHOSA ERHAHON aka GODDY ERHAHON, an Individual, RODEO REALTY INC dba RODEO REALTY BEVERLY HILLS; GREGORY DANIEL MEDSKER aka GREGORY MEDSKER, an Individual, AND DOES 1 THROUGH 100, INCLUSIVE,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          19STCV09659

 Hearing Date:   9/26/23

 Trial Date:        10/31/23

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff Mehdi Yahyanejad’s Motion to Compel re Defendant 1812 Investments, LLC’s Failure to Comply with Informal Discovery Conference Order, for an Award of Monetary Sanctions Against Defendant and its Counsel, and Issue and/or Evidentiary Sanctions Against Defendant.

 

Background

Plaintiff Mehdi Yahyanejad (Yahyanejad) sues Defendants 1812 Investments, LLC (1812 Investments), Joseph Harrison Soaris (Soaris), Godstime Eghosa Erhahon (Erhahon), Rodeo Realty Inc. (Rodeo Realty), Gregory Daniel Medsker (Medsker), and Does 1 through 100 pursuant to a March 20, 2019 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (3) Real Estate Seller’s Nondisclosure of Material Facts, (4) Intentional Fraud (Misrepresentation and Concealment), (5) Negligent Misrepresentation and Concealment, (6) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and (7) Negligence.

The claims arise from allegations that, among other things, on or about March 1, 2016, Plaintiff Yahyanejad purchased from 1812 Investments the real property known as 1553/1555 South Sierra Bonita Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90019 (Property) and that the Property was sold by 1812 Investments without disclosing construction work that had been performed on the Property without permits in 2015 or the defective conditions arising from that construction, i.e., windows that leaked when it rained. Plaintiff alleges not to have been aware of the window defects until 2017, and not to have been aware of the illegal construction performed on the Property until May 2018 when he received a Notice and Order to Comply from the City of Los Angeles that related to building violations in both units on the Property.

On May 9, 2023, the Court held an informal discovery conference (IDC) in this action pertaining to discovery that Plaintiff sought from 1812 Investments. Specifically, the IDC concerned two things. First, Plaintiffs sought an unequivocal confirmation from 1812 Investments that it had produced all documents responsive to certain inspection demands. The confirmation would be provided in an Inspection Demand Stipulation to be signed and submitted with the Court. Second, Plaintiff sought further responses to certain form interrogatories and requests for admission served by Plaintiff on 1812 Investments. At the IDC, “Defendant [1812 Investments] agree[d] to provide Plaintiff’s Counsel … further responses … [to this discovery] no later than June 5, 2023.”

On May 12, 2023, the parties filed a stipulation and proposed order informed by the outcome of the IDC. Motion for Prejudgment Interest

Legal Standard

Prejudgment interest compensates a plaintiff for the loss of the use of property or money during the period before a judgment is entered. (Segura v. McBride (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1041.) “It is commonly recognized that prejudgment interest represents the accretion of wealth that particular property could have produced during a period of loss.” (Newby v. Vroman (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 283, 289.) As such, “[it] is well established that prejudgment interest is not a cost, but an element of damages.” (North Oakland Medical Clinic v. Rogers (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 824, 830.) Prejudgment interest may be awarded even if it is not specifically authorized by the statute underlying the claims. (County of Solano v. Lionsgate Corp. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 741, 752.)

An award of prejudgment interest may be made where the claim is liquidated within the meaning of the statute, i.e., when the underlying damages are “certain” or “capable of being made certain by calculation” and the right to recover such damages is vested in the plaintiff on a particular day. (Civ. Code § 3287, subd. (a); Duale v. Mercedes-Benz USA, LLC (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 718, 728, citing Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1154, 1172.) A right to recover damages becomes vested at the time the damages at issue are sustained. (See, e.g., Williams v. Graham (1948) 83 Cal.App.2d 649, 653 [Where purchaser relying on fraudulent representations of vendor’s ability to convey, incurred rental liability for six months and made down payment upon the property, the right to recoup became vested on the day on which each separate amount was expended].)

“Interest is recoverable from ‘the time of breach where the amount of money due is liquidated, or from the time it becomes liquidated.’ [Citation.]” (Moreno v. Jessup Buena Vista Dairy (1976) 50 Cal.App.3d 438; accord. Maurice L. Bein, Inc. v. Housing Authority of City of Los Angeles (1958) 157 Cal.App.2d 670, 686 [Interest commences on the first day there exists both a breach and a liquidated claim].)

An award of prejudgment interest may also be made as to unliquidated amounts after judgment, beginning on a date prior to judgment, provided that such awards are discretionary and, in deciding whether to exercise its discretion, the court “consider[s] the circumstances, realizing a party cannot pay the amount due until it is determined what that amount was.” (Civ. Code § 3287, subd. (b); Union Pacific Railroad Company v. Santa Fe Pacific Pipelines, Inc. (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 134, 203 [quoted language].)

In absence of a contractual rate of interest, the rate of prejudgment interest is 10% per annum. (Civ. Code §3289, subd. (b).)

Order Granting Award of Prejudgment Interest: GRANTED, in Part.

Plaintiff Lin brings this motion “seeking an award of prejudgment interest in her favor at the rate of ten percent (10%) per annum on the verdict of $76,323.66 from the date of the breach on October 31, 2017 through September 26, 2023.” (Mot., Notice, p. 2.) Plaintiff argues that at trial, the Court determined that the breach here occurred on October 31, 2017, and that using an interest rate of 10% per annum for breach of contract and a judgment of $76,323.66 in favor of Plaintiff, Plaintiff is entitled to $20.91 in interest per day, which, between October 31, 2017 and September 26, 2023 (the date of this motion) amounts to $45,102.87. (Mot., pp. 3-4; see 8/25/23 Minutes, pp. 2-3 [“Court awards $76,323.66 [to] plaintiff for breach of contract against defendant” Chang and “finds the date of the breach of cont[r]act [w]as October 31, 2017”].)

In opposition, Defendant Chang argues two points. First, Chang argues that interest should be awarded pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 685.020 and that such an award, per that statute, can only accrue until after a judgment. Second, Chang argues that Plaintiff should not be entitled to almost six years of prejudgment interest because this matter would have proceeded to trial on June 10, 2022 but was continued because Plaintiff’s counsel appeared at trial without Plaintiff, after which a change of judicial officer in Department 40 caused a further continuance. (Opp’n, pp. 1-2.)

In reply, Plaintiff Lin argues that section 685.020 does not apply to prejudgment interest and that Plaintiff did not intentionally cause trial delays because trials are usually continued in litigation and because, among other things, on June 10, 2022, Defendant Chang did not object to a continuance of trial. (Reply, pp. 3-4.)

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Lin.

The Court first notes that, as argued by Plaintiff Lin, Code of Civil Procedure section 685.020 does not apply to prejudgment interest. (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.110 [“Nothing in this chapter [i.e., Code Civ. Proc., §§ 685.010-685.110] affects the law relating to prejudgment interest”].) Instead, the statutory section cited by Defendant Chang applies to postjudgment interest on a judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.020, subd. (a) [Except as provided in subdivision (b) [relating to a money judgment payable in installments], interest commences to accrue on a money judgment on the date of entry of the judgment].) Prejudgment interest necessarily involves something different: interest that accrues prior to the entry of judgment, not after.

The Court next determines that Plaintiff is properly entitled to prejudgment interest for dates between October 31, 2017 and the date of this hearing because such a claim is liquidated within the meaning of the statute. Plaintiff Lin secured a non-jury trial award of $76.323.66, a sum certain. (8/25/23 Minutes, p. 2 [“Court awards $76,323.66 [to] plaintiff for breach of contract against defendant”].) Moreover, Plaintiff Lin’s right to recover such damages vested on October 31, 2017, the date of the breach involving failure to pay 50% of the liabilities agreed upon by these parties. (8/25/23 Minutes, p. 3 [“Court finds the date of the breach of cont[r]act [w]as October 31, 2017”].)

The Court also determines that the delays in trial in this action do not constitute grounds to reduce any prejudgment interest award. Defendant Chang cites no controlling or persuasive authority to support his argument. (See Opp’n, p. 3.) Moreover, even if such authority exists, the Court finds that the circumstances described by Defendant Chang do not exceed the reasonable bounds of litigation—that is, trials being continued and changes in the judicial officer constitute regular occurrences in litigation—such that Defendant Chang’s argument lacks merit.

The Court last determines that the proper computation of the interest is $76,323.66, times 10% percent per annum, yielding an interest of $7,632.366 per annum, or, by dividing by 365 days per year, $20.91 per day, which is then multiplied by the number of days between October 31, 2017 and the date of this hearing (September 26, 2023) for a total of 2,156 days not including the final day, yielding $45,083.24 in owed prejudgment interest. The interest rate of 10% per annum is applied based on Civil Code section 3289, subdivision (b), and based on a lack of evidence before the Court that Plaintiff Lin and Defendant Chang agreed to a different interest rate.

Plaintiff Lin’s motion is thus GRANTED, in Part, in the amount of $45,083.24. 

Conclusion

Plaintiff Chia Ying Lin’s Motion for Award of Prejudgment Interest is GRANTED, in Part.

Defendant Keny Chang is ORDERED to remit payment of $45,083.24 to Plaintiff Chia Ying Lin within 90 days of this ruling.

Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication

Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact for trial or that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) A party may also seek summary adjudication of select causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty, which may be made by a standalone motion or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and proceeds in all procedural respects like a motion for summary judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (f)(1)-(2), (t); see Lilienthal & Fowler v. Superior Court (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1854-1855, questioned by dictum in Bagley v. TRW, Inc. (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1092, 1094, fn. 2 [finding that summary adjudication may be granted as to separate factual grounds supporting a claim stated as a single count because the separate grounds state a separate cause of action].) The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make prima facie showing no triable material fact issues. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) This burden on summary judgment or adjudication “is more properly one of persuasion rather than proof, since he must persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find, and not to prove any such fact to the satisfaction of the court itself as though it were sitting as the trier of fact.” (Id. at p. 850, fn. 11.) If the moving party meets this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a rebuttal prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Id. at p. 849.) “[I]n ruling on motions for summary judgment courts are to ‘“liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”’ [Citations].” (Cheal v. El Camino Hospital (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 736, 760.)

(Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 1, 173:6-17.) Other cites from the Neidlinger deposition reiterate this representation. (See Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 1, 43:1-44:7 [Neidlinger confirming she asked AmWins for insurance coverage in favor of Plaintiffs, with the same terms as the Lexington policy, but at a better price].) A copy of the Lexington quote provided to AmWins Access by Defendant Niedlinger is attached as Exhibit 6 to the Amended Compendium of Evidence. Other evidence also confirms that DeWitt asked AmWins to secure insurance limits for Plaintiffs matching those previously provided to Plaintiffs by Lexington. (Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 8, 9.)

UMF No. 9 refers to evidence showing that “[t]he AmWINS quote for the policy that it procured for DeWitt to provide to Plaintiffs contained the same coverage that Plaintiffs had under their prior policy issued by Lexington Insurance Company.” (Mot., Amended Sep. St., UMF No. 9, referencing parts of Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Exs. 1, 3, 5, 7.) Exhibit 5 shows the Lloyd’s policy that was secured by AmWins Access, which provided for “[d]welling coverage”—i.e., a replacement cost—of $632,141. (Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 5, COE p. 137, Coverage Limits, § 1.) Exhibit 7 shows DeWitt informing AmWins Access that the replacement cost limit for the Property under the Lexington policy was $632,000. (Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 7, COE p. 225.)

The Court finds that this evidence carries AmWins Access’s burden on summary adjudication. If AmWins Access was only asked to secure homeowner’s coverage matching that provided in the Lexington policy previously held by the Katleman Plaintiffs—i.e., $632,141 in replacement cost insurance by Lloyds, where the same insurance limit under Lexington was $632,000—then it is unclear how or why AmWins Access owed a duty to the Katleman Plaintiffs to secure insurance in a greater amount than $632,141.

In opposition, the Katleman Plaintiffs make varied arguments for why AmWins Access owed a duty to Plaintiffs to provide insurance coverage that would permit Plaintiffs to completely rebuild their home in the event of its total destruction. (Opp’n, pp. 11-13; see Opp’n, Nickerson Decl., Ex. 27 [Ptizer Construction Company, Inc.’s January 11, 2020 Estimate showing an approximate cost of $2,287,417.88 to rebuild the Property].)

None of these arguments, however, address AmWins Access’s position that AmWins access was explicitly asked to provide insurance coverage in the same amount as the Lexington policy, including the replacement cost term. Even Plaintiffs’ argument that AmWins Access made an inspection of the Property and determined that the replacement cost limit of $632,141 was “appropriate and adequate” does not show that AmWins Access had contractually agreed to provide guaranteed replacement cost insurance.

Moreover, Plaintiffs’ evidence to show that they were informed by AmWins Access that the replacement cost coverage in the Lloyd’s insurance policy would suffice to completely rebuild their home in the event of a total loss does not actually stand for that proposition or is unconvincing for lack of foundation. (See Opp’n, AMF Nos. 42, 135, 138.)

The AmWins Access PMQ deposition attached to the opposition shows testimony providing that the investigation performed by AmWins Access in setting the policy limits is used “to determine whether or not the [replacement cost coverage] limits are justified” and “to make sure that the insured is not overinsured.” (See Opp’n, Nickerson Decl., Ex. 7, AmWins PMQ Deposition, 24:23-25:4 [referenced in Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF No. 42.) Whether the amount of coverage is justified and whether Plaintiffs were overinsured involve questions vastly different than whether the replacement cost coverage would allow Plaintiffs to completely rebuild their home. As a result, any argument in the opposition relying on AmWins Access’s inspection of the Property in setting policy limits is unavailing because if the purpose of the inspection was to ensure that the replacement cost coverage limit in fact issued by Lloyd’s was indeed justified and that Plaintiffs were not overinsured, then there was no duty created as to guaranteed replacement cost coverage.

As for the Katleman Plaintiffs’ declaration, the Court finds that the declaration provides insufficient foundation for the reasons why Plaintiffs would appropriately be under the impression that the replacement cost coverage in the Lloyd’s insurance policy would allow them to completely rebuild their home in the event of a total loss. (See Opp’n, Katleman Decl., ¶ 6 [referenced in Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF No. 135]; see also Evidentiary Objections discussion supra, sustaining objection to this statement.) Moreover, AmWins Access’s alleged “represent[ation] … that the inspection was adequate, accurate, and proper and that [Plaintiffs] had enough insurance to cover the cost to rebuild [their] home in the event of a total loss, and that the insurance that we had was appropriate and adequate” (1) lacks sufficient foundation as to how and by whom these alleged representations were made by AmWins Access and (2) are undercut by the deposition of AmWins Access’s PMQ, which frames the inspection as relating to concerns of insurance coverage justification and overinsuredness of Plaintiffs, not complete reconstruction of the home in the event of a total loss. (See Opp’n, Katleman Decl., ¶ 10 [referenced in Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF No. 138]; see Evid. Code § 403 subd. (a)(4).)

Accordingly, the Court determines that Plaintiffs fail to carry their burden as to duty.

Summary adjudication of the TAC’s first cause of action is GRANTED.

B. Lack of Breach and Lack of Causation

Having granted summary adjudication on duty grounds, the Court need not reach arguments based on lack of breach or lack of causation.

II.

TAC, Third Cause of Action, Intentional or Negligent Misrepresentation: GRANTED.

Intentional misrepresentation involves (1) a knowingly false representation by the defendant, (2) made with an intent to deceive or induce reliance, (3) justifiable reliance by the plaintiff, and (4) resulting damages. (Service by Medallion, Inc. v. Clorox Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1807, 1816.)

Negligent misrepresentation involves (1) an assertion, as a fact, of that which is not true, (2) by one who has no reasonable ground for believing it to be true, (3) made with intent to induce the recipient to alter his position to his injury or his risk, (4) with justifiable reliance on the representation, and (5) resulting damage. (B.L.M. v. Sabo & Deitsch (1997) 55 Cal.App.4th 823, 834.)

The TAC’s third cause of action alleges misrepresentation against AmWins Access, AmWins Ins., and Does 1-25 on various grounds, including that: (1) these Defendants “represented to Plaintiffs that the Property was adequately and properly insured in the event of loss and/or damage to the Property, that Plaintiffs had adequate and proper coverage for loss of use of the Property in the event of loss and/or damage to the Property based on the appraisal and/or their own personal evaluation of the property, and that a proper and adequate inspection/appraisal of the Property had been conducted by E&S INSPECTIONS and/or others, and that the inspection/appraisal conducted by E&S INSPECTIONS was accurate, adequate and proper”; and (2) “said Defendants presented plaintiffs with a summary of coverage to sign at the inception of the policy which did not include co-insurance provisions and also represented that there was a loss extension on both the dwelling and the other structures.” These representations are alleged to have been made by and through Cody Kaintz on behalf of these Defendants, specifically in May, June, October, and November 2018. (TAC, ¶¶ 35-36.)

The Katleman Plaintiffs clarify in their opposition that the allegations that “Defendants presented plaintiffs with a summary of coverage to sign at the inception of the policy which did not include co-insurance provisions and also represented that there was a loss extension on both the dwelling and the other structures” are allegations strictly relating to the DeWitt Defendants, for which reason such representation “is not germane to the instant motion.” (Opp’n, p. 19, fn. 12.) The Court therefore limits its discussion to the “adequate and proper” allegations in the third cause of action because Plaintiffs’ concession permits summary adjudication of the loss extension allegations.

AmWins Access argues that summary adjudication should be granted against the TAC’s third cause of action because: (1) AmWins Access “never represented that the Lloyd’s policy provided ‘adequate and proper coverage’”; (2) AmWins Access “never represented that the inspection and resulting valuation conducted by E&S Inspections was ‘accurate, adequate, and proper’”; (3) AmWins Access “never represented that the policy provided a ‘loss extension’ on the structures that were insured”; and (4) “no one, not DeWitt nor AmWINS, ever represented to Plaintiffs before the policy was issued (or even after the policy was issued) that the Lloyd’s policy provided ‘adequate and proper coverage’ in order to induce Plaintiffs to purchase the Lloyds policy.” (Mot., pp. 15-16 [referencing Mot., Amended Sep. St., UMF Nos. 14, 15, 17, 18, 19, 20].)

In support of UMF No. 14—purporting to advance evidence showing that “AmWINS never represented to Plaintiffs or to anyone that the quote it obtained was for “appropriate and adequate” coverage for Plaintiffs”—AmWins Access refers to the deposition of Cody Kaintz, i.e., the AmWins Access employee that the TAC alleges made the false representations on behalf of AmWins Access, AmWins Ins., and Doe Defendants. (Mot., Amended Sep. St., UMF No. 14 [referencing Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 3, 133:23-134:17]; see TAC, ¶ 36.) A review of Mr. Kaintz’s deposition at the cited passage shows that Mr. Kaintz testified to the effect that “we [i.e., Mr. Kaintz and AmWins Access generally] never interacted with [the Katleman Plaintiffs]” and instead communicated “only with DeWitt.” (Mot., Amended Comp. Evid., Ex. 3, 133:23-134:6.)

The Court finds that this evidence carries AmWins Access’s burden on summary adjudication.

If AmWins Access and its employees never communicated with the Katleman Plaintiffs, then it is unclear how AmWins Access could have made misrepresentations to the Katleman Plaintiffs. Moreover, the TAC specifically alleges that Cody Kaintz made the misrepresentations at issue, but Mr. Kaintz’s deposition specifically provides that he never communicated with the Katleman Plaintiffs, thus contradicting the 36th paragraph in the TAC.

In opposition, the Katleman Plaintiffs argue that AmWins Access did make several representations to Plaintiffs that were untrue, i.e.: (1) “[a]t the time of binding, AmWINS misrepresented to the Katlemans that they had replacement cost coverage for their home, and that the cost to replace their home in the event of a total loss was $632,141[] (PSS 7-8, 10, 11, 113, 114, 135)”; (2) AmWins Access “maintained that their review was correct and once again misrepresented to the Katlemans that they had replacement cost coverage for their home, and that the cost to replace their home in the event of a total loss was $632,141” “(PSS 7-8, 10, 55-109, 81-82, 84, 87, 95, 103, 106, 107, 108, 109, 113,113, 121-128, 130-138)”; (3) AmWins Access represented “that the policy limits were ‘adequate[]’ (PSS 126)”; and (4) by failing to modify the dwelling portion/Coverage A portion of the policy, “AmWINS was representing to the Katlemans that the coverage for the dwelling was adequate[] (PSS 113).”

In reply, AmWins Access argues that “Plaintiffs offer no evidence that AmWINS ever represented to them that the policy was guaranteed to rebuild their home no matter what, i.e., that the policy provided guaranteed replacement coverage or that the cost to replace their home was guaranteed to be no more than the $632,141 (plus the additional 50% extended coverage) that the policy provided.” (Reply, p. 11 [emphasis removed].)

The Court finds that the Katleman Plaintiffs’ position and their supporting evidence do not carry their burden on summary adjudication.

The Court briefly notes that Plaintiffs’ separate statement refers to Cody Kaintz—the person the TAC alleges made the alleged misrepresentations to the Katleman Plaintiffs—primarily for the purpose of citing to the Kaintz deposition and, to a lesser degree, for the purpose of summarizing Cody Kaintz’s conduct in relation to the insurance policy. However, the separate statement makes no reference to representations made directly by Mr. Kaintz to the Katleman Plaintiffs. (See Opp’n, Sep. St.) At most, the separate statement makes references to indirect representations made by AmWins Access, which the Court discusses and finds insufficient below. (See Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF Nos. 113, 114, 126, 135.) The opposition separate statement therefore does not support paragraph 36 of the TAC.

However, “courts are encouraged to take a liberal approach in determining the scope of the pleadings, so long as those pleadings provide adequate notice to the opposing party of the theories on which relief is generally being sought.” (Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 1200, 1211.) Here, the TAC does plead, in relation to reliance, that all Defendants generally made representations of adequate coverage relating to the Property. (TAC, ¶ 40.) Thus, even though “[s]ummary judgment proceedings usually are limited to the issues framed by the pleadings” (Jones v. Awad, supra, at p. 1211), the Court considers whether the opposition sufficiently evidences, for summary adjudication purposes, that AmWins Access directly made representations to the Katleman Plaintiffs regarding the insurance coverage on the Property.

After review, the Court determines that the Katleman Plaintiffs fail in their opposition to reference any actual direct representations from AmWins Access to the Katleman Plaintiffs. The Katleman Plaintiffs cite a number of AMFs in support of misrepresentation. (See Opp’n, p. 19 [referencing Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF Nos. 7, 8, 10, 11, 113, 114, 126, 135].) Many of these AMFs do not relate to any representations by AmWins Access to the Katleman Plaintiffs and instead involve the process of issuing the insurance policy at issue to Plaintiffs. (See Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF Nos. 7, 8, 10, 11.) The remaining AMFs cited by Plaintiffs for the purpose of misrepresentation cite only to evidence purporting to show that because AmWins determined that the replacement cost provision in the Lloyd’s insurance policy was adequate in the amount of $632,141 and did not make any changes to this figure after its inspection of the Property or upon request, AmWins Access misrepresented the amount of money that would be necessary to completely replace the Property. (Opp’n, Sep. St., AMF Nos. 113, 114, 126, 135.) However, the Court determines that such conduct by AmWins Access does not involve a representation made by one party to another on which the latter party relied, but rather, involves terms of a contract that sounds in breach of contract more so than fraud.

To the extent that the Katleman Plaintiff’s declaration provides that “AmWINS and Dewitt Stern represented to [Plaintiffs] that the cost needed to rebuild a structure similar to [Plaintiffs’] home in the event of a total loss was $632,141” (Opp’n, Katleman Decl., ¶ 6; see also Evidentiary Objections discussion supra, sustaining objection to this statement), the Court finds that such evidence is insufficient for lack of foundation. The Katleman Declaration simply fails to provide sufficient information for the Court to determine who made such a representation or when and how that representation was made.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication of the TAC’s third cause of action.

III.

TAC, Sixth Cause of Action, Breach of Contract: GRANTED.

“A contract is a voluntary and lawful agreement, by competent parties, for a good consideration, to do or not to do a specified thing.” (Robinson v. Magee (1858) 9 Cal. 81, 83.) “To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) Implicit in the element of damage is that the defendant’s breach caused the plaintiff’s damage.” (Troyk v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1352, italics omitted.)

The sixth cause of action alleges that the Katleman Plaintiffs entered a contract with Defendants AmWins Access, AmWins Ins., and Does 1 through 25 pursuant to which these “Defendants provided an insurance quote to Plaintiffs which procure replacement cost coverage for the Property at 150% of value, as well as loss of use coverage,” which Plaintiffs accepted, only for these Defendants to breach the contract “by failing to obtain appropriate and adequate insurance for the full value of the Property in the event of loss and/or damage to the Property, and/or failing to obtain appropriate and adequate insurance for loss of use of the Property in the event of loss and/or damage to the Property.” (TAC, ¶¶ 66-68.)

The Court adopts its discussion in Sections I and II to find that there is no triable issue of material fact as to whether AmWins Access breached any alleged contract between the parties.

Even if such a contract exists, for summary adjudication purposes, AmWins Access was instructed by Dewitt to secure replacement cost insurance coverage of approximately $632,000 to match the Lexington policy, such that, there does not appear to be a contractual term to provide to the Katleman Plaintiffs “appropriate and adequate insurance for the full value of the Property in the event of loss and/or damage to the Property.” (See Section I supra; see also Opp’n, p. 17 [“AmWINS breached the agreement it had with the Katlemans because it did not provide the coverage that was promised under the contract, i.e. the amount of coverage needed to rebuild the home in the event of a total loss”].)

Moreover, to the extent that there was an inspection of the Property, the Katleman Plaintiffs’ own evidence provides that such inspection was for the purposes of determining whether the replacement cost coverage provided by Lloyds—$632,141—was justified and whether Plaintiffs were overinsured, not to determine the amount of monies required to completely rebuild the home in the event of a total loss. (See Section II supra; see also Opp’n, p. 17 [“AmWINS breached the agreement it had with the Katlemans because it did not provide the coverage that was promised under the contract, i.e. the amount of coverage needed to rebuild the home in the event of a total loss”].)

Finally, AmWINS provides testimony that there was no higher coverage that AmWINS was able to offer at the time because Lloyds was not willing to issue a policy with a higher coverage than what was available in the quote provided without a new valuation by a third-party evaluator. (UF 10-12.) Although Plaintiffs dispute this in their separate statement with cites to dozens of other “undisputed facts,” the Court cannot locate any specific piece of evidence to refute this. It appears that within a few weeks of Ms. Kaintz for AmWINS informing the broker that “[t]he only way the carrier will consider increasing the dwelling value is if another carrier generated inspection replacement cost estimator is provided,” the Woolsey Fire burned Plaintiffs’ house down. (Compare Exhibit 25, 26.)

Insofar as the Katleman Plaintiffs argue that “AmWINS was able to increase coverage without the involvement of Lloyds,” this argument is unavailing for two reasons. (See Opp’n, p. 18.) First, this argument relies on the purpose of the inspection report on the Property, which is an unavailing argument as described in the preceding paragraphs. Second, Plaintiffs fail to cite a contractual term between themselves and AmWins Access requiring that coverage be increased—or even that an internal inspection rather than only-outer-inspection be performed on the Property.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication of the TAC’s sixth cause of action.

IV.

TAC, Seventh Cause of Action, Breach of the Contractual Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing: GRANTED.

To prevail on a cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, the plaintiff must prove: (1) the existence of a contract between plaintiff and defendant; (2) plaintiff performed his contractual obligations or was excused from performing them; (3) the conditions requiring defendant’s performance had occurred; (4) the defendant unfairly interfered with the plaintiff’s right to receive the benefits of the contract; and (5) the plaintiff was harmed by the defendant’s conduct. (Merced Irr. Dist. v. County of Mariposa (E.D. Cal. 2013) 941 F.Supp.2d 1237, 1280 [discussing California law].) Allegations must demonstrate defendant’s conduct for failure or refusal to discharge contractual responsibilities was a conscious and deliberate act, not an honest mistake, bad judgment or negligence. (Ibid.) “‘[T]he implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is limited to assuring compliance with the express terms of the contract and cannot be extended to create obligations not contemplated by the contract.’” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank Nat. Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 206 [quoting Pasadena Live v. City of Pasadena (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1089, 1094].)

The TAC’s seventh cause of action alleges, in relevant part, that the Katleman “Plaintiffs suffered … harm because the Property was underinsured due to the intentional, negligent, careless, and/or reckless acts of the Defendants[] …, as well as said Defendants’ breach of contract,” constituting a breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing insofar as Defendants’ conduct “unfairly interfere[d] with the right of [Plaintiffs] to receive the benefits of the contract.” (TAC, ¶¶ 73, 77; see TAC, ¶¶ 72-81.)

The Court adopts the discussion in Sections I to III to determine that there is no triable issue of material fact as to whether the Katleman Plaintiffs were entitled to guaranteed replacement cost coverage—monies allowing Plaintiffs to completely rebuild the Property after it was destroyed in the Woolsey fire—for which reason AmWins access could not have “unfairly interfered with the plaintiff’s right to receive the benefit of the contract.” (Merced Irr. Dist. v. County of Mariposa, supra, 941 F.Supp.2d at p. 1280.)

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication of the TAC’s seventh cause of action. 

Conclusion

Defendant AmWins Access Insurance Services, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is GRANTED.

Petition to Transfer Structured Settlement Rights

Legal Standard

A transfer of structured settlement payment rights is void unless a court reviews and approves the transfer and finds that (a) the transfer of the structured settlement payment rights is fair and reasonable and in the best interest of the payee, taking into account the welfare and support of his or her dependents, and (b) the transfer complies with the requirements of Insurance Code Sections 10134 to 10139.5, will not contravene other applicable law, and that the court has reviewed and approved the transfer as provided in Section 10139.5. (See Ins. Code, § 10137, subds. (a)-(b).)

Section 10136 Requirements

No direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights by a payee to which Section 10136 applies shall be effective, and no structured settlement obligor or annuity issuer shall be required to make any payment directly or indirectly to a transferee, unless all of the provisions of Section 10136 of the Insurance Code are satisfied. (Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (a).)

First, the transfer agreement satisfies the disclosure form requirements set out in Insurance Code section 10136, subdivision (b) because the transfer agreement is accompanied by a disclosure addressed to J.T. and the disclosure contains all the statutorily required language of section 10136, subdivision (b). (See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (b); see also 9/25/23 Supp. Filings, Amended Ex. A, California Disclosure Statement.)

(See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (c); see also 8/21/23 Amended Petition, Ex. A, Absolute Assignment Agreement and Security Agreement, pp. 1-3 at §§ B.1-B.12 and 8 [right to cancel].)

Third, the transfer agreement does not violate Section 10138 as none of the contents prohibited by this statutory section are contained in the transfer agreement. (See Ins. Code, § 10136, subd. (d); see also 8/21/23 Amended Petition, Ex. A, Absolute Assignment Agreement and Security Agreement.)
