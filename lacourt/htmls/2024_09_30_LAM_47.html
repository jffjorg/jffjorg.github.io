DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 19STCV26804    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 30, 2024               DISMISSAL: January 28, 2022

                                                          

CASE:                         Felipe Padron Alvarado, Co-Trustee of the Felipe Padron Alvarado And Maria Lucia Padron Living Trust, et al. v. Jaime Renteria

 

CASE NO.:                 19STCV26804           

 

MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Felipe Padron Alvarado & Maria Lucia Padron

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 09/24/24

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         07/30/19: Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This was an action for partition of real property and dissolution of partnership. On January 28, 2022, the parties reached a settlement and dismissed the action, with the Court retaining jurisdiction to enforce the settlement.

 

Plaintiffs move to enforce the settlement agreement.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Enforce Settlement is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiffs move to enforce the settlement agreement.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Enforcement of settlement agreements is governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. This statute provides, in relevant part:

 

If the parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 664.6(a).) The Court is empowered under this section to resolve reasonable disputes over the terms of a settlement. (Machado v. Myers (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 779, 795.) The Court does not insert provisions into the parties’ agreement, but applies the rules of contractual interpretation to interpretation of the settlement. (Id. at 792.) When extrinsic evidence is necessary, the Court may decide the motion on declarations alone. (Richardson v. Richardson (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 91, 97.)

 

Analysis

 

            On January 28, 2022, the parties entered into a Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release of Claims to dispose of the subject property and apportion the proceeds from sale, thereby resolving the disputes asserted in the Complaint. (See Plaintiffs’ Exh. A.) The Court entered dismissal of the Complaint pursuant to that agreement and retained jurisdiction by agreement of the parties pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. (See January 28, 2022 Minute Order.)

 

            Counsel for Plaintiffs states that although the subject property was listed for sale, the property has not yet been sold. (Declaration of Frank A. Sanzo ISO Mot. lines 10-11.) Defendant Renteria passed away on October 5, 2022, and his spouse was appointed administrator of the estate. (Id. lines 11:13.) Plaintiffs appear to argue that Defendant’s successor-in-interest has not complied with the Settlement, but do not specify how estate has breached the agreement. The Court therefore finds that Plaintiffs have not demonstrated a basis for an order enforcing the settlement.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Enforce Settlement is DENIED.

 

            Moving Parties to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

 

Dated:  September 30, 2024                           ___________________________________

                                                                                    Theresa M. Traber

                                                                                    Judge of the Superior Court

DEMURRER TO ANSWER

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Iulia Robinson

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): No response on eCourt as of 09/24/24

Plaintiff demurs to the answer jointly filed by Defendants Kittrich Corporation and Robert Friedland.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff’s Demurrer to the Answer is OVERRULED as to the First, Seventeenth, Eighteenth, Twentieth, and Twenty-Second through Forty-Fifth Affirmative Defenses and otherwise SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff demurs to the answer jointly filed by Defendants Kittrich Corporation and Robert Friedland as to each of the forty-five affirmative defenses asserted in the answer.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 430.20 provides:

 

A party against whom an answer has been filed may object, by demurrer as provided in Section 430.30, to the answer upon any one or more of the following grounds:

 

(a)  The answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense.

 

(b)  The answer is uncertain. As used in this subdivision, “uncertain” includes ambiguous and unintelligible.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 430.20.)

 

            Regarding the standards for pleading an affirmative defense:

 

 (1) In General. An affirmative defense must be pleaded in the same manner as if the facts were set forth in a complaint. In other words, the general requirement of stating the ultimate facts applies and, where particularity in pleading is necessary in a complaint, it is equally necessary in an affirmative defense involving the issue. (Bruck v. Tucker (1871) 42 C. 346, 352; Greiss v. State Inv. & Ins. Co. (1893) 98 C. 241, 244, 33 P. 195; Bradbury v. Higginson (1914) 167 C. 553, 557, 140 P. 254 [if matter set up is equitable cause of action, answer must contain all averments essential to statement of cause of action as such]; see 19B Am.Jur. P.P. Forms (2007 ed.), Pleading, §85 et seq.)

 

(2) Argumentative Denials. The affirmative form in which the defendant phrases his or her answering averments does not make them new matter. If they merely contradict essential allegations of the complaint, they are simply denials in affirmative form (“argumentative denials”). (Frisch v. Caler (1862) 21 C. 71, 74; Goddard v. Fulton (1863) 21 C. 430, 436; Rancho Santa Margarita v. Vail (1938) 11 C.2d 501, 543, 81 P.2d 533; Jolley v. Clemens (1938) 28 C.A.2d 55, 65, 82 P.2d 51, supra, §1077.)

 

(6 Witkin Cal. Proc. Plead § 1122.)

 

            Affirmative defenses that are mere legal conclusions are insufficient:

 

The . . . answer alleged merely: “Plaintiff’s claims are barred in whole or in part by the doctrine of waiver,” and “Plaintiff's causes of action, or some of them, are barred by the doctrine of laches because plaintiff unreasonably delayed bringing suit, causing defendants to reasonably rely on the status quo.”

 

These affirmative defenses consist of legal conclusions that could survive neither a demurrer nor a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Citations omitted.)

 

(Westly v. Board of Administration (2003) 105 Cal. App. 4th 1095, 1117.) Rather than being “proffered in the form of terse legal conclusions,” affirmative defenses must plead facts “averred as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and are alleged in the complaint.” (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384.)

 

Furthermore, a demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a pleading is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. (See Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the pleading is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond--i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.  (See Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) "The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts."  (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the demurrer and file a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) However, an insufficient meet and confer process is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Haroon Manjlai in support of the demurrer states that counsel for Plaintiff met and conferred with Defendants’ counsel electronically on June 5, 2024 and telephonically on June 7, 2024, to no avail. (Declaration of Haroon Manjlai ISO Demurrer ¶¶ 4-5; Exhs. B-C.) Plaintiff has therefore satisfied the statutory meet and confer requirements.

 

Defective Notice of Demurrer

 

            Although Plaintiff purports to demur to each of the forty-five affirmative defenses alleged in the Answer, the Demurrer itself only identifies a subset of the affirmative defenses as deficient for failure to state claims or uncertainty. Specifically, the Demurrer identifies the first (Demurrer p.2:25-3:20), second (p.3:21-24); fourth (p.3:25-28), fifth (p.4-1-4), sixth (p.4:5-8), seventh (p.4:9-12); eighth (p.4:13-5), ninth (p.4:16-19), tenth (p.4:20-23); eleventh (p.4:24-27); twelfth (p.4:28-5:2), thirteenth (p.5:3-6), fourteenth (p.5:7-10), fifteenth (p.5:11-15), sixteenth (p.5:16-19), seventeenth (p.5:20-23), eighteenth (p.5:24-27), nineteenth (p.5:28-6:2), twentieth (p.6:3-6) and twenty-first (p.6:7-9-6) affirmative defenses as deficient for failure to state claims and uncertainty, but does not assert similar claims as to the other affirmative defenses. As Plaintiff did not properly give notice of a challenge to any of the other affirmative defenses as required, the Court will confine its analysis only to the affirmative defenses listed above. (See Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1320(a).) The Court will not consider Plaintiff’s arguments on other grounds or affirmative defenses as they are not asserted in the demurrer itself.

This defense, mislabeled as an affirmative defense, alleges that Plaintiff’s causes of action fail because they have failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for each of the causes of action asserted. Loskutoff (2019) 362 F.Supp. 3d 804, 818.) It is not an affirmative defense that relies on the pleading of new factual allegations, so it is not subject to a demurrer that challenges the sufficiency of the underlying allegations. Accordingly, the demurrer to this defense is not well-taken and is OVERRULED.

21.  Uncertainty

 

            Plaintiff demurs to each of the defenses addressed above for uncertainty. The Court rejects Plaintiff’s contentions. Even the affirmative defenses which are defective, as addressed above, are not so deficient as to meet the much stricter standard for uncertainty.

 

Leave to Amend

 

            When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) California law imposes the burden on the party who filed the pleading to demonstrate the manner in which they can amend their pleadings to state their claims.  (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) “Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the [pleading] shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given." (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.)

 

            Here, Defendants have not shown the manner in which they could amend the answer to properly state their defenses against Plaintiff’s claims. However, in light of the liberal standard for permitting amendment of the pleadings, and the defects in the answer arising merely from a paucity of alleged facts, the Court will exercise its discretion to grant leave to amend the answer.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Demurrer to the Answer is OVERRULED as to the First, Seventeenth, Eighteenth, Twentieth, and Twenty-Second through Forty-Fifth Affirmative Defenses and otherwise SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

BASTA, Inc.’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED.

 

BASTA, Inc. is awarded attorney’s fees in the amount of $35,970, to be disbursed from the interpled funds currently deposited with the Court.

 

The balance of the funds is to be disbursed to Plaintiff Jenna King.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

BASTA, Inc., former counsel for Plaintiff Jenna King, moves for an award of attorney’s fees from interpled funds in the amount of $35,970.

 

BASTA’s Evidentiary Objections

 

            BASTA asserts evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Thomas F. Mortimer in support of Plaintiff’s opposition to this motion. The Court rules on these objections as follows:

 

Objection No. 1: SUSTAINED for lack of personal knowledge. (Evid. Code § 702(a).)

 

Objection No. 2: SUSTAINED for lack of personal knowledge. (Evid. Code § 702(a).)

 

Objection No. 3: SUSTAINED as an improper legal conclusion. (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 638-39.)

 

Plaintiff’s Untimely Opposition

 

            Plaintiff filed and served her opposition to this motion on Friday, September 20, 2024. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1005(b), Plaintiff’s opposition was due on September 17, 2024, nine court days before the scheduled September 30, 2024 hearing date. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) Plaintiff offers no explanation for her late opposition, and the Court would be within its authority to disregard her untimely filing. However, as BASTA has prepared a reply brief and does not appear to have been prejudiced by the delay, the Court will consider the merits of Plaintiff’s opposition in ruling on this motion.

 

Procedural History

 

            This case began as an action for fraud and breach of lease filed by Plaintiff Jenna King and nine other individuals, alleging that Plaintiffs entered into commercial leases with the Defendants under the belief that they were renting for residential use. (Complaint ¶¶ 14-16.) Plaintiffs contended that Defendants misrepresented the status of the units as residential when they were permitted for commercial use only. (Id. ¶¶ 17-18.) At the time, all Plaintiffs, including Ms. King, were represented by BASTA, Inc.

 

            On July 1, 2019, the parties, including Ms. King, executed a short form settlement agreement, in which Defendants agreed, as relevant here, to pay Plaintiffs, as a group, $1 million in exchange for a full release of claims and settlement of the case. (Declaration of Thomas F. Mortimer ISO Opp. Exh. C.) After Plaintiff King signed the short form settlement agreement, a dispute arose between her and BASTA regarding the allocation of the settlement funds, which led to BASTA requesting to be relieved as counsel (November 12, 2019 Minute Order) and Plaintiff challenging the settlement agreement. The Court granted Defendants’ Motion to Enforce the Settlement and thereafter entered judgment on the settlement. (February 19, 2020 Minute Order; July 20, 2020 Judgment.) The judgment was affirmed on appeal, and Defendants were awarded costs on appeal. (February 23, 2022 Remittitur.)

 

            In 2023, Defendants in this action filed an unlawful detainer action against Plaintiff King, entitled Downtown Prime, LLC v. King, LASC Case No. 23STUD04483, which settled for a stipulated judgment for possession of the property, in exchange for the owners releasing $20,000 to Plaintiff King from her agreed-upon share of the settlement proceeds of $119,000 in this case, and waiving the Court’s award of attorney’s fees and costs. (Declaration of Thomas F. Mortimer ISO Mot. Enforce Settlement Exh. A.) BASTA, as Plaintiff’s former counsel, is asserting a lien against her recovery on the remaining $99,000 in settlement funds for the reasonable value of services rendered and unpaid costs advanced on her behalf during the representation. (Id. Exh. B.) On May 31, 2024, the Court granted Defendants’ motion to interplead the remaining $99,000 in settlement funds pending determination of the validity and amount of the lien asserted by BASTA. (May 31, 2024 Minute Order.) BASTA now moves for an award of attorney’s fees for services rendered.

 

Entitlement to Fees

 

            BASTA contends that it is entitled to an award of attorney’s fees as a lien on Plaintiff’s recovery from settlement.

 

            “A lien in favor of an attorney upon the proceeds of a prospective judgment in favor of his client for legal services rendered has been recognized in numerous cases. Such a lien may be created either by express contract […] or it may be implied if the retainer agreement between the lawyer and client indicates that the former is to look to the judgment for payment of his fee. [citations].” (Cetenko v. United California Bank (1982) 30 Cal.3d 528, 531.) “The attorney's lien survives even after discharge, although the attorney's recovery is limited to the reasonable value of services actually performed (i.e., quantum meruit), rather than the full percentage specified in the contract.” (Southern California Gas Co. v. Flannery (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 476, 494.) When such a discharge occurs “on the courthouse steps,” i.e., “where the client executes a settlement obtained after much work by the attorney,” the attorney is entitled to the full contingency to which they bargained as the reasonable value of the services rendered.” (Fracasse v. Brent (1972) 6 Cal.3d 784, 791.) In such circumstances, the right to fees is assessed under the terms of the contract. (Franklin v. Appel (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 875, 894, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in Arnall v. Superior Court (2010) 190 Cal.App.4th 360.) However, when an attorney voluntarily withdraws from representation, rather than being discharged, recovery in quantum meruit is only permissible when the attorney has justifiable cause for withdrawal. (Hensel v. Cohen (1994) 155 Cal.App.3d 563; 567; In re Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014; cf Pearlmutter v. Alexander (1979) 97 Cal.App.3d Supp. 16, 20.)

            BASTA contends that Plaintiff executed a retainer agreement on or about August 25, 2017 which provides that BASTA is entitled to 33% of any gross recovery after the first $10,000, or, in the event of BASTA’s justifiable withdrawal from representation, a “reasonable fee” for the legal services provided. (Declaration of Eric M. Post ISO Mot. ¶ 8a-b.) Such a fee is to be determined by considering:

 

(i) the amount of the fee in proportion to the value of the service performed; (ii) the relative sophistication of the BASTA and the Client; (iii) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (iv) the likelihood, if apparent to the Client, that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the BASTA; (v) the amount involved and the results obtained; (vi) the time limitations imposed by the Client or by the circumstances; (vii) the nature and length of the professional relationship with the Client; (viii) the experience, reputation, ability of the BASTA; (ix) the time and labor required; and (x) informed consent of the Client to the fee.

 

(Post Decl. ¶ 8b.) Plaintiff does not dispute that she signed the retainer agreement, nor does she dispute BASTA’s recitation of its terms. Instead, Plaintiff argues that BASTA is not entitled to recover attorney’s fees because its withdrawal from representation of Plaintiff was not justified. BASTA contends that withdrawal was justified, and, in fact, was mandated by the Rules of Professional Conduct. As set forth in Estate of Falco, an attorney who voluntarily withdraws on ethical grounds may recover attorney’s fees in quantum meruit, contingent on the attorney carrying the burden of proof to demonstrate: (1) counsel's withdrawal was mandatory, not merely permissive, under statute or State Bar rules; (2) the overwhelming and primary motivation for counsel's withdrawal was the obligation to adhere to these ethical imperatives under statute or State Bar rules; (3) counsel commenced the action in good faith; (4) subsequent to counsel's withdrawal, the client obtained recovery; and (5) counsel has demonstrated that his work contributed in some measurable degree towards the client's ultimate recovery.” (In Re Estate of Falco, supra, 188 Cal.App.3d at 1016.)

 

            BASTA argues that withdrawal was mandatory under Rule of Professional Conduct 1.16(a). This rule mandates withdrawal where the attorney knows that the client is asserting a position in litigation “without probable cause and for the purpose of harassing” and when the lawyer knows that the representation will result in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or the State Bar Act. (Cal. Rules Prof. Conduct Rule 1.16(a).) According to BASTA, Plaintiff asserted such a position by contending that she did not sign an agreement which her then-counsel personally witnessed her signing, and it was this position which necessitated withdrawal. Indeed, as found by this Court and affirmed on appeal, it is a settled fact that Plaintiff signed the short form Settlement Agreement and assented to the Settlement. (February 16, 2020 Minute Order, February 23, 2022 Remittitur.) It is also apparent from the record that this action was vigorously litigated before a settlement was reached, with challenges to pleadings and discovery motion practice which were handled by BASTA before its withdrawal as Plaintiff’s counsel. (See, e.g., July 2, 2018 Ruling on Motion for Protective Order; September 11, 2018 Ruling on Motion to Compel Inspection.) The record therefore strongly suggests that BASTA undertook this action in good faith. Further, it is undisputed that Plaintiff obtained recovery after the withdrawal, and that this recovery was obtained as a direct result of the settlement secured by BASTA before withdrawal.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff contends that BASTA’s withdrawal was not justifiable because BASTA merely withdrew because of Plaintiff’s refusal to settle and her contention that the settlement agreement was not valid. Both contentions have been extensively litigated and conclusively determined against Plaintiff. (February 16, 2020 Minute Order, February 23, 2022 Remittitur.) Plaintiff offers no new evidence in support of that position, instead presenting the same 2019 Declaration on which the prior rulings were based. Under the doctrine of “law of the case,” presentation of the same evidence warrants an identical conclusion. (People v. Burbine (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 1250, 1261.)

 

            BASTA has demonstrated to the Court’s satisfaction that it justifiably withdrew from representation of Plaintiff King and is therefore entitled to the reasonable value of services rendered up to the point of withdrawal pursuant to the attorney’s lien.

 

Reasonableness of Fees

 

            BASTA seeks an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $35,970.

 

            Reasonable attorney’s fees are allowable costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc § 1033.5(a)(10), (c)(5)(B).) In actions that are based on a contract, “where the contract specifically provides that attorney’s fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract… shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to other costs.” (Civil Code § 1717(a) [emphasis added].) A recovery of attorney’s fees is authorized even in noncontractual or tort actions if the contractual provision for fee recovery is worded broadly enough. (See Code Civ. Proc § 10211; Maynard v. BTI Group, Inc. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 984, 993 [agreement to award fees based on outcome of “any dispute” encompasses all claims, “whether in contract, tort or otherwise]; Lockton v. O'Rourke (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1051, 1076; Lerner v. Ward (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 155, 160.)

Reasonable attorney’s fees shall be fixed by the Court and shall be an element of the costs of suit. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1033.5(c)(5)(B).) Reasonable attorney’s fees are ordinarily determined by the Court pursuant to the “lodestar” method, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (See PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096; Margolin v. Regional Planning Com. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004 [“California courts have consistently held that a computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys' fee award.”].) “[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award….”  (Ibid.) In setting the hourly rate for a fee award, courts are entitled to consider the “fees customarily charged by that attorney and others in the community for similar work.” (Bihun v. AT&T Info. Sys., Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 976, 997 [affirming rate of $450 per hour], overruled on other grounds by Lakin v. Watkins Associated Indus. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 644, 664.)  The burden is on the party seeking attorney’s fees to prove the reasonableness of the fees. (Center for Biological Diversity v. County of San Bernardino (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 603, 615.) 

The Court has broad discretion in determining the amount of a reasonable attorney’s fee award, which will not be overturned absent a “manifest abuse of discretion, a prejudicial error of law, or necessary findings not supported by substantial evidence.” (Bernardi v. County of Monterey (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1393-1394.)  The Court need not explain its calculation of the amount of attorney’s fees awarded in detail; identifying the factors considered in arriving at the amount will suffice. (Ventura v. ABM Indus. Inc. (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 274-275.)

Here, Attorney Post has provided an itemized list of billing entries, reproduced from his contemporaneous billing records, describing the work performed on matters directly related to Plaintiff King’s claims. (Post Decl. ¶ 9.) The included list is not a direct copy of the billing records, but rather is a “synthesis” of those entries. (Id.) Attorney Post states that he has excluded (a) client meetings not involving Plaintiff aside from those which directly benefited her; (b) work with law enforcement agencies; (c) trial and appellate work for co-plaintiffs; (d) work related to Plaintiff King’s unlawful detainer; and (e) work relating to Plaintiff’s refusal to abide by the settlement and the subsequent dispute. (Id. ¶ 6.) Attorney Post also contends that he has excluded time which he considers “unproductive,” “conducting administrative tasks,” and “travel time.” (Id. ¶ 10.) After applying these exclusions, Attorney Post contends that he alone spent 160.5 hours of attorney time between first commencing work on the case in January 2018 and BASTA’s withdrawal from representation on November 12, 2019. (Post Decl. ¶ 9.) Attorney Post testifies to his skills, training, and experience, and states he is regularly awarded fees at rates ranging between $550 and $650 for his work at the trial level. (Id. ¶¶ 11-18.)

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that BASTA’s claimed fees are unreasonably inflated, because both the hourly rate and the number of hours claimed by BASTA are excessive. The Court finds this argument lacking in force because the total fee award sought is considerably reduced from the value of the hours and hourly rate claimed by Attorney Post. For example, based upon the number of hours claimed by Attorney Post, the fee award would reflect an hourly rate of $224.11. Attorney Post’s hourly rate is more than twice that number. Moreover, at the claimed hourly rate of $550, Attorney Post’s actual 160.5 hours would generate $88,275.00 in fees, again more than twice the total award sought here. The Court is not inclined to order further reduction of fees where the evidence presented indicates that the claimant has already sharply reduced its fee request.

            The Court therefore finds that BASTA, Inc. has demonstrated its entitlement to recover attorney’s fees and has demonstrated that the fees sought are reasonable.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, BASTA, Inc.’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED.

 

BASTA, Inc. is awarded attorney’s fees in the amount of $35,970, to be disbursed from the interpled funds currently deposited with the Court.

 

The balance of the funds is to be disbursed to Plaintiff Jenna King.
