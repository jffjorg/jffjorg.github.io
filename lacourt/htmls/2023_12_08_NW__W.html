DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV36655    Hearing Date: December 8, 2023    Dept: W

MELVY CARDONA, et al. v. SFV PORTFOLIO - 14732 BLYTHE OWNER, LP, et al.

 

motion for determination of good faith settlement  

 

Date of Hearing:        December 8, 2023                             Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        21STCV36655

 

Moving Party:            Defendant 14732 Blythe Associates L.L.C.

Responding Party:     Defendant SFV Portfolio – 14732 Blythe Owner LP   

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a habitability action. On July 26, 2023, Plaintiffs Melvy Cardona; Felipe Argueta; Ever Carbajal; Maymarie Carbajal, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona; Melanie Carbajal, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona; Michael Carbajal, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona, Bella Rodriguez, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona, Chanel Carbajal, a minor by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona; and Mason Cardona, a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Melvy Cardona filed a complaint against SFV Portfolio – 14732 Blythe Owner, LP and 14732 Blythe Associates, L.L.C. for 1. Failure to Provide Habitable Dwelling; 2. Breach of Covenant and Right to Quiet Enjoyment and Possession of the Property; 3. Nuisance 4. Negligence 5. Violation of Civil Code Section 1942.4. Plaintiffs allege the property suffered from several nuisances as well as untenantable and substandard conditions including cockroaches, mice, inoperable heating, mold, deteriorated closet, and so on.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement by Defendant 14732 Blythe Associates L.L.C. is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Defendant 14732 Blythe Associates L.L.C. moves the court for an order determining that Defendant’s good faith settlement with Melvy Cardona, Felipe Argueta, Ever Carbajal, Maymarie Carbajal, Melanie Carbajal, Michael Carbajal, Bella Rodriguez, and Chanel Carbajal was made in good faith pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6(a)(1) provides that a “party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors…” Under subdivision (a)(2), a settling party may give notice of settlement to the parties and court, together with an application for determination of good faith settlement and a proposed order. The application “shall indicate the settling parties, and the basis, terms, and amount of the settlement” and must include a proof of service. (CCP §877.6(a)(2).)

 

In determining the good faith of a settlement, the court may consider the affidavits served with the notice of hearing, and any counter-affidavits filed in response, or other evidence in the court’s discretion. (CCP §877.6(b).) The California Supreme Court, in Tech-Bilt Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, provided factors to be considered in determining the good faith of a particular settlement. The court explained that the policies and intent underlying section 877.6 “require that a number of factors be taken into account,” such as: a rough approximation of a plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability; the amount paid in settlement; the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs; a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial. (Tech-Bilt at p. 499.) Other relevant factors include financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, and the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of non-settling defendants. (Id.). This evaluation must be made “on the basis of information available at the time of settlement.” (Id.) The court further explained that settlement by a defendant “must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant’s liability to be.” (Id., citations omitted.) 

 

The non-settling tortfeasors or obligors bear the burden of demonstrating the absence of good faith in the settlement.  (CCP §877.6(d).) In order to demonstrate a lack of good faith, the non-settling party must show that the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark” as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of Section 877.6.  (Nutrition Now, Inc. v. Superior Court (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 209, 213.)

 

Defendant 14732 Blythe Associates L.L.C. (“Blythe Associates”) has filed an application for good faith settlement providing that Defendant and Plaintiff would settle for $100,000.00 in exchange for dismissal with prejudice and release of all of their claims against Defendant Blythe Associates and its insurers. Defendant contends the settlement was reached in order to avoid the substantial costs and uncertainties inherent to litigation. The court finds Defendants’ motion satisfies the Tech-Bilt factors.

 

Defendant SFV Portfolio – 14732 Blythe Owner LP (“SFV Portfolio”) opposes the motion on the grounds Defendant Blythe Associates’ settlement was not “reasonable” compared to the degree of its proportionate liability and damages claimed by Plaintiffs, the motion does not account for the Plaintiffs’ potential attorneys’ fees or punitive damages, the motion does not include information regarding the amount paid in settlement to each Plaintiff, and Blythe’s motion also fails to provide any information regarding her financial condition or insurance limits as indicated in Tech-Bilt.

 

Reasonableness

 

SFV Portfolio argues Plaintiff Melvy Cardona testified that she first saw the mold in 2018, before the property was sold to SFV Portfolio. SFV provides Plaintiff Melvy’s testimony that when the mold began in 2018, she informed Coastline (Blythe Associates’ management company) on numerous occasions of the mold but Coastline did nothing to remediate the situation. As a result, SFV contends Defendant Blythe Associates’ claim that Plaintiffs’ damages stem from SFV’s ownership of the property is very far from the truth of the circumstances in this case and their settlement with Plaintiffs is by no means proportionate or reasonable and only an attempt to cover the problematic circumstances of Plaintiffs’ lack of complaints to appropriate government agencies and Blythe Associates’ active failure to remedy the conditions in Plaintiffs’ unit during its ownership of the property. SFV also refers to a 998 Offer by Plaintiffs to SFV in 2023, where Plaintiffs sought at least $300,000.00 from SFV.

 

As noted by Blythe Associates, determination of good faith settlements are based on facts known at the time of the settlement. (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal. 3d 488, 499.) Here, plaintiffs have settled with Defendants Blythe Associates for more than the amount specified in the plaintiffs’ responses to discovery.  Per Plaintiffs’ discovery responses, the most that Plaintiff could be seeking from Blythe Associates for special damages is $56,669.95 which includes the total amount claimed from both defendants for non-rent special damages ($40,093.98 property damages + $1,664.93 other damages + $213.00 medical expenses + $14,698.04 rent damages). (Dorman Decl. ¶ 9.) The court concludes that the settlement for $100,000 is eminently reasonable, since it exceeds plaintiffs’ claimed injuries. This is not the situation where plaintiff has settled with a tortfeasor for a fraction of the likely damages in a case, thereby leaving the other defendants “holding the bag.” 

 

As such, the court cannot find the settlement to be so far “out of the ballpark” in relation to the Tech-Bilt factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute.

 

Attorney Fees/Punitive Damages

 

SFV Portfolio next argues Blythe Associates’ motion does not account for the Plaintiffs’ potential attorneys’ fees or punitive damages liability.

 

As noted above, determination of good faith settlements are based on facts known at the time of the settlement. According to Blythe Associates, the evidence presented shows Plaintiffs sought a combined $41,971.90 from both defendants and another $14,698.04 in rent damages for Blythe’s ownership period. SFV has not presented evidence showing that Blythe might be found liable for punitive damages.  Further, the settlement would appear to also compensate plaintiff for some of the attorney’s fees they would likely be able to claim in this suit, if they prevailed, given that it exceeds their claim for damages.  

 

Individual Plaintiffs

 

SFV Portfolio also opposes the motion on the grounds the motion fails to provide any information regarding the amount paid in settlement to each Plaintiff. SFV contends such information regarding any settlement is also necessary for purposes of set off. Moreover, SFV Portfolio contends the motion is solely based on its own self-serving conclusions and assumptions.

 

SFV does not provide authority that the settlement must include the proportionate amount to each Plaintiff. The court notes that it does have in the record the division between the minor plaintiffs and the parents: each of the five minor plaintiffs is to receive $4,000.  This division of the settlement proceeds has been approved the court. Thus, there is a record, should it be needed, of how much has been paid to each plaintiff as a result of this settlement. 

 

Financial Condition

 

SFV next argues Blythe has not disclosed her professional liability insurance coverage limits, and has provided no information about its finances, as required by Tech-Bilt. Although financial condition is a factor, the failure to provide such information does not preclude a good faith settlement. (Cahill v. San Diego Gas & Electric Co. (2011) 194 Cal. App. 4th 939, 968.) The party opposing a good faith determination has the burden to prove the settlement was not made in good faith.

 

Accordingly, Defendant 14732 Blythe Associates L.L.C.’s Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement is GRANTED.

Case Number: 22VECV00438    Hearing Date: December 8, 2023    Dept: W

ESPOSITO V. YOUSEF, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR AN AWARD OF ATTORNEY FEES TO THE PREVAILING PARTY UPON CONTRACT AND STATUTE

 

Date of Hearing:        December 8, 2023                             Trial Date:       None set. 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00438

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Samir Yousif and Yousif Family Trust

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Laurina Esposito and Gustavo Flores

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of a landlord-tenant dispute concerning a Porsche restoration business, ran by Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges that she and Defendants Samir Yousef (“Yousef”) and Yousef Family Trust (“Trust”) (collectively, “Defendants”) entered into a written lease permitting Plaintiff to rent the property located at 14520 Delano Street, Van Nuys, California in order to conduct her business operations. It is further alleged that, due to Covid-19, Plaintiff fell behind on her monthly rent payments. As a result, Defendants pursued an unlawful detainer action against her and achieved a default judgment against Plaintiff, despite her willingness to start resume rental payments and to cover past dues. Plaintiff was locked out from the subject property on February 15, 2022 and was improperly prevented from recovering her personal property in a reasonable manner. Consequently, Plaintiff claims that she is incurring lost business because she is unable to access her tools and equipment.

 

Plaintiff filed a Complaint on March 25, 2022 alleging: (1) Conversion; (2) Trespass to Chattels; (3) Interference with Contract; (4) Interference with Perspective Economic Advantage; (5) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; and (6) Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 

On June 27, 2023, the Court issued a tentative Statement of Decision, entering judgment for Defendants against Plaintiffs. Defendants now move for attorney fees.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion for an Award of Attorney Fees to the Prevailing Party upon Contract and Statute’s is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Samir Yousif and Yousif Family Trust, move the court, for a Court Order awarding reasonable attorney’s fees in the sum of $53,079.15, against Plaintiffs Laurina Esposito and Gustavo Flores, jointly and severally. Defendants make the motion on the grounds that Defendants are entitled to recover their attorney’s fees which they have incurred as the prevailing party in this action, based on an attorney’s fees provision in the written agreement between the parties.  

 

As a general rule, each party to litigation must bear its own attorney fees, unless otherwise provided by statute or contract. (CCP §1021.) However, Civil Code section 1717, subdivision (a) provides in pertinent part: “In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.” Contractual attorney's fees must ordinarily be sought by noticed motion, unless the parties stipulate otherwise. (See CCP § 1033.5(c)(5); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702(a).) A motion is necessary to determine both entitlement to, and the amount of, any fees. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1702(a) [motion procedure applies when court determines entitlement to fees, amount of fees, or both]; P R Burke Corp. v Victor Valley Wastewater Reclamation Auth. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1052.)

 

Defendants seek $53,079.15 in attorney fees. First, the court notes Defendants are entitled to attorney fees as the prevailing party. On June 27, 2023, the court issued a tentative Statement of Decision. (Kim Decl. ¶4, Exh. 2.) On July 18, 2023, the court issued an order adopting the proposed Statement of Decision with no objections made by the parties and on August 1, 2023, a judgment for Defendants was entered against Plaintiffs. (Kim Decl., ¶¶5-6, Exhs. 3-4.) The court issued Notice of Entry of Judgment on August 7, 2023. (Kim Decl. ¶5, Exh. 5.) Plaintiffs’ claims arose out of a commercial lease agreement between the parties. Paragraph 31 of the agreement provided a broad fees provision for “full[] reimbursement” of actual reasonable attorney’s fees incurred without limitation by any court fee schedule, for any actions, founded in tort, contract or equity. (Kim Decl. ¶3, Exh. 1.)

 

Next, the court finds the fees reasonable and proper, excluding the $6,000.00 in fees with predecessor counsel. Defendants contend defense counsel incurred 137.9 hours successfully defending the entirety of Plaintiffs’ complaints that only came to a resolution after a trial, despite Defendants’ pretrial attempts to settle the matter without a trial. Defendants provide itemized invoices, breaking down the hours as follows: 61.4 hours for Mr. Obagi, 31 hours for Mr. Kim, and 45.5 hours for administrative staff, prior to the instant motion. (Kim Decl. ¶12.) Defendants also request 6 hours, for a total of $2,975, as reasonable fees associated with this motion. (Kim Decl. ¶¶ 14-17.) However, upon review of the invoices and case at hand, Defendants have not substantiated the $6,000 in fees incurred by Defendants’ former counsel, Nader. (Obagi Decl. ¶19.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs first argue the attorney fees are not reasonable as trial was unnecessary and Defendant has not provided any documentation to support their claim. The court disagrees. As noted by Defendants, although Plaintiff feels Defendants should have moved for summary judgment as opposed to going to trial, the failure to bring a dispositive motion does not make the attorney fees unreasonable. (See Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1141 holding “[t]he amount of litigation on this issue typically lies in the plaintiff’s hands: having litigated the matter tenaciously, [the plaintiffs] ‘cannot… be heard to complain about the time necessarily spent by the [defendant] in response’.”)  Here, plaintiff pursued this case to trial and presented an affirmative case for relief, including calling witnesses on her behalf and disputing defendant’s evidence.  Further, contrary to her assertion, the motion is supported by itemized invoices. Plaintiffs confirmed they received the attachments at the hearing on November 14, 2023, when they were again emailed to her in open court by the moving party’s counsel. 

 

Plaintiff next argue Defense counsel has acted in bad faith and refer to State Bar allegations against Mr. Obagi. However, this argument is irrelevant to the matter at hand. Plaintiffs have not demonstrated Defendants or defense counsel have acted in bad faith, or engaged in any delay tactics or unethical conduct.

 

Accordingly, the court grants Defendants’ request for attorneys’ fees in the total, reduced amount of $ 47,079.15

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER discovery responses

[Tentative] Ruling

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

American Honda Motor Company, Inc., et al., United States District Court, Central District of California Case No. 2:18-cv-04007-MWF-PJW (RJN, Exhs. 1, 2.)

 

The court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

discussion

 

I.                    PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION SET ONE

 

Plaintiff Zovar moves the court for an order compelling further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One Nos. 20, 53-55. Plaintiff further moves for an order awarding sanctions in the amount of $2,245.00 against Defendant and its attorney of record, Nelson Mullins Riley & Scarborough, LLP.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

The following RPDs are at issue:

 

RPD 20: The operative dealership agreement, if any, on the date of sale of the SUBJECT VEHICLE between YOU and the dealership that sold the SUBJECT VEHICLE to Plaintiffs.

 

RPD 53: All DOCUMENTS evidencing complaints by owners of 2020 Honda Civic vehicles regarding any of the complaints that the SUBJECT VEHICLE was presented to YOUR or YOUR authorized repair facilities for repair during the warranty period.

 

RPD 54: All DOCUMENTS evidencing warranty repairs to 2020 Honda Civic vehicles regarding any of the components that YOU or YOUR authorized repair facilities performed repairs on under warranty.

 

RPD 55: All DOCUMENTS produced by YOU in discovery in the matter of Kathleen Cadena, et al., v. American Honda Motor Company, Inc., et al, United States District Court, Central District of California Case No. 2:18-cv-04007-MWF-PJW.

 

As for RPD 20, Defendant originally objected to the request on the grounds the request was vague, ambiguous, overly broad, unduly burdensome, oppressive, calls for private information, and as asking for information that is not relevant to the subject matter of this action.

 

In opposition, Honda argues the motion is moot as to RPD 20 as it has provided a further response. However, Plaintiff contends the further response is non-code complaint. Plaintiff contends a sample of its standard Automobile Dealers Sales and Service Agreement is not sufficient. The court agrees the response does not address the request. If Defendant does not have the operative agreement, the response must state such. If the operative agreement is in Defendant’s custody, possession, or control, Defendant must produce such.

 

As for RPD 53-55, Defendant objects to the demands on the grounds the documents are irrelevant to the issues in the case because they seek documents and information beyond the Subject Vehicle and which have no bearing on Plaintiff’s burden of proof.

 

Plaintiff argues the requests are relevant on the grounds the documents relate to similar customer complaints and warranty repairs. Plaintiff contends customer complaints relating to the defects and nonconformities in 2020 Honda CR-V vehicles that overlap with the same defects alleged in Plaintiffs’ vehicle are clearly relevant to AHM’s knowledge of ongoing and uncurable defects and nonconformities in Plaintiffs’ Vehicle. Moreover, the documents associated with the class action lawsuit in Cadena would undoubtedly contain relevant information in the form of various consumer complaints with similar defects associated with the Honda Sensing system in Plaintiff’s subject vehicle.

 

The court finds further responses are required. Evidence regarding other vehicles with similar defects as Plaintiff’s has been found to potentially be admissible at trial in a¿lemon law¿action.¿(Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 154; see also¿Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 971.)

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production Set One is GRANTED but only for complaints about the same make, model and year of the subject vehicle in California in response to Requests Nos. 53 and 54.  The motion is denied as to Request No 55, as plaintiff has failed to provide any information about this class action or to establish its relevance to the instant lawsuit. 

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310 (h) provides for the imposition of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes such a motion unless the court finds that the one subject to sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

There being substantial justification, with merit to both sides, the court denies the requests for sanctions.   

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES

 

Plaintiff Zovar moves the court for an order compelling further responses to Plaintiff’s First Set of Special Interrogatories and for monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,927.50 against Defendant and its attorneys of record, Nelson Mullins Riley & Scarborough, LLP. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds Defendant as not provided Code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatory No. 14.

 

A motion to compel further responses to form or specially prepared interrogatories may be brought if the responses contain: (1) answers that are evasive or incomplete; (2) an unwarranted or insufficiently specific exercise of an option to produce documents in lieu of a substantive response; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2030.300(a).)   

 

SROG 14 seeks the following:

 

If YOU contend that the SUBJECT VEHICLE does not qualify for repurchase under the provisions of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, please state all facts supporting YOUR contention.

 

Defendant initially objected to the interrogatory on the ground it is vague, ambiguous, overly broad, protected by attorney-client privilege, the work product doctrine, and/or the premature discovery of information and materials to be provided by an expert witness, and a response to this interrogatory would necessitate the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of documents, the burden or expense of which is substantially the same for Plaintiff as it is for AHM. However, AHM referred Plaintiff to and stated it would produce the service records received from Riverside Honda.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues the response was sufficient as it referred to and produced repair records it received from Ocean Honda of Soquel, Woodland Hills and Swickward Honda of Thousand Oaks (formerly Honda of Thousand Oaks). Nonetheless, Defendant contends although it is not obligated or required to do so under the Code, in the interest of cooperation, AHM served a further response to Special Interrogatory No. 14, summarizing the repair orders.

 

Plaintiff concedes the motion as moot and withdraws this request. However, Plaintiff still seeks sanctions.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300(d) provides that for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2030.300(d).)   

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff has proffered no evidence to support the position that AHM has acted without substantial justification with its discovery obligations. AHM’s initial response was sufficient and code-compliant.

 

Plaintiff contends the parties met and conferred on multiple occasions regarding Defendant’s discovery responses to Special Interrogatories; however, Defendant refused to provide further responses to the request at issue in this motion and did not provide further responses to the request at issue until the day their opposition was due.

 

There being substantial justification, with merit to both sides, the court denies the requests for sanctions.
