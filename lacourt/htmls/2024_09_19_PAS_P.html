DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV01198    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: P

[TENANTIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR NEW TRIAL


      I.          INTRODUCTION

On December 1, 2022, Plaintiff Franses Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”) filed this lawsuit against Defendant the City of Pasadena (“Defendant”), asserting a single cause of action for Dangerous Condition of Public Property, under Government Code § 835. Plaintiff alleges that on November 15, 2021, around 8:30 a.m., she was riding her Ninebot Kick electric scooter (the “e-scooter”) and wearing a helmet, when she encountered a 1.25-inch uneven section of the Marengo Avenue roadway in Pasadena, CA causing her to lose control of the e-scooter and sustain injuries.

Procedural History

On July 11, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for summary judgment. 

On July 18, 2024, the Court entered judgment in Defendant’s favor. 

On July 24, 2024, Defendant filed and served Notice of Entry of Judgment.

On August 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Intent to Move for New Trial. 

On August 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Motion for New Trial.

On August 26, 2024, Defendant filed its opposition to the Motion for New Trial.

On September 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply to Defendant’s Motion for New Trial.

 

    II.          LEGAL STANDARD

The determination of a motion for new trial rests so completely within the trial judge’s discretion that it will not be disturbed on appeal unless a manifest and unmistakable abuse of discretion clearly appears.  (Schelbaurer v Butler Mfg. Co (1984) 35 Cal3d 442, 452.)  The rationale is that the trial judge is familiar with the evidence, witnesses and proceedings of the case and is in the best position to determine whether a new trial should be granted.  (Jacoby v Feldman (1978) 81 Cal App 3d 432, 446.) 

      A motion for new trial asks the trial court to reexamine one or more issues of fact or law after a trial and decision by judge or jury.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§656, 657.)  Since the right to a new trial is purely statutory, exact compliance is required with the procedural steps for making and determining a motion for new trial.  Any significant departure from the procedural requirements deprives the trial court of power to grant the motion.  The grounds for new trial are also entirely statutory and, thus, the motion must be based on one or more of the following statutory grounds:

      (1) Irregularity in the proceedings (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (1)): Irregularity in the proceedings of the court, jury or adverse party (i.e., attorney misconduct), or any other order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial.

      (2) Jury Misconduct (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (2)): Jury misconduct during trial, misconduct during deliberations, or concealed bias on voir dire.

      (3) Accident or Surprise (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (3)): The moving party has to show three conditions: (a) an accident or surprise happened during the trial (i.e., something unforeseen); (b) which had a material adverse effect on the moving party’s case (i.e., prejudice); and (c) it could not have been guarded against or prevented through reasonable diligence by the moving party (this is the reason most motions are denied on this ground).  An accident or surprise must be shown, not merely a mistake or inadvertence and relief must be sought during trial.

      (4) Newly-Discovered Evidence (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (4)): There are three stringent requirements: (a) newly-discovered evidence; (b) material to the outcome of the case; and (c) reasonable diligence to discover and produce the evidence at trial (lack of such diligence is the most common reason for denying the motion on this ground).

      (5) Excessive or Inadequate Damages (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (5)): This ground in effect asks for a limited new trial to the issue of damages.  In deciding the motion for new trial on this ground, the trial court has the power and responsibility to reweigh the evidence.  “A new trial shall not be granted upon the ground of excessive or inadequate damages, unless after weighing the evidence the court is convinced from the entire record, including reasonable inferences therefrom, that the court or jury clearly should have reached a different verdict or decision.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §657.)  The judge is not permitted to substitute her/his judgment for that of the jury on the question of damages unless it appears from the record that the jury verdict was improper.  (The plaintiff can also consent to a reduction of damages in an amount determined by the court (a remittitur) in lieu of a new trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., §662.5, subd. (b).)

(6) Insufficient Evidence (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (6)): This is one of the most frequent grounds for new trial motions and is also one as to which the trial judge has the broadest power.  It is the trial judge’s responsibility to determine the weight of the evidence.  A new trial must be granted if the judge believes the weight of the evidence to be contrary to the finding of the jury.

(7) Verdict or Decision “Against Law” (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (6)): Given the restrictive interpretation of this ground, it is not widely used and is limited principally to the following situations: (a) verdict unsupported by evidence; (b) verdict contrary to instructions; (c) verdict or findings hopelessly uncertain or inconsistent; or (d) incomplete statement of decision.  This “against law” ground differs from the “insufficiency of the evidence” ground discussed above in that there is no weighing of evidence or determining credibility - i.e., this ground applies only where the evidence is without conflict in any material point and insufficient as a matter of law to support the verdict.  Further, as compared to the “error in law excepted to at trial” ground, this ground applies only where Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subdivision (7) does not.

      (8) Error in Law During Trial (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (7)): “Error in law, occurring at the trial and excepted to by the party making the application.”  The error must have been prejudicial - i.e., the error must likely have affected the outcome of the trial.  The error must also have been excepted to - i.e., timely raised during trial by motion or objection.

      A motion for new trial “made for a cause mentioned on the first, second, third and fourth subdivisions of Section 657, it must be made upon affidavits; otherwise, it must be made on the minutes of the court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §658.)  Thus, a motion for new trial based on irregularity in the proceedings, misconduct of the jury, accident or surprise or newly discovered evidence must be supported by affidavits.  Other motions based on insufficiency of the evidence, a verdict or decision against law, an error in law or excessive or inadequate damages must be made on the minutes of the court.  It is “implicit in the statement of the grounds of the motion whether it will be made upon affidavits or the minutes of the court, or both.”  (Nichols v. Hast (1965) 62 Cal.2d 598, 601.)   

      “An order granting summary judgment is properly challenged by a motion for a new trial. This is so, even though, strictly speaking, summary judgment is a determination that there shall be no trial at all.  A new trial may be granted under section 657, subdivision 7, if the original ruling was erroneous as a matter of law.”  (Brewer v. Remington (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 14, 23.) 

 

 III.          DISCUSSION

            A. Procedural Issues

            Plaintiff filed a Notice of Intent to Move for New Trial on August 2, 2024, 15 days after Notice of Entry of Judgment was filed and served on July 18, 2024.  The Notice of Intent is therefore timely under Code of Civil Procedure section 657.

            Plaintiff filed her memorandum of points and authorities on August 12, 2024, within 10 days of filing of the Notice of Intent to Move for New Trial.  Plaintiff’s supporting memorandum was timely filed under California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1600(a). 

            “Except as otherwise provided in Section 12a of this code, the power of the court to rule on a motion for a new trial shall expire 75 days after the mailing of notice of entry of judgment by the clerk of the court pursuant to Section 664.5 or 75 days after service on the moving party by any party of written notice of entry of judgment, whichever is earlier, or if that notice has not been given, 75 days after the filing of the first notice of intention to move for a new trial. If the motion is not determined within the 75-day period, or within that period as extended, the effect shall be a denial of the motion without further order of the court.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §660, subd. (c).) 

            According to Plaintiff, the 75-day deadline to rule on the motion expires on October 2, 2024.  However, based on the Court’s calculation, the deadline to rule will expire on October 1, 2024.  The clerk mailed Notice of Entry of Judgment on July 18, 2024.  Defendant mailed Notice of Entry of Judgment on July 19, 2024.  The earlier of these two dates started the 75-day period, which would be July 18, 2024.  The deadline for the Court to rule on this Motion for New Trial is therefore October 1, 2024. 

            B.  Plaintiff fails to submit affidavits in support of a Motion for New Trial under Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subdivisions (1), (3) and (4)

            In her Notice of Intent to Move for New Trial, Plaintiff identifies Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subdivisions (1), (3) and (4) as grounds for new trial.  Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subdivision (1) allows for new trial based on “irregularity in the proceedings of the court…or adverse party, or any order of the court or abuse of discretion by which either party was prevented from having a fair trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §657, subd. (1).)  Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subdivision (3) allows for new trial based on “accident or surprise, which ordinary prudence could not have guarded against.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §657, subd. (3).)  Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subd. (4) allows for new trial based on “newly discovered evidence, material to the moving party, which could not, with reasonable diligence, have been discovered and produced at trial.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §657, subd. (4).) 

            Each of these grounds must be supported by affidavits or declarations.  “When the application is made for a cause mentioned in the first, second, third and fourth subdivisions of Section 657, it must be made upon affidavits….”  (Code of Civ. Proc. §657.)  A motion for new trial “on grounds enumerated in the first four subdivisions of section 657 must be presented solely by affidavit.”  (Linhart v. Nelson (1976) 18 Cal.3d 641, 645.) 

            Plaintiff failed to submit any affidavits in support of his motion for new trial and relies solely on the minute order of the Court and the evidence submitted in connection with the motion for summary judgment.  Plaintiff’s motion for new trial based on Code of Civil Procedure section 657, subdivision (1), (3) and (4) is therefore denied.

            C.  Plaintiff fails to establish that a new trial should be granted as to the motion for summary judgment based on insufficiency of the evidence or error in the law

             Plaintiff argues the order granting summary judgment (1) failed to comply with the requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (g) that the order “specifically refer to the evidence proffered in support of and, if applicable, in opposition to the motion that indicates no triable issue exists”;  and (2) failed to mention Plaintiff’s evidence that the defect was a dangerous condition and that Defendant had actual or constructive notice, nor did the Court explain why this evidence did not create a triable issue of material fact. 

            Defendant denies that the Court’s July 11, 2024 order fails to comply with subsection (g). Defendant argues the July 11, 2024 order specifically references Plaintiff’s evidence in support of a dangerous condition and explained why it did not raise a triable issue of fact.  Defendant argues the Court provided a thorough explanation as to why the defect was trivial as a matter of law. 

            Plaintiff fails to establish that the July 11, 2024 Order was based on insufficient evidence or an error in the law.  Plaintiff’s motion is based on an alleged failure to satisfy a procedural requirement under subsection (g).  In pertinent part, subsection (g) provides, “Upon the grant of a motion for summary judgment on the ground that there is no triable issue of material fact, the court shall, by written or oral order, specify the reasons for its determination. The order shall specifically refer to the evidence proffered in support of and, if applicable, in opposition to the motion that indicates no triable issue exists. The court shall also state its reasons for any other determination. The court shall record its determination by court reporter or written order.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §437c, subd. (g).)

            Plaintiff fails to cite any authority holding that a new trial should be granted based on failure to satisfy the procedural requirements of subsection (g).  Failure to satisfy the requirements of subsection (g) is not automatic grounds for reversal:  “It is the validity of the ruling which is reviewable and not the reasons therefor.”  (Santa Barbara Pistachio Ranch v. Chowchilla Water Dist. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 439, 448-449.)  “The lack of a statement of reasons presents no harm where our independent review establishes the validity of the judgment.”  (Id. at 449 (failure to satisfy subsection (g)’s requirements was not harmless error where trial court gave no reasons at all for order denying and granting in part parties’ respective motions for summary judgment; appellate court precluded from undertaking meaningful review of issues given trial court’s complete failure to provide statement of reasons for order).)

            Even if the failure to satisfy subsection (g) were qualified as insufficient evidence or an error in the law, the July 11, 2024 Order fully satisfies the requirements under subsection (g).  A “key objective” of subsection (g) is to ensure that a court’s order include a statement of reasons adequate for “meaningful appellate review.”  (W.F. Hayward Co. v. Transamerica Ins. Co. 1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 1101, 1111.)  For the reasons stated below, the July 11, 2024 Order provides an adequate statement of reasons for meaningful appellate review.

            The minute order was 12 single-spaced pages and the Court discussed in detail Plaintiff’s evidence in opposition to the Motion for Summary Judgment.  Summary judgment was based on the Court’s finding that the defect was trivial as a matter of law.  The Court discussed Plaintiff’s evidence pertinent to the issue of triviality at length.  The Court cited Plaintiff’s evidence regarding the size of the defect and how the defect was caused, including her expert’s testimony and her deposition testimony.  (July 11, 2024 Minute Order, p. 8 of 12.)  The Court also discussed Plaintiff’s photographs of the defect and the conditions of the area on the day of the accident, citing to Plaintiff’s deposition.  (Id. at p. 9.) 

            The Court also listed Plaintiff’s evidence, including her reference to industry standards.  (July 11, 2024 Minute Order, p. 2.)  The Court stated, “After examining all the evidence, the Court finds the displacement in question to be trivial as a matter of law.”  (Id. at p. 8.)  This statement makes it sufficiently clear that the Court found the defect trivial as a matter of law after review of Plaintiff’s evidence, including the industry standards cited by her, and resolved any issues raised by the industry standards against her.  (Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782 (trial court's statement that it was “relying on all of the evidence makes it clear that it concluded…that the statute of limitations problem must be resolved against” plaintiff as an issue of law on summary judgment; statement therefore satisfied CCP §437c(g)’s requirements).) 

            The Court explained in detail, based on Plaintiff’s and Defendant’s evidence, its finding that the defect was trivial as a matter of law.  There is no requirement under subsection (g) that the Court address each piece of evidence submitted in opposition to the motion for summary judgment.  The Court specified the reasons for its determination and referenced the evidence submitted by Plaintiff indicating that “no triable issue exists.”  The Court discussed all evidence it found applicable to its finding that the defect was trivial as a matter of law. 

            Plaintiff argues the Court misconstrued her argument regarding why the 1.25-inch defect was not trivial.  However, misinterpretation of an argument raised in Plaintiff’s opposition is not an error in the law or insufficiency of evidence in support of the order.  The Court also disagrees that it misunderstood the argument.  The Court simply found the argument unconvincing. 

            With regard to Plaintiff’s claim that the Court failed to address her evidence of actual or constructive notice, actual or constructive notice is immaterial to the issue of triviality.  The Court’s discussion of the City’s actual or constructive notice was merely an alternate grounds to grant relief.  Any defect therein was not prejudicial.  A new trial cannot be granted for error of law unless the error was prejudicial (see Cal.Const. Art. VI, § 13.  The error must likely have affected the outcome of the trial: “If it clearly appears that the error could not have affected the result of the trial, the court is bound to deny the motion.”  (Bristow v. Ferguson (1981) 121 Cal.App.3d 823, 826.) 

            Plaintiff fails to establish that the July 11, 2024 order was based on an error in law or was supported by insufficient evidence.  Plaintiff’s Motion for New Trial based on CCP §657(6) and (7) is denied.      

 

            CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            Plaintiff’s Motion for New Trial is DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER FINDING DEFENDANT PRAPATSORN GROUP, INC.’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION MOOT, AND DENYING SANCTIONS

                          I.          INTRODUCTION

On April 28, 2023, Plaintiff Perla Mageno (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Prapatsorn Group, Inc. (“Defendant”) for violations of the Unruh Civil Rights Act, California Civil Code § 51.

On August 14, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to requests for admission. On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion, and on September 12, 2024, Defendant replied.

                        II.          LEGAL STANDARD

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.290 subdivision (a), parties may move for a further response to requests for admission where an answer to the requests are evasive or incomplete or where an objection is without merit or too general.

            Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (c).)  The motion must also be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (b).) 

            Finally, Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3)).

            Where the court grants a motion to compel further responses, sanctions shall be imposed against the party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, unless the party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (d).) 

                     III.          ANALYSIS

Plaintiff has now provided supplemental responses to the discovery at issue and Defendant does not dispute this. Thus, the motion is MOOT.

As to sanctions, Plaintiff argues that they are not warranted because Defendant did not meet and confer in good faith. Plaintiff contends that the meet and confer email as to the June 28, 2024 responses was lacking sufficient detail as the code requires an informal resolution as to each issue presented by the motion. The Court agrees that the single email is insufficient to show a good faith meet and confer attempt. The email is vague, general, and does not refer to any specific requests or responses. Further, counsel states that they are in trial and cannot elaborate any further because of time constraints. Counsel states that they will give Plaintiff additional time to respond, but a motion will be filed if a commitment is not made to provide non-evasive responses. (Sahelian Decl., ¶10, Exh. F.) The email does not present enough detail to allow Plaintiff to respond, and Defendant did not follow up to elaborate on what they found deficient and did not attempt to schedule a telephonic meet and confer. Accordingly, this does not satisfy the meet and confer requirement, and Plaintiff’s conduct does not appear sanctionable, especially since once the motion was filed, which provided sufficient detail regarding the deficiencies, Plaintiff provided responses. The Court also notes that Defendant’s reply only addresses the meet and confer attempts prior to the June 28, 2024 supplemental responses, which are the ones at issue.

                     IV.          CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The Court finds the motion MOOT and denies Defendant’s request for sanctions.

Moving Party is ordered to provide notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 23AHCV02957    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: P

[Tentative] Order on Demurrer with Motion to Strike


.         INTRODUCTION

          On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff Arianna Hertel (Plaintiff) filed suit against Allen Avenue Square North HOA (HOA), Professional Plumbing Services, Inc. (Plumbing), and Pasadena Condominium Management LLC (Management). Several months later she filed the operative first amended complaint alleging five claims for breach of contract, negligence, conversion[1], unjust enrichment[2], and fraudulent misrepresentation[3].

          Plaintiff alleged she is a resident at 125 N. Allen Avenue, Unit 116, Pasadena CA 91106 (subject property). After experiencing a plumbing issue at the subject property on August 21, 2023, HOA and Management (collectively defendants) called Plumbing to repair the subject property. However, Plumbing negligently tore out drywall and plaster without testing it; the material all contained asbestos, thereby contaminating Plaintiff’s residence. Plaintiff reported the issue to Defendants, but they refused to act, including declining to file a claim with their insurance carrier. Plaintiff had to file a claim on her individual insurance and retain vendors to address the asbestos abatement. She subsequently filed a third-party claim with Defendants’ carrier—Nationwide Insurance (Nationwide).

          Plaintiff further alleges while her vendors were engaged in abating the asbestos, Defendants requested access to the subject property to purportedly “assess” the damages to the “common areas”. However, they misrepresented their purpose and requested access to obtain an estimate for repairs to Plaintiff’s residence. Nationwide, thereafter, provided reimbursement to Defendants for damages to Plaintiff’s residence—including flooring, cabinets, countertops etc.—but Defendants refused to transfer the funds to her. Instead, they maintain that they will conduct the repairs themselves.

          Defendants demur to the third cause of action for conversion and the fifth cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation. Defendants also move to strike punitive damages allegations and prayer. (FAC, ¶¶37, 45, 54; prayer, ¶6.)

 

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (See Schultz v. Harney (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1622, as modified on denial of reh’g (Sept. 29, 1994).) The court “take[s] as true all properly pleaded material facts, but not conclusions of fact or law asserted in the complaint.” (Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 916.)

At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against them. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “The allegations of the pleading demurred to must be regarded as true.” (South Shore Land Co. v. Peterson (1964) 266 Cal.App.2d 725, 732.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (Ibid. [internal citations omitted].)

The court may strike out any “irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., §437) Motions may also target pleadings or parts of pleadings that are not filed or drawn in conformity with applicable laws, rules, or orders. (Code Civ. Proc., §437 subd., (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., §437.) “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)

Punitive damages are available in non-contract actions where a defendant is guilty of malice, fraud, or oppression. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c) [defining each term].) To succeed on a motion to strike punitive damages allegations, it must be said as a matter of law that the alleged behavior was not so vile, base, or contemptible that it would not be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228-1229.)

IV.     ANALYSIS

A.    Meet and Confer

In support of the demurrer with a motion to strike, Defendants submit the declaration of defense counsel Lisa Shyner. Counsel declares that on May 8, 2024, she spoke telephonically with opposing counsel. This declaration is sufficient under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41.

B.    Conversion

Conversion is “the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another.” (Lee v. Hanley, (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 1225 [quotations omitted].) To establish a claim for conversion, a plaintiff must plead: “(1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Ibid. [quotations omitted].)

Here, Plaintiff alleged she has an ownership right to any monies Nationwide paid to Defendants, Defendants refused to pay Plaintiff the monies issued by Nationwide, and she is damaged from being denied those monies. With respect to her ownership interest, Plaintiff alleges that she filed a third-party claim with Nationwide and that Defendants improperly circumvented her claim to obtain payment from Nationwide directly for repair work it has not done. (See FAC, ¶13.) Defendants argue, without any authority, that Plaintiff had no ownership rights to the funds at issue. Their argument is deficient as the court cannot ascertain whether they are raising a legal argument (that Plaintiff could never be entitled to this type of fund under the law) or a factual argument (that Plaintiff cannot prove she has any ownership right). If the latter, then Defendants would be disputing the factual underpinnings of the right to the sum which would not be a basis for a demurrer. If the former, then they cite no authority for the proposition that Plaintiff could not obtain any money from Nationwide. 

By analogy, many automobile insurance policies allow a third party to file a claim directly with the insurer for damage to their vehicle caused by the insured.  In such an instance, the third party is the claimant, not the insured.  Were the insurance company to pay the claim to the insured, who then refused to turn the proceeds over to the claimant, the law would seem to support a conversion claim.

The demurrer to the third cause of action is overruled.

C.    Fraudulent Misrepresentation

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal. 4th 631, 638.) “[F]raud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice. . . .This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Id. at p. 645 [internal quotation marks and ellipses omitted].)

          Here, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged a claim for fraudulent misrepresentation. She alleges defendant represented to her that they “needed to access her unit” to “assess” damages to the “common areas.”  She further alleges the representations were false at the time they were made and the request was actually so Nationwide could assess damage to Plaintiff’s residence on Defendants’ behalf. Defendants did this to obtain an insurance payment which included reimbursement for Plaintiff’s residence and the common areas. Plaintiff alleges that defendants then wrongfully obtained monies which were due to her for repairs to her home. Plaintiff does not allege who made the representations and what authority that person had to make representation on behalf of Defendants. She also does not allege whether the representations were oral or written. This is insufficient. Fraud must be pled with particularity and Plaintiff’s current allegations are deficient.

          The demurrer to this cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

D.    Motion to Strike Punitive Damages

Defendants move to strike the punitive damages allegations with respect to the claim for conversion (FAC, ¶37), unjust enrichment (FAC, ¶45), fraudulent misrepresentation (FAC, ¶54) and the prayer for punitive damages.

With respect to paragraph 54, the motion to strike is denied as moot based on the court’s ruling on the demurrer.

With respect to paragraphs 37 and 45 and the prayer for punitive damages, the Court agrees with Defendants that the allegations are insufficient. For unjust enrichment, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants, through their misstatements, were unjustly enriched because they received a significant monetary gain and benefit from Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s expenses. With respect to conversion, Plaintiff alleges Defendants wrongfully obtained monies due to her from her third-party claim with Nationwide. However, Plaintiff does not allege any conduct which falls within the definition of malice fraud or oppression; she has alleged two torts, but nothing going beyond the scope of the claims. (See Civ. Code, §3294.) The mere allegation that an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal. App. 3d 159, 166.)

As Plaintiff has not alleged grounds for punitive damages, the motion to strike paragraphs 37 and 45 and the prayer for punitive damages is granted.

V.      CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          The demurrer to the third cause of action for conversion is overruled.  The demurrer to the fifth cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation is sustained with leave to amend. Plaintiff has twenty days leave to amend upon notice of this ruling.  The second amended complaint should also correct the drafting errors discussed in the footnotes above.

          The motion to strike is granted in part and denied in part. The motion to strike paragraph 54 is denied as moot. The motion to strike paragraphs 37, and 45, and the prayer for punitive damages is granted.

          Moving party is ordered to give notice.
