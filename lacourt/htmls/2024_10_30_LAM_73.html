DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV16333    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 73

10/30/24

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

GARCIA, et al. v. KIA AMERICA, INC. (23STCV16333)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/ moving party:  Guy Mizrahi (JSGM Law LLP)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Matthew Proudfoot (Gates, Gonter, Guy, Proudfoot & Muench, LLP)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL DEFENDANT’S FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE (filed 3/19/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to the Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 14, 20, 23, 26, 39, 41 and 43 is GRANTED. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 30 days.

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to the Special Interrogatories, Set One, No. 25 is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

On July 12, 2023, Plaintiffs Elroy Garcia and Pauline Arredondo filed the instant action against Defendant KIA America, Inc. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of Express Warranty under The Song-Beverly Act

2.      Breach of lmplied Warranty of Merchantability under The Song-Beverly Act

3.      Civil Code § 1793.2(b) under The Song-Beverly Act

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On or about March 16, 2021, Plaintiffs acquired a 2021 Kia Sorento, vehicle identification number 5XYRK4LF2MG020013 (“Vehicle”), for paid and/or payable consideration. All owners and lessees of 2021 KIA vehicles receive multiple express warranties directly from Defendant for periods of up to 10 years or 100,000 miles. The Vehicle was delivered to Plaintiffs with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to loss of power, engine and related systems, check engine light, engine misfire, engine failure, powertrain system, auto stop/start, safety recalls, transmission and related systems, excessive days out of service and other defects. However, Defendant has failed to conform the Vehicle to the applicable warranties because said defects, malfunctions, misadjustments, and/or nonconformities continue to exist even after a reasonable number of attempts to repair was given.

 

On March 19, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs move to compel further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 14, 20, 23, 25, 26, 39, 41, and 43.

·       The Special Interrogatories at issue seek the identification of critical witnesses in this “lemon law” action. Specifically, Plaintiff seeks the identification of key witnesses, namely, those who inspected and tested the Vehicle and those who performed warranty repairs on the Vehicle; those who participated in the investigation relating to Plaintiff’s complaints of the Vehicle; the Person Most Knowledgeable of Defendant relating to warranty policies the person in charge of the customer relations department in the district or region having jurisdiction over Plaintiff’s complaint; those who made the decision not to repurchase and/or replace the Vehicle; and the supervisor who was in charge to make sure Defendant properly determines whether a vehicle should be repurchased or replace under the Song Beverly Act. Plaintiff is entitled to know the identity of these witnesses are in order to take their depositions and prepare for trial.

·       Defendant responded to the requests with boilerplate objections and Defendant’s responses are not code compliant.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendant served code-compliant responses.

·       Plaintiffs Seek To Compel Information Previously Produced By Defendant Or Not In Defendant’s Possession, Custody, Or Control, Contrary To California Code And Applicable Case Law. Defendant’s Responses Were Compliant With The Applicable California Code Sections, And Justifiably Preserved Warranted Objections When They Were Served.

 

Plaintiffs did not file a reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

A court may order a party to serve a further response to an interrogatory when any of the following apply: (1) an answer is evasive or incomplete; (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230¿is unwarranted¿or the required specification of those documents is¿inadequate; or (3) an objection is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2030.300(a).)¿ 

 

“[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2017.010.)¿¿ 

 

B.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party must make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).)¿ 

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel states she sent a meet and confer letters to Defendant's counsel regarding deficiencies with the discovery responses at issue. (Le Decl., ¶¶ 14-19.) However, Plaintiffs’ counsel states Defendant has not provided any further supplemental responses and has failed to produce any additional documents. (Le Decl., ¶ 20.) The Court finds the moving party has met its meet and confer obligation. Accordingly, the Court will consider the merits of the motion.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Plaintiffs move to compel further responses to Interrogatory Nos. 14, 20, 23, 25, 26, 39, 41, and 43.

 

a.       Interrogatory Nos. 14, 20 and 23

 

Interrogatory No. 14 states “IDENTIFY all PERSONS who performed warranty repairs upon the SUBJECT VEHICLE.”

 

Interrogatory No. 20 states “IDENTIFY all PERSONS who inspected or tested the SUBJECT VEHICLE during the RELEVANT PERIOD.”

 

Interrogatory No. 23 states “IDENTIFY all PERSONS who have knowledge of Plaintiffs’ notice(s) of DEFECT to YOU OR ANYONE ACTING ON YOUR BEHALF.”

 

Defendant objected to this request, including on the grounds that the request is vague, overly broad, the information is equally available to Plaintiffs, seeks documents that are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and is irrelevant. Defendant also states that subject and without waiving any objections, Defendant “refers Plaintiffs to the service records that [Defendant] requested from the independent authorized dealerships. See also [Defendant’s] Response to Form Interrogatory, Set One, No. 12.1. Investigation and discovery are ongoing and as such [Defendant] reserves the right to amend this response.” As to Interrogatory No. 23, Defendant states it refers Plaintiffs to the Warranty History Inquiry, Customer Care Case Reports and the Techline Assistance Center Case Reports, which contain confidential and proprietary information, and thus will only be produced subject to the Protective Order in this case.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to their case. The requested information is relevant to Plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly claims and asks Defendant to identify key witnesses. To the extent Defendant asserts a privacy privilege, it must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Here, Defendant has not provided a privilege log.

 

The motion to compel further response to Special Interrogatory Nos. 14, 20 and 23 is granted.

 

b.      Interrogatory No. 25

 

Interrogatory No. 25 states “IDENTIFY all PERSONS responsible for the customer relations department in the district or region having jurisdiction over Plaintiff’s complaints during the RELEVANT PERIOD.”

 

Defendant objects to this interrogatory as follows:

“1. This Interrogatory as vague, ambiguous, overly broad, burdensome, oppressive, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

2. This interrogatory is vague and ambiguous as to “responsible for the customer relations department” as to be unintelligible, burdensome and harassing.’”

 

The Court agrees that the interrogatory is vague, and Plaintiffs do not provide clarification as to persons “responsible for the customer relations department.” The motion to compel further response to Special Interrogatory No. 25 is denied.

 

c.       Interrogatory No. 26

 

Interrogatory No. 26 states “IDENTIFY YOUR present employee in California who is most knowledgeable regarding YOUR warranty policies, including but not limited to any policies YOU may have had regarding the replacement or reimbursement of allegedly defective automobiles, during the RELEVANT PERIOD.”

 

Defendant responds to this interrogatory as follows:

 

“1. KA objects to this Interrogatory as being vague, ambiguous, specifically as to the term “most knowledgeable.”

Without waiving these objections, KA responds as follows: There is not a single individual responsive to this Interrogatory. KA will produce an appropriate corporate representative in response to a proper notice pursuant to the Code of Civil Procedure. Investigation and discovery are ongoing and as such KA reserves the right to amend this response.”

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to their case. The requested information is relevant to Plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly claims and asks Defendant to identify key witnesses. To the extent Defendant asserts a privacy privilege, it must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Here, Defendant has not provided a privilege log.

 

The motion to compel further response to Special Interrogatory No. 26 is granted.

 

d.      Interrogatory Nos. 39, 41 and 43

 

Interrogatory No. 39 states “Identify the individual(s) whose responsibility it is to supervise to ensure that YOU are properly determining whether a vehicle should be repurchased or replaced pursuant to The Song-Beverly Warranty Act.”

 

Interrogatory No. 41 states “Identify all person(s) involved in YOUR investigation, including any individuals with whom you communicated regarding the subject vehicle.”

 

Interrogatory No. 43 states “Identify all individuals responsible for YOUR decision whether or not repurchase or replace the Subject Vehicle.”

 

Defendant objected to this request, including on the grounds that the request is vague, overly broad, lacks the requisite specificity, seeks privileged and confidential information, seeks documents that are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and is irrelevant.

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have demonstrated good cause as to why the information sought in these requests is necessary and relevant to their case. The requested information is relevant to Plaintiffs’ Song-Beverly claims and asks Defendant to identify key witnesses. To the extent Defendant asserts a privacy privilege, it must submit a privilege log identifying the documents and the grounds for establishing protection for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Here, Defendant has not provided a privilege log.

 

The motion to compel further response to Special Interrogatory Nos. 39, 41 and 43 is granted.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to the Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 14, 20, 23, 26, 39, 41 and 43 is GRANTED. Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses within 30 days.

Plaintiffs’ motion to compel further responses to the Special Interrogatories, Set One, No. 25 is DENIED.


Case Number: 24STCV21285    Hearing Date: October 30, 2024    Dept: 73

10/30/24

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

DANIEL LITMAN, et al. v. LOWLY PALACE, LLC (24STCV21285)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Daniel Litman and Paige Lopynski (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Elliot Gipson (Elliot Gipson PC)

 

demurrer AND MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE complaint (filed 9/25/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2024, Plaintiffs Daniel Litman and Paige Lopynski, collectively known as “BONNIE X CLYDE” (“Plaintiffs”), filed this action against Defendant Lowly Palace, LLC.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. On September 9, 2022, Plaintiffs and Defendant entered into a Recording Agreement (hereinafter “Agreement”) for the production and promotion of music by Plaintiffs. On June 9, 2023, the Agreement was amended (hereinafter “Amendment”) to increase the Recording Commitment and Marketing Fund for the Option Period. The Agreement, as amended, provided for a total Marketing Fund of $440,000, requiring mutual written approval for all marketing expenditures. Section 5B of the Agreement stipulates that the Marketing Fund shall be expended within 18 months of the final commercial release, which is now defined as August 2, 2024. Despite Plaintiffs’ repeated requests for written confirmations of expenditures, Defendant has explicitly stated their refusal to spend the marketing funds unless a mutually agreed upon plan, which Defendant has consistently obstructed, is reached. On August 12, 2024, Plaintiffs issued a formal Notice of Termination of the Recording Agreement dated September 9, 2022, as amended on June 9, 2023, between the Plaintiffs and Defendant. This termination notice was necessitated by the Defendant’s continued and material breaches of the Agreement.

 

The body of Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

C/A 1:  Anticipatory Breach of Contract

C/A 2:  Preliminary Injunction

C/A 3:  Declaratory Judgment

C/A 4: Contract Termination

C/A 5: Revocation of Licenses

 

On September 25, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike, arguing:

·       Defendant seeks the dismissal of Plaintiffs’ Complaint in its entirety; that the causes of action for preliminary injunction (Second Cause of Action), declaratory judgment (Third Cause of Action) contract termination (Fourth Cause of Action), revocation of licenses (Fifth Cause of Action) be dismissed without leave to amend; that Plaintiffs’ request for legal fees and costs be struck; that Plaintiffs’ transcriptions, summaries and opinions in its exhibits be struck; and that Exhibit I to the Complaint be struck in its entirety.

·       The Complaint alleges seven causes of action on the cover page but only five causes of action in its body.

·       Plaintiffs Do Not Allege Facts Sufficient to Constitute their Causes of Action.

·       The fourth and fifth causes of action for Contract Termination and Revocation of Licenses are not valid cause of action.

·       Plaintiffs’ Request for Attorneys’ Fees is Improper.

o   The Agreement and subsequent Amendment attached to Plaintiffs’ Complaint does not provide for attorneys’ fees in the event of a breach. Further, Plaintiffs have chosen to appear pro se and cannot be awarded attorneys’ fees.

·       Plaintiffs’ Exhibits Contain Inadmissible Hearsay and Should be Struck.

o   Plaintiffs submitted typed reproductions of each exhibit attached to the Complaint. These transcriptions constitute inadmissible hearsay and should be struck. Cal. Evid. Code § 1200.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs have adequately pleaded all necessary elements for their causes of action, including Anticipatory Breach of Contract, Preliminary Injunction, Declaratory Judgment, and entitlement to Attorneys’ Fees.

·       Plaintiffs request leave to amend Complaint to address the fourth and fifth causes of action

o   Plaintiffs acknowledge that revocation of licenses is a remedy associated with contract termination. Plaintiffs respectfully request leave to amend the Complaint to remove this as a separate cause of action and include it as part of the remedies sought.

·       The motion to strike should be denied.

 

Defendant filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs blatantly disregard page limits and have improperly filed the equivalent of two oversized memorandums in support of their Opposition to Defendant’s Demurrer. The Court should not consider Plaintiffs’ papers. California Rule of Court Rule 3.1113(g) and 3.1300(d).

·       Plaintiffs quoted nonexistent cases in their opposition.

·       Plaintiffs have not plausibly refuted any of the manifest deficiencies in their Complaint

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating he sent an email to Plaintiffs identifying the alleged deficiencies in the Complaint and had a telephonic meet and confer conference with Plaintiffs on September 17, 2024. (Gipson Decl., ¶¶ 3-5.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligation. Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

B.      Procedural Deficiencies with Plaintiffs’ Opposition

 

As an initial matter, Defendant asserts that the Court should refuse to consider Plaintiffs’ opposition.

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiffs’ opposition referenced nonexistent cases and was improperly oversized. Memorandums that exceed their respective page limits are deemed late. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rules 3.1113(g), 3.1300(d).) Here, Plaintiffs’ filed a 21-page Opposition and a 24-page Memorandum of Points and Authorities. Defendant asserts the Opposition and Memorandum combined contained thirty-seven pages of arguments - which exceeds the permitted fifteen pages. (Cal. Rule of Court 3.1113(d).) Although Plaintiffs are pro per litigants, in propria persona parties are treated like any other party and thus must adhere to the rules of procedure. (See, e.g., Nwosu v. Uba (2004) 122 Cal.App.4th 1229, 1246-47.) 

 

Nevertheless, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the opposition. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (d); Juarez v. Wash Depot Holdings, Inc. (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1197, 1202.) 

 

C.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

D.     First Cause of Action: Anticipatory Breach of Contract

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action arguing that Plaintiffs fail to plead the requisite elements.

 

The elements necessary to plead anticipatory breach of contract are: (1) plaintiff's full performance up to time of repudiation; (2) that he was ready, willing and able to complete performance, except for the expressed, unretracted and unequivocal repudiation; and (3) that he suffered damages thereby.  (Daum v. Superior Court, Sutter County (1964) 228 Cal.App.2d 283, 288-89.)   

 

An anticipatory breach occurs when one of the parties to a bilateral contract repudiates the contract expressly or impliedly.  (Mammoth Lakes Land Acquisition, LLC v. Town of Mammoth Lakes (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 435, 463.)  “When a promisor repudiates a contract, the injured party faces an election of remedies: he can treat the repudiation as an anticipatory breach and immediately seek damages for breach of contract, thereby terminating the contractual relation between the parties, or he can treat the repudiation as an empty threat, wait until the time for performance arrives and exercise his remedies for actual breach if a breach does in fact occur at such time.”  (Id.)  An essential element of an anticipatory breach is that repudiation by the promisor occur before his performance is due.  (Gold Mining & Water Co. v. Swinerton (1943) 23 Cal.2d 19, 29.)   

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant’s statements and actions clearly indicate an intention not to perform their contractual obligations regarding the expenditure of the Marketing Fund.

 

In Plaintiffs’ opposition, they argue they performed their obligations by delivering musical works to Defendant and submitting a marketing plan. However, these allegations are not pled in the Complaint. Therefore, Plaintiffs fail to allege their full performance of the contract and that they were ready, willing and able to complete performance.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

E.      Third Cause of Action: Declaratory Judgment

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ third cause of action fails.

 

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.”  (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, cleaned up.)  “[T]here is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved.”  (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366, cleaned up.)

 

The Complaint alleges that a genuine dispute exists between the parties concerning their respective rights and obligations under the Agreement. The Complaint further alleges Plaintiffs seek a declaratory judgment that Defendant is obligated to expend the Marketing Fund within the specified timeframe as stipulated in the Agreement, and that Defendant’s refusal to do so constitutes an anticipatory breach.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs fail to allege with particularity any facts that underlie the alleged “genuine dispute between the parties”, they simply conclude that a controversy exists. Moreover, Defendant argues that another form of adequate relief is available. Defendant contends that under their cause of action for Declaratory Judgment, Plaintiffs plainly ask the Court to adjudicate their cause of action for anticipatory breach of contract.

 

The Court agrees. The Complaint seeks a declaratory judgment that Defendant is obligated to expend the Marketing Fund within the specified timeframe as stipulated in the Agreement, and that Defendant’s refusal to do so constitutes an anticipatory breach.” (Complaint, ¶ 32; emphasis added.) Plaintiffs fail to show how their request for declaratory relief amounts to anything more than a claim for anticipatory breach.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

F.       Second, Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action: Preliminary Injunction, Contract Termination and Revocation of Licenses

 

Defendant demurs to the second, fourth and fifth causes of action arguing that they are not causes of action, and further no facts are sufficiently alleged to justify injunctive relief.

 

The Court agrees. “Injunctive relief is a remedy, not a cause of action.” (City of S. Pasadena v. Dep't of Transportation, 29 Cal. App. 4th 1280, 1293 (1994) (“A permanent injunction is merely a remedy for a proven cause of action. It may not be issued if the underlying cause of action is not established.”) (citation omitted); MaJor v. Miraverde Homeowners Assn., 7 Cal. App. 4th 618, 623 (1992) (“A preliminary injunction is an interim remedy designed to maintain the status quo pending a decision on the merits. It is not, in itself, a cause of action. Thus, a cause of action must exist before injunctive relief may be granted.”) (citing Shell Oil Co. v. Richter, 52 Cal. App. 2d 164, 168 (1942)); Allen v. City of Sacramento, 234 Cal. App. 4th 41, 65 (2015) (affirming that the trial court did not err in sustaining Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action for an injunction.); Dep't of Fair Emp. & Hous. v. Superior Ct. of Kern Cnty., 54 Cal. App. 5th 356, 384 (2020) (“[A] preliminary injunction may be sought only when the underlying cause of action on which the provisional remedy rests is presented for decision through the pleadings.”). Thus, Plaintiffs’ second cause of action for preliminary injunction is not a cause of action. In Plaintiffs’ opposition, they concede that their fourth and fifth causes of action are not causes of action.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the second, fourth and fifth causes of action is SUSTAINED.

 

G.     Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendant requested the Court to strike portions of the Complaint as follows:

1.      Complaint, Paragraph 40 [“Plaintiffs seek reimbursement for legal fees and costs, estimated at $30,000.”]

2.      Complaint, page 10, Paragraph E [“Legal fees and costs in the amount of $20,000.”]

3.      Complaint at Exhibit A, at pp. 19-26 (stamped 08-015)

4.      Complaint at Exhibit B, at pp. 31-32 (stamped 020-21)

5.      Complaint at Exhibit C, at pp. 34-36 (stamped 023-25)

6.      Complaint at Exhibit D, at pp. 38-41 (stamped 027-30)

7.      Complaint at Exhibit E, at pp. 43-48 (stamped 032-37)

8.      Complaint at Exhibit F, at pp. 50-52 (stamped 039-41), 56-64 (stamped 045-53)

9.      Complaint at Exhibit G, at pp. 66-68 (stamped 055-57), 71-76 (stamped 060-65)

10.  Complaint at Exhibit H, at pp. 78-79 (stamped 67-68), 82-87 (stamped 071-76)

11.  Complaint at Exhibit I, at pp. 89-108 (stamped 078-97)

12.  Complaint at Exhibit J, at pp. 110-118 (stamped 099-107)

13.  Complaint at Exhibit K, at pp. 120-125 (stamped 109-114), 128-131 (stamped 117-120)

 

a.       Attorneys’ Fees

 

Defendant moves to strike the request for attorneys’ fees. Defendants argues these requests are improper because Plaintiffs are appearing pro se and are not entitled to attorneys’ fees. Further, the Agreement and subsequent Amendment attached to Plaintiffs’ Complaint do not provide for attorneys’ fees.

 

Nevertheless, the Court notes that the Complaint is uncertain as the Complaint contains contradicting allegations. Plaintiffs allege they are requesting legal fees and costs estimated at $30,000 and then allege they are requesting legal fees and costs in the amount of $20,000. Plaintiffs also fail to point to a provision of the contract that provides for legal fees.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to strike attorneys’ fees is GRANTED.

 

b.      Exhibits

 

Defendant moves to strike portions of the Complaint’s exhibits because they contain inadmissible hearsay.

 

Plaintiffs submitted typed reproductions of each exhibit attached to the Complaint. (Complaint, Exhibits A-K.) The Court agrees that these transcriptions constitute inadmissible hearsay and should be stricken. (Cal. Evid. Code § 1200.) Further, Plaintiffs fail to authenticate, Exhibit I, which is purported to be a transcript of a phone call.

 

Therefore, Defendant’s motion to strike the requested portions of the Complaint’s Exhibits is GRANTED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED.
