I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Aznive Sarkissian (“Plaintiff”) alleges she was injured after slipping on a liquid on the floor of Defendant Don Chava Foods, Inc. dba La Corona Bar & Grill’s (“Defendant”) restroom. (See Complaint, ¶¶ GN-1, Prem.L-1.)Defendant now moves for summary judgment as to both Plaintiff’s causes of action for general negligence and premises liability.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On March 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging (1) General Negligence; and (2) Premises Liability.

On August 11, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

On August 11, 2023, Defendant filed its Cross-Complaint, alleging (1) Implied & Equitable Indemnity;(2) Contribution; (3) Comparative Fault; and (4) Declaratory Relief.

On February 13, 2024, this Court granted in part Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further responses to its Form Interrogatories and denied Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s further responses to its Special Interrogatories.

On August 8, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment (“Motion”).

On October 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition.

On October 25, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply.  

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (See Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing such motions, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) Thus, summary judgment or summary adjudication is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) [1]; Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.) 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate or establish an essential element. (See Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must be denied where the moving party’s evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition or where the opposition is weak. (See Leyva v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475; Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384 & 387.) When a plaintiff is the moving party, the plaintiff has a higher burden: it must “present evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not.” (See Aguilar, 25 Cal.4th at 845 (emphasis added).) “The burden of the moving party is to persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find.” (PP Mortgage, Ltd. v. Bizar (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 773, 776 (emphasis in original) (cleaned up).)

Once the moving party has met its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material fact exists to a cause of action or a defense thereto. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(o)(2).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Evidentiary Issues

The Court SUSTAINS Defendant’s evidentiary objection No. 1 on hearsay as to statements made by and agreements between “other women.” The Court OVERRULES Defendant’s objection No. 2 as the grounds all go to the weight, not the admissibility of these statements.

The Court OVERRULES Plaintiff’s objections even though they were not raised in compliance with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1354. Plaintiff’s counsel is cautioned that in the future, the Court may overrule the objections due to procedural noncompliance and forego the sort of substantive analysis it performed here.

B.             Undisputed Material Facts

Plaintiff alleges injuries from a slip and fall in Defendant’s restroom on March 12, 2021, at around 9:30 p.m. (See Defendant’s Reply to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) No. 1.) After Plaintiff’s fall, another customer in the restroom, Reina Ayon (“Ayon”), crawled under the door of the stall Plaintiff was in, unlocked the door, and went to look for help. (See UMF No. 9.) One of Defendant’s employees inspected the restroom around 9:00 p.m. the night of Plaintiff’s fall and the restroom was clean and dry. (See UMF No. 14.) There were no other reports of cleanliness issues or liquid on the floor the night of Plaintiff’s fall. (See UMF No. 15.) There have been no prior similar incidents of a customer slipping in the restroom. (See UMF No. 17.)  

C.             Triable Issues of Material Fact Exist.

Plaintiff levies two claims against Defendant: general negligence and premises liability. (See Complaint.) Defendant moves for summary judgment as to both.

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.” (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.) “The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises; accordingly, mere possession with its attendant right to control conditions on the premises is a sufficient basis for the imposition of an affirmative duty to act. But the duty arising from possession and control of property is adherence to the same standard of care that applies in negligence cases.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158 (cleaned up).) 

“To comply with this duty, a person who controls property must inspect

[the premises] or take other proper means to ascertain their condition and,

if a dangerous condition exists that would have been discovered by the exercise

of reasonable care, has a duty to give adequate warning of or remedy it.” (Staats

v. Vintner’s Golf Club, LLC (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 826, 833 (cleaned up).)

“Because the owner is not the insurer of the visitor’s personal safety, the owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is a key to

establishing its liability. Although the owner’s lack of knowledge is not a defense, [t]o impose liability for injuries suffered by an invitee due to [a] defective condition of the premises, the owner or occupier must have either actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition or have been able by the exercise of ordinary care to discover the condition, which if known to him, he should realize as involving an unreasonable risk to invitees on his premises.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206 (cleaned up).)

          Defendant makes three arguments: (1) There is no evidence that a dangerous condition was present; (2) Defendant did not have actual or constructive notice of any alleged dangerous condition; and (3) Plaintiff cannot establish that Defendant caused Plaintiff’s fall. (See Motion.)

In support of its arguments, Defendant submits evidence that Plaintiff did not observe any liquid on the floor of the restroom stall before or after she fell, and that she did not recall if her hands or clothes were wet after she fell (Defendant’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“Defendant’s Proposed UMF”) Nos. 6-8); that Ayon had used the same stall as Plaintiff just prior to Plaintiff’s use and the stall was clean and dry (Defendant’s Proposed UMF Nos. 3-4); that Ayon crawled under the stall door to unlock it for Plaintiff but that she did not see any liquid when she crawled under the door nor did her clothes get wet (UMF Nos. 9-11); that one of Defendant’s employees inspected the restroom around 9:00 p.m. the night of Plaintiff’s fall and the restroom was clean and dry (UMF No. 14); and that there were no other reports of cleanliness issues or liquid on the floor the night of Plaintiff’s fall, nor have there been prior similar incidents of a customer slipping in the restroom (UMF Nos. 15, 17).

The Court finds Defendant has submitted sufficient evidence to meet its initial burden of proof on summary judgment. Critical to Defendant’s argument is Ayon’s declaration. Ayon declares, in relevant part, “[a]t or just before 9:30p.m., I used the women’s restroom. The stall was not dirty nor was there any liquid on the floor when I used it. As I walked out of the stall, I held it open for an elderly woman who was waiting to use the restroom. Her granddaughter was with her. While I was washing my hands at the sink, I heard a thud. I looked under the stall walls and saw the woman on the ground. I crawled under the stall door and unlocked it for the woman. When I crawled under the door, I did not see any liquid on the ground and my clothes did not get wet.” (Declaration of Reina Ayon filed August 8, 2024 (“Ayon Decl.”), ¶ 3.) From this, Defendant contends the stall was not dirty and there was no liquid on the floor immediately before Plaintiff’s use of the same stall. Because both Plaintiff’s causes of action are centered on the presence of liquid on the floor, Defendant’s evidence to the contrary is sufficient to negate Plaintiff’s claims and meet Defendant’s initial burden of proof.

The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate a triable issue of material fact. The Court finds Plaintiff has met her burden. Plaintiff submits the declaration of Plaintiff’s granddaughter, Christine Sarkissian (“Christine”), who went to the restroom with Plaintiff at the time of Plaintiff’s fall. (See Declaration of Christine Sarkissian filed October 16, 2024 (“Christine Decl.”), ¶ 6.) Christine declares “[m]y grandmother went into the larger handicapped stall and I waited outside the stall. At the same time an unknown woman came out of the smaller stall. We heard the sound of my grandmother falling….the unknown woman got down quickly and crawled under the door to unlock it so that I could get in to assist her. [¶…The floor was wet with water that seemed to be coming from behind or under the toilet.” (Christine Decl., ¶¶ 7-8.)

Plaintiff thus submits admissible evidence disputing Ayon’s immediate prior use of the stall at issue, and, by way of, Ayon’s claims that there was no liquid on the ground when she used the stall, thereby creating a triable issue of material fact. Christine’s declaration also demonstrates a dispute as to whether there was liquid on the ground immediately after Plaintiff’s fall, as her declaration that there was water on the ground is in direct contrast to Ayon’s declaration that there was no liquid on the ground when she crawled under the door.

          Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

          IT IS SO ORDERED.

I.              BACKGROUND 

This is a claim and delivery of personal property action between Plaintiff Financial Services Vehicle Trust, by and through its servicer, BMW Financial Services NA, LLC (“Plaintiff”), and Defendants K & K Consulting Group (“K & K”) and Karo Kaladjiyan (“Kaladjiyan”). Plaintiff alleges Defendants stopped making payments on a 2023 BMW 1760X, VIN WBY53EJ03PCM73665 (“Vehicle”), and, as the owner and holder of a first priority security interest in the Vehicle, Plaintiff now has a right to take possession of the Vehicle. (See Complaint, pg. 3 ¶ 8.) Plaintiff seeks a writ of possession.

II.           PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On July 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract (2) Breach of Guaranty (3) Common Count (4) Claim & Delivery and (5) Conversion.

On August 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Application for Pre-Trial Writ of Possession against K & K (“Application”) as well as another against Kalaydjiyan which is currently set for hearing on November 6, 2024

Defendant has filed neither a responsive pleading in the action nor an opposition to this Application.  

III.        LEGAL STANDARD

“Upon the filing of the complaint or at any time thereafter, the plaintiff may apply pursuant to this chapter for a writ of possession by filing a written application for the writ with the court in which the action is brought.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 512.010(a).)[1] 

Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. § 512.010(b), the application must be submitted under oath and include: 

“(1) A showing of the basis of the plaintiff's claim and that the plaintiff is entitled to possession of the property claimed. If the basis of the plaintiff's claim is a written instrument, a copy of the instrument shall be attached. 

(2) A showing that the property is wrongfully detained by the defendant, of the manner in which the defendant came into possession of the property, and, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the reason for the detention. 

(3) A particular description of the property and a statement of its value. 

(4) A statement, according to the best knowledge, information, and belief of the plaintiff, of the location of the property and, if the property, or some part of it, is within a private place which may have to be entered to take possession, a showing that there is probable cause to believe that such property is located there. 

(5) A statement that the property has not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to a statute; or seized under an execution against the property of the plaintiff; or, if so seized, that it is by statute exempt from such seizure.” 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 512.010(b).)

Before the hearing on the Writ of Possession, the Defendant must be served with (1) a copy of the summons and complaint; (2) a Notice of Application and Hearing; and (3) a copy of the application and any affidavit in support thereof. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 512.030.) 

“The writ will be issued if the court finds that the plaintiff's claim is probably valid and the other requirements for issuing the writ are established.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 512.040(b).) “A claim has ‘probable validity’ where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 511.090.)   

Prior to the issuance of a writ of possession, the Plaintiff must file an undertaking “in an amount not less than twice the value of the defendant's interest in the property or in a greater amount,” unless the court finds the defendant has no interest in the property. (Code Civ. Proc. § 515.010(a), (b).) 

IV.         ANALYSIS

Plaintiff seeks a writ of possession for the Vehicle based on Defendant’s alleged breach of the Vehicle’s lease agreement (“Lease”). (See Application, ¶ 14; Declaration of Donald Skeen filed August 26, 2024 (“Skeen Decl.”).)

The Court finds Plaintiff has established probable validity of the Plaintiff’s claim to possession of the Vehicle. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 512.040(b), § 511.090.) Plaintiff has presented the Lease, indicating Plaintiff’s right to immediate possession upon Defendant’s default, including the right to electronically track the vehicle through the device equipped on the Vehicle, and evidence that Plaintiff is the lienholder. (See Application, ¶¶ 12, 16-17; Skeen Decl., ¶¶ 12, 16-17, Ex. 2.) Plaintiff has included a description of the vehicle and its value. (See Application, ¶¶ 8, 22; Skeen Decl., ¶¶ 8, 22, Exs. 1, 4.) Plaintiff has also included the required statements regarding the Vehicle’s location and status. (See Application, ¶¶ 18, 21; Skeen Decl., ¶¶ 18, 21.) Effective September 27, 2024, Defendant was served as required by Code of Civil Procedure § 512.030. (See Proof of Service filed September 19, 2024.)

The Court further finds Plaintiff’s claim is probably valid and the Vehicle is located at 78 N. Coolwater Rd., Bell Canyon, CA 91307. (See Application, ¶ 21; Skeen Decl., ¶ 21.)

Plaintiff has not filed an undertaking, but the Court finds an undertaking unnecessary. Per Code of Civil Procedure § 515.010(a), Defendant has no interest in the Vehicle because the amount Defendant owes under the Lease exceeds the market value of the Vehicle. (See Application, ¶¶ 15, 22; Skeen Decl., ¶¶ 15, 22, Exs. 3, 4.) Further, Defendant has no interest because they have no equity or ownership interest in the Vehicle as it is a true lease. (See Application ¶ 23; Skeen Decl., ¶ 23.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Application.

V.            CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Application for Writ of Pre-Trial Possession.
