I.            INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Jose R. Rocaleon filed this action on September 30, 2019, after sustaining injuries in a motorcycle crash while driving his Harley-Davidson 2018 Softail FXLR. Plaintiff asserts claims for: (1) negligence, (2) negligent products liability, (3) strict products liability for failure to warn, (4) strict products liability for defective design, and (5) strict products liability for defective manufacturing.

Defendants Harley-Davidson Motor Company Group, LLC (“Harley-Davidson”) (erroneously sued as “Harley-Davidson Motor Company, Inc.”) and TMCHB, Inc. dba Huntington Beach Harley-Davidson (substituted in for Doe 1 on April 29, 2020) (“TMCHB”) (collectively, “Defendants”) move for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of 4th and 5th causes of action for strict products liability based on theories of defective design and defective manufacturing.

Plaintiff’s complaint also named Precision Performance, LLC dba TNT Cycle Works (“Precision Performance”) as a defendant. On October 7, 2022, Defendants filed a cross-complaint against Precision Performance for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.

On October 11, 2023, Defendants filed a motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication.

On May 24, 2024, Defendants filed a motion for a protective order.

On July 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief, along with evidentiary objections and declarations of two experts and his counsel. Plaintiff also filed a notice of lodging of documents produced pursuant to protective order. As no motion to seal was filed by Defendants, the Court ORDERS these documents to be placed in the public record. (CRC 2.551(b).)

On July 9, 2024, Defendants filed reply papers in support of its motion for summary judgment/summary adjudication and motion for protective order.

II.          MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT/SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

A.   Legal Standard

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854.) The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)

“Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)

B.   Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff submits 37 objections to the Declaration of Leah Orloff (“Orloff”) on the grounds that the statements and opinions made within lack personal knowledge and foundation. Orloff testified in deposition on April 26, 2024, that she was not involved in the pre-development phase of products or testing of the “Softail platform” or the seat for the 2018 FXLR. (Plaintiff’s Ex. E, 30:18-31:24, 32:4-9, 38:10-13, 63:15-65:12.)

Objection No. 1 is overruled because Orloff has personal knowledge of her own role and obligations of her employment.

Next, Objection Nos. 2-5, 7-8, 10, 26, 27, 28, 29, 30, 31, 32, address Orloff’s ability to authenticate the Owner’s manual, service manual, PDI checklist, and engineering drawings attached to her declaration as well as other documents produced in discovery. As an employee who “supports and participates in investigation of field complaints”, the Court is satisfied that Orloff has sufficient knowledge of Harley-Davidson’s recordkeeping and operations to authenticate these documents. These objections are OVERRULED.

Objection Nos. 6 and 9 are OVERRULED. Orloff has personal knowledge of the materials that she reviewed and can identify them.

Objection Nos. 11 through 20 are OVERRULED. These objections are directed towards Orloff’s description of the manufacturing of the Subject Motorcycle, which is separate from pre-testing development.

Objection 21: SUSTAINED. Orloff’s claim that there was no issue with loose seat fasteners at the time the Subject Motorcycle was received by the dealership is based on a PDI checklist attached as Exhibit AA. Exhibit AA is blank and does not appear to concern specifically the Subject Motorcycle, therefore, her statement that “the PDI checklist reflects the Subject 2018 FXLR passed the dealership pre-delivery inspection” lacks foundation. 

Objection 23-25, 33-35, and 37 are SUSTAINED. As Orloff was not involved with the pre-development or product testing of the Softail platform, which includes the 2018 FXLR, she lacks foundation to attest to the “design objectives” or the tests performed. In response to Plaintiff’s objections, Defendants merely repeat what Orloff already states in her declaration. Although Orloff may have reviewed testing data, they are not attached to her declaration and it is not evident how Orloff became aware of the specific tests and design objectives, considering that Orloff testified in deposition that she does not work in the Durability Department.

Objection No. 36, related to warranty searches, is OVERRULED.

C.   Discussion

Plaintiff alleges that he sustained serious injuries and damages when the seat of his motorcycle dislodged while he was riding it. (Compl., ¶ 8.) The basic facts underlying his purchase of the motorcycle and those leading up to his accident are undisputed. For instance, the parties agree that Plaintiff purchased a 2018 Harley-Davidson FXLR Softail Motorcycle (“Subject Motorcycle”) on May 23, 2018 from TMCHB and the Subject Motorcycle had an odometer reading of seven miles. (UMF Nos. 1, 2.) Plaintiff took the Subject Motorcycle to Precision Performance for a 1,000 mile service on June 9, 2018, and took it home later that same day (UMF Nos. 3, 4.) The next morning, on June 10, 2018, Plaintiff rode the Subject Motorcycle to the Mount Wilson Observatory. (UMF No. 6.) He experienced no difficulty with its performance on the ascent. (UMF No. 7.)

1.   . Summary Judgment and The Existence of a Design Defect

Defendants argue that there is no evidence of a design defect because Plaintiff’s discovery responses are “noticeably void of any specificity on the defect claim or supporting evidence for the claim.” (Motion, p. 11.) Defendants cite to Plaintiff’s response to inspection demands and special interrogatories seeking documents and information supporting Plaintiff’s contention that a design defect existed, served on March 19, 2020. Plaintiff’s responses, while not the model of completeness, sufficiently identify the design defect by stating that Plaintiff’s seat “became detached during normal operation of the subject vehicle and the screw system to hold the seat down is defectively designed.” (Lonnie Decl., Ex. C, pp.6-7.) While this response is admittedly not detailed, it is not so “factually devoid” as to shift the burden to Plaintiff to prove the existence of triable issues. And even if it did, as further discussed below, Plaintiff sufficiently meets his burden to show that triable issues of material fact exist.

Alternatively, Defendants argue that the evidence negates any finding of a design defect under the risk-benefit test. “[A] product may be found defective in design . . . under either of two alternative tests.  First, a product may be found defective in design if the plaintiff establishes that the product failed to perform as safely as an ordinary consumer would expect when used in an intended or reasonably foreseeable manner.  Second, a product may alternatively be found defective in design if the plaintiff demonstrates that the product’s design proximately caused his injury and the defendant fails to establish, in light of the relevant factors, that, on balance, the benefits of the challenged design outweigh the risk of danger inherent in such design.” (Barker v. Lull Engineering Co. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 413, 432.)  

A non-exhaustive list of factors relevant to the risk-benefit test include “the gravity of the danger posed by the challenged design, the likelihood that such danger would occur, the mechanical feasibility of a safer alternative design, the financial cost of an improved design, and the adverse consequences to the product and to the consumer that would result from an alternative design.”  (Id. at p. 431.)  This test, in short, subjects a manufacturer to liability when ‘the design is not as safe as it should be—while stopping short of making the manufacturer an insurer for all injuries which may result from the use of its product.” (Kim v. Toyota Motor Corp. (2018) 6 Cal.5th 21, 30 (citation omitted).)

Defendants contend that the seat fastener design passes the risk-benefit test. To the extent that they rely on Orloff’s declaration to identify the types of validation tests which were performed on the 2018 FXLR seat, the Court sustains the objections to these descriptions of testing due to Orloff’s lack of personal knowledge of those testing methods and the lack of evidence explaining her knowledge of the testing and design process. Thus, the remainder of Orloff’s declaration endeavors to show that rear fastener system used on the 2018 FXLR seat passes the risk-benefit test. Orloff describes the four mounting points for the motorcycle seat and opines that the risk of the seat dislodging from the frame is low when the rider sits and applies their weight to the seat. (Orloff Decl., ¶¶ 20- 23.) Orloff explains that even without the rear fastener present, the seat is retained to the motorcycle by the rider’s weight while riding within normal motorcycle operating conditions because the weight of the rider places downward pressure and “nests” the seat within the frame to ensure there is no additional fore-aft or side-to-side movement of the seat. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 24.) Orloff further explains the importance of being able to remove the seat “with minimal tools” by a customer on the road in order to make adjustments and perform repairs/maintenance. (Id., ¶ 25-26.)

In opposition, Plaintiff relies on the expert declarations of John Lloyd (“Lloyd”), a human factors expert with a specialization in motor vehicle accident and motorcycle reconstruction. Lloyd describes the condition of the Subject Motorcycle and explains that the presence and direction of scrape marks on the top of the rear fender is consistent with Plaintiff’s account of the crash event, which is that the seat had dislodged from the frame. (Lloyd Decl., ¶ 12.) Lloyd states he can “reasonably envision that, without the rear retention screw, if a rider encountered some roadway imperfections which caused the motorcycle suspension to bounce, thereby affecting their weight applied through the seat, this could unload the under-seat isolator supports, allowing the seat to move” and disengaging it from the frame bracket.  (Id., ¶ 15.) Lloyd states that “[a]ny subsequent lateral force, such as that encountered while leaning the motorcycle in a curve, could shift the center of mass of the seated rider causing the seat and rider to slide off the traveling motorcycle.” (Ibid.) Lloyd opines that an alternate design to the rear retaining screw, such as a seat bolt post and lock nut with a shake-proof washer, would have prevented the seat from separating from the Subject Motorcycle while being operated. (Id., ¶ 17.)

On reply, Defendants argue that Lloyd’s opinions regarding alternative designs for the Subject Motorcycle’s seat screw system should be disregarded because it is speculative and he does not claim expertise as a design engineer or hold a degree in mechanical engineering. Though Defendants did not file formal evidentiary objections, the Court finds that any such objections would be overruled. Lloyd’s opinion is supported by his inspection of the Subject Motorcycle and his experience in human factors and motorcycle accidents. He also sufficiently sets forth his experience in motorcycle accident reconstruction and his personal experience with building custom motorcycles. (Lloyd Decl., ¶ 20.) Accordingly, Plaintiff raises a triable issue of fact regarding the existence of a design defect and Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is DENIED, as is its motion for summary adjudication of the Fourth Cause of Action.

Because Defendants only sought summary adjudication of the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action in the alternative, the Court need not evaluate whether triable issues of material fact exist with respect to Plaintiff’s First, Second, or Third Causes of Action.

2.   Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the Fifth Cause of Action

Defendants’ motion seeks summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the Fifth Cause of Action for manufacturing defect. To show that Plaintiff cannot establish that a manufacturing defect exists, Defendants submit Orloff’s declaration. Orloff is currently employed as a Principal Engineer in Product Integrity at the Harley-Davidson Product Development Center by Harley-Davidson. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 1.) She states that her role comprises three primary areas of responsibility: (1) support product development in warning labels and alerts and assigned platform work, (2) support and participate in investigations of field complaints to determine if issues should be further evaluated, and (3) provide technical support for internal and outside counsel. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 1.)

Orloff was asked to review documents and data concerning Plaintiff’s Subject Motorcycle and assess the design and history of the Subject Motorcycle. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 3.) She reviewed and analyzed the owner’s manual and service manual for the Subject Motorcycle, the Subject Motorcycle’s pre-delivery inspection checklist, warranty and customer contact data, test and validation data for the 2018 FXLR seat, engineering drawings for the 2018 FXLR, durability and test incident report for the 2018 FXLR, standard work instructions and control plans for the 2018 FXLR, seat renderings of the 2018 FXLR, as well as Plaintiff’s pleadings, deposition testimony, and written discovery responses. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 4.)

Orloff states that “[t]he specified torque of the seat screw is dynamically monitored and recorded every cycle and provides a documented and visual indication that the seat screw has been installed and achieved the specified torque. Specifically, a green light on the torque tool signals to the operator that the operation is a ‘PASS’, and if not green, signals in the log that it is a repair condition.” (Orloff Decl., ¶ 8.) At the end of the assembly line, there are three more inspections performed by at least two different operators. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 9.) The end-of-line inspection involves an inspector’s review of an individual motorcycle to determine if there are no missing or loose parts. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 10.) A roll test checks various electrical and mechanical functions while an operator rides the motorcycle on rollers. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 11.) A pre-crating inspection checks for loose or missing parts. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 12.) Any corrections, issues, or anomalies identified or documented during these three tests would be documented, but she did not find any. (Orloff Decl., ¶¶ 10-12.) After the tests are performed and the motorcycles are sent to a dealer, the dealer is required to perform another inspection, referred to as a Pre-Delivery Inspection (“PDI”), in order to check for loose or missing parts that may have been disturbed during shipping. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 13.) The PDI process involves a checklist with instructions, which include: (1) removing the seat and adjusting the suspension for the test rider, (2) installing the seat so that the fastener (or screw) is secured to a specified torque, and (3) “pull[ing] up on the seat to be sure it is secure” prior to a road test. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 14.)

By submitting evidence that there is no documentation reflecting manufacturing issues with the Subject Motorcycle (when such documentation would have existed) Defendants have met their burden to make a prima facie showing that the Subject Motorcycle did not have a manufacturing defect.

Plaintiff’s opposition brief focuses on whether there was a design defect or a failure to warn and his separate statement does not identify evidence of a factual dispute regarding the existence of any manufacturing defect. Instead, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ separate statement is procedurally defective. The Court agrees that the separate statement is deficient. The Court notes that CRC 3.1350(d)(1)(A) requires that the separate statement separately identify each cause of action, claim for damages, issue of duty, or affirmative defense that is the subject of the motion. The separate statement should include only material facts and not any facts that are not pertinent to the disposition of the motion. (CRC 3.1350(d)(2).) Defendants’ motion seeks summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s 4th and 5th causes of action. However, the separate statement only lists 59 undisputed “material” facts under the sole heading which states: “EACH CAUSE OF ACTION ASSERTED AGAINST THE DEFENDANTS FAILS BECAUSE THERE IS NO TRIABLE ISSUE OF MATERIAL FACT SUPPORTING AN ELEMENT OF EACH CAUSE OF ACTION.” There are no subheadings identifying the Fifth Cause of Action and setting forth facts which are specifically material to this claim.

A separate statement is intended to facilitate a court’s review of a motion of this type and Defendants have clearly failed to meet that obligation. Nevertheless, while the Court has discretion to deny a motion for summary adjudication on the grounds that the moving party fails to comply with the requirements governing a separate statement, the Court declines to exercise such discretion here. (See Truong v. Glasser (2009) 18 Cal.App.4th 102, 18.) Though Defendants’ separate statement improperly consolidates all material facts applicable to all causes of action, the total number of material facts is only 59. The separate statement is not so lengthy and unwieldy as to support a claim that the Plaintiff’s due process rights were violated. Plaintiff has effectively been able to address all 59 facts and state whether they are disputed, yet Plaintiff does not point to the existence of any evidence controverting Defendants’ prima facie showing that no triable issue exists as to the Fifth Cause of Action.

The Court also notes that although Plaintiff’s counsel requests a continuance of the hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(h) to conduct the depositions of Defendants’ employees, the request for additional discovery is related to Defendants’ product testing process, not its manufacturing process. (Azizi Decl., ¶ 13.) The party seeking a continuance must show that the facts to be obtained are essential to opposing the motion, that there is reason to believe these facts may exist, and that additional time is needed to obtain these facts. (See Braganza v Albertson's LLC (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 144, 152–153.) Plaintiff does not show how the proposed depositions would reveal facts essential to opposing the motion with respect to his claim that there was a manufacturing defect. Therefore, the request to continue the hearing is denied.

As Plaintiff fails to raise a triable issue of material fact with respect to the existence of a manufacturing defect, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication of the Fifth Cause of Action.

III.        MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

The court, for good cause shown, may order that depositions not be taken at all to protect a party or deponent against “unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.”. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b).) Here, Defendants wish to prevent Plaintiff from deposing the following individuals: Kyle Wick, Kirk Blomdahl, Edgar Camez, Jeff Jennings, and Paul Weiss. Defendants argue that the testimony from these depositions would be duplicative of the 17 hours of testimony that Plaintiff has already obtained from three individuals regarding pre-developmental testing documents for the 2018 FXLR. (Motion, p. 7.)

Defendants claim that the proposed deponents have no personal knowledge regarding pre-development testing and their knowledge is limited to the information in documents which have already been produced. Defendants also argue that the burden imposed to prepare these five individuals for depositions outweighs any possibility that the depositions would lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

These claims are substantiated only by the conclusory declaration of Rochelle L. Smith, who states that the depositions would “place a significant burden” on Harley-Davidson’s business and that the proposed deponents “do not have any percipient knowledge regarding the claims at issue in this lawsuit.” (Smith Decl., ¶ 8.) Smith broadly states that these employees have “numerous obligations as a result of their positions” who cannot “drop their obligations to prepare for depositions and forego a half a day, or more, of work to be deposed.” (Smith Decl., ¶ 9.) These vague statements that lack foundation do not demonstrate good cause for a protective order. (See Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261, 318.)

Defendants’ motion for a protective order is DENIED.

VI.    CONCLUSION

          In light of the foregoing, the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication of the Fourth Cause of Action is DENIED.

The motion for summary adjudication of the Fifth Cause of Action is GRANTED.

The motion for a protective order is DENIED.

Moving party to give notice.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On September 23, 2021, plaintiff Ahmad Heidari (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Marriott International Inc. (“Marriott”). This action arises from a slip and fall that occurred on a pool deck that had allegedly deteriorated anti-slip flooring. On February 24, 2021, Plaintiff added SWVP Warner Center Hotel LLC (“SWVP Warner”) and Merritt Hospitality LLC (“Merritt Hospitality”) as Doe 1 and Doe 2. Marriott was dismissed on March 29, 2021.

On September 23, 2021, the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) was filed. The FAC adds Nafiseh Heidari as a plaintiff (hereafter, collectively with Plaintiff, “Plaintiffs”).

On March 3, 2023, HEI Hotels, LLC (“HEI”) was added as Doe 3.

On April 19, 2024, SWVP Warner, Merritt Hospitality, and HEI Hotels (collectively, “Defendants”) filed this motion for an order clarifying a minute order issued on April 3, 2024 (“April 3 Order”), or alternatively, a protective order against additional discovery.

On June 10, 2024, the Court heard oral argument and continued the hearing to July 16, 2024. The Court additionally ordered supplemental briefing from the parties. Supplemental briefs and a copy of the court reporter’s transcript were filed on July 8, 2024.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (b), “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)¿

As it relates to new or different facts, circumstances, or law under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008, subdivision (a), “the moving party must provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.”¿ (Shiffer v. CBS Corp. (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 246, 255.) Furthermore, “facts of which the party seeking reconsideration was aware at the time of the original ruling are not “new or different.” (In re Marriage of Herr (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1468.)¿

III.        DISCUSSION

A.   Procedural Background

This case was initially assigned to Department 30 of the Spring Street Courthouse (“Department 30”). On January 12, 2024, after the parties submitted that they were ready for trial, the matter was assigned to the Honorable Patrick T. Madden in Department S28 of the Governor George Deukmejian Courthouse (“Department S28”) for trial.  

After being assigned the matter, Judge Madden issued a series of orders ruling on the parties’ motions in limine. On February 7, 2024, Judge Madden set an evidentiary hearing pursuant to section 402 of the Evidence Code to determine whether to allow testimony from Plaintiffs’ expert witness, Matthew Reynolds. The hearing took place on February 29, 2024, and March 1, 2024, and the parties subsequently submitted supplemental briefing on the issue on March 4, 2024.

On March 6, 2024, Judge Madden ruled in favor of Plaintiffs and permitted Mr. Reynolds’s testimony to be presented (“March 6 Order”). Judge Madden also permitted Plaintiffs to add new records and three new witnesses, Jeffrey Block, Bruce Greenfield, and Michael Van Do to the Exhibit and Witness Lists for trial. Judge Madden then gave Defendants the opportunity to depose these three newly-added witnesses and designate a new expert witness to address Mr. Reynolds’s opinions by vacating the trial date and the discovery cut-off date for both fact and expert witness discovery. The matter was returned to the Honorable Lynne M. Hobbs in Department 30 and a trial setting conference was scheduled for April 3, 2024.

Prior to the conference, on March 27, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration arguing that fact discovery has been reopened in its entirety and that the trial setting conference should be continued. Plaintiffs’ counsel claimed that due to Defendants’ gamesmanship, they only recently learned of the possibility of additional defendants who should be held responsible for the unlicensed installation of a pool deck and coping after the initial construction of the pool; Plaintiffs’ counsel stated that written discovery had been propounded on Defendants to determine the identities of those defendants involved in the installation.

In response, on March 29, 2024, Defendants submitted a statement in which they argued that Judge Madden’s ruling reopening discovery was without jurisdiction because it contravened Judge Hobbs’s earlier rulings on Plaintiffs’ previous requests to reopen discovery. Despite Defendants’ contention that whether to reopen discovery was an outstanding issue which needed to be addressed, the issue of reopening discovery was not addressed in the April 3 Order. In the April 3 Order, Judge Hobbs only scheduled the final status conference and trial (both of which have since been vacated after the case was re-assigned to this Independent Calendar court). On April 19, 2024, Defendants filed this motion for clarification or, in the alternative, a protective order.

B.   Defendants’ Request for Clarification

At the outset, although Defendants purport to seek clarification on whether discovery has been reopened by Judge Hobbs’s April 3 Order, Defendants’ motion is an untimely request to review the scope of the March 6 Order issued by Judge Madden which reopened discovery. Defendants claim that Judge Madden’s ruling “implicitly made clear” that any additional discovery could only be conducted by Defendants in response to Plaintiffs’ newly-admitted evidence. (Motion, p. 10.) As persuasively argued by Plaintiffs at oral argument and in their supplemental brief, this claim that only Defendants were entitled to conduct limited discovery is belied by the unqualified vacatur of the trial date, discovery cut-off date, and the date to designate expert witnesses and to conduct expert witness discovery. It is further belied by the fact that five days after Judge Madden issued his March 6 Order, Defendants issued subpoenas for Plaintiff’s medical records, which have nothing to do with the pool decking or testimony presented at the 402 hearing. (Cullen Supp. Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.)

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion for clarification is DENIED.      

C.   Defendants’ Motion for Protective Order

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ discovery requests for information and documents predating their acquisition of the hotel and all documents provided in connection with their purchase of the hotel is unduly burdensome and overbroad. Although Defendants attach copies of Plaintiffs’ written discovery requests, Defendants offer no admissible evidence demonstrating good cause for a protective order. There is no evidence that responding to Plaintiffs’ outstanding discovery would be unduly burdensome for Defendants or that the discovery sought is overbroad. Defendants essentially argue that it is too late for Plaintiffs to conduct this discovery and should have done so earlier. But whether it is “too late” to conduct discovery rests on the discovery cut-off date. Here, Judge Madden vacated the discovery cut-off deadlines. Therefore, Defendants’ arguments lack merit and their motion for a protective order is DENIED.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Defendants’ Motion is DENIED in its entirety.
