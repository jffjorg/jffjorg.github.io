I.                    Background

 

On December 21, 2022, Plaintiff Ely Sandoval (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendant William Zimmer (“Defendant”), alleging a single cause of action for Motor Vehicle Negligence. (Compl., p. 4.) The claim arose from a motor vehicle collision on January 2, 2021 that involved Plaintiff and Defendant at or near the vicinity of 18561 Soledad Canyon Road, City of Santa Clarita, County of Los Angeles, State of California 91351. (Compl., ¶¶ MV-1, MV-2.)

 

On June 20, 2024, after failure of both Plaintiff and Defendant to appear at the scheduled hearing, the Court dismissed Plaintiff’s Complaint without prejudice. (6/20/24 Order.)

 

On September 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Dismissal (the “Motion”). No Opposition has been filed.

 

On October 21, 2024, the Court conducted a hearing on the Motion and found that, while Plaintiff timely filed the Motion and established a right to relief, Plaintiff failed to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 by failing to file proof of service in compliance with Section 1005. Accordingly, the Court continued the Motion to allow Plaintiff to file proof of service of the moving papers in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b).

 

On November 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amended Notice of Motion and Motion that contained supplemental papers responding to the Court’s ruling on October 21, 2024. The supplemental papers include a declaration by Plaintiff’s Counsel and Proof of Service by mail of the Notice of Motion and Motion for an Order to Set Aside/Vacate the Dismissal on Defendant and Defendant’s insurance carrier on November 7, 2024. (Supp. Canter Decl., ¶¶ 9-11, Exh. 11.)

 

In light of these supplemental papers and the attached Proof of Service, the Court finds Plaintiff’s Motion is now in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b). Accordingly, the Court proceeds to decide the Motion on the merits.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for an order vacating the dismissal entered on June 20, 2024 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b).

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief.  (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

The second provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is mandatory, providing: “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties. However, this section shall not lengthen the time within which an action shall be brought to trial pursuant to Section 583.310.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

The general underlying purpose of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits.  (Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838-839.)  

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel, Frank Canter, (“Counsel”) contends that the failure to appear at the June 20, 2024 hearing was due to his inadvertent calendaring error as to the hearing date. (Motion, ¶¶ 13-17; Canter Decl., ¶¶ 3-4.) Counsel argues that this error occurred because the COVID-19 pandemic heavily impacted his law office and his law office suffered a major break-in, which involved damage to computers, that interfered with his ability to timely calendar and locate files for this case. (Canter Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, 6-8.)

 

The Court finds the motion was timely filed on September 24, 2024, within the six-month statutory period. Counsel’s declaration established that the June 20, 2024 dismissal was entered due to his excusable neglect in failing to properly calendar. Counsel noted that this calendaring error occurred because COVID-19 heavily affected his law office and a major break-in happened at his law office in September 2023, which included damage to computers, that interfered with his ability to timely calendar and locate files for this case. (Canter Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, 6-8.) Plaintiff’s resulting failure to appear caused the June 20, 2024 dismissal. (Canter Decl., ¶¶ 15-18). Therefore, pursuant to the mandatory provision of the Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), the Court finds Plaintiff has established a right to relief.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s unopposed Motion is GRANTED.

(a) If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

Additionally, the October 25th Minute Order notes: “Respondent Mary Louise Valenzuela appears in court, is handed a copy of the Court’s Tentative Ruling and is advised of today’s continuance order.” (10/25/24 Order.)

 

On October 31, 2024, Petitioner filed proof of personal service demonstrating Respondent was served with the Petition, Proposed Order, Civil Case Cover Sheet, Civil Case Cover Sheet Addendum and Statement of Location, Notice of Case Assignment, and General Order on June 27, 2024. (10/31/24 Proof of Service.) The Court finds Respondent was provided reasonable notice of the Petition in advance of the new hearing date.

                                                                                                  

II.                 Legal Standard

 

The Mobilehome Residency Law (“MRL”) gives management an injunctive relief remedy for violations of “reasonable” park rules and regulations. (Civ. Code, § 798.88.) Specifically, management may obtain a restraining order against a continuing or recurring violation of any “reasonable rule or regulation.” (Civ. Code, § 798.88, subd. (b); Rancho Santa Paula Mobilehome Park, Ltd. v. Evans (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 1139, 1142.) At the time of the hearing, the court shall issue the injunction if it finds by clear and convincing evidence the existence of a continuing or recurring violation of a “reasonable rule or regulation.” (Civ. Code, § 798.88, subd. (d).) The duration of the injunction may not exceed three years. (Civ. Code, § 798.88, subd. (d).)

 

III.              Discussion

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes the Petition is signed but not dated. (Petn., p. 7.)

 

Substantively, the Petition is verified by an authorized agent for Petitioner, although the name of whom is unknown as the verification signature is indiscernible. (Petn., Verification.) The Petition is supported by a copy of the Rental Agreement between Petitioner and Respondent (Petn., Exh.1); a copy of the Park Rules (Petn., Exh. 2); copies of eight written notices to Respondent and accompanying proofs of service (Petn., Exhs. 3-14); and photographs of continuing violations as of May 29, 2024. (Petn., Exh. 15.) The Rental Agreement states Respondent has resided on the Premises since March 8, 2007. (Petn., Exh. 1, p. 1.) The Rental Agreement incorporates the Park Rules, thereby requiring the residents to abide by the same. (Petn., Exh. 1, ¶ 13.)

 

Respondent has been in violation of Rules 10(A), 10(B), and 9(D) since July 7, 2023, and thereafter. (Petn., Exh. 3-4.) These violations include failing to upkeep landscaping, failing to remove debris, clutter, trash, and improper storage, and failing to repair damaged sewer pipes/lines. (Petn., ¶¶ 3-8.) On July 7, 2023, Petitioner issued two seven-day notices to comply with Park Rules to Respondent. (Petn., Exhs. 3-4.) Respondent did not comply with Park Rules by July 21, 2023, at which time Petitioner issued another seven-day notice. (Petn., Exhs. 5-6.) Respondent again did not comply with the Park Rules by August 3, 2023, at which time Petitioner issued two more seven-day notices. (Petn., Exhs. 7-8.) Respondent also has repeatedly violated Park Rule 4 since August 2023. (Petn., Exhs. 9-12.) These violations include threatening and harassing other Park members and failing to repair damaged sewer pipes/lines. (Petn., ¶¶ 16-20.) On August 23, 2023, Petitioner issued a seven-day notice to Respondent to comply with Park Rule 4(E). (Petn., Exhs. 9-10.) On November 9, 2023, Petitioner issued a seven-day notice to Respondent to comply with Park Rule 4(B). (Petn., Exhs. 11-12.)

 

On May 17, 2024, Petitioner issued a seven-day notice to comply with Park Rules as well as a letter warning that continued non-compliance with Park Rules could result in termination of the lease agreement.  (Petn., Exhs. 13-14.) The violations included harassment of other Park members and failure to upkeep landscaping, repair damaged sewer pipes/lines, remove unapproved storage items, maintain vehicle, supervise pet on Premises, and provide updated pet records to Park Management. (Petn., ¶ 22.) The letter noted that Respondent failed to comply with all previous notices of violations and stated Respondent’s current violations constituted the following: impermissible storage of items on porches, in carports or outside of storage sheds; damaged sewer pipe on Premises; insufficient maintenance of yard; unmaintained vehicle on Premises; having a pet outside in the Park without supervision; having a pet without providing up-to-date records to Park Management; and harassing or threatening other Park residents. (Petn., Exh. 15.) The Court notes that while the Petition states these violations were of Park Rules 9(D), 10(A), 10(B)(5), 19(N), 21(A), and 22(B) (Petn. ¶ 22), the May 2024 letter and notice state these violations were of Rules F(13), F(25), F(3), D(1), G(9), G(2), G(14), and H(7) in the “Park’s Residency Documents.” (Petn., Exh. 13, Exh. A, “Description of Violations – Current Violations.”) Given that the content of both rule sets substantially align and both are promulgated by the Park in accordance with the Rental Agreement, this discrepancy is not dispositive.

 

The violations of Park Rules 9(D), 10(A), 10(B), and 10(B)(5) for “landscaping, presence of prohibited miscellaneous items and trash, the expired tags on the vehicle stored on the Premises, and the overall lack of the maintenance of said vehicle” remained as of May 29, 2024, as demonstrated by photographs taken of the Premises. (Petn., ¶ 26; Petn., Exh. 15.)

 

Therefore, the Petition is supported by clear and convincing evidence of Respondent’s violations of the Park’s reasonable Rules and Regulations. Further, no response to the Petition has been filed showing why an injunction should not issued.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Petitioner Royce Cinderella, LLC’s Verified Petition for Injunctive Relief Pursuant to Civil Code Section 798.88 is GRANTED.

On January 31, 2024, Wawanesa General Insurance Co. (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Jose Rivas Aguero (“Defendant”) and Does 1-20, alleging a Motor Vehicle cause of action. No Opposition has been filed.

 

On October 22, 2024, the Court found that while Plaintiff substantially met the requirements as set forth in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, the Notice of Motion, Motion, and accompanying declaration were served by mail to the wrong address – specifically, the documents were served to “915 E 48th St” instead of “915 E 84th St”. II.                 Procedural Issues

Thus, the Court finds Defendant was still not properly served with the Notice of Motion, Motion, and accompanying declaration.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

            Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a), which states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

“Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . .. [citation].  A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

 

            A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires that a motion to amend must:  “[i]nclude a copy of the proposed… amended pleading… [and] state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be [deleted and/or added], if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the [deleted and/or additional] allegations are located.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1321(a).)

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also include “[a] separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify: (1) The effect of the amendment; (2) Why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1321(b).)

(Nivinskus Decl., ¶¶ 5, 10.) (Nivinskus Decl., ¶¶ 6-9.) Plaintiff has substantially met the requirements set forth in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff Vivek Shah (“Plaintiff”), proceeding pro se, filed a Complaint against Defendant Confirmed, LLC (“Defendant”) alleging a violation of California’s Pay Transparency Law, California Labor Code Section 432.3. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

On October 25, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint (“Demurrer”). No Opposition was filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

“As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).

III.              Discussion

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).) 

The Demurrer is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a). (Williams Decl.) According to the declaration, Defendant’s counsel called Plaintiff, who is proceeding in this matter in pro se, and the parties discussed Defendant’s intention to file a demurrer on the basis of mootness. (Williams Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff indicated he would oppose the demurrer. (Williams Decl., ¶ 4.) On October 23, 2024, Defendant’s counsel emailed Plaintiff setting forth the legal authority of the demurrer; later that day, Plaintiff responded indicating he would oppose the demurrer. (Williams Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.) The parties were unable to reach a resolution. (Williams Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

Thus, the Court finds the meet and confer requirement satisfied.

 

B.     Demurrer

 

Plaintiff’s Allegations in the Complaint

 

In the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges the following: At all relevant times, Plaintiff was unemployed and seeking employment. (Compl., ¶ 7.) In or around September 2024, Plaintiff was browsing through job postings on the Internet to apply for a job when Plaintiff came across several of Defendant’s job postings. (Compl., ¶ 8.) Defendant’s job postings appealed to Plaintiff; however, Plaintiff realized that “those job postings that he was reading through did not have a salary or hourly wage range provided.” (Compl., ¶ 8, Exh. A.) Since the pay scale was not included, Plaintiff was deterred from applying for the job. (Compl., ¶ 9.) Plaintiff states that he intended to apply for the job and accept employment by Defendant, if offered, “provided that the pay met his expectations and was consistent with industry standards for the position.” (Compl., ¶ 9.)

 

Plaintiff contends he continues “to suffer from harm because of Defendant’s existing non-compliance of the law. [Plaintiff] still intends to apply for a job with Defendant and still desires and continues to seek employment.” (Compl., ¶ 9.)

 

Plaintiff requests relief of: (1) An order declaring Defendant’s conduct violates the law; (2) an order of judgment in favor of Plaintiff; (3) an order enjoining Defendant’s conduct; (4) a private and public injunction; (5) nominal damages; and (6) reasonable attorney fees and costs. (Compl., Prayer.)

 

Moving Party Position

 

Defendant demurs to the Complaint on the basis the Complaint is moot because “Defendant has corrected the allegedly improper postings, as permitted under the Labor Code, and therefore the Court cannot provide the parties with effectual relief.” (Demurrer, p. 3.) Indeed, Defendant states that Defendant “removed its non-compliant posting even before being alerted of the problem with the posting, and all other job postings from [Defendant] are compliant with Labor Code Section 432.3.” (Demurrer, p. 7; Poirier Decl., ¶¶ 4-6, Exhs. A, B, C.) Accordingly, Defendant contends Plaintiff’s Complaint “does not allege any ongoing, intentional misconduct by [Defendant], and because he does not allege any economic or other injury that would warrant relief by the Court, Plaintiff’s Complaint is rendered moot.” (Demurrer, p. 7.)

 

                        Analysis

 

California Labor Code section 432.3 states, in pertinent part:

 

“(c) (1) An employer, upon reasonable request, shall provide the pay scale for a position to an applicant applying for employment.

(2) An employer, upon request, shall provide an employee the pay scale for the position in which the employee is currently employed.

(3) An employer with 15 or more employees shall include the pay scale for a position in any job posting.

…

(d) (1) A person who claims to be aggrieved by a violation of this section may file a written complaint with the Labor Commissioner within one year after the date the person learned of the violation. The complaint shall state the name and address of the employer and shall provide a detailed account of the alleged violation, as may be required by the Labor Commissioner.

(2) A person who claims to be aggrieved by a violation of this section may also bring a civil action for injunctive relief and any other relief that the court deems appropriate.

(3) The Labor Commissioner shall promptly investigate complaints alleging violation of this section.

(4) Upon finding that an employer has violated this section, the Labor Commissioner may order the employer to pay a civil penalty of no less than one hundred dollars ($100) and no more than ten thousand dollars ($10,000) per violation. The Labor Commissioner shall determine the amount of the penalty based on the totality of the circumstances, including, but not limited to, whether the employer has previously violated this section. For a first violation of subdivision (c), no penalty shall be assessed upon demonstration by the employer that all job postings for open positions have been updated to include the pay scale as required by this section.

(5) If an employer fails to keep records in violation of this section, there shall be a rebuttable presumption in favor of the employee’s claim.”

 

            (Cal. Labor Code, § 432.3, emphasis added.)

 

            Here, the Complaint alleges that Defendant is an employer with 15 or more employees who failed to include the pay scale for a position in one of its job postings – namely, an Account Manager position at Confirmed360. (Compl., ¶¶ 8, 10, Exh. A.) Thus, the Complaint pleads a violation of California Labor Code section 432.3.

 

            Defendant alleges and presents evidence that this was Defendant’s first violation of Labor Code section 432.3, Defendant remedied the infraction, and Defendant’s other job postings are code-compliant, which thus negates Plaintiff’s claims for relief and renders the Complaint moot. (Poirier Decl., ¶¶ 4-6, Exhs. A, B, C.) The Court disagrees. Plaintiff pled a violation of Labor Code section 432.3. Plaintiff also pled ongoing harm in that “he still intends to apply for a job with defendant and still desires and continue[s] to seek employment” as well as various requests for relief, including injunctive relief. (Compl., p. 3; Prayer.) The penalty outlined in California Labor Code section 432, subdivision (c) is separate and apart from other forms of relief requested by Plaintiff, and Defendant fails to show Plaintiff is completely without some right to relief, especially as the allegedly wrongful conduct may be repeated. (Center for Local Gov’t Accountability v. City of Los Angeles (2016) 247 Cal. App. 4th 1146, 1157 [“The voluntary cessation of allegedly wrongful conduct destroys the justiciability of a controversy and renders an action moot unless there is a reasonable expectation the allegedly wrongful conduct will be repeated”], emphasis added.) Moreover, Defendant’s arguments regarding the facts or evidence are misplaced at this stage in the proceedings. (E-Fab, Inc., supra, 153 Cal.App.4th at p. 1315.)

 

            Finally, Defendant raises various challenges to Plaintiff’s requests for relief and argues they are not supported by the allegations of the Complaint. However, a demurrer is not the proper vehicle to strike specific damages or relief sought. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (d).)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant Confirmed, LLC’s Demurrer to Complaint is OVERRULED.

 

Defendant’s Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint to be served and electronically filed within ten (10) days of this Court’s Ruling.
