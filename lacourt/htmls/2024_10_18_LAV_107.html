DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21VECV00814    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Ricardo Menjivar,

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

True Bullion LLC, et al.

Defendant.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

21VECV00814      107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Motion to Compel Further Responses  

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

(1) Grant in part and deny in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses; (2) Grant monetary sanctions against Defendants in the reduced amount of $2,180.00.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Ricardo Menjivar (“Plaintiff”) brings this action against his former employers, Defendants True Bullion LLC, Subculture Services LLC, Anthony Allen Anderson, and Agnes Viacrucis (“Defendants”). On June 23, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants and Does 1 to 100, alleging fourteen causes of action.

On August 6, 2021, Plaintiff served discovery requests on Defendants. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 2.) Defendants served responses with only objections on October 8, 2021. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 3.)

On December 14, 2021, the court issued a stay on discovery to rule on Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 8.) The court denied Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration and Defendants appealed. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 9.) The Appellate Court issued a remittitur returning the case to the trial court. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 9.) Defendants did not provide further responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 11.)

On July 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to compel further discovery responses (the “Motion”). Defendants filed an opposition on September 24, 2024. Plaintiff filed a reply on October 1, 2024.

On October 3, 2024, this court held an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”). The court continued the hearings on the motions to compel further discovery responses to October 18, 2024.

II.              PROCEDURE

 

a.     Timeliness

All motions to compel further responses to written discovery must be brought within 45 days of receiving the challenged responses, or as otherwise agreed upon in writing.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(b)(1), 2033.290(c).)¿ This deadline is jurisdictional, and courts are without authority to grant untimely motions.¿ (Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.) When verifications are provided electronically, the deadline is extended by two court days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

Here, the parties agreed to extend Plaintiff’s deadline to compel further responses. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 10; Ex. 4.) With the extension, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses was due on July 26, 2024. (Ex. 4.)

This court finds that Plaintiff timely filed the Motion.

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a “meet and confer” declaration evidencing a “reasonable and good faith attempt an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”¿ (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300(b), 2031.310(b)(2), 2033.290(b).)

The parties dispute whether Plaintiff made a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the issues before filing the Motion. Plaintiff’s counsel states he sent Defendants a meet and confer letter on October 22, 2021. (Ex. 1, Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel also states he met and conferred with Defendants on September 24 and 25, 2024. (Vardapour Reply Declaration ¶ 2.) Defendants’ counsel states “the parties did not meet and confer as to the specific issues Plaintiff is raising in the instant Motion.” (Kanno Opposition Declaration ¶ 2.)

Plaintiff is seeking Defendants’ further responses to his Form Interrogatories, General, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production, and Requests for Admission. (Motion.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s October 22, 2021, letter to Defendants raised the issues Plaintiff now raises in this Motion. III.           LEGAL STANDARD

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

Under, Evidence Code section 210, “’Relevant evidence’ means evidence . . . having any tendency to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.” “The test of relevance is whether the evidence tends logically, naturally, and by reasonable inference to establish material facts. The trial court has broad discretion in determining the relevance of evidence, but lacks discretion to admit irrelevant evidence.” (Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 148, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

A court “retains the discretion to weigh the burden of compliance against the likelihood of producing helpful information, to avoid duplicative production, and to narrow demands if appropriate to balance the reasonable concerns of both parties.” (Volkswagen of America, Inc. v. Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1497.)

 

Where responses to requests for admissions have been timely served but are deemed deficient by the requesting party, the requesting party may move for an order compelling the responding party’s further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)

 

Where a demanding party determines that a responding party’s responses to interrogatories are evasive or incomplete, or that said responses state objections that are too general or without merit, the demanding party may file a motion to compel the responding party’s further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(a).)

 

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that … [a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] … [a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] … [a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).)

 

Unless it would be unjust to do so under the circumstances, the Court imposes monetary sanctions against parties and/or attorneys that unsuccessfully make or oppose motions to compel further responses without substantial justification.¿ (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(d), 2031.310(h), 2033.290(d).)

II.              PROCEDURE

 

a.     Timeliness

All motions to compel further responses to written discovery must be brought within 45 days of receiving the challenged responses, or as otherwise agreed upon in writing.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. b.     Meet and Confer

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a “meet and confer” declaration evidencing a “reasonable and good faith attempt an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”¿ (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300(b), 2031.310(b)(2), 2033.290(b).)

The parties dispute whether Plaintiff made a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the issues before filing the Motion. Plaintiff’s counsel states he sent Defendants a meet and confer letter on October 22, 2021. (Ex. 1, Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel also states he met and conferred with Defendants on September 24 and 25, 2024. (Vardapour Reply Declaration ¶ 2.) Defendants’ counsel states “the parties did not meet and confer as to the specific issues Plaintiff is raising in the instant Motion.” (Kanno Opposition Declaration ¶ 2.)

Plaintiff is seeking Defendants’ further responses to his Form Interrogatories, General, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production, and Requests for Admission. (Motion.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s October 22, 2021, letter to Defendants raised the issues Plaintiff now raises in this Motion. Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

II.              PROCEDURE

 

a.     Timeliness

All motions to compel further responses to written discovery must be brought within 45 days of receiving the challenged responses, or as otherwise agreed upon in writing.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(b)(1), 2033.290(c).)¿ This deadline is jurisdictional, and courts are without authority to grant untimely motions.¿ (Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.) When verifications are provided electronically, the deadline is extended by two court days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

Here, the parties agreed to extend Plaintiff’s deadline to compel further responses. (Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 10; Ex. 4.) With the extension, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses was due on July 26, 2024. (Ex. 4.)

This court finds that Plaintiff timely filed its motion.

b.     Meet and Confer

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a “meet and confer” declaration evidencing a “reasonable and good faith attempt an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”¿ (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300(b), 2031.310(b)(2), 2033.290(b).)

The parties dispute whether Plaintiff made a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the issues before filing the Motion. Plaintiff’s counsel states he sent Defendants a meet and confer letter on October 22, 2021. (Ex. 1, Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel also states he met and conferred with Defendants on September 24 and 25, 2024. (Vardapour Reply Declaration ¶ 2.) Defendants’ counsel states “the parties did not meet and confer as to the specific issues Plaintiff is raising in the instant Motion.” (Kanno Opposition Declaration ¶ 2.)

Plaintiff is seeking Defendants’ further responses to his Form Interrogatories, General, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Production, and Requests for Admission. (Motion.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s October 22, 2021, letter to Defendants raised the issues Plaintiff now raises in this Motion. (Ex. 1.) Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

Under, Evidence Code section 210, “’Relevant evidence’ means evidence . . . having any tendency to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.” “The test of relevance is whether the evidence tends logically, naturally, and by reasonable inference to establish material facts. The trial court has broad discretion in determining the relevance of evidence, but lacks discretion to admit irrelevant evidence.” (Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 148, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

A court “retains the discretion to weigh the burden of compliance against the likelihood of producing helpful information, to avoid duplicative production, and to narrow demands if appropriate to balance the reasonable concerns of both parties.” (Volkswagen of America, Inc. v. Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1497.)

 

Where responses to requests for admissions have been timely served but are deemed deficient by the requesting party, the requesting party may move for an order compelling the responding party’s further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)

 

Where a demanding party determines that a responding party’s responses to interrogatories are evasive or incomplete, or that said responses state objections that are too general or without merit, the demanding party may file a motion to compel the responding party’s further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(a).)

 

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that … [a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] … [a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] … [a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).)

 

Unless it would be unjust to do so under the circumstances, the Court imposes monetary sanctions against parties and/or attorneys that unsuccessfully make or oppose motions to compel further responses without substantial justification.¿ (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(d), 2031.310(h), 2033.290(d).)

II.              PROCEDURE

 

a.     Timeliness

All motions to compel further responses to written discovery must be brought within 45 days of receiving the challenged responses, or as otherwise agreed upon in writing.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(b)(1), 2033.290(c).)¿ This deadline is jurisdictional, and courts are without authority to grant untimely motions.¿ (Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.) When verifications are provided electronically, the deadline is extended by two court days. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6(a)(3)(B).)

b.     Meet and Confer

Motions to compel further responses must always be accompanied by a “meet and confer” declaration evidencing a “reasonable and good faith attempt an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”¿ (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300(b), 2031.310(b)(2), 2033.290(b).)

Plaintiff’s counsel also states he met and conferred with Defendants on September 24 and 25, 2024. (Vardapour Reply Declaration ¶ 2.) Plaintiff’s counsel’s October 22, 2021, letter to Defendants raised the issues Plaintiff now raises in its motion. (Ex. 1, Vardapour Motion Declaration ¶ 4.) Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) 

Under, Evidence Code section 210, “’Relevant evidence’ means evidence . . . having any tendency to prove or disprove any disputed fact that is of consequence to the determination of the action.” “The test of relevance is whether the evidence tends logically, naturally, and by reasonable inference to establish material facts. The trial court has broad discretion in determining the relevance of evidence, but lacks discretion to admit irrelevant evidence.” (Donlen v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 138, 148, internal quotations and citations omitted.)

A court “retains the discretion to weigh the burden of compliance against the likelihood of producing helpful information, to avoid duplicative production, and to narrow demands if appropriate to balance the reasonable concerns of both parties.” (Volkswagen of America, Inc. v. Superior Court (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1497.)

 

Where responses to requests for admissions have been timely served but are deemed deficient by the requesting party, the requesting party may move for an order compelling the responding party’s further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(a).)

 

Where a demanding party determines that a responding party’s responses to interrogatories are evasive or incomplete, or that said responses state objections that are too general or without merit, the demanding party may file a motion to compel the responding party’s further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300(a).)

 

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that … [a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] … [a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] … [a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).)

 

Unless it would be unjust to do so under the circumstances, the Court imposes monetary sanctions against parties and/or attorneys that unsuccessfully make or oppose motions to compel further responses without substantial justification.¿ (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(d), 2031.310(h), 2033.290(d).)

The court ordered Defendants to provide further verified responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Request for Production, Form Interrogatories, Employment, Form Interrogatories, General, and Special Interrogatories within thirty days of the court’s ruling. (05/24/24 Minute Order p. 25; Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24, ¶¶ 29-30.)

 

Defendants did not serve further responses within thirty days of the court’s May 24, 2024, ruling. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24, ¶ 35.) On July 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for issue, evidence, and monetary sanctions against Defendants (the “Motion”). Defendants filed an opposition on September 24, 2024. Plaintiff filed a reply on September 30, 2024.

  1. Plaintiff Cynthia Abrams’s Notice Of Motion And Motion For Preliminary Injunction, filed on 10/15/2021.

 

The court need not take judicial notice of documents that are within the case file. Thus, Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice are denied.

 

III.            LEGAL STANDARD

 

“The court may impose an issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(b).) 

“The court may impose an evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030(c).)

“The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. The court may also impose this sanction on one unsuccessfully asserting that another has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process, or on any attorney who advised that assertion, or on both. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030(a).)

Discovery sanctions “should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.”¿¿(Deyo v. Kilbourne¿(1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793.)  “[D]iscovery sanctions exist ‘not to provide a weapon for punishment for past violations or penalty for past conduct but to secure compliance with orders of the court.’” (Electronic Funds Solutions, LLC v. Murphy¿(2005) 134 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1183.) Before issuing a sanction, a trial court “should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery and, in choosing a sanction, should attempt to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.”  (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)

IV.            THE MOTION, OPPOSITION, AND REPLY

a.     The Motion

 

Plaintiff seeks an order deeming certain issues admitted and preventing Defendants from providing evidence to the contrary. (Notice of Motion p. 2.) Plaintiff also seeks $13,335 in monetary sanctions against Defendants. (Notice of Motion pp. 2-3.)

 

Plaintiff argues this court should grant the Motion because Defendants did not comply with the court’s May 24, 2024, order to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests. (See Motion p. 2.) Plaintiff served her first set of discovery responses on December 6, 2021. (Motion pp. 6-7.) Plaintiff’s counsel submits a declaration stating Plaintiff received Defendants’ initial discovery responses on March 11, 2022. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 5.) On August 10, 2022, the court instructed Defendants to provide further responses and to produce documents in their native format. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 7.) Defendants did not provide further responses. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 7.) In June 2023, Plaintiff filed motions to compel further responses. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 11.) During a December 2023, hearing, the court instructed Defendants again to produce documents in their native formats. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 13.) Defendants produced additional discovery responses on May 13, 2024. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶¶ 24, 27.) On May 24, 2024, the court ordered Defendants to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests within thirty days of the court’s ruling. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶¶ 30-33.) Defendants did not serve further responses and Plaintiff filed this Motion. (See Motion; Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 35.)

 

b.     The Opposition

 

Defendants argue the court should deny the Motion because “Defendants have actively tried to work with Plaintiff regarding coordinating discovery in this case as well as the four related cases.” (Opposition p. 3.) Defendants counsel states that “Plaintiff’s counsel never requested an Informal Discover Conference to resolve these issues.” (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 3.) Defendants’ counsel also states they “agreed to provide responses and proposed a discovery plan with responses being produced in each of the five cases on a two-week interval.” (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 4.) Defendants’ counsel further states that “Plaintiff’s counsel has refused to work in good faith with Defendants”, “Plaintiff’s motions are mere gamesmanship to obtain a procedural advantage and unwarranted sanctions” and “Plaintiffs have used five separate matters as a vehicle to propound duplicative, unduly burdensome and harassing amounts of discovery.” (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶¶ 4, 7.) Defendants’ counsel states that Defendants paid the sanctions ordered by the court in its May 24, 2024, ruling on August 6, 2024, and the delay in payment was because of an issue with Plaintiff’s W9. (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 13.) Finally, Defendants’ counsel states Defendants provided additional supplemental responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests on August 16, 2024. (Opposition p. 3; Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 14; Ex. 10.)

 

c.      The Reply

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants did not comply with the court’s May 24, 2024, order because their responses were untimely and substantively deficient. (Reply pp. 2, 5.) Plaintiff also states she made “telephonic and written confer efforts both before and after [the] deadline.” (Reply p. 3.)

 

V.              DISCUSSION

 

a.     Issue and Evidence Sanctions

On May 24, 2024, the court ordered Defendants to provide further, verified, responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions (“RFAs”), Request for Production (“RFP”), Form Interrogatories, Employment, Form Interrogatories, General, and Special Interrogatories, within thirty days of the court’s ruling. (05/24/24 Minute Order p. 25.) The court also ordered Defendants to pay $9,584.18 in monetary sanctions. (05/24/24 Minute Order p. 25.)

Defendants state that they paid the sanctions ordered by the court on August 6, 2024. (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 13.) Defendants further state the delay was because Plaintiff did not provide her W9. (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 13.) Plaintiff’s reply does not dispute this. (Reply.) This court, therefore, finds that Defendants’ delay in paying sanctions was not an abuse of the discovery process.

Defendants also state that they served Plaintiff with further supplemental responses on August 16, 2024. (Kanno Declaration filed 09/24/24 ¶ 14; Ex. 10.) Plaintiff responds that Defendants’ further supplemental responses were untimely. and the responses do not substantively comply with the court’s May 24, 2024, order. (Reply pp. 2, 5.)

This court finds that Defendants’ further supplemental responses, though untimely, substantially comply with the court’s May 24, 2024, order.

First, Defendants were ordered to provide further, verified responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs #7-9. (05/24/24 Minute Order p. 5-7.) Defendants’ further supplemental responses contain verifications by each Defendant. (Ex. 16, pdf p. 121 (Subculture Services, LLC); p. 122 (True Bullion, LLC); p. 123 (Anthony Anderson); p. 124 (Agnes Viacrucis.)

Second, Defendants were ordered to verify their responses to Plaintiff’s RFP and to provide further supplemental responses to RFPs #44, #48, and #69. (05/24/24 Minute Order pp. 7-11.) Defendants’ further supplemental responses contain verifications by each Defendant. (Ex. 16, pdf p. 121 (Subculture Services, LLC); p. 122 (True Bullion, LLC); p. 123 (Anthony Anderson); p. 124 (Agnes Viacrucis.) Defendants also provided further supplemental responses to RFPs #44, #46, and #69. (Ex. 16 pdf pp. 4-6.)

Third, Defendants were ordered to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Employment, #216.1 and #217.1. (05/24/24 Minute Order pp. 11-16.) The court also noted that Defendant Subculture Services LLC’s responses were missing from Form Interrogatories #214.1 and #214.2 and ordered Subculture Services LLC to provide further responses. Defendants’ further responses to #214.1 and #214.2 include Subculture Services LLC’s responses. (Ex. 16 pdf pp. 14-15.) Further, Defendants have provided further supplemental responses to Form Interrogatories, Employment, #216.1 and #217.1. (Ex. 16 pdf pp. 15-53.)

Fourth, Defendants were ordered to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, General and to verify their responses. (05/24/24 Minute Order pp. 16-20.) Defendants’ further supplemental responses contain verifications by each Defendant. (Ex. 16, pdf. p. 121 (Subculture Services, LLC); p. 122 (True Bullion, LLC); p. 123 (Anthony Anderson); p. 124 (Agnes Viacrucis.) Defendants have also provided further supplemental responses to the remaining Form Interrogatories, General. (Ex. 16 pdf pp. 62-103.)

Finally, Defendants were ordered to provide further supplemental responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories and to verify their responses. (Minute Order 05/24/24 Minute Order pp. 20-22.) Defendants’ further supplemental responses contain verifications by each Defendant. (Ex. 16, pdf. p. 121 (Subculture Services, LLC); p. 122 (True Bullion, LLC); p. 123 (Anthony Anderson); p. 124 (Agnes Viacrucis.) Defendants have also provided further supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories #16, #18, #21, and #22. (Ex. 16 pdf pp. 109-120.)

Discovery sanctions exist to “secure compliance with orders of the court.” (Electronic Funds Solutions, LLC v. Murphy, supra, 134 Cal.App.4th at p. 1183.) Discovery sanctions should not exceed what is required to “protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.”¿¿(Deyo v. Kilbourne, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 793.)  Because Defendants’ further supplemental responses substantially comply with the court’s May 24, 2024, ruling, this court finds that issue and evidentiary sanctions are not warranted.

b.     Monetary Sanctions

Plaintiff seeks $13,335 in monetary sanctions. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 43.) This includes the $60.00 motion filing fee, 30.4 hours drafting, reviewing, and revising the Motion, declaration, request for judicial notice, and separate statements, and five hours for reviewing the opposition, drafting a reply, and appearing at the hearing. (Vardapour Declaration filed 07/26/24 ¶ 43.)

Defendants argue sanctions are not warranted because Plaintiff failed to meet and confer about the discovery issues. But this court finds no authority for the proposition that parties are required to meet and confer after the court has ordered a party to provide further discovery responses.

In its May 24, 2024, order, the court noted that “Defendants’ failures to serve verified responses fits their ongoing pattern of providing partial responses without satisfying their obligations under the Discovery Act.” (05/24/24 Minute Order pp. 7, 10, 16, 20, 22.) This court also notes that Defendants’ failure to comply with the court’s order fits with their ongoing pattern of failing to satisfy their obligations under the Discovery Act.

Defendants’ further responses were due thirty days after the court’s May 24, 2024, ruling. Defendants did not serve their further supplemental responses until August 16, 2024, which is nearly two months after they were due. Further, Defendants did not provide their further supplemental responses until after Plaintiff filed this motion. Disobeying a court order is considered a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.) Accordingly, this court finds that Defendants have engaged in a misuse of the discovery process. This court further finds that 35.4 hours of attorneys’ fees is excessive for this motion.

Thus, this court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the reduced amount of $4,560.00, reflecting the $60.00 motion filing fee, eight hours for preparing the Motion, and four hours for reviewing the opposition, drafting the reply, and appearing at the hearing, at $375 per hours.

VI.            CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, this court denies Plaintiff’s motion for issue and evidentiary sanctions but grants sanctions against Defendants in the reduced amount of $4,560.00. This amount is applied evenly among the four Defendants, so each Defendant is sanctioned in the total amount of $1,140.00.

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

On October 1, 2021, CMM, LLP (“Petitioner”) and Catherine Dunne (“Respondent”) entered into a Litigation Services Agreement for accounting and consulting services (the “Agreement”). (Ex. 4(b) p. 5.) The Agreement contained an arbitration provision. (Ex. 8(c) p. 2; Ex. 4(b) p. 3.) A dispute arose regarding non-payment of fees and costs and Petitioner filed a Demand for Arbitration. (Ex. 8(c) p. 2.)

 

The Arbitrator held a hearing on February 2, 2024. (Motion p. 2.) On February 6, 2024, the Arbitrator issued a final award in favor of Petitioner (the “Award”). (Ex. 8(c) p. 5.)

 

On July 8, 2024, Petitioner filed a petition to confirm arbitration award (the “Petition”). Respondent filed a general denial on August 27, 2024.

 

II.              MOVING PARTY’S POSITION 

 

Petitioner requests this court confirm the Award and enter judgment according to it. (Petition p. 3.) Petitioner attaches to the Petition a copy of the Agreement, which states: “In the event of any unresolved dispute or controversy under this Agreement relating to an amount of fees and costs, the aggregate of which is twenty-five thousand dollars ($25,000) or greater, such dispute shall be submitted to binding arbitration before a retired Judicial Officer of the Superior Courts of the State of California, with family law experience, and affiliated with either ADR Services of Los Angeles and/or Judicial Arbitration and Mediation Services of Los Angeles (“JAMS”).” (Ex. 4(b) p. 3.) Petitioner alleged respondent had an outstanding balance of $77,499.20 as of December 31, 2023. (Ex. 8(c) p. 4.) The Arbitrator issued an Award in Petitioner’s favor on February 6, 2024. (Ex. 8(c).) Petitioner attaches to the Petition a copy of the Award, which requires Respondent to pay Petitioner $66,749.20, including fees and accrued interests, and the Arbitration costs of $6,004.44. (Ex. 8(c) p. 5.)  In total, Respondent was ordered to pay Petitioner $72,753.64. (Ex. 8(c) p. 5; Petition p. 2.)

 

In addition to the arbitration award, Petitioner requests this court grant it $5,082.79 in interest, attorneys’ fees in the minimum amount of $2,457.64 (uncontested) or $4,405.28 (contested), and $545.48 in costs. (Motion p. 5; Petition p. 3)

 

III.            RESPONDING PARTY’S POSITION

 

Respondent filed a general denial alleging three affirmative defenses. First, Respondent argues that the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Second, Respondent argues she has been denied her constitutional rights of due process. Third, Respondent argues Petitioner failed to comply with Code of Civil Procedure sections 1280, et seq.

 

IV.            DISCUSSION

 

Service of the Petition and Notice of Hearing

 

“Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.) It must be served between ten days and four years from the date the award was served on the petitioner. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1288 & 1288.4.) The other party may respond to the petition. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.2.)

 

Pursuant to the Code of Civil Procedure, section 1290.4, “A copy of the petition and a written notice of the time and place of the hearing thereof and any other papers upon which the petition is based shall be served in the manner provided in the arbitration agreement for the service of such petition and notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., §1290.4(a).)  

 

But “if the arbitration agreement does not provide the manner in which such service shall be made and the person upon whom service is to be made has not previously appeared in the proceeding and has not previously been served in accordance with this subdivision . . . Service within this State shall be made in the manner provided by law for the service of summons in an action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1290.4(b)(1).) 

 

Here, The Agreement provides that “all notices and/or communications pertaining to this Agreement shall be made and given solely in writing and by recognized means of communication, including but not limited to facsimile, U.S. Mail, electronic mail, courier service or personal delivery.” (Ex. 4(b) p. 4.) The Final Award was served on Respondent on February 6, 2024. (Ex. 8(c) p. 6.)

 

The Proof of Service shows that the Notice of Hearing, Motion to Confirm Arbitration Award, and supporting documents were served to Respondent by mail on August 16, 2024. (Proof of Service filed 08/16/24.) Service by U.S. Mail is permissible under the Agreement. Further, August 16, 2024, is between ten days and four years from February 6, 2024. (Ex. 8(c) p. 6.) Thus, service of the Notice of Motion, Motion to Confirm Arbitration Award, and supporting documents was proper.

 

Further, Petitioner filed a Proof of Service showing the Petition and its supporting documents was served to Respondent by personal service on July 28, 2024. (Proof of Service filed 09/04/2024.) Service by personal delivery or courier service is permissible under the Agreement.  Further, July 28, 2024, is between ten days and four years from February 6, 2024. (Ex. 8(c) p. 6.) Thus, service of the Petition and supporting documents was proper.

 

Moreover, service by mail and personal service are permissible methods of service. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 415.30, 415.10.)

 

Accordingly, service was proper.

 

Filing Requirements

 

A petition to confirm arbitration award must:

 

(a)   Set forth the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate unless the petitioner denies the existence of such an agreement.

 

(b)   Set forth the names of the arbitrators.

 

(c) Set forth or have attached a copy of the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, if any.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.4) 

 

Here, a copy of the Agreement is attached to the Petition as Exhibit 4(b). The Agreement is signed by both parties. (Ex. 4(b) p. 5.) The Agreement provides that: “In the event of any unresolved dispute or controversy under this Agreement relating to an amount of fees and costs, the aggregate of which is twenty-five thousand dollars ($25,000) or greater, such dispute shall be submitted to binding arbitration before a retired Judicial Officer of the Superior Courts of the State of California, with family law experience, and affiliated with either ADR Services of Los Angeles and/or Judicial Arbitration and Mediation Services of Los Angeles (“JAMS”).” (Ex. 4(b) p. 3.) Moreover, the Petition states that the Hon. Riva G. Goetz (Ret.) was the arbitrator for this matter. (Petition p. 2.) Finally, a copy of the arbitration award and written opinion of the Hon. Riva G. Goetz (Ret.) is attached as Exhibit 8(c). Accordingly, the petition is in compliance with section 1285.4’s filing requirements.

 

Service of the Arbitration Award

 

“The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 1283.6.)

 

Here, the Award was served to Respondent by mail on February 6, 2024. Accordingly, this requirement is satisfied.

 

Substantive Issues

 

¿The Court shall vacate the award if any of the following conditions are proven: 

 

(1) The award was procured by corruption, fraud or other undue means.

(2) There was corruption in any of the arbitrators.

(3) The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced by misconduct of a neutral arbitrator.

(4) The arbitrators exceeded their powers and the award cannot be corrected without affecting the merits of the decision upon the controversy submitted.

(5) The rights of the party were substantially prejudiced by the refusal of the arbitrators to postpone the hearing upon sufficient cause being shown therefor or by the refusal of the arbitrators to hear evidence material to the controversy or by other conduct of the arbitrators contrary to the provisions of this title.

(6) An arbitrator making the award either: (A) failed to disclose within the time required for disclosure a ground for disqualification of which the arbitrator was then aware; or (B) was subject to disqualification upon grounds specified in Section 1281.91 but failed upon receipt of timely demand to disqualify himself or herself as required by that provision. However, this subdivision does not apply to arbitration proceedings conducted under a collective bargaining agreement between employers and employees or between their respective representatives.

(Code Civ. Proc. § 1286.2(a).) 

 

Only where both (1) the arbitrator abused his or her discretion and (2) there was resulting prejudice, can a trial court properly vacate an arbitration award.  (SWAB Financial, LLC v. E*Trade Securities, LLC (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1198.) 

¿

Respondent argues the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, she has been denied her constitutional rights of due process, and Petitioner failed to comply with Code of Civil Procedure sections 1280, et seq. (General Denial.)

 

As noted, Petitioner has complied with the statutory requirements. Further, Respondent’s General Denial does not present this court with any argument as to why the Award should not be confirmed. Accordingly, the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is granted.

 

Costs

 

The court shall award costs upon any Petition to confirm a fee arbitration award.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1293.2.) 

 

Although the cost request is in Petitioner’s Motion, the Petitioner did not check the box in the Petition stating it is seeking costs of the suit. (Petition p. 3.) Nevertheless, the court considers the Petitioner’s request for $545.48 in costs. (Motion p. 5.) This includes the filing fee and service fees. (Ex. A; Tudzin Declaration ¶ 9.)

 

This court finds that the costs sought by Petitioner in connection with the Petition are reasonable. Accordingly, Petitioner’s cost request is granted in the sum of $545.48.

 

Attorneys’ Fees

 

“Neither party to the arbitration may recover costs or attorney’s fees incurred in preparation for or in the course of the fee arbitration proceeding with the exception of the filing fee paid pursuant to subdivision (a) of this section.  However, a court confirming, correcting, or vacating an award under this section may award to the prevailing party reasonable fees and costs incurred in obtaining confirmation, correction, or vacation of the award including, if applicable, fees and costs on appeal.  The party obtaining judgment confirming, correcting, or vacating the award shall be the prevailing party except that, without regard to consideration of who the prevailing party may be, if a party did not appear at the arbitration hearing in the manner provided by the rules adopted by the board of trustees, that party shall not be entitled to attorney’s fees or costs upon confirmation, correction, or vacation of the award.”  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 6203(c).) 

 

Here, Petitioner seeks attorneys’ fees in the sum of $2,457.64 (if uncontested) or $4,405.28 (if contested). (Ex. A.) Petitioner calculates its attorneys’ fees using Local Rule of the Court, rule 3.214.

 

Respondent has not argued the requested attorneys’ fee award should be reduced in any manner. (Premier Medical Management Systems, Inc. v. California Insurance Guarantee Association (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 560 (emphasizing that opposing parties “submitted no evidence that the hours claimed by counsel were excessive,” and declining to “declare as a matter of law that the hours were unreasonable”).

 

This court finds that Petitioner’s attorneys’ fees request is reasonable under the circumstances. Accordingly, Petitioner’s request for attorneys’ fees in the sum of $4,405.28 is granted.

 

Post-Judgment Interest

 

Interest accrues on a money judgment at a rate of 10 percent per annum from the date of entry of judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 685.010(a), 685.020(a).)   

 

Here, Petitioner seeks $5,082.79 in prejudgment interest. That amount properly reflects the interest rate allowed under sections 685.010(a) and 685.020(a).

 

V.              CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants Petitioner’s petition to confirm the arbitration award. Petitioner’s requests for $545.48 in costs, $4,405.28 in attorneys’ fees, and $5,082.79 in prejudgment interest are also granted.
