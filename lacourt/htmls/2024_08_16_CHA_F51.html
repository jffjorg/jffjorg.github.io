Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F51, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2251.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. Case Number: 22CHCV01334    Hearing Date: August 16, 2024    Dept: F51

AUGUST 15, 2024

 

DEMURRER

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22CHCV01334

 

Demurrer Filed: 5/3/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Kathrin Strain (“Plaintiff”)

NOTICE: OK 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s entire fourth amended complaint (“4AC”).

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The demurrer is overruled. Defendant to file an answer to Plaintiff’s 4AC within 30 days of this hearing.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On 5/10/23, Plaintiff filed her second amended complaint, alleging against Defendant the following causes of action: (1) General Negligence; (2) Negligent Supervision; and (3) Negligent Hiring/Retention. On 10/11/23, Plaintiff filed her third amended complaint, alleging the same causes of action. On 4/5/24, Plaintiff filed her 4AC, alleging the same causes of action.

 

On 5/3/24, Defendant filed the instant demurrer. On 8/5/24, Plaintiff filed her opposition. On 8/9/24, Defendant filed its reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant demurs to each of Plaintiff’s causes of action on the basis that Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to constitute any of those causes of action.

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Defendant’s counsel declares that on 4/14/24, he sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding the issues raised in the instant demurrer but received no response. (Decl. of Jeffrey P. Wade, Jr., ¶¶ 3–4.) Therefore, the Court finds that counsel has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a). 

 

B.     Knowledge or Notice Requirement

 

Plaintiff brings this action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1, which sets forth the procedural requirements that must be followed when a plaintiff seeks to pursue a claim for childhood sexual assault. As this Court previously noted in its ruling sustaining Defendant’s prior demurrers, an action brought under Section 340.1 must allege that “the person or entity knew or had reason to know, or was otherwise on notice, of any misconduct that creates a risk of childhood sexual assault by an employee, volunteer, representative, or agent, or the person or entity failed to take reasonable steps or to implement reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of childhood sexual assault.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.1, subd. (c) [emphasis added].)

 

The knowledge or notice requirement “requires the victim to establish that the nonperpetrator defendant had actual knowledge, constructive knowledge (as measured by the reason to know standard), or was otherwise on notice that the perpetrator had engaged in past unlawful sexual conduct with a minor and, possessed of this knowledge or notice, failed to take reasonable preventative steps or implement reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of future unlawful sexual conduct by the perpetrator.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 549.)

 

The Doe court held that the victim plaintiffs’ “complaints fail to allege that defendants had knowledge of [the perpetrator’s] past unlawful sexual conduct with minors, which is the prerequisite for imposing upon these defendants liability for his subsequent sexual abuse of plaintiffs. That defendants had knowledge or notice of misconduct by [the perpetrator] that created a risk of sexual exploitation is not enough under the express terms of the statute. In the absence of sufficient allegations of knowledge or notice on the part of these defendants, their demurrers were correctly sustained and the actions against them properly dismissed.” (42 Cal.4th at 552.)

 

Here, as in Doe, this Court previously found that Plaintiff’s “factual allegations, even if broadly construed, similarly fail to allege that Defendant had knowledge of the perpetrator’s past acts of unlawful sexual conduct with minors.” (3/7/24 Min. Order, p. 3.) In her 4AC, Plaintiff added the following allegation: “On information and belief, Defendants LAUSD and DOES 1 to 50 knew, had reason to know, or was otherwise on notice of past incidents of unlawful sexual conduct by the Perpetrator with minors that triggered the duty on Defendant’s part to take preventive measure to avoid acts of unlawful sexual conduct by the perpetrator in the future, but took no action to obstruct, inhibit or stop such continuing conduct, or to help Plaintiff avoid the trauma caused by the Perpetrator’s wrongful conduct.” (4AC ¶ 36.)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s amendment is again insufficient because “here, Plaintiff was required to plead specific facts regarding LAUSD’s knowledge or what LAUSD should have known of the perpetrator’s past incidents with minors that would trigger the duty on LAUSD’s part to take preventive measure to avoid acts of unlawful sexual conduct by the perpetrator in the future. … There are no allegations that any adults at the school heard these rumors. Nor are there any allegations as to when these alleged rumors were circulated and by whom.” (Dem. 7:11–17.)

 

Plaintiff argues in opposition that the added allegations are sufficient to withstand demurrer “because actual knowledge need not be pled against organizations or entities that provide services primarily or exclusively for children.” (Pl.’s Opp. 5:22–25, citing Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 132.) Plaintiff further argues that she has “sufficiently plead facts that if true are demonstrative of notice to the district.” (Id. at 7:23–24, citing Doe, 42 Cal.4th at 550.)

 

As Defendant argues in reply, Plaintiff’s reliance on Lawndale is misplaced here because the procedural posture of the case involved summary judgment, a different standard from that for pleadings at the demurrer stage. (Def.’s Reply 4:4–8, citing 72 Cal.App.5th at 122.) Defendant further asserts that “there are absolutely no allegations that even come close to showing that LAUSD has actual or even constructive notice of prior misconduct. Plaintiff Strain is required to plead specific facts regarding employee knowledge or what the employees should have known of the alleged perpetrator’s past incidents with minors that would trigger the duty on LAUSD’s part to take preventive measure to avoid acts of unlawful sexual conduct by the alleged perpetrator in the future.” (Id. at 3:15–20, citing Doe, 42 Cal.4th at 551.)

 

Here, the Court finds that the added factual allegations sufficiently allege Defendant’s notice of the perpetrator’s past instances of sexual misconduct with minors. As Plaintiff argues, “Defendant seeks to introduce a more stringent pleading requirement than exists.” (Pl.’s Opp. 7:9.) The Doe court found that “the complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts. … a plaintiff should be able to rely on the doctrine of less particularity where he or she can plausibly allege that the nonperpetrator defendant withheld or concealed evidence of its knowledge or notice of the perpetrator’s past unlawful sexual conduct with minors.” (42 Cal.4th at 550–551.) Here, Plaintiff alleges such concealment in paragraphs 29 and 33 of the 4AC.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has alleged facts sufficient to support an action brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1. Accordingly, the demurrer is overruled on this basis.

 

C.    Constitutionality of AB 218

 

Defendant again posits that “Plaintiff’s suit is barred by Plaintiff’s failure to timely present a government claim, and because AB 218, which retroactively eliminated the requirement in 2020, is an unconstitutional gift of public funds.” (Dem. 7:24–27.)

 

The Court previously found that “the constitutionality of AB 218 is not properly before this Court at the demurrer stage. A demurrer tests the factual sufficiency of a pleading on its face. Here, the Court is satisfied that Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 does not currently mandate a plaintiff to present a government claim as a prerequisite to bringing the instant action. Accordingly, the Court declines to address the parties’ arguments concerning the constitutionality of AB 218 as they are not relevant to the Court’s analysis of the factual sufficiency of Plaintiff’s” pleading. (3/7/24 Min. Order, p. 4.)

 

Plaintiff argues that “this issue was previously raised, and denied, by the Court. It is properly rejected on that ground alone.” (Pl.’s Opp. 8:19–20, citing Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41, subd. (b).) Defendant argues in reply that “at the last hearing, counsel for LAUSD specifically inquired of the Court if it was precluded from making the arguments raised in the present pleadings. This Court indicated that no party was precluded from making any argument.” (Def.’s Reply 5:5–7.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court again declines to address the parties’ arguments concerning the constitutionality of AB 218 at the demurrer stage.

 

CONCLUSION 

 

The demurrer is overruled. Defendant to file an answer to Plaintiff’s 4AC within 30 days of this hearing.


Case Number: 23CHCV02888    Hearing Date: August 16, 2024    Dept: F51

AUGUST 15, 2024

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES

(Request for Production of Documents, Set One)

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23CHCV02888

 

Motion filed: 6/6/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiffs Eileen Pineda; and Jose Pineda (collectively, “Plaintiffs”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc. (“Defendant”)

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order compelling Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), Set One, Nos. 29–30, 32–33, 37, and 44.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is granted in part. Defendant to provide further code-compliant responses to Plaintiffs’ first set of RFPs, Nos. 29–30, 32–33, 37, and 44, within 30 days. The Court limits the scope of RFP Nos. 29, 30, 32, and 44 to consumer complaints regarding vehicles purchased in the State of California. The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $790.00.

 

Defendant is reminded to review the 5/3/19 First Amended General Order Re Mandatory Electronic Filing for Civil. When e-filing documents, parties must comply with the “TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS” which are set forth at page 4, line 4 through page 5, line 12 of the Court’s 5/3/19 First Amended General Order Re Mandatory Electronic Filing for Civil (particularly bookmarking declarations and exhibits). (CRC 3.1110(f)(4).) Failure to comply with these requirements in the future may result in papers being rejected, matters being placed off calendar, matters being continued so documents can be resubmitted in compliance with these requirements, documents not being considered and/or the imposition of sanctions.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiffs bring this action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civil Code § 1790 et seq.) for a vehicle they purchased on or around 12/5/20, for which Defendant issued the manufacturer’s express warranty. (Compl. ¶¶ 8–9.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendant “was unable to repair the vehicle in accordance with the written warranty or the consumer protection and warranty laws of the State of California.” (Id. at ¶ 5.) 

 

Plaintiffs further allege that Defendant had actual knowledge of a defect in computerized driver-assisting safety systems in the same model of vehicle as that purchased by Plaintiffs and continued to knowingly market and sell/lease vehicles equipped with the defect while concealing the existence of those defects from consumers such as Plaintiffs. (Id. at ¶¶ 23–25.)

 

On 9/27/23, Plaintiffs filed their complaint, alleging against Defendant the following causes of action: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; and (2) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment. On 5/30/24, Defendant filed its answer.

 

On 12/12/23, Plaintiffs served their RFPs, Set One, on Defendant. (Decl. of Thach Tran, ¶ 6.) On 1/16/24, Defendant served its responses thereto. (Id. at ¶ 8.)

 

On 6/6/24, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to their RFPs, Set One. On 8/5/24, Defendant filed its opposition. On 8/9/24, Plaintiffs filed their reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs seek to compel Defendants’ additional responses to their first set of RFPs, Nos. 29–30, 32–33, 37, and 44. “RFP Nos. 33 and 37 involve AHM’s goals and statistics associated with vehicle repurchases and warranty repair costs and agendas or minutes regarding the sensing defect and warranty spending.” (Pls.’ Reply 1:5–7.) “RFP Nos. 29-30, 32, and 44 involve similar customer complaints and warranty repairs relating to the defects and nonconformities in vehicles that overlap with the same defects alleged in Plaintiffs’ vehicle.” (Id. at 1:10–12.)

 

A.    Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel declares that on 2/13/24, he sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding the issues raised in the instant motion. (Tran Decl. ¶ 9.) On 5/23/24, counsel for the parties met and conferred telephonically, but were unable to informally resolve the dispute. (Id. at ¶ 10.) The Court therefore finds that counsel has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2). 

 

B.     Defendant’s Objections

 

Here, Plaintiffs argue, in part, that Defendant’s objections to the subject RFPs are without merit. (Pls.’ Mot. 12:2–6; Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (a).) The demanding party must take the initiative to obtain a judicial determination of the validity of any objection by moving to compel a further response, but the objecting party has the burden of justifying the objection. (See, e.g., Coy v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220.) Here, Defendant objected to each request at issue on the bases of, inter alia, relevance and undue burden.

 

a.                   Scope/Relevance 

 

Discovery is relevant if it is admissible as evidence, or “appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.) Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s objections to the subject RFPs on the basis that they seek irrelevant matter are meritless because “the information sought by Plaintiffs regarding the Honda Sensing defect may prove a widespread defect or nonconformity or show when it arose; and may also demonstrate American Honda’s knowledge of widespread problems and American Honda’s failure to act despite its knowledge.” (Pls.’ Mot. 7:27–8:2.)

 

Specifically, Plaintiffs contend that “information regarding similar customer complaints is highly relevant to prove that Defendant had knowledge of widespread defects and non-conformities in other 2020 Honda Accords as well as the Subject Vehicle. … Thus, the documents and evidence sought by RFP Numbers 29-30, 32, 44 are relevant to prove that Defendant did not act in good faith and thus willfully failed to comply with its obligations under the Song-Beverly Act.” (Id. at 8:3–9.) With respect to RFP Nos. 33 and 37, Plaintiffs argue that “evidence regarding Defendant’s practices in handling widespread defects such as the Honda Sensing defect is relevant to determining whether Defendant willfully violated the Song-Beverly Act when it refused to repurchase Plaintiffs’ Vehicle.” (Id. at 8:20–22.)

 

Defendant argues in opposition that “this case is solely about Plaintiffs’ own vehicle, its repair history, and how AHM responded to Plaintiffs’ concerns regarding the Subject Vehicle. Further, AHM’s statutory obligations, and whether a plaintiff is entitled to relief under Song-Beverly, is entirely unrelated to the handling, repurchase, or replacement of any other vehicles, even those of the same year, make, and model.” (Def.’s Opp. 8:18–22.) Defendant further argues that the requested documents are irrelevant to determine whether Defendant willfully violated the Song-Beverly Act, because “determining whether a defendant acted willfully is based on the repair history of the Plaintiffs’ vehicle.” (Id. at 10:14–15, citing Kwan v. Mercedes-Benz of North America, Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 174, 185–186 [“a manufacturer who refused a refund or replacement on the ground a reasonable number of repair attempts had not been made, without making any effort to gather the available information on repair history, might well be deemed to have acted willfully.”].)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has provided sufficient facts to establish the requisite good cause for production of the subject documents. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) In order to obtain civil penalties under the Song-Beverly Act, a plaintiff must establish that the defendant’s failure to comply was willful, which may be based on the defendant’s knowledge of a certain defect. (Civ. Code § 1794, subd. (c).) Information regarding vehicles other than Plaintiffs’ is relevant to the subject matter of this action as it could assist Plaintiffs in proving Defendant’s willful violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Documents responsive to these requests may reasonably lead to the discovery of information as to the nature and duration of the defects, Defendant’s knowledge of the defects, and Defendant’s inability to repair the defects.

 

Furthermore, the Court finds that Defendant’s reliance on Kwan is misplaced, where that case involved an appeal of the trial court’s jury instructions defining a “willful” violation of the Act. (23 Cal.App.4th at 177.) There, the Court of Appeal clarified that “such a violation is not willful if the defendant’s failure to replace or refund was the result of a good faith and reasonable belief the facts imposing the statutory obligation were not present.” (Id. at 185.) The Court of Appeal then looked to the available evidence, which included the repair history of the subject vehicle, to conclude that the defendant “was entitled to an instruction informing the jury its failure to refund or replace was not willful if it reasonably and in good faith believed the facts did not call for refund or replacement.” (Id. at 186.) This Court finds no rule stated in the Kwan opinion which limits the evidence relevant to a manufacturer’s “willful” violations of the Song-Beverly Act solely to the repair history of the subject vehicle. The Court of Appeal unequivocally stated that “a [manufacturer’s] decision [not to refund a consumer or repurchase a vehicle under the Act] made without the use of reasonably available information germane to that decision is not a reasonable, good faith decision.” (Ibid.) Here, the Court finds that the requested documents are relevant to aid in a factfinder’s determination of whether Defendant’s decision not to refund Plaintiffs or repurchase the subject vehicle was made reasonably and in good faith.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Defendant’s relevance objections to the subject RFPs are without merit. However, the Court limits the scope of RFP Nos. 29, 30, 32, and 44 to consumer complaints regarding vehicles purchased in the State of California.

 

b.                   Undue Burden

 

Plaintiffs further argue that Defendant’s objections to the subject RFPs on the basis that they are unduly burdensome and oppressive are meritless without any additional showing of any such undue burden or oppression. (Pls.’ Mot. 13:23–24.) The party objecting to a discovery request on this basis bears the burden of supplying evidence of “the quantum of work required.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549.) 

 

Here, the Court agrees with Plaintiffs that Defendant has not supplied evidence to show the “quantum of work required” to respond in full to the subject RFPs. Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendant’s objections to the requests to the extent that they are unduly burdensome or oppressive are without adequate support.

 

C.    Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

 

Here, Plaintiffs seek monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel in the amount of $2,035.00, encompassing: (1) 2 hours of Plaintiffs’ attorney’s time spent preparing the instant motion; (2) an anticipated 2 hours reviewing Defendant’s opposition and drafting a reply; and (3) an anticipated 1 hour attending the instant hearing, at counsel’s hourly rate of $395.00 per hour. (Tran Decl. ¶ 12.) Plaintiffs also seek to recover $60.00 in filing fees. (Id. at ¶ 13.)

 

Here, in partially granting the instant motion, the Court finds it reasonable to award Plaintiffs sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $790.00.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted in part. Defendant to provide further code-compliant responses to Plaintiffs’ first set of RFPs, Nos. 29–30, 32–33, 37, and 44, within 30 days. The Court limits the scope of RFP Nos. 29, 30, 32, and 44 to consumer complaints regarding vehicles purchased in the State of California. The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $500.00.
