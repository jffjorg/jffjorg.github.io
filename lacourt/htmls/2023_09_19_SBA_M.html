Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Claim of Exemption

RULING

            The court continues the hearing.

BACKGROUND

On February 15, 2019, plaintiff and judgment creditor Amelia Yuhas sued defendants and judgment debtors Nayeem Khan and Gizmo Media LLC for breach of contract and common counts.

On March 18, 2022, the court entered judgment for Amelia A. Yuhas against Gizmo Media, LLC, and Nayeem Ahmed Khan, jointly and severally, in the amount of $160,617.36. 

On July 7, 2023, judgment creditor filed a notice of opposition to the claim of exemption. On July 11, 2023, judgment creditor filed a notice of hearing on claim of exemption.

On August 9, 2023, the court found that judgment creditor incorrectly served the judgment debtors. The court ordered the moving party to reset the hearing.

On August 24, 2023, judgment creditor filed a notice of opposition to the claim of exemption and a notice of hearing on claim of exemption.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“As a general rule, all property of a judgment debtor is subject to enforcement of a money judgment. The California Constitution, however, requires the Legislature to protect ‘a certain portion’ of a debtor’s property from forced sale. (Cal. Const., art. XX, § 1.5.) The purpose of this requirement is to protect enough of the debtors’ property from enforcement to enable them to support themselves and their families, and to help shift the cost of social welfare for debtors from the community to judgment creditors. [¶] To that end, California has enacted a ‘comprehensive and precisely detailed scheme’ governing enforcement of money judgments. The kinds and degrees of property exempt from levy are described in [Code of Civil Procedure] sections 704.010 through 704.210. These provisions relate to property of the debtor that would ordinarily be subject to enforcement of a money judgment by execution or otherwise, but for the statute allowing the debtor to retain all or part of it to protect himself and his family. These exemptions are wholly statutory and cannot be enlarged by the courts. And although the burden of proof lies with the party claiming the exemption, exemption statutes are generally construed in favor of the debtor.”  Kilker v. Stillman (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 320, 329–330.

CCP § 704.225 provides “Money in a judgment debtor’s deposit account that is not otherwise exempt under this chapter is exempt to the extent necessary for the support of the judgment debtor and the spouse and dependents of the judgment debtor.”   

CCP § 704.220(a) provides “Money in the judgment debtor’s deposit account in an amount equal to or less than the minimum basic standard of adequate care for a family of four for Region 1, established by Section 11452 of the Welfare and Institutions Code and as annually adjusted by the State Department of Social Services pursuant to Section 11453 of the Welfare and Institutions Code, is exempt without making a claim.”  CCP § 704.220(a).  The minimum exemption is currently at $2,080.00.  Form EJ-156. 

DISCUSSION

Judgment creditor opposes judgment debtor’s claim of exemption.

Here, the total amount due to judgment creditor is approximately $160,657.36 with accruing daily interest.  Writ of Execution at § 18.  According to the Financial Statement filed with the Claim of Exemption, Judgment debtor has $350.00 in a Bank of America bank account, $1,300.00 in a Chase bank account, and $700.00 in cash.  Financial Statement at § 3.  Judgment debtor has a monthly income of $2,700.00. Financial Statement at § 2. Judgment debtor’s spouse has a monthly income of $5,500.00. Financial Statement at § 1.  Judgment debtor’s monthly expenses is $8,700.00.  Judgment debtor claims $700.00 in transportation and auto expenses and $500.00 in installment payments, but does in item 5, lists only $500.00 in car payments. Financial Statement at § 4. Judgment debtor also lists student loans but does not identify the amount. Financial Statement at § 5. Under CCP § 704.220, $2,080.00 is automatically exempt.  CCP § 704.220(a); Form EJ-156. 

The court requests clarification on the transportation and auto expenses and the installment payments. The court also requests further information about judgment debtor’s student loans, including how much judgment debtor pays each month.

Accordingly, the hearing is continued.

Petitioner Nick Marier’s Motion for an Order Compelling Arbitration

 

RULING

Plaintiff Nick Marier’s Motion for an Order Compelling Arbitration is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

A summons on the petition was filed on July 13, 2023. Petitioner Nick Marier then filed the instant Petition for an Order Compelling Arbitration (hereinafter, “Petition”). Petitioner alleges that on April 13, 2022 petitioner and respondents Belen Katzen and Barry Katzen entered into a Residential Income Property Purchase Agreement and Joint Instructions (hereinafter, “RPA”) for the sale of real property located at 620 S. Fir Ave., Inglewood, California 90301. Petition, ¶ 1. Under the provisions of the RPA the parties are to bring any dispute or claim in law or equity to arbitration. Petitioner asks that this Court grant an order for the parties to arbitrate because respondents refuse to do so. No opposition papers were filed; therefore, the petition is unopposed.       

 

DISCUSSION

 

Legal Standard –

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947. The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. CCP § 1281.2. This is usually done by presenting a copy of the signed, written agreement to the court. “A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to CCP sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference.” CRC, 3.1330. The moving party must also establish the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy. CCP § 1281.2. The filing of a lawsuit against the moving party for a controversy clearly within the scope of the arbitration agreement affirmatively establishes the other party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy. Hyundai Amco America, Inc. v. S3H, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 572, 577.

 

Analysis -

Here, petitioner has established the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties and has attached a copy that recites the provisions of the arbitration agreement verbatim. See Petition, Exh. A, ¶¶ 36 & 38. Additionally, petitioner argues that the refusal of respondents to arbitrate is demonstrated by their attempt to cancel the RPA arguing that petitioner provided “fraudulent proof of funds.”

 

Respondents accusation of “fraudulent proof of funds” likely stems from when petitioner mistakenly provided an incorrect document for the verification of funds. Petition, ¶ 7. Petitioner realized this error and later corrected it, providing the corrected proof of funds. Id. Petitioner also alleges that the “Cancellation of Contract” may have been prompted by petitioner exercising his right to purchase the subject property for a lower sum. Petition, ¶ 9. However, petitioner asserts that neither of these are valid reasons to cancel the RPA under its terms. After review, the court agrees. Moreover, the attempt to cancel the RPA is sufficient to establish the party’s refusal to arbitrate the controversy.    

 

CONCLUSION

Accordingly, Plaintiff Nick Marier’s Motion for an Order Compelling Arbitration is GRANTED.

Motion to Strike Answer

            The court considered the moving papers.

RULING

            The court grants the motion.

BACKGROUND

On April 3, 2023, plaintiff Brian Whitaker sued defendant Soo Good Snack Bar LLC, a California Limited Liability Company, for violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act and California Disabled Persons Act.

On July 5, 2023, defendant answered.

On August 22, 2023, plaintiff moved to strike defendant’s answer.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, upon terms it deems proper, strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. CCP § 436(b).

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff moves to strike defendant’s answer.

Preliminarily, plaintiff satisfied the meet and confer requirements. CCP § 435.5(a).  As plaintiff’s counsel attests, she tried to set up a telephonic meet and confer with defendant’s owner, Allen Russel. (Decl. Butler ¶¶ 6 – 9.) Russel did not respond. (Decl. Butler ¶ 10.)

Plaintiff moves to strike on the grounds that a natural person cannot represent a corporation. A corporation cannot appear in court by an officer who is not an attorney, and it cannot appear in propria persona. Paradise v. Nowlin (1948) 86 Cal. App. 2d 897, 898. However, defendant is not a corporation, but a limited liability company. The same reasoning might apply equally to a limited liability company, which, like a corporation, is legal entity separate and apart from its members.  Lippenberger v. Canal Props. (2009) 2009 Cal. App. Unpub. LEXIS 8367, *6 (citing Corp. Code, §§ 17003, 17101;  PacLink Communications Internat., Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 958, 963; Grosset v. Wenaas (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1100, 1108.

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.
