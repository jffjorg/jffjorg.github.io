DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 20STCV38285    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: B

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT B

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

KAI SMITH,

 

Plaintiff, 

 

         vs.

 

GAME SPORTS BAR AND GRILL,

 

Defendant.

 Case No.: 20STCV38285

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  OCTOBER 9, 2024

 

 

 

Defendant Tyrei Lacy’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff Kai Smith’s Complaint is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Defendant Tyrei Lacy (“Lacy”) moves to strike portions of plaintiff Kai Smith (“Plaintiff’)’s Complaint in connection to its allegations for punitive damages (Compl., Prayer, Lines 11-12), attorney’s fees (Compl., Prayer, Lines 12-13), and any allegations of personal liability as to Lacy.

 

The Complaint alleges that, on or about October 6, 2018, Plaintiff attended a birthday party at Game Sports Bar and Grill. (Compl. ¶ 6.) Later that night, as the bar was closing, Plaintiff made his way to the restroom while holding a bottle of champagne that he purchased. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges that, at this time, a bouncer confronted him, shouting that he couldn’t take the champagne bottle out of the bar. (Ibid.) Plaintiff explained that he just needed to use the restroom and began to walk away. (Ibid.) Plaintiff alleges that another bar employee took the bottle from Plaintiff, and then two to three more bouncers assaulted Plaintiff, punching his head, face, and upper body. (Ibid.) Plaintiff claims serious injuries as a result. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

 

On October 5, 2020, Plaintiff filed the Complaint against Defendants Game Sports Bar and Grill, LLC (“Game Sports”) and DOES 1 to 10 alleging four causes of action: (1) negligent hiring, retention, and supervision; (2) assault; (3) battery; and (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress. The Complaint seeks punitive damages.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255; accord Cyrus v. Haveson (1976) 65 Cal. App. 3d 306, 316-17.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (Smith v. Sup. Ct. (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.)

 

CCP § 3294(a), governing punitive damages, provides: “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”

 

Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (CCP § 3294(c)(1).) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.” (Ibid., § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” is “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Ibid., § 3294(c)(3).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Lacy moves the Court to grant an order striking the Complaint’s matter in support of punitive damages. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 436.)

 

Pursuant to CCP § 1005, all responsive pleadings must be filed within 30 days of proof of service. Here, Lacy attempted multiple times to quash service of summons, with the Court’s last denial on June 26, 2024. (June 26, 2024, Minute Order.) At this time, the Court instructed Lacy to file all responsive pleadings within 30 days.

 

Lacy filed the instant motion on September 16, 2024, well past the 30 day deadline. Additionally, the motion provides no explanation as to its untimeliness.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Tyrei Lacy’s Motion to Strike is DENIED. Defendant is ordered to file and serve an answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint within 20 days.

DEPARTMENT B

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

GARY ZACCARIA, AN INDIVIDUAL,

Plaintiff, 

 

         vs.

 

RALPHS GROCERY COMPANY, A OHIO CORPORATION, et al.,

Defendants.

 Case No.:  22STCV05301

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 9, 2024

 

Petitioner Gary Zaccaria’s Motions to Compel Respondent Teachers Insurance and Annuity Association of America’s Responses to Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One, Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Motion for Order to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted are granted. Respondent is ordered to provide verified responses, without objections, within 15 days. Respondent is also ordered to pay $1,580 in sanctions within 15 days.

 

Petitioner Gary Zaccaria (“Petitioner”) moves to compel Respondent Teachers Insurance and Annuity Association of America (“Respondent”)’s responses to Form Interrogatories and Special Interrogatories, Set One, Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Motion for Order to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted (collectively “Discovery”). As to each motion, Petitioner seeks sanctions in the amount of $1,460, comprised of a $60 filing fee and four hours of work per motion at a rate of $350 per hour, including one hour in preparing a reply brief and one hour for attending each hearing.

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

 

Where there has been no timely response to a demand for the production of documents, the demanding party may seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300(b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  (CCP § 2031.300(a).) Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel . . . responses to interrogatories or production demands, absent substantial justification or other circumstances that make imposing the sanctions unjust.” (CCP § 2030.290(c).) Similarly, “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (CCP § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).)

 

Petitioner moves to compel Respondent’s responses to Discovery because Respondent failed to provide any responses. On January 23, 2023, Petitioner propounded Discovery requests on Respondent. (Moossai Decl. ¶ 2.) Respondent’s discovery responses were initially due on February 24, 2023, but Petitioner granted Respondent’s request for an extension of time up to March 14, 2023. (Ibid. at ¶ 3.) No responses were received. (Ibid. at ¶ 4.) Petitioner attempted to contact Respondent’s counsel numerous times on this matter, however, to date, Respondent has failed to respond to Petitioner’s communications or discovery requests. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 5-6.) Here, Respondent has failed to timely comply with their discovery obligation because they have failed to provide timely responses to discovery. Accordingly, Respondent is ordered to provide verified responses to Petitioner’s Discovery requests, without objection, within 15 days of this ruling.

 

Considering Respondent’s total failure to comply with their discovery obligations, the Court orders sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,580 (representing 4 total hours of work at a rate of $350 per hour plus a $60 filing fee for each motion).

Case Number: 23STCV18617    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: B

Hearing:             10/09/2024

Re:                         23STCV18617 Developers Surety and Indemnity Company vs Cyndy’s Cleaning Services

 

 

 

Plaintiff Developers Surety and Indemnity Company’s Request for Default Judgment

 

Defendant: Cyndy’s Cleaning Services

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Plaintiff Developer Surety and Indemnity Company’s request for default judgment is GRANTED.

           

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from the September 2019 stabbing and subsequent death of Defendant Cyndy’s Cleaning Services (“Cyndy’s”)’s employee Kamryn Stone. (Complaint ¶¶ 4-5.) Decedent was stabbed while acting in the scope of his employment as a custodian at the Rose Bowl stadium. (Ibid. at ¶ 5.) On June 30, 2021, Barry F. Stone, individually and as successor-in-interest to the Estate of Kamryn Stone and Kristie Campbell-Stone, brought suit against ABM Industry Groups, LLC (“ABM”), and other defendants, alleging premises liability, negligence, negligent hiring, and assault and battery. (Ibid. at ¶ 4.) ABM contracted with Cyndy’s to provide custodial services on the night of the assault. (Ibid.) On December 28, 2022, ABM filed a cross-complaint against Cyndy’s in the underlying action, alleging Cyndy’s was negligent in their hiring, supervising, and retaining of its employees, thereby contributing to the death of the decedent. (Ibid. at ¶ 7.) Plaintiff Developers Surety and Indemnity Company (“Plaintiff”) insured Cyndy’s as its named insured and agreed to provide a defense to Cyndy’s in the underlying action. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that bodily injury is not covered under the commercial general liability policy which it provided to Cyndy’s. (Ibid. at ¶ 9.) As a result, on August 7, 2023, Plaintiff brought the instant action for declaratory relief from its duty to defend and indemnify Cyndy’s against all defendants in the underlying action.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (Code Civ. Proc., § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff seeks judicial declarations that (1) Plaintiff has no duty to defend Cyndy’s against all defendants in the underlying action; and (2) Plaintiff has no duty to indemnify Cyndy’s against all defendants in the underlying action. Plaintiff also seeks an award of costs incurred in the amount of $715.50.

 

Cyndy’s has failed to timely respond or appear. Plaintiff’s default packet meets all statutory requirements under CCP § 585. Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Cyndy’s Cleaning Services is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice and prepared a proposed Judgment for the Court’s signature.
