DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 21STCV07836    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

SABRINA PETERSON, an individual,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

CLIFFORD HARRIS, an individual PKA TI; TAMEKA HARRIS, an individual PKA TINY; and SHEKINAH JONES ANDERSON, an individual,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          21STCV07836

 Hearing Date:   October 21, 2024

 Trial Date:        December 10, 2024

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion to Enforce Court Order, or, in the Alternative, for Terminating Sanctions

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Sabrina Peterson (Peterson) sues Defendants Clifford Harris, Tameka Harris, and Shekinah Jones Anderson pursuant to a March 1, 2021 Complaint alleging claims of (1) Defamation (Libel Per Se), (2) Defamation (Trade Libel), (3) Invasion of Privacy (False Light), (4) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage, (5) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage, (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (7) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.

The claims arise from allegations that, among other things, after Plaintiff Peterson posted a video and messages to her Instagram account accusing Defendants Clifford and Tameka Harris (the Harris Defendants) of various forms of sexual and physical abuse, Defendants made social media posts relating to Plaintiff Peterson’s son, sexual acts that Plaintiff Peterson allegedly engaged in with the Harris Defendants, and other subjects, which led to Plaintiff Peterson being harassed by persons on Instagram, as well as to various damages being suffered by Plaintiff Peterson.

B. Relevant Procedural History

On June 2, 2023, the Second District Court of Appeal reversed in part an order denying an anti-SLAPP motion, ruling that five of the seven causes of action in the Complaint violate California’s anti-SLAPP statute and ordered Peterson to pay the Harris Defendants’ attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in connection with their successful anti-SLAPP motion.

Following remand and briefing before this Court, on October 17, 2023, the Court ordered Peterson to pay $96,702.70 to the Harris Defendants on or before January 15, 2024.

Peterson has failed to do so.

C. Motion Before the Court

On August 29, 2024, Defendants Clifford Harris and Tameka Harris (the Harris Defendants) filed the instant motion to enforce court order, or, in the alternative, for terminating sanctions. The Harris Defendants request that the Court enter judgment for attorneys’ fees and costs in the amount of $96,702.70 in favor of the Harris Defendants and against Plaintiff. Or, in the alterative, request that, as terminating sanctions, the Court dismiss the two remaining causes of action in the Complaint with prejudice.

On September 19, 2024, Peterson opposed the motion.

On October 3, 2024, the Harris Defendants replied.

The motion came before the Court for hearing on October 10, 2024. Following argument of counsel, the Court requested that the parties submit supplemental briefing on the issue of whether the Court may issue judgment in favor of the moving defendants based on the attorneys’ fees award they received as prevailing parties on their anti-SLAPP motion following appeal.

A further hearing on the motion was scheduled for October 21, 2024.

On October 17, 2024 both parties submitted their supplemental briefs.

 

II. Motion to Enforce Court Order

On June 2, 2023, the Second District Court of Appeal ruled that five of the seven causes of action in Peterson’s Complaint violate California’s anti-SLAPP statute and ordered Peterson to pay the Harris Defendants’ attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in connection with their successful anti-SLAPP motion. Following remand and briefing before this Court, on October 17, 2023, the Court ordered Peterson to pay $96,702.70 to the Harris Defendants on or before January 15, 2024. To date, Peterson has failed to do so. Accordingly, the Harris Defendants argue that they are entitled to an entry of judgment for attorneys’ fees and costs. (Mot. p. 8.)

Alternatively, the Harris Defendants argue that the Court should grant terminating sanctions dismissing the two remaining causes of action, with prejudice. The Harris Defendants argue that Peterson’s willful disobedience of the Court’s October 17 Order is a valid basis alone for issuing terminating sanctions, but that in addition, Peterson’s failure to prosecute the action further warrants terminating sanctions. (Mot. p. 9.) The Harris Defendants assert that Peterson has failed to meaningfully participate in this action. They argue that Peterson and Peterson’s counsel have failed to timely oppose motions, appear at hearings, or communicate with Defendants counsel in over a year. (Mot. p. 10.) Peterson’s attorney Tyron Blackburn’s application to appear pro hac vice was denied by this Court on March 22, 2024 for failure to provide certain required information and proof of payment to the State Bar, and no subsequent applications have been filed. The Harris Defendants further emphasize that, despite a trial date in December 2024, Peterson “has failed to serve a single document request, a single interrogatory, a single deposition notice, or issue a single subpoena.” (Mot. p. 10:3-4.)

In opposition, Peterson argues that the Court should stay enforcement of the October 17, 2023 Order until the conclusion of trial. (Opp. p. 3.) Peterson further argues that there is no authority for the imposition of terminating sanctions. (Opp. p. 4.) Finally, Peterson argues that she should not be faulted for failing to prosecute the action because the case was stayed while the matter was on appeal. (Opp. p. 5.)

In their supplemental briefing, the Harris Defendants argue that the Court can issue judgment in their favor based on the attorneys’ fees award they received as prevailing parties on their anti-SLAPP motion following appeal because an award of costs originating in the Court of Appeal, as opposed to the trial court, “represents an independent judgment, enforceable by any available means.” (Rostack Invs., Inc. v. Sabella (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 70, 78.) They argue that here, as in Rostack, the award of attorneys’ fees and costs had its origin in the order of the Appellate Court, and the amount of the award could then be determined in the trial court. (Harris Defendants Suppl. Brief., pp. 2-3; Rostack, supra, 32 Cal.App.5th at 78-9.)

In Peterson’s supplemental briefing, she argues that the attorneys’ fees and costs the Harris Defendants seek here originated in the anti-SLAPP statute, not the Court of Appeal, and thus are not immediately enforceable as a separate judgment. (Plaintiff Suppl. Brief., pp. 3-4.) Peterson argues that in Rostack, the cost award stemmed from Rule of Court rule 8.278, but here the cost award stems from the anti-SLAPP statute in Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16. Peterson argues that the Court of Appeal explained this distinction in Lucky United Properties Investment, Inc. v. Lee (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 635 (Lucky II). In Lucky II, the Court of Appeal states: “[n]o statute or rule similarly provides that an award of costs or fees incurred on appeal pursuant to other statutory authority (such as § 425.16(c)) is enforceable as a separate money judgment.” (Lucky II, supra, 213 Cal.App.4th at 655.)

Here, the Court of Appeal distinguishes that costs and fees awarded pursuant to the anti-SLAPP statute are not immediately enforceable as a separate judgment, unlike costs and fees awarded pursuant to other authority such as Rule of Court rule 8.278, (Compare, Rostack, supra, 32 Cal.App.5th 70, and, Lucky II, supra, 213 Cal.App.4th 635).

Accordingly, the Harris Defendants’ motion to enforce court order, i.e., enter judgment for attorneys’ fees and costs is DENIED.

III. Motion for Terminating Sanctions

            As an alternative to their motion to enforce court order, the Harris Defendants also move for terminating sanctions.

The Court DENIES the motion for terminating sanctions given Plaintiff’s recent substitution of attorney. However, the trial date set for December 10, 2024, is firm, absent good cause. Should the Defendants demonstrate Plaintiff’s failure to prosecute the case the Court may consider a further motion for terminating sanctions. (Dubois v. Corroon & Black Corp. (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1689 [affirming dismissal of case for failure to prosecute.)

IV. Conclusion

The Motion to Enforce Court Order, or, in the Alternative, for Terminating Sanctions is DENIED.

ROBERT HEFFLEY, JR., Successor In Interest To His Wife, SULMA HEFFLEY-RODRIGUEZ, and ROBERT HEFFLEY, JR.,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

Lakewood REGIONAL MEDICAL CENTER, INC., JOSHUA DANIELS, RN., ROWEN MECANO, RN, MATTHEW SANDOVAL MHA, and DOES 1 to 50, Inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          23STCV10643

 Hearing Date:   October 21, 2024

 Trial Date:        September 23, 2025

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Demurrer and Motion to Strike [RES ID # 3618]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiffs Robert Heffley, Jr.—individually and as successor in interest to his wife, Sulma Heffley Rodriguez (decedent)—sue Defendants Lakewood Regional Medical Center, Inc. (Lakewood), Joshua Daniels, RN, Rowen Mecano, RN, Matthew Sandoval MHA, and Does 1 through 50 pursuant to a July 2, 2024 Second Amended Complaint (SAC) alleging causes of action for: (1) Violation of the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Welf. & Ins. Code, § 15600, et seq.); (2) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (3) Negligence and (4) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.

For ease of reference, the Court refers to Plaintiffs by their first name. No disrespect is intended.

The claims arise from the following allegations. As a result of being diagnosed with metastatic breast cancer with lytic lesions in the L4-L5 vertebral bodies, Sulma was admitted to Lakewood, a care custodian operating a twenty-four-hour facility for custodial care. Sulma was hospitalized at Lakewood between May 11, 2022, and June 17, 2022. During that time, Sulma was dependent on Lakewood and its employees/other Defendants for her basic needs, such as personal hygiene and feeding, among other things. Nevertheless, during Sulma’s inpatient treatment, Sulma was subjected to mistreatment, negligent care, and neglect when Lakewood nurses repeatedly left Sulma sitting in her own feces and urine for prolonged periods of time over a period of days or weeks, developing bedsores. Robert, Sulma’s husband, repeatedly received calls from Sulma regarding this neglect, prompting Robert on numerous occasions to call the hospital to demand that Sulma be changed. Robert also visited Sulma and found her at times sitting in her own feces and urine, again demanding immediate attention for Sulma. Despite these protests, Lakewood staff indicated that they were short staffed, and that Sulma would need to wait. Multiple Lakewood staff noted that they were understaffed, including Sulma’s treating doctor (Tate) and nurses at Lakewood. Moreover, Defendant Daniels and Mecano separately admitted that Sulma was treated inhumanely and that Lakewood was short staffed. Sulma subsequently died around July 20, 2022.

Robert sues Defendants individually based on his own injuries in seeing Sulma suffer from the above conduct at Lakewood, as well as based on Sulma’s injuries, with Robert as successor in interest of Sulma.

B. Motion Before the Court

On August 2, 2024, Defendants demurred to the second cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress and the fourth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress on the grounds that each claim fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

The same day, Defendants also filed a motion to strike portions of the SAC.

On October 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed separate oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike. There is no reply in the file.

 

II. Demurrer

A.    Meet and Confer

The Defendants have satisfied the meet and confer requirement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 subdivision (a)(3)(A). (Arabian Decl. ¶ 3.) 

B.    Requests for Judicial Notice

Pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d), the Court may take judicial notice of “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States”.

The court however may not take judicial notice of the truth of the contents of the documents. (Herrera v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1375.) Documents are only judicially noticeable to show their existence and what orders were made such that the truth of the facts and findings within the documents are not judicially noticeable. (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 885.) 

Pursuant to the Plaintiffs request, the Court takes judicial notice of this Court’s June 25, 2024, Minute Order. (RJN, Ex. 1.)

C.    Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) This device can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a [general] demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.)

A demurrer for uncertainty tests whether the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10, subd. (f).) 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616, disapproved on other grounds in Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guar. Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 46 [holding claims for unfair business practices need not be pled specifically, impliedly disapproving Khoury].) As a result, a special demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed only at uncertainty existing in the allegations already made. (People v. Taliaferro (1957) 149 Cal.App.2d 822, 825, disapproved on other grounds in Jefferson v. J.E. French Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 717, 719-720 [statute of limitations question].) 

D.    Analysis

Defendants demur to (1) the second cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress and (2) the fourth cause of action for negligent infliction of emotional distress, on the ground that each claim fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

1.     Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051.)

            Defendants contend that the SAC only conclusory states that Robert suffered ‘severe’ emotional distress, does not plead facts demonstrating extreme or outrageous behavior on behalf of the Defendants, and does not establish that Defendants acted intentionally or recklessly. (Demurrer, pp. 2-4.)  

            Here, Plaintiffs have sufficiently plead a cause of action for IIED. The SAC alleges that Robert suffered severe emotional distress because he personally observed the Defendants failing to adequately assist Sulma with her personal hygiene, repeatedly leaving her to sit in feces and urine for prolonged periods of time and the inattentive care continued despite Robert’s multiple emails to Defendants requesting that nursing staff assist Sulma and expressing his concern and anger. (See, e.g., SAC ¶¶ 25-27, 30, 32-34, 38, 40.)  Robert further alleges that Defendants “acted with reckless disregard of the probability that [Robert] would suffer emotional distress, knowing that he was present when the conduct occurred …” (SAC ¶ 58.)  

            Thus, the Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED.

2.     Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

There is no independent tort of negligent infliction of emotional distress. (Delfino v. Agilent Technologies, Inc. (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 790, 818.) 

“[The] negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort but the tort of negligence ….” (Marllene F. v. Affiliated Psychiatric Inc. (1989) 48 Cal.3d 583, 588.) 

Regarding whether NIED is an independent cause of action, it “is already the law in California” that “there is no duty to avoid negligently causing emotional distress to another, … that damages for emotional distress are recoverable only if the defendant has breached some other duty[] [Citations[]],” and that the “tort is negligence, a cause of action in which a duty to the plaintiff is an essential element. (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 984-985; see id. at p. 985 [“[U]unless the defendant has assumed a duty to plaintiff in which the emotional condition of the plaintiff is an object, recovery is available only if the emotional distress arises out of the defendant’s breach of some other legal duty and the emotional distress is proximately caused by that breach of duty. Even then, with rare exceptions, a breach of the duty must threaten physical injury, not simply damage to property or financial interests. [Citations.]”].) 

Defendants argue that NIED is not an independent tort, but rather the tort of negligence. They further argue that Robert fails to plead facts sufficient to constitute a claim for NIED as either a direct victim or bystander because he was neither a direct victim of the Defendant’s negligent nor was physically present at the time of the injury producing event. (Demurrer pp. 5-8.)  

Here, the Court agrees that NIED is not an independent cause of action. Further, although the SAC also includes a cause of action for negligence, and NIED can be a theory of recovery for negligence, the SAC only alleges negligence as to Defendants treatment of Sulma, it does not allege any duty or breach of duty to Robert. (SAC ¶¶ 61-72.) As a result, there is no basis for Robert to assert a claim for NIED regarding any emotional distress he may have suffered.

            Thus, the Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

 

III. Motion to Strike

            The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b).)

The Defendants move to strike the entire second cause of action for IIED and the accompanying prayer for relief as well as the entire fourth cause of action for NIED as its accompanying prayer for relief.

Here, the Court has overruled the demurrer to the second cause of action for IIED and sustained the demurrer to the fourth cause of action for NIED.

Thus, the Defendants’ motion to strike the second cause of action for IIED and the accompanying prayer for relief is DENIED. The Defendants’ motion to strike the fourth cause of action for NIED and its accompanying prayer for relief is GRANTED.

IV. Conclusion

Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is OVERRULED as to the Second Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

 Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend, as the Fourth Cause of Action for Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress.

The Defendants’ motion to strike the Second Cause of Action for IIED and the accompanying prayer for relief is DENIED.

The Defendants’ motion to strike the Fourth Cause of Action for NIED and its accompanying prayer for relief is GRANTED.

Defendant’s Answer to the operative complaint shall be filed within 30 days of this ruling.
