Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

(1)   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatories (Form – Employment and Special), Set One

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motions are GRANTED.

            Defendant Kaleo Marketing, LLC is ordered to serve further responses to plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories – Employment, Nos. 209.2, 211.1, 214.1, 214.2, and 216.1 and Special Interrogatories, Nos. 18, 30, 36, 42, and 48, within 15 days.

            Defendant Kaleo Marketing, LLC is ordered to serve further responses in compliance with CCP §2031.230 and to produce responsive documents, if any, to plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Nos. 22-27 and 29-34, within 15 days.

            The court orders defendant Kaleo Marketing and attorneys Mohajerian PC and Al Mohajerian to pay sanctions to plaintiff in the amount of $2538 within 30 days in total for both motions.

BACKGROUND

On November 7, 2022, plaintiff Brittany Harman filed a complaint against Kaleo Marketing, LLC and Michael Oberlander for (1) pregnancy discrimination, (2) retaliation for taking pregnancy leave, (3) violation of Family Rights Act, (4) violation of pregnancy disability leave, (5) disability discrimination, (6) failure to engage in a good faith interactive process, (7) failure to provide reasonable accommodations, (8) retaliation for reasonable complaint to employer, and (9) wrongful termination in violation of public policy.

On December 14, 2022, defendants filed an amended answer.

            On February 23, 2023, the court sustained with leave to amend plaintiff’s demurrer to the FAA.

On March 27, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories – Employment, Nos. 200.1-6, 201.1-7, 207.1-2, 208.1-.2, 209.2, 211.1-3, 214.1-2, 215.1-2, 216.1, Special Interrogatories, Nos. 1-62, and Request for Production of Documents, Nos. 1-51.  The court issued sanctions against defendant and defendant’s attorney of record in the amount of $3,338 for both motions.

45-Day Rule:  This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP §1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).

Meet-and-Confer Requirement:  The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).

Separate Statement:  Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3). 

Interrogatories

CCP §2030.300 states:  “(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:   (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. . . . (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.  (b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories. . . .”

Request for Production of Documents

On receipt of a response to an inspection demand, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general.  CCP §2031.310(a).  A statement of compliance shall state that the production, inspection, and related activity demanded will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.  CCP §2031.220.  “A representation of inability to comply with [a] particular demand for inspection . . . shall affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand.  This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.  This statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”  CCP §2031.230.

A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).  “To establish ‘good cause,’ the burden is on the moving party to show both:  [1] Relevance to the subject matter (e.g., how the information in the documents would tend to prove or disprove some issue in the case); and [2] Specific facts justifying discovery (e.g., why such information is necessary for trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial).  The fact that there is no alternative source for the information sought is an important factor in establishing ‘good cause’ for inspection.  But it is not essential in every case.”  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1495.6 (citations omitted).  “Declarations are generally used to show the requisite ‘good cause’ for an order to compel inspection.  The declarations must contain ‘specific facts’ rather than mere conclusions.”  Id. at 8:1495.7 (citation omitted).  “The declarations may be on information and belief, if necessary.  However, in such cases, the ‘specific facts’ supporting such information and belief (the sources of the information) must also be alleged.”  Id. at 8:1495.8 (citation omitted).  “Most declarations are made by the attorney for the moving party, who is usually more familiar with the relevancy and ‘specific facts’ constituting ‘good cause’ for inspection.”  Id. at 8:1495.9.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

4th cause of action for violation of Bane Act, Civil Code §52.1

The Bane Act, Civil Code §52.1, provides protection from interference by threats, intimidation, or coercion or from attempts to interfere with a person’s state or federal statutory or constitutional rights.  To prevail on a cause of action for violation of section 52.1, a plaintiff must show that the interference with his rights was accomplished through “threats, intimidation, or coercion.”  The essence of a claim under the Bane Act is that defendants, by the specified improper means, i.e., threats, intimidation, or coercion, tried to or did prevent plaintiff from doing something he or she had the right to do under the law or to force the plaintiff to do something that he or she was not required to do under the law.  Shoyoye v. County of Los Angeles (2012) 203 Cal. App. 4th 947, 955-56.

The complaint alleges that on November 10, 2020, plaintiff was threatened, coerced and/or intimidated within the meaning of Civil Code §52.1 when he was assaulted, battered, and his freedom of movement was impeded.  Complaint, ¶80.  Plaintiff’s assault, battery, and restrictive movement was accomplished through threats, intimidation, and coercion by Strauther.  Id., ¶81.  Strauther used his position as a Ralphs security officer to effectuate his threats, coercion, and intimidation of plaintiff to violate his right simply for not wearing a mask.  Id., ¶82.

Defendant Finnell argues that plaintiff has not alleged that Finnell took any action personally against plaintiff or that Finnell aided, abetted, or participated in the alleged use of force or engaged in any intentional conduct.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that he has sufficiently alleged an agency relationship between Finnell and Strauther, and that Finnell hired, employed, supervised, and assigned Strauther to provide security guard services when Guardian was not licensed to do so.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient that an agency relationship existed between Finnell and Strauther such that Finnell could be liable for Strauther’s alleged conduct in violation of the Bane Act.  Rather the complaint alleges that Strauther was an employee and or agent of Ralphs and Guardian International.  Complaint, ¶12.  Further, under this cause of action, plaintiff alleges that Strauther was Ralphs’ security guard.  Also, there are no allegations that Finnell engaged in any conduct that would constitute a violation of the Bane Act.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

6th cause of action for IIED

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress [IIED] exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’”  Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-51.

The complaint alleges that defendants’ actions were outrageous in that they purposefully attempted to detain plaintiff and interfered with this freedom of movement simply for not wearing a mask.  These actions were intended to cause severe emotional distress to plaintiff.  As a result, plaintiff suffered severe emotional distress and defendants’ actions were a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.  Complaint, ¶91.

Defendant argues that the allegations do not show that he engaged in any intentional conduct against plaintiff.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient as to an agency relationship between Finnell and Strauther and “the illegal activities Finnell assigned Strauther to perform.”

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to meet the elements of an agency relationship between Finnell and Strauther such that Finnell could be held liable for Strauther’s alleged extreme and outrageous conduct.  Rather the complaint alleges that Strauther was an employee and or agent of Ralphs and Guardian International, as stated above.  Further, there are no allegations that Finnell engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

7th cause of action for negligent hiring, supervision, or retention

The elements of a claim for negligent hiring, supervision, and/or retention are that (1) the employer defendant hired the employee; (2) the employee was or became unfit to perform the work for which the employee was hired; (3) the employer knew or should have known the employee was or became unfit or incompetent and that this unfitness or incompetence created a particular risk to others; (4) that the employee’s unfitness or incompetence harmed the plaintiff; and (5) that the employee’s negligence in hiring, supervising, or retaining the employee was a substantial factor in causing the plaintiff’s harm.  CACI No. 426.  “[A]n employer can be liable to a third person for negligently hiring, supervising, or retaining an unfit employee.”  Doe v. Capital Cities (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 1038, 1054.  To establish a cause of action for negligent hiring, retention, or supervision, a plaintiff must show that the employer knew or should have known that hiring the employee created a particular risk or hazard and that particular harm occurs.  See Z.V. v. County of Riverside (2015) 238 Cal. App. 4th 889, 902; Doe, supra, 50 Cal. App. 4th at 1054.

The complaint alleges that Ralphs has been aware for some time that Strauther and Guardian International were operating without a license as a regulated company, unlicensed to possess a baton, failure to properly wear an identifiable uniform and improperly maintaining personnel records as required by California law and having previously assaulted customers and witnessed on the same evening in the presence of responding officers.  As a result of civil lawsuits and complaints, Ralphs knew, or should have known through an exercise of reasonable diligence that Strauther and Guardian International were unfit and incompetent to be allowed the continued authority and responsibility to serve in the capacity as security officers, and perform the work for which they were hired and retained.  Complaint, ¶94.  Nevertheless, in spite of Strauther and Guardian International known and well documented wrongdoing, Ralphs has continued to employ Strauther and Guardian International and the failure of these policies, practices, and procedures has allowed Strauther and Guardian International to retain authority within, thus encouraging the wrongful and malicious acts and omissions described above.  Id., ¶95.  With a deliberate indifference to the consequences, Ralphs failed to take all reasonable steps necessary to prevent Strauther and Guardian International from retaliating against plaintiff by allowing defendants to continuously allow follow plaintiff throughout the location on subsequent visits, failed to take plaintiff’s and others complaints against Strauther and Guardian International seriously, failed to timely and thoroughly investigated and document evidence of harassment and misconduct by Strauther and Guardian International against plaintiff and others, failed to create an environment of transparent accountability by reporting the results of its investigation of misconduct and crimes to the victims and BSIS, plaintiff and proper authorities, and failed to take timely and appropriate and corrective action by counseling, retraining, supervising, reprimanding, disciplining, reassigning, terminating, and prosecuting defendants when they commit wrongdoing.  Id., ¶96.  Ralphs negligent hiring, supervision, and retention was a substantial factor in causing the harm to plaintiff.  Id., ¶97. 

Defendant argues that plaintiff has not alleged any facts that Finnell personally owed a duty of care to plaintiff or that he had knowledge of or ratified Strauther’s alleged conduct.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that he adequately alleged the elements, including that Finnell owed a duty of care to all foreseeable persons, including plaintiff.  Plaintiff asserts that he alleges that Finnell hired, employed, supervised, and assigned Strauther to work at Ralphs in providing unlicensed security services, that Finnell permitted Strauther to carry a police-style baton without a license and to provide security guard services without wearing an approved uniform, and that Finnell knew or should have known that Strauther was “unfit or incompetent as an unlicensed armed security guard carrying illegal weapons.”

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient as the allegations under the 7th cause of action are directed to Ralphs’ negligent hiring, supervision, and retention.  See para. 97.  There are no allegations that Finnell owed a duty or that he breached a duty or that he caused any harm to plaintiff under this cause of action or that he was Strauther’s employer.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

Motion for Summary Judgment on Partition by Sale Cause of Action

 

            The court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.  See Interlocutory Judgment.

BACKGROUND

On January 13, 2023, plaintiff Daphne Mercado filed a complaint for partition by real property against Porfirio Jimenez and Wells Fargo Financial California, Inc. (“Wells Fargo”) for partition of the two-unit duplex at 3527 W. 132nd Street, Hawthorne, CA 90250, held as joint tenants by plaintiff, who is agreeable to sell, and defendant, who has refused to sell and also refused to buy out the interest of plaintiff.  Plaintiff and defendant are formerly boyfriend and girlfriend.  Defendant Wells Fargo is named pursuant to CCP §872.510 as an entity “having or claiming interests of record . . . in the estate as to which partition is sought.”  No affirmative relief is sought as to Wells Fargo.

            On March 2, 2023, defendant Porfifio Jimenez filed a cross-complaint for indemnification, apportionment of fault, and breach of contract.  The cross-complaint alleges that on September 6, 2002, the parties jointly purchased the real property located at 3527 West 132nd Street, Hawthorne.  At the time of purchase, the parties entered into an oral agreement to equally contribute towards the upkeep of the subject property and equally divide any profits when the subject property was eventually sold.  The parties also agreed that cross-defendant would manage, maintain, keep an accounting of the rents for the property.  Cross-complainant agreed to improve the property by splitting units and creating more living space so that space could be rented.  Cross-defendant breached the oral agreement by keeping rents and not sharing them with cross-complaint, by failing to keep an accounting, by failing to maintain the property and allowing it to go into disrepair and waste, by collecting Covid funds and not sharing them with cross-complainant, and by intentionally damaging the subject property.  As a result, cross-complainant has sustained damages in the sum of at least $150,000.

            On March 29, 2023, the court denied plaintiff’s motion for interlocutory judgment of partition and appointment of referee without prejudice.

            On May 11, 2023, the court denied plaintiff’s motion for interlocutory judgment and noted that the statutory scheme envisions an interlocutory judgment of partition, which must come as a result of a trial or dispositive motion to determine the parties’ interests and rights.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  “A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.  Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.  The defendant or cross-defendant shall not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.”  CCP § 437c(p)(1).

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Daphne Mercado requests summary judgment on the grounds that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, and plaintiff is entitled to judgment as a matter of law on the sole cause of action for partition by sale in the verified complaint.

Property may be partitioned by physical division, sale of the property and division of the proceeds, or court approved and supervised partition by appraisal.  CCP §§873.210-290, 873.510-850, 873.910-980.  Civil Code §872.210 states:  “(a) A partition action may be commenced and maintained by any of the following persons:  (1) A co-owner of personal property. (2) An owner of an estate of inheritance, an estate for life, or an estate for years in real property where such property or estate therein is owned by several persons concurrently or in successive estates.”  “’The primary purpose of a partition suit is, as the terminology implies, to partition the property, that is, to sever the unity of possession.’”  LEG Investments v. Boxler (2010) 183 Cal. App. 4th 484, 493 (citation omitted).  “A co-owner of property has an absolute right to partition unless barred by a valid waiver.  ‘The right of partition may be waived by contract, either express or implied.’”  Id. (citations omitted). 

CCP §872.120 states, “In the conduct of the action, the court may hear and determine all motions, reports, and accounts and may make any decrees and orders necessary or incidental to carrying out the purposes of this title and to effectuating its decrees and orders.”  CCP §872.120 does not give the court authority to order the sale of the property prior to holding a trial, determining whether plaintiffs have a right to partition, and making an interlocutory judgment.  “The interests of the parties, plaintiff as well as defendant, may be put in issue, tried, and determined in the action.”  CCP §872.610.  “(a) At the trial, the court shall determine whether the plaintiff has the right to partition.”  CCP §872.710.  “(a) If the court finds that the plaintiff is entitled to partition, it shall make an interlocutory judgment that determines the interests of the parties in the property and orders the partition of the property and, unless it is to be later determined, the manner of partition.”  CCP §872.720.  “Notwithstanding Section 872.201, the court shall order that the property be sold and the proceeds be divided among the parties in accordance with their interests in the property as determined in the interlocutory judgment . . . .”  CCP §872.820.   CCP §873.010(a) states, “The court shall appoint a referee to divide or sell the property as ordered by the court.” 

The statutory scheme envisions that the interlocutory judgment of partition will be issued after trial or after summary judgment.  See LEG Investments v. Boxler (2010) 183 Cal. App. 4th 484 (reversing denial of, and ordering trial court to grant, plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication on partition cause of action).  See also Williams v. Williams (1967) 255 Cal. App. 2d 648, 652 (affirming summary judgment in partition action for plaintiff).

Plaintiff presents evidence that on September 6, 2002, a grant deed was recorded transferring the property [two-unit duplex at 3527 W. 132nd St., Hawthorne, CA 90250] from a third-party, a non-party to this action to “Porfirio Jimenez and Daphne Mercado” as “joint tenants” in the Official Records of Los Angeles County as Instrument No. 02-2092562.  Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (PUMF) 1.  On September 6, 2002, a deed of trust secured by the property was recorded in favor of Washington Mutual Bank, FA, in the Official Records of Los Angeles County as Instrument No. 2022-093805.  PUMF 2.  On October 13, 2004, a full reconveyance of the 2002 Trust Deed was recorded in the Official Records of LA County as Instrument No. 04-2630677.  PUMF 3.  On March 30, 2005, a deed of trust secured by the property was recorded in favor of Wells Fargo in the Official Records of LA County as Instrument No. 05-0730275.  PUMF 4.  On September 23, 2022, a loan modification agreement secured by the property was recorded in favor of dismissed Wells Fargo in the Official Records of LA County as Instrument No. 2022-0932805.  PUMF 5.  On all the encumbrances, past and present, the names of both plaintiff and defendant appear as the “Borrowers.”  PUMF 6.  On January 13, 2023, plaintiff’s verified complaint was filed.  PUMF 7.  On February 24, 2023, a notice of pendency of action was recorded in relation to this case.  PUMF 8.  On March 2, 2023, defendant filed his answer, without a verification and with an improper general denial.  PUMF 9.  On March 6, 2023, defendant filed his verified FAA, with an improper general denial.  PUMF 10.

Plaintiff also presents evidence that plaintiff has never waived the right to partition the property.  PUMF 14.  The property is a duplex on a suburban lot.  PUMF 15.  Plaintiff does not agree to the voluntary method of partition by appraisal.  PUMF 16.  See plaintiff decl., and exhibits; Scott Talkov decl.

In referencing the elements of an interlocutory judgment of partition by sale under CCP §872.720, plaintiff asserts that the title history shows that plaintiff and defendant each hold a ½ interest in the property.  See also verified complaint and 2002 Deed.  She has never waived this statutory right to partition.  Plaintiff also asserts that the property cannot be divided in kind as it is a two-unit duplex under one APN with no surplus land.  Moreover, plaintiff argues, defendant’s answer contained an improper general denial.  “If the complaint is verified . . . the denial of the allegations shall be made positively or according to the information and belief of the defendant.”  CCP §431.30(d).

The court finds that plaintiff has met her burden of establishing that there is no defense to the sole cause of action for partition as she has proved each element of the cause of action.  She is thus entitled to an interlocutory judgment directing that the subject property be sold.  The court finds that each party’s interest in the subject property is 50%.  The court also finds that plaintiff has not waived her right to partition.  The court further determines that it is most equitable to partition the property by sale.  A referee shall be appointed to sell the property pursuant to CCP §873.010.  After consideration of Matthew Taylor’s declaration, the court appoints Matthew Taylor as referee in accordance with the Interlocutory Judgment.

The motion is GRANTED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

The demurrer to the FAA is OVERRULED.

BACKGROUND

On July 10, 2023, plaintiff Diana Hayden filed a complaint against Sofi Stadium, Stadco, and Live Nation for (1) negligence and (2) premises liability.  Plaintiff alleges that on October 17, 2021, she and her daughter attended a Rolling Stones concert at Sofi Stadium.  Complaint, ¶7.  Plaintiff decided to leave the concert early with the hope of avoiding the rush of the crowd.  Id., ¶8.  While walking to her car, plaintiff encountered a large crowd of concert goers who were being directed and forced by defendants through temporary guard rails.  Id., ¶9.  These rails were not sturdy, and the crowd began knocking them over in an effort to get to their vehicles faster because the force and energy of the crowd were also unintentionally knocking the rails over.  Id., ¶10.  This crowd of people slammed into plaintiff and caused her to be violently thrown like a ragdoll into adjacent bushes.  This crowd slamming into plaintiff would not have happened but for defendants negligently forcing the crowd into the rails and the improper placement of the rails, which were not suitable for the crowd or directing the crowd.  Also, defendants failed to take adequate crowd control measures and failed to have adequate security and crowd control personnel on hand to ensure crowd safety as they exited the premises.  Id., ¶11.  The result was substantial injury to plaintiff’s body and mind.  Id., ¶12.

On August 21, 2023, Stadco LA, LLC (erroneously sued and served as Sofi Stadium and Stadco) filed an answer.

On August 25, 2023, Stadco LA, LLC filed a FAA.

On August 30, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint correcting Live Nation’s name to Live Nation Worldwide, Inc.

On August 31, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment to complaint designating Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. as Doe 1.

On September 15, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment correcting Stadco’s name to Stadco LA, LLC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Plaintiff may demur to an answer on the ground of insufficient pleading of defenses.  CCP §430.20.  Under CCP §431.30(g), every affirmative defense “shall be separately stated, and the several defenses shall refer to the causes of action which they are intended to answer, in a manner by which they may be intelligibly distinguished.”  “The same pleading of ‘ultimate facts’ rather than ‘evidentiary’ matter or ‘legal conclusions’ is required as in pleading the complaint.”  Weil and Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, 6:459.  “The answer must aver facts ‘as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and which are alleged in the complaint.’”  Id. (quoting FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 384).  “In general, whatever defendant bears the burden of proving at trial is ‘new matter,’ and thus must be specially pleaded in the answer.”  Civ. Proc. Before Trial, 6:431.  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff demurs to defendant Stadco LA, LLC’s First Amended Answer and the 1st through 8th affirmative defenses on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a defense and are uncertain.

            Plaintiff argues that the affirmative defenses fail to set forth any facts whatsoever.

            In opposition, defendant argues that the court should overrule the demurrer based on California’s liberal pleading standard that “only requires the plaintiff be put on notice of the nature of the defenses, and because modern discovery procedures can readily clarify any ambiguities.” 

            The court finds that the allegations are sufficient as to each of defendant’s affirmative defenses in its amended answer as defendant has plead the ultimate facts in support.

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice.

            The court considered the moving and opposition papers.  The court had continued the hearing to allow plaintiff to file an opposition.

RULING

            The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to punitive damages.  The motion is DENIED as to “In addition, Plaintiffs are entitled to attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to California Civil Code section 1942” contained in para. 39

BACKGROUND

            On March 10, 2023, Bonnie Busk filed a complaint against Equity Residential and Equity Residential, LLC for (1) negligent breach of implied warranty of habitability, (2) intentional breach of implied warranty of habitability (3) nuisance (negligence), (4) nuisance (intentional), (5) negligence, (6) IIED, (7) breach of implied covenant of quiet use and enjoyment, (8) negligent violation of statutory duties, (9) intentional violation of statutory duties, and (10) retaliation/constructive eviction.

            On May 30, 2023, defendant filed a stipulation regarding named defendants that Equity Residential Management, L.L.C. is the proper defendant.

            On June 1, 2023, plaintiff dismissed Equity Residential.

            On June 1, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC for (1) negligent breach of implied warranty of habitability, (2) nuisance (negligent), (3) negligence, (4) breach of implied covenant of quiet use and enjoyment, (5) negligent violation of statutory duties, and (6) retaliation/constructive eviction.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“The court may, upon a motion . . . , or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense.  (b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.  (c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

Civil Code §3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages in non-contract cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice . . . .”  The Court in Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-95, found that “[s]omething more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages.  There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or ‘malice,’ or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.”

“’Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(1).  

As the Court noted in College Hospital v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 713, Section 3294 was amended in 1987 to require that, where malice is based on a defendant’s conscious disregard of a plaintiff’s rights, the conduct must be both despicable and willful.  The Court in College Hospital held further that “despicable conduct refers to circumstances that are base, vile, or contemptible.”  Id. at 725 (citation omitted).

Civil Code §3294(b) states: “With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

A claim for punitive damages may not be based on conclusory allegations of oppression, fraud or malice, but instead must be based on factual allegations which support such a conclusion.  See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1041-42 (Court of Appeal issued a peremptory writ directing trial court to issue order striking plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages because “[t]he sole basis for seeking punitive damages are . . . conclusory allegations” which were “devoid of any factual assertions supporting a conclusion [defendants] acted with oppression, fraud, or malice”).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Equity Residential Management, L.L.C. requests that the court strike “intentionally” at para. 8, “willfully, knowingly, and purposefully” at para. 36, “intended to” at para. 36, para. 39, and prayer for punitive damages.

The FAC alleges that in March 2021, during her tenancy, plaintiff reported her concern that there might be mold as a result of a prior leak in or about the premises.  In response, defendants’ team inspected the premises and found no evidence of any leak or moisture and only some growth on a living room chair, which defendants cleaned.  Defendants attributed her problems to humidity in the apartment.  Defendants recommended that plaintiff run her air conditioning or open her windows to properly ventilate the unit.  Then, in July 2021, plaintiff again reported her concern that there might be mold in the premises.  In response, defendants’ team again inspected the premises and found no evidence of any leak, moisture, or mold therein, again attributing her problems to humidity in the apartment.  Defendants recommended, as before, that plaintiff open her windows to properly ventilate the unit.  And in September 2021, plaintiff again reported her concern that there might be mold in the premises.  In response, defendants’ team again inspected the premises and found no evidence of any leak or moisture, again attributing her problems to humidity in the apartment.  FAC, ¶7.  By September 2021, plaintiff, then 79 years old, became extremely lethargic, had difficulty breathing and was physically incapable of any activity.  Her condition had deteriorated to such an extent that she had to be transported to the hospital due to pressure in her chest.  In addition, her pet dog nearly died, and began suffering from allergies, hair loss and lethargy, requiring numerous visits to a vet.  Thereafter, in mid-September 2021, plaintiff engaged a laboratory to inspect the premises, as a result of which the presence of microbial contaminants, specifically Chaetomium and Penicillium/Aspergillus were, for the first time during plaintiff’s tenancy, detected inside and affecting the premises.  As a result, plaintiff was forced to vacate the premises.  At all times during plaintiff’s tenancy, defendants either knew or should have known of the existence of said water intrusion and moisture leaks, but intentionally, recklessly, or negligently omitted to advise plaintiff of said conditions.  Id., ¶8.

Defendant argues that the allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages as they do not show that defendant acted with malice, fraud, or oppression.  Defendant asserts that plaintiff has not alleged any supporting specific facts for her allegations, such as that there was in fact a water intrusion or moisture leak, where it was located, and how defendant knew or should have known about it.  Further, defendant contends, plaintiff alleges that each time that she complained of possible mold, defendants’ team inspected the apartment for a possible water intrusion and did not find one.  Defendant also argues that the FAC lacks adequate allegations that any corporate officer, director, or managing agent of defendant ratified the actions alleged to be wrongful.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages, including at paras. 8, 36, and 39.

The court rules as follows:  The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to support a claim for punitive damages.  To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness.  Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.  Rather, a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he [or she] knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.”  Id. at 90.  Further, the allegations must be sufficient for a reasonable jury to conclude that defendant’s conduct was “despicable” defined as “base, vile or contemptible.”  College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725.  “Nonintentional conduct comes within the definition of malicious acts only when a party intentionally performs an act which he knows, or should know, it is highly probable harm will result.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 907.  Defendant’s alleged conduct or lack of conduct does not constitute “despicable” conduct.  The allegations do not show malice, oppression, or fraud.

The motion is thus GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to punitive damages.  The motion is DENIED as to “In addition, Plaintiffs are entitled to attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to California Civil Code section 1942” contained in para. 39.  Defendant does not address attorney’s fees.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.
