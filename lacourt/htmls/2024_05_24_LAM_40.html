DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV24690    Hearing Date: May 24, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

Robert Drummond, as trustee of the Drummond Family Trust of 1991,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

Armin Monfared, Innovative Realty, Inc., Bye Bye House, Inc., Stephen Couig, Paul Hanson, Michael Marshall and Does 1 to 50,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV24690

 Hearing Date:   5/24/24

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion to Set Aside Default of Paul Hanson Pursuant to Discretionary Provision of Code of Civil Procedure Section 473 or, in the Alternative, Pursuant to the Mandatory Provision of Section 473.

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Robert Drummond, as trustee of the Drummond Family Trust of 1991 sues Defendants Armin Monfared, Innovate Realty, Inc. (sued as Innovative Realty, Inc.; hereafter, Innovative), Bye Bye House, Inc. (BBH), Stephen Couig, Paul Hanson, Michael Marshall and Does 1 to 50 pursuant to an October 10, 2023, Complaint alleging claims of (1) Specific Performance, (2) Breach of Contract, (3) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (4) Fraud - Deception, (5) Business and Professions Code sec. 17200, et seq., and (6) Negligence.

The claims arise from allegations relating to Defendants’ failure to meet contractual obligations for the purchase of 11911 Wagner Street, Los Angeles, California 90230, with Defendants misrepresenting that they would purchase the Property at the escrow price when Defendants knew that they would not purchase the property at the escrow price if they were not able to sell the property during escrow, and when Defendants discovered they would not be able to sell the property at an adequate profit, Defendants submitted a cancellation of escrow.

B. Service and Default

On October 20, 2023, Defendant Hanson was served with the summons and Complaint by personal service.

On November 21, 2023, Plaintiff Drummond secured an entry of default against Defendant Hanson.

C. Motion to Set Aside Default

On January 18, 2024, Defendant Hanson filed a motion to set aside the default entered against him. The motion is premised on mistake, inadvertence, surprise and/or excusable neglect, both in relation to discretionary and mandatory relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473. The essence of the motion is that defense counsel thought he had an agreement via email for Plaintiff’s counsel not to file defaults against defense counsel’s clients even beyond the statutory window to respond, but that Plaintiff’s counsel went ahead and filed those defaults, resulting in this motion.

On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff Drummond filed an opposition to Defendant Hanson’s motion. The motion challenges the characterization of the facts by the moving papers.

On May 15, 2024, Defendant Hanson replied to the opposition.

Defendant Hanson’s motion is now before the Court.

 

II. Motion to Set Aside Default: GRANTED.

A. Legal Standard

Section 473 subdivision (b) of the Code of Civil Procedure provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief from a judgment, dismissal, and/or order or other proceeding taken against a party through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b) [mandatory relief more narrowly targeted to defaults, default judgments, and dismissals]; Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.)

The provision of section 473, subdivision (b) provides that a court must vacate any resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against an attorney’s client whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entering of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by the attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect, unless the court finds that the fault or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

The affidavit need only attest to the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect in causing the default or default judgment—the reasons for it need not be explained. (Martin Potts & Associates, Inc. v. Corsair, LLC (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 432, 439.)

Attestation that one of these reasons existed is sufficient to obtain relief unless the trial court finds that the dismissal did not occur because of these reasons. (Graham v. Beers (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 1656, 1660.) This is because “the purpose of the mandatory relief provision under section 473, subdivision (b) is achieved by focusing on who is to blame, not why.” (Martin Potts, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 439.)

Mandatory relief under section 473, subdivision (b) may be denied where dismissal [or default] resulted from intentional conduct rather than a mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (See Pagarigan v. Aetna U.S. Healthcare of Cal., Inc. (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 38, 45-46.)

B. Analysis

The Court finds in favor of Defendant Hanson based on the mandatory portion of section 473 where there are reasonable arguments to be made on both sides as to mistake.

The emails referenced by the moving papers exist in the gray area of what defense counsel should or should have not understood from the language in Plaintiff’s counsel’s emails. (Mot., Gore Decl., Exs. A [now-defense counsel but status unclear at time of email, requests that no defaults be entered against Defendants including Hanson until confirmation that, as defense counsel suspects, defense counsel’s firm has been retained], B [Plaintiff’s counsel refuses to entertain holding off on defaults for “defendants you don’t represent” but notes that Plaintiff’s counsel is proposing an early settlement and would follow up with more detail], C [Plaintiff’s counsel follows up with an early settlement offer for “your potential clients[]”].)

With the benefit of time and context, a review of the emails shows it is more likely than not that Plaintiff’s counsel (1) refused to consider holding off on default when representation was not certain while (2) nevertheless sharing a global settlement in light of the future entry of defense counsel into the litigation.

The interpretation by defense counsel is not outside the realm of what a practitioner acting in good faith could interpret from these emails. It is a reasonable mistake to think that the defaults would not be entered where opposing counsel first refused to consider not entering default against Defendants where representation was not confirmed only to share a global settlement and explain the terms of that settlement in depth shortly thereafter. For defense counsel, likely moving from one task to the next, it is reasonable to perhaps not have thought that opposing counsel will grant some grace to the request to withhold on defaults, and based on counsel’s declaration taking full responsibility, the Court is satisfied that relief is appropriate under the mandatory portion of section 473. (Mot., Gore Decl., ¶¶ 5-7.)

The Court notes mandatory relief is appropriate as to defaults. Even if it were not, courts liberally grant relief from default when the error is corrected promptly and within six months. Code of Civil Procedure section 473.

The Court also notes that Defendant Hanson has properly filed a proposed Answer as an Exhibit to this motion. (Mot., Gore Decl., Ex. D.)

The Court next determines that while section 473 entitles Plaintiff’s counsel to fees, the Court will only be granting half of the fees requested, i.e., $750, not $1,500.

Default was entered a day after the statutory period to respond, with Plaintiff’s counsel not following up with defense counsel regarding whether representation had been confirmed. (Compare 11/8/23 Proof of Service, Defendant Hanson [served on 10/20/23], with 11/21/23 Entry of Default, Defendant Hanson; see Mot., Gore Decl., ¶ 5 [no follow up before default].) After the prior emails between counsel, this entire motion practice could have been avoided with a simple follow up.

The declarations from counsel also make clear that they have been having discussion as to setting this default aside, with counsel ultimately failing to agree to a resolution, and with both directing the fault for the lack of success in stipulating to set aside the default on the other. (Compare Mot., Gore Decl., ¶ 9, with Opp’n, Kielty Decl., ¶¶ 5-8, Exs. A-B.)

Still, because defense counsel could have been more diligent regarding confirming representation, the Court finds that the best balance would be to split the fault on the fees here.

Fees are thus GRANTED in part in the amount of $750, as limited to defense counsel, who has taken full responsibility for the mistake here. 

III. Conclusion

Motion to Set Aside Default of Paul Hanson Pursuant to Discretionary Provision of Code of Civil Procedure Section 473 or, in the Alternative, Pursuant to the Mandatory Provision of Section 473 is GRANTED in part as follows:

(1) GRANTED as to setting aside the November 21, 2023, default against Defendant Paul Hanson; and

(2) GRANTED in part as to sanctions in the amount of $750.

The Ryan Firm is ORDERED to remit sanctions of $750 to Plaintiff Robert Drummond within ten days of this ruling.

II. Showtime Defendants, Demurrer [CRS# 8142]

A. Requests for Judicial Notice

Per the Showtime Defendants’ request, the Court takes judicial notice of various filings and orders in this action. (Demurrer & Mot., RJN, p. 1, Exs. A-H.)

Per Plaintiff Rueda’s request, the Court takes judicial notice of various filings and orders in this action. (Opp’n, RJN, pp. 2-3, Exs. 1-11.)

B. Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212, superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) “[E]ach evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

A demurrer may only be used to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) Courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds in Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

C. Demurrer, TAC, Ninth Cause of Action, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: SUSTAINED, without leave to amend.

The Court SUSTAINS the Showtime Defendants’ demurrer, without leave to amend.

The Court notes that the below discussion involves allegations taken as true for demurrer purposes. No evidentiary findings are being made. The tense of the discussion is one for ease of discussion, accepting the allegations in the TAC as true where sufficiently pleaded.

1. Relevant Law

An amended complaint filed after the statute of limitations has expired is time-barred unless it relates back to a timely-filed original complaint. (Scholes v. Lambirth Trucking Co. (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 590, 597.) Under California law, relation back applies only if the amendment (1) is based on the same general set of facts, (2) involves the same injury and accident, and (3) refers to the same instrumentality alleged against the defendant in the original complaint. (Foxborough v. Van Atta (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 217, 230; Coronet Mfg. Co. v. Super. Ct. (1979) 90 Cal.App.3d 342, 345, 347-348.)

2. Analysis

IIED has a two-year statute of limitations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 335.1.)

In their demurrer, the Showtime Defendants argue that the TAC’s allegations of 2015 or 2016 intimidation or coercion ran their limitations period as of, at the latest, 2018, which is long before the 2024 TAC in this case, or even the Doe amendments in 2019.

The Showtime Defendants are correct that they were not “named defendants” in the IIED claim until the January 10, 2024, Second Amended Complaint was filed.

However, Rueda argues that because the Showtime Defendants were named as Doe Defendants (specifically, CBS as Doe 1 and Showtime as Doe 2) on September 11, 2019, and the Does were included as defendants in the IIED claim, those amendments relate back to what was then the operative complaint, the First Amended Complaint, which was filed on August 25, 2016.

Yet the Doe Amendments were always a bit of a bad fit, in that, as noted by this Court in its ruling on the motion for judgment on the pleadings, it was unclear as to what conduct the Showtime Defendants were alleged to have engaged in directly or indirectly. Specifically, the Court noted that in the FAC, Does 1-3 were alleged to be “men,” and Does 1-6 were alleged to have engaged in harassment, whereas CBS and Showtime are corporate entities, who can only act through others. (12/21/23 minute order at pp. 11-12.)

Indeed, the Showtime Defendants had expressed even before that motion for judgment on the pleadings that it has been unclear as to what allegations were being made against them in the Ninth Cause of Action when the Doe Amendments were made. For example, in the second appeal in this case, the court of appeal noted that the CBS Defendants argued that “Rueda has not alleged the CBS Defendants themselves participated in any assault, harassment, or other bad act.” (July 26, 2023 Opinion at p. 18.) The court noted that the CBS Defendants should or could make such an argument in a demurrer. (Id. at p. 20.)

Here, the sequence of events is important. The FAC, filed in 2016, included CBS and Showtime as named defendants to five causes of action, but not in the ninth claim for IIED. Plaintiff then added those corporations as Doe Defendants in 2019, which added them to the ninth cause of action, but, as later found by this Court, the Doe 1 and 2 allegations did not fit contentions that would relate to a corporate overseer, but rather fit allegations relating to an individual person allegedly hired by or acting at the behest of a corporate overseer.  

So things stood until after the second appeal was issued in 2023, when the CBS Defendants filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings to clarify this ambiguity (or have the claim dismissed altogether). The Court allowed leave to amend, and the amendments eventually led to the TAC, which revealed the names of two new alleged bad actors – Richard Palmer and Liam Vaughan – in the paragraphs previously using the Doe moniker, and alleged that the Showtime Defendants were liable in an agency fashion for the acts of these men and others still unknown to the Plaintiff. (TAC, 42-47, 83-85.)

It is in this context that Rueda is seeking to have the TAC relate back to the allegations made in the FAC as filed on August 25, 2016, arguing that the 2019 Doe Amendments adding CBS and Showtime to the ninth cause of action themselves related back to the FAC.

After consideration, the Court rejects this argument. As the court of appeal noted in Scherer v. Mark (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 834, 839 (Scherer), the purpose of the Doe amendment is where a plaintiff is “ignorant of the true name of the defendant.”  (See also Code Civ. Proc. § 474.) There, the court held that “[e]ven though the original complaint correctly pleaded a cause of action against unknown Does, plaintiff did not meet the statutory requirements of section 474 with reference to [her doctor] respondent Dr. Mark . . . [where t]he undisputed facts are that the defendant Mark was known to plaintiff at the time of the filing of her original complaint.” (Scherer at p. 840.)

Even if other cases have held that even ignorance of the facts giving rise to the cause of action against the person can still provide a basis for a later Doe amendment (Scherer at pp. 840-841), the Court does not find that doctrine applicable here. The undisputed facts are that the identity of CBS and Showtime was well known to plaintiff as of the filing of the FAC because some five other claims were made against them in the FAC; that the Doe Amendments, as the FAC was drafted, purportedly alleged that CBS and Showtime themselves engaged in harassing conduct; and that the first time that plaintiff alleged that the Showtime Defendants were responsible for the harassing comments of others was in the TAC. Plaintiff did not, for example, include in the FAC two sets of Doe Defendants – one for those hiring the “thugs” and one for the “thugs” – and then brought in CBS and Showtime in the former set of Does. Plaintiff could also have filed a motion for leave to amend back in 2019 when he claims he finally realized that CBS and Showtime were involved in the threats, which could have clarified things a great deal. Instead, Rueda used a Doe amendment, which only muddied the waters until the claims were dismissed with leave to amend. In this fact-specific context, the Court does not find that relation back should apply some 8 years after the FAC was filed and 5 years after the ambiguous Doe amendments.

The Showtime Defendants also argue, and the Court agrees, that the November 2017 allegations in paragraph 50 of the TAC do not relate back for the independent reason that it was not alleged for the first time until 2024—both as to an unidentified individual flashing a gun at Rueda and Rueda leaving Los Angeles—well beyond the two-year limitations period.

The Court does not find that it has ever previously ruled that the IIED claims were timely as to the Showtime Defendants, and the citation to its 12/21/23 ruling regarding whether the CBS/Showtime Defendants appeared in the three-year time period under Code of Civil Procedure 583.220 is inapposite.

The Showtime Defendants’ demurrer is thus SUSTAINED, without leave to amend, as to the TAC’s ninth cause of action, as alleged against the Showtime Defendants.

Based on this result, the Court does not address the remaining points for demurring to the TAC’s ninth cause of action.

B. Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)

For the purposes of a motion to strike pursuant to Sections 435 to 437 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the term “pleading” generally means a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint, (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (a)), and an immaterial allegation or irrelevant matter in a pleading entails (1) an allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense, (2) an allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense, or (3) a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subds. (b)(1)-(3), (c)).

C. Analysis

1. Motion to Strike, TAC, ¶ 50: GRANTED, without leave to amend.

The Court adopts its discussion from Section II above to determine that the TAC’s 50th paragraph alleges liability against the Showtime Defendants based on factual grounds beyond the statute of limitations.

For this reason, the Showtime Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED, without leave to amend, specifically as to the remaining two sentences (first and last) of the 50th paragraph of the TAC—(portions of which were already stricken in the anti-SLAPP motion)—insofar as they are alleged against the Showtime Defendants.

3. Motion to Strike, TAC, ¶ 11: DENIED.

a. Relevant Law

Through participation in a civil conspiracy, a coconspirator effectively adopts as his or her own the torts of other coconspirators within the ambit of the conspiracy. In this way, a coconspirator incurs tort liability co-equal with the immediate tortfeasors. (City of Industry v. City of Fillmore (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 191, 211-212, quoting Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 510-511.)

In order to maintain an action against a defendant on a theory of vicarious liability arising out of his joining a conspiracy, the alleged facts must show either expressly or by reasonable inference that he had knowledge of the object and purpose of the conspiracy, that there was an agreement to injure the plaintiff, that there was a meeting of the minds on the objective and course of action, and that as a result one of the defendants committed an act that resulted in the injury. (See Schick v. Lerner (1987) 193 Cal.App.1321, 1328 [within context of already formed conspiracy].)

Courts analyze whether the conspiracy allegations are supported by properly pleaded ultimate facts while disregarding the conclusory allegations in the pleading, e.g., allegations that rely solely upon talismanic allegation of conspiracy, which amount to nothing more than attempting to fashion a potpourri of theories unsupported by properly pleaded ultimate facts. (Ibid.)

b. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Rueda, assuming that there remain any portion of these allegations that relate to causes of action other than the ninth, which has been dismissed.

The Court, for consistency, directly adopts the reasoning of its December 2023 order, as applied to the identically pled TAC in relation to agency, as ruled in relation to Defendants Pacquiao’s and Davidson’s motions for judgment on the pleadings.

“An allegation of agency constitutes an averment of ultimate fact, which we accept as true on a demurrer. But where factual allegations are based on information and belief, the plaintiff must allege information that leads the plaintiff to believe that the allegations are true.” (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1077, 1106.) “[G]eneral allegations of agency that are contradicted by, or inconsistent with, facts specifically pleaded, will be disregarded.” (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), Plead §918.)

For purposes of this motion for judgment on the pleadings, the Court accepts as true Plaintiff’s allegation that the Doe defendants, i.e. including the “goons” from Wildcard Gym, were agents of the named Defendants, including the Showtime Defendants. This allegation of ultimate fact must be accepted as true, even if it is a general allegation and regardless of Plaintiff’s ultimate ability to prove it. Indeed, “we accept as true even improbable alleged facts, and we do not concern ourselves with the plaintiff’s ability to prove the factual allegations.” (Marina Pacific Hotel and Suites, LLC v. Fireman’s Fund Insurance Company (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 96, 104-105.)

Moreover, Plaintiff is not alleging an ordinary agency akin to a real estate broker or an employee. Plaintiff is alleging that the Showtime Defendants instructed these Does to illegally intimidate Plaintiff into accepting the settlement offer proposed by Davidson. Plaintiff supports his general allegation of agency with specific allegations of the circumstances indicating that the “goons” were acting on behalf of the Showtime Defendants. Whether Plaintiff can prove the claims is of course not germane to a decision at this stage of the case.

The Showtime Defendants’ motion to strike is thus DENIED as to striking paragraph 11 from the TAC.

For this reason, the Showtime Defendants’ motion to strike is MOOT as to the TAC’s 86th and 88th paragraphs, with both paragraphs stricken according to notice.

 

IV. Freddie Roach, Demurrer [CRS# 4961]

A. Requests for Judicial Notice

Per Plaintiff Rueda’s request, the Court takes judicial notice of the Court’s December 21, 2023, order in this action, vis-à-vis Defendants Pacquiao and Davidson. (Opp’n, RJN, p. 2, Exs. A.)

B. Legal Standard

See Section II.A.

C. Demurrer, TAC, Ninth Cause of Action, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: OVERRULED.

1. Relevant Law

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’” (Hughes, supra, 46 Cal.4th at pp. 1050-1051.)

2. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Rueda.

The Court has accepted the FAC’s agency allegations above, and the conspiracy allegations remain in the 13th paragraph of the TAC. (See Section V.B.2. infra.)

Moreover, the TAC sufficiently alleges conspiracy grounds against Defendant Roach.

The TAC alleges a connection between Les Moonves and Creative Artists Agency (CAA) agent Nick Khan. That connection was one in which Les Moonves explicitly told Nick Khan to “handle this guy,” meaning Rueda, and Moonves would “pay half” of whatever it would take to “take [Rueda] off his hands.” (FAC, ¶ 35.) The connection is an explicit directive rather than a suggestive, will no one rid me of this meddlesome priest, comment, strengthening the sufficiency of the pleading. Moreover, the financial incentive is clear and a clear motivator: If Nick Kahn settles the matter, he gets a fee comprised of 50% of the payout that Gabriel Rueda accepts.

The TAC alleges Nick Kahn’s affirmative assent to this agreement, with Nick Kahn making statements regarding incidents in boxing of coercing others into settling debts for less money than originally agreed upon, with cash being king to street guys, connoting enforcers who do the coercing in exchange for payment. (FAC, ¶ 36.) This allegation fleshes out a conspiracy, the aim and actors of which are clear: Les Moonves, acting through Nick Kahn, will use enforcers to intimidate and coerce Gabriel Rueda into taking a reduced amount of money, with “street guys” to be hired as enforcers a la alleged King-Ali intimidation, and if they are successful in achieving the ends of this conspiracy to coerce Rueda of allegedly agreed upon finder’s fee, then Nick Kahn would be paid an amount equal to half of Rueda’s reduced payout.

In paragraphs 35 and 36, the TAC sufficiently alleges the formation of the conspiracy, its initial actors, its objective, and the benefits to the actors in achieving the end of the conspiracy. To the extent any of the applicable replies argues that these allegations do not sufficiently tie in Les Moonves, the Court disagrees. Les Moonves is clearly tied to a conspiracy to Nick Kahn as discussed above. Even if the allegations involve some innuendo, the actors, objectives, and benefits of the conspiracy are clear through the allegations in these paragraphs.

Next, the TAC alleges growth of the conspiracy. The TAC alleges a connection between Nick Kahn and Defendant Keith Davidson: Nick Kahn forwards emails between Plaintiff and CBS (now Paramount Global) to Defendant Davidson. (TAC, ¶ 37.)

After that, Davidson is alleged to have engaged in conduct to that may be “so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community,” i.e., taking actions to threaten a person’s employment, such as calling that person’s employer to inform the employer that the employee was blackmailing a highly influential television executive that patrons the business—an allegation explicitly alleged as applicable to Defendant Roach. (TAC, ¶ 41.)

Thereafter, a number of allegations not challenged on demurrer or motion to strike vis-à-vis Freddie Roach, detail a campaign of intimidation and harassment against Rueda. (TAC, ¶¶ 42-47.)

These allegations sufficiently allege IIED for pleading purposes.

Thus, even if Defendant Roach is not directly involved in these allegations, the Court has found two bases for find vicarious liability against him—agency and conspiracy—as based on the alleged conduct of Defendant Davidson, who allegedly engaged third-party enforcers, and whose conduct and the conduct of the enforcers sufficiently amounts to a case for IIED at the pleadings stage.

Freddie Roach’s demurrer is thus OVERRULED.

 

V. Freddie Roach, Joinder to Showtime Defendants’ Motion to Strike [CRS# 4961]

A. Legal Standard

See Section III.A.

B. Analysis

1. Motion to Strike, TAC, ¶ 50: GRANTED, without leave to amend.

The Court adopts its discussion from Section II above to determine that the TAC’s 50th paragraph alleges liability against Freddie Roach based on factual grounds beyond the statute of limitations.

The Court specifically notes that the statute of limitations window is the same between the Defendants because there is no reason to apply a different date of discovery as between Defendants in relation to the allegations of the 50th paragraph, which is part of the same web of agency and conspiratorial liability between Defendants.

For this reason, Defendant Roach’s joinder to the Showtime Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED, without leave to amend, specifically as to the remaining two sentences (first and last) of the 50th paragraph of the FAC—sans SLAPP-stricken allegations—insofar as they are alleged against Freddie Roach.

2. Motion to Strike, TAC, ¶ 11: DENIED.

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff Rueda.

The Court, for consistency, directly adopts the reasoning of its December 2023 order, as applied to the identically pled TAC in relation to agency, as ruled in relation to Defendants Pacquiao’s and Davidson’s motions for judgment on the pleadings.

“An allegation of agency constitutes an averment of ultimate fact, which we accept as true on a demurrer. But where factual allegations are based on information and belief, the plaintiff must allege information that leads the plaintiff to believe that the allegations are true.” (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1077, 1106.) “[G]eneral allegations of agency that are contradicted by, or inconsistent with, facts specifically pleaded, will be disregarded.” (5 Witkin, Cal. Proc. (6th ed. 2021), Plead §918.)

For purposes of this motion for judgment on the pleadings, the Court accepts as true Plaintiff’s allegation that the Doe defendants, i.e. including the “goons” from Wildcard Gym, were agents of the named Defendants, including the Showtime Defendants. This allegation of ultimate fact must be accepted as true, even if it is a general allegation and regardless of Plaintiff’s ultimate ability to prove it. Indeed, “we accept as true even improbable alleged facts, and we do not concern ourselves with the plaintiff’s ability to prove the factual allegations.” (Marina Pacific Hotel and Suites, LLC v. Fireman’s Fund Insurance Company (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 96, 104-105.)

Moreover, Plaintiff is not alleging an ordinary agency akin to a real estate broker or an employee. Plaintiff is alleging that Freddie Roach instructed these Does to illegally intimidate and harass Plaintiff into accepting the settlement offer proposed by Davidson. Plaintiff supports his general allegation of agency with specific allegations of the circumstances indicating that the “goons” were acting on behalf of Freddie Roach. Whether Plaintiff can prove the claims is of course not germane to a decision at this stage of the case.

Freddie Roach’s joinder to the Showtime Defendants’ motion to strike is thus DENIED as to striking paragraph 11 from the TAC.

3. Motion to Strike, TAC, ¶¶ 86, 88: GRANTED as limited to “and non-payment of his finder’s fee,” without leave to amend.

a. Relevant Law

“[D]amages for mental suffering and emotional distress are generally not recoverable in an action for breach of an ordinary commercial contract in California.” (Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 558-559.)

b. Analysis

The Court finds in favor of Freddie Roach.

The oppositions to the motions challenging the above noted portion of these paragraphs do not convince the Court that the alleged “harm” in paragraph 86 is not alleging harm that sounds in contract where the harm is specifically alleged to have resulted from “non-payment of his finder’s fee.” Paragraph 86 is clear. Plaintiff suffered either harm in the form of “non-payment of his finder’s fee,” or Plaintiff suffered harm as a result of pain and suffering, defined as “difficulty at work” and “non-payment of his finder’s fee.” The allegation is not drawn in conformity with California law as explained in Erlich and is thus improper.

Freddie Roach’s motion to strike is GRANTED as to the TAC’s 86th paragraph. The Court STRIKES “and non-payment of his finder’s fee” from that allegation, as requested in the notice for the joinder, as against Freddie Roach.

The Court also GRANTS relief as to emotional distress damages that clearly arise from a breach of contract. The opposition does not address Erlich. Simply, there is a difference between emotional distress damages arising from the threats and intimidation, and those arising from non-payment of contractually owed monies, where paragraph 88 falls into the latter category.

2. Motion to Strike, TAC, ¶¶ 3, 22, 24, 26, 32, 53, 56, 64: GRANTED in part, without leave to amend.

The Court finds in favor of Defendant Paquiao.

The Court agrees that the challenged allegations are extraneous absent the breach of contract claims against Defendant Pacquiao.

Moreover, the allegations are not relevant or material in any way to the remaining basis for liability alleged in the TAC as against Defendant Paquiao. For example, “based on Pacquiao’s agreement,” “Pacquiao [has] not paid Rueda anything,” and “Pacquiao ratified that agreement also agreeing to the fee” all seek to rehash liability against Defendant Pacquiao for nonpayment of the finder’s fee because they are contract-based grounds for liability, where contract issues were summarily adjudicated in favor of Defendant Pacquiao. VIII. Freddie Roach, Second Deposition, Joined by Showtime Defendants: GRANTED with conditions.

A. Legal Standard

“Once any party has taken the deposition of any natural person, including that of a party to the action, neither the party who gave, nor any other party who has been served with a deposition notice pursuant to Section 2025.240 may take a subsequent deposition of that deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.610, subd. (a).)

However, “for good cause shown, the court may grant leave to take a subsequent deposition, and the parties, with the consent of any deponent who is not a party, may stipulate that a subsequent deposition be taken.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.610, subd. (b).)

Section 2025.610 does not preclude taking one subsequent deposition of a natural person who has previously been examined as a PMQ or pursuant to a court order under Section 485.230 for the identity, location, and value of property in which the deponent has an interest, and does not authorize the taking of more than one subsequent deposition for the limited purpose of a right to attachment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.610, subds. (c)-(d) [not applicable here].)

The total time for deposition by all counsel is generally limited to seven hours. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.290, subd. (a).) However, a court shall allow additional time, beyond any limits imposed by this section, if needed to fairly examine the deponent or if the deponent, another person, or any other circumstance impedes or delays the examination. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.290, subd. (a).) Subdivision (a) and parts of subdivision (b) have been found by California courts to impose merely presumptive restrictions that are applicable only if the court does not order otherwise, thus giving courts ample discretion as to additional deposition time. (Certainteed Corp. v. Superior Court (2014) 222 Cal.App.4th 1053, 1060-1062 (Certainteed) [seven-hour limit and 14-hour limit in complex cases merely presumptive], superseded by statute on other grounds as discussed in Cahill Construction Co., Inc. v. Superior Court (2021) 66 Cal.App.5th 777, 786-787 (Cahill) [Legislature responds to Certainteed by enacting law becoming Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.295].)

B. Analysis

The Court GRANTS Freddie Roach’s motion, with conditions.

The Court will reopen discovery as it stated in the Court’s December 21, 2023, ruling regarding Defendant Davidson’s motion to compel deposition. (12/21/23 Minutes, Davidson Compel Deposition, p. 8.)

However, the Court will discuss with counsel at oral argument how much time will be needed for the deposition, particularly where the Showtime Defendants are no longer operative Defendants on the ninth cause of action.
