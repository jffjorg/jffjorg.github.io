DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21SMCV00498    Hearing Date: June 10, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

June 10, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21SMCV00498

MOTION

Motion for Leave to Amend Second Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Myriad Pictures, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises out of a dispute over the production and distribution rights to the film franchise Jeepers Creepers. Plaintiff Myriad Pictures, Inc. alleges it holds the rights of first negotiation and last refusal to the franchise.

 

Plaintiff alleges the defendants produced and released a fourth installment of the film franchise without their knowledge or involvement. Plaintiff also alleges the defendants are currently producing a fifth installment of the film franchise without their knowledge or involvement.

 

On March 16, 2021, Plaintiff filed its original Complaint, which included defendant Screen Media. Plaintiff filed its First Amended Complaint (FAC) on July 7, 2021.

 

On July 25, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File its Second Amended Complaint (SAC). Plaintiff filed its SAC on July 26, 2023.

 

MOTION

 

On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the present Motion for Leave to Amend SAC.   Plaintiff moves for leave to amend the complaint to name three additional defendants: Seth Needle (Needle), David Fannon (Fannon), and the Estate of Mark Damon (Damon).

 

Plaintiff alleges Fannon serves as Executive Vice President for defendant Chicken Soup for the Soul Entertainment, Inc. (CSSE) and as President for defendant Screen Media Ventures LLC (Screen Media).

 

Plaintiff alleges Needle served as Executive Vice President of Global Acquisitions and Co-productions for defendant Screen Media. Plaintiff also alleges Needle was an Executive Producer on the production of the fourth Jeepers Creepers film.

 

Plaintiff alleges Damon served as a Producer and Chief Executive Officer for Foresight Unlimited, which Plaintiff alleges was acquired by defendant CSSE.

 

            Plaintiff’s motion is unopposed.   

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Amendments to Pleadings: General Provisions

 

            Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”

 

            To wit, without notice to the other party the Court has wide discretion to allow either party (i) to add or strike the name of a party or (ii) to correct a mistake in the name of a party or a mistake in any other respect “in furtherance of justice” and “on any terms as may be proper.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); see also Marriage of Liss (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1429.) Alternatively, after notice to the other party, the Court has wide discretion to allow either party to amend pleadings “upon any terms as may be just.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1). Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure section 576 states “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.”

 

            Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, leave to amend is liberally granted. (Berman vs. Bromberg (1986) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945; Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 488-489 [this has been an established policy in California since 1901] (citing Frost v. Whitter (1901) 132 Cal. 421, 424; Thomas v. Bruza (1957) 151 Cal.App.2d 150, 155).) The Court of Appeal in Morgan v. Superior Court held “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.” (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530, citations omitted.) Moreover, “it is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [opposing party did not establish harm by the delay in moving to amend the complaint].)

 

            “The court may grant leave to amend the pleadings at any stage of the action.” (Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 6:636 (hereafter Weil & Brown).) Denial of a motion to amend is rarely justified if the motion is timely made and granting the motion will not prejudice the opposing party. (Id. at ¶ 6:639, citations omitted.) However, if the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the judge has discretion to deny leave to amend. (Id. at ¶ 6:655, citations omitted. Absent prejudice, any claimed delay alone is not grounds for denial. “If the delay in seeking the amendment has not misled or prejudiced the other side, the liberal policy of allowing amendments prevails. Indeed, it is an abuse of discretion to deny leave in such a case even if sought as late as the time of trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:653 (citing Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565).) “Prejudice exists where the amendment would result in a delay of trial, along with loss of critical evidence, added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc. . . . But the fact that the amendment involves a change in legal theory which would make admissible evidence damaging to the opposing party is not the kind of prejudice the court will consider.” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:656, citations omitted.)

 

            “Even if some prejudice is shown, the judge may still permit the amendment but impose conditions, as the Court is authorized to grant leave ‘on such terms as may be proper.’” (Weil & Brown, supra, at ¶ 6:663, citation omitted.) For example, the court may cause the party seeking the amendment to pay the costs and fees incurred in preparing for trial. (Id. at ¶ 6:664 (citing Fuller v. Vista Del Arroyo Hotel, 42 Cal.App.2d 400, 404).)

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324: Procedural Requirements

 

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must:

 

(1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleadings, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments;

(2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and

(3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.

 

            In addition, under Rule 3.1324(b), a motion to amend a pleading before trial must be accompanied by a separate declaration that specifies the following:

 

(1) the effect of the amendment;

(2) why the amendment is necessary and proper;

(3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and

(4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Plaintiff seeks to amend the SAC to add three additional defendants. The proposed defendants are individuals who purportedly serve (or served) as executives for named defendants – CSSE and Screen Media (See Motion, pp. 3-4.).  In support of the motion, Plaintiff filed two declarations.

 

First, the Declaration of Kirk D’Amico, President and Chief Executive Officer of Plaintiff, addresses the production and distribution of the Jeepers Creepers motion picture franchise, but the declaration does not provide information related to the proposed defendants:  Needle, Fannon and Damon.  Thus, the Court finds that the D’Amico declaration is not compliant with Rule 3.1324(b).

 

Second, the Declaration of Robert Paredes, counsel for Plaintiff, states all parties attended the March 16, 2024 mediation except for Screen Media and CSSE (Paredes Decl. ¶ 2). It also describes “online articles” regarding CSSE’s “volatile financial condition and penny stock status” (Paredes Decl. ¶ 3). Parades attaches as Exhibit 1 an article titled “5 Investors Betting Big on Chicken Soup for the Soul (CSSE) Stock.”  The  article notes the penny stock recently spiked more than 100% in one day without explanation, and opined it could be part of a “pump-and-dump scheme” or due to retail “meme stock” investors. Yet the declaration does not provide information related to the proposed defendants:  Needle, Fannon and Damon.  Thus, the Court finds that the Paredes’ declaration is not compliant with Rule 3.1324(b).

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            For the reasons stated, the Court denies without prejudice Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend the Second Amended Complaint as procedurally defective under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).  In particular, Plaintiff has not advanced a declaration in support of the motion that specifies the following:  (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.

 

            The Court orders Plaintiff to provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.

MOTION

Motion to Enter Judgment Pursuant to Settlement

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Alexandra Ezralow, as Trustee of the June Trust U/T/D 7-6-21

OPPOSING PARTY

none

 

MOTION

 

Plaintiff Alexandra Ezralow, as Trustee of the June Trust U/T/D 7-6-21 (“Plaintiff”) moves to enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the written settlement agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant Erin Mihok (“Defendant”) under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6.  Plaintiff’s motion is unopposed.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 provides that “[i]f parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6.) In ruling on a motion to enter judgment, the court acts as a trier of fact. The court must determine whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement. To do so, the court may receive oral testimony in addition to declarations. (Kohn v. Jaymar-Ruby, Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1530, 1533.)

 

The issue on a motion to enforce settlement agreement under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6 is whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement agreement. (See Viejo v. Bancorp. (1989) 217 Cal.App.3d 200, 209, fn. 4 [“a court's power to make factual determinations under section 664.6 is generally limited to whether the parties entered into a valid and binding settlement agreement”].)  In other words, the only issue before the court is whether an agreement exists; not whether the agreement has been breached.

 

Here, following a February 6, 2024 mediation, the parties entered into a settlement agreement, providing that Defendant shall pay Plaintiff $37,000 by wire transfer no later than March 8, 2024, and in the event that amount is not paid on or before March 8, 2024, Defendant agrees that judgment may be entered against her in the amount of $50,000, less any payments received by Plaintiff.  (Fidler Decl. ¶¶ 7-9 and Ex. 1.)  Subsequently, Defendant did not pay Plaintiff any amount in accordance with the settlement agreement.  (Id. at ¶¶ 10-13.)  As such, Plaintiff requests the Court enter judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $50,000.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s evidence sufficient to demonstrate that a valid and binding settlement agreement exists between the parties, requiring Defendant to wire Plaintiff $37,000 on or before March 8, 2024, or else a judgment may be entered against Defendant in the amount of $50,000, less any payments received by Plaintiff, and that Plaintiff has not since received any payments from Defendant.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

            Therefore, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion and orders that judgment shall be entered in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in accordance with the terms of the settlement agreement.  Plaintiff shall file and serve a proposed Judgment in conformance with the Court’s ruling on or before June 24, 2024, along with a courtesy copy of the proposed Judgment to be delivered to Department 207.

 

            Further, on the Court’s own motion, the Court will vacate the Order to Show Cause Re: Dismissal (Settlement) set on July 24, 2024, and instead, the Court will set an Order to Show Cause re Entry of Judgment on July 24, 2024 at 8:30 A.M. in Department 207.  No appearance will be necessary on July 24, 2024 if a Judgment has been entered beforehand. 

 

            Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and shall file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.

This case is a landlord/tenant dispute regarding the conditions of real property Plaintiffs and Cross-Defendants Jennifer Flynn Adams and Christoper Adams (together, “Plaintiffs”) leased, with the intent to purchase. 

 

Plaintiffs demur to the Cross-Complaint filed by Defendant and Cross-Complainant E&TB Investments LLC (“Defendant”) on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, is uncertain, and lacks an attorney signature, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivisions (b), (e), (f), and (g).  Plaintiffs also move to strike various portions of the Cross-Complaint on the same grounds.  Defendant opposes both motions and Plaintiffs reply. 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Defendant requests judicial notice of the following:

 

1. The Second Amended Complaint of Cross Defendants filed in the instant action as Plaintiffs in their case in-chief on or about March 8, 2024, attached hereto as Exhibit “1”;

 

2. A Certified Copy from the Delaware Secretary of State of Cross Complainant E&TB Investments LLC’s Certificate of Formation, attached hereto as Exhibit “2”;

 

3. Application to Register a Foreign Limited Liability Company filed by Cross Complainant E&TB Investments LLC with the California Secretary of State on May 7, 2019, attached hereto as Exhibit “3”;

 

4. Statement of Information filed by Cross Complainant E&TB Investments LLC with the California Secretary of State on August 24, 2023, attached hereto as Exhibit “4”;

 

5. Search Results for all entities having the name “Bessemer” conducted on the California Secretary of State’s website on May 28, 2024, attached hereto as Exhibit “5”; and

 

6. Verified Cross Complaint of E&TB Investments LLC filed in the instant action on April 8, 2024, attached hereto as Exhibit “6”.

 

With respect to Exhibits 1 and 6, judicial notice may be taken of records of any court in this state.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d)(1).)  Because these documents are part of the Court’s record for this case, the Court may take judicial notice of them.  (Ibid.)   However, “while courts are free to take judicial notice of the existence of each document in a court file, including the truth of results reached, they may not take judicial notice of the truth of hearsay statements in decisions and court files.  Courts may not take judicial notice of allegations in affidavits, declarations and probation reports in court records because such matters are reasonably subject to dispute and therefore require formal proof.”  (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 [cleaned up].)  Accordingly, the Court takes judicial notice of the existence of the Second Amended Complaint and Verified Cross-Complaint filed in this matter as court records, but not the truth of the allegations contained therein.

 

With respect to Exhibits 2-4, in general, official notices, statements, and certificates made by state entities such as the Secretary of State or the Franchise Tax Board are properly the subject of judicial notice as documents reflecting official acts of the state’s executive department, pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c).  (See e.g., Friends of Shingle Springs Interchange, Inc. v. County of El Dorado (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1470, 1483–1484.)  Therefore, the Court takes judicial notice of Exhibit 2.

 

However, “materials prepared by private parties and merely on file with the state agencies” may not be properly judicially noticed as an official act of a legislative, executive, or judicial department of the United States or any state of the United States.  (People v. Thacker (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 594, 598.)  Moreover, as discussed above, courts may not take judicial notice of hearsay statements contained in official records.  Therefore, while the Court could take judicial notice of the fact that Exhibits 3 and 4 are on file with the relevant state agencies, and any legal implications thereof, the Court cannot take judicial notice of the truth of the matters asserted therein.  Because Defendant offers these exhibits to prove its standing in this case, the Court declines to take judicial notice of Exhibits 3-4.

 

Regarding Exhibit 5, Defendant has provided no legal basis for the Court to take judicial notice of the results of a private search conducted on the California Secretary of State’s website on May 28, 2024.  Therefore, the Court declines to take judicial notice of Exhibit 5.

ANALYSIS

 

1.     DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In testing the sufficiency of a cause of action, a court accepts “[a]s true all material facts properly pled and matters which may be judicially noticed but disregard contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  [A court also gives] the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (290 Division (EAT), LLC v. City & County of San Francisco (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 439, 450 [cleaned up]; Hacker v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 270, 280 [“in considering the merits of a demurrer, however, “the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

 

Further, in ruling on a demurrer, a court must “liberally construe” the allegations of the complaint “with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  

 

In summary, “[d]etermining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the rule is that if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated, or may be intermingled with a statement of other facts irrelevant to the cause of action shown, or although the plaintiff may demand relief to which he is not entitled under the facts alleged.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

A.    UNCERTAINTY

 

A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the pleading is so bad that the responding party cannot reasonably respond - i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against him or her.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  Where a demurrer is made upon the ground of uncertainty, the demurrer must specify exactly how or why the pleading is uncertain.  (See, e.g., Fenton v. Groveland Comm. Services Dist. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 797, 809.) 

 

Plaintiffs contend the Cross-Complaint is uncertain because it is not properly verified or signed by an attorney. 

 

First, the Court notes the Cross-Complaint is electronically signed by Derrick F. Coleman, Esq.

 

Second, attached to the Cross-Complaint is the following electronically-signed verification:

 

I, Steven Dwyer, state:

I am an authorized representative of Bessemer Trust of California, N.A., in its capacity as co-trustee of the Madison Trust, in its capacity as co-manager of E&TB Investments LLC and in said capacity am authorized to make this verification on behalf of E&TB Investments LLC. I have read the foregoing Verified Cross-Complaint of E&TB Investments LLC and know its contents. I am informed and believe and on that ground state that the matters are true and accurate to the best of my own knowledge except as to those matters which are stated on information and belief, and as to those matters I believe them to be true.

I declare under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.

Executed on April 8, 2024.

 

Plaintiffs contend that Steven Dwyer must either prove that he is an officer of E&TB or else explain in the affidavit why he is signing instead of an officer.  The Court disagrees. On its face, the verification provides the requisite foundation for Dwyer’s verification.  Moreover, as Defendant points out, a verification is not required.  Should discovery reveal that the Cross-Complaint is not properly verified, Plaintiffs may move to strike the Cross-Complaint.

 

In any event, Plaintiffs do not demonstrate that any portions of the Cross-Complaint are so bad that Plaintiffs cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are directed against them.  The Court thus declines to sustain Plaintiffs’ demurrer on the basis of uncertainty. 

 

B.    STANDING

 

With respect to standing, the Cross-Complaint provides as follows:

 

7. Cross-Complainant is the owner of certain real property located in Topanga Canyon, including a single-family home and detached garage (“Property”).

 

8. In June 2018, Cross-Defendants executed a written lease with Cross-Complainant’s predecessor-in-interest pursuant to which Cross-Defendants leased the Property for a one-year term commencing on June 24, 2018, and ending on June 24, 2019 (“Lease”). A true and correct copy of the Lease, the terms of which are incorporated herein by reference, is attached hereto as Exhibit 1.

 

(Cross-Complaint at ¶¶ 7-8.)

 

            Plaintiffs contend this allegation provides insufficient detail to determine whether Defendant actually has standing to sue.  According to Plaintiffs, “Questions abound. Who is the “predecessor-in-interest” that signed the lease, or who is claimed to have had a legitimate right to collect rent? Was it a valid lease? When did Cross-Defendants take possession? Why does the Cross-Complainant now have the right to sue under a lease entered into by another party?”

 

            Plaintiffs seek to impose a heightened pleading requirement that the law does not require. To the contrary, the Cross-Complaint adequately alleges that Defendant has standing to sue on the written lease agreement because Defendant is allegedly the successor-in-interest to that agreement.  Moreover, in response to Plaintiffs’ questions about the lease, a copy of the alleged lease at issue is attached to the Cross-Complaint, signed by “Tara Broida of the Madison family Trust,” which is arguably Defendant’s predecessor-in-interest, with a lease term to commence on June 24, 2018.  Plaintiffs’ remaining questions are factual issues to be determined at later stages of the litigation.  For pleading purposes, Defendant has adequately alleged standing, and Plaintiffs have not established otherwise.

 

C.    FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

                                                         i.          First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.”  (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) 

 

The Cross-Complaint alleges:

 

7. Cross-Complainant is the owner of certain real property located in Topanga Canyon, including a single-family home and detached garage (“Property”).

 

8. In June 2018, Cross-Defendants executed a written lease with Cross-Complainant’s predecessor-in-interest pursuant to which Cross-Defendants leased the Property for a one-year term commencing on June 24, 2018, and ending on June 24, 2019 (“Lease”). A true and correct copy of the Lease, the terms of which are incorporated herein by reference, is attached hereto as Exhibit 1.

 

9. Upon the expiration of the one-year term of the Lease on June 24, 2019, Cross-Defendants continued to occupy the Property, and by its terms, the Lease converted to a month-to-month lease.

 

10. In consideration for the use of the Property, Cross-Defendants agreed, among other things, to pay rent on the first calendar day of each month in the amount of $5,500 per month.

 

11. After the commencement of the Lease, Cross-Defendants were habitually late on their rent payments, and paid rent days, weeks, and even months late. Since August 2022, Cross-Defendants have failed and refused to pay any rent at all.

 

12. Tenant and Cross-Defendant Flynn Adams signed the Lease in June 2018.

 

13. Flynn Adams and Chris Adams did not pay the $5,500 in rent for September 2022.

 

14. Flynn Adams and Chris Adams did not pay the $5,500 in rent for October 2022.

 

15. Flynn Adams and Chris Adams did not pay the $5,500 in rent for November 2022.

 

[…]

 

30. Flynn Adams and Chris Adams did not pay the $5,500 in rent for February 2024.

 

31. Flynn Adams and Chris Adams did not pay the $5,500 in rent for March 2024.

 

32. Flynn Adams and Chris Adams did not pay the $5,500 in rent for April 2024.

 

33. The Lease does not contain a purchase option clause.

 

34. The Lease does not allow or provide for the Tenant to have any legal right to purchase the Property.

 

35. The Tenant never submitted any signed written offer to purchase the Property.

 

36. The Lease contains a binding integration clause.

 

37. The Lease provides that any modification or change must be in a writing signed by both landlord and tenant.

 

38. Despite their failing and refusing to pay any rent since August 2022, Cross-Defendants have continued to reside at and enjoy the Subject Property since that time, except for brief periods of time when Cross-Defendants may have temporarily resided elsewhere.

 

39. As of the filing of this Cross-Complaint, the amount of unpaid rent due and owing by Cross-Defendants for the period since August 2022 alone is no less than $110,000, not including additional rent amounts that Cross-Defendants failed to pay for prior months.

 

[…]

 

42. As alleged above, Cross-Complainant and Cross-Defendants are parties to a written Lease pursuant to which Cross-Defendants agreed to, among other things, pay rent in the amount of $5,500 per month.

 

43. As alleged above, Cross-Defendants have materially breached their obligations under the Lease.

 

44. Cross-Complainant has substantially performed its obligations under the Lease. In the alternative, Cross-Complainant’s performance has been excused, including without limitation, as a result of Cross-Defendants’ material breaches of the Lease.

 

45. As a result of Cross-Defendants’ breaches of the Lease, Cross-Complainant has sustained damages in the amount of at least $110,000, plus additional damages to be proven at trial, as well as pre-judgment interest, attorneys’ fees, and other amounts.

 

(Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 7-39; 42-45.)

 

            Thus, the Cross-Complaint adequately alleges a breach of contract.

 

            Plaintiffs essentially reiterate their arguments above, that Defendant has not conclusively established that Plaintiffs owe it rent under the agreement.  As discussed above, Defendant’s allegations are sufficient at the pleadings stage.

 

                                                       ii.           Second Cause of Action – Declaratory Relief

 

Plaintiffs contend the Cross-Complaint’s request for declaratory relief is unripe because Defendant has failed to demonstrate Plaintiffs are obligated to pay Defendant rent.  As discussed above, the Court rejects this argument.  Defendant has adequately alleged that Plaintiffs owe Defendant rent, as the current owner of the property and the successor-in-interest to the lease agreement Plaintiffs signed in June 2018.

 

D.    CAUSE OF ACTION FOUNDED UPON A CONTRACT

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (g) provides a party may demur on the grounds that “In an action founded upon a contract, it cannot be ascertained from the pleading whether the contract is written, oral, or is implied by conduct.

 

Here, the Cross-Complaint alleges, “8. In June 2018, Cross-Defendants executed a written lease with Cross-Complainant’s predecessor-in-interest […]”  (Cross-Complaint at ¶ 8.)  Further, a copy of the written lease is attached to the Cross-Complaint.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s demurrer pursuant to Section 430.10, subdivision (g) is overruled.

 

2.     MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.) 

 

Here, Plaintiffs reiterate their arguments made in support of the demurrer and move to strike the entire Cross-Complaint, or in the alternative, various substantive passages therefrom, on the grounds that (1) it was not signed by an attorney; (2) the verification is defective; and (3) Defendant has not demonstrated standing.  As discussed above, the Cross-Complaint was electronically signed by Defendant’s counsel; the verification is not defective on its face; and (3) Defendant has adequately alleged standing and Plaintiffs have not demonstrated otherwise.

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs’ motion to strike is denied.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Plaintiffs’ Demurrer and denies Plaintiffs’ Motion to Strike the Cross-Complaint in their entirety.

 

Further, the Court orders Plaintiffs to file an Answer to the Cross-complaint on or before June 28, 2024.

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay

MOVING PARTIES

Plaintiffs Stephen R. Hofer, Stephen R. Hofer Law Corporation d/b/a Aerlex Law Group, Aerlex Tax Services, LLC, and Aerlex Tax Servies, LLP

OPPOSING PARTIES

Defendants Vicky Boladian and Boladian Aviation Law Group, APC

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from a dispute between former business partners.  On October 15, 2023, Plaintiffs Stephen R. Hofer (“Hofer”); Stephen R. Hofer Law Corporation d/b/a Aerlex Law Group (“ALG”); Aerlex Tax Services, LLC (“ATS LLC”); and Aerlex Tax Services, LLP (“ATS LLP”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) brought suit against Defendants Vicky Boladian (“Boladian”) and Boladian Aviation Law Group, APC (“BALG”) (together, “Defendants”) alleging thirteen causes of action: (1) breach of contract – settlement agreement; (2) breach of contract – operating agreement and buy-sell agreement; (3) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (4) breach of fiduciary duty; (5) intentional misrepresentation; (6) fraud in the inducement; (7) wrongful dissociation; (8) conversion; (9) intentional interference with contractual relations; (10) computer data access and fraud act; (11) receiving stolen property; (12) unlawful business practices; and (13) declaratory relief.

 

The Complaint alleges Hofer founded ALG in 2005 and hired Boladian in 2008.  (Compl. ¶ 8.)  Subsequently, Hofer formed ATS LLC, in which Hofer owned 55% and Boladian owned 45%.  (Id. at ¶ 9.)  Around 2017-2018, a dispute arose between Hofer and Boladian, resulting in a lawsuit and arbitration proceedings.  (Id. at ¶ 11.)  In September 2020, Hofer and Boladian settled their dispute and entered into three separate agreements.  (Id. at ¶ 12.)

 

In March 2023, concerned about California law prohibiting LLCs from rendering “professional services,” Boladian allegedly approached Hofer about converting ATS LLC into an LLP, which Hofer did, with the understanding that the change in corporate form would not impact the parties’ ownership interests or the terms of the ATS LLC Operating Agreement or Buy-Sell Agreement.  (Compl. ¶ 17.)  In August 2023, Boladian quietly formed her own law firm, BALG, and “surreptitiously” transferred information, property, and staff from ATS (LLP) to her new firm.  (Id. at ¶ 18.)  In September 2023, Boladian told Hofer she was withdrawing from LLP, dissolving the partnership by operation of law.  (Id. at ¶ 20.)

 

Shortly after filing suit, on November 1, 2023, Plaintiffs moved ex parte for a temporary restraining order and an order to show cause why a preliminary injunction should not issue essentially enjoining Boladian from continuing to provide tax and legal services to clients of the  now-defunct entity in which Boladian and Hofer were both partners.  On November 2, the Court denied the ex parte application, finding that Plaintiffs had not demonstrated an inadequate remedy at law.  (Minute Order, Nov. 2, 2023.)

 

On November 16, 2023, Plaintiffs moved for a preliminary injunction, seeking essentially the same relief.  On December 12, 2023, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ motion for a preliminary injunction finding, among other things, that the record did not establish the cited non-compete clause was applicable or enforceable or that either the entities in question would suffer irreparable harm and that the status quo would be to allow Boladian to continue to service the clients as she had previously done.  (Minute Order, Dec.12, 2023.)

 

On April 23, 2024, Defendant and Cross-Complainant Vicky Boladian brought a cross-complaint against Plaintiffs for (1) express contractual indemnity; (2) implied contractual indemnity; (3) equitable indemnity; (4) breach of contract; and (5) breach of fiduciary duty.

 

            Plaintiffs now move to compel Defendants to arbitration and to stay the action, pending the outcome of those proceedings, pursuant to the arbitration provisions in the September 2020 agreements.  Defendants oppose the motion and Plaintiffs reply.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            In connection with the Reply, Plaintiffs have requested judicial notice of the following documents filed in connection with the parties’ prior lawsuit:

 

Exhibit A: Motion to Compel Arbitration filed by Hofer and ALG

 

Exhibit B: Opposition to Motion to Compel Arbitration filed by Boladian and ATS

 

Exhibit C: Reply in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration filed by Hofer and ALG

 

Exhibit D: Order Granting Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

The Court does not generally consider reply evidence, as such deprives the opposing party of a fair opportunity to respond.  (San Diego Watercrafts, Inc. v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 308, 316 [“due process requires a party be fully advised of the issues to be addressed and be given adequate notice of what facts it must rebut in order to prevail”]; see also Wall Street Network Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171.) 

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs’ request for judicial notice is denied.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.     MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION – LEGAL STANDARDS

 

            “[T]he advantages of arbitration include a presumptively less costly, more expeditious manner of resolving disputes.  It follows a party to a valid arbitration agreement has a contractual right to have its dispute with another party to the contract resolved quickly and inexpensively.”  (Henry v. Alcove Investment, Inc. (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 94, 99–100 [cleaned up].)  Thus, “on petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; see also

EFund Capital Partners v. Pless (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 1311, 1320 [the language in section 1281.2 compelling arbitration is mandatory].) The right to compel arbitration exists unless the court finds that the right has been waived by a party’s conduct, other grounds exist for revocation of the agreement, or where a pending court action arising out of the same transaction creates the possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2, subds. (a)-(c).)   

 

            “On a petition to compel arbitration, the trial court must first determine whether an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists.  Because the existence of the agreement is a statutory prerequisite to granting the petition, the petitioner bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence.  The party seeking arbitration can meet its initial burden by attaching to the petition a copy of the arbitration agreement purporting to bear the respondent's signature.”  (Bannister v. Marinidence Opco, LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 541, 543-544 [cleaned up].)  The party seeking to compel arbitration must also “plead and prove a prior demand for arbitration and a refusal to arbitrate under the agreement.”  (Mansouri v. Superior Court (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 633, 640-641.) 

 

            And while the moving party on a motion to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, [a] party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842 [cleaned up]; see also Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236 [“The party seeking arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement, and the party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving any defense, such as unconscionability”].) 

 

2.     ENFORCEABLE ARBITRATION AGREEMENTS

 

Plaintiffs move to compel Defendants to arbitration on the basis of three separate but interrelated agreements reached in September 2020 in connection with a settlement of legal disputes Boladian asserted against Hofer in 2017: (1) a Settlement Agreement; (2) the ATS LLC Amended and Restated Operating Agreement (“Operating Agreement”); and (3) the ATS LLC Amended and Restated Buy-Sell Agreement (“Buy-Sell.”) 

 

The parties to the Settlement Agreement are Boladian on the one hand and Hofer and ALG on the other, and the Settlement Agreement concerns the parties’ conduct with respect to ATS LLC.  (Ex. C.)  Paragraph 28 of the Settlement Agreement provides:

 

28. In the event of a dispute among the Parties regarding the enforcement or interpretation of this Agreement or any right of any Party hereto, the parties shall first discuss their respective positions and attempt to resolve their disputes at an in-person meeting. Either Party may, but is not required to, be accompanied at such meeting by counsel of their choosing. In the event an in-person meeting does not result in a resolution of the dispute, the Parties shall promptly seek resolution of their dispute through mediation by a mutually acceptable mediator who shall be a retired Los Angeles Superior Court Judge. In the event that mediation is unsuccessful, the Parties shall refer their dispute to arbitration with ADR Services in Los Angeles County, California. Such dispute shall be heard by a mutually acceptable arbitrator, or in the event the Parties are unable to agree, by an arbitrator randomly assigned by ADR Services. The prevailing party, as determined by the arbitrator, shall be entitled to recover his/her reasonable attorney’s fees and costs. In no event shall litigation be commenced by either Party without first proceeding through each step of the dispute resolution process outlined herein, which shall be considered conditions precedent to the ability to file suit in any court of this State.

 

(Ex. C at ¶ 28.)

 

            The ATS LLC Operating Agreement is signed by both Hofer and Boladian, both individually and on behalf of ATS LLC.  Section 5.1 of the Operating Agreement provides:

 

In the event of a dispute among the Members/Managers regarding the interpretation of this Agreement or any right of any Party hereto, or if the Members/Managers are unable to reach an agreement regarding a proposed action, the Members shall first discuss their respective positions and attempt to resolve their disputes at an in-person meeting of the Members/Managers. Any Member/Manager may, but is not required to, be accompanied at such meeting by counsel of his or her choosing. In the event an in-person meeting does not result in a resolution of the dispute, the Members/Managers shall promptly seek resolution of their dispute through mediation by a mutually acceptable mediator who shall be a retired Los Angeles Superior Court Judge. In the event that mediation is unsuccessful, the Members/Managers shall refer their dispute to arbitration with ADR Services in Los Angeles County, California. Such dispute shall be heard by a mutually acceptable arbitrator, or in the event the Members/Managers are unable to agree, by an arbitrator randomly assigned by ADR Services. The prevailing party, as determined by the arbitrator, shall be entitled to recover his/her reasonable attorney’s fees and costs in addition to any damages assessed by the arbitrator. In no event shall litigation be commenced by either Member/Manager without first proceeding through each step of the dispute resolution process outlined herein, which shall be considered conditions precedent to the ability to file suit in any court of this State. In the event that legal proceedings are initiated, this Agreement shall be construed in accordance with and be governed by the substantive laws of the State of California without regard to the application of conflicts of laws principles, and the dispute shall be determined in Los Angeles County, California in an arbitrational forum or civil court of competent jurisdiction therein.

 

(Ex. D at p. 15.)

 

            The ATS LLC Buy-Sell Agreement is between Hofer and Boladian “as the sole members of ATS [LLC]” and restricts Hofer and Boladian’s ability to transfer their membership interests in ATS LLC and contains a covenant not to compete.  (Ex. E at pp. 1, 17-19.) 

 

            Thus, both parties agree that the 2020 agreements between Hofer and Boladian exist and contain arbitration provisions.  However, Boladian contends (1) neither BALG nor ATS LLP are parties to the 2020 Agreements, as such the arbitration provisions do not bind those parties; (2) the arbitration provisions are narrow and do not cover the disputes at issue; (3) Plaintiffs waived the right to arbitration by bringing an untimely demand after litigating in court for several months; and (4) Plaintiffs’ breaches of the arbitration agreements estops them from enforcing the arbitration provisions.

 

3.     WAIVER

 

            “Waiver of the right to arbitrate does not require a voluntary relinquishment of a known right.  For example, a party may waive the right by an untimely demand even without any intent to forgo the procedure.  In this circumstance, waiver is similar to a forfeiture arising from the nonperformance of a required act.”  (Hoover v. American Income Life Ins. Co. (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 1193, 1203 (hereafter Hoover) [cleaned up].)  “Although participation in litigation of an arbitrable claim does not itself waive a party's right later to seek arbitration, at some point, litigation of the issues in dispute justifies a finding of waiver.”  (Id. at p. 1204.)  On the issue of what constitutes a “reasonable time” to demand arbitration, the Hoover court noted:

 

There is no fixed stage in a lawsuit beyond which further litigation waives the right to arbitrate. Rather, the court views the litigation as a whole in determining whether the parties' conduct is inconsistent with a desire to arbitrate.  A defendant's removal of a case filed in state court to federal court does not by itself constitute an implicit waiver of the right to compel arbitration.  But, a defendant's removal of a case to federal court, coupled with participation in several months of litigation, waives the right to arbitrate because electing to proceed in federal court on an arbitrable dispute is presumptive waiver of the right to arbitrate.

 

(Ibid. [cleaned up].)  On the issue of prejudice, the Hoover court stated:

 

The presence or absence of prejudice from the litigation is a determinative issue.  Because of the strong policy favoring arbitration, prejudice typically is found only where the petitioning party has unreasonably delayed seeking arbitration or substantially impaired an opponent's ability to use the benefits and efficiencies of arbitration.  Prejudice sufficient for waiver will be found where instead of seeking to compel arbitration, a party proceeds with extensive discovery that is unavailable in arbitration proceedings.

 

(Id. at p. 1205 [cleaned up].)  Similarly, in finding there was no waiver to arbitrate, the California Supreme Court noted, in part:

 

Although we have found no California or United States Supreme Court decisions on point, other courts that have addressed this issue hold that a petitioning party does not waive its arbitration rights merely by seeking to change judicial venue of an action prior to requesting arbitration. In so holding, those courts reason that a party is not required to litigate the issue of arbitration in an improper or inconvenient venue, and that a party's position on venue does not necessarily reflect a position on arbitrability. We agree with that reasoning, and find it consistent with California and federal case law holding that a waiver determination requires the consideration of all circumstances, including the absence or presence of prejudice.

 

(St. Agnes Medical Center v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1187, 1205 (hereafter St. Agnes) [cleaned up].) 

 

            In Hoover, the appellate court held that the defendant waived its right to arbitration by “actively litigating this action for more than a year and causing prejudice to Hoover.”  (Hoover, supra, 206 Cal.App.4th at p. 1203.)  In particular, the Hoover court determined that the defendant waived its right because it “[d]id not introduce the question of arbitration for almost a full year and [the defendant] conducted litigation in a style inconsistent with the right to arbitrate.  The two failed efforts to remove the case to federal court and [the defendant’s] recalcitrant responses to discovery suggest its policy has been one of delay rather than seeking a more prompt and expeditious resolution through arbitration.  During that time, [the defendant] availed itself of discovery mechanisms like depositions not available in arbitration.  [The defendant] also solicited putative class members, in an effort to reduce the size of the class. Hoover was certainly strongly affected and prejudiced by [the defendant’s] delay, causing significant legal expenses.”

(Id. at p. 1205 [cleaned up]; but see St. Agnes, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1201 [“a waiver generally does not occur where the arbitrable issues have not been litigated to judgment].) 

 

            Moreover, more leeway is generally afforded to a defendant who takes certain preliminary defensive actions against a lawsuit before moving to compel arbitration, than a plaintiff, whose filing of the suit is, in itself, inconsistent with asserting the right to compel arbitration.  (Christensen v. Dewor Developments (1983) 33 Cal.3d 778, 782.)  However, the mere filing of action does not result in waiver by the plaintiff.  (Id. at p. 783.)

 

Courts consider the following factors in determining whether a party has waived the right to arbitration:  “(1) whether the party's actions are inconsistent with the right to arbitrate; (2) whether the litigation machinery has been substantially invoked and the parties were well into preparation of a lawsuit before the party notified the opposing party of an intent to arbitrate; (3) whether a party either requested arbitration enforcement close to the trial date or delayed for a long period before seeking a stay; (4) whether a defendant seeking arbitration filed a counterclaim without asking for a stay of the proceedings; (5) whether important intervening steps [e.g., taking advantage of judicial discovery procedures not available in arbitration] had taken place; and (6) whether the delay affected, misled, or prejudiced the opposing party.”  (St. Agnes, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1196 [cleaned up].)    

 

Here, Defendants claims that Plaintiffs waived the right to compel arbitration by filing suit in this court, and litigating for over five months, including but not limited to the following:

 

    • ex parte application for a temporary restraining order
    • motion for preliminary injunction
    • opposing Defendants’ demurrer to the complaint
    • propounding written discovery requests and refusing to extend the deadline for Defendants to respond to such discovery requests
    • opposing Defendants’ ex parte application to extend the deadline to respond to such discovery requests
    • attending an Informal Discovery Conference related to such discovery requests
    • setting various depositions for Boladian and BALG employees.

 

(Sohal Decl. ¶¶ 7, 11-18; see also Court’s Docket.)  Further, Defendants claim that they have expended approximately 210 hours from November 1, 2023 to March 30, 2024 on this action.  (Id. at ¶ 19.)  Based upon the matters that have transpired to date, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have engaged in tactics that are fundamentally incompatible with the expediency, efficiency, and cost-effectiveness associated with utilizing arbitration.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Hofer waived the right to arbitrate by filing suit in this court and substantially and vigorously litigating it for over seven months as set forth above.  As such, Defendants would be prejudiced if Hofer were able to compel arbitration at this juncture.[1]

 

CONCLUSION

 

            Therefore, having found Plaintiffs, in particular Hofer, have waived the right to compel arbitration by filing suit in this court and actively litigating, including multiple motions and engaging in substantial discovery for over seven months, the Court denies Plaintiffs’ motion to compel arbitration.
