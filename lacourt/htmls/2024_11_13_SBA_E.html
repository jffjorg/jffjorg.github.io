DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV27968    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

MORRIS J. RIVERS, an Individual,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

20STCV27968

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

 

INGLEWOOD CROSSROADS HOMEOWNERS’ ASSOCIATION; CROSSROADS PROPERTIES; HOA PREMIER MANAGEMENT, LLC; and DOES 1-50, inclusive,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             November 13, 2024

 

Moving Parties:          Defendant Inglewood Crossroads Homeowners Association

 

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Morris J Rivers

 

HEARING: Motion to Compel Further Responses

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.

 

RULING

             The Court grants the Motion to Compel Further Responses as to the Request for Production, Set One, the Form Interrogatories, Set One, and the Special Interrogatories, Set One

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On July 24, 2020, Plaintiff Morris J Rivers filed the Complaint against Inglewood Crossroads Homeowners Association, Crossroads Properties, and HOA Premier Management, LLC, alleging causes of action for negligence and premises liability.

 

            On October 11, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with the Request for Production, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

            On December 13, 2022, Plaintiff requested an extension due to an illness, which both parties agreed to.

           

On January 16, 2023, Plaintiff provided objections to all three discovery requests.

 

            On February 14, 2023, Defendant provided a meet and confer correspondence relating to Plaintiff’s responses. Defendant requested Plaintiff’s agreed responses on or before February 28, 2023.

 

            Plaintiff has failed to provide further responses to the Request for Production, Set One, Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

            On October 17, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motions to Compel Further Discovery to the Request for Production, Set One, and Form Interrogatories, Set One.

 

            Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

           

A party must respond to interrogatories and requests for production of documents within 30 days after service.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).)  If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).)  The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories or the production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020, subd. (a), 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response … unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290 (d). )

 

            The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant requests that the Court compel Plaintiff to provide verified responses to the Request for Production, Set One, compel Plaintiff to provide verified further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and compel Plaintiff to provide verified further responses to the Special Interrogatories, Set One,

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion to compel must be “accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450.)

 

Here, Defendant has made multiple attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff, including a letter sent on August 27, 2024. (Hall Decl. ¶ 6-10.) Therefore, the Court finds that Defendant satisfied the meet and confer requirement.

 

Requests for Production

 

Defendant requests that the Court compel Plaintiff to provide verified responses to the Request for Production, Set One.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff's responses to the Request for Production of Documents, Set One, are unverified and, therefore, effectively constitute no response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.250 subd. (a); see Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632,636.) Plaintiff’s responses to requests Nos. 1 through 24 include identical boilerplate objections and a generic statement that the documents are equally available to Defendant, with no verification.

 

Defendant has made multiple attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff, who requested extensions due to illness but has yet to provide substantive responses. Defendant argues that compelling, verified responses is essential to adequately prepare a defense and fulfill the discovery obligations required for a fair trial.

 

Plaintiff has failed to file an opposition.

 

Defendant has shown that Plaintiff has not complied with Code Civ. Proc., Section 2031.250 subdivision (a) by failing to provide verified responses to the Request for Production Set One. Furthermore, in response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co., supra, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) Plaintiff has failed to oppose this motion and, thus, has not justified his objections.

 

Therefore, the Court waives Plaintiff’s objections and grants the Motion to Compel further verified responses to Defendant’s Request for Production, Set One.

 

Form Interrogatories

 

Defendant requests the Court compel Plaintiff to provide verified further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories are incomplete, evasive, and nonresponsive.  Defendant contends Plaintiff’s responses fall short, as they consist only of boilerplate objections without substantive answers. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (a).)

 

Defendant further notes that Plaintiff agreed to provide substantive responses before the October 15, 2024, mediation, yet has not done so. Consequently, Defendant requests the Court compel Plaintiff to provide verified, complete responses without objections to allow Defendant to prepare a proper defense.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co., supra, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) Plaintiff has not filed any opposition or justified any objections. Thus, Defendant has shown that Plaintiff’s responses are evasive and incomplete. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (a).)

 

Therefore, the Court waives Plaintiff’s objections and grants Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery as to Form Interrogatories, Set One.

 

Special Interrogatories

 

Defendant requests the Court compel Plaintiff to provide verified further responses to Defendant’s Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s responses to Special Interrogatories are incomplete, evasive, and nonresponsive. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (a).). Defendant contends that Plaintiff's responses are inadequate, consisting solely of boilerplate objections without substantive answers.

 

For example, in Special Interrogatory No. 4, which asks Plaintiff to describe how the incident occurred, Plaintiff responded with objections instead of an answer, labeling the request as irrelevant, overly broad, and burdensome. This same objection was repeated across all 45 interrogatories.

 

Defendant argues that these objections hinder Defendant’s ability to understand Plaintiff’s claims and prepare for trial. Since Plaintiff had agreed to provide responses before an October 15, 2024, mediation but failed to do so, Defendant requests that the Court compel Plaintiff to provide complete, verified responses without objections.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co., supra, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) Plaintiff has not filed any opposition or justified any objections. Thus, Defendant has shown that Plaintiff’s responses are evasive and incomplete. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300 subd. (a).)

 

Therefore, the Court waives Plaintiff’s objections and grants Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Discovery as to Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

Sanctions

 

Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery is a sanctionable misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d).) If a motion to compel a party to respond to discovery is successful, the court must impose a monetary sanction against that party unless the sanction would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc § 2030.290 subd. (c), 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The monetary sanction includes the moving party’s reasonable expenses, such as attorney’s fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

 

Defendant requests the Court impose sanctions totaling $985 regarding the Request for Production, Set One, $985 regarding the Form Interrogatories, Set One, and $985 regarding the Special Interrogatories, Set One. For the Request for Production, Defendant’s Counsel states they spent 2.5 hours on the motion, 2 hours on analyzing the opposition and writing the reply, and .5 hours attending the hearing at $185 an hour plus a $60 filing fee. (Hall Decl. ¶ 12.) The requested sanctions for the Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Special Interrogatories, Set One are calculated in exactly the same manner as the Request for Production, Set One. The Court strikes the 2 hours for the opposition and replies for all motions given the lack of opposition. Thus, the Court finds that Defendant is entitled to sanctions equaling 7.5 hours for writing the motions and .5 hours for attending the hearing at $185 an hour plus $180 in filing fees, totaling $1,660.

 

Therefore, the Court imposes sanctions on Plaintiff in the amount of $1,660.

PRIME HEALTHCARE CENTINELA, LLC., dba CENTINELA HOSPITAL MEDICAL CENTER; PRIME HEALTHCARE SERVICES, LLC; & VERITAS HEALTH SERVICES, INC; and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive,

Hearing Date:             November 13, 2024   

 

Moving Parties:          Defendants Prime Healthcare Centinela, LLC., dba Centinela Hospital

Medical Center; Prime Healthcare Services, LLC; and Veritas Health Services, Inc.

 

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Fadriko Dillon

 

HEARING: DEMURRER TO FIFTH AMENDED COMPLAINT

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

RULING

            The Court sustains the Demurer without leave to amend.

 

BACKGROUND

            On May 26, 2023, Plaintiff Fadriko Dillon filed the Complaint against Defendants Centinela Hospital Medical Center, Prime Healthcare Services, LLC, and Veritas Health Services, Inc.

 

            On June 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint.

 

            On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed the Second Amended Complaint.

 

            On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Third Amended Complaint.

 

            On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Fourth Amended Complaint.

 

            On September 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Fifth Amended Complaint against Defendants, alleging causes of action for premises liability and general negligence arising from an alleged gunshot wound received while outside Defendant’s hospital.

 

            On October 11, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Demurrer to the Fifth Amended Complaint.

 

            On October 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Declaration of Lisa Saperstein in lieu of an opposition.

 

            On November 5, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply.

           

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012)

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

            The instant case involves an incident outside Centinela Hospital Medical Center on May 27, 2021, where Plaintiff Fadriko Dillon was shot and injured by an unidentified third party. Plaintiff claims that the security guards at the hospital should have either thoroughly checked all entrants into the emergency department or taken unspecified steps to prevent the attack.

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) 

 

Defendants emailed Plaintiff regarding the Demurrer on September 13, and Plaintiff responded on September 30. The Court finds this correspondence satisfies the meet and confer requirement.

 

            Plaintiffs Declaration

 

            Plaintiff has filed the Declaration of his attorney, Lisa Saperstein as his only defense to Defendant’s Demurrer. However, a Demurrer only looks to the face of the pleadings and judicially noticeable facts. (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) Therefore, the Court cannot consider Saperstein’s Declaration while ruling on the instant Demurrer.

 

Cause of Action 1 – Premises Liability

 

            To succeed on a case from premises liability, the plaintiff must prove that the defendant was the owner, occupant, or lessor of the premises, was negligent in its use, maintenance, or management, and such negligence caused injury, damage, loss, or harm to the plaintiff. (Brooks v. Eugene Burger Management Corp. (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1611, 1619.)

 Defendants argue that the Court should again dismiss the Plaintiff’s premises liability claim, as it has done previously, due to insufficient allegations showing a duty in the Fifth Amended Complaint. Defendants contend that there is no legal duty to screen every person entering the emergency department for weapons. Defendants argue that a duty to provide specific security measures requires both a high degree of foreseeability of danger and an assessment of the burden on the business. (See Ann M. v Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal. 4th 666, 678-679; Delgado v. Target Corp. (2014) 59 Cal. 4th 312, 326.) Plaintiff's cited incidents, a police shooting, and a suicide, are too distant in time and context to establish the foreseeability of third-party gun violence in the hospital. Additionally, Plaintiff’s reliance on general studies about workplace violence does not show that gun violence was specifically foreseeable at the hospital, nor does it establish a legally recognized duty to implement such extensive security measures. Consequently, Defendants ask the Court to dismiss the premises liability cause of action, as Plaintiff has not demonstrated the foreseeability or the feasibility of the proposed duty.

             The existence of a legal duty is a question for the court to determine, and “foreseeability is a ‘crucial factor’ in determining the existence and scope of a legal duty.” (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill (2005) 36 Cal.4th 224, 237, citing Ann M., supra, 6 Cal.4th at pp. 674, 676.) However, a high degree of foreseeability is required in order to find that the scope of a landlord’s duty of care includes the hiring of security guards. . . . [T]he requisite degree of foreseeability rarely, if ever, can be proven in the absence of prior similar incidents of violent crime on the landowner’s premises. To hold otherwise would be to impose an unfair burden upon landlords and, in effect, would force landlords to become the insurers of public safety, contrary to well established policy in this state.” (Ann M., supra, 6 Cal.4th at p. 679, footnote omitted.) “Heightened foreseeability is satisfied by a showing of prior similar criminal incidents (or other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults in that location) and does not require a showing of prior nearly identical criminal incidents. (Delgado, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 245.) 

            The Court previously found that the Fourth Amended Complaint failed to state a claim for premises liability because Plaintiff failed to plead facts that showed Defendants owed Plaintiff a duty to check every entrant into the emergency department for weapons. While the Fourth Amended Complaint stated that two prior gun violence incidents had occurred at the hospital, Plaintiff had not provided enough detail for the Court to compare how similar the events were to the alleged incident.

            The Fifth Amended Complaint fails to go into further detail about the prior incidents referenced in the Fourth Amended Complaint. Plaintiff does add facts regarding the dangers of emergency rooms and litigation involving a mental health facility across the street. However, none of these facts show “prior similar criminal incidents,” which would lead to the heightened foreseeability required to find a legal duty. (Delgado, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 237, 245.) Furthermore, Plaintiffs have now had six attempts to state a cause of action and failed to cure the defects that plagued the Fourth Amended Complaint. Thus, the Court finds that there is no reasonable probability the defects can be cured by amendment. (Hebert v. Los Angeles Raiders, Ltd. (1991) 23 Cal. App. 4th 414, 425-26.

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer as to the first cause of action without leave to amend.

Cause of Action 2 – General Negligence

 

             To succeed in a claim for negligence, the plaintiff must prove that the defendant owed a legal duty to the plaintiff, that the defendant breached the duty, causation, and damages to the plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s general negligence claim lacks a clearly defined duty and is based on the same grounds as the previously dismissed Complaints. They contend that the Fifth Amended Complaint fails to establish a recognized legal duty and contains vague allegations about security failures without specifying whether the issue was due to inadequate training, insufficient staffing, or lack of access control. Defendants argue that California law does not recognize a duty to screen all entrants, nor does it impose heightened security requirements without prior similar incidents. Because the Plaintiff’s negligence claim is based on an unrecognized, vague duty, Defendants request that the Court dismiss it.

 

            The Court previously found that the Fourth Amended Complaint failed to state a claim for negligence because Plaintiff failed to plead facts that showed Defendants owed Plaintiff a heightened duty of care to protect Plaintiff from the criminal acts of a third party. While the Plaintiff pleaded facts to show that there were prior incidents of violence and provided general statistics about hospital safety, the Court found these facts insufficient to create a heightened legal duty because they did not show a concrete connection between the prior incidents and Plaintiff’s alleged incident.

The Fifth Amended Complaint fails to go into further detail about the prior incidents referenced in the Fourth Amended Complaint. Plaintiff does add facts regarding the dangers of emergency rooms and litigation involving a mental health facility across the street. However, none of these facts show “prior similar criminal incidents,” which would lead to the heightened foreseeability required to find a legal duty. (Delgado, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 237, 245.) Thus, Plaintiff has failed to plead a cognizable duty Defendant owed Plaintiff. Furthermore, Plaintiff has now had six attempts to state a cause of action and has failed to cure the defects that plagued the Fourth Amended Complaint. Thus, the Court finds that there is no reasonable probability the defects can be cured by amendment. (Hebert, supra, 23 Cal. App. 4th 414, 425-26.)

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer as to the second cause of action without leave to amend.

HEARING: SPECIAL DEMURRER/PLEA IN ABATEMENT

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

 

RULING

The Court sustains the Plea in Abatement and Stays the action pending disposition of MARQUISE LEWIS vs KELLY SERVICES GLOBAL, LLC. (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782.)

 

BACKGROUND

            On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff Orion Serup filed the Complaint against Defendant Canoo INC. alleging a PAGA claim for Violation of California Labor Code § 2698 arising from Plaintiff's time as an employee of Defendant.

 

            On October 11, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Special Demurrer to/Plea in Abatement of Plaintiffs Complaint.

 

            On October 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code Civil Procedure section 430.10(c) expressly provides that a party may demur where “there is another cause of action pending between the same parties on the same cause of action.”  (See People ex rel. Garamendi v. American Autoplan, Inc., (1993) 20 Cal. App. 4th 760, 770.)  The pendency of another action is classified as a plea in abatement and, as such, is “not favored.”  This disfavor is expressed in a number of rules: (1) The objection itself is strictly limited so that, whether raised by demurrer or answer, the defendant must show that the parties, the cause of action, and issues are identical and that the same evidence would support the judgment in each case. . . (2) demurrer will not be sustained if the complaint also shows that the former action is no longer pending. . . (3) even if objection is good demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend and the action should not be dismissed.”  (California Union Ins. Co. v Trinity River Land Co. (1980) 105 Cal App 3d 104, 108.)

 

In determining whether another action is pending between the same parties on the same cause of action, the facts in the two causes of action are compared to determine what primary right has been invaded. California follows the "primary right theory," which holds that every judicial action must involve the following elements: a primary right possessed by the plaintiff, and a corresponding primary duty devolving upon the defendant; a wrong done by the defendant that consisted of a breach of such primary right and duty; a remedial right in favor of the plaintiff, and a remedial duty resting on the defendant springing from this wrong; and the remedy or relief itself. Of these elements, the combined primary right, duty, and wrong constitute the cause of action. (Bush v Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1374, 1384.)

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

The Court may take judicial notice of records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States. (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (d).) The Court may also take judicial notice of “Official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.” (Evid. Code § 452 subd. (c).)

 

            Defendant requests that the Court take judicial notice of the version of California Labor Code 2699 in effect immediately prior to its amendment in 2024, the Class Action and PAGA Complaint filed by Marquise Lewis against defendants including Canoo Inc., dated and file-stamped July 8, 2024 (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782), the Minute Order dated August 9, 2024 (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782), the Initial Status Conference Order (Complex Cases/Class Actions) dated and file-stamped August 9, 2024 (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782), the Notice of Removal (from Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782 to United States District Court, Central District of California, Case No. 2:24-cv-06781) dated and file-stamped August 9, 2024, the Order Setting Scheduling Conference, dated and file-stamped August 15, 2024 (United States District Court, Central District of California, Case No. 2:24-cv-06781), the Online Civil Docket for Case No. 2:24-cv-06781, viewed as of October 10, 2024, and the Online Case Summary (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782), viewed as of October 10, 2024.

 

            The Court grants Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice in full, pursuant to Evidence Code sections 452(d) and (h).

 

DISCUSSION

 

Meet and Confer

 

Before filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (emphasis added, CCP § 430.41(a).)

 

On October 7, 2024, the parties conferred over teams regarding the Demurrer. The Court finds this sufficient to meet the meet and confer requirements.

 

Cause of Action 1 – Violation of California Labor Code § 2698

 

            Marquise Lewis filed a complaint, including a PAGA claim, in Los Angeles Superior Court on July 8, 2024, against Canoo Inc., Canoo Sales, LLC, Canoo Technologies, Inc., and Kelly Services. (Case No. 24STCV16782) Despite removal to federal court, the state court has scheduled a non-appearance review for January 10, 2025. Lewis’s complaint alleges employment with all defendants, claiming Labor Code violations, unfair competition, and a PAGA claim for civil penalties on behalf of Canoo employees from May 2023 to judgment. Each cause of action applies to all defendants.

 

            Plaintiff Orion Serup filed the instant representative PAGA claim against Canoo Inc. in Los Angeles Superior Court on August 15, 2024. The claim seeks civil penalties for Labor Code violations on behalf of all Canoo employees from May 29, 2023, to the final judgment. Plaintiff’s allegations largely mirror those in the Lewis Case.

 

Defendant requests that the Court abate or stay all further proceedings in the instant action pending the termination of the Lewis Case.

 

Abatement

 

            Defendant argues that the Court should stay proceedings until the termination of the Lewis Case because the two cases involve the same parties and the same claim.

 

            Defendant argues that both the Lewis and instant PAGA cases involve the same essential parties, the State of California and Defendant, as PAGA actions are brought on behalf of the state to enforce labor law, regardless of the individual plaintiff. (Iskanian v. CLS Transp. Los Angeles (2014) LLC, 59 Cal. 4th 348, 386.) As California is the real party in interest, any PAGA case binds all affected employees and serves to protect their rights under state labor law. Since the Lewis case already represents all allegedly aggrieved employees, including those in the instant case, covering violations from May 2023 onward, the instant case is redundant.

Furthermore, Defendant argues that the Lewis case and the instant case involve identical representative PAGA claims, as both plaintiffs seek to enforce the same primary right: California’s right to impose penalties on employers for Labor Code violations. Since PAGA claims are brought on behalf of the state, the rights asserted by each plaintiff are fundamentally the same. (See Howitson v. EvansHotels, LLC (2022) 81 Cal. App. 5th 475, 488.)  The instant case, solely focused on a PAGA claim, is thus entirely encompassed by the Lewis case, which also includes individual and class claims but asserts the same representative right under PAGA.

Plaintiff argues that the instant PAGA case should not be abated, as it seeks penalties for Labor Code violations distinct from those in the Lewis case, which includes both class action claims and additional defendants. Additionally, Lewis brings claims against Defendant for wages while the instant case is seeking penalties under PAGA.  Plaintiff asserts that staying the instant case would unfairly delay its resolution due to procedural requirements in the Lewis class action, such as class certification, which are irrelevant to PAGA claims. Plaintiff further emphasizes that PAGA’s purpose is to facilitate enforcement of labor laws, given limited state resources, and that California law supports a liberal interpretation of PAGA to ensure timely protection of employee rights. Consequently, Plaintiff requests that the court deny the motion to abate the instant case to ensure efficient enforcement of the California Labor Code.

 

The Court finds that the parties in the instant case and the parties in the Lewis Case are identical because both cases involve PAGA claims, and thus are brought on behalf of the State of California against Defendant. (Iskanian, supra, 59 Cal. 4th 348, 386.)

When addressing whether two causes of action are identical, the Court compares the facts alleged in the two actions. The causes of action are identical if both causes of action allege invasion of the same primary right. (Bush, supra, 10 Cal. App. 4th 1374, 1384.) A “primary right” is based on the harm suffered and not the particular theory of recovery asserted by the Plaintiff. (Bay Cities Paving & Grading, Inc. v. Lawyers' Mutual Ins. Co. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 854, 860.)

 The Lewis case asserts class action claims and a representative PAGA claim arising out of Defendant’s failure to provide meal and rest periods, failure to pay wages, failure to provide wage statements, failure to indemnify, and unlawful business practices. (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782 Complaint.) In the instant case, Plaintiff alleges a representative PAGA claim arising out of Defendant’s failure to provide employment records, failure to pay wages, failure to provide meal and rest breaks, failure to provide wage statements, and failure to reimburse. (Complaint p. 3.) Thus, both cases allege the same harm based on Defendant’s failure to follow California labor law. While Plaintiff argues that the causes of action are distinguishable because different claims and different remedies are sought, the primary right of a cause of action is based on the harm suffered and not the particular theory of recovery. (See, Bush, supra, 10 Cal. App. 4th 1374, 1384.)

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Plea in Abatement and Stays the action pending disposition of the Lewis Case. (Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 24STCV16782.)

 

Exclusive Concurrent Jurisdiction

 

The exclusive concurrent jurisdiction doctrine provides that a second-in-time action should be stayed even in the absence of absolute identity of parties and causes of action if doing so would avoid potentially conflicting results or a waste of judicial resources. (See People ex rel. Garamendi v. Am. Autoplan, Inc. (1993) 20 Cal. App. 4th 760, 769-70.)

 

Defendant argues that the instant case should be stayed under the doctrine of exclusive concurrent jurisdiction, this doctrine prevents conflicting results and judicial resource waste by giving priority to the first court to assume jurisdiction. Here, the federal court handling the Lewis case has the authority to resolve all PAGA-related claims and provide any relief that could be granted in the instant case. (Huff v. Securitas Servs. USA, Inc. (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 745, 750.) Multiple courts addressing overlapping PAGA suits against Canoo could duplicate efforts and lead to inconsistent outcomes. Consequently, Defendant requests a stay of the instant case until the Lewis case is resolved.

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s concerns about inconsistent rulings and judicial waste are unfounded, as concurrent PAGA actions are commonly permitted. Plaintiff asserts that there is no risk of conflicting outcomes regarding specific claims. Under PAGA, separate actions by different employees against the same employer are allowed (see Tan v. GrubHub, Inc. (N.D.Cal. 2016) 171 F.Supp.3d 998, 1012–1013.), and collateral estoppel would prevent any redundant suits if an unfavorable judgment is reached first. California precedent also supports parallel proceedings, as res judicata would address any future duplicative judgments. Plaintiff urges the court to prioritize the legislative intent of PAGA for prompt labor law enforcement, noting that distinct claims and parties, in the instant case, minimize any potential duplication with Lewis.

 

The Court denies Defendant’s request to Stay proceedings under the Exclusive Concurrent Jurisdiction doctrine as moot.
