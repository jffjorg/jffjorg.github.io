DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 22STCV08835    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: 56

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA  

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT 

 

 

 

JANE DOE.,  

Plaintiff, 

vs. 

 

MICHELSON LABORATORIES, INC.., and DOES 1 to 100, inclusive,  

                                                                               

Defendants.                                

 

      CASE NO.: 23STCV27119 

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:  

DEFENDANT JOSEPH SOLIZ’S DEMURRER AND MTOION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT 

 

Date: April 30, 2024 

Time: 8:30 a.m. 

Dept. 56 

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Joseph Soliz. (“Defendant Soliz”) 

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) 

 

The Court has considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.  

 

 

BACKGROUND 

This is an action for invasion of privacy and sexual battery. The Third Amended Complaint (TAC) alleges that Plaintiff was required by her employer, Michelson Laboratories, Inc., to undergo a drug test and physical examination at Defendant Southern California Immediate Medical Center’s facility. (TAC ¶¶ 9–10.) When Plaintiff went to the facility, she was made to undergo an unjustified  x-ray, prior to which she was directed by a male x-ray technician to take off her clothes. (TAC ¶¶ 12–14.) This technician watched Plaintiff while she undressed, and once she got on the x-ray table, the technician digitally penetrated her sex organs, stating that it was part of the procedure. (TAC ¶¶ 15–18.)

The original complaint was filed on March 11, 2022. The TAC alleges four causes of action:

1.     Invasion of Privacy

2.     Sexual Battery

3.     Negligent Hiring and Retention

4.     Premises Liability

 

On December 8, 2023, this court sustained the demurrer of Defendants Southern California Immediate Medical Center and Thomas Roccapalumbo to the fourth cause of action for premises liability, and granted the motion to strike as to the prayer for punitive damages under Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13.

 

Defendant Soliz was added as Doe defendant 7 on November 22, 2023 and filed the present demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and motion to strike the TAC (the “Motion”) on April 23, 2024.  Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Demurrer and the Motion on April 10, 2024.  Defendant Soliz filed a reply on April 23, 2024.

 

DISCUSSION 

Demurrer

A demurrer should be sustained only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Pro., §§ 430.30, et seq.) In particular, as is relevant here, a court should sustain a demurrer if a complaint does not allege facts that are legally sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (See id. § 430.10, subd. (e).) As the Supreme Court held in Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311: “We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. . . . Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Id. at p. 318; see also Hahn. v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 [“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. [Citation.]”)

 

“In determining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer … if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Such demurrers “are disfavored, and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.” (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Insurance Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848.)

 

A demurrer should not be sustained without leave to amend if the complaint, liberally construed, can state a cause of action under any theory or if there is a reasonable possibility the defect can be cured by amendment. (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1081.) The demurrer also may be sustained without leave to amend where the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead render it probable plaintiff cannot state a cause of action. (Krawitz v. Rusch (1989) 209 Cal.App.3d 957, 967.)

 

Analysis

Defendant Soliz here demurs to the first cause of action for invasion of privacy, on the ground that it does not plead the basis for liability against him, but rather asserts an invasion of privacy claim against Doe 1, Southern California Immediate Medical Center, and Thomas Roccapalumbo for taking unauthorized x-rays and having a policy permitting the same. (Demurrer at pp. 4–5; TAC ¶¶ 25–26.) No specific allegation is included against Doe 7 (Defendant Soliz), and although he concedes that correspondence with Plaintiff’s counsel revealed he was intended to be added as Doe 2 — i.e. the party accused of sexually assaulting Plaintiff — the invasion of privacy claim includes no allegations against Doe 2 either. (Demurrer at p. 3, fn. 1.)

 

Plaintiff in opposition argues that Defendant Soliz has already stipulated to the filing of a fourth amended complaint (the “4AC”) that would include him in the invasion of privacy claim by inserting allegations that he looked at Plaintiff while she disrobed for the x-ray. (Opposition at pp. 3–4.) Because not all parties have stipulated to the amendment, the motion for leave to file the 4AC is currently set for May 8, 2024. (Opposition at pp. 4–5.)

 

Here, because the current operative complaint contains no allegation against Defendant Soliz identifying the basis for pleading a cause of action for invasion of privacy, the demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the first cause of action against Defendant Soliz, with leave to amend.

 

 

Motion to Strike

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435(b)(1)). The notice of motion to strike a portion of a pleading shall quote in full the portions sought to be stricken except where the motion is to strike an entire paragraph, cause of action, count or defense. (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1322.)

 

The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or form any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437(a)). The court then may strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading and strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend. (Perlman v. Municipal Court (1979) 99 Cal.App.3d 568, 575.)

 

Defendant Soliz moves to strike the prayer for punitive damages on the grounds that any such pleading is barred by Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13. That statute states:

 

In any action for damages arising out of the professional negligence of a health care provider, no claim for punitive damages shall be included in a complaint or other pleading unless the court enters an order allowing an amended pleading that includes a claim for punitive damages to be filed. The court may allow the filing of an amended pleading claiming punitive damages on a motion by the party seeking the amended pleading and on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294 of the Civil Code.

(Code Civ. Proc. § 425.13, subd. (a).)

 

Although directed towards claims arising out of “professional negligence,” the statute also applies to claims for intentional torts against medical providers. (See Country Villa Claremont Healthcare Center, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 428, 431–32.) The statute has specifically been applied to claims for sexual battery against medical providers, where the plaintiff sustained the battery while a patient of the provider. In United Western Medical Centers v. Superior Court (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 500, 503, the court held that section 425.13 applied to bar punitive damages claimed against a hospital on a sexual assault claim, where the claim against the hospital was “expressly based on hospital's alleged failure to “supervise and protect and safeguard” the party. (Id. at p. 504.) And in Cooper v. Superior Court (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 744, 752, held that section 425.13 applied to a sexual assault claim arising “in the course of gynecological services.” (Id. at p. 752.)

 

However, it has been recognized that “a cause of action against a health care provider for sexual battery would not, in most instances, fall within the statute because the defendant's conduct would not be directly related to the manner in which professional services were rendered.” (Central Pathology Service Medical Clinic, Inc. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 181, 192.) While this language was interpreted as dictum in Cooper, the court recognized it was “obviously correct as a factual matter,” before distinguishing its application to a claim for sexual assault arising specifically from gynecological services:

 

Central Pathology noted merely that “in most instances” a claim of sexual battery would not be related to the manner in which professional services are rendered. Clearly this is so. Most types of services rendered by health care providers do not involve the genitalia. Thus if a podiatrist, ophthalmologist or dentist touches or manipulates a patient's genitalia, there will usually be no arguable connection between such conduct and the rendition of legitimate health care. If a patient consults an orthopedic surgeon for treatment of bursitis in an elbow, there would usually be no occasion for the surgeon to touch or manipulate that patient's genitalia. A doctor consulted for nutritional advice about cholesterol levels would not likely have a legitimate medical reason for manipulating genitalia. The list of examples of this type is endless. A doctor rendering gynecological care, by contrast, cannot render the full panoply of gynecological services without touching, probing or otherwise manipulating a woman's genitalia. Thus when a gynecologist is accused, as here, of committing a sexual battery in the course of rendering gynecological services, that accusation is necessarily “directly related” to the manner in which the gynecological services were rendered.

(Cooper, supra, 56 Cal.App.4th at p. 751.)

            The above language is instructive here. Defendant Soliz is not alleged to be a gynecologist, and he does not argue that this was the case. He is rather alleged to have sexually touched Plaintiff while conducting an x-ray. There is “no arguable connection between such conduct and the rendition” of the x-ray. (Cooper, supra, 56 Cal.App.5th at p. 751.) Defendant Soliz in reply argues only that the x-ray itself was related to the provision of medical services [Reply at p. 2]; however, the connection at issue is not that between the x-ray and medical services, but between the acts constituting sexual battery and medical services. Defendant Soliz offers no such connection in his Motion.

 

            The motion to strike is therefore DENIED.

            The Court has considered the moving papers.

 

BACKGROUND

            The currently operative First Amended Complaint (the “FAC”) alleges: (1) negligence; and (2) negligent hiring, retention, and supervision.     In relevant part, the FAC alleges that Plaintiff was sexually abused by Norbert Stuart Volk (“Volk”), a teacher employed by the Defendant from approximately 1969 to 1970. (FAC ¶¶ 18-19.) The FAC alleges that Defendant had reason to know of Volk’s misconduct that created a risk of childhood sexual abuse. (FAC ¶ 15.)  Defendant filed its Answer to the FAC on June 22, 2022.

            On March 15, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Leave to Amend its Answer (the “Motion”). No opposition has been filed.

DISCUSSION

Leave to amend is permitted under the Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) and section 576. The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified. . ..” “Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . .. [citation].  A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)  

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).)

 

Defendant seeks leave to file an amended Answer to add an affirmative defense under Education Code § 44808, which precludes liability under certain situations for conduct that occurred off the school site.

Defendant provides a copy of its proposed Amended Answer with a supporting declaration of its counsel stating why Defendant seeks leave to amend. (See Declaration of Matther R. Hicks; Exhibit 2.) Counsel states that he conducted a deposition of the Plaintiff wherein Plaintiff testified that the alleged abuse by Norbet Volk occurred both on campus at George Washington Carver Junior High School and off-campus. (Id. ¶ 3; Exhibit 1 p. 37-39, 47-49.) Counsel further swears that the purpose and effect of the amendment is to add an affirmative defense under Education Code § 44808, which counsel argues may provide Defendant with an immunity defense from liability, at least as it pertains to any alleged abuse off-campus from Carver. (Id. ¶ 4.) Thus, counsel contends that the proposed amendment is necessary and proper to ensure the Defendant’s due process right to allege and try an affirmative defense under law. (Id.)

 

The Court finds good cause in granting the Motion.  Here, the Defendant satisfies the requirements under CRC rule 3.1324 because it states what affirmative defense is to be included in its Amended Answer, which is an immunity defense under Education Code § 44808. The Motion includes a copy of the proposed Amended Answer and a declaration specifying the amendments' effect, necessity, propriety, and date of discovery. Defendant provides a sufficient reason for the delay as Defendant was only recently made aware after taking Plaintiff’s deposition. Furthermore, the proposed Amended Answer redlines the changes to be made to the answer. Therefore, because Defendants have satisfied the requirements under CRC Rule 3.1324, the Motion is GRANTED.

RESPONDING PARTY: None 

 

The Court has considered the moving papers.  No opposition or reply papers have been filed as of April 26, 2024.  

 

BACKGROUND 

 

  On May 18, 2023, Plaintiff Tohid Naeem (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative Complaint against Defendant Nidal A. Barakat (“Defendant”) for Violations of Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance.  On July 31, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer.  

 

On January 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint. No opposition was filed to the Motion; however, on January 31, 2024, this Court continued this matter to allow Plaintiff to serve Defendant with the Motion and to give Defendant the opportunity to respond.  

 

On February 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed another Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint. On March 19, 2024, this Court again continued this matter to allow Plaintiff to serve Defendant with the Motion and supporting documents at the address on record for Defendant.

           

            On March 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint. Plaintiff also filed proof of service by first-class mail at the address on record for Defendant. No opposition has been filed as of April 26, 2024.

 

DISCUSSION  

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 473 permits the trial court in its discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) Code of Civil Procedure Section 576 provides that any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding. (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.) An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion. (Id.) If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend and where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion. (Morgan v. Superior Court of Cal. In and For Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) Where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party, the liberal rule of allowance prevails. (Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564.) 

 

Under California Rules of Court (“CRC”), Rule 3.1324, a motion for leave to amend a pleading must be accompanied by a declaration that sets forth: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made sooner. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).) 

 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)

 

Here, the Minute Order of March 19, 2024 required Plaintiff to serve Defendant with the Motion and supporting documents at the address on record for Defendant within five days of the issuance of the order. On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with the Motion and supporting documents at Defendant’s correct address. Therefore, the Court finds Plaintiff has complied with the Minute Order of March 19, 2024.

 

Further, the Motion is accompanied by the Declaration of Plaintiff, which provides (1) the second amended complaint is entirely different from the original complaint; (2) the amendment is necessary and proper because the original complaint did not fully encompass the scope of the issue; (3) the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were not discovered until recently as Plaintiff regained access to an online document and image storage account; and (4) the request for amendment was not made earlier due to Plaintiff’s inability to retrieve relevant information stored online and secure legal representation. (Declaration of Tohid Naeem, ¶¶ 2-5.) Thus, the Court finds Plaintiff has complied with Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

 

Lastly, the Motion includes a copy of the proposed amended pleading. Since the proposed amended pleading is entirely different from the original pleading (which merely states “Violations of Tenant Anti-Harassment Ordinance”), the Court finds that Plaintiff has complied with Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a) even though the Motion does not state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted and where, and does not state what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading and where.

 

Accordingly, the Motion for Leave to File Second Amended Complaint filed by Tohid Naeem on March 29, 2024 is GRANTED.
