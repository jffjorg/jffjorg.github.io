DEPT:

June 25, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV17528

MOTIONS: 

Petition for Expedited Minors Compromise

MOVING PARTY:

Petitioner Fanny Perez

OPPOSING PARTY:

Unopposed

 

 

The Court has reviewed the amended expedited petition filed June 11, 2024 by Petitioner Fanny Perez (Petitioner) on behalf of Claimant Ruth Herrera, age 13. The Court denies the petition without prejudice for the following reasons:

 

The Court noted in the previous petition that item 9 indicated that Claimant was not fully recovered but the following injuries were temporary: “a nondisplaced fracture of the sesamoid bone of the left foot, bruising.” Petitioner indicates orthotics have been prescribed. (Min. Order, 5/9/24.) The Court also noted that Petitioner did not provide any medical records or other evidence in attachment 9 to support this assertion.

 

In this revised petition, Petitioner again does not include any medical record/reports describing Claimant’s current condition. This must be provided in attachment 9.

 

Since it appears there are $6,045.64 in total liens, the value in item 13a(5) should be amended accordingly. 

 

Attachment 13g does not contain statements from all the medical providers seeking reimbursement.

 

Petitioner again requests $12,000 in attorney fees which represents 40% of the gross settlement. The Court previously found support for only 33.33% of the gross settlement, based on the attorney declaration. (Min. Order, 5/9/24.) Here, Petitioner filed the same attorney declaration, but did not lower the requested amount. Furthermore, no fee agreement was attached.

ANALYSIS

 

I. Compel Further Responses to Request for Production

 

According to the joint discovery statement, Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Request for Production, Set One number 10.

 

            A. Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(a) provides that on receipt of a response to a request for production of documents, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses if:¿ 

¿ 

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.¿ 

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.¿ 

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.¿¿ 

 

A response consisting of an objection must specify with particularly the document being objected to, and, if based on privilege, must provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of this claim, including if necessary, a privilege log. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240 (b), (c).) 

¿ 

The motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery and include a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b).)

 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).)¿¿¿ 

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310 (h).)

 

B. Discussion

 

Request for Production, Set One, number 10 asks the following: “Produce all Documents that constitute, evidence, or Relate to any formal or informal Communication between Your insurer and any Person, other than Your attorney, that Relate to any invoice, estimate, bid, proposal, or other request for payment or authorization Relating to any repair of Plaintiff’s Vehicle subsequent to the Incident.”

 

Defendant responded with various objections, including based on attorney client privilege and work product, and stated: “Responding party states that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry in an effort to locate the items requested has been made and responding party will comply with the production, inspection, copying, testing, sampling, and related activity demanded, in whole, and all documents or things in the demanded category that are in responding party’s possession, custody and control and to which no objection is being made, will be included in the production as follows: Exhibit D – One hundred and eleven (111) color photographs/digital images depicting responding party’s vehicle. Exhibit E – Plaintiff’s vehicle damage repair estimate.”

 

In the joint statement, Plaintiff states he has limited the scope of this request to: “1) the initial repair estimate provided to State Farm by the body shop, any supplements or revisions thereto, and communication related to same; and 2) email and other correspondence from the body shop to State Farm and any replies thereto.” Plaintiff contends that Defendant produced emails from State Farm to the body shop and State Farm’s repair estimates, but not correspondence from the body shop. Plaintiff asserts the documents exist due to Defendant’s response in Form Interrogatory 17.1.

 

Defendant argues it produced communications between State Farm and the body shop, and is not in possession, custody or control of additional documents. To the extent that Defendant is in possession, custody, or control of documents sent by the body shop to State Farm, whether or not Plaintiff can separately subpoena the body shop is not a proper objection. However, Defendant argues she issued a deposition subpoena for Beverly La Brea Auto Body, requesting documents, but none were provided.

 

In light of the above and considering that Defendant’s responses state that a diligent search was made, the documents are not in Defendant’s possession or control, the motion to compel further responses to Request for Production number 10 is denied. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230.)

 

The Court declines to award sanctions finding that Plaintiff acted with substantial justification.

 

 

II. Compel Further Response to Special Interrogatories

 

            A. Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300(a) provides that “on receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:¿ 

¿ 

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.¿ 

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.¿ 

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ 

 

The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(b)(1).) “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).)¿¿ 

¿ 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(d).)¿¿¿

 

B. Discussion

 

According to the joint discovery statement, Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One numbers 10, 11, 13, 19, 20, 22-29, 31, 32, and 34.

 

Number 10 asks Defendant to state the amount of damages she “believe[s]” that Plaintiff incurred as a result of the loss of use of Plaintiff’s vehicle. Number 11 asks for facts in support of that value. Number 13 asks to identify documents in support of the value.

 

Defendant asserted various objections, including based on attorney client privilege and work product. Defendant also responded that after reasonable inquiry, she lacks personal knowledge of the subject matter.

 

The interrogatory as phrased appears to request Defendant to state information as to her personal knowledge, not her legal theory or contention. (See, e.g., Sabetian v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2020), 57 Cal.App.5th 1054, 1086 [distinguishing between questions that called for personal knowledge and contention questions].) Accordingly, since Defendant asserted that she lacks personal knowledge of the amount of damages Plaintiff incurred, the response is sufficient. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220(c) [“If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”.]

 

Number 19 asks to describe in detail all actions “you took” with respect to repair damages to Plaintiff’s vehicle. Defendant responded “none as to this responding party.”

 

Here, Defendant is the only named defendant in this action; State Farm is not a named defendant but is apparently defending the action under the terms of the insurance policy. Although documents may be said to be in the possession, custody, or control of the defendant that is in the insurance company’s possession, custody, or control (see Clark v. Superior Court (1960) 177 Cal.App.2d 577, 579 [“In such a situation the attorneys for the insurance company must be treated as the defendants’ attorneys and any material which is in their possession, custody or control solely because of this relationship is also in the possession, custody or control of the defendants…”].), that does not necessarily mean that all actions taken by the insurance company are actions either taken by or known about by the insured. Again, this interrogatory as phrased appears to request Defendant to state information as to her personal knowledge, not her legal theory or contention.

 

Similarly, because the following seek communications between Defendant or State Farm and the body shop (non-parties to this action), and Plaintiff has responded that she lacks personal knowledge, the Court denies the motion for further responses to numbers 20, 22-25, and 26-29.

 

Numbers 31, 32, and 34 relate to the amount of damages Defendant “believe[s]” that Plaintiff incurred for repairing his vehicle. Defendant responded with objections and that after a reasonable inquiry, she lacks personal knowledge. Therefore, the responses are sufficient.

 

As a result, the motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories is denied.

 

The Court declines to award sanctions finding that Plaintiff acted with substantial justification.

 

III. Compel Further Responses to Request for Admissions

 

            A. Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.290(a) provides that on receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply:¿ 

¿ 

(1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete.¿ 

(2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.¿ 

 

If a party fails to serve a timely response, they waive any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010). The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(a).)  

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.290(d).)  

 

            B. Discussion

 

Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses to Request for Admission, Set One, numbers 11-15, and 17.

 

Request numbers 11–14 seek admissions that Defendant, or her agent or representative, on various dates, refused to repair Plaintiff’s vehicle unless he signed a release of liability.

           

            Plaintiff argues that these requests establish “Defendant’s own estimate of Plaintiff’s recoverable repair costs, as well as the timeline of Defendant’s back-and-forth haggling with the body shop, which was the cause of a significant portion of the loss of use period.” The RFAs in question do not relate to this, but rather seek to establish that State Farm refused to repair. Plaintiff has not established that this is a proper subject of this lawsuit, where State Farm is not a party. (See, e.g., Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Insurance Companies (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287.) Accordingly, the motion to compel further is denied.

 

Request 15 seeks an admission that Defendant, or her agent or representative have not paid to repair Plaintiff’s vehicle. Defendant has stated that she will provide an amended response.

 

Request 17 seeks an admission that Defendant, or her agent or representative refused Plaintiff’s request to disburse any undisputed repair costs. Defendant has stated that she will provide an amended response.

 

            The Court declines to award sanctions, finding that both parties acted with substantial justification.

 

IV. Form Interrogatories

 

Plaintiff seeks further responses to Form Interrogatories, number 17.1 which requests facts, contact information of people with knowledge and identifying supporting documents, that support responses that are not an unqualified admission. Plaintiff argues Defendant did not provide responses to Request for Admissions numbers 2–7 and 19. Plaintiff further argues that Defendant must complete 17.1 for admissions that contained only objections (numbers 11–18 and 20–22).

 

Defendant represents she will provide supplemental responses for admission numbers 2–7, 15, 17, and 19.

 

Because Defendant asserts she will provide supplemental responses, the Court finds this motion is moot. Therefore, the motion to compel further is denied. The Court declines to award monetary sanctions.

 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Request for Production, Set One.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One.

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One.

 

Plaintiff to provide notice and file a proof of service of such.

CASE NUMBER:

23STCV06764

MOTIONS: 

Motion to Compel the Deposition of Watermark Security Group, Inc.’s Employee, Enedino Espinoza

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant Target Corporation

OPPOSING PARTY:

Specially Appearing Related Party Enedino Espinoza

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action stems from a stabbing at a Target store. One of the plaintiffs in this consolidated case, Brayden Medina Molina, a minor, (“Plaintiff”) is one of the victims who brought this action against Target Corporation, Watermark Security Group, Inc., Brookfield Properties (USA) LLC, and Does 1 to 50 (“Molina case”). The assailant was eventually shot and subdued by Enedino Espinoza, while working as a Watermark Security guard, who has brought a separately related action (23STCV19129 “Espinoza case”).

 

Defendant Target Corporation (“Defendant”) now moves to compel the deposition of Enedino Espinoza, as an employee of co-defendant Watermark Security Group, Inc. On June 14, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s ex parte application shortening the time for this motion. The opposition was due June 20, 2024, and the reply was due June 24, 2024. (Min. Order, 6/14/24.)

 

Specially Appearing Related Party Enedino Espinoza (“Espinoza”) has filed an opposition. Defendant replies.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450(a) provides: “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”¿ Section 2025.450 requires the Court to compel the deposition unless it finds a valid objection was served under section 2025.410.¿ 

¿ 

A motion brought to compel a deposition “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition … by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

If a motion to compel deposition is granted, sanctions are mandatory in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)¿ 

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

The Declaration of Jessica Farley does not set forth facts describing a meet and confer effort to schedule Espinoza’s deposition, but attaches an email correspondence from June 3, 2024. (Farley Decl., Exh. 3.)[1]

 

JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Pursuant to the request of Espinoza, the Court takes judicial notice of Watermark’s motion for summary judgment filed June 17, 2024, Index of Exhibits in Support, and Notice of Lodging Electronic Media in Support. The Court takes judicial notice of the existence of these documents in its record, but not the truth asserted within. (Evid. Code § 452(d).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Here, Defendant argues that it noticed Espinoza’s deposition to Watermark Security Group, Inc. (“Watermark”) on May 16, 2024, set for May 29, 2024. (Farley Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) Defendant contends Espinoza is still employed by Watermark and thus, subject to section 2025.450. (Id. ¶ 9.) Watermark did not object to the deposition, but Espinoza’s counsel in the Espinoza case served a notice of objection on May 23, 2024. Espinoza objected on the basis that service was defective because Espinoza’s defense counsel in the Molina case was not served. (Id., Exh. 2.) Espinoza also objected that that counsel was not available at the unilaterally set date and had not signed the stipulated Protective Order in the Molina case. (Id.) Espinoza further objected that the notice is vague in that it “appears to be noticing the deposition of Espinoza as a Plaintiff in his case…” (Id.) Counsel further objected that counsel was unavailable. (Id.)

 

 Defendant contends that it has since attempted to schedule Espinoza’s deposition and that all the defendants agreed to depose Espinoza on a single date. On May 31, 2024, Espinoza’s deposition counsel provided two possible dates: June 7 and June 10. (Farley Decl., Exh. 3.) Espinoza’s plaintiff’s counsel did not confirm the dates and has not provided alternative dates. (Id. ¶ 6.) In opposition, Espinoza’s counsel admits that on June 7, 2024, she informed Defendant that she was off work that date, and likely off on June 10 due to a medical procedure. (Opp., 7; Rand-Lewis Decl. ¶ 34.)

 

Espinoza also argues he is an “unnamed defendant” of Plaintiff, is separately represented, and thus Defendant was required to subpoena Espinoza or reach an agreement with his counsel Weinberger. Also pursuant to stipulation of the parties reached on March 25, 2024, the deposition of Espinoza will cover all the related cases in this matter. Therefore, Espinoza’s plaintiff’s counsel needed to be served the notice. (Rand-Lewis Decl. ¶ 14, Exh. B.) Espinoza argues that Defendant was aware in March 2024 that he was being represented by separate counsel for this deposition, yet only served the notice of deposition on Watermark on May 16, 2024. (Id. ¶ 2.) 

 

In reply, Defendant argues section 2025.280 requires Espinoza to appear whether he has independent counsel or not. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.280(a) [“The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.”].) In Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 603, the court reviewed Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.280 and explained that “[i]f the deponent is the party’s employee, officer, or director, an entity has substantial practical control to compel the attendance of these individuals, including the ability to terminate an employee who refuses to appear at a noticed deposition or to end the relationship with its officer or director.”

 

Here, Espinoza does not dispute that he is currently an employee of Watermark. A deposition notice to Watermark was sufficient under the plain language of section 2025.280 to compel his attendance. Whether or not Espinoza has independent, conflict counsel to assist him for the substance of the deposition, does not change his status as an employee for purposes of section 2025.280. Moreover, conflict counsel provided alternative dates for the deposition in June, and Espinoza still failed to schedule the deposition. Neither conflict counsel nor Watermark has objected to the deposition.

 

To the extent that Espinoza objects to topics relating to him as a “plaintiff” as opposed to an “employee,” this issue is not presently before the Court as Espinoza has never been deposed. However, the Court notes that Espinoza has not provided any authority for the argument that only one deposition may be permitted, even though there are two separate (albeit related) cases pending. The Court had previously encouraged the parties to attempt to complete the deposition in one 7-hour session[2] to avoid any further motion practice regarding the scope of a second deposition. Any stipulation reached by the defendants to narrow the issues in a possible second deposition benefits Espinoza, and Espinoza’s position here risks multiple depositions of himself.[3] If the parties cannot agree, then the Court will hear any necessary further motion about a second deposition at the appropriate time.

 

Espinoza also appears to object on the basis that he is entitled to depose Defendant’s witnesses first because he has been waiting six months. Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.020 “sets out the general rule that the various tools of discovery may be used by each party in any order, and one party’s discovery ‘shall not operate to delay the discovery of any other party.’ … However, if a party shows ‘good cause,’ the trial court ‘may establish the sequence and timing of discovery for the convenience of parties and witnesses and in the interests of justice.’” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 550-51 [also noting that the moving party did not file a section 2019.020 motion].) Here, Defendant seeks to depose Espinoza as it relates to a trial preference case. Accordingly, and although Espinoza did not file a motion pursuant to section 2019.020 but rather raised the objection in an opposition, the Court considers the convenience of the parties and witnesses and the interests of justice, and concludes that a prompt deposition of Espinoza is warranted.

 

            Accordingly, the Court grants the motion to compel.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel the Deposition of Watermark Security Group, Inc.’s Employee, Enedino Espinoza is GRANTED. Espinoza shall appear for deposition within 5 days.

CASE NUMBER

MOTION

Motion to Continue Trial

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant City of Los Angeles

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Khosrow Mahgerefteh

 

MOTION

 

Defendant City of Los Angeles (“Defendant”) moves to continue trial and all related dates. Plaintiff Khosrow Mahgerefteh (“Plaintiff”) opposes.

 

BACKGROUND

 

The complaint was filed on April 3, 2023. Trial was initially set for September 30, 2024.

 

Defendant’s answer was filed on May 22, 2023.

 

On April 29, 2024, Defendant filed and served a motion for summary judgment. The hearing for summary judgment is currently scheduled for October 25, 2024.

 

Trial is currently set for September 30, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Legal Standard

 

 “Continuances are granted only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring a continuance.”  (In re Marriage of Falcone & Fyke (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 814, 823.)  A trial court has broad discretion in considering a request for a trial continuance.  (Pham v. Nguyen (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 11, 13-18.)  California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332 sets forth factors for the Court to consider in ruling on a motion to continue trial. 

 

“To ensure the prompt disposition of civil cases, the dates assigned for a trial are firm. All parties and their counsel must regard the date set for trial as certain.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(a).)

 

“A party seeking a continuance of the date set for trial, whether contested or uncontested or stipulated to by the parties, must make the request for a continuance by a noticed motion or an ex parte application under the rules in chapter 4 of this division, with supporting declarations. The party must make the motion or application as soon as reasonably practical once the necessity for the continuance is discovered.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(b).)

 

“Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits. The court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance. Circumstances that may indicate good cause include:

 

(1)   The unavailability of an essential lay or expert witness because of death, illness, or other excusable circumstances;

(2)   The unavailability of a party because of death, illness, or other excusable circumstances;

(3)   The unavailability of trial counsel because of death, illness, or other excusable circumstances;

(4)   The substitution of trial counsel, but only where there is an affirmative showing that the substitution is required in the interests of justice;

(5)   The addition of a new party if:

(A) The new party has not had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial; or

(B)  The other parties have not had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial in regard to the new party’s involvement in the case;

(6)   A party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; or

(7)   A significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case as a result of which the case is not ready for trial.”

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c).)

 

“In ruling on a motion or application for continuance, the court must consider all the facts and circumstances that are relevant to the determination. These may include:

 

(1)   The proximity of the trial date;

(2)   Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party;

(3)   The length of the continuance requested;

(4)   The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance;

(5)   The prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance;

(6)   If the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay;

(7)   The court’s calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials;

(8)   Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial;

(9)   Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance;

(10)  Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and

(11)  Any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(d).)

 

A party may move for summary judgment “at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(1).)¿ Notice of the motion and supporting papers must be served on all other parties at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing.¿ (Id., subd. (a)(2).)¿ The motion must be heard no later than 30 days before the date of trial, unless the court for good cause orders otherwise.¿ (Id., subd. (a)(3).)¿¿¿ 

 

Discussion

 

Defendant requests the Court continue trial in this case to three months after the October 25, 2024 summary judgment hearing. A party that timely files a motion for summary judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c has a right to have their motion heard before the start of trial. (Cole v. Superior Court (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 84, 88.) If served electronically, a motion for summary judgment must be made at least 105 days before trial, plus two court days. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(a)(2), (3).) Therefore, a motion for summary judgment in this case needed to be served by June 13, 2024.

 

 Defendant timely served its motion for summary judgment on April 29, 2024. Defendant argues that the earliest date available for a summary judgment hearing was October 25, 2024—after the September 30, 2024 trial date. (Dishchyan Decl. ¶ 2.) Since that date is after trial, Defendant requests that the Court continue the trial date to allow for the motion for summary judgment to be heard.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff does not dispute that Defendant has a right to be heard on the motion before trial, but instead asks the Court to advance the hearing date to August 30, 2024. Plaintiff argues prejudice would occur if trial were postponed but gives no further details. Given the Court’s heavy calendar and the fact Plaintiff has not described how they would be prejudiced by a short continuance, the Court declines to advance the hearing date.  

 

Therefore, since Defendant has filed a timely summary judgment motion and there have been no prior continuances, the Court finds good cause to continue trial.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants the motion to continue trial.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

The Court GRANTS in part Defendant’s motion to continue trial.

Trial is continued to November 26, 2024 at 8:30 a.m. in Department 32 of the Spring Street Courthouse.
