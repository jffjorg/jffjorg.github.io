DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV37369    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 7, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV37369A

MOTION: 

Motion for Leave to Amend

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Urban Soccer 5 Center LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendants Lisa Marie Vernola Salas, individually and as trustee of the Vernola Family Trust and Joan Vernola, as trustee of the Vernola Family Trust

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Urban Soccer 5 Center LLC (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Lisa Marie Vernola Salas, individually and as trustee of the Vernola Family Trust and Joan Vernola, as trustee of the Vernola Family Trust (“Defendants”) for breach of a commercial lease agreement.  Plaintiff seeks leave to amend to file a second amended complaint.  Defendants oppose the motion.

ANALYSIS

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by the code.”  Thus, the Court has wide discretion to allow either party to amend pleadings “upon any terms as may be just.” 

Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties and, therefore, leave to amend is liberally granted.  (Berman vs. Bromberg (1986) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.)  “[I]t is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where the opposing party was not misled or prejudiced by the amendment.”  (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048.)

Under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend a pleading must include a copy of the proposed amendment and state the allegations the moving party proposes to add or delete. Rule 3.1324 also requires the moving party to submit declaration stating the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff relies on the declaration of its counsel, Michelangelo Tatone (“Counsel”).  Counsel states Plaintiff seeks leave to assert a claim against Sebastien Abonnel (“Abonnel”).  He adds, “During the discovery process, specifically through a document produced by the Defendants, a document revealed the extent of Mr. Abonnel’s conspiracy with the current Defendants.”  (Declaration of Michelangelo Tatone, ¶ 9.)  Counsel fails to aver when Plaintiff received this document from Defendants and what it purports to state.  As such, Plaintiff fails to explain why Plaintiff did not seek leave to amend earlier.  Further, in its proposed second amended complaint, Plaintiff purports to also add Soccer Center LLC and GS Sports Investments LLC as Defendants.  However, Counsel does not explain why these parties must be added to the action and the bases for doing so in keeping with rule 3.1324.  Accordingly, the Court denies the motion because it lacks evidentiary support.   

Plaintiff is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

Plaintiff Urban Soccer 5 Center LLC (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel further responses from Defendant Lisa Vernola Salas (“Defendant”) to Requests for Production of Documents, set one (“RPD”) and Requests for Admissions, set one (“RFA”).  Defendant opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to requests for production of documents or requests for admissions unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, 2033.290.)

In Defendant’s supplemental responses to RPD Nos. 8, 9, 15, and 16, Defendant states, “After a diligent and reasonable search, Responding Party is unable to comply because the requested documents have been lost or no longer exist and cannot be located.”  Defendant’s response is proper.  If a party cannot respond to an RPD, the responding party must state that “a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand . . . ,” and must “specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)  Defendant states it made a diligent search and reasonable inquiry and it could not produce responsive documents because the documents are either lost or no longer exist.  No further response is warranted.

Defendant’s responses to RPD Nos. 61, 62, and 73 likewise suffice.  Defendant identifies documents it contends are responsive and states, “Otherwise, after a reasonable and diligent search, no other documents have ever existed.”  No further response is necessary.

In response to RPD No. 24, Defendant responded, after asserting objections, “To the extent the documents exist, and [are] within Responding Party's possession, custody and control, [Responding Party] will comply as previously agreed and will produce, labeled as Tabs A-P.”  Defendant thus provided responsive documents after objecting to the RPD, “leaving nothing to address in a motion to compel.”  (American Federation of State, County & Municipal Employees v. Metropolitan Water Dist. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 247, 269.)  For the same reason, Plaintiff cannot move to compel further responses to RPD Nos. 66, 67, 68, 69, 74, and 78, to which Defendant responded, after asserting objections.  The motion to compel further responses to the RPD is denied.

After asserting objections, Defendant provided qualified responses to RFA Nos. 32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 37, 38, 39, and 40.  Defendant’s responses to the RFA must be “as complete and straightforward” as possible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220.)   Defendant denied qualified statements.  Defendant’s responses are proper.  (See St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 781.)  No further responses are warranted.  Therefore, the motion to compel further responses to the RFA is denied.

The Court concludes Plaintiff’s motions are without merit and Plaintiff's filing of them constitutes an abuse of the discovery process.  The Court, therefore, awards sanctions.  The Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and its counsel of record, Michelangelo Tatone, in the amount of $1,800, which represents six hours of attorney time to prepare the oppositions to the motions and to attend the hearing at $300 per hour.  Plaintiff and its counsel of record, jointly and severally, are to pay sanctions in the amount of $1,800 to Defendant, by and through counsel, within 30 days of notice of this ruling.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 7, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

24STCV20340

MOTION: 

Demurrer to Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant F21 OpCo, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff Anne Heiting

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Anne Heiting (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant F21 OpCo, LLC (“Defendant”) for violations of Penal Code section 638.51.  Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s complaint and moves to strike portions of the complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the motions.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s claim under Penal Code section 638.51, subdivision (a), which provides a person may not install or use a trap and trace device without first obtaining a court order.  A “trap and trace device” is a mechanism or process that captures the incoming electronic or other impulses that identify the originating number or other dialing, routing, addressing, or signaling information reasonably likely to identify the source of the communication.  (Pen. Code, § 638.50, subd. (c).)  However, a provider of electronic communication services may use a trap and trace device to operate, maintain, and test its electronic communication service; protect its rights or property; protect users of the service from abuse or unlawful use of service; record the fact a wire or electronic communication was initiated or completed to protect either the provider or user of the service from fraudulent, unlawful, or abusive use of service; or if the provider obtains consent from the user.  (Pen. Code, § 638.51, subd. (b).)

Plaintiff alleges Defendant is a retail company which sells clothing and accessories and operates a website, and Defendant installed software on its website, which enables Defendant to learn the location, source, and identity of consumers who access its website (Complaint, ¶¶ 1-2); TikTok created the software, which gathers information about users of Defendant’s website and compares it to existing data TikTok accumulated about its users (id., ¶¶ 13-14); and the software is “a process to identify the source of electronic communication by capturing incoming electronic impulses and identifying dialing, routing, addressing, and signaling information generated by users, who are never informed that the website is collaborating with the Chinese government to obtain their phone number and other identifying information” (id., ¶ 18).  Plaintiff alleges, therefore, the software is a trap and trace device, which Defendant installed in violation of Penal Code section 638.51.  For pleading purposes, Plaintiff’s allegations suffice.

Both parties rely extensively on federal opinions not from the Supreme Court of the United States, which do not bind this Court.  (Mesler v. Bragg Mgt. Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 299.)  The parties also cite trial court orders.  “Neither federal district court procedures nor decisions are binding on this court.” (Thomas G. Ferruzzo, Inc. v. Superior Court (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 501, 503.)  Likewise, “a written trial court ruling has no precedential value . . . .” (Santa Ana Hospital Medical Center v. Belshe (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 819, 831.)  The Court disregards these citations. 

Defendant argues Penal Code section 638.51 does not apply to Plaintiff’s use of Defendant’s website, but rather only applies “to third parties to a communication.”  (Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Support of Demurrer, p. 12.)  Defendant is incorrect.  By its terms Penal Code section 638.51 applies to any person’s use of a trap and trace device.  (Pen. Code, § 638.50, subd. (c).)  If, as Plaintiff alleges, Defendant’s software identified Plaintiff without Plaintiff’s consent or without a valid reason to do so, Defendant is liable.  

Nonetheless, Defendant argues it is not liable because Defendant used the software at issue “to operate, maintain, and test a wire or electronic communication service . . . .”  (Pen. Code, § 638.51, subd. (b)(1).)  Defendant’s purpose in using the software is an issue of fact, which the Court cannot reach on demurrer.  Defendant must raise this argument through other means, such as an evidentiary motion.

Defendant also argues Penal Code section 638.51 applies only to devices connected to telephone lines.  Again, Defendant is incorrect.  By its terms, the statute applies to “electronic or wire communication service . . . .”  (Pen. Code, § 638.51, subd. (b)) and does not require connection to telephone lines.  “Only when the language of a statute is susceptible to more than one reasonable construction is it appropriate to turn to extrinsic aids, including the legislative history of the measure, to ascertain its meaning.”  (Diamond Multimedia Systems, Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 19 Cal.4th 1036, 1055.)  As Penal Code section 638.51 is unambiguous, the Court need not look outside its text.

Defendant further argues Plaintiff lacks standing because Plaintiff fails to allege she suffered damages.  The Court disagrees because Plaintiff may recover statutory damages, even if Plaintiff did not suffer actual damages.  (Pen. Code, § 637.2, subd. (a).)

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages and attorney fees.  Plaintiff is not entitled to punitive damages on her statutory claim.  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1256.)  Therefore, the Court strikes the prayer for punitive damages without leave to amend.  Likewise, Plaintiff fails to cite any statutory provision under which Plaintiff is entitled to attorney fees.  Thus, the Court strikes the prayer for attorney fees with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint is overruled.

Defendant’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages is granted without leave to amend, while the motion to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for attorney fees is granted with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 20 days of notice of this order. 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
