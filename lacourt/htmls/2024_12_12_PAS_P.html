DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21GDCV00974    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION

I.         INTRODUCTION

             Plaintiffs Futang Ji, Yi-Ching Wu, and Yong Zong (“Plaintiffs”) initiated this action against Defendants Punta Gorda Acquisitions, LLC, and various affiliated entities on July 27, 2021. Plaintiffs filed their operative Fourth Amended Complaint (FrAC) on July 24, 2024, naming as Defendants Punta Gorda Acquisitions, LLC; Punta Gorda Senior Living, LP dba The Palms of Punta Gorda Assisted Living; Madison Realty Equities, LLC; GT Madison Realty, LLC; Gary Langendoen; Grandtag Business Consultants, Ltd.; Jameson Leung; Rene Leung; and Does 1 through 50, inclusive.

The FrAC alleges ten causes of action: (1) Breach of Third-Party Beneficiary Contract; (2) Intentional Misrepresentation; (3) Negligent Misrepresentation; (4) Conspiracy to Convert Property; (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (6) Violation of California Business and Professions Code section 17200; (7) Unjust Enrichment; (8) Conspiracy to Commit Fraud; (9) Fraudulent Transfer; and (10) Aiding and Abetting Fraud.

On November 1, 2024, the Court held a hearing on Defendant Grandtag’s Motion to Quash service of summons and granted the motion. On November 5, 2024, Grandtag provided notice of the Order.  On November 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a motion for reconsideration of the Court’s ruling. On November 27, 2024, Grandtag opposed the motion for reconsideration. No reply has been filed by Plaintiffs.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008(a) provides that “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order . . . [may] make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit¿what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”¿¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(a).) A trial court has discretion with respect to granting a motion for reconsideration.¿ (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court¿(2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212.)¿ 

            “A motion for reconsideration may only be brought if the party moving for reconsideration can offer ‘new or different facts, circumstances, or law which it could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered and produced at the time of the prior motion. (Citations.) A motion for reconsideration will be denied absent a strong showing of diligence.” (Garcia v.¿Hejmadi¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690;¿Forrest v. State of California Dept. Of Corporations¿(2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 183, 202¿disapproved of and overruled on unrelated grounds in¿Shalant¿v.¿Girardi¿(2011) 51 Cal.4th 1164, 1172 (footnote 3);¿New York Times Co. v. Superior Court¿(2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212–213;¿Baldwin v. Home¿Sav.¿of¿America.¿(1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1199.)  

III.      ANALYSIS

Procedural requirement

Plaintiffs’ motion meets the procedural requirements under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008(a). The motion was timely filed on November 15, 2024, within 10 days after service of the Court’s order. Plaintiffs also submitted a declaration from their counsel, Keren E. Gesund, in support of the motion, as required by Section 1008(a), to explain the grounds for reconsideration.

Analysis

Plaintiffs argue that they have presented new or different facts or circumstances that justify reconsideration of the Court’s order granting Grandtag’s motion to quash service of summons. Plaintiffs contend that Grandtag purposefully availed itself of California’s jurisdiction by acting through California-based GT Madison Realty, LLC, and orchestrating fraudulent asset transfers (Motion, p. 8). Plaintiffs also claim that the Court failed to explicitly address their requests for jurisdictional discovery and judicial notice, which they argue warrant reconsideration (Motion, pp. 10–12).

Grandtag opposes, asserting that Plaintiffs’ motion is procedurally deficient under Code of Civil Procedure Section 1008(a) because it fails to identify genuinely new or different facts, circumstances, or law (Opposition, p. 4). Grandtag further argues that Plaintiffs are merely rehashing prior arguments and attempting to relitigate issues already decided in the original ruling (Opposition, p. 6).

Plaintiffs cite Exhibit 6 of their Fourth Amended Complaint to support their claim that Grandtag controlled GT Madison and its California operations (Motion, p. 6). Plaintiffs also cite legal authorities such as SK Trading Internat Co. Ltd. v. Superior Court (2022) to bolster their argument for jurisdiction and highlight fraudulent transfers involving GT ABC, LLC (Motion, p. 8). However, Plaintiffs fail to justify why Exhibit 6, or these authorities were not cited during their opposition to the motion to quash. Grandtag correctly notes that Exhibit 6 was part of Plaintiffs’ own pleading and available at the time of the original motion (Opposition, p. 2). Grandtag also emphasizes that all legal authorities cited by Plaintiffs were published well before the hearing (Opposition, p. 3).

The Court agrees with Grandtag. “[F]acts of which the party seeking reconsideration was aware at the time of the original ruling are not ‘new or different.’” (In re Marriage of Herr (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1468 [citing Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690].) Plaintiffs purported new evidence and arguments do not meet the statutory standard under Section 1008(a). Moreover, Plaintiffs fail to explain why these facts or authorities could not have been presented earlier. 

Plaintiffs also argue that the Court failed to explicitly rule on their request for jurisdictional discovery (Motion, p. 11). However, the November 5, 2024, the order denied the request by granting the motion to quash. Plaintiffs failed to demonstrate how discovery would likely uncover evidence to establish jurisdiction, which is necessary to justify such a request. This omission does not constitute a new or different fact under Section 1008(a).

Finally, Plaintiffs claim that the Court’s lack of an explicit ruling on their late-filed Request for Judicial Notice justifies reconsideration (Motion, pp. 10–11). However, Plaintiffs filed the Request for Judicial Notice on October 28, 2024, only four days before the November 1, 2024, hearing. The Court finds that the untimely filing does not alter the merits of the decision and does not meet the standard for reconsideration.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have failed to meet the requirements of Section 1008(a), including providing genuinely new or different facts, circumstances, or law. Plaintiffs’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

IV.       CONCLUSION

 Plaintiffs’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

Grandtag is to give notice.

Case Number: 22GDCV00119    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT JAE PARK’S MOTION TO STAY CROSS-COMPLAINT


I.         INTRODUCTION

            This matter arises from disputes concerning the management and financial operations of Hyundai of Glendale, LLC. Defendant and Cross-Complainant Jae Park (“Park”), the former General Manager of Hyundai of Glendale, LLC, has been accused by Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Hyundai of Glendale, LLC (“Hyundai Glendale”) and Cross-Complainant Auto Holdings, LLC (“Auto Holdings”) of fraudulent financial practices, including inflating revenues, misrepresenting financial data, and misappropriating funds. 

On March 9, 2022, Hyundai Glendale filed this instant action against Park. On August 15, 2022, Park filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (FACC) against Hyundai Glendale, Auto Holdings, LLC, and John Hurry. On December 19, 2022, Hyundai Glendale filed a Second Amended Complaint (SAC) against Park. On October 15, 2024, Auto Holdings filed a Cross-Complaint against Park, alleging operational misconduct and fraud spanning from September 2016 through March 2022.

On November 14, 2024, Park filed a motion for stay of proceedings, seeking to stay Auto Holdings’ Cross-Complaint in this action pending resolution of a pending Nevada action. Park argues that the claims in both cases overlap significantly and that a stay is necessary to avoid duplicative litigation and inconsistent rulings. On November 27, 2024, Auto Holdings filed an opposition to the motion, contending that its Cross-Complaint includes broader allegations of operational misconduct distinct from the Nevada case. On December 4, 2024, Park filed a reply, reiterating his position.

The Nevada action was filed in Douglas County, Nevada by Auto Holdings, LLC and Investment Services Capital, LLC (“ISC”), a related entity, on June 7, 2022, and subsequently amended on February 9, 2024, to include additional fraud claims. Trial for the Nevada case is currently set for August 11, 2025.

           

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

“Trial courts generally have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of¿justice and to promote judicial efficiency.”¿¿Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo¿(1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.¿ Trial judges have inherent powers to manage and fashion procedures to control litigation to insure the orderly administration of justice.¿¿Cottle v. Superior Court¿(1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1376-79;¿see¿also¿CCP §§ 128(a)(3) and (a)(5).¿

Code of Civil Procedure section 404.5 states the following: “Pending any determination of whether coordination is appropriate, the judge making that determination may stay any action being considered for, or affecting an action being considered for, coordination.”  CCP § 404.5. 

 

California Rules of Court Rule 3.300(a) states the following: 

A pending civil case is related to another pending civil case, or to a civil case that was dismissed with or without prejudice, or to a civil case that was disposed of by judgment, if the cases: 

(1) Involve the same parties and are based on the same or similar claims; 

(2) Arise from the same or substantially identical transactions, incidents, or events requiring the determination of the same or substantially identical questions of law or fact. 

(3) Involve claims against, title to, possession of, or damages to the same property; or 

(4) Are likely for other reasons to require substantial duplication of judicial resources if heard by different judges. 

Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.300(a).

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Park moves to stay the cross-complaint filed by Auto Holdings under Code of Civil Procedure sections 128(a)(8), 187, and 410.30. Park argues that the claims in the cross-complaint are duplicative of those in the pending Nevada action and are designed to harass him by causing unnecessary litigation costs. Park further contends that staying the cross-complaint will avoid conflicts with the Nevada court, which is better suited to adjudicate the issues, given the advanced stage of proceedings in that forum.[1]

Park specifically argues that the Cross-Complaint is duplicative litigation to the Nevada action. Both cases involve allegations that Park fraudulently induced investments by providing false pro formas, inflated expected net profits, and misrepresented profits for Hyundai of Glendale. (Motion, p. 5; Reply, pp. 2–3.) Park highlights that the Nevada complaint already addresses these issues in detail, asserting claims for fraudulent inducement and negligent misrepresentation. (Motion, Exhibit 4, ¶¶ 14–22.) Park further contends that the Nevada court is the appropriate forum for these claims because the Nevada action was filed earlier, involves overlapping discovery, and is already set for trial in August 2025. (Motion, pp. 7–9; Reply, pp. 3–5.)

In opposition, Auto Holdings argues that the Cross-Complaint is broader than the Nevada complaint, encompassing claims of operational fraud from September 2016 through March 2022. Auto Holdings contends that these allegations—such as Park’s inflated financial reports and mismanagement—are distinct from the fraudulent inducement claims in Nevada. (Opposition, p. 4; Exhibit K)

Auto Holdings asserts that its cross-complaint is compulsory under California law because it arises from the same transactions at issue in Park’s own cross-complaint. Auto Holdings claims that staying its cross-complaint would hinder the court’s ability to fully adjudicate the issues raised by Park. (Opposition, pp. 5–6.) Auto Holdings also contends that Park waived his objections to the Cross-Complaint by failing to raise the issue earlier through a demurrer of affirmative defense. (Opposition, p.6) Finally, Auto Holdings argues that a stay would unduly prejudice its ability to litigate operational fraud claims, which are directly related to Park’s allegations. Auto Holdings asserts that proceeding with its cross-complaint is necessary to offset the damages claimed by Park. (Opposition, p. 7.)

Under California law, trial courts have the inherent power to stay proceedings in the interests of justice and to promote judicial efficiency. (Freiberg v. City of Mission Viejo (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1484, 1489.) Courts also have inherent authority to fashion procedures to manage litigation and ensure the orderly administration of justice. (Cottle v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1376–79; see also CCP §§ 128(a)(3), (a)(5).)

Further, under Code of Civil Procedure section 404.5, courts may stay an action when related cases involve overlapping claims or issues affecting judicial coordination. Similarly, California Rules of Court Rule 3.300(a) defines cases as related if they involve the same parties, arise from substantially identical transactions, or present overlapping questions of law or fact. The Court finds these principles applicable here.

The Court reviewed the Cross-Complaint filed by Auto Holdings and the Complaint in the Nevada action and finds that there is substantial overlap in claims and allegations. Specifically, both actions assert that Park made fraudulent financial representations, including inflating revenue figures, misrepresenting financial data, and diverting company funds for personal use. (Motion, p. 5; Reply, pp. 2–3.) Both cases assert claims for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, and breach of fiduciary duty. The Court finds that the allegations in both cases arise from the same transactions and occurrences, spanning the same time frame of 2016–2022, and involve the same parties. (Motion, Exhibits 3 and 4.)

Although the Nevada Complaint emphasizes the pre-investment phase and allegations of fraudulent inducement, while the California Cross-Complaint focuses on operational fraud and misconduct post-investment, these distinctions do not negate the significant factual overlap. Both actions will require determination of substantially identical questions of law and fact, including whether Park's financial representations were fraudulent, whether fiduciary duties were breached, and resulting damages.

Allowing the California Cross-Complaint to proceed while the Nevada case remains pending would waste judicial resources and risk inconsistent rulings. Both cases involve overlapping discovery, and the Nevada action is already set for trial in August 2025. Proceeding in California would unnecessarily duplicate efforts already undertaken in Nevada, further complicating litigation.  Furthermore, as noted above, the earliest trial date in this matter would be summer 2026, ample time for the Nevada matter to resolve.  Staying the California Cross-Complaint would not prejudice Auto Holdings. The allegations in both cases are substantially similar, and resolution of the Nevada action would likely narrow the issues for adjudication in this forum. Any remaining claims can be addressed in California after the Nevada case concludes.

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that the claims in the California Cross-Complaint substantially overlap with those pending in the Nevada action. Resolution of the Nevada case will materially affect and potentially narrow the issues in this case. Judicial efficiency, consistency in rulings, and fairness to the parties all favor granting Defendant Park’s motion to stay the Cross-Complaint filed by Auto Holdings.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Park’s motion to stay the Cross-Complaint pending resolution of the Nevada case.

 

            IV.       CONCLUSION

The Court GRANTS Park’s Motion for a Stay of Proceedings.

The Parties are directed to provide a joint status report after any final judgment in the Nevada case.

Park is to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV02300    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: P




Case Number: 23AHCV02639    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

I.         INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Jing Yun Xu (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendants Kuo Hua Ma (“Ma”) and Anita J. Hsiao (“Hsiao”), asserting causes of action for General Negligence and Premises Liability. The claims arise from an alleged assault and battery that occurred on May 8, 2022, at a commercial property located at 9415-9417 Las Tunas Drive in Temple City, California, where Plaintiff operates a hair salon. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Ma, who worked at an adjacent business, assaulted her during a confrontation over parking, causing serious bodily injuries.

On November 9, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this action. On February 15, 2024, Defendant Hsiao filed a demurrer, and on June 10, 2024, the Court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend.

Subsequently, On June 11, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) against Defendant Hsiao and Ma, asserting claims for General Negligence and Premises Liability. On July 15, 2024, Hsiao filed the instant demurrer. This demurrer is unopposed.

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Defendant Hsiao’s counsel, David D. Samani, declares: "On and before July 10, 2024, my office communicated with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the foregoing Demurrer. As of the filing of this Demurrer, we were unable to informally resolve this dispute." (Samani Decl., ¶ 2.) Accordingly, the Court finds that the meet-and-confer requirement has been satisfied.

The Court finds that Defendant has complied with the meet and confer requirement.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Pursuant to CCP § 335.1, an action for injury to an individual cause by the neglect of another must be filed within two years. 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendant Hsiao demurs to the FAC arguing that the causes of action for General Negligence and Premises Liability fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e). Defendant contends that the FAC does not adequately plead heightened foreseeability of criminal conduct or proximate causation necessary to impose liability on a landlord.

First cause of action: Negligence

A negligence cause of action requires duty, breach of the duty, causation, and damages. (Antiglio v. Corning, Inc. (1998) Cal.4th 604,614.) “A landlord generally owes a tenant the duty, arising out of their special relationship, to take reasonable measures to secure areas under the landlord’s control against foreseeable criminal acts of third parties.” (Castaneda v. Olsher (2007) 41 Cal.4th 1205, 1213, internal citations omitted.) 

Defendant Hsiao demurs to this cause of action, arguing that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a claim for negligence. Specifically, Hsiao contends that the FAC lacks factual allegations demonstrating heightened foreseeability of the criminal act or causation linking Hsiao’s actions to the alleged harm. 

The Court agrees with Defendant Hsiao. Here, the FAC alleges that Defendant “knew or should have known” of co-defendant Ma’s violent tendencies; however, Plaintiff fails to plead specific facts supporting this statement. Conclusory assertions of knowledge without factual detail are insufficient to establish foreseeability. The FAC does not provide allegations of prior incidents, threats, or conduct by Ma that would have placed Defendant Hsiao on notice of a heightened risk of violence.

Moreover, the FAC does not adequately plead that Defendant Hsiao owed a duty of care or that her actions or omissions proximately caused the assault. Plaintiff’s allegations describe an abrupt, unexpected attack, without any connection to the property’s condition or Defendant’s conduct. Without specific factual allegations demonstrating foreseeability and causation, the negligence claim cannot proceed.

Given that this is Plaintiff’s second attempt to plead a negligence claim, and no additional facts have been alleged to implicate Defendant Hsiao, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to this cause of action without leave to amend.

Second cause of action: Premise Liability

Premises Liability is a special form of negligence alleged as to property owners. “To impose liability for injuries suffered by an invitee due to the defective condition of the premises, the owner or occupier ‘must have either actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition or have been able by the exercise of ordinary care to discover the condition, which if known to him, he should realize as involving an unreasonable risk to invitees on his premises.”(Girvetz v. Boys’ Market (1949) 91 Cal.App.2d 827, 829-830.)

The FAC alleges that Hsiao “knew or should have known that Defendant Kuo Hua Ma had violent tendencies, such that the assault on Plaintiff was foreseeable. As a result, Plaintiff Jing Yun Xu was assaulted by Defendant Kuo Hua Ma, thereby incurring multiple bodily injuries. Defendant Anita J. Hsiao's conduct as a landlord was the proximate cause of the assault on Plaintiff Jing Yun Xu.” (FAC, p. 5.)

The Court finds that the FAC fails to allege sufficient facts to support a claim for premises liability. While the FAC asserts that Defendant Hsiao “knew or should have known” of Ma’s violent tendencies, it does not provide any specific factual allegations establishing actual or constructive knowledge of such tendencies. The conclusory assertion of knowledge is insufficient to demonstrate that Defendant Hsiao was aware of a dangerous condition on the premises or that she should have discovered such a condition through ordinary care. The FAC lacks allegations of prior incidents, complaints, or other facts that would have alerted Defendant Hsiao to the alleged risk posed by Ma.

Additionally, the FAC does not identify a specific dangerous condition on the property that created an unreasonable risk of harm to Plaintiff. The allegations describe an intentional act of violence by a third party, Ma, rather than a defect or hazardous condition on the premises that Hsiao could have remedied. 

As discussed above, this is Plaintiff’s second attempt to plead this claim, and no new facts have been alleged to cure the deficiencies previously identified. Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as to this cause of action without leave to amend.

 

IV.       ORDER

            The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend.

            Defendant is to give notice.
