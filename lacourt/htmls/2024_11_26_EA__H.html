DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19PSCV00643    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: H

Rizvi, et al. v. Diligent Lending, LLC, et al., Case No. 19PSCV00643

 

The Court is inclined to set an evidentiary hearing pursuant to Paragraph 3.3 of the Settlement Agreement, for February 7, 2025, at 10:00 a.m.

 

Background 

Plaintiffs Syed M. Hammad Rizvi (“Rizvi”), Afsah Moinuddin (“Moinuddin”) and Eagle Ridge Property Management, LLC (“Eagle Ridge”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

          Rizvi and Moinuddin are married and formed Eagle Ridge to hold title to their primary residence located at 10068 Eagle Ridge Court, Rancho Cucamonga, CA (“Eagle Ridge Residence”). In March 2018, Rizvi met Vincente Cuevas (“V. Cuevas”) outside of a partially completed home located on a 1.24-acre lot at 1533 Calle Cristina, San Dimas, California (“the Cristina Property”). V. Cuevas represented that he was the owner and builder of the Cristina Property. After Rizvi consulted with his banker and informed V. Cuevas that he was unable to purchase the Cristina Property, V. Cuevas advised Rizvi that he could help Rizvi structure the purchase and arrange a loan so he could afford to buy it. 

          V. Cuevas subsequently introduced Rizvi to Kevin Cornell (“Cornell”) of KWC Realty Services, Inc. dba Priority One Mortgage (“Priority One”).  In April 2018, Cornell told Rizvi that he could obtain a purchase and construction loan to finish the construction of the home and that there would be at least one year of reserve funds (later modified to six months) to pay for the construction and monthly interest payments while the construction was being completed. V. Cuevas also promised Rizvi that he would refer $1.2 million of the purchase price for at least 5 years. 

          Rizvi decided to purchase the Cristina Property and engaged Cornell and Priority One to assist as the mortgage broker. Cornell advised Rizvi that he would help fill out the salient information in the Uniform Residential Loan Application (“Loan Application”) and told Rizvi to ignore the asset, liability, or income sections on the application. The Loan Application Cornell initially sent to Rizvi was partial only and indicated that the loan was for the purchase of a primary residence. Cornell also advised Rizvi that Plaintiffs should take title to the Cristina Property under Eagle Ridge. Plaintiffs signed the purchase agreement on April 19, 2018. 

          On May 9, 2018, Cornell provided Rizvi with a completed Loan Application, which modified the purpose of the property from “primary residence” to “investment.” On June 18, 2018, Plaintiffs executed a promissory note with Diligent Lending, a hard money lender, for the principal sum of $2,699,000.00. Per the terms of the promissory note, Plaintiffs were to make interest only payments of $25,033.15 per month, with an interest rate of 10.99%, and a 28.99% default interest rate (“Note”). A deed of trust in the principal amount of the Note was recorded. 

          On May 28, 2018, Cornell sent a short form deed of trust to Rizvi and told him to sign and return it. A deed of trust in the amount of $940,000.00 was then recorded in second position in favor of Cuevas and Jesus Anthony Rubio (identified therein as “Tony Rubio;” hereinafter, “Rubio”) which references a note, even though no such note exists; although Plaintiffs executed a note for $2,040,000.00 in favor of V. Cuevas and Rubio, Cornell and V. Cuevas told Plaintiffs that this $2,040,000.00 reflected the amount owed to Rubio by V. Cuevas previously secured by the Cristina Property in first position as well as V. Cuevas’s equity in the Cristina Property that he was willing to forego for at least five years. The $2,040,000.00 note is not reflected in the escrow instructions or closing statement but indicates that it is secured by a deed of trust on the Cristina Property. Cornell, Priority One, and Diligent Lending purposefully and fraudulently characterized the Note as a business purpose loan. 

          Shortly after escrow closed, on August 23, 2018, Rizvi received a notice from Diligent Lending demanding that he start making monthly interest payments of $25,033.15 on the Note. Rizvi asked Diligent Lending about the 6 months interest reserve that was supposedly built into the loan but was told by Cornell and Diligent Lending that there were no reserve funds. Plaintiffs had to refinance the Eagle Ridge Residence and get equity out to save the Cristina Property. Now that Plaintiffs had to use the equity in the Eagle Ridge Residence to maintain the Note payments, though, Plaintiffs did not have sufficient funds for a down payment to refinance. Plaintiffs have since defaulted on their obligations under the Note. 

On July 18, 2019, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Diligent Lending, Priority One, Cornell and Does 1-100 for:

          1.       Fraud—Intentional Misrepresentation,

          2.       Breach of Fiduciary Duty,

          3.       Negligence,

          4.       Unfair Business Practices in Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200,

          5.       Unjust Enrichment, and

          6.       Common Count: Money Had and Received. 

On December 6, 2019, the Court granted Diligent Lending’s petition to compel arbitration as to Plaintiffs and ordered the matter stayed as to Plaintiffs and Diligent Lending only. 

On January 24, 2020, V. Cuevas filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against Rizvi, Diligent Lending, Priority One and Moes 1-50 for:

          1.       Breach of Contract,

          2.       Comparative Negligence, and

          3.       Contribution. 

On January 29, 2020, Plaintiffs dismissed Diligent Lending, without prejudice. On March 11, 2020, V. Cuevas dismissed Rizvi, without prejudice. 

On June 8, 2020, Case Nos. 19PSCV00643 and 19PSCV00656 were deemed related; Case No. 19PSCV00656 was designated as the lead case. 

On October 20, 2020, Priority One and Cornell filed a First Amended Cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against Andrew A. Dioli (“Dioli”), FMC Lending Inc. (“FMC”), Bayshore Advisors, Inc. (“Bayshore”), Rushmyfile.Inc. (“Rushmyfile”), V. Cuevas, Vince Cuevas, Shahid Iqbal, and Roes 1-10 for:

          1.       Indemnity,

          2.       Contribution, and

          3.       Declaratory Relief. 


On December 29, 2020, Dioli, FMC, Bayshore, and Rushmyfile filed a cross-complaint, asserting causes of action against Priority One, Cornell, and Moes 1-10 for:

          1.       Fraud,

          2.       Negligence,

          3.       Indemnity,

          4.       Contribution,

          5.       Declaratory Relief, and

          6.       Breach of Implied in Fact Contract.

On June 7, 2021, the Court dismissed Priority One and Diligent Lending from V. Cuevas’ cross-complaint.

On January 16, 2024, the Court dismissed Dioli, et al.’s cross-complaint and struck FMC, Bayshore, and Rushmyfile’s answer.

On May 9, 2024, a “Stipulation Re Settlement” was filed. On August 23, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an unconditional “Notice of Settlement of Entire Case.”

On August 29, 2024, a “Stipulation to Dismiss and Request that the Court Retain Jurisdiction Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 664.6 [and] Order” was entered.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s motion to enforce settlement. For the reasons set forth herein, the Court is inclined to set an evidentiary hearing pursuant to Paragraph 3.3 of the settlement agreement.

 

Legal Standard

“If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside of the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).)


Discussion

Plaintiffs move the Court for an order, per Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6, entering judgment against Priority One and Cornell in the principal amount of $299,250.00, or in the alternative in the principal amount of $52,250.00, and request that the Court hold an evidentiary hearing and receive evidence for the purpose of granting an order enforcing the terms of the Settlement Agreement and Release (“Agreement”) between the parties, and finding that Priority One and Cornell made material misrepresentations in the Agreement pursuant to Paragraph 3.4 of the Agreement.

 

Evidentiary Objections

At the outset, the Court addresses Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Christopher Hook. The Court sustains the objections as to Paragraph 7 and overrules the objection as to the second sentence of Paragraph 11.

 

Merits

On August 22, 2024, Plaintiffs, Priority One, and Cornell entered into the Agreement, wherein Priority One and Cornell agreed to pay Plaintiffs $53,000.00 in exchange for a dismissal without prejudice, with the Court reserving jurisdiction under section 664.6 to enforce the terms of the Agreement. (Rizvi Decl., ¶ 12, Exh. 4; Chamberlin Decl., ¶ 4.) The Agreement provides, in relevant part, as follows:

          “2. Payment to Rizvi. In exchange for the releases and promises set forth below, Cornell agrees to pay the total sum of fifty-three thousand dollars ($53,000.00) to Rizvi to settle the Action (the ‘Settlement Amount’) as follows:

          2.1 Beginning on September 1st, 2024, the payment of the Settlement Amount shall be paid to Rizvi each month in two installments of $750 on the 1st and 15th of each month until the Settlement Amount is paid in full. The last payment, due on July 1st, 2027, will be in the amount of $500.00 to complete the payment of the Settlement Amount. . .

          2.4 Cornell will have a five (5) day grace period. If the five (5) day grace period is exceeded, Rizi [sic] may enter judgment in the amount of $53,000 minus amounts actually paid and Cornel [sic] stipulates to the entry of that judgment.” (Id., ¶ 2.)

The Agreement additionally provides, in relevant part, as follows:

 

          “3. Financial Documents. Cornell represents that, other than the Settlement Amount, he is financially unable to respond to a judgment or greater settlement payment. Rizvi is relying on this representation in deciding to enter into this Agreement. To substantiate his representation, Cornell shall transmit to Rizvi’s counsel the most recent personal and business tax returns, one-year of personal and business bank statements, and documents sufficient to show the deposition of the proceeds of the 2022 loan for $2.7 million against Cornell’s house (the ‘Financial Documents’).

 

          3.1     Cornell will sign a declaration, under the penalty of perjury, attesting to the completeness and accuracy of the Financial Documents submitted to Rizvi’s counsel.

          3.2     Cornell will deliver the Financial Documents to Rizvi’s counsel within thirty (30) days after the execution of the final Settlement Agreement. . .

          3.3     If it is shown by Rizvi, through an evidentiary hearing, that Cornell has made material misrepresentations in this Agreement, in the Financial Documents or in his declaration, Rizvi may move the Court to enter judgment against Cornell in the amount of $300,000 less amounts actually paid under this Agreement and Cornell stipulates to that judgment. The amount of $300,000 represents an actual loss sustained by Rizvi in connection with the Transaction alleged in the Action and is not punitive.” (Id., ¶ 3).

Plaintiff’s counsel Bekah Chamberlin (“Chamberlin”) represents as follows:

          On September 9, 2024, Chamberlin sent an email to Priority One and Cornell’s counsel regarding the delinquent first payment of $750.00, which was due on September 1, 2024, with the reminder that the second payment was coming due on September 15, 2024. (Chamberlin Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 6, pp. 1-2.) On September 16, 2024, Priority One and Cornell’s counsel responded with confirmation from Priority One and Cornell that the first payment was sent on September 13, 2024. (Id.) On September 23, 2024, Chamberlin sent an email to Priority One and Cornell’s counsel regarding the delinquent second payment of $750.00 and the financial documents which were due that day, but Priority One and Cornell’s counsel never responded. (Id., ¶ 7, Exh. 6, p. 1.) Priority One and Cornell have failed to provide any financial documents or a verifying declaration. (Id.)

Plaintiff confirms that he has received only one payment of $750.00 on September 13, 2024, as of the October 11, 2024 filing date of the motion. (Rizvi Decl., ¶ 14.)

Priority One and Cornell, in response, oppose the motion to the extent that it seeks entry of judgment against them in the amount of $299,250.00, rather than the $53,000.00 sum agreed to in the Agreement. Curiously, Priority One and Cornell’s opposition is not accompanied by a declaration from Cornell himself.

Priority One and Cornell’s counsel, Christopher Hook (“Hook”), attests that he attempted to send Plaintiff’s counsel the agreed upon financial documents and declaration and believed that it had been transmitted on that date, but learned for the first time on October 11, 2024, when he received the instant motion, that his September 23, 2024 email had not gone through. (Hook Decl., ¶ 9.) Hook represents that he immediately re-sent these documents to Plaintiff’s counsel by emailing them a Dropbox link to the documents. (Id., ¶ 10, Exh. B.)

Plaintiff, in turn, points out that Priority One and Cornell did not provide all of the documents set forth in the Agreement (including personal bank statements and documents showing the disposition of the $251,760.94 proceeds of the 2022 loan against Cornell’s house) and did not include a declaration under penalty of perjury attesting to the completeness and accuracy of the documents.

Under the circumstances presented above, the Court is inclined to set an evidentiary hearing pursuant to Paragraph 3.3 of the Agreement.   

Case Number: 23PSCP00315    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: H

Swift Financial, LLC v. LokiTap LLC, et al., Case No. 23PSCP00315

 

ORDER ON PETITIONER’S MOTION FOR ASSIGNMENT ORDER

Swift Financial, LLC as Servicing Agent for WebBank’s Motion for Assignment Order is GRANTED.


Background

On July 13, 2022, Petitioner Swift Financial, LLC (as Servicing Agent for WebBank) (“Petitioner”) filed a “Petition to Confirm Contractual Arbitration Award” (“Petition”) as against Respondents LokiTap LLC (“LokiTap”), Andrei Mirzoian (“Mirzoian”), and Does 1-10. On July 25, 2023, Petitioner filed a “Notice of Motion and Motion on Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award.”

On September 29, 2023, the Court entered “Judgment on Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award.” On December 21, 2023, Plaintiff filed (and served via mail) a “Notice of Entry of Judgment.”

On December 22, 2023, an abstract of judgment was issued. On January 24, 2024 and March 26, 2024, writs of execution were issued.

Pending before the Court is Petitioner’s motion for an order instructing LokiTap and Mirzoian (together, “Judgment Debtors”) to assign their interests in any and all monies, proceeds, receivables, and payables received by them from Apple, to Petitioner, to the extent necessary to pay Petitioner’s judgment.

 

Legal Standard

“Except as otherwise provided by law, upon application of the judgment creditor on noticed motion, the court may order the judgment debtor to assign to the judgment creditor . . . all or part of a right to payment due or to become due, whether or not the right is conditioned on future developments, including but not limited to the following types of payments:

(1) Wages due from the federal government that are not subject to withholding under an earnings withholding order.

(2) Rents.

(3) Commissions.

(4) Royalties.

(5) Payments due from a patent or copyright.

(6) Insurance policy loan value.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (a).)

“The notice of the motion shall be served on the judgment debtor. Service shall be made personally or by mail.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (b).)

“Subject to subdivisions (d), (e), and (f), in determining whether to order an assignment of the amount of an assignment pursuant to subdivision (a), the court may take into consideration all relevant factors, including the following:

(1) The reasonable requirements of a judgment debtor who is a natural person and of persons supported in whole or in part by the judgment debtor.

(2) Payments the judgment debtor is required to make or that are deducted in satisfaction of other judgments and wage assignments, including earnings assignment orders for support.

(3) The amount remaining due on the money judgment.

(4) The amount being or to be received in satisfaction of the right to payment that may be assigned.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (c).)


“A right to payment may be assigned pursuant to this article only to the extent necessary to satisfy the money judgment.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (d).)

“When earnings or periodic payments pursuant to a pension or retirement plan are assigned pursuant to subdivision (a), the amount of the earnings or the periodic payments assigned shall not exceed the amount that may be withheld from a like amount of earnings under Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 706.010) (Wage Garnishment Law).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (e).)

“Where a specific amount of the payment or payments to be assigned is exempt by another statutory provision, the amount of the payment or payments to be assigned pursuant to subdivision (a) shall not exceed the amount by which the payment or payments exceed the exempt amount.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.510, subd. (f).)


Discussion

Petitioner moves the Court for an order instructing “Judgment Debtors” to assign their interests in any and all monies, proceeds, receivables, and payables received by them from Apple, to Petitioner, to the extent necessary to pay Judgment Creditor’s judgment on full, including accrued interest through the date of payment.

In support of Petitioner’s motion, Petitioner’s counsel Darren Schlecter (“Schlecter”) represents as follows:

          Judgment was entered on September 29, 2023 in this case in favor of Petitioner and against Judgment Debtors, jointly and severally, in the amount of $138,937.23. (Schlecter Decl., ¶ 2.) Petitioner received one payment in the amount of $1,255.42 through a previous levy conducted on or about June 4, 2024, which was applied first to interest on the judgment. (Id., ¶ 3.) There is now due and owing the amount of $153,821.38 through November 26, 2024; interest accrues from the date of entry of judgment and post-judgment costs incurred at the daily rate of $38.07/day. (Id.) The judgment is final and enforcement thereof is not stayed. (Id., ¶ 4.)

          On January 31, 2024, a subpoena was directed to and served on Apple (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. A); in response thereto, Apple’s local counsel confirmed that she would cooperate in achieving a stipulated assignment order (Id., ¶ 7.) Petitioner’s counsel has been in regular communication with Apple’s local counsel since February 2024 regarding seeking an assignment. (Id., ¶ 13.) The instant motion was brought after the Court rejected a stipulated confidentiality order to address Apple’s concerns in early June 2024 and efforts to attempt to reach a consensual assignment with Apple also failed. (Id., ¶ 8.)

          Petitioner’s counsel is informed that Judgment Debtors’ business appears to still be operating and that they have several apps, including but not limited to, Pentix Tetris, Pentix APK, and Pentix Tournament. (Id., ¶¶ 10-12, Exhs. B and C.) He is informed and believes that the following right to payment(s) is/are or will become due to Judgment Debtors: “Any and all rights to payments, royalties, compensation from Apple due to Judgment Debtors Andrei Mirzoian and/or Lokitap, LLC for any and all applications on Apple’s iTunes Store, including but not limited to Pentix Tetris, Pentix APK and Pentix Tournament.” (Id., ¶ 5.) Petitioner’s counsel is also informed and believes that the right to payment(s) sought to be assigned herein has not been previously assigned. (Id., ¶ 9.)

The unopposed motion is granted.   

Case Number: 24PSCV00619    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: H

Jayewardene v. Mercury Insurance Company LLC, et al., Case No. 24PSCV00619

ORDER ON DEMURRERS AND MOTIONS TO STRIKE 

1.       Defendant Maral Chorbajian’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED. 

2.       Defendants Abraham Chorbajian, Eli Chorbajian, and Maral Chorbajian’s Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is GRANTED. 

3.       Defendants Mercury Insurance Company LLC and Mercury Insurance Services’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED. The Court will hear from Plaintiff as to whether leave to amend is requested and if so, will require an offer of proof. 

4.       Defendants Mercury Insurance Company LLC and Mercury Insurance Services’ Motion to Strike Punitive Damages from Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is DENIED as MOOT. 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Sean Jayewardene (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he sustained injuries and damages in a June 12, 2023 motor vehicle accident.

On April 22, 2-24, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC” or the “Operative Complaint”), asserting a motor vehicle cause of action against Mercury Insurance Company and Mercury Insurance Services (sued as “Mercury Insurance”) (together, “Mercury”), Abraham Chorbajian (“A. Chorbajian”), Eli Chorbajian (“E. Chorbajian”), and Maral Chorbajian (“M. Chorbajian”) (together, the “Chorbajians”). 

Pending before the Court are: (1) the Chorbajians’ demurrer and motion to strike and (2) Mercury’s demurrer and motion to strike. A Case Management Conference is set for October 1, 2024.

II.      Legal Standards

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) 

 “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.) 

III.     Discussion

A.       The Chorbajians’ Demurrer

The Chorbajians demur, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Mercury or M. Chorbajian.

At the outset, the Chorbajians may not demur on behalf of Mercury, nor may E. Chorbajian and A. Chorbajian demur on behalf of M. Chorbajian. The Court’s analysis is limited to M. Chorbajian only as the moving party.

M. Chorbajian argues that she is an improper party because she “is neither the driver nor the owner of the defendant’s vehicle.” (Motion, 2:13-15.) However, this contention is extrinsic to the face of the Operative Complaint. Plaintiff has identified M. Chorbajian as a “defendant[] who operated a motor vehicle.” (FAC, ¶ MV-2.) Accordingly, the Court overrules M. Chorbajian’s demurrer. 

          B.       The Chorbajian’s Motion to Strike

The Chorbajians move the Court for an order striking out Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages (i.e., FAC, ¶ 14(a)(2).).

Punitive damages may be awarded in an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract upon clear and convincing evidence that a defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)

“Malice” is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) “Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(2).) “Fraud” is defined as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (c)(3).)

A “conclusory characterization of defendant’s conduct as intentional, willful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of ‘oppression, fraud, or malice. . .’” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.)

The Operative Complaint is devoid of any factual allegations showing that the Chorbajians’ conduct was malicious, oppressive and/or fraudulent to support a claim for punitive damages. The motion is granted.

C.       Mercury’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike

Mercury demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against it.

Mercury’s demurrer is sustained. The Operative Complaint is devoid of any factual allegations as against Mercury.

In light of the ruling on the demurrer, the Court denies as moot Mercury’s motion to strike punitive damages from the Operative Complaint.
