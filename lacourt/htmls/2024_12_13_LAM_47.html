DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV41132    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     December 13, 2024                TRIAL DATE: January 21, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         Dermot Damian Givens v. Formosa Gardens HOA et al..

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV41132

 

(1)   MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

(2)   MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION (x2)

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1) Plaintiff Dermot Damian Givens. (2) Defendants Formosa Gardens HOA, Alexsei Durack, & Kevin Khuat;

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1) Defendants Formosa Gardens HOA, Alexsei Durack, & Kevin Khuat. (2) Plaintiff Dermot Damian Givens.

 

CASE HISTORY:

  • 11/08/21: Complaint filed.
  • 05/03/22: First Amended Complaint filed.
  • 06/13/22: Cross-Complaint filed.
  • 08/12/22: First Amended Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for fraud and enforcement of governing documents for a homeowner’s association under the Davis-Stirling Act. Plaintiff sued Formosa Gardens HOA (“Defendant HOA”) and Alexsei Durack and Kevin Ngoc Khuat (“Individual Defendants”) alleging breach of governing documents, multiple violations of the Davis-Stirling Act (Civ. Code § 5200 et seq.), fraud, theft in violation of Penal Code section 496, conversion, intentional interference with contractual relations, and declaratory relief.

 

            The parties filed cross-motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication.  

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is GRANTED IN PART as to the first through seventh and eleventh causes of action and otherwise DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary judgment.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, a plaintiff moving for summary judgment satisfies the initial burden to show that there is no defense to a claim by proving each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(1).) Similarly, the cross-defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) In both cases, Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a movant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the moving party has met its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(1)-(2).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

Defective Notice of Motion and Separate Statement

 

            Although Plaintiff purports to move for summary adjudication as an alternative to summary judgment, Plaintiff’s Notice of Motion does not separately identify the causes of action on which summary adjudication is sought. (See Cal. Rules of Court Rule. 3.1350(b).) Consequently, Plaintiff’s separate statement does repeat these issues for summary adjudication, verbatim, nor does it separately identify the facts which are material with respect to each cause of action. (Id. Rule 3.1350(b); (d).) The failure to provide a separate statement is, in the Court’s discretion, grounds for denial of the motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(b)(1).) Moreover, appellate precedent has consistently encouraged trial courts to require strict compliance with the requirements for separate statements. (United Community Church v. Garcin (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 327, 335 [superseded by statute on other grounds]; North Coast Business Park v. Nielsen Construction Co. (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 22, 30-32.)

 

Owing to the deficiency in the Notice of Motion, Plaintiff has failed to give Defendants proper notice of the issues at hand for summary adjudication, although the motion does give valid notice of intent to move for summary judgment. Thus, even if the Court were to overlook the defective Separate Statement, the Court could consider this motion as a motion for summary judgment only. For the reasons stated below, however, Plaintiff’s motion must also be denied on the merits.

Analysis

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary judgment against the HOA Defendant on the grounds that the HOA Defendant does not have standing to defend against this suit. The Davis-Stirling Common Interest Development Act, codified at Civil Code section 4000 et seq., governs common interest developments in California, including, inter alia, the establishment and conduct of homeowners’ associations. Section 5980 of the Act bestows standing to sue and be sued on homeowners’ associations. (Civ. Code § 5980.) Plaintiff asserts that the HOA Defendant does not have standing to defend against this action because it has not complied with the other requirements of the Act. Plaintiff does not specify what requirements have been violated, does not offer any evidence tending to establish any violation, and does not set forth any authority for the position that a violation of the Act deprives a homeowners’ association of standing to sue and be sued. Mere assertions are not sufficient to carry Plaintiff’s burden on summary judgment. Consequently, the burden of proof does not shift to Defendant to demonstrate a triable issue of fact, and Plaintiff is not entitled to summary judgment.

 

Conclusion

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition by a plaintiff is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a defendant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

First Cause of Action: Breach of Governing Documents

 

            Defendants move for summary adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of governing documents.

            As alleged in the operative First Amended Complaint, Plaintiff owned a condominium at 1628 North Formosa Avenue, Unit B, Los Angeles CA 90046. (FAC ¶ 1.) The individual Defendants Durack and Khuat owned Units A and E in the same development, respectively. (FAC ¶¶ 3-4.) The development is managed by a Homeowner’s Association, whose establishing Declaration of Covenants, Conditions, and Restrictions and governing Bylaws are provided in the pleadings. (FAC ¶¶ 15-16; Exhs. A-B.) According to Plaintiff, the HOA has largely ignored its obligations and is effectively defunct. (FAC ¶¶ 18-32.) Plaintiff alleges that he purchased Unit B in 2004 and sold the property in 2008. (FAC ¶ 47-48.) After he originally sold the Unit, Plaintiff alleges that the new owner failed to pay HOA dues, resulting in liens being asserted against the property until the new owner was foreclosed upon in August 2017. (FAC ¶¶ 49-51.) The foreclosure purchaser, Wells Fargo, allegedly did not pay HOA assessments during its ownership and transferred title to HJK & JJL, LLC in October 2019. (FAC ¶ 53.)

 

In the meantime, Plaintiff alleges that, following a lawsuit brought by Defendant Durack, a new election of the HOA Board of Directors was conducted, naming Durack and Khuat the sole Board members in May 2019. (FAC ¶ 38.) Plaintiff alleges the election was illegitimate. (FAC ¶ 39.) Plaintiff contends the new HOA Board did not record a lien against the property for Wells Fargo’s unpaid assessments, but rather secured HJK & JJL’s assent to a hold-harmless agreement to pay Wells Fargo’s assessments. (FAC ¶ 56.) Nevertheless, the assessments went unpaid. (Id.) Plaintiff reacquired the property on June 23, 2020, and, shortly thereafter, the new Board of Directors demanded payment for an assessment lien totaling $15,894, which Plaintiff allegedly paid. (FAC ¶¶ 57-63.) This lawsuit followed.

 

Plaintiff’s first cause of action alleges that Formosa Gardens HOA has violated numerous provisions of the CC&Rs and Bylaws, including, inter alia, having an insufficient number of directors, failing to conduct regular meetings and maintain minutes and financial statements, failing to require owners to pay assessments due and payable, and issuing further assessments without notice nor a vote of the members. (See FAC ¶¶ 67-87.) Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot maintain this cause of action because he is no longer an owner of property within the development. Under section 5975 of the Davis-Stirling Act, an association’s covenants and restrictions “may be enforced by any owner of a separate interest or by the association, or by both. (Civ. Code § 5975(a).) As the Court of Appeal has stated, “[o]ne who no longer owns land in a development subject to reciprocal restrictions cannot enforce them, absent showing the original covenant parties intended to allow enforcement by one who is not a landowner.” (Farber v. Bay View Terrace Homeowners Assn. (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1007, 1011, citing B.C.E. Development, Inc. v. Smith (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 1142, 1147-48.) According to the evidence presented by Defendants, Plaintiff was foreclosed upon with respect to his original unit, Unit B, in July 2023. (Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Fact No. 12.) Moreover, although Plaintiff was granted equitable title to Unit A subject to his ability to complete the terms of a land sale contract in a separate action, he did not comply with those terms. (SSUMF No. 14.) An order for Plaintiff’s ejectment from Unit A was issued on October 24, 2024. (Order for Ejectment filed October 24, 2024, Case No. 22STCV20402.) Thus, Defendants contend Plaintiff’s ownership interest has terminated, depriving him of standing to enforce the governing documents. As Defendants have offered evidence tending to support this conclusion, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate a triable issue of fact either as to his ownership or as to the intent of the covenanters to allow non-owners to enforce the governing documents.

 

Plaintiff’s opposition asserts that Defendant “cites no law” that a subsequent change of ownership terminates Plaintiff’s right to maintain this action and baselessly asserts that the Court has previously addressed this issue. Although Farber does not directly address the consequence of a transfer of ownership with respect to ongoing litigation, the language of the rule quoted above appears to address that circumstance by its plain language. Moreover, Plaintiff offers no evidence that he has maintained an ownership interest or that the governing documents contemplate their enforcement by one who is not an owner. Plaintiff has entirely failed to carry his burden to demonstrate a triable issue of fact, and Defendants are therefore entitled to summary adjudication of this cause of action.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the first cause of action is GRANTED.  

 

Second Through Seventh Causes of Action: Violation of Civil Code Sections 5205; 5300; 5310; 5500; 5616; and 5806

 

            Defendants move for summary adjudication of the second through seventh causes of action for violation of Civil Code sections 5205; 5300; 5310; 5500; 5616; and 5806, arguing that Plaintiff does not have standing to enforce these provisions because he is no longer an owner. As Defendants state, only owners have standing to seek redress for violations of the Davis-Stirling Act. (Martin v. Bridgeport Community Assn. Inc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1024, 1037 [tenant does not have standing to enforce Davis-Stirling Act because the Act conveys rights upon owners].) Thus, the same evidence which tends to establish that Plaintiff cannot prevail on the first cause of action carries that same burden for these claims. (See SSUMF Nos. 17, 22; Order for Ejectment filed October 24, 2024, Case No. 22STCV20402.) The burden of proof therefore shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate a triable issue of fact.

 

            As Plaintiff offers the same conclusory, unsupported assertions in defense of these causes of action as with the first cause of action, Plaintiff does not offer evidence demonstrating a triable issue of fact in this respect. Defendants are therefore entitled to summary adjudication of these causes of action.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the second through seventh causes of action is GRANTED.

Eighth Cause of Action: Fraud

 

            Defendants move for summary adjudication of the eighth cause of action for fraud.

 

“The elements of fraud that will lead to a tort action are: (a) misrepresentation; (b) knowledge of falsity; (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage. (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant[s] to understand fully the nature of the charge made. (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.) “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Ibid.) “[G]eneral and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that he was defrauded by the individual Defendants into paying $15,894 in past due assessments which were not actually owed based on their false representations that they were the legitimately elected Board of Directors. (FAC ¶¶ 132-140.) Defendants contend that this cause of action lacks merit because Plaintiff cannot establish either justifiable reliance or resultant damage. With respect to damages, Defendants contend that the undisputed facts show that Plaintiff paid only $1,250 in fees and current dues, with the remaining $14,644 in back dues paid by the seller of Unit B, not Plaintiff. This contention is not sufficient to prove an absence of damages, as the basis for liability is that Defendants had no right to any of that money. (FAC ¶ 137.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.) Defendants have not demonstrated their entitlement to summary adjudication on this basis.

 

            As to justifiable reliance, Defendants contend that Plaintiff admitted in deposition that he never considered the election of the new Board of Directors as legitimate. (See SSUMF No. 61; Defendants’ Exh. K [Deposition of Plaintiff] Vol. II pp. 86:20-94:8.) However, close reading of that deposition reveals that Plaintiff did not testify to his knowledge at the time of the allegations, but rather to his then-current understanding, as of January 2024. (Id.) Defendants’ evidence therefore fails to demonstrate the absence of justifiable reliance by Plaintiff on Defendants’ representations. Defendants are therefore not entitled to summary adjudication on this basis.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the eighth cause of action is DENIED.

Ninth Cause of Action: Conversion

 

            Defendants move for summary adjudication of the ninth cause of action for conversion.

 

To state a claim for conversion, a plaintiff must plead “(1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.)

 

Plaintiff’s conversion claim alleges that Defendants wrongfully took possession of $15,894 in Plaintiff’s money by falsely asserting that he owed that money to the HOA. (FAC ¶¶ 144-46.) Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot prevail on this cause of action because he only paid $1,250 in fees and current dues. This contention does not defeat Plaintiff’s allegation that Defendants had no right to any of that money. “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.) Defendants have not demonstrated their entitlement to summary adjudication on this basis.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the ninth cause of action is DENIED.

MOTION TO ENFORCE SETTLEMENT

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract, fraud, and conversion. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants agreed to sell the application rights for a cannabis retail license to Plaintiffs for $100,000, but, after receiving the money, demanded a further $60,000 for the application number necessary to make use of those rights.

 

Defendants John and Erica Montgomery and Montgomery Enterprises, Inc. move to enforce a settlement agreement.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendants John and Erica Montgomery and Montgomery Enterprises, Inc. move to enforce a settlement agreement.

 

Legal Standard

 

            Enforcement of settlement agreements is governed by Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. This statute provides, in relevant part:

 

If the parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 664.6(a).) The Court is empowered under this section to resolve reasonable disputes over the terms of a settlement. (Machado v. Myers (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 779, 795.) The Court does not insert provisions into the parties’ agreement but applies the rules of contractual construction to interpret the settlement. (Id. at 792.) When extrinsic evidence is necessary, the Court may decide the motion on declarations alone. (Richardson v. Richardson (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 91, 97.)

 

Enforcement of Settlement

 

            The Montgomery Defendants seek to enforce a putative settlement agreement between Plaintiffs, on the one hand, and the moving Defendants, on the other, in which Plaintiffs purportedly agreed to dismiss this action in exchange for one dollar deemed received at the time the agreement is signed. (Declaration of John Montgomery ISO Mot. Exh. A.) The copy of the settlement agreement provided appears to be signed by these parties, dated September 7, 2024. (Id.) However, the agreement also states that “John Montgomery has agreed to assist Memory and her attorney in any way possible to get refunded the 100k cash for the Daniel Wise/Wise Endevors [sic] case.” (Id.) This provision of the Agreement appears to relate to Plaintiffs’ allegations against Daniel Wise and Wise Endeavors asserted in the First Amended Complaint. (See FAC ¶¶ 20-25.) The terms of this agreement appear to have some import to Plaintiffs’ claims against other Defendants who have not yet been served with the summons and complaint. Taken together with the de minimis settlement payment, the Court has concerns regarding the propriety of this agreement, especially where other parties to this action have not yet been given notice. The Court therefore declines to enforce the settlement until such time as the remaining Defendants are before the Court so that they may be given notice and an opportunity to be heard.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Enforce Settlement is DENIED without prejudice.

Legal Standard

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 533 states:

 

In any action, the court may on notice modify or dissolve an injunction or temporary restraining order upon a showing that there has been a material change in the facts upon which the injunction or temporary restraining order was granted, that the law upon which the injunction or temporary restraining order was granted has changed, or that the ends of justice would be served by the modification or dissolution of the injunction or temporary restraining order.

(March 6, 2024 Minute Order.) Moreover, the injunction does not require the posting of an undertaking, which entitles Defendant to its dissolution as a matter of law. (Neumann v. Moretti (1905) 146 Cal. 31, 33-34.) Thus, on both the factual record and legal authority, Defendant is entitled to dissolution of the injunction.

MOTION TO DISMISS PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 583.310

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Dan Mintz, Bing Wu, DMB Entertainment and H2F Entertainment, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Cenigent Health Enhancement Medical Institute

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         05/08/14: Complaint filed. 

·         05/06/16: First Amended Complaint filed. 

·         09/06/16: Second Amended Complaint filed. 

·         11/17/17: Case stayed pending arbitration. 

·         01/14/19: Arbitration ordered terminated. 

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            Plaintiff allegedly provided medical treatment and medical research services to Defendants. Defendants have not paid outstanding invoices for such services totaling over $1.5 million. 

 

            Defendants move to dismiss this action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion to Dismiss is DENIED.

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendants move to dismiss this action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310 requires any action to be brought to trial within five years of the date of its commencement. (Code Civ. Proc. § 583.310.) This period runs from the date of filing of the original complaint and is not affected by amendment. (See Davalos v. County of Los Angeles (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 57, 63.) Dismissal of an action which is not brought within the five-year period is mandatory, either on a party’s motion or sua sponte, unless the Plaintiff can establish an extension, excuse, or exception expressly provided by statute. (Code Civ. Proc. § 583.360.) Statutory extensions of time include written stipulations (Code Civ. Proc. § 583.330(a)) and oral agreements before the Court, if entered in the minutes or reflected in a transcript. (Id. subd. (b).) The calculation is also tolled at any time where the Court’s jurisdiction is suspended, prosecution or trial was stayed or enjoined, or if bringing the action to trial was impossible, impracticable, or futile. (Code Civ. Proc. § 583.340.)

 

Plaintiff’s original Complaint was filed on May 8, 2014. (See Complaint.) Thus, by operation of Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310, the original deadline to bring this action to trial was May 8, 2019. During the pendency of the action, the case was stayed pending arbitration from November 17, 2017 to January 14, 2019, a total of 423 days. (See November 17, 2017 Minute Order; January 14, 2019 Minute Order.) The parties agree that this stay extended the deadline to bring this action to July 4, 2020, a Saturday and holiday, which therefore would have required this action to be brought to trial by July 6, 2020. (Code Civ. Proc. § 12a; Holland v. Dave Altman’s R.V. Center (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 477, 480.) The parties then repeatedly stipulated over the next four years, the repeated warnings of the Court notwithstanding, to further extensions of the deadline ahead of the looming cutoff date. (See June 30, 2020 Minute Order [extending to September 26, 2021]; February 18, 2021 Minute Order [March 26, 2022]; January 20, 2022 Minute Order [October 1, 2022]; July 11, 2022 Minute Order [December 9, 2022]; November 15, 2022 Minute Order [June 30, 2023]; May 22, 2023 Minute Order [August 21, 2023]; July 21, 2023 Minute Order [March 31, 2024]; December 12, 2023 Minute Order [August 31, 2024].) The most recent stipulated extension was the December 12, 2023 order extending the deadline to August 31, 2024 by stipulation of the parties, “without prejudice to defendants’ right to seek a further continuance, if necessary.” (December 12, 2023 Minute Order.)

 

Defendants argue that because the August 31, 2024 deadline has passed, this action must be dismissed pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310. Plaintiff, in opposition, first argues that Defendants have waived their right to dismiss this action by stipulating to extensions of the five-year deadline, citing Smith v. Bear Valley Milling & Lumber Co. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 590, at 599. This case does not stand for the proposition offered by Plaintiff that a stipulation outright waives the right of dismissal. Rather, Smith holds that “[a] stipulation to extend the trial beyond the five-year period necessarily waives the right to a dismissal of the action […] which would otherwise accrue because of such delay.” (Smith v. Bear Valley Milling & Lumber Co. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 590, 599 [emphasis added].) Put differently, Smith stands for the axiomatic principle that a party cannot stipulate to an extension of the five-year rule and then move to dismiss before the extension has run.

 

More forcefully, however, Plaintiff argues that this action was stayed in its entirety with respect to all parties commencing on February 20, 2024 due to a bankruptcy proceeding commenced by Defendant Bing Wu. (February 20, 2024 Notice of Stay.) That stay was lifted on September 9, 2024, based on evidence that the bankruptcy proceeding was dismissed. (September 9, 2024 Minute Order.) Thus, by operation of Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340(b), the deadline to bring this action to trial was extended a further 202 days, to Friday, March 21, 2025. Defendants, anticipating this argument, contend that bankruptcy stays only toll the five-year period as to the bankrupt. Indeed, that is the general rule. (See, e.g., Santa Monica Hospital Center v. Superior Court (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 1026, 1036.) Moreover, our Supreme Court has expressly stated that Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340(b) only applies when a stay encompasses all proceedings and does not include partial stays. (Bruns v. E-Commerce Exchange Inc. (2011) 51 Cal.4th 717, 723.) Here, that is exactly what transpired: the action was stayed in its entirety as to all parties, not merely to Defendant Wu. Therefore, Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340 subdivision (b) tolled the deadline to bring the action during the pendency of that stay. Although Defendants argue that Plaintiff was not diligent in bringing this action to trial, that contention is immaterial, as diligence is only relevant when an extension is asserted under subdivision (c) on the grounds that other circumstances rendered it “impossible, impracticable, or futile” to bring the case to trial. (Bruns, supra, 51 Cal.4th at 730-31.) In this case, the action was stayed as to all parties for 202 days, and, therefore, section 583.330(b) requires that the deadline to prosecute this action be extended as to all parties by 202 days, placing the cutoff on Friday, March 21, 2025.

 

As the proper statutory deadline is the future date of March 21, 2025, and trial is set to commence on February 11, 2025, Defendants are not entitled to dismissal of this action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310.  

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Dismiss is DENIED.
