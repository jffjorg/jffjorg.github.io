DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19SMCV01976    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: M

Plaintiff requests that the attorney's fee award reflect additional $900.00 to Dr. Siegemund for the fees her counsel incurred in connection with attending the April 23, 2024, hearing. The Court agrees that its order should have included this additional amount.  

Plaintiff’s motion to amend the April 23, 2024, minute order nunc pro tunc is GRANTED. (CCP 473(d).) The Court strikes the following language from the order: “the motion for fees is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $21,902.50.” and replaces it with the following: “the motion for fees is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $22,802.50.”


Case Number: 21SMCV01401    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Bowmont Holdings LLC, et al., v. CAS Construction, et al.

CASE NO.:                21SMCV01401

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Compliance/Production

HEARING DATE:   6/7/2024

 

Legal Standard

If a party permits inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents in response to a request for production of documents, but thereafter fails to permit the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling in accordance with that party's statement of compliance, the demanding party may move for an order compelling compliance. (CCP § 2031.320(a).) The court “shall” impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel compliance with a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (CCP § 2031.320(b).)

 

ANALYSIS 

 

Plaintiff Haig Tacorian moves the Court for an order compelling Defendant City of Los Angeles (“City”) and Defendant City of Los Angeles Department of Water and Power (“DWP”) (collectively, “Defendants”) to comply and produce all documents responsive to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Sets One) Nos. 21-23, 28-32, and 34-56 within 10 days. (CCP § 2031.320.)

 

On December 22, 2023, Plaintiff propounded each Defedant certain Requests for Production of Documents (Set One), which consisted of 56 requests. (Cowles Decl.,, Exs. A, B.) On March 15, 2024, following meet and confer efforts, Defendants provided supplemental responses, agreeing to allow inspection of the documents responsive to Request Nos. 21-23, 28-32, and 34-56. (Id., Exs. G, H.) There is no dispute that Defendants agreed to provide the responsive documents. Plaintiff met and conferred regarding the outstanding production, but received no response. (Id., ¶¶ 8-9.) As of April 24, 2024, Defendants have not produced a single responsive document. (Id., ¶ 9.)

 

Defendants do not provide a reason why compliance with their statement should not be compelled. They assert that this motion fails to meet the requirements of a motion to compel further responses. However, this is not such a motion. Defendants also raise issues regarding the facts of the underlying dispute, which are not relevant to this motion to compel compliance. Defendants admit that they are in the process of providing documents “as quickly as it can given staffing and budgetary limitations” at the City. With this statement, they admit that they owe production. Moreover, Defendants neither cite any evidence of their staffing issues or budget, nor any authority which shows that their staffing and budget issues justify their failure to comply with their statement of compliance. For instance, Defendants do not show that production would be overly burdensome or oppressive. Defendants also do not explain their reasonable efforts to comply in order to justify their rate of production. As such, there is no reason to deny the motion. For the same reasons, Defendants are also subject to sanctions.

 

Accordingly, the motions to compel compliance are GRANTED. Production must be completed within 15 days of this order.

 

Plaintiff request $9,270.00 in sanctions against Defendants. As a result of Defendants’ failure to comply with their discovery obligations, Counsel claims 9 hours preparing the two motions to compel and the supporting papers ($475.00 x 9 = $4,275.00), 1.5 hours to review and revise the moving papers ($595.00 x 1.5 = $892.50), an anticipated 3 hours to review any oppositions and to prepare replies ($475.00 x 3 = $1,425.00), an anticipated 4.5 hours to prepare for the hearing and to attend the hearing in Santa Monica ($595.00 x 4.5 = $2,677.50). (Cowles Decl., ¶ 11.) Considering this record, the court is not inclined to grant the full sanctions request. The motions are relatively routine motions to compel compliance. The court finds that a reasonable fee in this instance would be $4,217.50, accounting for 7 hours of attorney time at $475 per hour, and 1.5 hours of attorney time at $595 per hour, plus $120.00 in costs. Accordingly, sanctions are imposed against Defendants the City of Los Angeles and the City of Los Angeles Department of Water and Power, jointly and severally, in the amount of $4,337.50. Sanctions are to be paid within 30 days.


Case Number: 21SMCV01662    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Clayton v. Malibu West Swimming Club, et al.

CASE NO.:                21SMCV01662

MOTION:                  Motion to Lift Discovery Stay (CCP 425.16(g).)

HEARING DATE:   6/7/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

Discovery is automatically stayed upon the filing of an anti-SLAPP motion. (CCP, § 425.16(g); The Garment Workers Center v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1156, 1161.) “The court, on noticed motion and for good cause shown, may order that specified discovery be conducted notwithstanding this subdivision.” (Id.) “If the plaintiff makes a timely and proper showing in response to the motion to strike, that a defendant or witness possesses evidence needed by plaintiff to establish a prima facie case, the plaintiff must be given the reasonable opportunity to obtain that evidence through discovery before the motion to strike is adjudicated.” (Lafayette Morehouse, Inc. v. Chronicle Publishing Co. (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 855, 868.) “The trial court, therefore, must liberally exercise its discretion by authorizing reasonable and specified discovery timely petitioned for by a plaintiff… when evidence to establish a prima facie case is reasonably shown to be held, or known, by defendant or its agents and employees. (Id. see Matson v. Dvorak (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 539, 548 [on prong 2 of the anti-SLAP analysis, plaintiff must demonstrate, through admissible evidence, “a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited”]; see also Sipple v. Foundation for National Progress (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 226, 247 [trial court did not abuse its discretion in refusing to permit limited discovery when the appellant did not explain what additional facts he expected to uncover and why discovery is necessary to carry his burden].) The fact that the evidence necessary to establish the plaintiff’s prima facie case is in the hands of the defendant or third party is only one factor. (Garment Workers, supra, 117 Cal.App.4th at 1162.) “The trial court should consider whether the information the plaintiff seeks to obtain through formal discovery proceedings is readily available from other sources or can be obtained through informal discovery.” (Id.)

 

EVIDENTIARY ISSUES

 

Plaintiff’s objections are OVERRULED.

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiffs Samantha and Royce Clayton move for an order lifting the discovery stay pursuant to section 425.16(g). Plaintiffs argue that they are entitled to conduct limited discovery tailored to establishing motive (regarding racial discrimination claims) and whether Riddick’s actions and decision-making were made in good faith or were unreasonable, arbitrary, or capricious (regarding the breach of the CC&R claims). Plaintiffs seek discovery regarding the following topics, in the form of depositions and related requests for production of documents as to Riddick and the Person Most Knowledgeable of Malibu West: 1) “me too” evidence or “comparative evidence” (e.g., how other similarly situated homeowners at Malibu West, including Riddick, were treated when they asked for approval of plans and for variances); 2) decision making related to the rejections of Plaintiffs’ plans; 3) Malibu West’s approval of Riddick’s plans for an ADU and approval of plans for the rebuild of his home; 4) statements made by the Riddicks in their lawsuit regarding the support provided to them by Malibu West for the ADU; 5) admissions/statements the Riddicks made to the press regarding unpermitted ADUs at Malibu West being allowed; 6) other unpermitted ADUs at Malibu West; and 7) the basis for threatening and seeking injunctive relief against Plaintiffs.

 

The Court agrees evidence of the Board/Riddick’s motive would be generally required to make a prima facie showing under the intentional infliction of emotional distress claim and race discrimination claims (FEHA, Unruh). (Government Code section 12955, et seq.; Civ. Code section 51 et seq.; see Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051 [a defendant’s conduct is “outrageous” when it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community].) Further, the CC&R-based claims (breach of CC&Rs, Davis-Sterling Act, Declaratory Relief, Equitable Relief) require evidence of the Board/Riddick’s bad faith or unreasonable, arbitrary, or capricious decision making. (TAC para. 130.) Defendants Riddick and Malibu West also would necessarily have unique information on their motives and/or bad faith. Thus, the Court has discretion to allow limited discovery into such matters.

 

That said, plaintiffs may only be afforded a “reasonable opportunity” to obtain evidence through discovery before the special motion to strike is adjudicated. The record here shows Plaintiffs were already afforded such an opportunity. The case has been pending for several years prior to Riddick’s motion to strike. The initial complaint was filed on October 15, 2021. The operative third amended complaint (TAC) relies on mostly the same allegations stated in the original complaint. Plaintiffs challenged the Defendants’ denial of their requests for a variance, and accused the Board of racial bias. Moreover, throughout the pendency of the suit, the parties engaged in regular discovery. (See Newcomb Decl., ¶¶2-7.) The TAC was then filed on December 1, 2023. Riddick was later added as Doe 1 on March 14, 2024. Plaintiff could have propounded discovery (and presumably did propound discovery) on the board’s motivations, which necessarily included Riddick’s motivations.  Plaintiff thus had 3.5 years to litigate these issues as to the Board’s motives, including Riddick. In fact, Plaintiff likely discovered some basis for Riddick’s personal liability through such efforts, and accordingly added him into the action. Plaintiff therefore already had plenty of opportunity to discover information on the exact topics cited above.

 

On this procedural posture, where Plaintiff had the opportunity to conduct discovery already, Plaintiff has not shown good cause for further discovery. Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.


Case Number: 24SMCV01816    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Afsar, et al., v. Marrari Inc., et al.

CASE NO.:                24SMCV01816

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel SDT

HEARING DATE:   6/7/2024

 

 

Legal Standard

 

If a nonparty disobeys a deposition subpoena, the subpoenaing party may seek a court order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) section 1987.1 compelling the nonparty to comply with the subpoena within 60 days after completion of the deposition record. (CCP § 2025.480(b); see also UnzippedApparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 123, 127.) The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP § 2025.480(b).)

 

Under CCP section 1987.1(a), “If a subpoena requires … the production of … documents, [or] electronically stored information …, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person [including a party] … may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.” The court may also “make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (CCP § 1987.1(a).)

 

Sanctions are available under both CCP § 1987.2 and CCP § 2025.480. CCP § 1987.2 states: “[T]he court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification . . .” CCP § 2025.480(j) states: “The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel an answer or production, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” Further, per CCP § 2020.240, “[a] deponent who disobeys a deposition subpoena in any manner described in¿subdivision (c) of Section 2020.220¿may be punished for contempt under Chapter 7 (commencing with¿Section 2023.010) without the necessity of a prior order of court directing compliance by the witness. The deponent is also subject to the forfeiture and the payment of damages set forth in¿Section 1992.” 

 

ANALYSIS 

 

Plaintiffs Sanaz Afsar, MSK, AK, and Saied Kashani move for an order compelling third-party Vollmer-Gray Laboratories Inc. to produce all documents responsive to the subpoena for business records issued on April 17, 2024. Plaintiffs also seek monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,450 against Vollmer-Gray. Plaintiffs contend that Vollmer-Gray failed to respond or object to the subpoena and thereby waived all objections.

 

Plaintiffs fail to show proper service of the third-party deposition subpoena. A business records subpoena requires personal service of the subpoena to any officer, director, custodian of records, or to any agent or employee authorized by the organization to accept service of a subpoena. (CCP sec. 2020.220(b)(2).) On April 19, 2024, Plaintiffs served the subject subpoena on Vollmer-Gray for production of Vollmer-Gray's report (if any) and communications, photos, etc. of the fire vehicle. (Kashani Decl., Ex. A.) The server, Amir Akbari, provides a declaration which shows he hand-served the person at the front “apparently in charge of the office” at Vollmer-Gray's address. (Akbari Decl.) However, the person “apparently in charge of the office” is not a proper person to serve personally on behalf of the corporation. As noted above, the personal service must be on a person authorized by the organization to accept service of a subpoena. The record contains no evidence that the “lady” was authorized to accept service. Further, the record does not suggest she was an officer, director, or custodian of records. In light of this, Plaintiff fails to show proper service. Therefore, the court lacks jurisdiction over third party Vollmer-Gray.

 

The Court further notes that the discovery was premature, and gave a too-short date for production. (CCP 2020.410; 2025.210 et seq.)

 

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.
