DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCP01071    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: H

Annex Walnut Automotive Colors & Supply, Inc. v. Rouillard, et al.,
Case No. 23STCP01071

ORDER ON MOTIONS FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

1.       Cross-Defendant Rachel M. Darvish’s Amended Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to the Third Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress is DENIED.

2.       Cross-Defendant Keyvan Rahbar’s Amended Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Annex Walnut Automotive Colors & Supply, Inc. (“Annex”) alleges as follows:

          Annex is a distributor of automotive and industrial coatings and paint. Rachel M. Darvish (“Darvish”), Keyvan Rahbar (“Rahbar”), and Maryam Rouillard (“Rouillard”) each own 33% of the outstanding shares and voting power of Annex. Rouillard has been responsible for the day-to-day management and operations of Annex. Annex seeks to wind up and dissolve. Rouillard has never distributed any income to Darvish and Rahbar but has reported to the IRS distributions to both. In December 2022, Rouillard informed Darvish and Rahbar of her plans to retire, that she had arranged to sell sale of Annex to one its competitors, Wesco Group, LLC (hereinafter “Wesco Group”), and that she and Wesco Group had agreed on a total estimated purchase price of $1,308,385.00. On January 30, 2023, Rouillard changed Annex’s Statement of Information on file with the California Secretary of State to name herself as agent for service of process and its only director and Chief Executive Officer.

          On March 6, 2023, Darvish, Rahbar, and Rouillard met to discuss how the sale and distribution of the proceeds would be handled; during that meeting, Rouillard became angry and physically assaulted Darvish. Rouillard interfered with the sale due in part to the March 6 incident, causing Wesco Group to withdraw from the transaction. On or about May 1, 2023, Darvish and Rahbar contacted Wesco Group and were able to revive Wesco Group’s initial offer to purchase Annex.

          On May 17, 2023, in order to effectuate the sale, Wesco Group submitted a list of documents to be produced by Annex. Rouillard is one of two individuals affiliated with Annex who has access to this information, the other person being Rouillard’s nephew, Amir Karimpour (“Karimpour”). Rouillard has failed to cooperate and has directed Annex’s employees, including Karimpour, not to cooperate and supply the requested information. Rouillard has contacted representatives of Wesco Group, as well as other third parties and potential buyers, and informed them that she will prevent the sale of Annex to Wesco Group or to those third-party buyers.

On June 8, 2023, Annex filed a “Verified First Amended Petition,” asserting causes of action against Rouillard, John Kobes, Calvin Kobes, and Does 1-20 for:

1.       Request for Court Supervision Over Voluntary,

2.       Dissolution—California Corporations Code Section 1904,

3.       Breach of Fiduciary Duty,

4.       Conversion,

5.       Fraud,

6.       Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations, and
7.       Declaratory Relief.


On July 7, 2023, an “Order on Petitioner Annex Walnut Automotive Colors & Supply, Inc.’s Motion for the Appointment of a Receiver” was filed.

On August 17, 2023, the Court related Case No. 23PSCV01022 to the instant case and designated the instant case as the lead case. 

On November 20, 2023, Rouillard filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC” or the “Operative Cross Complaint”), asserting causes of action against Darvish, Rahbar, Annex and Roes 21-50 for:

1.       Breach of Fiduciary Duty,

2.       Aiding and Abetting Breach of Fiduciary Duty,

3.       Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress,

4.       Fraud,

5.       Common Count, and

6.       Declaratory Relief. 

On November 21, 2023, an “Order Authorizing Receiver Kevin Singer to Draw Upon Annex Walnut Automotive Colors & Supply, Inc., Wells Fargo Line of Credit Account” was entered. 

On December 22, 2023, an “Order to Show Cause Re: Contempt Against Defendant” was entered. 

On April 2, 2024, a “Revised Order Scheduling Auction Hearing and Sale of Annex Walnut Automotive Colors & Supply, Inc.” was entered. 

On May 16, 2024, an “Order Confirming Sale of Annex Walnut Automotive Colors & Supply, Inc. to Rachel Darvish and Keyvan Rahbar and/or Their Assignee(s)” was entered. 

Pending before the Court are (1) Darvish’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the third cause of action in the Operative Cross Complaint and (2) Rahbar’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the third, fourth, and sixth causes of action in the Operative Cross Complaint. For the reasons stated herein, both motions are denied.
 

II.      Legal Standard

The rules governing demurrers are generally applicable to a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999; Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (d) [“The grounds for motion. . . shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. Where the motion is based on a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. . ., the matter shall be specified in the notice of motion, or in the supporting points and authorities, except as the court may otherwise permit”].)

 

A motion by a plaintiff may only be made on the grounds “that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(A).) A motion by a defendant may only be made on the grounds that (1) “[t]he court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint” or (2) “[t]he complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c).)

III.     Discussion

          A.       Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (by Darvish)

Darvish moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the third cause of action (i.e., for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress) in the Operative Cross Complaint.

The Court notes that on December 26, 2023, Darvish filed a demurrer to the Operative Cross Complaint. As to the third cause of action, Darvish asserted that “the facts upon which the cause of action is predicated are within the scope of the litigation privilege. Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(e).” (12/26/23 Dem., 2:14-16.) Darvish further purported to quote paragraphs 33 and 34 of the Operative Cross Complaint and argued that “allegations of malfeasance must be brought as a derivative claim and the majority electing to pursue liquidation is absolutely privileged.” (Id., 7:16-8:8.)

On February 9, 2024, Judge Peter Hernandez overruled Darvish’s demurrer with respect to the third cause of action. Darvish, in this instant motion, asserts that the third cause of action fails in part because it “alleges liability on account of Annex and the shareholders initiating the liquidation action,” which is privileged. (Motion, 3:21-4:17). This argument, however, was raised previously and rejected.

A motion for judgment on the pleadings may be brought on the same grounds as an unsuccessful demurrer only if “there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (g); Farber v. Bay View Terrace Homeowners Ass’n (2006) 141 Cal.App.4th 1007.) Darvish has failed to provide the Court with any such showing.

Further, while Darvish also argues that Rouillard’s allegation concerning a phone call made by Rahbar to Rouillard’s elderly mother “in the later hours of the night” does not constitute intentional infliction of emotional distress, the Court declines to consider such argument, as a motion for judgment on the pleadings cannot be granted as to part of a cause of action.

Darvish’s motion is denied.

          B.       Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings (by Rahbar)

Rahbar moves for judgment on the pleadings as to the third, fourth and sixth causes of action (i.e., for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Fraud and Declaratory Relief, respectively) in the Operative Cross Complaint.

Again, on December 26, 2023, Darvish filed a demurrer to the Operative Cross Complaint. On December 28, 2023, Rahbar filed a joinder to Darvish’s demurrer. On February 9, 2024, Judge Hernandez granted Rahbar’s joinder, overruled Darvish’s demurrer to the third and sixth causes of action, and overruled Darvish’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action as moot (i.e., inasmuch as the fourth cause of action was not asserted against her).

Rahbar, in this instant motion, repeats Darvish’s argument that the third cause of action fails in part because it “alleges liability on account of Annex and the shareholders initiating the liquidation action,” which is privileged. (Motion, 3:21-4:17.) This argument, however, was raised previously and rejected, as was Rahbar’s argument that the sixth cause of action failed because it was dependent upon other, flawed causes of action. Rahbar has failed to provide the Court with any showing that “there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer.” Again, the Court declines to consider Rahbar’s argument with respect to Paragraph 41 (pertaining to Rouillard’s elderly mother, discussed above) in the third cause of action, as a motion for judgment on the pleadings cannot be granted as to part of a cause of action.

Finally, the Court determines that Rouillard’s fourth cause of action has been sufficiently pled. The elements of fraud are: (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 184 [quotations and citation omitted].) “[F]raud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384 [quotations and citation omitted].) A plaintiff asserting fraud must “plead facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469 [quotations and citation omitted].)

Rahbar fails to accurately quote Paragraphs 47 and 48 from the Operative Cross Complaint. Rouillard has alleged, in relevant part, that Rahbar specifically promised her compensation for her work at least once, in October 2022, and she made retirement decisions based on his misrepresentations. Rouillard has also alleged that she lent money to Annex when the business on occasion within the last 24 months needed additional funds and was promised that any monies lent would be repair from the sale of Annex. All the elements of fraud are specifically pled: that Rouillard misrepresented that she would be fairly compensated, that it happened in October 2022 (or on occasions within the last 24 months with respect to the lending of monies), and that she reasonably agreed to work without fair present compensation (or lend monies) because she believed she would be paid in the future based on her long-standing relationship with Rahbar.

Rahbar’s motion is denied. 

Case Number: 24PSCV01311    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: H

Wang v. Liu, et al., Case No. 24PSCV01311

Proposed Intervenor United Financial Casualty Company’s Motion for Leave to Intervene is GRANTED.

 

Background

Plaintiff Min Wang (“Plaintiff”) allegedly sustained injuries and damages in a May 2, 2022 motor vehicle accident.

On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting a negligence cause of action against Bin Liu, DLJMK, Inc. (“DLJMK”), Zhi Qin Cai Zhang, and Does 1-25.

On June 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed three “Amendment[s] to Complaint,” wherein MJKC, Inc., JKM Warriors, Inc., and Katerine Lineth Cai Lane were substituted in lieu of Does 1-3, respectively.

A Case Management Conference and Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to File Proof of Service are set for January 28, 2025.

Pending before the Court is United Financial Casualty Company’s motion to intervene.

 

Legal Standard

 

“A nonparty shall petition the court for leave to intervene by noticed motion or ex parte application. The petition shall include a copy of the proposed complaint in intervention or answer in intervention and set forth the grounds upon which intervention rests.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (c).) 

 

“The court shall, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if either of the following conditions is satisfied:

(A) A provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene.

(B) The person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person’s ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(1).)

 

“The court may, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 387, subd. (d)(2).)

 

Discussion

Proposed Intervenor United Financial Casualty Company (“Proposed Intervenor”) moves for an order to intervene on behalf of its insured, DLJMK, to file an Answer-In-Intervention and to stay discovery requests against DLJMK.

“Intervention may . . . be allowed in the insurance context, where third party claimants are involved, when the insurer is allowed to take over in litigation if its insured is not defending an action, to avoid harm to the insurer.” (Royal Indemnity Co. v. United Enterprises, Inc. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 194, 206.) The only manner in which an insurer may defend or exercise the powers of its insured suspended corporation is by intervening in the action under Code of Civil Procedure section 387 and asserting any defenses on behalf of its insured.  (Kaufman & Broad Communities, Inc. v. Performance Plastering, Inc. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 212, 220.)

 

“An insurer’s right to intervene in an action against the insured, for personal injury or property damage, arises as a result of Insurance Code section 11580.”[FN1] (Reliance Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 383, 386.) 

The motion originally came on calendar on October 10, 2024; at that time, the Court continued the hearing to November 5, 2024 and directed counsel for Proposed Intervenor to file and serve a supplemental declaration supporting Proposed Intervenor’s assertion that DLJMK is a “defunct corporation that was terminated” (Motion, 3:28; see also, 7:1 and 8:4-5).

On October 22, 2024, Proposed Intervenor filed (and electronically served upon Plaintiff’s counsel) the “Supplemental Declaration of Katrina Wang in Support of Motion for Leave to Intervene.” Wang attests therein that DLJMK is a “defunct corporation that was terminated” and attaches a printout from the California Secretary of State reflecting DLJMK’s “Terminated” status as Exhibit A. (Wang Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.) Wang also confirms that Proposed Intervenor is the insurance carrier for DLJMK. (Id., ¶ 6). 

The Court determines that intervention is appropriate. Proposed Intervenor has a direct and immediate interest in the litigation, because it may be required to satisfy any judgment entered against its insured, DLJMK, which is presently in terminated status. In addition, intervention by Proposed Intervenor will not enlarge the issues in this case, as Proposed Intervenor seeks to assert the same defenses which would have been asserted by DLJMK. Proposed Intervenor merely seeks to “step into the shoes” of DLJMK.

The motion is granted.  

 

[FN1] Insurance Code § 11580 reads as follows:

“A policy insuring against losses set forth in subdivision (a) shall not be issued or delivered to any person in this state unless it contains the provisions set forth in subdivision (b). Such policy, whether or not actually containing such provisions, shall be construed as if such provisions were embodied therein.

(a) Unless it contains such provisions, the following policies of insurance shall not be thus issued or delivered:

(1) Against loss or damage resulting from liability for injury suffered by another person other than (i) a policy of workers' compensation insurance, or (ii) a policy issued by a nonadmitted Mexican insurer solely for use in the Republic of Mexico.

(2) Against loss of or damage to property caused by draught animals or any vehicle, and for which the insured is liable, other than a policy which provides insurance in the Republic of Mexico, issued or delivered in this state by a nonadmitted Mexican insurer.

(b) Such policy shall not be thus issued or delivered to any person in this state unless it contains all the following provisions:

(1) A provision that the insolvency or bankruptcy of the insured will not release the insurer from the payment of damages for injury sustained or loss occasioned during the life of such policy.

(2) A provision that whenever judgment is secured against the insured or the executor or administrator of a deceased insured in an action based upon bodily injury, death, or property damage, then an action may be brought against the insurer on the policy and subject to its terms and limitations, by such judgment creditor to recover on the judgment.”
