Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.



Case Number: 22TRCV01061    Hearing Date: November 30, 2023    Dept: M

Torrance Dept. M

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

EDDIE J. SUBER, JR., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             November 30, 2023   

 

Moving Parties:          Plaintiff Cassandra Woods    

Responding Party:      Defendants Eddie J. Suber, Jr. and Norma J. Suber as trustees, et al.

Motion for Order Permitting Discovery of Financial Condition

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE

BACKGROUND

On October 31, 2022, plaintiff Cassandra Woods filed a complaint against defendants Eddie J. Suber, Jr. and Norma J. Suber as Trustees of the Eddie J. Suber, Jr. and Norma J. Suber Revocable Trust, DTD 7-20-01; LB Property Management, Inc., a California Corporation; De Miranda Management, Inc., a California Corporation; and DOES 1 through 20, inclusive for (1) statutory breach of warranty of habitability (Civ. Code §§ 1941, 1941.1), (2) tortious breach of implied warranty of habitability, (3) negligence, (4) breach of contract, (5) nuisance, (6) breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment, (7) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (8) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (9) violation of the Inglewood stabilization ordinance – harassment, and (10) violation of Civil Code §1942.4.

Trial is set for November 27, 2024.  

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Civil Code §3294(a) states in pertinent part, “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”

Civil Code §3294(c)(1) defines malice as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  “Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”  Civil Code §3294(c)(2).

Civil Code §3295(c) states that, “No pretrial discovery by the plaintiff shall be permitted with respect to the evidence referred to in paragraphs (1) and (2) of subdivision (a) unless the court enters an order permitting such discovery pursuant to this subdivision. . . . Upon motion by the plaintiff supported by appropriate affidavits and after a hearing, if the court deems a hearing to be necessary, the court may at any time enter an order permitting the discovery otherwise prohibited by this subdivision if the court finds, on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented, that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294.  Such order shall not be considered to be a determination on the merits of the claim or any defense thereto and shall not be given in evidence or referred to at the trial.”

“Before a court may enter an order permitting discovery of a defendant’s financial condition, it must (1) weigh the evidence submitted in favor of and in opposition to motion for discovery, and (2) make a finding that it is very likely the plaintiff will prevail on his claim for punitive damages. In this context, we interpret the words ‘substantial probability’ to mean ‘very likely’ or ‘a strong likelihood’ just as their plain meaning suggests. We note that the Legislature did not use the term ‘reasonable probability’ or simply ‘probability,’ which would imply a lower threshold of ‘more likely than not.’” Jabro v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal. App. 4th 754, 758.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Cassandra Woods requests an order permitting plaintiff to take discovery regarding the financial condition, including profits gained from the wrongful conduct, of defendants Eddie J. Suber, Jr. and Norma J. Suber as Trustees of the Eddie J. Suber, Jr. and Norma J. Suber Revocable Trust, DTD 7-20-01, LB Property Management, Inc., and De Miranda Management, Inc.

            The verified complaint alleges that plaintiff moved into the property at 135 North Hillcrest Blvd., #4, Inglewood, on April 1, 2019, pursuant to a lease agreement.  Complaint, ¶¶1, 20.  She pays a rental amount of $1604.  Id., ¶21.  On September 25, 2019, she started to notice water intrusion and mold present at the property.  Id., ¶22.  She also identified other substandard issues present at the property.  Id., ¶23.  Plaintiff provided actual notice to defendants of the issues present at the property.  Id., ¶24.  Plaintiff specifically provided notice to defendants on November 5, 2019.  Id., ¶25.  Plaintiff continuously notified defendants of the same issues.  Id., ¶26.  Said issues have substantially interfered with the quiet enjoyment and peace of mind of plaintiff at the property.  Id., ¶27.  Defendants as owner or agents of the owner of the property are responsible to repair substandard issues at the property.  Id., ¶29.  Said issues are substantial defects affecting plaintiff’s health and safety.  Id., ¶30.  Defendants having full notice and not performing any adequate repairs, have a conscious disregard for the safety of plaintiff.  Id., ¶31.  In spite of plaintiff’s pleas, the issues continued to intensify over time.  Id., ¶34.  Plaintiff has reported said issues to the LA County Health Department reporting multiple health code violations.  Id., ¶35.  Defendants were notified by the LA County Health Department of the multiple health code violations.  Id., ¶36.

            Plaintiff argues that she has a substantial probability that she will prevail on her punitive damages claim.  She contends that she has more than adequately pled facts showing that defendants acted with malice and oppression in failing to remedy the severe water intrusion and other defects present throughout the property despite knowledge of the issue.  She argues that pretrial discovery of defendants’ financial condition is warranted to allow plaintiff to meet her burden of proof and present such evidence at trial.

completed that day.  The person assigned to address the issue left a huge hole underneath her sink.  Cassandra Woods decl., ¶4.  On November 5, 2019, plaintiff sent follow-up email to complete the work and a second email that the kitchen sink started to overflow again.  Repairs were not scheduled for that day.  Id., ¶5.  She states that from November 18, 2019 through August 2022, she continuously provided notice to defendants of the failure to repair any issues at the property.  Id., ¶7.  She attached “some of the several screen shots of the written notifications” provided by plaintiff via the online portal.  Id., ¶8.  On August 18, 2022, she filed a complaint with the LA County Dept. of Public Health regarding the cockroach infestation, water intrusion, mold, malfunctioning appliances, and peeling paint.  Id., ¶9, Exh. 3.  The County sent a Courtesy Notice to Abate, compliance date of September 20, 2022.  Id., ¶11.  She states that on September 15, 2022 through May 2023, she continuously had issues with the property and provided continuous written notice of said issues.  Defendants failed to engage in repairs.  Id., ¶12.  On September 20, 2022, she called the County and filed a second complaint.  Id., ¶13.  On September 21, 2022, plaintiff submitted another work order.  “I submitted no less than 3 work orders to the Defendants, and none were responded too.”  Id., ¶14.  On September 22, 2022, the County sent a 2nd Notice to Abate, with compliance date of October 6, 2022.  Id., ¶15.  On September 23, 2022, she submitted another work order. She attached photographs of the property, but they are not dated.

            In reply, plaintiff contends that defendants failed to provide any affidavits to dispute plaintiff’s factual statements or defend defendants’ position.   Plaintiff also objects to the documents attached to defense counsel’s declaration as lacking authentication.  Plaintiff contends that the repair reports were after plaintiff filed her complaint in October 2022.  She also argues that she has multiple causes of action that entitle her to punitive damages.

            The court rules as follows:  The court finds that plaintiff has not established that there is a “substantial probability” that she will prevail on her punitive damages claim at this stage.  Defendants presented evidence of numerous responses to the work orders submitted by plaintiff and invoices dating from 2021 to 2023 of maintenance and repair work done at plaintiff’s unit, including as to leaks, roach infestation, and stove.  Such effort and expense tend to show that defendants did not engage in despicable conduct or intend to harm plaintiff.  As to the instances in October and November 2019, the evidence indicates that defendants did not respond that same day but that they did respond.  Whether the issue was not “addressed with any urgency” does not show malice.  Plaintiff does not indicate any issues in 2020 or proof of any complaints or work order requests or of defendants’ failure to respond.  As to the notice to abate, County inspection, and work order requests occurring in September through November 2022, defendants showed numerous invoices of either attempts to repair or repairs.

The court also notes that although the motion can be brought at any time, discovery is ongoing, and trial is not set until November 2024.  At a later date, plaintiff may request that defendants “stipulate to a process by which [the defendant] would gather documents pertaining to [its] financial condition, bring them to trial under seal and make them immediately available” in the event that the jury's findings make punitive damages available.  See Jabro, supra, at 756.  This procedure “is a frequently used and effective means of handling the matter when a claim for punitive damages is alleged,” and may be suggested or facilitated by the court.  Id.  Also, a plaintiff may do nothing pretrial and instead wait until liability is established to ask the court for the order described in Civil Code §3295(c).  Mike Davidov Co. v. Issod (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 597, 609.

            Accordingly, the motion is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

BACKGROUND

            On October 12, 2023, applicant City of Manhattan Beach filed an application for warrant to enter and abate a dangerous condition on the property located at 116 9th Street, Manhattan Beach, Los Angeles County Assessor’s Parcel No. 4179-014-017.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Health and Safety Code §17980.6 states, in part:  “If any building is maintained in a manner that violates any provisions of this part, . . . or any provision in a local ordinance that is similar to a provision in this part, and the violations are so extensive and of such a nature that the health and safety of residents or the public is substantially endangered, the enforcement agency may issue an order or notice to repair or abate pursuant to this part.  Any order or notice pursuant to this subdivision shall be provided either by both posting a copy of the order or notice in a conspicuous place on the property and by first–class mail to each affected residential unit, or by posting a copy of the order or notice in a conspicuous place on the property and in a prominent place on each affected residential unit.”

Health & Safety Code § 17980(c)(1), states in pertinent part, “Whenever the enforcement agency has inspected or caused to be inspected a building and has determined that the building is a substandard building or a building described in Section 17920.10, the enforcement agency shall commence proceedings to abate the violation by repair, rehabilitation, vacation, or demolition of the building.  The enforcement agency shall not require the vacating of a residential building unless it concurrently requires expeditious demolition or repair to comply with this part, the building standards published in the California Building Standards Code, or other rules and regulations adopted pursuant to this part.  The owner shall have the choice of repairing or demolishing.  However, if the owner chooses to repair, the enforcement agency shall require that the building be brought into compliance according to a reasonable and feasible schedule for expeditious repair.  The enforcement agency may require vacation and demolition or may itself vacate the building, repair, demolish, or institute any other appropriate action or proceeding, if any of the following occur:  (A) The repair work is not done within the period required by the notice.  (B) The owner does not make a timely choice of repair or demolition.  (C) The owner selects an option which cannot be completed within a reasonable period of time, as determined by the enforcement agency, for any reason, including, but not limited to, an outstanding judicial or administrative order.”

CCP §1822.50 states:  “An inspection warrant is an order, in writing, in the name of the people, signed by a judge of a court of record, directed to a state or local official, commanding him to conduct any inspection required or authorized by state or local law or regulation relating to building, fire, safety, plumbing, electrical, health, labor, or zoning.”

CCP §1822.51 states:  “An inspection warrant shall be issued upon cause, unless some other provision of state or federal law makes another standard applicable.  An inspection warrant shall be supported by an affidavit, particularly describing the place, dwelling, structure, premises, or vehicle to be inspected and the purpose for which the inspection is made.  In addition, the affidavit shall contain either a statement that consent to inspect has been sought and refused or facts or circumstances reasonably justifying the failure to seek such consent.”

CCP §1822.52 states:  “Cause shall be deemed to exist if either reasonable legislative or administrative standards for conducting a routine or area inspection are satisfied with respect to the particular place, dwelling, structure, premises, or vehicle, or there is reason to believe that a condition of nonconformity exists with respect to the particular place, dwelling, structure, premises, or vehicle.”

DISCUSSION

Applicant City of Manhattan Beach requests an abatement order against the real property located at 116 9th Street, Manhattan Beach, Los Angeles County Assessor’s Parcel No. 4179-014-017, owned by the William Victor Revocable Trust, trustee William Victor.  See Ezra Siegel decl., Exh. C.

The City seeks:

(1)   Authority to enter the real property for the sole and exclusive purpose of demolishing the three-story residential apartment building (structure), and abating any nuisance thereupon, including but not limited to, removing debris or foliage constituting a fire hazard on the property;

(2)   Authority to enter the real property with or without the owner’s presence, and to restrict entry onto the property, including by the owner and his agents and representatives;

(3)   That the warrant may be executed following 24 hours in writing, which will be posted on the property;

(4)   That the warrant be effective from the order date and for a period not to exceed 90 days, and shall be returned to the court within that period;

(5)   That the warrant may be executed only between the hours of 8:00 a.m. and 6:00 p.m. and by City staff members, technical assistants, law enforcement, personnel, attorneys, contractors, engineers, and other experts whose professional and expert advice and assistance are necessary to remedy the conditions that exist on the property.

The City contends that the property is a nuisance and contains extensive building code, fire code, health and safety code, and municipal code violations that substantially endanger the health and safety of the public, in violation of Health & Safety Code §17980(a).  On July 29, 2022, a fire severely damaged the three-story residential apartment on the property.  On August 2, 2022, the City’s Senior Building Inspector Doug Atkisson inspected the structure and placed a “red-tag” on it, deeming it unsafe to occupy.  Ryan Heise (Building Official for City of Manhattan Beach) decl., ¶3, Exhs. A, B.  On August 11, 2022, Victor’s insurance company sent a structural engineer to inspect the structure.  The engineer deemed the structure “not suitable for occupancy.”  The engineer recommended “an inspection by the Engineer after all debris is removed to further assess the condition of the structure.”  The engineer indicated that the structure could be entered for the sole purpose of removing personal property.  The City modified the red-tag to yellow-tag under the assumption that Victor would follow his engineer’s recommendations to remove the debris, re-inspect, and to secure permits to remediate the fire damage.  Id., ¶4, Exh. C.  Between August and November 2022, the City communicated steps for the fire damage repair to Victor.  On November 22, 2022, Victor submitted initial construction plans through his engineer.  On December 8, 2022, declarant Heise informed Victor’s engineer that the scope of work proposed in the plans did not match the site conditions.  For example, the engineer proposed keeping the existing roof rafters although there was visible fire damage.  Id., ¶5.  On December 30, 2022, Victor submitted revised plans.  The City conducted further reviews, and on January 30, 2023, the City returned the plans with corrections.  The City did not approve the permit and waited for Victor to resubmit corrected plans but never did.  Over the next several months, declarant Heise had multiple discussions with Victor, several contractors, architects, and engineers, none of whom committed to doing the work for Victor.  Id., ¶6.  On May 11, 2023, Heise conducted an exterior inspection of the structure and observed structural damages and further deterioration.  The structure was deemed unsafe and “red-tagged.”  Id., ¶7, Exh. D.

Heise further states in his declaration that on May 18, 2023, he sent Victor a letter advising that the structure was in violation of the 2022 Cal. Building Code, section 116.  Id., ¶8, Exh. E.  On April 14, 2023, the City initiated a code enforcement case due to complaints from surrounding residents.  On May 16, 2023, the City’s Code Enforcement Office issued and sent Victor a First Notice of Violation, requiring Victor to submit a Statement of Intent by May 25, 2023, which was not received.  Id., ¶9, Exh. F.  The City attempted to set up a meeting with Heise, Victor, and Kevin Bass, but, after rescheduling three times, Victor failed to appear.  Id., ¶10.  On August 2, 2023, the City received complaints of trespassers inside the property.  Id., ¶11, Exh. G.  On October 3, 2023, Victor submitted a letter to the City stating what was required to make the property safe enough to remove personal property.  Id., ¶12.  On October 27, 2023, Heise’s office posted a Notice and Order to Abate under Health & Safety Code §17980.6 on the property on a conspicuous place.  Id., ¶13, Exh. H.  Heise opines that the conditions of the structure are unsafe and pose a great threat to the neighboring properties and the general public and that the structure is a dangerous condition and requires immediate demolition to abate the nuisance and ensure the safety of the public and nearby residents.  The building is a substandard building in violation of Health & Safety Code §17980(c)(1).  Id., ¶14.  The cost of the demolition is estimated at $55,300, and will include removal of personal property and hazardous waste removal.  Id., ¶5.  See also declaration of the City’s Fire Marshal Kevin Bass, who states that “[c]onsidering the condition of the Structure, the ability to easily ignite another fire to the building and/or the abutting vegetation, and the danger to the surrounding residents, the general public, and the responding emergency personnel, I have determined that the Structure is an ‘Unsafe Structure’ and dangerous condition in violation of the Fire Code.”  Kevin Bass decl., ¶5.

The City asserts that on September 6, 2023, the City held a Council meeting, in which Victor and his attorney were present, and after hearing testimony, the Council declared the property a nuisance and gave Victor 30 days to abate it.  See Ezra Siegel decl., ¶6, Exh. E.

There is no opposition.

            The court rules as follows:  The court finds that applicant City has shown that expeditious demolition of the property is required in compliance with Health & Safety Code §17980(c)(1).  Owner William Victor was given a reasonable time and opportunity to repair or abate the purported nuisance and dangerous condition on the property and he has not done so.

RULING

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the 5th cause of action in the FAC.  The motion to strike is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On May 3, 2023, plaintiff Janie Vieyra filed a complaint against General Motors, LLC for violation of (1) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(d), (2) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(b), (3) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(a)(3), (4) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability, and (5) fraudulent concealment.

On August 25, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . . , or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

            Demurrer

Defendant GM demurs to the 5th cause of action for fraudulent concealment in the FAC on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

            The FAC alleges that on February 15, 2017, plaintiff entered into a warranty contract with GM regarding a 2018 Cadillac XT5 vehicle, which was manufactured and or distributed by GM.  Subject vehicle was purchased at Penske Cadillac of South Bay in Cerritos.  FAC, ¶6.  The warranty contract contained various warranties, including the bumper-to-bumper warranty, powertrain warranty, and emission warranty.  Id., ¶7.  Defects and nonconformities to warranty manifested themselves within the applicable express warranty period, including transmission defects, infotainment defects, electrical defects, suspension defects, body defects, brake system defects, and engine defects.  Id., ¶11.  GM has failed to either promptly relace the vehicle or to promptly make restitution in accordance with Song-Beverly.  Id., ¶14.

            The FAC further alleges that on July 11, 2020, with 19,861 miles on the odometer, plaintiff presented the vehicle to defendant’s authorized repair facility with various concerns, including airbag concerns.  The repair facility performed warranty repairs and represented to plaintiff that the vehicle had been repaired.  Id., ¶21.  On July 13, 2021, with 28,155 miles on the odometer, plaintiff presented the vehicle to defendant’s authorized repair facility with various concerns, including airbag concerns.  The repair facility performed warranty repairs and represented to plaintiff that the vehicle had been repaired.  Id., ¶22.  On September 28, 2021, with 29,923 miles on the odometer, plaintiff presented the vehicle to defendant’s authorized repair facility with various concerns, including airbag concerns.  The repair facility performed warranty repairs.  The vehicle remained at the repair facility for 32 days after which the repair facility represented to plaintiff that the vehicle had been repaired.  Id., ¶23.

5th cause of action for fraudulent inducement - concealment

“’The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of fact.’”  Hambridge v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal. App. 4th 124, 162 (citation omitted).  “One who willfully deceives another with intent to induce him to alter his position to his injury or risk, is liable for any damage which he thereby suffers.”  Civil Code §1709.

“[T]o establish fraud through nondisclosure or concealment of facts, it is necessary to show the defendant ‘was under a legal duty to disclose them.’”  OCM Principal Opportunities Fund v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 835, 845.  Nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud when:  (1) there is a fiduciary relationship between the parties; (2) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.  Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum Commission v. Insomniac, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal. App. 4th 803, 831.

Rules of specifically pleading how, when, where, to whom, and by what means, misrepresentations were communicated, is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations, and not to concealment.  See Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal. App. 4th 1356, 1384.  The specificity requirement of fraud is “relaxed when it is apparent from the allegations that the defendant necessarily possesses knowledge of the facts.”  Quelimane Co. v. Steward Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal. 4th 26, 27.

The FAC alleges that GM committed fraud by allowing the vehicle to be sold to plaintiff without disclosing that the vehicle and its 8-speed transmission were defective and susceptible to sudden and premature failure.  Plaintiff purchased the vehicle equipped with GM’s defective 8-speed transmission.  FAC, ¶64.  Prior to plaintiff acquiring the vehicle, GM was well aware and knew that the 8-speed transmission installed on the vehicle was defective but failed to disclose this fact to plaintiff prior to and at the time of sale and thereafter.  Id., ¶65.  Specifically, GM knew that the 8-speed transmission had one or more defects that can result in various problems, including hard or harsh shifts, jerking, hesitation on acceleration, surging and/or inability to control the vehicle’s speed, acceleration, or deceleration.  These conditions present a safety hazard and are unreasonably dangerous to consumers because they can suddenly and unexpectedly dangerous to consumers because they can suddenly and unexpectedly cause the driver to be unable to control the speed and acceleration/deceleration of the vehicle.  Such unexpected inability to control the vehicle’s speed and acceleration/deceleration thereby exposes plaintiff and her passengers to serious risk or accident and injury.  Id., ¶66.  GM acquired its knowledge of the Transmission Defect prior to plaintiff acquiring the vehicle, through sources not available to consumers such as plaintiffs, including pre-production and post-production testing data, early consumer complaints about the Transmission Defect made directly to GM and its network of dealers, aggregate warranty data compiled from GM’s network of dealers, testing conducted by GM in response to these complaints, as well as warranty repair and part replacements data received by GM from GM’s network of dealers.  Id., ¶67.

The FAC also alleges that plaintiff is a reasonable consumer who interacted with sales representatives, considered GM’s advertisement, and/or other marketing materials concerning GM vehicles prior to purchasing vehicle.  Had GM and its dealership revealed the Transmission Defect in these disclosures, plaintiff would have been aware of it and would not have purchased the vehicle.  For example, GM marketed and sold its new 8-speed automatic transmissions as having “world-class performance” rivaling top performance vehicles, lightning-fast and smooth shifting, along with improved fuel efficiency, among other representations.  GM’s own press release dated January 13, 2014 introduced the new 8-speed transmission as being “tuned for world-class shift-response times,” and “delivering shift performance that rivals the dual-clutch/semi-automatic transmissions found in many supercars—but with the smoothness and refinement that comes with a conventional automatic fitted with a torque converter.”  Id., ¶68.  However, prior to plaintiff’s purchase of the vehicle, GM was internally referring the 8-speed transmission as a “neck snapper.”  GM engineers even considered stopping production in 2015 (but did not) and in 2016, President Johan de Nysschen acknowledged customer frustration surrounding the Transmission Defect internally and meeting with its authorized repair facility.  Nonetheless, GM continued to conceal the Transmission Defect from consumers, including in its marketing materials, and advised any complaining customers that poor shifts were “normal.”  Id., ¶69.  GM’s Mark Gordon lamented in February 2019 that “shift quality issues are an ongoing concern with the 8-speed transmission.  Unfortunately, these issues have been through an Op-ex and a service solution is not going to be developed due to cost.”  Id., ¶70.  From September 2014 to at least February 2019, GM issued many service bulletins and service bulletin updates to its dealers in the U.S but not its customers, acknowledging problems of harsh shifting, jerking, clunking, and delays in acceleration or deceleration relating to the 8-speed transmission.  Id., ¶71.  While GM knew about the Transmission Defect, and its safety risks since prior to plaintiff’s purchase of the vehicle, GM concealed and failed to disclose the defective nature of the vehicle, its 8-speed transmission to plaintiff prior to and at the time of sale.  Had plaintiff know of the Transmission Defect, plaintiff would not have purchased the vehicle.  Id., ¶72.  GM continued to conceal the defect and its inability to repair it, making it difficult for plaintiff to discover GM’s wrongdoing.  Id., ¶73.  GM was under a duty to plaintiff to disclose the defective nature of the vehicle, its transmission, its safety consequences and/or the associated repair costs.  Id., ¶75.

                        Statute of limitations

“A demurrer based on a statute of limitations will not lie where the action may be, but is not necessarily, barred.  In order for the bar of the statute of limitations to be raised by demurrer, the defect must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint; it is not enough that the complaint shows that the action may be barred.”  Geneva Towers Ltd. Partnership v. City and County of San Francisco (2003) 29 Cal. 4th 769, 781 (citation omitted).

The FAC alleges tolling of the statute of limitations under equitable tolling, the discovery rule, equitable estoppel, the repair rule, and/or class action tolling.  FAC, ¶¶27-43.  Plaintiff alleges that she discovered defendant’s wrongful conduct shortly before filing the complaint as the vehicle continued to exhibit symptoms of defects following defendant’s unsuccessful attempts to repair them.  Id., ¶28.

Defendant argues that this cause of action is barred by the three-year statute of limitations under CCP §338 and that plaintiff cannot invoke the delayed discovery rule. 

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the complaint was timely filed and that the running of the statute of limitations does not appear “clearly and affirmatively” on the face of the FAC.  Plaintiff also asserts that the claim did not accrue as of the date of sale; rather it accrued once plaintiff discovered the facts constituting the fraud.  Plaintiff contends that she alleges that the wrongful conduct began on September 14, 2017, when she purchased the subject vehicle and that the fraudulent inducement-concealment continued throughout various repair visits to GM’s authorized repair facilities and/or dealerships.  She argues that she had not way of uncovering GM’s deception with respect to the Transmission Defect given that GM performed repairs under warranty and claimed that nothing was wrong with the vehicle or that the vehicle had been repaired.  Plaintiff also argues that the discovery rule delayed expiry of plaintiff’s claim.

The court finds that whether the statute of limitations bars the claim does not appear “clearly and affirmatively” and that, in any event, the allegations are sufficient as to tolling based on delayed discovery.

            Whether allegations are sufficient

            Defendant further argues that plaintiff has not pled with the requisite specificity and facts to support the elements.  Defendant contends that the FAC does not allege whether plaintiff had any interaction with GM before or after the sale, GM’s knowledge of the alleged “defects,” and how GM intended to “defraud” plaintiff.

            Defendant also argues that plaintiff fails to allege a transactional relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose on the part of GM.

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations meet the elements.  Further, plaintiff argues, a “transactional relationship” is not required under California law for the manufacturer to have a duty to disclose.

The court finds that the allegations are sufficient to support the elements at the pleading stage for fraudulent concealment.  Plaintiff alleges that defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact.  See FAC, ¶¶64, 69, 74, 77.  Plaintiff alleges that defendant had a duty to disclose the fact.  See FAC, ¶75.  See OCM Principal Opportunities Fund, L.P. v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 835, 859 (“Under California law, a vendor has a duty to disclose material facts not only to immediate purchasers, but also to subsequent purchasers when the vendor has reason to expect that the item will be resold.”); see also Khan v. Shiley Inc. (1990) 217 Cal. App. 3d 848, 858 (“[A] manufacturer of a product may be liable for fraud when it conceals material product information from potential users.”).  Plaintiff alleges that defendant intentionally concealed the fact with an intent to defraud.  The court can infer that defendant placed the vehicle into the stream of commerce, presumably for profit.  Plaintiff was unaware of the fact, and she would not have purchased the vehicle, had she known of such defects and sustained damages.  See FAC, ¶¶68, 72.   Further, plaintiff alleges that defendant had exclusive knowledge.  See FAC, ¶¶66, 67.  Moreover, noting that the specificity requirement is “relaxed” when defendant possesses knowledge of the facts and reading the allegations liberally and in context, the court finds that plaintiff has pled with sufficient particularity.

            Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

            Motion to Strike

            Defendant GM requests that the court strike the demand for punitive damages in the prayer for relief.

            Defendant argues the same as in its demurrer that plaintiff has not pled a viable fraud claim and such a claim is barred by GM’s defenses.

            In light of the ruling on the demurrer overruling it as to the 5th cause of action for fraudulent concealment, the motion to strike is DENIED.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.
