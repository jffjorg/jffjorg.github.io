Case Number: 19STCV23068    Hearing Date: October 1, 2024    Dept: W

JINING ZHOU vs SAID MOHAMADREZA SIADATAN, et al.

 

defendant’s motion to compel further responses from plaintiff jining zhou

 

Date of Hearing:        October 1, 2024                                Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        19STCV23068

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Shenshen New World II, LLC dba Sheraton Universal Hotel

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Jining Zhou

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Lee Decl. ¶¶6-8.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 1, 2019, Plaintiff Jining Zhou (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Said Mohamadreza Siadatan, Silverado Senior Living, Inc., Shenzhen New World II, LLC dba Sheraton Universal Hotel (“the Sheraton”), Enterprise Rental, and Does 1 through 100 for damages arising a motor vehicle v. pedestrian accident in a parking lot. On September 16, 2020, Plaintiff filed amendments to complaint naming Towne Park, LLC (“Towne”) as Doe 1 and Mile Hi Valet Service, Inc. (“MHVS”) as Doe 2. The trial date is currently set for December 30, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Shenzhen New World II, LLC’s Motion to Compel Further Responses From Plaintiff Jining Zhou to Request for Admissions, Set One is MOOT

 

Defendant Shenzhen New World II, LLC’s Motion to Compel Further Responses From Plaintiff Jining Zhou to Form Interrogatories, Set Two is MOOT

 

Defendant Shenzhen New World II, LLC’s Motion to Compel Further Responses From Plaintiff Jining Zhou to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two is MOOT

 

DISCUSSION

 

The motions to Compel Further Responses from Plaintiff Jining Zhou to Requests for Admissions, Set One; Compel Further Responses from Plaintiff Jining Zhou to Form Interrogatories, Set Two; and Compel Further Responses from Plaintiff Jining Zhou to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two are MOOT. Plaintiff Zhou served further responses on May 7, 2024.

 

Defendant Sheraton still seeks sanctions against Plaintiff Zhou and his counsel of record, jointly and severally, in the amount of $3,497.50 per motion. Defendant Sheraton asserts they were forced to file multiple motions because Plaintiff refused provide non-deficient discovery responses to Sheraton's subject discovery. Although the IDC was May 7, 2024, Sheraton’s deadline to file these motions was April 26, 2024 and thus, was forced to bring these motions due to Plaintiff’s delay in bringing the responses promised.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Sheraton fails to establish the relevant factors necessary for this court to exercise its discretion in granting a large sanctions penalty. Moreover, the issue was resolved and mooted before the IDC was had on May 7, 2024 yet Defendant Sheraton pressed forwarded with the motions.

 

The court finds sanctions warranted. Although Plaintiff served responses before the IDC, Plaintiff was aware of Sheraton’s deadline to file the instant three motions. Moreover, the original responses were due over 4 months before Plaintiff served the responses. However, the court agrees the request to pay over $10,492.50 in sanctions is unreasonable.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS the request for sanctions in the reduced amount of $2,260.00 for the first motion plus $335 for the second motion and $335 for the third motion. The court finds the motions nearly identical. Moreover, only one appearance was necessary for the motions and meeting and conferring was part of counsel’s statutory obligations.

defendant eugene dula, m.d.’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action in the complaint

Moving Party:            Defendant Eugene Dula, M.D.

Responding Party:     No opposition  

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (McColgan Decl. ¶¶5-6.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On April 29, 2024, Plaintiffs Elias Olson and Rosalinda Olson filed a complaint against Dr. Eugene Dula, M.D. for medical malpractice and medical negligence. Plaintiffs allege Plaintiff Elias Olson is a patient of Defendant Dr. Dula and on November 2, 2022, Mr. Olson underwent a computed tomography scan at Defendant’s bequest for preventive testing purposes. Plaintiff further alleges the next day, Dr. Dula received a comprehensive report detailing the results of the CTscan and on November 19, 2022, Plaintiff presented to the emergency room at Kaiser Hospital with severe abdominal pain. Plaintiff discovered it was a mass.

 

Since the demurrer was filed, plaintiff Rosalinda Olson has filed a one page opposition, indicating that plaintiff Elias Olson passed away on July 28, 2024 and she seeks to represent the estate.  Further, she indicates that she intends to drop herself as a named plaintiff, and is looking for an attorney to help her with all of this.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Eugene Dula, M.D.’s Demurrer to the First and Second Causes of Action in the Complaint is SUSTAINED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Eugene Dula, M.D. demurs to the first and second causes of action in the complaint on the grounds there are no claims brought by Ms. Rosalinda Olson although she is listed as a plaintiff in the caption of the lawsuit, the complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to support a claim for medical practice, Mr. Olson may only bring one cause of action to vindicate a primary right, and the claims are time barred under the one-year provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5. Defendant Dr. Dula also moves to strike the words “malicious” and “willful disregard” from the complaint.

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 (internal citations omitted).) 

 

Statute of Limitations

 

Defendant Dr. Dula demurs to the complaint on the grounds the claim is barred by the one-year provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5 applies.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5¿mandates that an action for injury or death against a health care provider based on alleged professional negligence must be commenced within three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers or should have discovered the injury with reasonable diligence, whichever comes first.¿ The statute emphasizes that “[i]n no event shall the time for commencement of legal action exceed three years unless tolled for one of three specific reasons (fraud, intentional concealment, or presence of a foreign body).  (CCP. § 340.5.)  

 

Defendant Dr. Dula argues the one year-provision applies because as alleged, on November 19, 2022, Mr. Olson was informed of the mass. (Compl. ¶20.) Because Plaintiff discovered the injury on that date, the one-year provision applies. Therefore, Plaintiff should have brought his complaint no later than November 19, 2023; however, the complaint was not brought until April 29, 2024. The complaint also alleges they served their Code of Civil Procedure section 364 Notice of Intent on January 31, 2024. However, this was served after the statute of limitations ran. Accordingly, the Notice did not extend the statute of limitations by 90 days per Section 364.

 

Unless Plaintiff proffers one of the exceptions to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5, the court is inclined to sustain the demurrer without leave to amend. The court will briefly address Defendant Dr. Dula’s other arguments below.

 

Mrs. Olson

 

Defendant Dr. Dula demurs to the complaint on the grounds that although Mrs. Olson is listed as a plaintiff in the caption of the lawsuit, no claims are brought on her behalf.

 

A complaint filed by someone other than the real party in interest is subject to general demurrer on the ground that it fails to state a cause of action. (Doe v. Lincoln Unified School Dist. (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 758.)

 

The only allegations concerning Mrs. Olson are that Dr. Dula owed her a duty. (Complaint ¶10.) However, it long has been held that an essential element of a cause of action for medical malpractice is a physician-patient relationship giving rise to a duty of care. (Felton v. Schaeffer (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 229, 235, review den. Aug. 1, 1991; Keene v. Wiggins (1977) 69 Cal.App.3d 308, 313–314.) Because there are no allegations of a physician-patient relationship or any other duty, the court finds Mrs. Olson’s claims fail to state a cause of action against Dr. Dula.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED on these grounds. Ms. Olson has indicated she may be withdrawing as a plaintiff in any event.   If Olson intends to proceed as the personal representative or on behalf of the estate, she must take appropriate steps to substitute into the case in that capacity.  

 

Primary Right

 

Defendant Dr. Dula next demurs to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiffs’ claims for medical malpractice and medical negligence rely on the same facts in the complaint and both involve the same primary right – the right to be free from negligent medical treatment and care – violating the rule that a plaintiff is entitled to a single recovery for "each item of compensable damage" despite the number or nature of legal theories alleged. (Crowley v. Katleman (1994) 8 Cal.4th 666, 681, as modified Nov. 30, 1994.)

 

“The primary right theory is a theory of code pleading that has long been followed in California. It provides that a ‘cause of action’ is comprised of a ‘primary right’ of the plaintiff, a corresponding ‘primary duty’ of the defendant, and a wrongful act by the defendant constituting a breach of that duty. [Citation.] The most salient characteristic of a primary right is that it is indivisible: the violation of a single primary right gives rise to but a single cause of action. [Citation.] … […] The primary right theory … is invoked … when a plaintiff attempts to divide a primary right and enforce it in two suits. (Crowley, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 681.)

 

Defendant Dr. Dula argues Mr. Olson can use different legal theories when seeking recovery for a violation of a primary right. But where the theories of recovery arise from the same primary right, as here, plaintiffs cannot “split” the cause of action into separate claims. (Crowley, supra, 8 Cal.4th at 681-682.) In the instant case, the complaint does not allege facts sufficient to establish two claims against Dr. Dula for medical malpractice and medical negligence.

 

The court agrees each cause of action alleges invasion of the same “primary right”. The court is not sure that this is the proper grounds for demurrer, however. Either way, the causes of action are duplicative of one another.  The court therefore sustains the demurrer to the claim for medical negligence, without leave to amend.

JOSEPH KAMKAR vs OSCAR PARADA

 

demurrer to the complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        October 1, 2024                                Trial Date:       None Set  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        24VECV03617

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Oscar Parada

Responding Party:     No opposition

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an unlawful detainer action.  On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff Joseph Kamkar filed an unlawful detainer complaint against Defendant Oscar Parada for nonpayment of rent.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Oscar Parada’s Demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Oscar Parada demurs to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiff failed to give proper three-day notice and Defendant did not receive personal service of the summons and complaint.

 

The basic elements of unlawful detainer are “(1) the tenant is in possession of the premises; (2) that possession is without permission; (3) the tenant is in default for nonpayment of rent; (4) the tenant has been properly served with a written three-day notice; and (5) the default continues after the three-day notice period has elapsed.”  (Kruger v. Reyes (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th Supp. 10, 16.)   

 

The court finds the complaint proper. Defendant generally concludes that the 3-day notice was defective on its face but fails to explain how. When ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true.  (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) Here, Plaintiff has attached the 3-day notice, which appears to have been properly served on Defendant. Defendant also argues he did not receive personal service, contrary to the proof of service attached to the complaint.  However, the proper action would have been a motion to quash service. (See Delta Imports, Inc v. Municipal Court (1983) 146 Cal.App.3d 1033, 1036.) Defendant’s reliance on Hinman v. Wagnon (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 24 is misplaced since that was a demurrer as to whether the notice itself was sufficient – not whether the party was properly served.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is OVERRULED.
