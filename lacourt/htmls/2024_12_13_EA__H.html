DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV03996    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: H

Deuschel v. Atlas Springs Company, Inc., et al., Case No. 23PSCV03996

 

ORDER ON DEMURRER

The Court will reach the merits of Defendants’ demurrer provided that Defendants cure the notice deficiency identified below at or before the time of hearing or Plaintiff appears at the December 13, 2024 hearing despite the notice deficiency. Assuming these conditions are met, the Court intends to rule as follows: Defendants’ demurrer is sustained as to the third cause of action but is otherwise overruled. The Court grants Plaintiff 20 days’ leave to amend. Any First Amended Complaint filed by Plaintiff must be signed. In the event Plaintiff chooses not to amend his complaint, Plaintiff must file a signature page for his complaint forthwith.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Michael Deuschel (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows: 

          Plaintiff, an adaptive person, owns a 1998 Dodge B3500 converted camper van (the “subject vehicle”) that serves as his dwelling. Plaintiff contracted with Atlas Springs Company, Inc. (“Atlas Springs”) to repair the subject vehicle in June 2021. Atlas Springs’ Chief Executive Officer is Fernando Gutierrez (“Gutierrez”). When Plaintiff complained about Atlas Springs’ failed products and workmanship, Atlas Springs stole the subject vehicle and extorted him to sign a liability release before they would release the subject vehicle to him.

On December 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Atlas Springs and Gutierrez (together “Defendants”), as well as Does 1-20 for:

           1.       Breach of Contract,

     2.       Negligent Repair,

     3.       Product Liability,

     4.       Personal Injury,

     5.       The Americans with Disabilities Act, 42 U.S.C. § 121011 et seq.,

     6.       California Civil Code § 51 et seq., The Unruh Civil Rights Act,

     7.       Negligence, and

     8.       Intentional and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress. 

Pending before the Court is Defendants’ demurrer to the complaint. 


II.      Legal Standard

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

 

III.     Discussion 

Atlas Springs and Gutierrez (together, “Defendants”) demur, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, to the first through eighth causes of action Plaintiff’s complaint, on the basis that they each fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and are uncertain.

 

          A.       Notice 

At the outset, the demurrer originally came on calendar for hearing on October 2, 2024; the Court, at that time, continued the hearing to the instant date and directed Defendants to give notice to Plaintiff “forthwith and to file proof of service of said notice at least nine court days prior to the continued hearing date.” 

There is nothing on the docket reflecting that Defendants’ counsel provided notice of the December 13, 2024 hearing date to Plaintiff. The following analysis, then, is contingent upon Defendants’ counsel filing a proof of service, on or before the hearing, reflecting notice to Plaintiff of the instant hearing date. 

The Court notes that Plaintiff made an appearance on October 2, 2024, when the hearing was continued. In the event Plaintiff appears on December 13, 2024, the Court will proceed to the merits of the demurrer.

 

          B.       Meet and Confer         

On October 2, 2024, the Court elected to continue the hearing on the instant demurrer to December 13, 2024 on the basis that it was not accompanied by a Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 meet and confer declaration. Defendants’ counsel has since filed a compliant declaration.

 

          C.       Merits 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff’s complaint includes causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Negligent Repair, (3) Product Liability, (4) Personal Injury, (5) The Americans with Disabilities Act (42 U.S.C. § 121011 et seq.), (6) California Civil Code § 51 et seq. (The Unruh Civil Rights Act), (7) Negligence, and (8) Intentional and Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress. 

Defendants purport to demur to each and every cause of action in the complaint; however, the demurrer contains no analysis of the sixth through eighth causes of action. The Court summarily overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth through eighth causes of action on this basis.

 

First Cause of Action (i.e., Breach of Contract) 

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) 

Defendants assert that Plaintiff fails to specify the essential terms of the contract and what provisions were breached. The Court disagrees. Plaintiff has alleged that “Defendants evaluated Plaintiff’s 1998 Dodge van for suspension repairs/rebuild and front wheel alignment,” “advised [him] to rebuild his front and rear suspension,” “instructed him to obtain specific front-end steering and suspension replacement parts,” “advised him to add two new rear leaf springs (per side) to the existing five-leaf-springs sets for additional support,” and “agreed to install new bushings and u-bolts during their installation.” (Complaint, ¶ 6.) 

Plaintiff has alleged that he “accepted the contract,” “obtained the replacement parts for his front and rear suspensions and steering at his expense” and “presented the parts to Defendants.” (Id., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff has alleged that “Defendants rebuilt the front-end suspension and steering,” “manufactured two new rear leaf-springs and installed them to existing five-leaf-spring sets in the rear of the van for a total of seven leaf-springs, per side,” “removed the existing shock mounts that were welded to the rear axle and rewelded two new shock mounts in place,” “installed two new rear shocks” and subcontracted the alignment to a nearby alignment mechanic/shop,” thereafter advising that the work was complete. (Id., ¶¶ 8-12.) Plaintiff has alleged that “he paid for the work and Defendants returned [his] keys and vehicle to him.” (Id., ¶ 12.) 

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants breached the contract by misrepresenting the status of the alignment, claiming it was realigned when it was not, and by making “multiple workmanship errors,” which are identified in Paragraph 8.[FN1]

Defendants also assert that Plaintiff “fails to provide evidence of damages” and complain that he “fails to provide any invoices, receipts, or specific amounts paid that would substantiate the alleged costs incurred.” (Dem., 4:22 and 5:4-5.) Plaintiff need not attach documentary evidence of his damages to his complaint. Plaintiff has alleged that he was damaged because he “had to pay for urgent repairs” and “must obtain additional repairs.” (Id., ¶ 10.)[FN2] Plaintiff has also alleged that Defendants refuse to repair their flawed work. (Id., ¶¶ 11 and 13.)[FN3]

Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

 

Second Cause of Action (i.e., Negligent Repair)

A claim for negligent repair requires pleading the elements of negligence, that is “duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.” (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.)

Defendants assert that Plaintiff “fails to specify what repairs were conducted, how they were negligent, and how this alleged negligence directly resulted in damages.” (Dem., 6:11-13.) They further assert Plaintiff has not alleged the existence of a duty. The Court disagrees. Plaintiff has alleged the existence of a duty in Paragraph 27 and has pled breach, causation, and damages as set forth above.

Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action is overruled.

 

Third Cause of Action (i.e., Product Liability)

“[I]n a products liability case the plaintiff in order to recover in strict liability in tort must prove that he was injured by a defect in the product and that the product was defective when it left the hands of the retailer or manufacturer; whereas to recover in negligence the plaintiff must prove the same two elements plus an additional element, namely, that the defect in the product was due to negligence of the defendant.” (Jiminez v. Sears, Roebuck & Co. (1971) 4 Cal.3d 379, 383.)

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants are the manufacturer of the rear leaf springs. (Complaint, ¶ 29.) Plaintiff, however, has not alleged that the product itself was defective or that it caused him injury.

Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained, with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Fourth Cause of Action (i.e., Personal Injury)

Defendants assert that Plaintiff has failed to allege that their conduct was the proximate cause of his injuries. (Dem., 6:24-25.) The Court disagrees. “The term ‘personal injury’ . . . includes not only physical injury, but also affront or assault to the emotional well-being of a person.” (Chatton v. National Union Fire Ins. Co. (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 846, 855.) 

In addition to the allegations set forth above, Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants tried to get Plaintiff to sign a unilateral release of liability and that, after he refused, they stole the subject vehicle, refused to return it, and “threatened that they would make him miss his medical appointments by withholding his van from him until he signed.” (Complaint, ¶¶ 13, 15 and 16.) Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants’ acts and omissions “caused personal injury to Plaintiff (Promisee), who sustained injuries other than economic loss.” (Complaint, ¶ 31.)

Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is overruled.

Fifth Cause of Action (i.e., The Americans with Disabilities Act, 42 U.S.C. § 121011 et seq.)

“A Title III claim [of the ADA] . . . requires that a plaintiff establish that (1) he or she is disabled within the meaning of the ADA; (2) that the defendants own, lease, or operate a place of public accommodation; and (3) that the defendants discriminated against the plaintiff within the meaning of the ADA.” (Roberts v. Royal Atlantic Corp. (2d Cir. 2008) 542 F.3d 363, 368).

The Court determines that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged these elements, in Paragraphs 15-19, 36-39, and 41. Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action is overruled. 


Any First Amended Complaint filed by Plaintiff must be signed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 446(a), 128.7(a).) In the event Plaintiff chooses not to amend his complaint, Plaintiff must file a signature page for his complaint forthwith.

Case Number: 24PSCV00336    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: H

140 North Orange Investors, LLC v. Baldwin Sun, Inc., Case No. 24PSCV00336

ORDER ON MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

Counsel for Defendant Baldwin Sun, Inc.’s (i.e., David D. Fu) Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED, effective upon the filing of a proof of service showing service of the signed order upon the Client at the Client’s last known address. The Court sets an Order to Show Cause Re: Representation of Corporation for March 3, 2025, at 8:30 a.m.

 

Background

This is an unlawful detainer action involving the commercial premises located at 140 N. Orange Avenue in the City of Industry (“subject property”).

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiff 140 North Orange Investors, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint for Unlawful Detainer against Baldwin Sun, Inc. and Does 1-10.

On May 13, 2024, “Judgment Pursuant to Stipulation for Judgment for Possession of Real Property” was entered.

Pending before the Court is David Fu’s motion to be relieved as counsel of record to Defendant Baldwin Sun, Inc. (the “Client”). For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted, effective upon the filing of proof service showing service of the signed order upon the Client.

 

Discussion

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398.)

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure § 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil form (MC-053)). The Court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court. (Cal. Rules Court, rule 3.1362(e).)

David Fu attests that “[t]he attorney-client relationship has deteriorated to the point where the attorney can no longer ethically continue representation.”

Fu states that he served the Client by mail at the Client’s last known address with copies of the motion papers served with his declaration and that he has confirmed within the past 30 days that the address is current, by mail, return receipt requested.

The motion is not opposed. The Court determines that the requirements of Rules of Court Rule 3.1362 enumerated above have been sufficiently met.

Accordingly, the motion is granted, effective upon the filing of a proof of service showing service of the signed order upon the Client at the Client’s last known address.  The Court sets an Order to Show Cause Re: Representation of Corporation for March 3, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. 

Case Number: 24PSCV02775    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: H

Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC v. ASE Construction, Inc., et al., Case No. 24PSCV02775

 

ORDER ON APPLICATIONS FOR WRIT OF POSSESSION

          1.       Plaintiff Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC’s Application for Writ of Possession After Hearing [i.e., as to Defendant ASE Construction, Inc.] is GRANTED, with no bond.  

          2.       Plaintiff Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC’s Application for Writ of Possession After Hearing [i.e., as to Defendant Sergio Moreno Morales] is GRANTED, with no bond.

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Crossroads Equipment Lease and Finance, LLC (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

          On February 12, 2021, Plaintiff and ASE Construction, Inc. (“ASE Construction”) entered into a Master Equipment Finance Agreement (“Agreement No. 1”), wherein Plaintiff agreed to finance the purchase of one 2021 Freightliner Model 114SD tractor truck, VIN No. 3ALHG3FE8MDML9466, with Superior SSTl00SD-21 Dump Body Serial number 9329 with Super Tag High Lift Gate (“9466 Freightliner”). Sergio Moreno Morales (“Morales”) executed a written guaranty on that date (“Guaranty No. 1”). On November 12, 2023 and thereafter, ASE Construction breached the terms of Agreement No. 1 by failing to make payments due. On April 8, 2021, Plaintiff and ASE Construction entered into another Master Equipment Finance Agreement (“Agreement No. 2), wherein Plaintiff agreed to finance the purchase of one 2021 Freightliner Model 114SD tractor truck, VIN No. 3ALHG3FE9MDMM2330, with Superior SST100 Supertag Tapered Truck Body Serial number 9339 (“2330 Freightliner”). Morales executed a written guaranty on that date (“Guaranty No. 2). On October 7, 2023 and thereafter, ASE Construction breached the terms of Agreement No. 2 by failing to make payments due. 

On August 27, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against ASE Construction, Morales and Does 1-100 for:

          1.       Breach of Written Agreements (2 Counts),

          2.       Breach of Guaranty (2 Counts),

          3.       Account Stated,

          4.       Unjust Enrichment,

          5.       Open Book Account,

          6.       Claim and Delivery, and

          7.       Conversion. 

On November 25, 2024, the Clerk entered default against ASE Construction and Morales. 

A Case Management Conference and an Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to File Proof of Service are set for January 29, 2025.

Pending before the Court are Plaintiff’s applications for writs of possession, whereby Plaintiff seeks to recover two tractor trucks now in the possession of ASE Construction and Morales. For the reasons stated herein, the applications are granted.

 

II.      Discussion 

Plaintiff applies for writs of possession after hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 512.010 et seq. Plaintiff seeks to recover the 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner, both in the possession of ASE Construction and Morales.

“Claim and delivery is a remedy by which a party with a superior right to a specific item of personal property (created, most commonly, by a contractual lien) may recover possession of that specific property before judgment.” (Waffer Internat. Corp. v. Khorsandi (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 1261, 1271.)

Procedural Considerations: No writ of possession may issue, except after a hearing on a noticed motion (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.020, subd. (a)) or by ex parte application satisfying the conditions of section 512.020, subdivision (b). Prior to the section 512.020 hearing, the defendant must be served with all of the following: “(a) A copy of the summons and complaint; (b) A Notice of Application and Hearing; (c) A copy of the application and any affidavit in support thereof.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.030, subd. (a).) “If the defendant has not appeared in the action, and a writ, notice, order, or other paper is required to be personally served on the defendant under this title, service shall be made in the same manner as a summons is served under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 413.10) of Title 5.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.030, subd. (b).)  

The “Notice of Application and Hearing” must inform the defendant of all of the following: (a) A hearing will be held at a place and at a time, to be specified in the notice, on plaintiff’s application for a writ of possession; (b) The writ will be issued if the court finds that the plaintiff's claim is probably (not actually) valid and the other requirements for issuing the writ are established; (c) If the defendant desires to oppose the issuance of the writ, he must file with the court either an affidavit providing evidence sufficient to defeat the plaintiff's right to issuance of the writ or an undertaking to stay the delivery of the property in accordance with section 515.020; (d) The notice must contain the following statement: “If you believe the plaintiff may not be entitled to possession of the property claimed, you may wish to seek the advice of an attorney. Such attorney should be consulted promptly so that he may assist you before the time set for the hearing.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.040.)

The Court determines that adequate notice was provided to ASE Construction and Morales.[FN] 


MERITS: As for the merits of the application, the Court must make its determinations “upon the basis of the pleadings and other papers in the record; but, upon good cause shown, the court may receive and consider additional evidence and authority produced at the hearing or may continue the hearing for the production of such additional evidence, oral or documentary, or the filing of other affidavits or points and authorities.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.050.) The Court must issue writ of possession if the plaintiff establishes the “probable validity” of his/her/its claim to possession of the property and the undertaking requirements of section 515.010 are satisfied. (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.060; RCA Service Co. v. Superior Court (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 1, 3.) “Probable validity” exists “where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 511.090; RCA Service Co., supra, 137 Cal.App.3d at 3.)

Probable validity, in this case, has been established pursuant to the declaration of Plaintiff’s general counsel Rebecca Elli (“Elli”) and the exhibits attached thereto. Elli attests, and/or the relevant documents reflect, as follows: 

          On or about February 12, 2021, ASE Construction entered into Agreement No. 1, wherein Plaintiff agreed to finance the purchase of the 9466 Freightliner. (Elli Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 1.) Morales executed Guaranty No. 1 on that same date. (Id., ¶ 11, Exh. 3.) Plaintiff perfected its interest in the 9466 Freightliner by obtaining a Certificate of Title from the State of California Department of Motor Vehicles (“DMV”). (Id., ¶ 5, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff performed all of the terms and conditions of the Agreement No. 1 required to be performed by it. (Id., ¶ 6.) On or about November 12, 2023 and thereafter, ASE Construction breached the terms of Agreement No. 1 by failing to make payments due. (Id., ¶ 7.) Per the terms of Agreement No. 1, upon ASE Construction’s default, Plaintiff elected to accelerate the balance due thereunder, the principal balance of which is $139,042.21 as of August 9, 2024. (Id., ¶ 8.) No part of this sum has been paid, despite demand therefore, and there is now due, owing, and unpaid from ASE Construction a total of $140,741.21 as of August 9, 2024, in addition to interest thereon at 18% per annum thereafter. (Id., ¶ 9.)

          On or about April 8, 2022, ASE Construction entered into Agreement No. 2, wherein Plaintiff agreed to finance the purchase of the 2330 Freightliner. (Id., ¶ 14, Exh. 4.) Morales executed Guaranty No. 2 on that same date. (Id., ¶ 21, Exh. 6.) Plaintiff perfected its interest in the 2330 Freightliner by obtaining a Certificate of Title from the State of California DMV. (Id., ¶ 15, Exh. 5.) Plaintiff performed all of the terms and conditions of the Agreement No. 2 required to be performed by it. (Id., ¶ 16.) On or about October 7, 2023 and thereafter, ASE Construction breached the terms of Agreement No. 2 by failing to make payments due. (Id., ¶ 17.) Per the terms of Agreement No. 2, upon ASE Construction’s default, Plaintiff elected to accelerate the balance due thereunder, the principal balance of which is $135,350.54 as of August 9, 2024. (Id., ¶ 18.) No part of this sum has been paid, despite demand therefore, and there is now due, owing and unpaid from ASE Construction a total of $135,425.21 as of August 9, 2024, in addition to interest thereon at 18% per annum thereafter. (Id., ¶ 19). Both Agreements Nos. 1 and 2 and Guaranties Nos. 1 and 2 provide for attorneys’ fees should legal action be instituted to enforce the terms of the agreements. (Id., ¶¶ 10, 13, 20 and 23.) 

          Plaintiff is informed and believes that the 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner are in the possession or under the control of ASE Construction and Morales and are currently located behind a locked gate at 15909 Fellowship Court in La Puente, based on GPS data from the 2330 Freightliner. (Id., ¶¶ 25, 26 and 41.) The last GPS “ping” from the 9466 Freightliner was also from the same location. (Id., ¶ 41.) This is Morales’s residence. (Id.) Plaintiff has made demand upon ASE Construction and Morales for the return of the 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner, without success. (Id., ¶ 28.) ASE Construction and Morales have also failed to provide evidence of insurance on the 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner, despite Plaintiff’s demand for this information. (Id., ¶ 40.)

           Elli is required as part of her duties with Plaintiff to know the market price for used trucks and to keep in constant contact with truck re-marketers. (Id., ¶ 36.) Based on her experience and on a review of industry-standard J.D. Power values and in consultation with Plaintiff’s in-house remarketing team, Elli estimates the retail value of the 9466 Freightliner is $145,000.00 to $155,000.00 and the retail value of the 2330 Freightliner is $145,000.00 to $155,000.00, assuming they are in good condition. (Id.)

          Elli reports that “Defendants” have filed two bankruptcy cases within the past year, but that both cases were subsequently dismissed. (Id., ¶ 39.) The 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner have not been taken for a tax, assessment, or fine, pursuant to statute, and have not been seized under an execution against Plaintiff’s property. (App., ¶ 8; see also, Elli Decl., ¶ 29.)

 

Based on the aforementioned showing, the Applications are granted.

Undertaking: A bond must be posted as part of the undertaking unless the Court finds that the defendant has no interest in the property.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 515.010, subd. (b).) The undertaking must be at least in an amount equal to twice the value of the defendant’s interest in the property (market value less all liens, etc.).  (Id.) The defendant’s interest is based on the market value of the property less (1) any amount due and owing on any conditional sales contract or security agreement, (2) all liens and encumbrances on the property, and (3) any other factors to determine the defendant’s interest in the property.  (Id.)

Again, Elli has advised that $140,741.21 is owed on Agreement No. 1 and that $135,425.21 is owed on Agreement No. 2, as of August 9, 2024 and that the value of both the 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner is between $145,000.00 and $155,000.00. The Court considers the fact that the above valuation assumes the 9466 Freightliner and the 2330 Freightliner are both in good condition, which, as Elli attests, “is unlikely since Defendants are not paying for them.” (Elli Decl., ¶ 36.) The Court also considers the fact that interest continues to accrue on the balances owed and that Agreements Nos. 1 and 2 and Guaranties Nos. 1 and 2 all make provision for attorneys’ fees. 

Under these circumstances, the Court will not order the imposition of a bond.
