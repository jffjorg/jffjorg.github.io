DEPARTMENT 34 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV22304    Hearing Date: November 9, 2023    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion for Terminating and/or Issue, Evidence, and Monetary Sanctions for Failure to Comply with the Court’s June 24, 2022 Order

 

Moving Party: Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Ari Chazanas

Resp. Party:    None

 

The Motion is GRANTED.

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Answer to the Complaint is STRICKEN.

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Cross-Complaint is DISMISSED with prejudice.

 

Monetary sanctions are DENIED.

 

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On June 15, 2021, Plaintiff Ari Chazanas filed his Complaint against Defendant Vazgen Hakobian (sued as Vazgen Hakopian) on causes of action of breach of contract, fraud, negligence, restitution pursuant to Business and Professions Code section 7031, and money had and received.

 

On October 29, 2021, Defendant filed his Answer to the Complaint.

 

On November 12, 2021, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Vazgen Hakobian filed his Verified Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Ari Chazanas and Cross-Defendants Lotus West Properties, Inc., Demetra Chazanas, and Demetra Design Corp.

 

On March 7, 2022, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant and the other Cross-Defendants each filed their respective Answer to the Verified Cross-Complaint.

 

On June 24, 2022, the Court granted Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s motions to compel form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for admissions. Notably, Defendant/Cross-Complainant did not respond to the discovery requests, and Defendant/Cross-Complainant did not oppose the motions compelling responses to the discovery requests.

 

On July 17, 2023, the Court granted Defense Counsel’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. When granting Defense Counsel this relief, the Court noted that “[t]he risk of prejudice to Defendant here is minimal. Trial has not been scheduled in this matter, giving Defendant sufficient time to find new representation should he so choose.” (Minute Order dated July 17, 2023, p. 4.)

 

On October 17, 2023, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant filed his Motion for Terminating and/or Issue, Evidence, and Monetary Sanctions for Failure to Comply with the Court’s June 24, 2022 Order. In support of his Motion, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant concurrently filed: (1) Memorandum of Points and Authorities; (2) Declaration of James Kawahito; and (3) Proposed Order.

 

No opposition or other response has been filed to the Motion.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 gives the court the discretion to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in a misuse of the discovery process. A court may impose terminating sanctions by striking pleadings of the party engaged in misuse of discovery or entering default judgment. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d).) A violation of a discovery order is sufficient for the imposition of terminating sanctions. (Collison & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1620.) Terminating sanctions are appropriate when a party persists in disobeying the court's orders. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 795–796.)

 

A terminating sanction is a "drastic measure which should be employed with caution." (Deyo, supra, at p. 793.)

 

"A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction." (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279–280.)

 

While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery." (Deyo, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d at p. 793.) "[A] court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations." (Id.) Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information. (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971.)

 

"A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions . . . (1) absent unusual circumstances, there must be a failure to comply with a court order, and (2) the failure must be willful." (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327; but see Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1291 ["willfulness is no longer a requirement for the imposition of discovery sanctions."].)

 

II.       Discussion

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant moves the Court to: (1) strike Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Answer to the Complaint; (2) dismiss with prejudice Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Cross-Complaint; (3) in the alternative, hold Defendant/Cross-Complainant in contempt of court and prohibit him from participating in the proceedings until he is in full compliance with the Court’s Minute Order dated June 24, 2022; and (4) sanction Defendant/Cross-Complainant in the amount of $1,500.00 to the Court and $5,600.00 to Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant. (Motion, pp. 9:27–28, 10:1–4.)

 

According to Counsel for Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant, Defendant/Cross-Complainant has not complied with the Court’s Minute Order dated June 24, 2022, in that he has not served any of the discovery responses or paid the amounts he was sanctioned. (Decl. Kawahito, ¶¶ 2, 4.)

 

        The Court agrees with Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s arguments. There is no indication that Defendant/Cross-Complainant has served discovery responses or that he has otherwise complied with the Court’s Order dated June 24, 2022. There is also no indication that any sanction would result in Defendant/Cross-Complainant serving responses to Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant’s discovery requests. Rather, considering Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s failure to serve discovery responses, oppose motions to compel, or oppose this motion for terminating sanctions, it appears that Defendant/Cross-Complainant has given up on this case.

 

        In addition, Defendant failed to appear at the Status Conference on September 11, 2023 and did not file a Status Conference report for that hearing.  It appears to the Court that Defendant has not only ignored this Court’s orders, but has chosen no longer to participate in this litigation.

 

        Given that the Court is imposing terminating sanctions, further monetary sanctions for Plaintiff would be excessive. (See Shakespeare, King John, Act 4, Scene 2

“To gild refined gold, to paint the lily,
To throw a perfume on the violet,
To smooth the ice, or add another hue
Unto the rainbow, or with taper-light
To seek the beauteous eye of heaven to garnish,
Is wasteful and ridiculous excess.”)

 

III.     Conclusion

 

The Motion is GRANTED.

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Answer to the Complaint is STRICKEN.

 

Defendant/Cross-Complainant’s Cross-Complaint is DISMISSED with prejudice.

 

An OSC re Entry of Default Judgement is scheduled for the previous trial date of Feb. 28, 2024.

II.       Legal Standard

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding. The motion may be made at any time after 60 days have elapsed since the general appearance in the action or proceeding of each party against whom the motion is directed or at any earlier time after the general appearance that the court, with or without notice and upon good cause shown, may direct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (1)(a).)

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. That is because of the general principle that a party who seeks a court’s action in his favor bears the burden of persuasion thereon. There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850, citation omitted.)

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.” (Aguilar, supra, at p. 850; Smith v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1474, [applying the summary judgment standards in Aguilar to motions for summary adjudication].)

 

“On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law. If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839, citation omitted.)

 

“The trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a fact finder to determine whose version is more likely true. Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court's evaluation of credibility.” (Binder, supra, at p. 840, citations omitted; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Dep’t of Transp. (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

 

“On a motion for summary adjudication, the trial court has no discretion to exercise. If a triable issue of material fact exists as to the challenged causes of action, the motion must be denied. If there is no triable issue of fact, the motion must be granted.” (Fisherman's Wharf Bay Cruise Corp. v. Super. Ct. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 309, 320, citation omitted.)

 

III.     Discussion

 

A.      The Parties’ Arguments

 

Defendant moves the Court to grant summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication as to the issues in this matter. (Motion, p. 16:8–9.)

 

Defendant argues that it did not breach the policy contract because: (1) the policy excludes losses caused by wear, tear, deterioration, inherent vice, latent defect, and/or defects in design, materials, and workmanship; and (2) losses caused by a slow leakage of water over time are excluded from coverage under the policy. (Motion, pp. 9:22, 10:12–13, 12:13–14.) Thus, Defendant cannot be liable for breach of contract on a bad faith cause of action. (Id. at p. 15:23–25.)

 

        Plaintiffs disagree, arguing: (1) that the wear, tear, deterioration, inherent vice, latent defect, and/or defects in design, materials, and workmanship exclusion does not preclude coverage for Plaintiffs’ loss; (2) that the continuous seepage of water exclusion does not preclude coverage for this loss; and (3) that Defendant acted in bad faith by wrongfully denying coverage for Plaintiffs’ claims. (Opposition, pp. 7:19–21, 9:3–4, 11:3–4.)

 

        Defendant reiterates its arguments in its Reply.

 

B.      The Policy and its Exclusions

 

The Insurance Policy at issue is long and complex, with provisions for what Defendant will and will not cover under the Insurance Policy.

 

As it relates to dwellings, this provision discusses what the Insurance Policy covers:

 

“We will pay for accidental direct physical loss to the property described in Coverage A, unless the loss is excluded or limited in Section I – Losses Not Insured or otherwise excluded or limited in this policy. However, loss does not include and we will not pay for, any diminution in value.” (Defendant’s Table of Contents, Exh. 4, p. 12 [actual page 51 of 308], emphases omitted.)

 

        The provisions continue on the following pages, which includes the following coverage provision:

 

12.   Abrupt and accidental discharge or overflow of water, steam, or sewage from within a plumbing, heating, air conditioning, or automatic fire protective sprinkler system, or from within a household appliance.

 

This peril does not include loss:

 

a.          to the system or appliance from which the water, steam, or sewage escaped; or

 

b.       Caused by or resulting from:

 

(1)       freezing;

 

(2)       water or sewage from outside the residence premises plumbing system that enters through sewers or drains, or water that enters into and overflows from within a sump pump, sump pump well, or any other system designed to remove subsurface water that is drained from the foundation area; or

 

(3)       the pressure from or presence of tree, shrub, or plant roots.

 

13.  Abrupt and accidental tearing asunder, cracking, burning, or bulging of a steam or hot water hearing system, an air conditioning system, an automatic fire protective sprinkler system, or an appliance for hearing water.

 

        This peril does not include loss caused by or resulting from freezing.

 

(Defendant’s Table of Contents, Exh. 4, p. 14 [actual page 53 of 308], emphases omitted.)

 

        The Insurance Policy also includes provisions on what is not covered.

 

1.          We will not pay for any loss to the property described in Coverage A that is caused by one or more of the items below, regardless of whether the loss occurs abruptly or gradually, involves isolated or widespread damage, arises from natural or external forces, or occurs as a result of any combination of these:

 

. . .

 

f.      wear, tear, decay, marring, scratching, deterioration, inherent vice, latent defect, or mechanical breakdown;

 

. . .

 

3.          We will not pay for, under any part of this policy, any loss that is caused by one or more of the items below, regardless of whether the loss occurs abruptly or gradually, involves isolated or widespread damage, occurs on or off the residence premises, arises from natural or external forces, or occurs as a result of any combination of these:

(3)       waves (including tidal wave, tsunami, and seiche);

 

(4)       tides or tidal water;

 

(5)       overflow of any body of water (including any release, escape, or rising of any body of water, or any water held, contained, controlled, or diverted by a dam, levee, dike, or any type of water containment, diversion, or flood control device);

 

(6)       spray or surge from any of the items c.(1) through c.(5) described above, all whether driven by wind or not;

 

(7)       water or sewage from outside the residence premises plumbing system that enters through sewers or drains, or water or sewage that enters into and overflows from within a sump pump, sump pump well, or any other system designed to remove subsurface water that is drained from the foundation area;

(a)       and is:

 

(i)                continuous;

(v)               slow; or

We also will not pay for losses arising from condensation or the presence of humidity moisture, or vapor that occurs or develops over a period of time; or

C.      Discussion

 

According to Defendant, Plaintiff suffered water damages after “a pipe leading to the refrigerator valve box was broken”, “there was mold behind the refrigerator”, and “the refrigerator water line was never connected to the valve.” (Motion, p. 6:2, 6:6, 6:10–12, 6:15–16.)

 

While “accidental direct physical loss to the property described” is covered by the Insurance Policy, Defendant argues that these damages are not covered because: (1) “[t]he failure in the plumbing line feeding the icemaker box was the result of wear, tear, deterioration, inherent vice, latent defect, and/or a defect in design, materials or workmanship”; and (2) “[t]he water damage at Plaintiffs’ property was the result of a slow leakage of water from the plumbing line feeding the icemaker box that occurred over a period of time.” (Motion, p. 6:23–27; Defendant’s Table of Contents, Exh. 4, pp. 12, 15–17.)

 

        According to Plaintiffs, they “suffered a sudden water loss when a pressurized PVC pipe inside the wall completely served at the L-joint approximately one month after they moved into their new home. . . . Without any further investigation as to the cause of loss or rate and flow of water, [Defendant] revoked coverage and denied Plaintiffs’ claim based on the continuous or repeated seepage and leakage exclusion.” (Opposition, p. 1:2–5, 1:13–14; Defendant’s Table of Contents, Exh. 4, p. 17.)

 

        It appears to the Court that Defendant’s claim that “[t]he facts are undisputed”, is incorrect.

 

Defendant cites various pieces of evidence  – including emails from the mitigation company, plumbing reports from different companies, and various declarations – to claim that there was an ongoing leak due to a defect, which would omit these damages from policy coverage,  Plaintiffs cite various pieces of evidence – including various depositions and declarations – to claim that there was a spontaneous severing of a pipe without evidence of a defect, which would mean these damages are covered by the Insurance Policy. (See, for example, Separate Statement, Item 16; see also Response to Separate Statement, Item 16.)

 

        There are numerous triable issues of material fact, including the cause of the water damage and whether Defendant acted in bad faith. Neither summary judgment nor summary adjudication are appropriate.

The Motion is DENIED.

 


Case Number: 23STCV00269    Hearing Date: November 9, 2023    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion to Compel Responses to General Form Interrogatories, Employment Form Interrogatories, and Specially Prepared Interrogatories, Sets No. One [and] Request for Monetary Sanctions

 

Moving Party: Plaintiff Joseph Arevalo

Resp. Party:    None

 

SUBJECT:        Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Sets No. One and Two [and] Request Imposing Monetary Sanctions

 

Moving Party: Plaintiff Joseph Arevalo

Resp. Party:    None

 

SUBJECT:        Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set No. One, Admitted and Conclusively Established [and] Request Imposing Monetary Sanctions

 

Moving Party: Plaintiff Joseph Arevalo

Resp. Party:    None

                                   

 

The ROGs Motion and the RPDs Motion are GRANTED. Defendants shall provide initial responses to the ROGs and RPDs within 14 days of the issuance of this Order.

 

The RFAs Motion is GRANTED. The RFAs are DEEMED ADMITTED.

 

All of Plaintiff’s Requests for Monetary Sanctions are DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On January 5, 2023, Plaintiff Joseph Arevalo filed his Complaint against Defendants Edgebanding Services, Inc. and Martin Ramirez on causes of action arising from Plaintiff’s employment with Defendants.

 

On May 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint.

 

On October 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed: (1) Motion to Compel Responses to General Form Interrogatories, Employment Form Interrogatories, and Specially Prepared Interrogatories, Sets No. One [and] Request for Monetary Sanctions (“ROGs Motion”); (2) Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Sets No. One and Two [and] Request Imposing Monetary Sanctions (“RPDs Motion”); and (3) Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set No. One, Admitted and Conclusively Established [and] Request Imposing Monetary Sanctions (“RFAs Motion”). In support of each of these discovery motions, Plaintiff concurrently filed a Proposed Order. Plaintiff also concurrently filed one Proof of Service.

 

No oppositions or other responses have been filed to the discovery motions.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          ROGs Motion and RPDs Motion

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure requires a response from the party to whom form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and demand requests are propounded within 30 days after service of the requests, unless the time is extended by agreement of the parties. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.260, subd. (a), 2030.270, subd. (a), 2031.260, subd. (a), 2031.270, subd. (a).) If a party fails to serve timely responses, "the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b).) By failing to respond, the offending party waives any objection to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

 

For a motion to compel, all a propounding party must show is that it properly served its discovery requests, that the time to respond has expired, and that the party to whom the requests were directed failed to provide a timely response. (See Leach v. Super. Ct. (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905, 906.) Indeed, "[o]nce [a party] 'fail[ed] to serve a timely response,' the trial court had authority to grant [opposing party's] motion to compel responses." (Sinaiko Healthcare Counseling, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 405.)

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel motions for interrogatories or requests for production, unless the Court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey an order compelling answers, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c), 2031.300, subd. (c).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves the Court to compel Defendants to serve responses to the form interrogatories (“FROGs”), special interrogatories (“SROGs”), and requests for production of documents (“RPDs”) that Plaintiff propounded on July 25 and 26, 2023. (ROGs Motion, p. 10:13–16 and Exh. A, p. 8, Exh. C, p. 10; RPDs Motion, p. 11:6–9 and Exh. A, p. 17, Exh. B, p. 12.) According to Plaintiff’s Counsel, Defendants have not responded to these discovery requests. (ROGs Motion, Decl. LaCour, ¶¶ 8, 14–15; RPDs Motion, Decl. LaCour, ¶¶ 10, 17.)

 

There is no evidence before the Court that would indicate Defendants responded to these discovery requests.

 

The Court GRANTS the ROGs Motion and the RPDs Motion. Defendants shall provide initial responses to the ROGs and RPDs within 14 days of the issuance of this Order.

 

C.      Sanctions

 

For each of these motions, Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions against Defendants and Defense Counsel.

 

Defendants failed to serve timely responses to the ROGs and RPDs. A monetary sanction would normally be appropriate.

 

However, for each of these two motions, Plaintiff’s Counsel requests $2,500.00 based on a rate of $925.00, without any explanation for how many hours were incurred or why such a high rate is reasonable. In any case, given the boilerplate, repetitive motions filed, $2,500.00 is a patently unreasonable sanction, much less $5,000.00.

 

“If . . . the Court were required to award a reasonable fee when an outrageously unreasonable one has been asked for, claimants would be encouraged to make unreasonable demands, knowing that the only unfavorable consequence of such misconduct would be reduction of their fee to what they should have asked in the first place. To discourage such greed, a severer reaction is needful.” (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635 [cleaned up].)¿

 

“A fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether.” (Chavez v. City of Los Angeles (2010) 47 Cal.4th 970, 990; Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1137; Serrano, supra, 32 Cal.3d at p. 635.)

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for sanctions.

 

D.      Conclusion

 

The ROGs Motion and the RPDs Motion are GRANTED. Defendants shall provide initial responses to the ROGs and RPDs within 14 days of the issuance of this Order.

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions are DENIED.

 

II.       RFAs Motion

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure requires a response from the party to whom the request for admissions is directed within 30 days after service of the request for admissions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)

 

If the party fails to serve a timely response, “the party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).)

 

The requesting party may then “move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanction under Chapter 7.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).)

 

A court will deem requests admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves the Court to deem admitted the truth of the matters specified in the Requests for Admission (“RFAs”) that Plaintiff propounded on July 25, 2023. (RFAs Motion, p. 4:5–7 and Exh. A, p. 7.) Plaintiff’s Counsel declares that no responses were served. (RFAs Motion, Decl. LaCour, ¶¶ 6, 8, 15.)

 

There is no evidence before the Court that would indicate Defendants responded to the RFAs.

 

The Court GRANTS the RFAs Motion. The RFAs are DEEMED ADMITTED.

 

C.      Sanctions

 

Plaintiff again requests monetary sanctions of $2,500.00 against Defendants and Defense Counsel for this motion.

 

        The purpose of monetary sanctions is to reimburse the prevailing party for the time spent on preparing the discovery motion.  Monetary sanctions encourage “voluntary compliance with discovery procedures by assessing the costs of compelling compliance against the defaulting party.” (Argaman v. Ratan (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 1173, 1179.) 

 

Defendants failed to serve timely responses to the RFAs. Thus, monetary sanctions would normally be appropriate.  However, for the exact same reasons as with the sanctions requested for the ROGs Motion and the RPDs Motion, monetary sanctions are not appropriate here.  Further, the Court seriously doubts that counsel spent exactly 2.7 hours – at $925/hour – on each of these motions.

 

        The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions.

 

D.      Conclusion

 

The RFAs Motion is GRANTED. The RFAs are DEEMED ADMITTED.

 

Plaintiff’s Request for Monetary Sanctions is DENIED.
