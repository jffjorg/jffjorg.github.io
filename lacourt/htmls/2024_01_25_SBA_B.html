Hearing Date:                          January 25, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Defendant City of Santa Monica

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Diane Burnett

Demurrer to Complaint

 

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd cause of action.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer within 20 days.

BACKGROUND

On March 29, 2023, plaintiff Diane Burnett filed a complaint against City of Santa Monica for (1) negligence and (2) negligence and statutory liability. 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant City of Santa Monica demurs to the 2nd cause of action on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action. 

            The complaint alleges that on August 9, 2022, plaintiff was a fare paying passenger aboard defendant City’s Big Blue Bus.  She was seriously injured when the bus driver of the Big Blue Bus failed to restrain her wheelchair to the bus.  Upon a sudden and extreme maneuver by the bus driver while driving, plaintiff was thrown across the bus, her wheelchair tipped over, and she was injured.  Complaint, ¶7.  The incident occurred at or near the 96th station bus stop of the Big Blue Bus.  Id., ¶8.  At the time of the incident, the City’s Big Blue Bus was a common carrier operating a bus line under Civil Code 2168, and during which plaintiff was a passenger for hire.  Id., ¶9.

            2nd Cause Of Action For Negligence/Statutory Liability

            Under Government Code §835, a public entity is “liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, that the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, that the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and either:  (a) A negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition; or (b) the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition . . . a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition." Gov. Code §835.

            “’Dangerous condition’ means a condition of property that creates a substantial (as distinguished from a minor, trivial or insignificant) risk of injury when such property or adjacent property is used with due care in a manner in which is reasonably foreseeable that it will be used.”  Gov. Code §830(a).

Under the 2nd cause of action, the complaint alleges that defendants owned, leased, controlled, maintained, managed, operated, and/or were otherwise in possession of the Big Blue Bus.  Complaint, ¶21.  On August 9, 2022, defendants City and Does and/or their management, administrative, designers, planners, engineers, maintenance personnel, inspectors, contractors, and/or other employees, staff, agents, or contractors, acting within the course and scope of their duties and/or employment failed to properly install, maintain, repair, operate, monitor, and/or control the subject Big Blue Bus.  The Big Blue Bus had a handicap area where individuals with wheelchairs could be strapped in and restrained.  The fact that the bus driver did not restrain plaintiff coupled with his erratic driving constituted a dangerous condition created by the negligence of the driver.  Plaintiff would not have experienced the injuries she did had this dangerous condition been absent or remedied.  Id., ¶22.  These failures, acts, and omissions were inherently dangerous and created a peculiar risk and nuisance.  Defendants, acting within the course and scope of their duties and through negligent or wrongful acts or omissions created, or allowed to be created, a dangerous condition under Gov. Code §835.  The installation, maintenance, repair, operation, monitoring, safety and/or control of the Big Blue Bus was done negligently, inadequately, and improperly and created a hazard, trap, and dangerous condition under Gov. Code §835.  Id., ¶23.

            Defendant argues that the allegations are conclusory and that it is entitled to specificity in pleading.  To state a claim against a public entity, “every fact essential to the existence of statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity . . . .”  Searcy v. Hemet Unified School Dist. (1986) 177 Cal. App. 3d 792, 802 (citation omitted).  Further, defendant contends that this claim appears to be based on the bus driver’s purported failure to restrain plaintiff and his driving and she does not contend that any physical condition of the bus led to her injuries.  “[A] dangerous condition of public property requires ‘a physical deficiency in the property itself.’” Salas v. Department of Transportation (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 1058, 1070 (citation omitted).

            In opposition, plaintiff argues that the allegations are sufficient, and that Gov. Code §835 does not use the word “physical” and refers only to “dangerous condition of its property.”  Plaintiff also asserts that she alleged that she was not physically “strapped in and restrained” to the bus in the handicap area.  She contends that “[o]bviously, the straps used to restrain individuals with wheelchairs were physically attached to the bus, and indeed were a part of the bus.”  Thus, plaintiff argues, she alleges that the bus was in a physical condition that led to her injuries as the straps of the bus were not physically affixed in order to restrain plaintiff and the wheelchair while the bus was moving.

            The court rules as follows:  “‘[A] claim alleging a dangerous condition may not rely on generalized allegations but must specify in what manner the condition constituted a dangerous condition.’  A plaintiff’s allegations, and ultimately the evidence, must establish a physical deficiency in the property itself.  A dangerous condition exists when public property ‘is physically damaged, deteriorated, or defective in such a way as to foreseeably endanger those using the property itself,’ or possesses physical characteristics in its design, location, features or relationship to its surroundings that endanger users. . . . ‘[P]ublic liability lies under section 835 only when a feature of the public property has ‘increased or intensified’ the danger to users from third party conduct.’”  Cerna v. City of Oakland (2008) 161 Cal. App. 4th 1340, 1347-1348 (citations omitted).  “[T]o constitute a dangerous condition under Government Code section 835, public

property must possess a physical defect which creates a substantial, as distinguished from a minor,

trivial or insignificant, risk of injury. (Gov. Code § 830(a).)  Harmful conduct in and of itself cannot form a basis for recovery without a direct causal connection with the physical defect.” Bartell v. Palos Verdes Peninsula Sch. Dist. (1978) 83 Cal. App. 3d 492, 497.  “Property is not ‘dangerous’ within the meaning of the statutory scheme if the property is safe when used with due care and the risk of harm is created only when foreseeable users fail to exercise due care.”  Brenner v. City of El Cajon (2003) 113 Cal. App. 4th 434, 439 (citation omitted).

            The court finds that the allegations are insufficient.  Plaintiff does not adequately describe the “dangerous condition”—i.e., a physical deficiency as to the bus.  Rather plaintiff alleges that the driver did not use due care in using the wheelchair straps to restrain plaintiff’s wheelchair and drove erratically, which is not a “dangerous condition” within the meaning of the statutory scheme.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd cause of action.

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 2nd cause of action.

Defendant City is ordered to file an answer within 20 days.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment

 

            The court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

            The motion is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On December 3, 2003, plaintiff Larry Flynt, ind. and dba Hustler Casino filed a complaint against defendant John K. Horne.    On February 3, 2004, a default was entered against defendant.  On March 18, 2004, a default judgment was entered against defendant.  On January 23, 2014, an application for and renewal of judgment was filed.  On October 18, 2023, an application for and renewal of judgment was filed.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“The court may, upon terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . .”  CCP §473(b).

DISCUSSION

            Pursuant to CCP §473, defendant (self-represented) requests that the court vacate the default judgment against him.

            As the court noted above, the default judgment was entered on March 18, 2004, and the judgment was renewed in January 2014 and October 2023.

            Defendant states in his declaration that based on inadvertence, he never received notice of the case.  He states that the very first knowledge he received of the case was in October 2023.  He explains that when the case was first filed the address where he was purportedly served was a postal service address.  He states that “early in the 2000s, due to financial difficulties” he could no longer afford to keep the mailbox service.  He did not leave a forwarding mailing address.  In September 2019, as he was without a permanent address and desperately needed one, he returned to the same postal service address.  He states that the only difference is the box number, which is now #510.  He further states that because the owners of the service know him they recognized that although plaintiff used the “old #375” they inserted the renewal in his new box.  John Horne decl.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the motion is untimely under CCP §473(b) and defendant failed to file a proposed pleading.  Plaintiff contends that defendant was properly served with the summons and complaint on December 9, 2003 at 269 Beverly Drive, #375, Beverly Hills; served with the request for entry of default on January 22, 2004; a copy of the request for court judgment on March 16, 2004; a copy of the notice of entry of judgment on March 30, 2004; notice of renewal of judgment on January 29, 2014; and notice of renewal of judgment on November 1, 2023.

            The court rules as follows: 

            As to CCP §473(b), the motion is untimely as it was not filed within six months of entry of judgment.

Renewal of a judgment is a ministerial act performed by a court clerk upon receipt of an application for renewal.  CCP §§683.120(b), 683.150(a).  CCP §683.150(a) states, that “Upon the filing of the application, the court clerk shall enter the renewal of the judgment in the court records.”  CCP §683.170 allows a judgment debtor to move to vacate the renewed judgment on any ground that would be a defense to an action on the judgment, within 60 days after service of the notice of renewal pursuant to CCP §683.160.  Defendant did not timely file a motion under CCP §683.170.

            The motion is thus DENIED.

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.
