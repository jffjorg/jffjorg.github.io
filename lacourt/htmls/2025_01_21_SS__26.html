Case Number: 21STLC08739    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 26

State Farm v. Alfaro et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement Agreement, and Enter Judgment is GRANTED. JUDGMENT TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT NICHOLAS ALFARO IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,200.00 PRINCIPAL AND $365.00 COSTS.  

ANALYSIS:

On December 10, 2021, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this subrogation action against Defendants Nicholas Alfaro (“Defendant”) and Manuel Oscoro, who was subsequently dismissed. Defendant filed an answer on February 28, 2022. On November 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed a copy of the parties’ settlement agreement with a request for dismissal and retention of jurisdiction under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. The Court dismissed the action pursuant to the stipulation on the same date. (Order for Dismissal, 11/17/23.) 

On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment. To date, no opposition has been filed.

Discussion

The instant motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6, which states in relevant part: 

If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).) Prior to January 1, 2021, “parties” under section 664.6 meant the litigants themselves, not their attorneys.  (Levy v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 578, 586.) The current statute provides that “parties” includes “an attorney who represents the party” and an insurer’s agent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (b).) The settlement must include the signatures of the parties seeking to enforce the agreement, and against whom enforcement is sought. (J.B.B. Investment Partners, Ltd. v. Fair (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 974, 985.) The settlement agreement complies with the statutory requirements set forth above because it was signed by both parties and their attorneys. (Motion, Reese Decl., Exh. A, p. 3.) 

Furthermore, the request for retention of jurisdiction must be made in writing, by the parties, before the action is dismissed for the Court’s retention of jurisdiction to conform to the statutory language. (Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 433 [“If, after a suit has been dismissed, a party brings a section 664.6 motion for a judgment on a settlement agreement but cannot present to the court a request for retention of jurisdiction that meets all of these requirements, then enforcement of the agreement must be left to a separate lawsuit.”].) The parties’ request for retention of jurisdiction complies with these requirements because it was made in writing to the Court before the action was dismissed. (Motion, Reese Decl., Exh. A, ¶3.) 

The settlement provides that Defendant would pay Plaintiff $6,500.00 through an initial payment from Defendant’s insurer of $5,000.00, followed by Defendant’s monthly payments starting on October 1, 2023. (Id. at Exh. A, ¶2.) The settlement agreement also provides that in the event of Defendant’s default, Plaintiff may seek judgment in the settlement amount, less any monies paid, plus costs not to exceed $500.00. (Id. at ¶3.) Payments of $5,300.00 were made towards the settlement, after which Defendant defaulted. (Id. at ¶¶4-6 and Exh. C.) Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to entry of judgment against Defendant in the amount of $1,200.00 principal ($6,500.00 - $5,300.00) and costs of $365.00. (Id. at ¶8.) 

Conclusion 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement Agreement, and Enter Judgment is GRANTED. JUDGMENT TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT NICHOLAS ALFARO IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,200.00 PRINCIPAL AND $365.00 COSTS.

Case Number: 22STLC03997    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 26

State Farm v. Vazquez, et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement Agreement, and Enter Judgment is GRANTED. JUDGMENT TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT JUAN VAZQUEZ IN THE AMOUNT OF $4,500.00 PRINCIPAL AND $500.00 COSTS.  

ANALYSIS:

On June 15, 2022, Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this subrogation action against Defendant Juan Vazquez (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer on August 10, 2022. On July 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a copy of the parties’ settlement agreement with a request for dismissal and retention of jurisdiction under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. The Court dismissed the action pursuant to the stipulation on the same date. (Order for Dismissal, 07/19/23.)

On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement, and Enter Judgment. To date, no opposition has been filed. 

Legal Standard 

The instant motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6, which states in relevant part: 

If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement. 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).) Prior to January 1, 2021, “parties” under section 664.6 meant the litigants themselves, not their attorneys.  (Levy v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 578, 586.) The current statute provides that “parties” includes “an attorney who represents the party” and an insurer’s agent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (b).) The settlement must include the signatures of the parties seeking to enforce the agreement, and against whom enforcement is sought. (J.B.B. Investment Partners, Ltd. v. Fair (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 974, 985.) The settlement agreement complies with the statutory requirements set forth above because it was signed by both parties and their attorneys. (Motion, Reese Decl., Exh. A, p. 3.)

Furthermore, the request for retention of jurisdiction must be made in writing, by the parties, before the action is dismissed for the Court’s retention of jurisdiction to conform to the statutory language. (Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 433 [“If, after a suit has been dismissed, a party brings a section 664.6 motion for a judgment on a settlement agreement but cannot present to the court a request for retention of jurisdiction that meets all of these requirements, then enforcement of the agreement must be left to a separate lawsuit.”].) The parties’ request for retention of jurisdiction complies with these requirements because it was made in writing to the Court before the action was dismissed. (Motion, Reese Decl., Exh. A, ¶3.) 

The settlement provides that Defendant would pay Plaintiff $9,474.04 through an initial payment from Defendant’s insurer of $4,974.04, followed by Defendant’s monthly payments starting on April 15, 2023. (Id. at Exh. A, ¶2.) The settlement agreement also provides that in the event of Defendant’s default, Plaintiff may seek judgment in the settlement amount, less any monies paid, plus costs not to exceed $500.00. (Id. at ¶3.) Payments of $4,974.00 were made towards the settlement, after which Defendant defaulted. (Id. at ¶¶4-6 and Exh. C.) Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to entry of judgment against Defendant in the amount of $4,500.00 principal ($9,474.04 - $4,974.04) and costs of $500.00. (Id. at ¶8.)

Conclusion 

Plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion to Vacate Dismissal, Enforce Settlement Agreement, and Enter Judgment is GRANTED. JUDGMENT TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT JUAN VAZQUEZ IN THE AMOUNT OF $4,500.00 PRINCIPAL AND $500.00 COSTS.

Case Number: 23STLC04641    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 26

Sunrun Inc. v. Rivera, et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Plaintiff Sunrun, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.  

ANALYSIS: 

Plaintiff Sunrun, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) brought this action for (1) possession of personal property; (2) breach of contract; (3) unjust enrichment; and (4) quantum meruit against Defendant Victor M. Rivera (“Defendant”) on July 24, 2023. Defendant filed an answer in properia persona on August 16, 2023. On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the first Motion for Summary Judgment. The first Motion came for hearing on August 14, 2024 and was denied on the grounds that it was mis-noticed as a motion for summary judgment but actually sought summary adjudication of the second cause of action for breach of contract. (Minute Order, 08/14/24.) 

On August 30, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed the causes of action for possession of personal property, unjust enrichment, and quantum meruit. Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment on October 18, 2024. No opposition has been filed to date.

Discussion 

Allegations in the Complaint 

The Complaint alleges that the parties entered into a lease agreement for a certain photovoltaic solar system (“the Solar System”) on February 28, 2017. (Compl., ¶4 and Exh. A.) Pursuant to the lease, Plaintiff installed the Solar System on the real property located at 7002 Calvin Avenue, Reseda, California. (Ibid.) In exchange, Defendant promised to purchase all the energy produced by the Solar System by making monthly lease payments in the initial amount of $62.00 per month, subject to a 2.5% increase annually, for a term of twenty (20) years. (Ibid.) Plaintiff completed installation of the Solar System on April 19, 2017. (Id. at ¶6.) Around November 19, 2019, Plaintiff noted that the Solar System meter had stopped functioning and initiated a field service appointment to determine the reason. (Id. at ¶¶7-8.) On information and belief, Defendant removed, or authorized the removal of the Solar System from the Subject Property because other solar panels and related equipment had been installed on the Subject Property in place of Plaintiff’s Solar System. (Id. at ¶9.) Defendant served Plaintiff with a Notice of Material Breach on December 15, 2019. (Id. at ¶10 and Exh. B.) Defendant did not cure the default and is in breach of the contract. (Id. at ¶¶11-13.) Plaintiff is entitled to certain remedies, including, but not limited to, terminating the Contract and requiring Defendant to pay to Plaintiff a payment equal to the estimated prepayment of all future monthly lease payments to be made during the initial term the Contract (the “Make Whole Payment”). (Id. at ¶14.) The Make Whole Payment due as of the filing of the Complaint is $18,357.00. (Id. at ¶15.) The contract also provides that Plaintiff may disconnect or take possession of the Solar System upon default. (Id. at ¶16.) Plaintiff is entitled to immediate and exclusive possession of the Solar System and immediate payment of the Make Whole Payment. (Id. at ¶¶18, 25.)

Summary Judgment

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on the Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, which provides “[a] party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a) [emphasis added].) On a motion for summary judgment, a moving plaintiff must show that there is no defense by proving each element of every cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Then the burden shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the causes of action or a defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Additionally, in ruling on the Motion, the Court must view the “evidence [citations] and such inferences [citations], in the light most favorable to the opposing party.” (Intrieri v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 72, 81 [citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843].)

The elements for a claim of breach of contract are: (1) the contract; (2) plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) resulting damages. (Otworth v. So. Pacific Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458; Lortz v. Connell (1969) 273 Cal.App.2d 286, 290.) Plaintiff presents the following facts in support of its initial burden of proof.

On February 28, 2017, the parties entered into the Sunrun BrightSave Lease Agreement (“the Contract”) for Defendant to lease a certain photovoltaic solar system (“the Solar System”). (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 1; Gazotti Decl., ¶4 and Exh. A., p. 21) In exchange, Defendant promised to purchase all the energy produced by the Solar System by making monthly lease payments to Plaintiff in the initial amount of $62.00 per month, subject to a 2.5% increase annually, for a term of twenty (20) years. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 1; Gazotti Decl., ¶4 and Exh. A, §B.) Plaintiff completed its installation of the Solar System at 7002 Calvin Ave, Reseda, California (“the Subject Property”) on April 29, 2017. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 2; Gazotti Decl., ¶6.) From that date, the Solar System was fully operational. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 2; Gazotti Decl., ¶6.) Under the Contract, Plaintiff would design, install, retain ownership of, maintain, and monitor the Solar System for the duration of the Contract. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 3; Gazotti Decl., ¶5.) Defendant breached the Contract by removing the Solar System without Plaintiff’s authorization. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 4; Gazotti Decl., ¶7.) Plaintiff performed all its obligations under the Contract. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 5; Gazotti Decl., ¶10.) Defendant owes $18,375.00 under the Contract as of the filing date of this Motion. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 6; Gazotti Decl., ¶¶8-12 and Exh. B.)

This evidence carries Plaintiff’s initial burden of proof as to each element of the cause of action for breach of contract. The burden now shifts to Defendant to demonstrate the existence of a triable issue of material fact as to any of these elements. Defendant, however, has not filed any opposition to the Motion.

Conclusion 

Therefore, Plaintiff Sunrun, Inc.’s Motion for Summary Judgment is GRANTED.

Oldziewksi v. Mitigation, Inc. et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Petitioner Judy C. Oldziewski’s Petition to Release Property from Mechanic’s Lien is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.  

ANALYSIS: 

Petitioner Judy C. Oldziewski (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition for Release of Mechanic’s Lien against Respondent Mitigation, Inc. (“Respondent”) on September 10, 2024. Petitioner filed a proof of service by mail on October 8, 2024. Respondent filed an objection on January 14, 2025. 

Discussion 

Petitioner moves to have the mechanic’s lien recorded against their property by Respondent released. Under Civil Code section 8484 the petition for release order must be verified by the petitioner and allege the following:

(a) The date of recordation of the claim of lien. A certified copy of the claim of lien shall be attached to the petition.

(b) The county in which the claim of lien is recorded.

(c) The book and page or series number of the place in the official records where the claim of lien is recorded.

(d) The legal description of the property subject to the claim of lien.

(e) Whether an extension of credit has been granted under Section 8460, if so to what date, and that the time for commencement of an action to enforce the lien has expired.

(f) That the owner has given the claimant notice under Section 8482 demanding that the claimant execute and record a release of the lien and that the claimant is unable or unwilling to do so or cannot with reasonable diligence be found.

(g) Whether an action to enforce the lien is pending.

(h) Whether the owner of the property or interest in the property has filed for relief in bankruptcy or there is another restraint that prevents the claimant from commencing an action to enforce the lien. 

(Civil Code, § 8484.) Respondent filed an objection to the Petition, which includes a request for judicial notice of the release of the mechanic’s lien recorded on December 17, 2024. (Obj., RJN, Exh. 1.) The Court takes judicial notice of the release pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c) and section 453. In light of the release of the subject lien, the instant Petition is deemed moot. 

Conclusion 

Petitioner Judy C. Oldziewski’s Petition to Release Property from Mechanic’s Lien is PLACED OFF CALENDAR AS MOOT.

Case Number: 24STLC00287    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 26

Pena v. Engeln, et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant Kimberly Engeln’s Motion for Leave to Withdraw Improperly Propounded Discovery and Re-Propound Discovery Within CCP § 94 Limits; or, in the alternative, Motion for Leave to Conduct Additional Discovery Pursuant to CCP § 95 is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

On January 17, 2024, Plaintiff Daisy Pena (“Plaintiff”) filed the Complaint in this action for motor vehicle against Defendant Kimberly Engeln (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer on September 3, 2024.  

On November 25, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion for Leave to Withdraw Improperly Propounded Discovery and Re-Propound Discovery Within CCP § 94 Limits; or, in the alternative, Motion for Leave to Conduct Additional Discovery Pursuant to CCP § 95. Plaintiffs filed an opposition on December 30, 2024 and Defendant replied on January 2, 2025. 

Discussion 

The request to withdraw discovery propounded on Plaintiff is brought on the grounds that it is necessary to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 94, which limits the number of interrogatories and requests for production of documents to 35 total in the limited jurisdiction court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 94, subd. (a).) As Plaintiff points out in opposition, however, the Motion cites neither statutory nor caselaw authority for the withdrawal of discovery after a party has already served responses. Defendant’s reply refers to Doe v. United States Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424 for the broad proposition that discovery is necessary to ensure fair resolution of disputes but points to no authority relevant to the specific facts of this action. In fact, Defendant fails to even provide a pin citation for the relevant portions of the case. The request to withdraw the already propounded discovery, to which Plaintiff has responded, therefore, is denied. 

Defendant alternatively moves for leave to propound additional discovery pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 95, which states in relevant part:

(a) The court may, on noticed motion and subject to such terms and conditions as are just, authorize a party to conduct additional discovery, but only upon a showing that the moving party will be unable to prosecute or defend the action effectively without the additional discovery. In making a determination under this section, the court shall take into account whether the moving party has used all applicable discovery in good faith, and whether the party has attempted to secure the additional discovery by stipulation or by means other than formal discovery.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 95, subd. (a).) Defendant has not shown that they will be unable to defend this action effectively without the additional discovery, which is required by the statute. The declaration of defense counsel in support of the Motion makes a vague assertion that restricting discovery would “hinder Defendant’s ability to fully defend the case.” (Motion, Hemati Decl., ¶7.) The specific nature of the discovery propounded and the responses received are not explained nor analyzed. Plaintiff’s opposition explains that responses to all the document demands and to 20 special interrogatories were served on Defendant. (Oppo., Haddad Decl., ¶7 and Exh. C.) Neither the Motion, nor reply, explains what additional discovery is crucial to the defense in light of these responses. Therefore, Defendant has not demonstrated that legal or factual grounds exist,  to propound additional discovery.

Conclusion 

Defendant Kimberly Engeln’s Motion for Leave to Withdraw Improperly Propounded Discovery and Re-Propound Discovery Within CCP § 94 Limits; or, in the alternative, Motion for Leave to Conduct Additional Discovery Pursuant To CCP § 95 is DENIED.

Case Number: 24STLC00423    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 26

Nowhere Holdco, LLC v. CSI Group, Inc. et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Plaintiff Nowhere Holdco, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication, is DENIED. 

ANALYSIS:

Plaintiff Nowhere Holdco, LLC (“Plaintiff”) brought this action for (1) breach of contract; (2) open book account; (3) account stated; and (4) unjust enrichment against Defendant C.S.I. Group, Inc. dba CSI Group, Inc. (“Defendant”) on January 22, 2024. Defendant filed an answer on February 13, 2024.

On September 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication. Defendant filed an opposition on January 7, 2025 and Plaintiff replied on January 15, 2025.

Discussion 

Allegations in the Complaint

Plaintiff alleges that between February 2022 and about May 2023, the parties entered into an open book account, memorialized by various invoices and credit memos, for the purchase and sale of nitrile gloves.(Compl., ¶6 and Exh. A.) Defendant issued a credit memo to Plaintiff in the amount of $2,070.00 but Defendant refused to honor it. (Id. at ¶7.) Plaintiff demanded Defendant refund the $2,070.00 memorialized by the Credit Memo and Defendant’s refusal to honor it is a breach of the parties’ agreement. (Id. at ¶8.) The Credit Memo is an agreement based on a book account. (Id. at ¶9.)

Summary Judgment

Plaintiff moves for summary judgment on the Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, which provides “[a] party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a) [emphasis added].) On a motion for summary judgment, a moving plaintiff must show that there is no defense by proving each element of every cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Then the burden shifts to the defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the causes of action or a defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) Additionally, in ruling on the Motion, the Court must view the “evidence [citations] and such inferences [citations], in the light most favorable to the opposing party.” (Intrieri v. Superior Court (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 72, 81 [citing Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843].)

The elements for a claim of breach of contract are: (1) the contract; (2) plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) resulting damages. (Otworth v. So. Pacific Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458; Lortz v. Connell (1969) 273 Cal.App.2d 286, 290.) Plaintiff presents the following facts in support of its initial burden of proof.

Defendant issued a credit memo in the amount of $2,070.00, which is a contract. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 1; Shiring Decl., ¶7 and Exh. A.) Defendant issued the Credit Memo to Plaintiff to memorialize its debt to Plaintiff in the amount of $2,070.00 and that Defendant would remit that amount to Plaintiff. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 4; Shiring Decl., ¶9.) Defendant refused to honor the Credit Memo. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 2; ¶8.) Plaintiff demanded the refund of the $2,070.00 memorialized in the Credit Memo from Defendant. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 1; Shiring Decl., ¶9.) Plaintiff performed all its obligations under the Credit Memo except those that were prevented or excused by Defendants and/or third parties. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 5; Shiring Decl., ¶19.) Defendant owes Plaintiff $2,080.00. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 9; Shiring Decl., ¶15 and Exh. A.) Defendant’s breach has caused Plaintiff to suffer damages of $2,070.0. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 12; Shiring Decl., ¶7 and Exh. A.)

Plaintiff’s evidence fails to demonstrate that no triable issues of material fact exist regarding a contract between the parties that was breached by Defendant and resulted in damages of $2,070.00 to Plaintiff. First, the Credit Memo that purports to memorialize the parties’ agreement does not include Plaintiff’s name, “Nowhere Holdco, LLC.” Instead, the document refers to an entity called “Erewhon Distribution Center.” (Motion, Shiring Decl., Exh. A.) “Erewhon Distribution Center” is not mentioned as a party in the Complaint, which frames the issues for summary judgment or adjudication. (See Hutton v. Fidelity National Title Co. (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 486, 493.) Also, Plaintiff does not further explain how the Credit Memo amounts to a contract, as opposed to simply a statement of the debt owed by Defendant to Plaintiff. It does not appear that the Credit Memo imposes any obligation upon Plaintiff, and it is unclear exactly what obligation it imposes on Defendant. (See Civ. Code, § 1549 [“a contract is an agreement to do or not to do a certain thing.”].) Finally, the Motion confusingly states that the Credit Memo is for $2,070.00 but that Defendant owes Plaintiff $2,080.00. The $2,080.00 amount appears multiple times in Plaintiff’s supporting declaration. (See Motion, Shiring Decl., ¶¶16, 19, 21.) As Plaintiff has not carried its initial burden of proof on the breach of contract cause of action, the request for summary judgment of the Complaint is denied.

The Court additionally notes that the Motion fails to address the third and fourth causes of action for account stated and unjust enrichment, respectively. Code of Civil Procedure section 437c provides “[a] party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a) [emphasis added].) On a motion for summary judgment, a moving plaintiff must show that there is no defense by proving each element of every cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1). By failing to address the third and fourth causes of action, Plaintiff has not met its burden for summary judgment.

Summary Adjudication

Plaintiff alternatively moves for summary adjudication of (1) the breach of contract cause of action; (2) the open book account cause of action; and (3) all the affirmative defenses have no basis is law or fact. 

For the reasons stated above, summary adjudication of the breach of contract cause of action is denied. 

The cause of action for open book account must demonstrate the existence of a written record of the credits and debts between parties to a contract. (CACI No. 372.) The contract may be oral, in writing, or implied by the parties’ words and conduct. (Ibid.) Plaintiff’s evidence of the account running from October 2022 to February 2023 is attached at Exhibit D to the supporting declaration. (Motion, Separate Statement, Fact No. 14; Shiring Decl., ¶13 and Exh. D.) However, of those documents, only the purchase orders dated February 9, 2023 and February 20, 2023 are between named Plaintiff and Defendant. The other orders and invoices do not include Plaintiff’s name. Therefore, summary adjudication of the cause of action for open book account is also denied.

Finally, Plaintiff requests summary adjudication of the affirmative defenses on the grounds that they have no basis in law or fact. In support of this contention with respect to each affirmative defense, the Motion broadly cites to the entire evidentiary record. (Motion, p. 11:21-12:10.) Litigants, however, are required to “reference the number or letter of the exhibit, the specific page, and, if applicable, the paragraph or line number.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1113(k).) Citing the entire evidentiary record does not comply with this requirement. The Court cannot guess which part of the supporting declaration and exhibits supports the specific facts asserted. To the extent Plaintiff’s separate statement with respect to this issue cites only the last paragraph of its counsel’s declaration, the Court finds no facts asserted therein. Only legal conclusions regarding the viability of each affirmative defense, which are insufficient to carry Plaintiff’s burden of proof, are asserted. Therefore, the request for summary adjudication with respect to the affirmative defenses is denied.

Conclusion

Plaintiff Nowhere Holdco, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication, is DENIED.
