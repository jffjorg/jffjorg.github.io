DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV02320    Hearing Date: April 25, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ALEX CONTRERAS

 

         vs.

 

ENTRON HOLDING, INC.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV02320 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: April 25, 2024

 

Sunlight’s motion to quash is GRANTED.

 

On 2/20/2023, Plaintiff Alex Contreras (Plaintiff) filed suit against Entron Holding, Inc., All in Federal Credit Union, and American Contractors, Indemnity Company, alleging: (1) violation of the Consumer Legal Remedies Act; (2) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200; (3) failure of consideration; (4) violation of the Song-Beverly Act; (5) breach of the implied warranty of merchantability; (6) breach of implied warranty of fitness; and (7) violation of Business and Professions Code section 7071.5.

 

            On 2/29/2024, Non-Party Sunlight Financial, LLC (Sunlight) moved to quash the Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records (the Subpoena) served by Plaintiff.

 

Discussion

 

            Sunlight seeks to quash the Subpoena on the grounds that: (1) it was not properly served as a non-California resident; (2) the Subpoena requests personal information of consumers without providing proper notice to consumers; and (3) the Subpoena seeks electronically-stored information that is not reasonably accessible because of undue burden and expense.

 

            After review the Court agrees.

 

            As to the first contention, The California Code of Civil Procedure provides that a California subpoena can be served on any deponent “who is a resident of California at the time of service.” (CCP. § 2020.220 (emphasis added).) Per Contreras’ proof of service of the Subpoena, Sunlight was served on January 9, 2024, at 2710 Gateway Oaks Dr. Ste. 150 N, Sacramento, CA 95833-3502. (Hider Decl., Exh. B.) However, Sunlight is a resident of Delaware and North Carolina, not California. (Carpenter Decl. ¶ 2.) As a non-California resident, it was improper to serve Sunlight in California with a California subpoena.

 

Most states, including California, Delaware, and North Carolina, have adopted a version of the Uniform Interstate Depositions and Discovery Act (UIDDA). The UIDDA provides the proper procedure for obtaining discovery from a resident of a state other than the state where the action is pending.

 

Here, as a resident of North Carolina and Delaware, Contreras could have served Sunlight with a subpoena domesticated in either state. (See N.C.G.S. § 1F-3 (North Carolina’s adoption of the UIDDA, providing that an out of state subpoena must be submitted to the clerk of the county in which discovery is sought, so that a North Carolina subpoena can be issued and served); 10 Del. Code § 4311 (same for Delaware).)

 

California courts have held that subpoena jurisdiction does not reach out-of-state documents held by an out-of-state witness. (See Boal v. Price Waterhouse & Co. (1985) 165 Cal.App.3d 806, 810 (finding that, “if a subpoena is served on a non-party, and requires the personal appearance of a custodian not resident in California, other means must be resorted to secure the documents”); John Deere Co. v. Cone (!962) 239 S.C. 597, 604 (finding a subpoena ineffectual “because the courts of this state are without jurisdiction over persons or property outside of its territory”).

 

In opposition, Plaintiff cites Corporations Code section 2110 to argue that delivery of process against a foreign corporation to a designated corporate agent shall constitute valid service on the corporation. However, the Corporations Code does not define process, and the case law citing section 2110 relates to service of summons, not a subpoena.

 

Because the subpoena was not properly served on Sunlight, a non-resident of California, the Court agrees it must be quashed. However, the Court still addresses the two additional arguments raised by Sunlight in anticipation of future motion practice.

 

As to the contention that the requisite notice was not provided, the Subpoena seeks “All records RELATED to complaints YOU have received about Zenernet LLC doing business as Zenernet LLC from February 6, 2018 to present.” (Hider Decl., Ex. A, p. 3, ¶ 8.) These records consist of complaints made by other consumers, and thus constitute personal records under Section 1985.3(a)(1). Section 1985.3 requires that a party issuing a Subpoena for the production of personal records first serves a notice on the consumer. (CCP. § 1985.3(b).) The Subpoena here contains no such notice (See Hider Decl., Ex. A), and failure to comply with Section 1985.3 “shall be sufficient basis for the witness to refuse to produce the personal records sought by a subpoena duces tecum.” (CCP. § 1985.3(k).)

 

Finally, as to the third contention, Sunlight argues that in order to comply with the Subpoena, it would be required to: “(i) run large-scale searches in these databases of every current and former employee of Sunlight to identify email communications or records related to Contreras’s residential solar panel system; (ii) host the document collections on a third-party e-discovery platform; and (iii) engage outside counsel to manually review the documents to evaluate whether each is responsive, privileged, or otherwise require redactions. Id. ¶ 6.)” (Motion, 5: 24-28.) Sunlight indicates that Plaintiff has not limited custodians, proposed any search terms, or otherwise proposed ways to reduce the burden on Sunlight. In order to produce all of the records sought in the Subpoena, Sunlight and its counsel would be required to develop search terms to search these databases, and then manually review each document for responsiveness, and for any necessary redactions.

 

After review, the Court agrees that the subpoena, as written, is overly broad and would be unduly burdensome in its existing form. However, the Court does not conclude that the requests are so irreparably overbroad and burdensome that it would be impossible to tailor the requests to an appropriate scope. Accordingly, should Plaintiff intend to re-serve a subpoena Sunlight, Plaintiff is directed to narrow its scope and adequately develop and define search terms. The Court urges the parties to meet and confer.

 

Based on the foregoing, Sunlight’s motion to quash is granted.

 

 

It is so ordered.
