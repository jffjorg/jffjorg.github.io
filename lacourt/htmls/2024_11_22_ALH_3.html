I.            INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff BE 830 Lake, LLC (“Plaintiff”) moves for an order awarding $109,459 in attorneys’ fees incurred as part of its attempt to enforce its judgment against defendant East Coast Foods, Inc. dba Roscoe’s House of Chicken & Waffles (“Defendant”). Plaintiff also seeks fees associated with Defendant’s appeal of the Court’s previous order awarding attorney fees and this fee motion.

On November 8, 2024, defendant filed an opposition brief and supporting declaration.

On November 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief and supplemental declaration.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

The most common method for calculating reasonable attorneys’ fees begins with the “lodestar” approach which is calculated as¿“the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (Margolin v. Regional Planning Com. (1982) 134 Cal.App.3d 999, 1004-1005.) “[A]n award of attorney fees may be based on counsel's declarations, without production of detailed time records. (Raining Data Corp. v. Barrenechea (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1375.) “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence. General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.” (Premier Medical Management Systems, Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.)

III.        DISCUSSION

It is undisputed that the Lease underlying this action provides the prevailing party with attorneys’ fees. Here, Plaintiff seeks to recover $109,459.00 in legal fees from August 29, 2023, through October 23, 2024, consisting of 134.8 hours of work. Plaintiff states that approximately $60,000 can be attributed solely to Plaintiff’s efforts to enforce the judgment, and approximately $10,000 can be attributed solely to Plaintiff’s opposition to Defendant’s appeal of the previous attorneys’ fees award. Plaintiff asserts that the remainder of the fees are a hybrid of the foregoing categories, including fees incurred in connection with this motion.

As an initial matter, the Court rejects Defendant’s argument that its bankruptcy should be considered in determining Plaintiff’s fee award. (Opp., p. 10.) Defendant previously raised this issue and cited to the exact same cases included in its opposition brief. The Court did not find Defendant’s cited authorities persuasive then and does not find them persuasive now.

Both of Plaintiff’s attorneys, Thomas Lombardi and Matthew Anderson, billed at an hourly rate of $675 for work performed in 2023 and $695 for work performed in 2024. (Motion, p. 10.) Defendant opposes these rates as unreasonable because it contends that the vast majority of the work performed by Plaintiff’s counsel was clerical/ministerial and does not justify billing at partner rates. Defendant contends that the work performed to enforce the judgment consisted of filling out Judicial Council forms, filing them, and making telephone calls to the Sheriff, which is clerical in nature and does not require the application of partner rates. (Opp., p. 7.) Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s counsels’ rates should be reduced to $100 per hour, which is the amount charged by Plaintiff’s counsel for their clerical person’s time. (Opp., p. 8.) based on the amount at which counsel’s clerical work is billed.

On reply, Plaintiff argues that the allegedly “clerical” work was actually legally substantive because it required legal research, communications, and continuous negotiations with defense counsel, as well as the preparation of four stipulations regarding Defendant’s payments against the judgment and writs of execution, which involved complicated interest calculations. The Court has reviewed the stipulations and other documents and disagrees with Defendant’s characterization of this work as clerical. Plaintiff further explains in its reply brief that the stipulations were necessary because Defendant “refused to make payments against the Judgment unless Defendant could continue to occupy the Premises, which Defendant did through the end of June 2024.” (Reply, p. 5.) The stipulations were drafted to confirm that the payments were to be applied to the judgment and not to rent, and confirmed and preserved Plaintiff’s right to possession of the Premises. (Ibid.) Given Plaintiff’s explanation, and the fact that each billing entry is typically 0.1 to 0.5 hours for each task, the Court declines to reduce the hourly rate charged by Plaintiff’s counsel or reduce the number of hours worked in connection with enforcing the judgment.

Next, Plaintiff seeks approximately $10,000 in connection with work performed to address Defendant’s appeal of the prior attorneys’ fee award, including time spent researching and preparing a motion to dismiss the appeal, as well as the effect of the appeal on collection efforts. Defendant filed the notice of appeal on February 5, 2024, and the appeal was dismissed on July 9, 2024, with the remittitur issued on September 19, 2024. Defendant argues that Plaintiff should not recover any fees related to the appeal because no opening brief was ever filed and the appeal was dismissed for reasons which did not involve Plaintiff, therefore Plaintiff was not required to perform any work. (Opp., p. 5.) Unless defense counsel kept Plaintiff’s counsel apprised of his intent to not pursue the appeal, defense counsel cannot now complain about any work performed in response to Defendant’s decision to file an appeal. The amount of fees claimed equates to about 14.4 hours of work, which is not unreasonable given that the appeal was effectively pending for 5 months and part of the work was determining whether the appeal had any merit or effect on Plaintiff’s collection efforts. Therefore, absent identifying any particular tasks which Defendant deems to be specifically unnecessary, the Court denies the request to omit the fees incurred in connection with Defendant’s appeal from the fees award.

Last, Defendant challenges Plaintiff’s request for $23,282.50 for the 33.5 hours of work involved in preparing this fee motion. Defendant states that this instant motion is a reiteration of Plaintiff’s previous fee motion and, therefore, the award should be reduced by half. Plaintiff acknowledges that portions of its fee motion overlap with its prior fee motion, but asserts that these portions consist only of a total of 2 pages out of 12. Plaintiff also emphasizes that the supporting documents, declaration, and exhibits are unique to the present motion. Having reviewed all the moving and reply papers, the Court finds that 33.5 hours is an excessive amount of time to prepare this relatively straightforward motion, which does not require any complex legal analysis. Therefore, the Court reduces the request by $6,602.50 for an award of $16,680 for the work performed for this motion, consisting of 24 hours of work at counsel’s hourly rate of $695.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s motion for fees is GRANTED subject to a $6,602.50 reduction for a total fee award of $102,856.50.
