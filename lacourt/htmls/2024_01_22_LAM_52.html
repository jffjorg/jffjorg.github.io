DEPARTMENT 52 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Case Number: 22STLC05413    Hearing Date: January 22, 2024    Dept: 52

Cross-Complainant Tywon White’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Deposition Subpoena for Production of Business Records Against Sunlight Financial, LLC

Cross-complainant Tywon White moves to compel non-party Sunlight Financial, LLC (Sunlight) to comply with category Nos. 1-4 and 8 of White’s deposition subpoena for production of business records.  White further moves to compel Sunlight to provide an affidavit from its custodian of records pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2020.430(a)(2) and Evidence Code section 1561.

Authority for Motion to Compel Compliance

Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1(a) provides, “If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of” records, the court “may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.  In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.480(a) provides, “If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.”

Notice to Consumer

Sunlight generally objected to the subpoena on the basis that it “seeks personal records pertaining to at least one consumer or business partners’ consumer without providing that consumer’s written authorization to release personal records or a copy of the proof of service of the notice to the consumer.”  (Valdez Decl., Ex. 2, p. 2.)  When a party serves a “subpoena duces tecum for the production of personal records, the subpoenaing party shall serve or cause to be served on the consumer whose records are being sought a copy of the subpoena duces tecum” and a specified notice to the consumer.  (CCP § 1985.3(b).) 

That procedure does not apply because White does not seek “personal records” under Code of Civil Procedure section 1985.3(a)(1).  That subdivision defines “ ‘Personal records’ ” as copies of documents “pertaining to a consumer and which are maintained by any ‘witness’ which is” one of numerous categories of service providers or institutions, such as healthcare providers, banks, attorneys, accountants, and schools.  (Ibid.)  Sunlight has not shown that it falls within any of those categories.  White therefore was not required to follow the procedure under Code of Civil Procedure section 1985.3. 

Sunlight’s objections on this basis are overruled.

Category Nos. 1-4

            White shows adequate grounds to compel Sunlight to produce documents in response to category Nos. 1-4.  Nos. 1 and 3 demand agreements between Sunlight and parties Entron Holding, Inc. and Alliant Credit Union “related to financing the sale and installation of photovoltaic systems, that were in effect in 2022.”  Nos. 2 and 4 demand Sunlight’s agreements with Entron or Alliant, respectively, regarding the photovoltaic system installed at White’s property.  To these requests, Sunlight responded, “Sunlight is awaiting the entry of a protective order or acceptance of the discussed draft confidentiality agreement prior to production of any documents related to this request.”  (Valdez Decl., ¶ 11, Ex. 4.)  Sunlight also asserted numerous objections it made to all the requests.  (Id., Ex. 2.) 

            White shows good cause for these demands.  The requests are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  All are sufficiently tailored to this dispute.  Sunlight does not establish a valid basis for any of its objections.  It did not oppose this motion.  It did not “promptly move for a protective order.”  (CCP § 2025.420(a).)  Moreover, White offered to enter a protective order, but White and Sunlight could not agree on the terms.  (Valdez Decl., Ex. 5.)

            Sunlight’s objections to category Nos. 1-4 are overruled.

Category No. 8

            White shows adequate grounds to compel Sunlight to produce documents in response to category No. 8.  This category demands, “All records RELATED to complaints YOU have received about Entron Holding, Inc. doing business as Enerpower from January 1, 2019 to present.”  Along with its general objections (Valdez Decl., Ex. 2), Sunlight responded, “Subject to and without waiving the attached objections and previously mailed objection letter, Sunlight has produced the enclosed, responsive documents after a reasonable search of its records.  Sunlight reserves the right to supplement its production in the event more responsive documents are found.”  (Id., Ex. 4, p. 3.)

            This response is insufficient.  A deponent responding to a deposition subpoena for the production of business records must provide “[a]n affidavit in compliance with Section 1561 of the Evidence Code.”  (CCP § 2020.430(a)(2).)  It must be an “affidavit of the custodian or other qualified witness” (Evid. Code, § 1561(a)) and therefore must be signed under penalty of perjury.  The affidavit must also include other statements, including that the deponent provided “a true copy of all the records described in the subpoena duces tecum.”  (Id., subd. (a)(2).)  Sunlight’s unverified response merely indicates it has produced some documents.  It does not state under penalty of perjury that it has produced all documents in its possession within the category demanded.  White is entitled to an order compelling Sunlight to provide a compliant affidavit from its custodian of records or other qualified witness. 

For the same reasons as for category Nos. 1-4, Sunlight does not justify its objections.  Sunlight’s objections to category No. 8 are overruled. 

Disposition

            Cross-complainant Tywon White’s motion to compel non-party Sunlight Financial, LLC to comply with deposition subpoena is granted.  Sunlight Financial, LLC is ordered to produce all documents responsive to category Nos. 1-4, subject to the protective order proposed by White, within 30 days.  Sunlight Financial, LLC is ordered to produce all documents responsive to category No. 8 within 30 days.  Sunlight Financial, LLC is ordered to provide an affidavit in compliance with Evidence Code section 1561 within 30 days. 


Case Number: 23STCV17071    Hearing Date: January 22, 2024    Dept: 52

Defendant Triller Hold Co LLC’s Motion to Set Aside Default

Defendant Triller Hold Co LLC (Triller) moves to set aside its default. 

Triller shows valid grounds for relief from default.  Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) provides, “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”  “Because the law favors disposing of cases on their merits, ‘any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default.’ ”  (Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 980.)

Triller’s default resulted from its mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.  Plaintiff Shayan Rostam personally served the summons on Triller on July 24, 2023, via its registered agent for service of process, Paracorp Inc.  (Kandel Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Paracorp Inc. sent “all notices, including any notices of service of process to Triller, only to” the email address for Triller’s former general counsel, Craig Zimmerman.  (Lee Decl., ¶ 5.)  Zimmerman left Triller unexpectedly on June 5, 2023.  (Id., ¶¶ 3-4.)  “At the time of Mr. Zimmerman’s departure, Triller expected that he would transfer all of his tasks to other members of the in-house legal department, including” in-house attorney Evan Lee, “or to Triller’s outside counsel.”  (Id., ¶ 6.)  “Zimerman did not transfer the Paracorp notification account to anyone else.  No one at Triller knew of this fact.”  (Id., ¶¶ 7-8.) 

The clerk entered Triller’s default on August 30, 2023.  On September 1, 2023, attorney Evan Lee discovered the oversight (Lee Decl., ¶ 9) when he noticed “no one at Triller had received any routine notifications from Paracorp, including service of process notifications, for some time” (id., ¶ 10).

Triller presents sufficient evidence that its default resulted from mistake or excusable neglect.  When former general counsel Craig Zimmerman left the company, Triller mistakenly failed to update its contact information with Paracorp Inc.  Triller and its responsible employees made a simple, human mistake.  Triller’s mistake is excusable.    

Disposition 

Defendant Triller Hold Co LLC’s motion to set aside default is granted.  The court hereby vacates the default entered against defendant Triller Hold Co LLC.  Defendant Triller Hold Co LLC shall file its proposed responsive pleading (the motion to compel arbitration attached as Exhibit D to the declaration of Alexander Kandel) forthwith.
