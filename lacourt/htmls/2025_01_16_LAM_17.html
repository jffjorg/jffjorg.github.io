DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV01271    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

RIVIERA PROPERTY GROUP, LLC

                          

         vs.

 

SOUTHLAND BUILDING & REMODEL, INC.

                                         

 Case No.:  23STCV01271

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 16, 2025

 

Keith Sidlo’s motion for an order substituting him in the place of Riviera Property Group, LLC as the plaintiff in this action is GRANTED.

 

On 1/19/2023, Plaintiff Riviera Property Group, LLC (Plaintiff) initiated this action against Southland Building & Remodel, Inc., Superior Moulding, Inc., Roberto Castaneda, Fleetwood Aluminum Products, Inc., My Town Building & Remodeling, Inc., Valenzuela Drywall ,Inc., Zepeda  General Building, LLC, Juan Zepeda, and Lloyd’s of London, alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) equitable indemnity; (3) breach of contract; (4) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (5) declaratory relief; and (6) bad faith.

 

On 10/31/2024, Keith Sidlo moved the Court for order substituting him in the place of Riviera Property Group, LLC as the plaintiff in this action.

 

The motion is unopposed.

 

Discussion

 

Keith Sidlo seeks a Court for order substituting him in the place of Riviera Property Group, LLC as the plaintiff in this action, on the grounds that Plaintiff Riviera Property Group LLC has assigned its interest in this action to him.

 

Under California law, every action must be prosecuted by the real party in interest (CCP § 367). The court may allow the substitution of a new plaintiff where it is determined that the named plaintiff is not the proper party to maintain the alleged claims, provided that the amendment does not present an entirely new set of facts and the defendant is not prejudiced. (Demetriades v. Yelp, Inc. (2014) 228 Cal. App. 4th 294.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff submitted evidence to show that the original Plaintiff, Riviera Property Group, LLC, has assigned its interest in the claim to Mr. Sidlo. Moreover, the substitution of Mr. Sidlo as the plaintiff will not prejudice the defendants. The amendment does not present an entirely new set of facts and does not impose any additional liability on the defendant. Mr. Sidlo is simply stepping into the shoes of the original plaintiff and will prosecute the same claims.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Keith Sidlo’s motion for an order substituting him in the place of Riviera Property Group, LLC as the plaintiff in this action is granted.

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  January    , 2025

                                                                                                                                                          

   Hon. Jon R. Takasugi
   Judge of the Superior Court

 Hearing Date: January 16, 2025

 

 

Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. This action is stayed pending the completion of arbitration.

 

            On 8/2/2024, Plaintiff Pep Talk, LLC (Plaintiff) filed suit against Greg Harman, Grid Communications, Inc. dba Gridpbx, and Quest Digital Solutions (collectively, Defendants), alleging: (1) civil extortion; (2) false promise; and (3) violations of Business & Professions Code section 17200.

 

            On 11/4/2024, Defendants moved to compel arbitration and stay this action pending the completion of arbitration proceedings.

 

            This motion in unopposed.

 

Legal Standard

 

Where the Court has determined that an agreement to arbitrate a controversy exists, the Court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy …unless it determines that…  grounds exist for rescission of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) Among the grounds which can support rescission are fraud, duress, and unconscionability. (Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 239.) The Court may also decline to compel arbitration wherein there is possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 (c).)

 

Discussion

 

I.                   Defendants’ Burden

 

The party moving to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving [the] existence [of an arbitration agreement] by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) The moving party also bears the burden of demonstrating that the claims fall within the scope of the arbitration agreement. (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

 

A.    Existing Agreement

 

Here, Defendants submitted evidence that Plaintiff entered into a written Price Schedule on 4/26/2023 and 4/27/2024, which incorporated a Service Provider Master Services Agreement, which itself contained an arbitration provision. (Motion, Exh. A.)

 

Section 29.0 of the Service Provider Master Services Agreement provides that “[a]ll claims and disputes arising under or relating to this Agreement are to be settled by binding arbitration in the County and State of Orange County, California” (Ibid.)

 

Given that Defendants have established by a preponderance of the evidence that an arbitration agreement exists, and that Plaintiff’s claims are covered by that agreement, the burden shifts to the Plaintiff to establish that the arbitration clause should not be enforced. (Pinnacle, 55 Cal.4th at p. 236.)

 

II.               Plaintiff’s Burden 

 

The party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence any defense, such as unconscionability or duress. (Pinnacle, supra, 55 Cal.4th at p. 236.)

 

Here, Plaintiff failed to oppose this motion and thus has not set forth any grounds for the unenforceability of the arbitration provision. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration is granted. This action is stayed pending the completion of arbitration.

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: BC669038    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CYNTHIA LOPEZ

                          

         vs.

 

KENNETH LOPEZ

 

                                         

 Case No.:  BC669038

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 16, 2025

 

 

Defendant’s claim of exemption is DENIED IN PART, GRANTED IN PART. Defendant’s claim of exemption is GRANTED as to the Wells Fargo account, but DENIED as to the Fidelity Accounts. 

 

On 11/4/2015, Plaintiff Cynthia Lopez filed suit against Kenneth Lopez. Plaintiff’s first amended complaint (FAC) alleges causes of action for: (1) defamation-libel; (2) defamation-slander; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress; (5) malicious prosecution; (6) abuse of process; and (7) injunctive relief.

 

            On 8/5/2024, Defendant noticed a hearing for a claim of exemption.

 

Discussion  

 

            Defendant filed a claim of exemption, claiming a Wells Fargo account and two investment accounts are exempt from levy.

 

Courts consider several factors when determining whether funds in retirement accounts are exempt. These factors are: (1) the debtor's intent regarding the design and use of the retirement plan; (2) the timing of the plan's creation relative to other events; (3) the degree of control the debtor has over the plan; (4) compliance with, or violation of, IRS and plan rules; and, (5) how the funds are actually used. (O'Brien v. AMBS Diagnostics, LLC (2019) 38 Cal.App. 5th 553, 561 (2019).

 

Defendant argues that the subject accounts are exempt for levy.

 

As for the Wells Fargo account, Defendant submitted documentation to show that the Wells Fargo Bank account at issue had, and has, less than $2,170 in it. (Lopez Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. D). As such, Defendant contends it is exempt from levy under CCP Section 704.220(a), and the California Department of Social Services’ determination that, between July 1, 2024, and June 30, 2024, the exempt amount in Region 1 for a family of 4 is $2,170. (Walton Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A).

 

As for the two investment accounts, Defendant submitted evidence that the two accounts are retirement accounts: one is a traditional IRA, the other is a SEP-IRA. As such, Defendant contends that the Court cannot order the levy upon any assets of that account unless they exceed:

 

(1) the amount “necessary to provide for the support of the judgment debtor when the judgment debtor retires and for the support of the spouse and dependents of the judgment debtor, taking into account all resources that are likely to be available for the support of the judgment debtor when the judgment debtor retires.” (CCP § 704.115(e)); less

 

(2) State and Federal income taxes due on the retirement amounts. (Code Civ. Proc. § 704.115(e)).

 

Here, as of 11/12/2024, the balance in Mr. Lopez’ SEP IRA and Traditional IRA Fidelity accounts is $420,523.45. (Lopez Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. E)

 

Defendant argues that a levy on these accounts would violate California law, because it would deprive him of the amount necessary to provide for his support (CCP § 704.115(e)):

 

Mr. Lopez is currently 55 years old. (Lopez Decl. ¶ 4). He lives with his parents and can live there without paying rent—although he tries to help out with household expenses when able, something he states he has not been able to do recently because of the downturn in property valuation work. (Lopez Decl. ¶ 4). Mr. Lopez’s projected life span, by the Social Security Administration, at age 65, is 16.95 more years after he attains age 65. (Walton Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. B). Thus, if Mr. Lopez retires at age 65, he will need to provide for himself and his wife for another 17 years. His average monthly expenses for the last three months have been $2,587—without having paid any rent, because he is living with his parents. (Lopez Decl. ¶ 5-8, Exh. F-I; Walton Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. C). Multiplying Mr. Lopez’s projected life expectancy of 17 years by 12 months yields 204 months he is projected to survive past age 65. (Walton Decl. ¶ 5). Multiplying 204 months by California’s absolute minimum standard for having a month’s worth of expenses in the bank account of $2,170 equates to $442,680. (Walton Decl. 5)

 

            In opposition, and through supplemental briefing, Plaintiff argues that “the undisputed evidence shows that the Debtor’s intent in the establishment of an alleged SEP-IRA retirement account, in lieu of his prior standard Fidelity brokerage account, was to transfer funds which at the time were not exempt from collection to his new (and secondary to his traditional IRA account) “retirement” account for the purpose of preventing Creditor from collecting on her judgment in this matter.” (Supp. Briefing 12/13/2025, p. 4: 17-22.) In other words, Plaintiff contends that Defendant has intentionally manipulated his contributions and made transfers in order to frustrate efforts to collect in this matter.

 

            More specifically, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-          Creditor offered evidence of Defendant’s gross and net income and the Fidelity statements showing the total contributions for several years. A review of the evidence before the court shows Defendant contributed over the maximum amount from 2017-2023. (Boone Decl. #1, ¶¶14-26, 30-35, Exs. 6-9, and 11-12; Supp. Briefing 12/13/2025, p. 5: 9-28.) Plaintiff’s evidence suggests that the chart shows that $130,752.30 of Defendant’s contributions to his SEP-IRA over those 7 years, from 2017 through 2023, were illegal.

 

-          While Defendant was over-contributing to his SEP-IRA with non-exempt funds, he was simultaneously over-contributing to his second “retirement” account –his traditional IRA. Plaintiff provided evidence that in 2022 and 2023, Defendant contributed at least $21,500.00, by transferring funds from non-exempt accounts, into his Fidelity “Traditional IRA.” Debtor’s over-contributions to his Traditional IRA are likely underestimated as he failed to disclose this account and has yet to produce all statements requested by subpoena. Here, again, Defendant violated IRS rules on contribution limits to traditional IRAs. (Boone Decl., #1, ¶¶37-40, Exs. 1, 7 (Bates #1427 and #1971).

 

-          At his Debtor’s Exam on November 15, 2023, Defendant testified he used his Fidelity SEP-IRA to “borrow” money or make withdrawals from his SEP-IRA to “pay bills” (Boone Decl. #1, ¶27, Ex. 4, p. 42:8-24 and p. 43:5-20). Thus, Defendant effectively uses his SEP-IRA like a holding account, maintaining monies in the account, but then withdrawing funds to immediately pay bills, rather than keeping funds in his personal (non-exempt) Wells Fargo checking account. (Boone Decl.#1, ¶27)

 

-          Additional testimony from the Defendant indicates that he regularly transfers funds from Fidelity into his Wells Fargo checking account (rather than keeping the funds at Wells Fargo) to pay “expenses.” (Boone Decl.#1, ¶28, Ex. 4, p. 46:2-14, p. 48:9-17 and 20-22, p. 49:15-20).

 

-          Defendant testified: he could not "recall" when he opened the Fidelity SEP-IRA account (Boone Decl.#1, Ex. 4, p. 33:3-28 and p.14 34:1- 2); he could not recall how much he put into the account to open it (Boone Decl. #1, Ex. 4, p. 34:3- 9); the source of 2023 deposits was his income (Boone Decl.#1 Ex. 4, p. 34:10-24); he had no “contribution agreement" or plan for his SEP-IRA. (Boone Dec. Ex. 4, p. 35:9-25); and, he does not track how much he contributes each year to his SEP-IRA (Boone Dec. Ex. 4, p. 60:8-10). In addition, the Debtor failed to testify about, or provide any documentation regarding, his second “retirement” account, his traditional IRA, including failing to provide any account or contribution agreement or statements. (Boone Decl. #1, ¶¶2-10,13, 37-40, 43 and 46, Exs. 1-4, and Ex. 14, pp. 34:14-20, 36:4-25, 37:1-19).

 

Notably, Defendant has not disputed any of these claims or figures, nor has he offered any persuasive counter-evidence on the issue.

 

            Moreover, Plaintiff disputes Defendant’s analysis calculating $442,680 as the amount necessary to provide for his support after retirement:

 

The only evidence Debtor and his attorneys submitted is the late-filed evidence submitted 9 court days ahead of the previous hearing. That evidence consisted of two declarations Debtor’s counsel Walton offered a declaration on the debtor’s life expectancy and the amount of money exempt from a bank levy under CCP 704.220 and an “analysis” of Debtors spending. The “analysis” shows that Debtor is spending on average $2,589.99 per month. The “analysis” does not however show what the Debtor is spending his money on.

 

After a review of the Debtor’s credit card statements supplied with Debtor’s declaration, it is clear why this “analysis” doesn’t break down debtor’s expenses. The only point Walton’s declaration appears to make is that the Debtor’s life expectancy multiplied by the minimum amount necessary to support a family of four pursuant to CCP 704.220 is more than what is in Debtor’s retirement accounts currently. Inconveniently for this “analysis” is the fact that Debtor is not now supporting, nor is there any evidence he will ever be supporting, a family of four. The analysis does not take into account the substantial additional assets the Debtor has, including the undisclosed and sizeable KJL trust, the fact that the Debtor will inherit  his parents sizeable estate, any financial assets or future contribution from his wife, and that the Debtor has an additional 10 years to contribute to his retirement.

 

            (12/13/2024 Supp. Briefing, p.10:5-27.)

 

            After review, the Court finds insufficient evidence to support Defendant’s claim of exemption as to the Fidelity accounts. More specifically, the Court finds that Defendant’s overfunding-of and regular utilization of these accounts to pay regular expenses suggests improper sheltering of funds to avoid payment towards judgment. Moreover, Defendant’s calculations of the amount needed to provide for his support are not supported by the submitted evidence. As such, to allow the levy would not run counter to established law. 

 

            As such, the Court denies Defendant’s claim of exemption as to this amount. However, Plaintiff’s supplemental briefing did not address the Wells Fargo account (which is not a retirement account), and thus the Court considers this to be a concession that this account is not subject to levy. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s claim of exemption is denied in part, granted in part. Defendant’s claim of exemption is granted as to the Wells Fargo account, but denied as to the Fidelity Accounts.  

 

 

It is so ordered.
