DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV01838    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: W

KIMBERLY J. SQUIRE v. SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA ORTHOPEDIC INSTITUTE, L.P., et al.

 

motions to compel discovery

 

Date of Hearing:        May 9, 2024                           Trial Date:       February 3, 2025

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV01838

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire

Responding Party:     Defendants Noble Orthopedic Medical Corporation, Eric I. Ferkel, M.D., and Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a medical malpractice action. Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire alleges Defendants were negligent in the surgery on her leg performed in 2020. Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ actions led to Plaintiff suffering a pulmonary embolism in the hospital. Further, their course of treatment post-surgery caused Plaintiff to endure a second injury and a second surgery, as Defendants had her put on too much weight too soon.

 

On April 26, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Noble Orthopedic Medical Corporation, Eric I. Ferkel, M.D., Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P. and Valley Presbyterian Hospital for medical negligence.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P. to Requests for Production is GRANTED in part and denied in part.

II.                  Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Eric Ian Ferkel to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED as to SROG Nos. 5 and 8; DENIED a to SROG Nos. 3, 4, 11, and 13.

III.                Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Noble Orthopedic Medical Corporation to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED as to SROG Nos. 5 and 8; DENIED a to SROG Nos. 3, 4, 11, and 12.

IV.               Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P.  to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED as to SROG Nos. 5 and 8; DENIED a to SROG Nos. 3, 4.

V.                 Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Eric Ian Ferkel to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

VI.               Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Noble Orthopedic Medical Corporation to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

VII.             Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P. to Form Interrogatories is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    PLAINTIFF KIMBERLY J. SQUIRE’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES BY SOUTHERN CALIFORNIA ORTHOPEDIC INSTITUTE, L.P. TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION

 

Plaintiff Kimberly J. Squire moves the court for an order compelling further responses by Defendant Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P. to Requests for Production of Documents, Nos. 1 – 6 and for sanctions in the amount of $7,125.00 as reasonable costs and attorney fees against Defendants Ferkel, Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P., and Noble Orthopedic Medical Corporation and their counsel of record for the instant motion and Plaintiff’s eleven concurrently filed motions.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1).)

 

Plaintiff seeks further responses to RFP Nos. 1, 2, 3, and 4 on the grounds Defendant only agreed to provide “a copy of the office chart of Ms. Squire” and “emails.” Plaintiff contends this is not “all DOCUMENTS” or “all COMMUNICATIONS.” Plaintiff avers the responses further fail to meet the standards of Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220 where the responding party agrees that the documents or things in the demanded category that are in the responding party’s possession, custody, or control will be produced (except to the extent of any objections). To the extent Defendant claims an inability to comply, Plaintiff contends Defendants must fully comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230. As for RFP Nos. 5 and 6, Plaintiff argues the objections are nuisance objections.

 

As this court has previously held, the court finds good cause for the discovery as to RFP Nos. 1-4.  If Noble truly is not in possession of the requested documents, it must comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230. Moreover, Defendant must clearly state that it has provided all documents in its possession, custody or control. As for RFP Nos. 5 and 6, the court agrees with defendant that the requests are vague, ambiguous and overbroad. As such, Defendant need not provide further responses.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses by Southern California Orthopedic Institute, L.P. to Requests for Production is GRANTED in part and denied in part. Defendant to provide further responses to requests 1-4 within 20 days, without objections.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied as previously discussed in the court’s submitted April 30, 2024 minute order.

Case Number: 23VECV02049    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: W

NICHOLAS VILCHIS vs MICHELLE KARASICK, et al.

 

MOTION TO AMEND DEFENDANTS SOCAL SELF STORAGE, LLC’S AND SOCAL SELF STORAGE-COLORADO BOULEVARD, LP’S RESPONSES AND OBJECTIONS TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS, SET ONE

 

Date of Hearing:        May 9, 2024                           Trial Date:       None set

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV02049

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Socal Self Storage, LLC and Socal Self Storage–Colorado Boulevard, LP

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Nicholas Vilchis

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 10, 2023, Plaintiff Nicholas Vilchis filed a complaint against Defendants Michelle Karasick, Matthew Vasquez, Socal Self Storage-Colorado Boulevard LP (“SSS-COLORADO”), Socal Self Storage LLC (“SSS”), and On The Move Inc. for 1. Negligence/Negligence Per-Se; 2. Premises Liability; and 3. Negligent Hiring, Retention & Supervision. Plaintiff alleges while in a parking lot owned and operated by SSS and SSS-Colarado, Defendant Karasick caused her truck to crash into a dolly nearby, which was positioned in front of Plaintiff, causing Plaintiff to fall over and sustain severe neck injuries.

 

On August 4, 2023, On The Move, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against SSS and SSS-Colorado for equitable indemnity, implied indemnity, contribution, and express indemnity.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Motion to Amend Responses and objections to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits evidentiary objections to the declaration of Zojeila I. Flores offered in support of Defendant’s Motion to Amend SoCal Self Storage, LLC’s and SoCal Self Storage—Colorado Boulevard, LP’s Responses and Objections to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One.

 

The court OVERRULES objections nos. 1-4.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Socal Self Storage, LLC and Socal Self Storage–Colorado Boulevard, LP move the court for leave to file amended responses and objections to Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.300. Specifically, they seek to amend response to Request for Admission No. 15.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.300 provides that “[a] party may withdraw or amend an admission made in response to a request for admission only on leave of court granted after notice to all parties.”  (CCP § 2033.300(a).)  “The court may permit withdrawal or amendment of an admission only if it determines that the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.”  (CCP § 2033.300(b).)  “The court may impose conditions on the granting of the motion that are just, including, but not limited to, the following: [¶] (1) [a]n order that the party who obtained the admission be permitted to pursue additional discovery related to the matter involved in the withdrawn or amended admission[;] [¶] (2) [a]n order that the costs of any additional discovery be borne in whole or in part by the party withdrawing or amending the admission.”  (CCP § 2033.300(c).)

 

Defendants argue Socal Defendants mistakenly and inadvertently admitted at least one Green Truck depicted in Exhibit A of Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions Set One was improperly parked. At the time, the admission was made based on an employee of SSS-Colorado looking at Plaintiff’s Exhibit A photograph and believing the green truck behind Plaintiff’s truck was parked improperly. After Defendants’ professional engineer inspected the subject property in February 2024, Defendants learned the green truck originally thought to be parked improperly, was actually parked properly in a marked loading zone. Therefore, the correct response to Requests for Admission No. 15 should have stated “Deny”.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendants’ motion fails to establish that mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect caused both SoCal Defendants to twice admit the truth of RFA 15(sic). Plaintiff contends Defendants’ authorized agent, Allan Farnsworth, did not respond to RFA 15(sic) based on information and belief. Rather, his response, twice made on behalf of each SoCal Defendant, was a clear unequivocal admission absent any objections. Moreover, rather than submitting declarations of the unidentified “professional engineer” and unidentified employee, the motion is based entirely on Defense Counsel’s word. Plaintiff further argues they will suffer prejudice if the discovery is amended because Defendants precluded Plaintiff from deposing or otherwise challenging the opinion of Defendants’ unnamed engineer and unnamed employee. Defendants have not provided any justification for relief from objections. Plaintiff argues if the court is inclined to grant the motion, Plaintiff should be permitted to comment on any change made by Defendants in any forthcoming amended response to RFA 15(sic).

 

The court finds the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, and/or excusable neglect. Defendants have presented evidence the initial response was based on an employee reviewing the Exhibit A. (Flores Decl. ¶4.) The employee mistakenly believed the Green Truck was improperly parked. (Flores Decl. ¶4.) Once further investigation was conducted, Defendants learned the area where the green trucks were parked as depicted in Plaintiff’s Exhibit A Photograph were marked loading areas. (Flores Decl. ¶6.) This is sufficient and any doubts in applying section 2033.300 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief. (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1420.) Moreover, the court finds Plaintiff has not shown prejudice. Plaintiff may continue discovery against the employee or originally examined Exhibit A and/or Defendants’ expert. (See CCP §2033.300(c).)

 

The court declines Plaintiff’s request he be “permitted to comment on any change SoCal Defendant’s make to RFA 15(sic) throughout the case and at trial.” As noted above, Plaintiff may pursue additional discovery into the amended admission.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion to Amend Responses and objections to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set One is GRANTED.


Case Number: 23VECV03809    Hearing Date: May 9, 2024    Dept: W

JUAN CARLOS ANTONIO, et al. vs MARTHA MEJIA CAMPOS, et al.

 

DEFENDANTs’ demurrer with motion to strike the first amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        May 9, 2024                                       Trial Date:       None Set

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03809

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Juan Carlos Antonio and Belen Solano

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Reyes Decl. ¶¶3-5.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 31, 2023, Plaintiffs Juan Carlos Antonio and Belen Solano filed a complaint against Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson for Violation Rent Stabilization Ordinance, Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, Unlawful and Unfair Business Practices in Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200, and Implied Warranty of Habitability. The parties entered into a rental agreement. Plaintiffs allege the unit they rented, however, was unpermitted and had several habitability issues. Plaintiffs further allege Defendants did not properly file a Declaration of Intent to Evict in Order to Comply with a Government Agency’s Order.

 

On March 7, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Defendants’ Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants Martha Mejia Campos and Cristine Annete Mejia Johnson demur to the first and fourth causes of action on the grounds Plaintiff has failed to state facts sufficient to support a claim for Violation of Rent Stabilization Ordinance or Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability.

 

First Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action of for Violation of Rent Stabilization Ordinance on the grounds Plaintiffs do not have a private right of action to sue under the RSO.

 

“Adoption of a regulatory statute does not automatically create a private right to sue for damages resulting from violations of the statute. Such a private right of action exists only if the language of the statute or its legislative history clearly indicates the Legislature intended to create such a right to sue for damages.” (Vikco Ins. Services, Inc. v. Ohio Indem. Co. (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 55, 62.)

 

Defendants argue in the instant matter, Plaintiffs’ FAC fails to cite any authority providing a private right of action to sue for violation of the RSO. Plaintiffs only cite to LAMC § 151.05 which merely refers to registration of a rental unit, notification of registration to tenants, and posting of the registration notice. Defendants contend said municipal code does not provide any private right of action in support of Plaintiffs’ first cause of action. Likewise, LAMC §§ 151.02, 151.04, and 151.09(A) do not afford Plaintiffs a private right of action to sue for violation of the RSO.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Los Angeles Municipal Code section 151.10 does provide for a private right of action. Section 151.10 provides “Any person who demands, accepts or retains any payment of rent in excess of the maximum rent or maximum adjusted rent in violation of the provisions of this chapter, or any regulations or orders promulgated hereunder, shall be liable in a civil action to the person from whom such payment is demanded, accepted or retained for damages of three times the amount by which the payment or payments demanded, accepted or retained exceed the maximum rent or maximum adjusted rent which could be lawfully demanded, accepted or retained together with reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs as determined by the court.” (emphasis added.) Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants for violation of sections 151.04 and 151.05. However, Plaintiff has not alleged or demonstrated that 151.05 entitles Plaintiff to damages pursuant to section 151.10. (See Lyles v. Sangadeo-Patel (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 759, 766.) While Section 151.04 would entitle Plaintiff to damages, Plaintiff has not alleged an actual violation. Plaintiff’s allegations generally allege Defendant has not complied with Section 151.04(C) and continues to fail to comply with the section because it did not correct the issues for the unpermitted rental unit. However, that section refers to demand or requirement of electronic funds transfer. As a result, some of the allegations do not support Plaintiff’s private right of action under Section 151.10. The complaint should focus on only the illegal collection of rent if that is what Plaintiff complains thereof.

 

There is no private right of action for violations of sections 151.05 and 151.11; those sections allow the tenant to withhold the rent, but not sue for liability. (Lyles v. Sangadeo-Patel (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 759, 768.)

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.            

 

Fourth Cause of Action

 

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for implied warranty of habitability on the grounds the cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim against Defendants.

 

The essential elements of a cause of action for breach of implied warranty of habitability are: (1) a materially defective condition of the property affecting habitability; (2) the defective condition was unknown to the tenant at the time of occupancy; (3) the effect on habitability of the defective condition was not apparent on reasonable inspection; (4) notice was given to the landlord within a reasonable time after the tenant discovered, or should have discovered, the breach; and (5) damages. (Quevedo v. Braga (1977) 72 Cal.App.3d Supp. 1, 7—8 (Quevedo), overruled on other grounds by Knight v. Hallsthammar (1981) 29 Cal. 3d 46, 55 [disapproving of the element that landlord must have been allowed reasonable time to repair].) 

 

Defendants argue Plaintiffs fail to allege that notice was provided to Defendants about a material defective condition affecting the premises habitability, fails to allege that Plaintiffs provided notice to the Defendants of the condition within a reasonable time after Plaintiffs’ discovery of the condition, and fails to allege that Defendants were given reasonable time to correct the deficiency. Plaintiff’s FAC also fails to allege specific damages caused by the alleged breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues an illegally converted unit without certificate of occupancy or permitted electrical, plumbing, etc. are presumptively defective. Plaintiff contends one would imagine that the illegal conversion was done in a manner that the Property owner would be aware of, or at least have constructive knowledge, of the illegal condition. Moreover, the complaint outlines the facts satisfying the elements of implied warranty of habitability. (FAC ¶¶78-96.)

 

The court finds the allegations insufficient. A tenant can reasonably expect that the landlord will maintain the property in a habitable condition by repairing promptly any conditions, of which the landlord has actual or constructive notice, that arise during the tenancy.  (Peterson v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1185, 1205-26.) As such, Plaintiff’s allegations Defendant had knowledge of the fact the rental unit never had proper licenses for rental or construction permits is sufficient to meet the notice requirements. However, it is unclear from the complaint that that is the material defect Plaintiff complains thereof. The fourth cause of action is dedicated to issues with the unit but fails to allege how that is related to Defendants’ knowledge that the unit was constructed without proper licenses or construction permits. Plaintiff’s complaint need allege only the relevant facts.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendants move to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint including Plaintiff’s demand for rent and request for relocation assistance as well as Plaintiff’s request for attorney fees.

 

Defendants first argue Plaintiffs’ demand for past rent collected in the amount of $67,000.00 and relocation assistance in the amount of $20,850.00 are improper as Plaintiffs’ FAC fails to allege any specific Los Angeles Municipal Code or ordinance that Defendants allegedly violated, nor do Plaintiffs cite any authority providing them a private right of action to sue for violation of the RSO. More importantly, Plaintiffs fail to allege any statute or case law entitling them to seek recovery of past rent collected and relocation fees under the Los Angeles Municipal Code.

 

As the court has sustained the demurrer, the motion to strike is moot. The court notes Plaintiff must allege the section for relocation assistance/fees. Section 151.10 provides the rent paid in excess could be lawfully demanded, accepted or retained together with reasonable attorneys’ fees and costs as determined by the court.

 

Next, Defendants argue Plaintiffs cannot recover attorney fees in connection with the first, second, and fourth causes of action on the grounds Plaintiffs’ FAC fails to allege any specific LAMC or ordinance Defendants allegedly violated. If Plaintiff can properly allege a cause of action against Defendants, Plaintiff has grounds for attorney fees pursuant to Section 151.10 and unlawful violation of Business and Professions Code 17200. However, the court agrees Plaintiff has not alleged recovery of attorney fees under the second and fourth cause of action. There are no allegations supporting such a recovery. As for the third cause of action, “[i]f a plaintiff prevails in an unfair competition law claim, it may seek attorney fees as a private attorney general pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1021.5.” (Walker v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1158, 1179.) Accordingly, the request for attorney fees is sufficient at this time with respect to that cause of action.  Since leave to amend is granted, plaintiffs should clarify the cause of action for which they seek fees and the statutory or other basis for such fees.
