DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 22CHCV01321    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 5/2/24                                                                       TRIAL DATE: 8/12/24

Case #22CHCV01321

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

 

Motion filed on 3/25/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Oliva Morena Posada aka Oliva M. Posada

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order compelling Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. to produce a Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian of Records to be deposed in accordance with CCP 2025.230.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP in the amount of $2,490.00.

 

RULING: The motion is granted as set forth below.

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff Olivia Morena Posada aka Olivia M. Posada’s (Plaintiff) lease  of a 2022 Honda CR-V (the Vehicle) pursuant to a “Closed-Ended Motor Vehicle Lease Agreement – California” (Lease Agreement).  Plaintiff contends that the Vehicle is defective and Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (Honda) has been unable to service or repair the Vehicle to conform to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of opportunities.  Plaintiff further contends that despite the foregoing, Honda has failed to promptly replace the Vehicle as required under the Song-Beverly Act.    

 

As a result, on 12/7/22, Plaintiff filed this action against Honda for: (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty and (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty.  Honda filed an answer to the complaint on 1/3/23. 

 

On 12/14/23, Plaintiff served Honda with a Notice of Deposition of Honda’s Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian(s) of Record.  (Tran Decl., Ex.A).  Although the deposition was noticed for 1/25/24, Plaintiff requested that Honda’s counsel provide alternative dates by 1/4/24, if Honda or its counsel were not available on the noticed date.  (Tran Decl., Ex.B).  Honda did not serve any objections to the deposition notice.  (Tran Decl.).

 

On 1/24/24, Plaintiff sent Honda a video link to enable Honda’s witness to participate in the deposition.  (Tran Decl., Ex.B).  Honda did not appear for the 1/25/24 deposition claiming that they had no record of receiving the notice.  Id.  Thereafter, Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email to Honda offering an additional 3 business days to provide dates for the deposition.  Id.  Honda responded by indicating that it was reaching out for dates but did not provide any alternative dates and ignored a follow-up email sent on 2/7/24.  Id. 

 

On 3/25/24, Plaintiff filed and served the instant motion seeking an order compelling Honda to produce a Person(s) Most Qualified and Custodian of Records to be deposed in accordance with CCP 2025.230.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP in the amount of $2,490.00.  Honda has not opposed or otherwise responded to the motion. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

The undisputed evidences shows that Plaintiff timely served Honda with a Notice of Deposition.  See CCP 2025.270(a); (Tran Decl., Ex.A).  Additionally, the matters for examination in the Notice of Deposition were reasonably particularized as required.  CCP 2020.310; CCP 2025.230;  (Tran Decl., Ex.A).  Plaintiff has also shown the requisite good cause for compelling the subject deposition as the information and documents sought are not privileged and are relevant to Plaintiff’s claims under the Song-Beverly Act.  See CCP 2017.010; CCP 2025.450(b)(1); (Tran Decl., Ex.A).  As noted above, Honda failed to serve any objections to the deposition notice.  As such, Honda has waived any objections.  CCP 2025.410(a).  Further, Honda failed to respond to Plaintiff’s last meet and confer effort before the motion was filed and served.  CCP 2025.450(b)(2). 

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling Honda to produce its Person(s) Most Qualified and to produce documents at deposition pursuant to the Notice of Deposition served on 12/14/23.  CCP 2025.450(a). 

 

Plaintiff is also entitled to an award of sanctions against Honda and its counsel, Clark Hill LLP, in the amount of $1335.00 (2 hours to prepare motion + 1 hour to prepare for and attend the hearing at $425/hour = $1275.00 + $60.00 filing fee) for their failure to comply with their discovery obligations.  CCP 2025.450(g)(1); (Tran Decl. ¶¶9-10).    

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted.  Honda is ordered to produce is Person(s) Most Qualified and to produce documents at deposition within the next 15 days.  Sanctions are payable within the next 15 days.

 


Case Number: 23CHCV03526    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 5/2/24

Case #23CHCV03526

 

DEMURRER & MOTION TO STRIKE TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Demurrer & Motion to Strike filed on 4/2/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant General Motors LLC

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Elya Braden

NOTICE: ok

 

Demurrer is to the 4th and 5th causes of action:

            1.  Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty

            2.  Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty

            3.  Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Civil Code 1793.2

            4.  Fraud

            5.  Violation of Business & Professions Code 17200

            6.  Negligent Repair

 

RELIEF REQUESTED IN MOTION TO STRIKE: An order striking the prayer “For…exemplary or punitive damages… .” (Prayer, Item f, p.23:20).

 

RULING: The demurrer is sustained with 30 days leave to amend.  The motion to strike is placed off calendar as moot.   

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises from Plaintiff Elya Braden’s (Plaintiff) purchase of a 2020 Chevrolet Bolt (the Vehicle) on 11/7/20.  (FAC ¶6).  Plaintiff alleges that the Vehicle was not safe or functional because the batteries may ignite when they are either fully charged or fall below the seventy-mile range.  (FAC ¶18).

 

On 11/17/23, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendant General Motors LLC (General Motors) and Defendant Paradise Chevrolet for: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; (3) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Civil Code 1793.2; (4) Fraud; (5) Violation of Business & Professions Code 17200 and (6) Negligent Repair.  After meet and confer efforts failed to resolve the issues General Motors had with Plaintiff’s original complaint, on 12/18/23, General Motors filed and served a demurrer to the 4th and 5th causes of action and motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages.  On 3/1/24, before the hearing on the demurrer, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint which alleges the same six causes of action as the original complaint.  As such, the hearing on the demurrer was placed off calendar.  (See 3/12/24 Notice of Withdrawal; 3/13/24 Minute Order). 

 

After meet and confer efforts failed to resolve the issues General Motors had with the 4th and 5th causes of action in the First Amended Complaint, General Motors filed and served the instant demurrer to the 4th and 5th causes of action and motion to strike which seeks an order striking the prayer “For…exemplary or punitive damages… .”  Plaintiff filed a late combined opposition to  the demurrer and motion to strike and General Motors has filed a reply to the opposition.  Despite the late filing and service, the Court considered the opposition in ruling on the demurrer and motion to strike.  See CRC 3.1300(d). 

 

ANALYSIS

 

General Motors’ Request for Judicial Notice (RJN) is granted. 

 

General Motors demurs to the 4th and 5th causes of action on the ground that they fail to set forth sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.  CCP 430.10(e).

 

4th Cause of Action – Fraud

 

Plaintiff’s fraud claim is based on alleged affirmative misrepresentations in the marketing the Vehicle as having long range capability and fraudulent concealment with regard to mileage range and battery safety.  (See FAC ¶¶92-105).

 

The elements of a claim for fraud based on misrepresentation are: (1) misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation and (5) resulting damages.  Cansino (2014) 224 CA4th 1462, 1469.  The elements of a claim for fraud based on concealment are: (1) the defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant had a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as they  did if they had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff sustained damage.  Jones (2011) 198 CA4th 1187, 1198  Whether based on affirmative misrepresentations or concealment, fraud claims must be pled with specificity meaning with facts showing how, when, where, to whom and by what means the allege fraud occurred.  Lazar (1996)  12 C4th 631, 645; Tarmann (1991) 2 CA4th 153, 157; Blickman Turkus, LP (2008) 162 CA4th 858, 878. 

 

With regard to the allegations within the fraud cause of action, Plaintiff fails to allege: (1) the identity of the General Motors employee that made the representations to, or concealed the facts from, Plaintiff; (2) the employee’s authority to speak and act on behalf of General Motors; (3) General Motor’s knowledge about alleged defects in the Vehicle at the time of Plaintiff’s purchase, (4) what specific General Motors representations were reviewed and relied on in purchasing the Vehicle; (5) how long prior to purchasing the Vehicle the representations were reviewed, if even reviewed pre-purchase, and which representation therein was relied upon; and (6) whether those materials were prepared by General Motors or someone else (such as an independent dealership).  Plaintiff also failed to plead specific facts showing that General Motors intended to defraud Plaintiff by either making affirmative statements or failing to disclose material facts.  See Tenzer (1985) 39 C3d 18, 30 (“something more than non-performance is required to prove the defendant’s intent not to perform his promise”).

 

Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts regarding her justifiable reliance on the purported misrepresentations regarding the long-range use and charging capabilities of the vehicle.  It is not clear where, when or if Plaintiff saw/heard such representations before purchasing the Vehicle and/or from whom.  (See FAC ¶92 (representations “made on GM’s website, among other places,” “echoed by GM’s dealership personnel…”, ¶95 “false and misleading representations…were part of an extensive advertising campaign…”).

 

To the extent the fraud/misrepresentation claim is based on Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) mileage range estimates, such cannot form the basis for a fraud claim.  See Gray (9th Cir. 2014) 554 Fed.Appx. 608, 609; Paduano (2009) 169 CA4th 1453, 1470; (RJN, Park Decl., Ex.A); (FAC ¶20).

 

Plaintiff has also failed to sufficiently plead General Motors had knowledge of the purported defects before Plaintiff purchased the Vehicle and/or that Plaintiff’s Vehicle actually suffered from any purported defect.  Plaintiff alleges General Motors had general knowledge regarding battery issues with earlier model Bolt vehicles.  (See FAC ¶¶21-27).  Plaintiff never specifically alleges what issues she had with the subject vehicle.  (See FAC ¶15 and preceding paragraphs) 

 

The First Amended Complaint also mentions representations made by General Motors’ Vice-President Steve Hill regarding providing Plaintiff with a new battery for the Vehicle and then reneging on such promise.  (See FAC ¶¶50-57).  Such representation was made after Plaintiff’s purchase; therefore, Plaintiff could not have relied on the representation in making the purchase.  Additionally, such allegations are not specifically included in the fraud cause of action.  (FAC ¶¶92-105).  As such, it is not clear whether Plaintiff intended to base the fraud claim on this representation and/or how she was damaged by same (i.e., by not selling the Vehicle or requesting that General Motors repurchase the Vehicle sooner which Plaintiff alleges was General Motors’ earlier practice?)  (See FAC ¶¶53-56).   

 

“There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.’”  LiMandri (1997) 52 CA4th 326, 336.  Here, no fiduciary relationship existed between Plaintiff and General Motors.  The remaining three circumstances require a transaction between the parties giving rise to a duty to disclose.  Id. at 336-337.  Such a transaction has been held to require direct dealings between the plaintiff and defendant.  Bigler-Engler (2017) 7 CA5th 276, 312, 314-315.

 

Here, Plaintiff admits that he did not buy the Vehicle from General Motors and concludes that the dealership from which he actually purchased the Vehicle was the agent of General Motors.  (FAC ¶¶6-11).  Even if the agency allegations are deemed sufficient to support the finding of a transactional relationship between Plaintiff and General Motors, Plaintiff has failed to plead facts to establish that the concealment claim is not barred by the economic loss rule.       

 

“[T]he economic loss rule allows a plaintiff to recover in strict products liability in tort when a product defect causes damage to ‘other property,’ that is, property other than the product itself.  The law of contractual warranty governs damage to the product itself.”  Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc. (2004) 34 C4th 979, 989 citing Jimenez (2002) 29 C4th 473, 482-483.  “[W]here a purchaser’s expectations in a sale are frustrated because the product he bought is not working properly, his remedy is said to be in contract alone, for he has suffered only ‘economic’ losses.” Robinson Helicopter Co., Inc., supra at 988.  Further, emotional injuries incidental to purely economic damages based on fraudulent concealment are also barred by the economic loss rule. See Butler-Rupp (2005) 134 CA4th 1220, 1229.  Here, it is not clear what damage Plaintiff is alleging other than that she would not have purchased the Vehicle and that she suffered anxiety and fear after the purchase.  (FAC ¶¶48, 104, 105).      

 

Plaintiff’s reliance on Dhital (2022) 84 CA5th 828 for the proposition that the fraud claim is not barred by the “economic loss rule” is unpersuasive.  First, Dhital is under review by the Supreme Court of California as to the economic loss rule; therefore, it is not citable.  Additionally, the Court of Appeal has recently held that unless there is a specific assertion of agency in the complaint, there is no showing of agency between Ford and the authorized dealerships.  See Ochoa (2023) 89 CA5th 1324 (review granted 7/9/23).  Here, Plaintiff has failed to allege sufficient facts to support the conclusory allegation that “[t]he dealership is an agent of GENERAL MOTORS for purposes of the sale.”  (See FAC ¶6).    

 

5th Cause of Action - Violation of Business & Professions Code 17200

 

A claim for violation of the unfair competition law (UCL) may be based on allegations of unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business practices by the defendant.  See Business & Professions Code 1700, 1705.  To the extent Plaintiff’s claim is based on fraudulent conduct by General Motors, it fails for the same reasons set forth above regarding the fraud cause of action.

 

The unlawful prong of the UCL “borrows violations of other laws and treats them as unlawful practices,” which the UCL then “makes independently actionable.”  Cel–Tech Communications, Inc. (1999) 20 C4th 163, 180.  Here, Plaintiff has failed to identify any underlying law on which the claim is based.  (FAC ¶¶133-143).

 

As to the unfair prong, Plaintiff has failed to sufficiently allege an established public policy which General Motors’ conduct has violated or that its conduct was immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits which are necessary to state a claim.  See McKell (2006) 142 CA4th 1457, 1473.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The demurrer is sustained for the reasons set forth above.  Due to the liberal policy of allowing leave to amend and because this is only the First Amended Complaint and the first time Plaintiff’s pleading has been ruled on by the Court, Plaintiff is given the opportunity to try to cure the defects in the 4th and 5th causes of action.  

 

The motion to strike addresses the prayer for relief in relation to the 4th cause of action.  Due to the ruling on the demurrer, the motion to strike is placed off calendar as moot. 

 

A Second Amended Complaint is due within 30 days.
