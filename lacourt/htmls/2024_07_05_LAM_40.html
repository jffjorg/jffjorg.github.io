DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 23STCV18241    Hearing Date: July 5, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

AIDEE JACQUELINE TINOCO BUCIO

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KHOWSROW BENJI, an individual; ANNA BENJI, an individual; DOES 1 THROUGH 100, inclusive.

                        Defendants.

______________________________________

KHOSROW BENJI, an individual,

                        Cross-Complainant,

            v.

AIDEE JACQUELINE TINOCO BUCIO, an individual; and ROES 1 through 5, inclusive,

                        Cross-Defendants.

 Case No.:          23STCV18241

 Hearing Date:   7/5/24

 Trial Date:        11/12/24

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio’s Demurrer to Defendant/Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji’s Cross Complaint [Res ID # 3125].

 

I. Background

On February 26, 2024, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji filed a Cross-Complaint for Damages in this action, which alleges claims of (1) Negligence, (2) Assault, (3) Battery, (4) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED), and (5) Physical Elder Abuse against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio (Tinoco Bucio) and Roes 1 through 5.

On March 18, 2024, Tinoco Bucio filed a demurrer challenging the Cross-Complaint’s five causes of action.

On June 24, 2024—eight court days before this hearing—Khowsrow Benji filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (FAXC), which omits the assault claim and adds facts in support of the remaining four causes of action.

On June 26, 2024—four court days before this hearing—Tinoco Bucio filed a reply arguing that the Court should reject the proposed FAXC as untimely and contending that the FAXC is otherwise defective as drafted.

Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer is now before the Court.

 

II. Sua Sponte Motion to Strike: FIRST AMENDED CROSS-COMPLAINT STRICKEN.

A. Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)

B. Court’s Determination

The Court STRIKES the June 24, 2024, First Amended Cross-Complaint (FAXC).

“A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time … after a … motion to strike is filed but before the … motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the … motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 472, subd. (a).) “All papers opposing a motion so noticed shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days … before the hearing.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) The right is limited to the amendment of a complaint initiating an action to a first amended complaint. (Hedwall v. PCMV, LLC (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 564, 572-579 [holding, as a matter of first impression, that a party’s right to amend a pleading as a matter of course under Code of Civil Procedure section 472, subdivision (a) is limited to the original pleading commencing the action].)

Here, Khowsrow Benji filed the FAXC eight court days prior to this hearing rather than nine court days prior to the hearing. While the Court could ordinarily permit this amendment by making a case-specific order for that relief subject to due process (Rutherford v. Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 966-967), here, the original Cross-Complaint and FAXC show similar shortcomings as pointed out by Bucio (reply, pp. 3-5). For this reason, the Court instead exercises its discretion to sua sponte strike the June 24, 2024, FAXC and instead analyze the allegations in the Cross-Complaint as against Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer arguments.

 

III. Demurrer

A. Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212, superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) “[E]ach evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) Courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

A demurrer may only be used to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

B. Analysis

1. Demurrer, Cross-Complaint, First Cause of Action, Negligence: OVERRULED.

a. Relevant Law

“‘The elements of a cause of action for negligence are … “(a) a legal duty to use due care; (b) a breach of such legal duty; [and] (c) the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.”’” (Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.)

b. Court’s Determination

The Court finds in favor of Khowsrow Benji.

In her demurrer, Tinoco Bucio argues that the negligence claim fails to allege a duty and a breach of duty.

The Court disagrees.

Liability for negligent conduct may only be imposed where there is a duty of care owed by the defendant to the plaintiff or to a class of which the plaintiff is a member. (Richards v. Stanley (1954) 43 Cal.2d 60, 63.) A duty of care may arise through statute or by contract. Alternatively, a duty may be premised upon the general character of the activity in which the defendant engaged, the relationship between the parties or even the interdependent nature of human society. (See Valdez v. J. D. Diffenbaugh Co. (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 494, 505.) Whether a duty is owed is simply a shorthand way of phrasing what is “‘the essential question whether the plaintiff’s interests are entitled to legal protection against the defendant’s conduct.’” (Dillon v. Legg (1968) 68 Cal.2d 728, 734, citation omitted.) “The existence of a duty of care is a question of law decided on a case-by-case basis.” (M.W. v. Panama Buena Vista Union School Dist. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 508, 516.)

Here, the Cross-Complaint sufficiently alleges a duty from Tinoco Bucio to Khowsrow Benji, namely, the duty of a housekeeper to exercise reasonable care in the course of her occupation and to follow the instructions of the homeowner that hired the housekeeper. (See, e.g., Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 8-12.)

The Cross-Complaint also alleges a breach of that duty: failure to follow specific instructions from the homeowner, which led the homeowner to walk over surfaces that should not have been cleaned and were slippery, causing Khowsrow Benji to fall and injure himself. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 8-16, 17-23.)

Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint’s first cause of action is thus OVERRULED.

2. Demurrer, Cross-Complaint, Second Cause of Action, Assault: SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

a. Relevant Law

“The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm. [Citations].” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669 (So), as modified on denial of reh’g (Jan. 28, 2013).)

b. Court’s Determination

Here, the Cross-Complaint fails to allege apprehension, which is a necessary component of assault. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 8-16, 24-30.)

Moreover, the allegations in the Cross-Complaint show that these allegations cannot be cured to sufficiently allege an assault claim against Tinoco Bucio. This is because Khowsrow Benji alleges that he fell after he, sight unseen and with no apprehension, approached the patio area that Tinoco Bucio washed with soap against Benji’s explicit instructions.

Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint’s second cause of action is thus SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

3. Demurrer, Cross-Complaint, Third Cause of Action, Battery: SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

a. Relevant Law

“The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching. [Citations.]” (So, supra, 212 Cal.App.4th at p. 669.)

b. Court’s Determination

Here, the Cross-Complaint (1) fails to allege that Tinoco Bucio ever touched Khowsrow Benji (the touch here allegedly being cleaning a patio floor and leaving it wet/slippery as to cause Benji to slip and fall) and (2) fails to allege intent to harm.

As for touch, even though battery is not limited to direct body-to-body contact, the tort usually requires that one person make contact with the body of another such as by throwing water on the other person. (See, e.g., Mount Vernon Fire Ins. Co. v. Busby (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 876, 881 [throwing water onto someone as battery].) Here, however, the Cross-Complaint alleges that Khowsrow Benji, of his own volition, made contact with a patio floor that should not have been cleaned. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 8-16, 31.) Otherwise stated, Tinoco Bucio did not physically touch Khowsrow Benji nor did Tinoco Bucio force a third object (e.g., water, bat, umbrella, etc.) to make contact with Khowsrow Benji. Rather, Benji walked into the patio area and slipped on a surface that should not have been washed by Tinoco Bucio. (Ibid.) More than anything, the Cross-Complaint alleges that it should have been foreseeable to Tinoco Bucio that washing the patio after Khowsrow Benji instructed Tinoco Bucio not to wash that patio could result in a person slipping while that person in fact crossed the washed patio. Such a claim sounds in negligence, not in intentional tort.

As for intent, the Cross-Complaint fails to allege this element other than in conclusory fashion. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 32.) Nor are there allegations in the Cross-Complaint that would allow the Court to infer that malicious intent underlay Tinoco Bucio’s actions in washing the patio floor.

Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint’s third cause of action is thus SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

4. Demurrer, Cross-Complaint, Fourth Cause of Action, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

a. Relevant Law

“A cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress exists when there is ‘(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.’ A defendant’s conduct is ‘outrageous’ when it is so ‘extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.’ And the defendant’s conduct must be ‘intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.’” (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051.)

b. Court’s Determination

The Court adopts its intent reasoning from the battery subsection above to determine that the IIED claim is conclusorily alleged as to the intent to cause harm to Khowsrow Benji.

Moreover, the Court determines that the type of injury described in the Cross-Complaint, even if accepted, does not constitute outrageous conduct so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerable in a civilized community. Indeed, the Cross-Complaint fails to allege that Tinoco Bucio intentionally washed the patio floor so that the Benji Defendants would be injured. Instead, the Cross-Complaint alleges that Tinoco Bucio washed the patio floor against express instruction and that Khowsrow Benji slipped on the patio floor when his wife asked him to grab cookies and tea from the house and to bring the same to the outdoor dining area. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 8-15.) Thus, the Cross-Complaint alleges a failure to follow instructions regarding where to clean by Tinoco Bucio and a coincidental fall by Khowsrow Benji when he attempted to retrieve tea and cookies from the house, as prompted by his wife, not by Tinoco Bucio.

Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint’s fourth cause of action is thus SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

5. Demurrer, Cross-Complaint, Fifth Cause of Action, Physical Elder Abuse: SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

a. Relevant Law

The Elder Abuse Act defines abuse as: “[p]hysical abuse, neglect, financial abuse, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering” (Welf. & Ins. Code, § 15610.07, subd. (a)); or “[t]he deprivation by a care custodian of goods or services that are necessary to avoid physical harm or mental suffering” (Welf. & Ins. Code, § 15610.07, subd. (b)).

“Physical abuse” means any of the following:

(a) Assault, as defined in Section 240 of the Penal Code;

(b) Battery, as defined in Section 242 of the Penal Code;

(c) Assault with a deadly weapon or force likely to produce great bodily injury, as defined in Section 245 of the Penal Code;

(d) Unreasonable physical constraint, or prolonged or continual deprivation of food or water;

(e) Sexual assault under various meanings; and

(f) Use of a physical or chemical restraint or psychotropic medication under certain conditions.

(Welf. & Ins. Code, § 15610.63.)

b. Court’s Determination

The Court SUSTAINS Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer to the Cross-Complaint’s fifth cause of action because the fifth cause of action is derivative of the “physical abuse” alleged in the second and third causes of action, against which the Court sustained Tinoco Bucio’s demurrer without leave to amend.

As to all of the above claims the Court would sustain, the Court will nonetheless entertain oral argument as to whether Cross-Complainant can meet his burden show that there are facts that could be alleged to justify leave to amend that do not directly contradict these allegations. Leave to amend should not be granted unless plaintiff can establish that facts exist sufficient to state a cause of action.  (Community Assisting Recovery, Inc. v. Aegis Security Ins. Co. (2001) 92 Cal. App. 4th 886, 895.)  Plaintiff has the burden of establishing in what manner it would be possible to amend the complaint.  (Fuller v. First Franklin Financial Corp (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 955, 962-963.)

 

IV. Conclusion

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Aidee Jacqueline Tinoco Bucio’s Demurrer to Defendant/Cross-Complainant Khowsrow Benji’s Cross Complaint [Res ID # 3125] is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part as follows:

(1) OVERRULED as to the Cross-Complaint’s first cause of action; and

(2) SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the Cross-Complaint’s second to fifth causes of action.

The Court will nonetheless entertain oral argument as to whether Cross-Complaint can show that there are facts that could be alleged to justify leave to amend.

I. Background

On February 12, 2024, Defendant Union Mutualista De San Jose (Union Mutualista) served a first set of production requests and request for admission on Plaintiff Rafael Davila Madrigal (RPDs, Set One; RFAs, Set One) and a first set of form interrogatories and requests for admission on Plaintiff Alicia Brisila Davila (FROGs, Set One; RFAs, Set One).

Based on Plaintiffs’ nonresponse to this discovery, on March 27, 2024, Union Mutualista filed motions to (1) compel initial responses to FROGs Set One, from Alicia Brisila Davila, (2) compel initial responses to RPDs, Set One, from Rafael Davila Madrigal, and (3)-(4) deem the truth of RFAs, Set One, admitted as against both Plaintiffs. All four motions request monetary sanctions. The motions attach proofs of service showing service of the four motions on Plaintiffs in pro per at their Wilmington, California residence.

No oppositions, replies, or notices of non-opposition appear in the record.

Union Mutualista’s motions are now before the Court.

 

II. Motions to Compel Initial Discovery Responses or Deem Admitted and Requests and Requests for Sanctions

A. Motions to Compel Initial Discovery Responses or Deem Admitted: GRANTED.

1. Legal Standard

A motion to compel an initial response can be made on the ground that a party did not serve a timely response to interrogatories or a demand to produce. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a) [interrogatories], 2031.300, subd. (a) [demand to produce]; see Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404 (Sinaiko).) To establish this ground, a movant must show:

(1) Proper service (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.080, subd. (a) [interrogatories], 2031.040 [demand to produce], 2033.070 [admission requests]);

(2) Expiration of the deadline for the initial response 30 days after service or on date agreed to by parties (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.260, subds. (a), (b) [interrogatories], 2031.260, subds. (a), (b) [demand to produce], 2033.250, subds. (a), (b) [admission requests]); and

(3) No timely response (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 [interrogatories], 2031.300 [demand to produce], 2033.280, subd. (b) [admission requests]).

A court must deny a motion to compel initial discovery where the discovery sought is outside the scope of discovery. (See CBS, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 12, 19; see also Code. Civ. Proc., § 2017.010 [scope of discovery].)

2. Analysis

The Court finds in favor of Union Mutualista.

Union Mutualista provides proof of service of RPDs, Set One, and RFAs, Set One, on Rafael Davila Madrigal, and proof of service of FROGs, Set One, and RFAs, Set One, on Alicia Brisila Davila, with service occurring on February 12, 2024. (All Motions, Schiller Decl., Exs. 1 [discovery and proof of service], 2 [printout of USPS delivery confirmation].)

Union Mutualista provides evidence that the deadline for initial responses has passed—which, by the Court’s estimate, occurred Friday, March 15, 2024, 30 days after service by mail, plus two court days for service by certified mail. (All Motions, Schiller Decl., ¶¶ 2-5.)

Union Mutualista provides evidence that Plaintiffs never responded to the discovery that Union Mutualista served on them on February 12, 2022. (All Motions, Schiller Decl., ¶ 6.)

Last, a brief review shows that the first set of discovery contains requests within the scope of discovery for this action because the interrogatories, production requests, and admission requests at issue involve issues germane to the claims before the Court.

Union Mutualista’s motions are thus GRANTED as to (1) compelling initial responses to RPDs, Set One, from Rafael Davila Madrigal, (2) compelling initial responses to FROGs, Set One, from Alicia Brisila Davila, and (3)-(4) deeming the truth of the matters set forth in RFAs, Set One, as admitted against Rafael Davila Madrigal and Alicia Brisila Davila.

B. Requests for Sanctions: GRANTED.

1. Legal Standard

The Court must impose monetary sanctions against anyone—party, nonparty, or attorney—who unsuccessfully makes or opposes the motion. As relates to interrogatories and production requests, the Court can deny sanctions if it finds that the person to be sanctioned acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of the sanctions unjust, a standard not applicable to admission requests. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (c) [interrogatories], 2031.300, subd. (c) [demand to produce], 2033.280, subd. (c); see Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at p. 404 [interrogatories and demand to produce]; Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636 [admission request sanctions are mandatory; no discretion].)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

2. Analysis

The Court finds in favor of Union Mutualista.

The failure to respond to authorized methods of discovery is a sanctionable discovery abuse. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010, subd. (d), 2023.030, subd. (a).)

Here, such an abuse has occurred through Plaintiffs’ non-response to FROGs, Set One, RPDs, Set One, and/or RFAs, Set One.

These abuses justify an award of sanctions equal to the amounts requested by Union Mutualista, which seeks compensation for the reasonable time spent on these motions by counsel.

Sanctions are thus GRANTED in the amount of (1) $1,060 in relation to FROGs, Set One, as against Alicia Brisila Davila, (2) $1,110 in relation to RPDs, Set One, as against Rafael Davila Madrigal, (3) $810 in relation to RFAs, Set One, as against Alicia Brisila Davila, and (4) $1,260 in relation to RFAs, Set One, as against Rafael Davila Madrigal.
