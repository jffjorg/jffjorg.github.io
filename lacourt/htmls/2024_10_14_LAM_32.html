DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV05811    Hearing Date: October 14, 2024    Dept: 32

 

BFF CONSTRUCTION, INC., et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

R&M BRO’S CONSTRUCTION INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV05811

  Hearing Date:  October 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

r&m’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production (CRS# 9362)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 15, 2023, Plaintiffs BFF Construction, Inc. (BFF) and RB Realty Management, LLC (RB) filed this action against Defendants R&M Bro’s Construction Inc. (R&M) and Miguel Duarte Olmos (Duarte). Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint on March 5, 2024, alleging (1) breach of contract, (2) implied indemnity, (3) negligence, (4) conversion, (5) fraud, (6) money had and received, (7) breach of contract, (8) conversion, and (9) money had and received.  

            The case involves a construction project that took place in Ventura County. The property is owned by RB. BFF was the general contractor on the project, and R&M was the subcontractor. Duarte owns and operates R&M. The complaint alleges that R&M performed its work negligently, causing multiple shutdowns and delays. BFF terminated R&M from the project, and Duarte allegedly retaliated by vandalizing the property and destroying or stealing certain materials. R&M also allegedly absconded with money meant for paying BFF’s vendors. R&M and Duarte have also cross-complained against BFF and RB, seeking damages for unpaid work and alleged physical injuries.

            On September 13, 2024, R&M filed the instant motion to compel BFF’s further responses to requests for production. BFF filed its opposition on October 1, 2024. R&M filed its reply on October 7, 2024.    

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).) The party seeking production of documents bears the initial burden of showing good cause through a fact-specific showing of relevance. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) Once this showing is made, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections. (Ibid.)

DISCUSSION

            A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Id., § 2016.040.)

            R&M’s evidence in support of the motion does not demonstrate sufficient meet and confer regarding the particular RFPs at issue. (See Tiwana Decl.) R&M’s email merely concluded that “RFP Request Nos. 2 through 65 are deficient to the extent that most of those requests have been ignored.” (Id., Ex. E.) Broadly concluding that nearly all of the RFP responses are deficient does not constitute a good faith attempt to address “each issue” in the motion. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)

            R&M’s reply declaration contains no additional evidence of meet and confer on the RFPs beyond the same broad conclusions reflected in the original declaration. (See Tiwana Reply Decl., Ex. C [“the responses to RFP Request Nos. 2 through 65 are deficient to the extent that most of those requests have been ignored”]; Ex. D [“Request Nos. 3 thru 65 have been evaded in spite of extensive meet and confer”].) 

            Given the volume of requests at issue, the Court is not inclined to compel further responses without a showing that the parties met and conferred on the specific issues raised in the motion. The lack of adequate meet and confer is evident in R&M’s own separate statement, which shows that BFF properly responded to many of the RFPs by stating that it will produce all responsive non-privileged documents in its possession. Clearly, BFF did not ignore or evade most of the RFPs, as claimed in R&M’s correspondence. A proper meet and confer could have narrowed the actual issues in contention.   

Some of R&M’s arguments pertain to the completeness of BFF’s production. However, BFF’s production is not at issue in this motion, as this is a motion to compel further responses. In any case, R&M’s skepticism as to the completeness of the production is not a reason to compel a further response or production.

CONCLUSION

            R&M’s motion to compel further responses 


 

BFF CONSTRUCTION, INC., et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

R&M BRO’S CONSTRUCTION INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV05811

  Hearing Date:  October 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

BFF’s motions to compel further responses to form interrogatories (CRS# 3501, 4869)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 15, 2023, Plaintiffs BFF Construction, Inc. (BFF) and RB Realty Management, LLC (RB) filed this action against Defendants R&M Bro’s Construction Inc. (R&M) and Miguel Duarte Olmos (Duarte). Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint on March 5, 2024, alleging (1) breach of contract, (2) implied indemnity, (3) negligence, (4) conversion, (5) fraud, (6) money had and received, (7) breach of contract, (8) conversion, and (9) money had and received.  

            The case involves a construction project that took place in Ventura County. The property is owned by RB. BFF was the general contractor on the project, and R&M was the subcontractor. Duarte owns and operates R&M. The complaint alleges that R&M performed its work negligently, causing multiple shutdowns and delays. BFF terminated R&M from the project, and Duarte allegedly retaliated by vandalizing the property and destroying or stealing certain materials. R&M also allegedly absconded with money meant for paying BFF’s vendors. R&M and Duarte have also cross-complained against BFF and RB, seeking damages for unpaid work and alleged physical injuries.

            On September 13, 2024, BFF filed the instant two motions to compel R&M’s and Duarte’s further responses to form interrogatories. R&M and Duarte filed their joint opposition on October 1, 2024. BFF filed its reply on October 7, 2024.   

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).) The party seeking production of documents bears the initial burden of showing good cause through a fact-specific showing of relevance. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) Once this showing is made, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections. (Ibid.)

MEET AND CONFER

            A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that BFF has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Ardalan Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. FROG No. 8.7

            FROG No. 8.7 asks R&M and Duarte to state the total income lost as a result of the incident, and to show how that amount was calculated. “Incident” is defined as the circumstances and events surrounding R&M’s work on the subject properties. Specifically, Duarte alleges that he suffered injuries after falling down a 25-foot hole on the construction site.

            R&M’s and Duarte’s responses state that Duarte has been unable to perform a significant portion of his job since the injury and has been forced to forgo certain opportunities and projects. The responses claim that the calculation is subject to expert analysis and that discovery is ongoing. This is a sufficient response to this particular FROG. BFF may propound additional discovery if it seeks further information.   

            The motions are DENIED as to FROG No. 8.7.    

II. FROG No. 15.1

            FROG No. 15.1 asks R&M and Duarte to identify facts, witnesses, and documents supporting each denial of a material allegation and each affirmative defense. Duarte’s response contained only objections, and R&M’s response contained objections and a narrative summary of its theory of the case. Neither R&M nor Duarte identified each material denial or affirmative defense, or provided the requisite information on each. This renders the responses defective. 

            A party “is entitled to demand answers to its interrogatories, as a matter of right . . . [and] the burden of justifying any objection and failure to respond remains at all times with the party resisting an interrogatory.” (Williams v. Sup. Ct. (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) R&M and Duarte do not attempt to justify the objections in their responses. Their only argument is that BFF did not properly respond to FROG No. 15.1 either. However, the sufficiency of BFF’s discovery responses has no bearing on R&M’s and Duarte’s obligations.

R&M and Duarte argue that they provided more information in response to this FROG than BFF did in its response to the same FROG. But that does not mean R&M and Duarte provided responsive information. As stated above, FROG No. 15.1 asks for discrete information on each material denial and affirmative defense. It does not ask for “a 900+ word factual summary of their case.” (See Opp. 6:9-10.) The fact that R&M and Duarte have produced 3,892 pages of documents has no bearing on whether their response to FROG No. 15.1 is sufficient.

The motions are GRANTED as to FROG No. 15.1.

CONCLUSION

            BFF’s motions to compel further response to form interrogatories are GRANTED in part as set forth above. Sanctions are denied. 


 

BFF CONSTRUCTION, INC., et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            v.

 

R&M BRO’S CONSTRUCTION INC., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV05811

  Hearing Date:  October 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

r&m’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories (CRS# 0687)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 15, 2023, Plaintiffs BFF Construction, Inc. (BFF) and RB Realty Management, LLC (RB) filed this action against Defendants R&M Bro’s Construction Inc. (R&M) and Miguel Duarte Olmos (Duarte). Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint on March 5, 2024, alleging (1) breach of contract, (2) implied indemnity, (3) negligence, (4) conversion, (5) fraud, (6) money had and received, (7) breach of contract, (8) conversion, and (9) money had and received.  

            The case involves a construction project that took place in Ventura County. The property is owned by RB. BFF was the general contractor on the project, and R&M was the subcontractor. Duarte owns and operates R&M. The complaint alleges that R&M performed its work negligently, causing multiple shutdowns and delays. BFF terminated R&M from the project, and Duarte allegedly retaliated by vandalizing the property and destroying or stealing certain materials. R&M also allegedly absconded with money meant for paying BFF’s vendors. R&M and Duarte have also cross-complained against BFF and RB, seeking damages for unpaid work and alleged physical injuries.

            On September 13, 2024, R&M filed the instant motion to compel BFF’s further responses to form interrogatories. BFF filed its opposition on October 1, 2024. R&M filed its reply on October 7, 2024.   

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).) The party seeking production of documents bears the initial burden of showing good cause through a fact-specific showing of relevance. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) Once this showing is made, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections. (Ibid.)

MEET AND CONFER

            A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that R&M has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Tiwana Decl. ¶¶ 5-7; Ardalan Decl., Ex. B.)

DISCUSSION

I. FROG No. 305

            FROG Nos. 305.12 and 305.13 ask about BFF’s repairs of any “construction claim” or “construction defect claim” that BFF alleges existed. During meet and confer, the parties agreed on narrowed definitions for “construction claim” and “construction defect claim” and also agreed that BFF would answer the FROGs despite not owning the subject property. (Tiwana Decl. ISO MTC RFPs, Ex. B.)  

BFF’s responses merely reiterate its allegation that R&M absconded with materials and left BFF to hire other contractors to complete the work. This does not provide all of the information requested in the FROGs, which have discrete subparts seeking specific information.

BFF also responded that it would produce documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230. However, BFF has not made the requisite showing to invoke section 2030.230, i.e., that answering the FROGs would require “the making of a compilation, abstract, audit, or summary of” documents, and the burden of doing so is the same for both parties. Reference to external documents is not warranted merely because the answer to an interrogatory may be found within. If BFF can answer the interrogatories, it must simply do so. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.220(a) [“Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits”].)

The motion is GRANTED as to FROG Nos. 305.12 and 305.13.   

II. FROG No. 311

            FROG Nos. 311.2, 311.3, and 311.4 ask about diagrams, reproductions, models, reports, and inspections concerning any “construction claim” or “construction defect claim.” In response to FROG Nos. 311.2 and 311.3, BFF denied the existence of responsive documents. In response to FROG No. 311.4, BFF stated that Bahram Fata (BFF’s principal) “inspected the both property sites on a regular, and ongoing basis, including on or about January 13, 2023.”

            BFF’s answers are responsive. R&M mainly takes issue with BFF’s objections. However, BFF is entitled to assert objections to preserve them while providing responsive information. R&M’s skepticism about BFF’s claim that it has no responsive documents is not a reason to compel a further response.

            The motion is DENIED as to FROG Nos. 311.2, 311.3, and 311.4.

 

 

III. FROG No. 313

            FROG No. 313.1 asks BFF to describe each “construction claim” or “construction defect claim” that was concealed from it. FROG No. 313.2 asks BFF to describe each concealment or misrepresentation made to it in connection with the provision of services or supplies to the subject property.

            BFF objected to the requests and did not respond. In its opposition, BFF argues that the FROGs are either unrelated to its fraud claim or unrelated to the subject property. This is an overly narrow conception of relevance. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) “Construction claims” and “construction defect claims,” as defined by the parties in meet and confer, are relevant to the issues in the complaint and cross-complaint. If BFF contends that information on a “construction claim” or “construction defect claim” was concealed from it, then it must answer FROG No. 313.1. R&M’s alleged fraud about being a subcontractor on the project is sufficiently related to the subject property. Thus, BFF must detail that alleged fraud in response to FROG No. 313.2.

            The motion is GRANTED as to FROG Nos. 313.1 and 313.2. 

IV. FROG No. 314

            FROG No. 314.7 asks BFF if it contracted out any of the work it was to perform on the subject property to another person or entity, and to provide certain information if so. BFF objected and did not respond.

            BFF argues that the interrogatory is harassing, burdensome, and irrelevant because there were two construction projects on the subject property spanning years. BFF also argues that it is vague whether subcontracted work is responsive to the request because the definition of “you” is broad.

These arguments are without merit. BFF fails to demonstrate how providing the discrete information requested by the FROG would be unduly burdensome just because there were two construction projects. BFF also declares the information to be irrelevant without explanation. Whether BFF contracted out the work on the property is relevant to the claims being asserted in this action, which arise from work on the property. Lastly, the request is not vague. It is a standard form interrogatory drafted by the Judicial Council. The definition of “you” is clear, and “contracted out” clearly includes subcontracting.

The motion is GRANTED as to FROG No. 314.7.

V. FROG No. 321

            FROG Nos. 321.1 through 321.11 ask various questions about BFF’s work on the subject property. The interrogatories are related to the subject matter of the litigation or are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)

            The motion is GRANTED as to FROG Nos. 321.1 through 321.11.

CONCLUSION

            R&M’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories is GRANTED in part as set forth above. Sanctions are denied.

 


Case Number: 23STCV27862    Hearing Date: October 14, 2024    Dept: 32

 

AMERICA SANCHEZ,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

LOCKHEED MARTIN, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV27862

  Hearing Date:  October 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

plaintiff’s motion for leave to file first amended complaint

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On November 14, 2023, Plaintiff America Sanchez filed this action against Defendants Lockheed Martin and Henry Roman, asserting causes of action for (1) sex discrimination, (2) sexual harassment, (3) retaliation, (4) failure to prevent, (5) negligent hiring, (6) violation of Labor Code section 1102.5, and (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

            On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for leave to file a first amended complaint. Defendant Lockheed Martin filed its opposition on October 1, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

“The plaintiff and defendant, respectively, may be allowed, on motion, to make a supplemental complaint or answer, alleging facts material to the case occurring after the former complaint or answer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 464(a).) “A ‘supplemental’ pleading is used to allege facts occurring after the original pleading was filed.” (Foster v. Sexton (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 998, 1032.) “In contrast, the additional allegations in an ‘amended’ pleading address matters that had occurred before the original pleading was filed.” (Ibid.) The facts in a supplemental complaint must be “material to the case,” meaning “in furtherance and consistent with the original action.” (Gonzales v. Arbelbide (1957) 155 Cal.App.2d 721, 727.) “[C]ourts should exercise liberality in permitting the filing of supplemental pleadings when the alleged ‘occurring-after’ facts are pertinent to the case.” (Flood v. Simpson (1975) 45 Cal.App.3d 644, 647.)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff’s proposed FAC adds causes of action based on: (i) disability discrimination; (ii) failure to accommodate; (iii) failure to engage; and (iv) retaliation. While these new claims are based on facts occurring after Plaintiff filed the original complaint, they are “material to the case” and “consistent with the original action.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 464(a); Gonzales, supra, 155 Cal.App.2d at p. 727.) The new claims arise from further discrimination occurring within the course of the same employment. Plaintiff also alleges retaliation in the proposed FAC, which relates to both the earlier claims and the new ones, i.e., Plaintiff was retaliated against for reporting the earlier sexual harassment and later disability discrimination.

            While Plaintiff could file a separate action, it would inevitably be related and likely consolidated, as the motive for Plaintiff's termination is related to the allegations in the original complaint. Thus, it is more efficient to allow Plaintiff to file an amended complaint with the new allegations.  The court has discretion to permit amendments that set forth new claims arising after commencement of the action. (Hong v. Financial Corporation of America (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 960, 966 [Employee defamation claim against employer arose after harassment action was filed.)

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file first amended complaint is GRANTED.  Amended Complaint to be filed within 5 days of this order.

JOHN HENSLEY,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

EDWARD JOSEPH RACHAL, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  24STCV01480

  Hearing Date:  October 14, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

motion to consolidate

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff John Hensley filed this action against Edward Joseph Rachal (Rachal) and numerous other defendants. The complaint asserts causes of action for (1) negligence, (2) negligent hiring, (3) vicarious liability, (4) premises liability, and (5) negligent undertaking.

            The complaint stems from injuries Plaintiff suffered after being shot by Rachal, a security guard working near a parking garage located at 832 South Francisco Street, Los Angeles. Besides Rachal, the defendants consist of individuals and entities that allegedly hired Rachal, employed security at the location of the incident, or owned or operated the parking garage.

            Prior to this action, on September 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed another action titled John Hensley v. Edward Joseph Rachal, et al. (22STCV31707). That complaint contained causes of action for (1) negligence, (2) negligent hiring, (3) assault, (4) battery, (5) negligent infliction of emotional distress, (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (7) punitive damages. The complaint arose from the same incident, i.e., Rachal shooting Plaintiff.

            On September 17, 2024, Defendant L&R Auto Parks, Inc. (erroneously sued as Joe’s Auto Parks 212 E. 7th – Spoleto, LLC) filed the instant motion to consolidate the two actions. Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition on October 1, 2024.

LEGAL STANDARD

While a notice of motion to consolidate must “[b]e filed in each case sought to be consolidated,” the “memorandums, declarations, and other supporting papers must be filed only in the lowest numbered case.” (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.350(a)(1)(C), (a)(2)(A).) In other words, the motion itself must be heard in the earlier case. Furthermore, “[u]nless otherwise provided in the order granting the motion to consolidate, the lowest numbered case in the consolidated case is the lead case.” (Id., Rule 3.350(b).)   

DISCUSSION

            This Court cannot hear the merits of the motion to consolidate because the motion must be heard in the lowest numbered case, 22STCV31707. (See Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 3.350(a)(2)(A).)

CONCLUSION

            Defendant L&R Auto Parks, Inc.’s motion to consolidate is DENIED.
