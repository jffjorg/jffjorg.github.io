DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV37953    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Thursday, October 17, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   22STCV37953

CASE NAME:                        Jacob Shaw v. Honda Performance Development, Inc., et al.

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendants Experis US, LLC and Manpower Group US, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Jacob Shaw

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Demurrer with Motion to Strike the Fifth Amended Complaint

OPPOSITION:                        12 September 2024

REPLY:                                  18 September 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Defendants’ demurrer to the ninth cause of action for wrongful termination in violation of public policy is sustained with 10 days leave to amend. Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages is granted without leave to amend. The court sets a Non-Appearance OSC RE: Amended Complaint for November 6, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

                                                Moving parties to give notice.

                                                                                   

 

Background

 

On December 5, 2022, Jacob Shaw (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Honda Performance Development, Inc.; Experis US, Inc.; Experis US, LLC; Manpower Group US, Inc. (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1 to 50.  On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint.  

 

On July 7, 2023, the parties stipulated to allow Plaintiff to file the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). 

 

On October 4, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to file a Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”). The TAC alleges fifteen causes of action for: (1) disability discrimination, (2) failure to engage in the interactive process, (3) failure to accommodate disability, (4) retaliation in violation of Gov. Code § 12940(m)(2), (5) violation of the California Family Rights Act, (6) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment, (7) violation of Labor Code section 233 and 234, (8) wrongful termination in violation of public policy; (9) failure to pay overtime wages; (10) failure to provide meal periods, (11) failure to authorize and permit rest periods, (12) failure to pay all wages due upon separation, (13) failure to furnish accurate itemized wage statements, (14) failure to maintain required records, and (15) unfair and unlawful business practices. On November 15, 2023, the demurrer with motion to strike the first to seventh and ninth through fifteenth cause of action of the TAC was sustained with leave to amend.

 

On December 15, 2023, Plaintiff filed the Fourth Amended Complaint (“4AC”) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the TAC. Defendants demurred solely to Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for wrongful termination in violation of statute and public policy and moved to strike the request for punitive damages. On April 30, 2024, the court sustained the demurrer and granted the motion to strike.

 

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Fifth Amended Complaint (5AC) alleging the same fifteen causes of action as the 4AC.

 

On July 26, 2024, Defendants Experis US, LLC and Manpower Group, Inc. filed a demurrer to the ninth cause of action for wrongful termination in violation of public policy with a motion to strike the 5AC’s punitive damages. Plaintiff opposed the motion and Defendants Experis US, LLC and ManpowerGroup, Inc. submitted a reply. The matter is now before the court.

 

discussion

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

A.        Demurrer 

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (CCP, § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿“To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.”¿(C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)¿For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded.¿ (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.)¿A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.”¿(Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) The complaint does not need to be a model pleading, as the policy of the law is to construe pleadings liberally so that “cases will be tried on their merits rather than disposed of on technicalities of pleadings.” (Porten v. University of San Francisco (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 825.)

 

B.        Motion to Strike 

 

¿Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (CCP, § 435(b)(1); CRC, rule 3.1322(b).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP, § 436(a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)¿¿¿¿ 

 

C.        Leave to Amend 

 

“Where the defect raised by a motion to strike or by demurrer is reasonably capable of cure, leave to amend is routinely and liberally granted to give the plaintiff a chance to cure the defect in question.” (CLD Construction, Inc. v. City of San Ramon (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1146.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) “It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if the plaintiff shows there is a reasonable possibility any defect identified by the defendant can be cured by amendment.” (Ross v. Creel Printing & Publ'g Co. (2002) 100 Cal. App. 4th 736, 748.)

 

II.        Demurrer[1]

 

A.        Ninth Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination in Violation of Statute and Public Policy

 

“The elements of a claim for wrongful discharge in violation of public policy are (1) an employer-employee relationship, (2) the employer terminated the plaintiff’s employment, (3) the termination was substantially motivated by a violation of public policy, and (4) the discharge caused the plaintiff harm.” (Yau v. Allen (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 144, 154.) To support a tortious discharge claim, the policy must be “supported by either constitutional or statutory provisions. Second, the policy must be ‘public’ in the sense that it ‘inures to the benefit of the public’ rather than serving merely the interests of the individual. Third, the policy must have been articulated at the time of the discharge. Fourth, the policy must be ‘fundamental’ and ‘substantial.’” (Stevenson v. Superior Court (1997) 16 Cal.4th 880, 889–890 [footnote omitted].)

 

In addition to wrongful termination, Plaintiff pleads that “Defendants…and/or constructively wrongfully terminated Plaintiff’s employment.” (5AC ¶ 157.) A constructive discharge occurs when there is (1) an employer-employee relationship, (2) the employee is subjected to working conditions that violate public policy, (3) the employer intentionally created or knowingly

permitted these working conditions, (4) the conditions were so intolerable that a reasonable person in the employee’s position would have no reasonable alternative except to resign, (5) The employee did resign because of the conditions, (6) the employee was harmed, and (7) the working conditions were a substantial factor in causing the employee’s harm. (CACI No. 2432; See also Turner v. Anheuser-Busch, Inc. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1238, 1245.)

 

First, Defendants are correct in that Plaintiff cannot claim wrongful termination in violation of public policy for his employment contract not being renewed. “Decisional law does not allow a plaintiff to sue for wrongful termination in violation of public policy based upon an employer's refusal to renew an employment contract.” (Touchstone Television Prods. v. Super. Ct. (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 676, 680.) The 5AC alleges that Plaintiff was a contract employee with Defendants Honda, Experis and Manpower, as they were his joint and/or single employers. (5AC, ¶¶ 8, 19, 24, 25.) In or about June 2022, Plaintiff was informed that his contract would not be renewed for the coming year. (Id., ¶ 46.) Much like the decision in Touchstone to not renew the plaintiff’s contract potentially being motivated by complaints about working conditions, here Plaintiff’s contract not being renewed potentially because of his inability to travel does not change the principle that refusing to renew a contract set to expire is not actionable in tort. (Touchstone Television Prods., at p. 682; 5AC, ¶¶ 33, 34.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff redirects the court’s attention to the constructive wrongful termination claim also housed in the ninth cause of action. The 5AC alleges that following the decision to not renew Plaintiff’s contract with Defendant Honda, Defendants Manpower and Experis continued to employ him but chose not to place him in another position even though he believes he was eligible. (5AC, ¶ 51.) As a result, Plaintiff was put “in a position where he would earn no income indefinitely.” (Id. ¶ 52.) Plaintiff reasons that this position of earning no income indefinitely is tantamount to a long unpaid suspension that is so intolerable it forced him to resign. (Opposition, at p. 5:22-24.)

 

In reply, Defendants suggest that since Plaintiff alleged in prior complaints that “Defendants” refers to all named defendants, including Honda, Manpower, and Experis, it is contradictory to now allege that “Defendants” terminated his employment, but he remained employed by Manpower. (Reply, at p. 2:10-22.) The court finds the argument unpersuasive as the 4AC did allege that it was Defendant Honda who decided not to renew Plaintiff’s contract with Defendant Honda while also alleging that “Plaintiff continued to be an employee of Defendants Manpower and Experis…” (4AC, ¶ 43.) Here, the 5AC similarly states: “After Defendants’ wrongful refusal to renew Plaintiff’s contract with Defendant Honda, Plaintiff continued to be an employee of Defendants Manpower and Experis and believed he was eligible to be placed with other clients of Defendants Manpower and Experis.” (5AC, ¶ 51.) Accordingly, Defendants position that there is a contradiction in the pleadings is unsupported and not evidence of a sham pleading.

 

Defendants also argue that Plaintiff analogizing his situation to an unpaid suspension is insufficient because deprivation of pay generally doesn’t support a constructive discharge claim. Defendants cite Vazquez v. Franklin Mgmt. Real Est. Fund, Inc., which concluded that a driver not being properly reimbursed by his employer for milage expenses that reduced his pay to below minimum wage pled sufficient facts to support intolerable working conditions. (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 828-829.) Here, the 5AC alleges Plaintiff continued to be employed by Manpower, and received no pay as Manpower did nothing to place him in a new position. (5AC, ¶¶ 51, 52.) No pay at all is far less tolerable than sub-standard pay, therefore the intolerable working conditions element has been properly pled. Moreover, the 5AC alleges that Plaintiff endured discrimination and retaliation due to his disability, allegations that are sufficient to find that working conditions were intolerable. (5AC, ¶¶ 41, 52, 60, 99, 159.)

Finally, the 5AC alleges that Plaintiff was “forced to seek other employment options.” (5AC, ¶ 52.) “Constructive discharge occurs when the employer's conduct effectively forces an employee to resign.” (Turner v. Anheuser-Busch, Inc. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1238, 1244.) The employee resigning is a key element to the claim, which has not been pled on the face of the 5AC. The 5AC fails to specify that seeking other employment options, Plaintiff did resign from working with Defendants. Therefore, taking the allegations in the 5AC as true, Plaintiff has sufficiently pled all but the resignation element of his constructive discharge claim.

 

III.      Motion to Strike

 

Defendants maintain that Plaintiff fails to plead sufficient facts to support a claim for punitive damages against Defendants. Accordingly, Defendants move to strike the following portions of the 5AC:

 

  • Paragraphs 66-67, 79-80, 93-94, 104-105, 127-128, 136-137, 152-153, 163-164;
  • Page 39, Prayer for Relief, Paragraph 5: “For punitive … damages where available by law;”

 

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civ. Code § 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.)¿ The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Ibid.)¿¿“Malice” is defined in Civ. Code, § 3294 (c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” (Civ. Code §, 3294(c)(2).) The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 847, 891.) Fraud means “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(3). 

 

When the defendant is a¿corporation, “the oppression, fraud, or malice must be perpetrated, authorized, or knowingly ratified by an officer, director, or managing agent of the¿corporation.” (Wilson v. Southern California Edison Company (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 123, 164; see Civ. Code, § 3294(b).) Furthermore, a managing agent includes “only those corporate employees who exercise substantial independent authority and judgment in their corporate decision making so that their decisions ultimately determine corporate policy.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 564, 566-667 (White).) Here, Plaintiff’s 5AC alleges that his supervisors were “officers, directors, or managing agents of Defendants who were acting on behalf of Defendants. (5AC ¶¶ 67, 80, 94, 105, 118, 128, 137, 153, 164.) The Manpower employees the 5AC identifies are Business Development Manager Grey Warren, Consultant Services Manager Sarah Hughes, and Vice President of Sales and Operations Anthony Del Preto. (5AC ¶¶ 25, 27, 36, 46.) However, Plaintiff does not specifically allege facts showing that these employees exercised substantial independent authority and judgment in their corporate decision-making so that their decisions ultimately determine corporate policy.

 

The 5AC does not allege that Anthony Del Petro, by virtue of being the Vice President of Sale and Operations, was a managing agent of Defendants who “exercise substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.” (White, supra, 21 Cal.4th at p. 577.) “In order to demonstrate that an employee is a true managing agent under section 3294, subdivision (b), a plaintiff seeking punitive damages would have to show that the employee exercised substantial discretionary authority over significant aspects of a corporation's business.” (Id., at p. 577.) Here, the 5AC must specifically allege that Del Petro exercised substantial discretionary authority. Moreover, the 5AC fails to state what prompt corrective measures Del Petro could have taken as Vice President of Sales and Operations. The 5AC also fails to allege that Del Petro acted with malice, fraud, or oppression in failing to take non-specified corrective measures. “ ‘Rather, the critical inquiry is the degree of discretion the employees possess in making decisions that will ultimately determine corporate policy.’ ” (Kelly-Zurian v. Wohl Shoe Co. (1994) 22 Cal.App.4th 397, 421.)

 

The 5AC merely states that Ms. Hughes was Plaintiff’s contact at Manpower who had authority to sign contracts related to Plaintiff’s employment. (5AC ¶ 25.) The 5AC does not state that she had the discretionary authority to determine corporate policy, thus making her a managing agent of Defendants. Even if Hughes was a managing agent, her alleged conduct is insufficient to show that she acted with malice, oppression, or fraud. Similarly, Plaintiff fails to allege that Greg Warren, as the Business Development Manager had discretionary authority to determine corporate policy.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Manpower/Experis, including Hughes, Warren, and Del Preto, did nothing to stop Honda’s illegal conduct in spite of their obligation to do so, and failed to take prompt corrective measures within their control. (5AC, ¶ 36.) However, Plaintiff alleges nothing to indicate that Hughes and Del Preto had the legal authority to stop or remedy Honda’s conduct or force Honda to renew Plaintiff’s contract. Additionally, Warren’s failure to place Plaintiff with a new Manpower client does not sufficiently indicate a willful and conscious disregard of Plaintiff’s rights or cruel and unjust hardship.

 

Plaintiff has now had five opportunities to amend his pleadings, and the court has granted Defendants’ request to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages on November 15, 2023, April 30, 2024, and June 18, 2024. Given that Plaintiff has had four opportunities to amend the pleadings, the court is not persuaded that Plaintiff can successfully amend the pleadings to allege a claim of punitive damages. Therefore, the Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages is granted without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the ninth cause of action for wrongful termination in violation of public

policy is sustained with 10 days leave to amend. Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages

 is granted without leave to amend. The court sets a Non-Appearance OSC RE: Amended

Complaint for November 6, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. Moving parties to give notice.
