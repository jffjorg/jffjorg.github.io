DEPARTMENT 53 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 18STCV08570    Hearing Date: April 22, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

anthony aiello , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

sommer ray beaty , et al.;

 

Defendants.

18STCV08570

 

 

Hearing Date:

April 22, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion to compel

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Sommer Ray Beaty

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Anthony Aiello

Motion to Compel

The court considered the moving and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

The court did not consider the opposition papers filed and served by plaintiff Anthony Aiello on April 10, 2024, because the opposition was not (1) filed with the court at least nine court days before the hearing on this motion (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b)), and (2) served on defendant Sommer Ray Beaty at least nine court days plus two court days for electronic service before the hearing on this motion (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1005, subd. (b), 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(B)).

DISCUSSION

Defendant Sommer Ray Beaty (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order compelling plaintiff Anthony Aiello (“Plaintiff”) to produce documents responsive to her Requests for Production in Defendant’s Amended Notice of Taking Deposition of Plaintiff, which was served on Plaintiff on March 25, 2021.  (Selik Decl., Ex. A-1.)

First, the court finds that Defendant did not submit a separate statement as required.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(5) [“The motions that require a separate statement include a motion: [¶¶] (5) To compel or to quash the production of documents or tangible things at a deposition”].)  Here, although Defendant states in her motion that Plaintiff testified that several categories responsive to the document demands were in his possession and would be forthcoming, Defendant did not identify the categories of documents which Plaintiff testified that he had possession of and instead referred the court to separate documents in support of that assertion.  (Mot., pp. 4:8-11, 7:8-14.)  However, Defendant was required to file a separate statement that  (1) sets forth this information and (2) is “full and complete so that no person is required to review any other document in order to determine the full request and the full response.”  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1345, subd. (c).)   

Second, the court finds that Defendant has not met her burden to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production” of the documents requested by failing to identify the document demands as to which Plaintiff testified that he had possession of, but did not produce, responsive documents.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).)  

Thus, because Defendant (1) did not comply with the separate statement requirement, and (2) did not show good cause justifying production, the court denies Defendant’s motion.  (Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893 [court has discretion to deny motion that does not comply with separate statement requirement]; Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(1).)

ORDER

            The court denies defendant Sommer Ray Beaty’s motion to compel.          

The court orders plaintiff Anthony Aiello to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

DATED:  April 22, 2024

Robert B. Broadbelt III

Judge of the Superior Court

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Tina Vo (“Vo”) and Cathy Kay (“Kay”) filed the operative Second Amended Complaint in this action on February 5, 2024, alleging 10 causes of action against defendants Cristina Marie Dam (“Dam”), Liberate Hollywood, Inc. (“Liberate”), and Liberation Inc.

On July 17, 2023, Dam and Liberate (collectively, “Cross-Complainants”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Vo and Kay (collectively, “Cross-Defendants”), alleging seven causes of action against them for (1) breach of fiduciary duty (direct); (2) breach of fiduciary duty (derivative); (3) aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duty; (4) fraud in the inducement – intentional misrepresentation; (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (6) negligent misrepresentation; and (7) negligence.

Cross-Defendants now move the court for an order (1) sustaining their demurrer to each cause of action alleged in the Cross-Complaint, and (2) striking from the Cross-Complaint various allegations and the requests for punitive damages and attorney’s fees.

DEMURRER

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty (direct) because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Dam has sufficiently pleaded the existence of a fiduciary relationship between the parties.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).)

“A fiduciary relationship is any relation existing between parties to a transaction wherein one of the parties is in duty bound to act with the utmost good faith for the benefit of the other party.”  (Hodges v. County of Placer (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 537, 546 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)  “‘Traditional examples of fiduciary relationships in the commercial context include trustee/beneficiary, directors and majority shareholders of a corporation, business partners, joint adventurers, and agent/principals.  [Citations.]’  [Citation.]”  (Id. at p. 547.)  Cross-Complainants have alleged that (1) cross-defendant Vo convinced cross-complainant Dam to provide 10 percent in equity to each of the Cross-Defendants in exchange for a $250,000 capital investment (Cross-Compl., ¶ 29); (2) pursuant to their agreement, Kay became an officer of Liberate and took a part in the management of the business on behalf of Cross-Defendants (Cross-Compl., ¶ 30); (3) Kay began to take over much of the control over Liberate from Dam (Cross-Compl., ¶ 32); (4) Cross-Defendants became officers of Liberate when they requested management control over the business; and (5) Cross-Defendants were “insiders and officers with management duties and control over” Liberate (Cross-Compl., ¶ 67).  The court finds that these allegations are sufficient to allege the existence of a fiduciary relationship between Dam and Cross-Defendants as business partners.  (Hodges, supra, 41 Cal.App.5th at p. 547.)

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty (derivative) because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since (1) Cross-Complainants have alleged the existence of a fiduciary relationship between the parties for the same reasons as set forth above (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 29-30, 32, 75), (2) Cross-Complainants have alleged more than “[t]he mere absence of an asset” in support of this cause of action, and (3) this cause of action is not ambiguous or unintelligible and therefore is not uncertain.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e), (f); Hodges, supra, 41 Cal.App.5th at p. 547; Demurrer, p. 8:14-15.)

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action for aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duty because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainants have alleged facts establishing that Cross-Defendants assisted or encouraged the other in breaching her fiduciary duties to Cross-Complainants by alleging that (1) Cross-Defendants demanded that dividend payments be made to Kay (Cross-Compl., ¶ 32), showing knowledge and assistance in the breach of fiduciary duty based on their improper demand for payments that no other shareholders received, and (2) Cross-Defendants knew that Liberate was not operating as planned when they withdrew capital from the business and when they demanded Dam write a check for $250,000 (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 40-41), showing knowledge and assistance in the breach of fiduciary duty based on their improper demand for the investment reimbursement (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 83, 86-87).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e), (f); Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 83 [alleging breaches of fiduciary duty to be their demands for dividends payments and for investment reimbursement], 86 [alleging early demand for dividend payments to be sabotage], 88 [alleging demand for payments that no other shareholder received to be self-dealing and breach of fiduciary duty]; Nasrawi v. Buck Consultants LLC (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 328, 343 [elements of a claim for aiding and abetting breach of fiduciary duty].)

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action for fraud in the inducement – intentional misrepresentation because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainants have alleged the facts supporting this cause of action with sufficient particularity[1] by alleging (1) who made the subject representations (i.e., Cross-Defendants) (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 96, 100), (2) to whom they made the representations (i.e., Dam), and (3) the timeframe during which the representations were made, i.e., (i) during the relationship between Kay and Dam (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 25-27, 36), which is alleged to have commenced in July 2017 (Cross-Compl., ¶ 20) and ended in September 2017 (Cross-Compl., ¶ 37), and (ii) the day after Kay and Dam ended their relationship in September 2017 (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 37-38, 47).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 96 [alleging representations to be that Kay and Vo would have a family together and that Vo would contribute to the business for the business’s success], 98, 100 [Vo misled Dam to believe that the second agreement was a valid agreement between the parties when it was illusory], 109; Foster v. Sexton (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 998, 1028 [fraud must be pleaded with particularity].)

The court overrules cross-defendant Kay’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Kay since Dam has alleged facts establishing that Kay’s conduct was “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community” and therefore has alleged the element of extreme and outrageous conduct.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-1051 [internal quotations and citations omitted]; Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 20-21, 33 [cross-defendant Kay “tried to stab Ms. Dam with scissors”].)

The court sustains cross-defendant Vo’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Dam has not alleged facts establishing that Vo’s conduct was “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community” and therefore has not alleged the element of extreme and outrageous conduct as to Vo.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Hughes, supra, 46 Cal.4th at pp. 1050-1051 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

The court sustains Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the sixth cause of action for negligent misrepresentation because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainants did not identify any misrepresentations in support of this cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Borman v. Brown (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1048, 1060 [elements of negligent misrepresentation].)  Although the court acknowledges that Cross-Complainants have alleged the existence of various alleged misrepresentations in the general background portion of the Cross-Complaint, the facts in support of this cause of action do not specify which representations form the bases for this cause of action.  Instead, Cross-Complainants have alleged that “Cross-Defendants owed a duty to Cross-Complainant” and “breached their duty to use reasonable care in making business decision and demanding dividend payments from” Cross-Complainants.  (Cross-Compl., ¶ 131.)

The court overrules Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action for negligence because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Cross-Complainants have alleged that (1) Cross-Defendants owed them a duty, (2) Cross-Defendants breached their duty to use reasonable care in making business decisions and demanding dividend payments from Cross-Complainants, and (3) as a result of their breach, Cross-Complainants have suffered damages “with the closure of the business due to Cross-Defendants’ conduct that destroyed Ms. Dam’s ability to run the business.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 136, 138-139; Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687; Lopez v. Southern Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795 [“Ordinarily, negligence may be pleaded in general terms”].)

MOTION TO STRIKE

Cross-Defendants move the court for an order striking from the Cross-Complaint            (1) paragraphs 67, 69, 75, 77, 83, 100, 108, 122, 131, 137, and 138 as irrelevant; (2) the requests for punitive damages made in paragraphs 73-74, 81-82, 94-95, 114, 129, paragraph four in each prayer for relief as to the first through fifth causes of action, and paragraph six in the prayer labeled “On All Causes of Action;” and (3) the requests for attorney’s fees and costs made in paragraph 3 in each prayer for relief as to the first through fifth and seventh causes of action, paragraph seven of the sixth cause of action, and paragraph two in the prayer labeled “On All Causes of Action.”

First, the court denies as moot Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike paragraph 131 because (1) this paragraph is alleged in support of the sixth cause of action, and (2) the court has sustained Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to this cause of action.

Second, the court denies Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike paragraphs 67, 69, 75, 77, 83, 100, 108, 122, 137, and 138 because Cross-Defendants have not shown that the allegations made in those paragraphs are irrelevant, false, or improper.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1638 [“use of the motion to strike should be cautious and sparing” and should not be used as “a procedural ‘line item veto’ for the civil defendant”].)

Third, the court denies Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike the requests, prayers, and supporting allegations for punitive damages because Cross-Complainants have alleged facts establishing that Cross-Defendants are guilty of fraud.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)

Fourth, the court grants Cross-Defendants’ motion to strike Cross-Complainants’ prayers for “Attorney Fees” as set forth in (1) paragraphs 3 of the prayers on the first, second, third, fourth, fifth, and seventh causes of action, (2) paragraph 7 of the prayer on the sixth cause of action, and (3) paragraph 2 of the prayer on “All Causes of Action,” because Cross-Complainants did not identify a statute or contract to support their requests for attorney’s fees.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1021.)

ORDER

            The court overrules cross-defendants Cathy Kay and Tina Vo’s demurrer to cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam and Liberate Hollywood, Inc.’s first through fourth and seventh causes of action.

            The court overrules cross-defendant Cathy Kay’s demurrer to cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam’s fifth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

            The court sustains cross-defendant Tina Vo’s demurrer to cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam’s fifth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

            The court sustains cross-defendants Cathy Kay and Tina Vo’s demurrer to cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam and Liberate Hollywood, Inc.’s sixth cause of action for negligent misrepresentation.

            The court grants cross-defendants Cathy Kay and Tina Vo’s motion to strike cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam and Liberate Hollywood, Inc.’s prayers for attorney’s fees.

            The court grants cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam and Liberate Hollywood, Inc. 20 days leave to file a First Amended Cross-Complaint that cures the deficiencies set forth above.

            The court orders cross-complainant Cristina Marie Dam and Liberate Hollywood, Inc.

to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM WAIVER (FORM INTERROGATORIES)

Defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order granting her relief from her waiver of objections regarding the Form Interrogatories, Set One, served on her by plaintiff Emilio Reyes (“Plaintiff”).

“The court, on motion, may relieve [a] party from th[e] waiver [of objections upon failing to serve a timely response to interrogatories] on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: [¶] (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2020.230, and 2030.240[,] [and] [¶] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

First, the court finds that Defendant’s motion and request to be relieved from her waiver of objections are not moot and therefore rules on the merits of Defendant’s motion.

Second, the court finds that Defendant has shown that she has since served verified responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories that are in substantial compliance with sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2020.230, and 2030.240.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a)(1); Def. Ex. R [Def. Responses to Pl. Form Interrogatories].)

Third, the court finds that Defendant has shown that her failure to serve a timely response to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories was the result of her mistake and excusable neglect.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a)(2).) 

Defendant has submitted her declaration, in which she states that (1) Plaintiff served the subject discovery on September 24, 2020; (2) four days later, on September 28, 2020, Defendant filed and served her special motion to strike Plaintiff’s operative complaint; (3) on October 12, 2020, Defendant sent a letter to Plaintiff that included a statement that she was objecting to the discovery; (4) the court granted Defendant’s special motion to strike in its entirety on June 2, 2021; (5) the Court of Appeal issued its remittitur on August 12, 2022, affirming the court’s order as to five causes of action and reversing the order as to two causes of action; and (6) after the remittitur was issued, Defendant did not keep track of the deadline by which to respond to Plaintiff’s discovery because she thought the requests were moot.  (McIntosh Decl., ¶¶ 6-9, 12-14; Def. Ex. I.)  The court finds that Defendant’s declaration is sufficient to show that she failed to serve timely responses to the Form Interrogatories based on (1) her mistaken belief that the discovery was moot, and (2) her mistake and excusable neglect in failing to calendar her deadline to respond to this discovery given that this action was stayed pending the resolution of her special motion to strike and the appeal of the court’s order granting her motion.  (McIntosh Decl., ¶ 14.)

The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion for relief from her waiver of objections to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

Fourth, the court denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions made in his consolidated opposition papers.

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM WAIVER (SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES)

Defendant moves the court for an order granting her relief from her waiver of objections regarding the Special Interrogatories, Set One, served on her by Plaintiff.

“The court, on motion, may relieve [a] party from th[e] waiver [of objections upon failing to serve a timely response to interrogatories] on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: [¶] (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2020.230, and 2030.240[,] [and] [¶] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

First, the court finds that Defendant’s motion and request to be relieved from her waiver of objections are not moot and therefore rules on the merits of Defendant’s motion. 

Second, the court finds that Defendant has shown that she has since served verified responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories that are in substantial compliance with sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2020.230, and 2030.240.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a)(1); Def. Ex. S [Def. Responses to Pl. Special Interrogatories].)

Third, the court finds that Defendant has shown that her failure to serve timely responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories was the result of her mistake and excusable neglect based on (1) her mistaken belief that she thought the discovery was moot upon the granting of her special motion to strike, and (2) her mistake and neglect in failing to keep track of the deadline by which she was required to respond to this discovery.  (McIntosh Decl., ¶ 14.)

The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion for relief from her waiver of objections to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

Fourth, the court denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions made in his consolidated opposition papers.

MOTION FOR RELIEF FROM WAIVER (DOCUMENT DEMANDS)

Defendant moves the court for an order granting her relief from her waiver of objections regarding the Inspection Demand for Documents served by Plaintiff.

“The court, on motion, may relieve [a] party from th[e] waiver [of objections for failure to serve a timely response to a demand for inspection] on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: [¶] (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280[,] [and] [¶] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

First, the court finds that Defendant’s motion and request to be relieved from her waiver of objections are not moot and therefore rules on the merits of Defendant’s motion.

Second, the court finds that Defendant has shown that she has since served verified responses to the document demands that are in substantial compliance with sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a)(1); Def. Ex. T [Def. Responses to Pl. Demand for Documents].)

Third, the court finds that Defendant has shown that her failure to serve timely responses was the result of mistake and excusable neglect based on (1) her mistaken belief that the discovery was moot upon the granting of her special motion to strike, and (2) her mistake and neglect in failing to properly calendar the deadline by which she was required to respond to this discovery.  (McIntosh Decl., ¶ 14.)

The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion for relief from her waiver of objections to Plaintiff’s Inspection Demand for Documents.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

Fourth, the court denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions made in his consolidated opposition papers.

            The court orders defendant Alexandra R. McIntosh, APC to give notice of this ruling.
