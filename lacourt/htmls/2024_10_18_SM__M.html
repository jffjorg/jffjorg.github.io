DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21SMCV01474    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Hay, et al., v. 1012 2nd St. Homeowners’ Association, et al.

CASE NO.:                21SMCV01474

MOTION:                  Motion to Tax/Strike Costs

HEARING DATE:   10/18/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

In general, the “prevailing party” is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs for suit in any action or proceeding. (CCP § 1032(b); Santisas v. Goodin (1998) 17 Cal.4th 599, 606.) “Prevailing party” includes the party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant. (CCP § 1032 (a)(4).) 

 

“Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (CCP § 1033.5(c)(2).) “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal. App. 4th 761, 774.) On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs. (Id.) 

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff Lani Hay moves to strike the Memoranda of Costs filed by Defendants 1012 2nd Street LLC, Saeed Babaeean, and Soheil Mehrabanian.  Plaintiff argues that Defendants are not “prevailing parties.” As discussed at the prior hearing on the motion for attorneys’ fees, Defendants are prevailing parties because Plaintiff dismissed them without securing a settlement or obtaining any relief against them. Likewise, based upon the record before the Court, the Court does not find that Defendants are the alter egos of the HOA Defendants, such that Plaintiff’s settlement with the HOA would apply to Defendants. Critically, no injustice will occur, since Plaintiff has already secured her litigation objectives against the HOA. Allowing separate, dismissed Defendants to recover costs as allowed by statute would not be an injustice.

 

Plaintiff asserts that costs should be denied because Defendants refused to participate in an alternative dispute resolution. Civil Code § 5960 states: “In an enforcement action in which attorney's fees and costs may be awarded, the court, in determining the amount of the award, may consider whether a party's refusal to participate in alternative dispute resolution before commencement of the action was reasonable.” Plaintiff alleged that on February 11, 2021, Plaintiff sent a letter to the HOA (not Defendants) requesting that the parties engage in an alternative dispute resolution in an attempt to resolve this lawsuit prior to filing. (Compl., ¶ 49.) Otherwise, the court observes no evidence on the record that would show Defendants (Mehrabanian, Babaeean, and the LLC) refused to participate in any ADR prior to commencement of suit. 

 

Defendant Mehrabanian claimed $615.41 in motion filing fees. Defendant 1012 2nd Street LLC claimed $460.98 in costs, including $435.00 in motion filing fees, $150 in jury fees, and $30.41 in electronic filing fees. Defendant Babaeean claimed $3,738.06 in fees, which included $2,684.50 in mediation fees allowable under the CC&Rs.

banian Each of these items of costs are expressly allowable by statute or by the CC&Rs. (CCP §1033.5.) Such fees for motions and other costs, required by the Court, are reasonable and necessary for litigation.

 

Accordingly, the motions are DENIED.


Case Number: 24SMCV00025    Hearing Date: October 18, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:             Prasad v. Nathan, et al. 

CASE NO.:                   24SMCV00025

MOTION:                     Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint  

HEARING DATE:   10/18/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD 

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (CCP §§ 430.30, 430.70.) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732, internal citations omitted.) 

 

A special demurrer for uncertainty is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him/her. (CCP § 430.10(f); Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Moreover, even if the pleading is somewhat vague, “ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Ibid.)  

 

“Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227.) It is an abuse of discretion for the court to deny leave to amend where there is any reasonable possibility that plaintiff can state a good cause of action. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on plaintiff to show¿in what manner¿plaintiff can amend the complaint, and¿how¿that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading.¿(Id.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendants International Church of Divine Awakening (“ICODA”), and its directors, Chase Thomas (“Devaraj”), Manya Natan (“Manya”), and Michael Sletta (“Sletta”) (collectively the “ICODA Defendants”) demurrer and move to strike Plaintiff Gyanmitra Prasad’s First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). ICODA demurs to the 5th-12th causes of action. Defendants Devaraj and Manya demur to the 10th-12th causes of action. Defendant Sletta demurs to the 5th, 6th, and 9th-12th causes of action.

 

On October 17, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application and exercises its discretion to consider Plaintiff’s late filed opposition.

 

The Ministerial Exception

 

ICODA Defendants argue that the ministerial exception bars all claims against them.

Employment discrimination and wrongful termination suits brought on behalf of a minister challenging a church’s decision to fire them are barred by the so-called “ministerial exception.” (Hosanna-Tabor Evangelical Lutheran Church & Sch. v. E.E.O.C., (2012) 565 U.S. 171, 196.) The Supreme Court has recently clarified the purpose of the ministerial exception:

 

“The First Amendment provides that ‘Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof.’ Among other things, the Religion Clauses protect the right of churches and other religious institutions to decide matters ‘of faith and doctrine’ without government intrusion. State interference in that sphere would obviously violate the free exercise of religion, and any attempt by government to dictate or even to influence such matters would constitute one of the central attributes of an establishment of religion. The First Amendment outlaws such intrusion. 

 

The independence of religious institutions in matters of ‘faith and doctrine’ is closely linked to independence in what we have termed ‘matters of church government.’ This does not mean that religious institutions enjoy a general immunity from secular laws, but it does protect their autonomy with respect to internal management decisions that are essential to the institution’s central mission. And a component of this autonomy is the selection of the individuals who play certain key roles. 

 

The “ministerial exception” was based on this insight. Under this rule, courts are bound to stay out of employment disputes involving those holding certain important positions with churches and other religious institutions….

 

When the so-called ministerial exception finally reached this Court in Hosanna-Tabor, we unanimously recognized that the Religion Clauses foreclose certain employment discrimination claims brought against religious organizations. The constitutional foundation for our holding was the general principle of church autonomy to which we have already referred: independence in matters of faith and doctrine and in closely linked matters of internal government.” 

 

(Our Lady of Guadalupe School v. Morrissey-Berru (2020) 591 U.S. 732, 746-748, internal citations omitted.)

 

Caselaw has defined the ministerial exception defense as including the following elements: 1) the defendant-employer is religious group; 2) the plaintiff-employee qualifies as a “minister;” and 3) the claim turns “on an ecclesiastical inquiry or ‘excessive[ly] entangle[s]’ the court in religious matters.” (Sumner v. Simpson University, (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 577, 580, citing Hosanna-Tabor, supra, 565 U.S. at 176-177.)

 

Court have not adopted a “rigid formula” for deciding when an employee qualifies as a minister. (Hosanna-Tabor, supra, 565 U.S. at 190-191.) Courts focus on a category of employees whose functions are “essential to the independence of practically all religious groups. These include those who serve in positions of leadership, those who perform important functions in worship services and in the performance of religious ceremonies and rituals, and those who are entrusted with teaching and conveying the tenets of the faith to the next generation.” (Id. at 200 [conc. opn. of Alito, J.].) For instance, in Hossanna-Tabor, the court found the plaintiff to be a minister where she was a “called” teacher at a school operated a Lutheran Church. (Id., at 177-179, 191-192.) The church held her out to be a “minister,” issued the teacher a “diploma of vocation” with the title “Minister of Religion, Commissioned,” the teacher's had a significant degree of religious training, the teacher held herself out as a minister by accepting the formal call to religious service and by claiming a special housing allowance on her taxes, and the teacher’s duties included conveying the church's message and played a substantial role in carrying out its mission. (Ibid.) Considering these factors, the Supreme Court found that the teacher was a “minister” within the exception.

 

While this exception certainly applies to termination claims by ministers, not all causes of action related to termination of ministers are barred by the rule. In Sumner, despite finding that plaintiff was a minister, the plaintiff’s breach of contract cause of action was not barred by the ministerial exception. The Sumner court explained:

 

There is no dispute regarding the reason for Sumner’s termination. Sumner’s argument with defendants’ stated reason for terminating her, insubordination, is that she could not have been insubordinate because that would require her to have refused to follow an order of her supervisor, and she was never given an order that she refused to follow. Furthermore, Sumner’s actions that were claimed to have been insubordinate involved sending emails to the Simpson University community regarding her prior termination. Reviewing Sumner’s contract cause of action will not require the court to wade into doctrinal waters because review of the breach of contract claim does not require a review of Sumner’s religious qualification or performance as a religious leader. Defendants have never claimed to have terminated Sumner for religious reasons, only because she was insubordinate. Defendants voluntarily circumscribed their own conduct by entering into the contract with Sumner, and the contract can be enforced without breaching the institution’s religious autonomy.

 

(Emphasis Added.)

 

The Sumner court contrasted the facts of their case with Lee v. Sixth Mount Zion Baptist Church of Pittsburgh (3d Cir. 2018) 903 F.3d 113. There, the terms of the employment contract established that the pastor could be removed for failing in the role of spiritual leadership and furthering the mission of the church. (Id. at 120.) The church fired the pastor for purportedly failing to provide adequate spiritual leadership, as demonstrated by decreased church contributions and attendance during the pastor's tenure. (Ibid.) The court commented that the pastor's role “in causing decreased giving and reduced membership in the Church requires a determination of what constitutes adequate spiritual leadership and how that translates into donations and attendance—questions that would impermissibly entangle the court in religious governance and doctrine prohibited by the Establishment Clause.” (Id. at 121.) Since Lee was fired for failing to provide adequate spiritual leadership, the cause would necessarily require that the trial court analyze the adequacy of his spiritual leadership and thereby entangle the court in religious matters. Thus, the ministerial exception applied in Sumner.

 

In this case, the first two elements of the ministerial exception are met. There is no reasonable dispute that Defendants are part of a religious organization or that Plaintiff alleges he was a minister within the meaning of the exception. ICODA is a nonprofit, tax-exempt religious corporation. (FAC ¶ 3.) Until Plaintiff’s termination, Plaintiff served as “President,” “Spiritual Director,” and “Board Director” of ICODA. (¶¶ 1, 13-19, 44-47.) The FAC further alleges that in 2019, Premodaya removed Plaintiff from all “spiritual” or “pastoral” roles, and ceased consulting Plaintiff on spiritual matters. (¶ 55.) Later, in August 2021, Premodaya told Plaintiff that he is “not going to be Spiritual Director anymore.” (¶ 56.) On March 18, 2022, all of the other ICODA directors signed and sent a letter to Plaintiff, noticing a special meeting of the Board of Directors to remove Plaintiff as Spiritual Director from the Board, and as President. (¶ 62.) On March 31, 2022, ICODA sent a letter to Plaintiff, terminating Plaintiff’s employment. (¶ 68.) In further correspondence, ICODA told Plaintiff that he was no longer an employee, officer or director of ICODA, and his removal was necessitated solely on the basis of crucial and urgent ecclesiastical issues. (¶ 71.)

 

Plaintiff suggests that he was removed as director months or years prior to 2022, perhaps in 2019. However, the FAC does not provide sufficient factual allegations showing that this was the case. Plaintiff alleges the conclusion that Premodaya “removed” Plaintiff from spiritual/pastoral roles in 2019. (FAC ¶ 55.) However, he also alleges that he was fired from his position as director in March 2022. (FAC ¶ 1.) Further, he alleges that in August 2021, Premodaya told Plaintiff that he would not be Spiritual Director anymore. (¶¶ 19, 54.) In December 2021, Premodaya “unilaterally” sent a resignation letter and demanded Plaintiff resign, which Plaintiff refused. (¶ 59, Ex. C.) In January 30, 2022, ICODA sent an employment separation agreement to Plaintiff, the terms of which required Plaintiff to resign from his positions as “President, Spiritual Director, member of the ICODA Board of Directors, Chairman of the ICODA Board of Directors . . ..” (¶ 60, Ex. D, § 2.) If Plaintiff was removed as Spiritual Director in 2019 or 2021, then he could not have also been removed as Spiritual Director in 2022. (¶¶ 62-68, Exs. E-G.) The well-pled, specific facts and exhibits show that Plaintiff was a director in 2022 and removed in March 2022. On the other hand, the FAC states a mere conclusion that Plaintiff was removed earlier in 2019. In opposition, Plaintiff does not offer any facts in support of his theory that he was fired from his director position prior to March 2022.  

 

The Court agrees that the FAC, on its face, shows that the defense applies to the wrongful termination, intentional infliction of emotional distress (arising from termination), and negligent infliction of intentional distress (arising from termination) claims. Such claims for wrongful termination and resulting emotional distress are per se barred by the ministerial exception. Supreme Court precedent confirms that it is “impermissible for the government to contradict a church's determination of who can act as its ministers.” (Hosanna-Tabor, supra, 565 U.S. at 185.) The above claims would require the court to disturb or, at a minimum, question ICODA’s ecclesiastical decision regarding its Spiritual Director. Thus, the wrongful termination and related tort claims would be barred.

 

The other claims are likely not barred by the exception. The ministerial exception is applied liberally and extends to any acts or statements, including tort claims, that are “part and parcel” of a termination decision. (Gunn v. Mariners Church, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 206, 217 [at summary judgment, ministerial exception immunized defamation/invasion of privacy tort liability for post-termination statements to congregation explaining that plaintiff was terminated for immoral homosexual acts].) Caselaw recognizes that the ministerial exception applies to otherwise actionable claims of defamation and invasion of privacy, when based on statements “related to the hiring, firing, discipline or administration of clergy.” (Higgins v. Maher (1989) 210 Cal.App.3d 1168, 1175.) Courts have recognized difficulty in determining whether a particular dispute is ecclesiastical or simply a civil law controversy in which church officials happen to be involved. (Id. at 1170.) In Higgins, however, the complaint made expressly clear that all of the alleged defamation occurred while the bishop carried out his ecclesiastic functions. (Id.) On the other hand, if it is not clear on the face of the complaint, then the issue is better left to summary adjudication. (See Gunn, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th at 215-216 [despite dismissal at MSJ, Gunn complaint contained “no facts” which showed an “ecclesiastical purpose for any statements being made to the entire congregation after Gunn's employment was terminated” distinguishing it from Higgins,].)

 

Liberally interpreting all facts in the light most favorable to plaintiff, the defamation does not certainly show that alleged defamation was “part and parcel” with the termination process. The FAC identifies three potentially defamatory statements. First, a December 15, 2022, letter by Wiener calling Plaintiff a liar, among other things. (FAC ¶¶ 78-79.) Second, a December 18, 2022, letter by the ICODA Board to the entire ICODA community, calling Plaintiff a disgruntled ex-employee seeking revenge" that was trying to "con" members, that made "false and baseless accusations and threats to extract money from ICODA", and harassed ICODA members, among other things. (¶ 80.) While caselaw suggests that statements following termination could be part and parcel to termination, the extreme delay between the termination and defamation suggest that this was not “part” of the termination process. The FAC also does not suggest that the cause would require the Court to determine any ecclesiastical inquiries or review religious matters. Simply put, each allegedly defamatory assertion does not have any bearing on religious topics. Instead, the defamation pertains to Plaintiff’s activities following his dismissal without any reference to religious issues. The ministerial exception defense therefore cannot be adjudicated at the pleading stage as to this cause.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the seventh through ninth causes of action for wrongful termination, intentional infliction of emotional distress, and negligent infliction of emotional distress.

 

Fifth and Sixth Causes for Failure to Prevent Harassment

 

Religious nonprofit corporation employers are exempt from FEHA, as such churches are expressly not an “employer” as defined by FEHA. (Gov. Code § 12926(d).) Plaintiff claims that the fifth and sixth causes are rooted entirely in common law and thus not subject to this exclusion. However, Plaintiff does not cite any authority for such a common-law duty. Plaintiff only cites authority for the generic rules which apply for liability against corporate agents in their individual capacities. (PMC, Inc. v. Kadisha (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 1368; Francis T. v. Village Green Owners Assn. (1986) 42 Cal.3d 490.) The authorities do not recognize the existence of a common law duty for an employer or its agents to prevent harassment of their employees. Likewise, Plaintiff cites insufficient authority for any special relationship between himself and the ICODA Defendants as an adopted, adult son of Defendant Premodaya. (Rotolo v. San Jose Sports & Entertainment, LLC, (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 307, 325.) Further, the alleged harassment occurred (and was therefore not prevented) in 2015 at the latest, more than 8 years prior to the filing of this action. Thus, leave to amend would appear futile.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave as to the failure to prevent harassment causes of action.

 

Tenth and Eleventh Causes - Derivative Standing

 

Defendants argue that the FAC fails to state a derivative claim.

 

It is well settled that only parties with a real interest in a dispute have standing to seek its adjudication. (CCP § 367.) "A litigant's standing to sue is a threshold issue to be resolved before the matter can be reached on the merits." (Boorstein v. CBS Interactive, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 456, 465.) Generally, derivative claims are brought on behalf of a corporation to recover for the benefit of the corporation itself. (Jones v. H F Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93, 106.) Because the injury is suffered by the legal entity, the directors or shareholders have no direct cause of action or right of recovery. (Grosset v. Wenaas (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1100, 1108.) Instead, certain persons within a corporation may bring a derivative action to enforce the corporation's rights and redress its injuries if the management fails or refuses to do so. (Ibid.) Generally, the party must have a recognized interest in the corporation at the time of the relevant transaction. (Id. at 1111-1115; see, e.g., Corp. Code §17709.02(a)(1) [as to LLCs].) Whether a claim is derivative or personal in nature depends on the whether gravamen of the harm is to the individual plaintiff or the corporation. (Jones, supra, 1 Cal.3d at 106.)

 

The gravamen of the purported derivative action is for the recovery of harm to Plaintiff’s individual interests, and not ICODA’s corporate interests. Plaintiff pleads damages that are entirely personal to himself and not the corporation. Plaintiff alleges as a proximate result of the conduct of ICODA, Devaraj and Manya, Plaintiff suffered mental and emotional distress and damage, humiliation, fear, guilt and shame; was damaged in his ability to perform his work and maintain normal relationships, and was prevented from seeking or obtaining employment. (FAC ¶¶ 164-165.) As such, the gravamen of the action is for recovery of Plaintiff’s personal damages. Simply put, Plaintiff has not pled any grounds for a derivative action.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the derivative causes of action.

 

Twelfth Cause of Action for Defamation

 

Defendants demur to the defamation cause of action on statute of limitations grounds.

 

“Where the dates alleged in the complaint show the action is barred by the statute of limitations, a general demurrer lies . . .. The running of the statute must appear ‘clearly and affirmatively’ from the face of the complaint. It is not enough that the complaint might be time-barred.” (Geneva Towers Ltd. Partnership v. City of San Francisco (2003) 29 Cal.4th 769, 781.)

 

A cause of action for defamation is subject to the 1-year statute of limitations. (CCP § 340(c).) The cause accrues when the defamatory statement is initially published to a third party. (Shively v. Bozanich (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1230, 1247.) The discovery rule, which postpones accrual until plaintiff discovers or has reason to discover the factual basis for a claim, generally does not apply to defamation claims when the basis for a claim has been published in the public record or has been the subject of publicity. (Id. at 1248.) The discovery rule “has been applied when the defamatory statement is hidden from view” for example, “in a personnel file that generally cannot be inspected by the plaintiff.” (Id. at 1249.) However, the discovery rule has never applied to the publication of a defamatory statement contained in a wide publication such as in a book or newspaper. (Id. at 1250.) The "discovery rule may be applied to breaches which can be, and are, committed in secret, and moreover, where the harm flowing from those breaches will not be reasonably discoverable by plaintiff until a future time." (April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 832; see Hebrew Academy of San Francisco v. Goldman (2007) 42 Cal.4th 883,894 [delayed discovery rule does not apply to defamation claims unless the defamatory statement was hidden from view because it was communicated in an inherently secretive manner]; Christoff v. Nestle USA, Inc. (2009) 47 Cal.4th 468,483 [same]; cf. Manguso v. Oceanside Unified School District (1979) 88 Cal.App.3d 725, 727, 731 [delayed discovery rule may apply to libel claim based on contents of letter placed in a confidential file in defendant's personnel office and not discovered by plaintiff until almost 16 years later].)

 

The FAC cites one public statement: On December 18, 2022, Aden, Manya and Devaraj, acting in their official capacities, sent a letter to the entire ICODA community, a copy of which is attached as Exhibit I which called Plaintiff "a disgruntled ex-employee seeking revenge," accused Plaintiff of trying to "con" members, making "false and baseless accusations and threats to extract money from ICODA," "harassing" ICODA, "disseminating his hatred and revenge" and other derogatory statements. (FAC ¶ 172.) The instant action was not filed until more than one year later on January 2, 2024. Plaintiff allegedly learned of the existence of the letter on January 5, 2023. However, Plaintiff admits that this statement was published publicly and not secretly. Thus, there are no pled circumstances which would toll the limitations period from the date of publication to the date of discovery. Moreover, equitable tolling could not apply since Plaintiff allegedly discovered the cause well within time to file suit.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave as to the defamation claim.

 

Conclusion

 

The demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the 5th-9th and 12th causes of action. The demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend as to the 10th and 11th causes of action. The motion to strike is MOOT per the ruling on demurrer.
