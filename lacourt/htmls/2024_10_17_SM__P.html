Case Number: 22SMCV01418    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Roth v. Arakelian Enterprises, Inc., Case no. 22SMCV01418

Hearing date October 17, 2024

Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff Roth’s Deposition

Plaintiff Roth in pro per sues defendants Arakelian Enterprises, Inc. dba Athens Services and its employee Caraballo for property damage to plaintiff’s vehicle. Defendants alleges plaintiff failed to respond to requests for discovery and move to compel plaintiff’s deposition and request sanctions. Plaintiff opposes and requests sanctions against defense counsel.

A party may move to compel deposition per Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2025.450(a). Defendants attempted to take plaintiff’s deposition twice, on 7/22/2024 and 8/22/2024. The court ruled on 8/1/2024 that defendants could file a motion to compel deposition and for sanctions. Decl. Herzog para. 14, ex. M; Min. Order 8/1/2024. Plaintiff asked to reschedule a previously set 8/22/2024 deposition to 9/3/2024. Decl. Herzog para. 16.

Plaintiff failed to appear on for a properly noticed deposition on 9/3/2024 and did not serve written objection. Decl. Herzog para. 16. Plaintiff argues the deposition notice was in the incorrect font, so invalid and that he did object, but defendants did not receive the objections.

Plaintiff accuses defense counsel Herzog of lying in her declaration (Decl. Roth paras. 11-15) and seeks $3,712.50 for alleged discovery abuse, plus costs. To request sanctions, plaintiff would have to file a motion to that effect, not simply include such a request in an opposition to the motion to compel. There is no basis to award costs, which request is denied.

Regarding defendant’s motion to compel, this issue has been ongoing, and plaintiff has failed to appear and has not provided a reasonable basis for his failure to appear. Plaintiff has been on notice since 8/1/2024. Min. Order 8/1/2024. He subsequently failed to appear. Plaintiff brought this suit; he is required to sit for deposition.

Defendants seek attorney’s fees at $400 an hour for six hours spent preparing the motion and an additional hour at hearing totaling $3,600, plus $60 in filing fees. Decl. Herzog para. 18. The court finds the hourly rate reasonable but finds six hours to prepare this motion to be unreasonable. In addition, the hearing is not likely to last a full hour. The court awards a total of four hours at $400/hour = $1,600 payable within 30 days. Further, the deposition is to occur within 15 days of the date of this motion. GRANTED.


Case Number: 23SMCV05070    Hearing Date: October 17, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Scholer v. Bohrer, Case no. 23SMCV05070

Hearing date October 17, 2024

Defendants Bohrer and Bohrer Construction’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ FAC

Plaintiffs Scholer sue defendants Stephen Bohrer, Bohrer Construction, Mendez Master Concrete, Inc., Mendez Leon, American Contractors Indemnity Company, and Leiva dba JJ Electric for breach of contract, fraud and conversion. Defendants Bohrer and Bohrer Construction (“BC”) demur to the first four COAs in the FAC, arguing they do not state facts sufficient to constitute a valid claim per Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e).

“The function of a demurrer is to test the sufficiency of the complaint as a matter of law.” Holiday Matinee, Inc. v. Rambus, Inc. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1413, 1420. A complaint “is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts” Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550, but plaintiff must set forth the essential facts of the case “with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint [the] defendant with the nature, source and extent” of the plaintiff’s claim. Doheny Park Terrace Homeowners Ass’n., Inc. v. Truck Ins. Exchange (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 1076, 1099.

First COA: Breach of Contract

The court previously sustained moving parties’ demurrer to the breach of contract claim without leave to amend. The first cause of action should only be pled as against Stephen Bohrer.

Second COA: Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

The elements for a cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing are: (1) the parties entered into a contract; (2) plaintiff performed; (3) all conditions required for defendant’s performance occurred or were excused; (4) defendant’s conduct prevented plaintiff from receiving the benefits expected under the contract; (5) defendant did not act fairly and in good faith; and (6) damage. CACI 325.

BC is not a proper party under this claim, as a claim for breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing necessarily arises from a breach of contract. Plaintiffs do not dispute this.

Defendants argue plaintiffs attempted to add duties to the contract between defendant Bohrer and plaintiffs that are an expansion of the original contract, in violation of Careau & Co. v. Sec. Pac. Bus. Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395. Defendants argue plaintiffs’ allegations are duplicative of the breach of contract claim. Plaintiffs alleged that defendant Bohrer:

•              failed to provide defect-free installations that were code compliant and approved by Los Angeles Building and Safety inspectors. FAC para. 38.

•              engaged in work outside the scope of the agreed upon contracts without the prior written approval of plaintiffs. Id.

•              failed to timely secure and provide lien releases to plaintiffs. Id.

•              failed to document the progress of construction and to complete the construction on time and on budget. Id.

•              failed to provide adequate supervision of the construction. Id.

Plaintiffs’ allegations are sufficient to state a claim for breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing. Plaintiffs alleged a contract under which they fulfilled their obligations by making payments. FAC para. 14. Plaintiffs allege they did not receive the benefits of the contract and suffered harmed.

Defendants argue plaintiffs fail to allege unfair or bad faith actions; plaintiffs allege defendants acted unfairly by willfully failing to properly manage the work. FAC para. 38. This is sufficient to survive demurrer.

Third COA: Violation of Business & Professions Code Sections 7159, 7159.4 and 7159.6.

Defendants argue there is no civil cause of action for violations of Bus. & Prof. Code §§7159, 7159.4 and 7159.6. Violations of §7159 and §7159.5 constitute misdemeanors. Section 7159.6 states extra work or change orders in violation of a contract are not enforceable against a buyer.

Plaintiffs argue these statutes were intended to protect homeowners, so violations thereof give rise to civil claims. Simply because a statute is designed to protect a class does not inherently imbue that class with the right to bring a civil cause of action, absent express statutory authority.

Case law holds a home improvement contract may be enforceable despite violating sec. 7159. See Asdourian v. Araj (1985) 38 Cal.3d 276, 292. “The Legislature intended neither that the express misdemeanor penalty provision of the statute would be exclusive nor that all contracts made in violation of the statute would be void, thereby opening the door to enforcement of nonconforming contracts in appropriate cases” Arya Group, Inc. v. Cher (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 619, 615. Neither statute nor case law supports plaintiffs’ contentions. Sustained without leave to amend.

Fourth COA: Fraud

The court overruled defendants’ previous demurrer to plaintiffs’ fourth COA for fraud, finding the claim was not subject to the economic loss rule. Plaintiffs argue the fourth COA was not substantially changed via the FAC, so demurrer is not permitted under Code Civ. Proc. §430.41.

Defendants argue they previously demurred on both economic loss rule grounds and under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e). The court did not address the Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e) argument, which defendants renew. Min. Order 2/23/2024.

The elements of a fraud claim are: (a) misrepresentation; (b) knowledge of falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) damage. See Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638. Defendants argue plaintiffs fail to plead the fraud cause of action with sufficient particularity.

Plaintiffs allege: (1) defendants made misrepresentations regarding additional payments required to complete the work. FAC para. 48; (2) defendants knew those additional payments would not result in the completion of the work. FAC para. 49; (3) they would not have made the additional payments if not for defendants’ misrepresentations, which were intended to induce reliance. FAC paras. 49-51; (4) defendants made misrepresentations to induce plaintiffs to retain BC as general contractor. FAC para. 53; and (5) damages exceeding $500,000. FAC para. 54. These allegations are sufficient to establish a COA for fraud.

Plaintiffs alleged intentional misrepresentations, which induced them to make payments to defendants. Overruled.

Defendants’ demurrer as to the first and third COAs is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. Defendants’ demurrer as to the second and fourth COAs is OVERRULED, noting that BC should be removed from the second COA.
