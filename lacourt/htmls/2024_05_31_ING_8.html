DEPARTMENT 8 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV00525    Hearing Date: May 31, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling 

¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 May 31, 2024¿ 

¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                  22TRCV00525

 

CASE NAME:                        Sikder Holdings International, Inc., et al. v. Michael Ball, et al.

¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Plaintiffs, Sikder Holdings International, Inc., R & R Beverages Group PTE, LTD., Lisa Haque, and Nasim Sikder. 

¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant Ultracor, Inc. (No Opposition)

¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set.                

¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion for an Order that Requests be Deemed Admitted, Set Two, Against Defendant, Ultracor

                                                (2) Request for Sanctions

                                               

                                               

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) GRANTED

                                                (2) DENIED without prejudice.

 

 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿ 

¿ 

A. Factual¿ 

 

On June 29, 2022, Plaintiffs, Sikder Holdings International, Inc, R&R Beverages Group PTE, LTD, Lisa Haque, and Nasim Sikder filed a Complaint against Defendants, Michael Ball, Marsha NA Nongkhai, aka Asha Kai, Ultracor, inc., and DOES 1 through 25. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty (CWater); (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty (Denim Technologies); (4) Fraud/Concealment; (5) Conversion; and (6) Declaratory Relief.

 

Per the moving papers, on March 28, 2024, Plaintiffs served Defendant, Ultracor with Requests for Admission, Set Two. Plaintiffs contend that Ultracor has completely ignored their requests, having failed to serve any responses to the RFAs which are now long overdue. As such, they have brought this Motion to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted.

 

B. Procedural  

 

            On May 6, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion to Deem the Truth of the Matters Specified in  that Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

¿II. ANALYSIS 

 

A. Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.280(c), the court shall make the order deeming the truth of the matters admitted unless the responding party serves before the hearing a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Code Civ. Proc § 2033.220. Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.220 requires that each answer either admits, denies or specifies that the responding party lacks sufficient information or knowledge. As stated in Demyer v. Costa Mesa Mobile Home Estates, the moving party is not required to meet and confer before bringing this action. (Demyer v. Costa Mesa Mobile Home Estates, 36 Cal.App.4th 393, 395.)¿¿ 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿ 

 

            B. Discussion 

 

Plaintiffs note that on March 28, 2024, they served its Requests for Admission, Set Two on Defendant Ultracor. However, Plaintiffs assert that Ultracor did not respond in any way to the RFAs, neither with objections, admission nor denials.   Because of this, the Court’s tentative ruling is to GRANT Defendant’s motion. However, the Court also notes that on July 3, 2023, it granted Ultracor’s former counsel’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. There is nothing in the Court’s records to indicate whether Ultracor has obtained new counsel since July of last year. However, even if Ultracor has not replaced counsel, the Motion to Relieve its former counsel was almost a year ago. Ultracor is responsible for responding to discovery even if it does not have counsel. As such, this Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ motion and deems the Requests in the RFA set to be deemed admitted.  

 

            C. Sanctions

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2023.010(d)). 

 

            Along with Plaintiffs’ Motion to Deem Requests for Admission, Set Two, as admitted, Plaintiffs also included a Request for Monetary Sanctions in the amount of $936.25. This amount is based on counsel for Plaintiffs, Rosa Kwong’s hourly rate of $250, spending 2.5 hours preparing Plaintiffs, a projected 0.5 hours to review the opposition to the motion, and prepare the reply papers, and 0.5 hours to attend and participate in the hearing as well as $61.25 in filing fees. Here, the Court notes that it finds the amount of hours and the hourly rate to be reasonable. However, this Court also notes that no opposition has been filed and thus, the 0.5 hours allotted to analyzing the opposition brief and reply papers was not utilized as projected. Thus, the Court would be inclined to grant the Request for Monetary Sanctions in the lowered amount of $811.25. However, this Court notes that the granting of this motion is devastating to Defendant, Ultracor’s claim. The Requests for Admission that this Court is tentatively inclined to deem as admitted are nearly tantamount to admitting Ultracor’s liability, and thus this Court denies this request for monetary sanctions, without prejudice to be reasserted if and when Ultracor seeks relief from the deemed admissions.   

¿ 

III. CONCLUSION  

¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiffs’ Motions to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted is GRANTED. Requests for Monetary Sanctions are Denied without prejudice.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 May 31, 2024¿¿ 

¿¿ ¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                   23TRCV00974

¿¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Giuseppe Sanzone; Caroline Petersen v. FCA US LLC, et al.

¿¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                 (1) Plaintiffs, Giuseppe Sanzone; Caroline Petersen

¿¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        (1) Defendants, FCA US LLC

 

TRIAL DATE:                        May 27, 2025¿ 

MOTION:¿                           (1) Motion for Additional Sanctions

¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                  (1) ARGUE

 

 

¿¿ 

 

¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

On April 3, 2023, Plaintiffs, Giuseppe Sanzone and Caroline Petersen (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, FCA US LLC, and DOES 1 through 10. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act – Breach of § 1793.2(b); and (4) Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200.

 

Plaintiffs’ motion in this case notes that on August 11, 2023, they served on Defendant, Requests for Production of Documents, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Admission, and Form Interrogatories, Set One. Despite this, Plaintiffs contend that as of the filing of this motion, Plaintiffs had not received any of Defendant’s responses. Plaintiffs even contend that although not required prior to filing a motion to compel initial responses, on October 24, 2023, Plaintiffs attempted to meet and confer with defense counsel, requesting verified substantive responses before filing this motion. However, Plaintiffs assert that despite multiple follow up attempts, Defendant has failed to provide any substantive responses to the discovery. As a result, Plaintiff brought a Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Admission, and Form Interrogatories Set One.

 

On January 16, 2024, the Court GRANTED the motion, except that the Court DENIED the motion for to deem the requests for admission as admitted. The Court ordered FCA to provide verified amended or further responses, without objection, including responses to the RFAs within twenty (20) days. This Court also awarded Plaintiff sanctions in the amount of $2,500 to be paid by Defendant FCA to Plaintiffs’ counsel, within 20 days, with the sanctions ordered payable by defense counsel’s office given the declaration of attorney neglect explaining why the responses were not prepared until after service of the subject motion. These responses and sanctions were due to Plaintiff’s counsel no later than February 5, 2024. Plaintiff also contends that Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to meet and confer with Defendant to avoid this continuous cycle of motions to no avail, but that as of the date of Plaintiff’s filing of this motion, no verified discovery responses had been served by Defendant, and no sanctions paid.

 

On March 29, 2024, Plaintiff brought a Motion for Terminating Sanctions based on Defendant’s failure to comply with the Court’s Order. This Court DENIED that Motion for Terminating Sanctions but GRANTED Monetary Sanctions in the amount of $2,500 payable to counsel for the Plaintiff by April 19, 2024.

 

However, Plaintiffs now bring this Motion for Additional Monetary Sanctions as they assert that Defendant has once again failed to comply with this Court’s order. As of the date of the filing of this motion, Plaintiffs assert that Defendant has not paid any of the sanctions ordered by this Court’s March 29, 2024 order. Plaintiffs contend that they believe monetary sanctions are ineffective at compelling Defendant’s adherence to this Court’s orders, and thus under the concept of progressive discipline the Court should impose stiffer or additional sanctions to secure compliance with the Discovery Act and with the Court’s discovery orders.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On April 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion for Additional Monetary Sanctions. On May 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a notice of non-opposition to Plaintiffs’ motion.  Also on May 22, 2024, less than two hours after Plaintiff’s statement of non-opposition, Defendants’ counsel filed a tardy opposition brief, which the Court elects to consider on the merits.  It was five days late, 3 court days late.

 

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply brief.

 

¿II. ANALYSIS¿ 

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

 If a party fails to comply with a court order compelling discovery responses or attendance at a deposition, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions. Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450, subd. (h) (depositions); § 2030.290, subd. (c) (interrogatories); § 2031.300, subd. (c) (demands for production of documents); § 2033.290, subd. (e) (requests for admission). Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . .  [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . .” Section 2023.010 provides that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery. . .”  

  

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390, quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 [citation omitted].)    

    

“Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.” (Id., citing Lang, supra, 77 Cal. App. 4th at pp. 1244-1246 [discussing cases]; see, e.g., Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1617-1622 [terminating sanctions imposed after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery]; Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 481, 491, disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCutchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, n. 4 [terminating sanctions imposed against plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes].)  

 

Discussion

 

            Preliminarily, this Court notes that Defendant’s opposition brief is untimely. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1005(b), “[a]ll papers opposing a motion so noticed shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) Here, Defendant’s opposition papers were filed nine days before this hearing, including non-court days, which was thus five days late.  Despite this, the Court uses its discretion to review the opposition brief and consider it on its (tardy) merits.  On the merits, it is apparent why the opposition papers were late: defense counsel did not take whatever steps were necessary to ensure compliance with the Court’s very clear order on the deadline for payment of the monetary sanctions.  Ms. Dao’s declaration and opposition brief urge the Court to find that defense counsel’s failure to comply with a Court Order is mooted by the late payment of the earlier-ordered monetary sanctions.  One full moth late.  Perhaps before the hearing defense counsel will take a more conciliatory stance given the record before the Court. 

 

            The chronology of events on this motion is as follows: On March 29, 2024, this Court ordered that Defendant pay $2,500 in monetary sanctions to Chad David, counsel for Plaintiff by April 19, 2024.  The Court even had defense counsel make an entry in counsel’s personal calendar to ensure no law firm calendaring snafu might cause that deadline to be missed.  But it was.  Per the Opposition brief, the monetary sanctions were not paid until May 20, a month late.  When Defendant failed to pay the Court-ordered monetary sanctions, which is a failure to obey a court order, Plaintiffs brought a Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Request for Additional Monetary Sanctions on April 30, 2024.   As of service of the instant motion on defense counsel, the monetary sanctions were already nearly two weeks overdue.  It was not until three more weeks later that Defense counsel paid Plaintiff’s counsel. As such, this Court will not deny Plaintiffs’ motion outright as Defendant has requested in its opposition brief.

 

            In this Court’s March 29, 2024 minute order, it CLEARLY indicated that although at the last hearing, Defendant’s abuse of the discovery process was not enough to warrant terminating sanctions, the conduct of Defendant or defense counsel may warrant more serious sanctions in the future. Despite this Court’s explicit warning from the Court, Defendant nonetheless failed to abide by this Court’s March 29, 2024 order. The excuse provided is, quite simply, not acceptable.  Defense Counsel is blaming the failure to abide by this Court’s order on the asserted fact that Defense Counsel’s office department that processes checks was allegedly backlogged. No law firm could effectively operate if its check-processing department were backed up for an entire month. 

 

           

The Court is aware that law firms handling high-volume litigation like Song-Beverly Act cases creates pressures especially on junior lawyers.  Often the dockets for junior lawyers can be daunting or even overwhelming, and the Court makes allowances for such things when deadlines are missed and delays occur.  To have opposing counsel calling for severe sanctions against lawyers or clients adds to the pressures.  But at the same time, even junior lawyers must accept responsibility for tracking deadlines, managing dockets, and complying with orders especially once a prior mistake or neglect has been the subject of law and motion activity in a pending litigated case.  Missing deadlines is big deal, but can often be fixed.  Missing deadlines imposed by a judge is a bigger deal.  The Court is not sure that this has been acknowledged by the Opposition.  Plaintiffs here argue that monetary sanctions have not been effective in securing Defendant’s compliance with this Court’s lawful orders.  The Court is not sure that argument is accurate, which is why the Court did not consider it proper to punish the client with something like a terminating sanction for the misconduct of counsel.  But it would be accurate to argue that monetary sanctions have not been effective in securing the timeliness of defense counsel’s compliance with court orders including an order to pay monetary sanctions by a date certain. 

 

The Court’s tentative is to allow defense counsel to ARGUE the appropriate judicial response to this unfortunate chronology of events.  The Court’s intention is to allow defense counsel, whether Ms. Dao or someone else from her office, to propose to the Court a proper judicial response to the rather astonishing set of facts that includes an entire month’s delay in payment of Court-ordered monetary sanctions with no justification or excuse offered besides a backlog in the law firm’s accounting department.  It is not surprising that Plaintiff’s counsel would bring a further sanctions motion after the payment deadline passed.  Whether defense counsel will offer to defray some, most or all of the legal fees Plaintiffs expended in bringing this motion, or compose a letter of apology, and / or some other option, the Court will put this matter on the second call of the docket so as to avoid the need for other counsel in other cases to hear the substance of the argument.

I. BACKGROUND¿ 

¿ 

A. Factual¿ 

           

            On October 17, 2023, Plaintiff, Citibank, N.A. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, Karen S. Okihara (“Defendant”) alleging a cause of action for: (1) Common Counts. Plaintiff alleges that it lent money in the amount of $25,036.04 to Defendant and that Defendant still owes this amount in unpaid debt despite Plaintiff’s demand.

 

            On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with Requests for Admission. Plaintiff contends that Defendant has failed to respond to discovery by the date it was due, and has not served any responses even as of the date it filed this motion, or per representations of counsel at the recent Case Management Conference, even as of early May of 2024. As such, Plaintiff has brought this Motion to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted.

 

B. Procedural  

 

            On May 2, 2024, Defendant filed a Motion to Deem the Truth of the Matters Specified in  that Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted. To date, no opposition has been filed.

 

¿II. ANALYSIS 

 

A. Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.280(c), the court shall make the order deeming the truth of the matters admitted unless the responding party serves before the hearing a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Code Civ. Proc § 2033.220. Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.220 requires that each answer either admits, denies or specifies that the responding party lacks sufficient information or knowledge.   The moving party is not required to meet and confer before bringing such a motion where no response of any kind was received. (Demyer v. Costa Mesa Mobile Home Estates, 36 Cal.App.4th 393, 395.)¿¿ 

 

            B. Discussion 

 

Plaintiff’s moving papers indicate that on March 19, 2024, it served its Requests for Admission, Set One on Defendant. However, Plaintiff contends that Defendant has failed to serve any objections, admissions, denials, or any other written responses. Because of this, the Court’s tentative ruling is to GRANT Defendant’s motion. The Court also notes that Defendant is represented by counsel per the single-page Answer filed by said counsel on February 22, 2024.  Despite this, Defendant failed to respond to the responses as well as failed to respond via opposition to this motion. However, Plaintiff has attached the Proof of Service of the Request on Defendant’s attorney. As of the day before the hearing, the Court has no evidence of any response to the RFAs nor the motion.  Thus, this Court GRANTS this motion and deems the subject RFA to be admitted.

III. CONCLUSION  

¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motions to Deem Requests for Admission as Admitted is GRANTED.
