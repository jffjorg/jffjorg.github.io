Case Number: 18PSCV00182    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: G

Motion by Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc. for Terminating Sanction of Default Judgment Against Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, Jenny Liu, Sunny Way Trucking Inc. or in the Alternative Issue Sanctions and Request for Monetary Sanctions

Respondent: Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, Juan Liu, and Sunny Way Trucking Inc.

Motion by Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc. or Leave of Court to Amend the Complaint

Respondent: Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, Juan Liu, and Sunny Way Trucking Inc.

Motion by Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc. to Permit Belated Service of Expert Information and To Allow Plaintiffs’ Experts to Testify at Trial

Respondent: Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, Juan Liu, Sunny Way Trucking Inc., and Sunny Way Corporation

TENTATIVE RULING

Motion by Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc. for Terminating Sanction of Default Judgment Against Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, Jenny Liu, Sunny Way Trucking Inc. or in the Alternative Issue Sanctions and Request for Monetary Sanctions is DENIED.

Motion by Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc. for Leave of Court to Amend the Complaint is DENIED.

Motion by Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma, Yunhan Chen, and Topstar Freight Inc. to Permit Belated Service of Expert Information and To Allow Plaintiffs’ Experts to Testify at Trial is DEEMED MOOT.

BACKGROUND

This is an action for contractual fraud. Plaintiffs Lingzhi Ma and Yunhan Chen are Chinese nationals. In 2015, they entered into an agreement with Defendants Xuefei Zhang, Lihong Liu, and Juan Liu in which they agreed to form and invest funds in Plaintiff Topstar Freight Inc. (Topstar) for the purpose of obtaining an EB-5 investor visa for Chen. Pursuant to the agreement, Ma and Chen invested $800,000 in Topstar. Subsequently, Ma and Chen allegedly discovered Zhang and the Lius had managed Topstar for their own benefit and were transferring Topstar’s profits to Defendant Sunny Way Trucking Inc. (Sunny Way Trucking). They also allege Zhang and the Lius refused to return their investments.

On December 12, 2018, Ma, Chen, and Topstar (collectively Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Zhang, the Lius, Sunny Way Trucking, and Does 1-20, alleging the following causes of action: (1) anticipatory breach of contract, (2) money had and received, (3) conversion, (4) breach of contract, (5) money had and received, (6) conversion, (7) intentional misrepresentation, (8) negligent misrepresentation, (9) fraudulent concealment, (10) breach of fiduciary duty, (11) constructive fraud, (12) unfair competition, and (13) declaratory relief.

On July 7, 2021, Plaintiffs filed an amendment to the Complaint that replaced Doe 4 with Defendant Sunny Way Corporation (Sunny Way).

On May 28, 2024, the court stayed the present action following notice of pending bankruptcy proceedings that involved Sunny Way Trucking.

On October 4, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the present motions for sanctions and leave to amend. On October 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the present motion to permit the use of belatedly served expert information.

A hearing on the present motions is set for October 24, 2024, along with a hearing on a motion to be admitted pro hac vice and a final status conference/hearing on lifting of bankruptcy stay. A non-jury trial is also set to commence on November 4, 2024.

ANALYSIS

Motion for Terminating or Issue Sanctions

Plaintiffs move for terminating or issue sanctions against Zhang, the Lius, Sunny Way, and Sunny Way Trucking (collectively Defendants) for Defendants’ alleged failure to comply with the court’s discovery orders on October 10, 2019, and December 19, 2019. For the following reasons, the court DENIES their motion.

Legal Standard

The court may impose a monetary sanction, issue sanction, evidence sanction, terminating sanction, or contempt sanction on a party who engages in conduct that misuses the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d), (g).) Because the purpose of sanctions is remedial and not punitive, sanctions should “serve to remedy the harm caused to the party suffering the discovery misconduct.” (Kwan Software Engineering, Inc. v. Hennings (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 57, 74.) “[A]bsent unusual circumstances, nonmonetary sanctions are¿warranted only if a party willfully fails to comply with a court order.” (Aghaian v. Minassian (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 603, 618-619.) Terminating sanctions should only be ordered when there has been previous noncompliance and it appears a less severe sanction would not be effective. (Link v. Cater (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1326.)

Discussion

As an initial matter, Plaintiffs and Defendants dispute whether the present motion was timely brought. (Motion, p. 6:22-9:4; Opp., p. 1:10-28.) The deadline for discovery motions to be heard is fifteen days before the initial trial date and a continuance or postponement of the trial does not extend this deadline unless parties have agreed so. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020, 2024.060.) Nonetheless, the court has discretion to consider discovery motions on a later date by taking into account the following factors: “(1) [t]he necessity and the reasons for the discovery[;] (2) [t]he diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier[;] (3) [a]ny likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party[; and] (4) [t]he length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050, subd. (a), (b).)

This action was initially set for trial on March 23, 2021. (02/25/2020 Minute Order.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020, the last date the present motion could have been heard was March 8, 2021. While the initial trial date of March 23 was repeatedly continued pursuant to stipulations signed by the parties, these extensions did not extend the deadline for the present motion to be heard without the parties’ agreement. In bringing the present motion, Plaintiffs fail to point to any stipulation or agreement to extend the discovery motion deadline that renders the present motion timely. Accordingly, the present motion may only be heard if Plaintiffs first obtained leave pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050, subdivision (a). Plaintiffs, however, failed to do so and instead proceeded with the present motion, disregarding these requirements. (See Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1587, 1587 & fn. 26 [noting plaintiff could combine a motion to compel with a motion to reopen discovery].)

To the extent Plaintiffs may claim the present motion includes a motion to extend the discovery motion deadline, it does not. Nowhere in the notice of the present motion does it mention a request to extend the discovery motion deadline or a reference to Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050. Even if the present motion is generously interpreted to include a motion to extend the deadline, it fails to adequately address the factors outlined in Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050, subdivision (b). Specifically, it does not demonstrate how Plaintiffs were diligent in obtaining discovery from Defendants and why they delayed filing the present motion until now.

On October 10, 2019, the court ordered Zhang, the Lius, and Sunny Way Trucking to provide verified responses to Chen’s first set of form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for production of documents by October 30, 2019. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 28-29; Appendix of Exhibits (AOE), Ex. 17.) Notably, the court’s ruling was based on motions that were only brought by one of the three plaintiffs, Chen, and did not include Sunny Way. While Defendants failed to provide responses to the special interrogatories, counsel states Defendants’ counsel agreed to provide all accounting data and reports for Sunny Way Trucking and Topstar by October 29, 2019. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 30, 83.) Plaintiff’s counsel asserts that Defendants subsequently failed to do so. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 34.)

On December 19, 2019, the court ordered Defendants to provide verified responses to Chen’s second set of requests for production of documents by January 8, 2020. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 39; AEO, Ex. 25.) Notably, this ruling was also only based on a motion brough by Chen and did not include Sunny Way. While Defendants served Plaintiffs with documents on January 8, 2020, Plaintiffs’ counsel states the production was scant, lacked bates stamps, and failed to identify the  requests to which the documents were responsive. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 43-44.)

In August 2021, Plaintiffs’ counsel sent a letter to Defendants’ counsel that admonished them from continuing to withhold documents, including Topstar’s transportation contracts, client lists, personnel and payroll records, banking records, vehicle and container information, location records, ownership records, registration records, licensing records, repair records, and maintenance records as well as lists of Topstar’s assets, receivables, debts, and liabilities. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 49.) In June 2023, Plaintiffs’ counsel “began reviewing the voluminous pleadings, correspondence, and bank records received through subpoena.” (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 53.) On January 31, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel emailed Defendants’ counsel stating the parties needed to complete discovery and noting Defendants had failed to turnover any meaningful business records. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 55.) Specifically, Plaintiffs’ counsel noted Defendants had failed to provide employee information and returns. (AEO, Ex. 31.) Plaintiff’s counsel also warned that Plaintiffs would be seeking issue sanctions prior to trial. (AEO, Ex. 31.)

On April 11, 2024, the court reset the trial date from July 2, 2024, to June 10, 2024. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 57.) On April 23, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel served deposition notices on Zhang and Lihong Liu that set depositions for May 2, 2024, and May 3, 2024. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 58.) The deposition notices also requested production of documents that had been previously requested in Chen’s two sets of requests for production of documents. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 58-61.) By the date of the first deposition on May 2, 2024, Defendants failed to produce any documents. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 64.) After Defendants’ counsel represented that Defendants had the documents which were “not in a form for electronic transmission,” Plaintiffs’ counsel agreed to continue Zhang’s deposition to May 8, 2024, and Lihong Liu’s deposition to May 9, 2024, in order for Defendants’ counsel to provide the documents for Plaintiffs’ counsel. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 65.)

On May 7, 2024, Defendants’ counsel began emailing documents to Plaintiffs’ counsel that were not bates stamped, lacked verified responses that identified which documents were responsive to which requests, and appeared to mostly consist of bank records that Defendants had already previously provided. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 68-70.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states outstanding documents that Defendants have failed to provide include Topstar’s employment records, contracts or invoices for transactions between Topstar and Sunny Way, fuel card statements, Topstar’s financial records, documentation of Sunny Way’s payment for Topstar drivers, insurance documents, and repair receipts. (Ensberg Decl., ¶ 71.)

But despite this noncompliance by Defendants, Plaintiffs’ counsel never sought to bring a motion to compel or for motion for sanctions during the period from May 7, 2024, to May 28, 2024, when this action was automatically stayed by bankruptcy proceedings. Instead, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed a motion in limine to preclude Defendants from contradicting admissions that were previously deemed admitted, a brief to permit the testimony of Plaintiffs’ expert at trial, and trial documents including a joint statement of the case, a joint witness list, a notice of waiver of jury trial, a trial brief, an exhibit list, and a special verdict form. However, the court finds that Plaintiffs’ claim that the extreme remedy of terminating or issue sanctions is warranted because Plaintiffs were denied key documents is directly contradicted by the fact that Plaintiffs were initially ready to proceed to trial without them.

Furthermore, Plaintiffs do not point to any attempt to compel further responses from Defendants after Defendants’ initial responses to the court’s 2019 orders were deemed incomplete. Instead, Plaintiffs’ counsel waited until the proverbial eleventh hour to attempt to obtain these documents via deposition notices served in April 2024 and set in May 2024 for a trial that was set to begin in June. And while the June trial date had been advanced by twenty-two days, this extra time does not excuse Plaintiffs’ delay since Plaintiffs’ current counsel was aware of the outstanding discovery issues as early as June 2023 and even sent correspondence to Defendants’ counsel regarding the missing discovery in January 2024.

Plaintiffs fail to provide the court with an explanation as to why Plaintiffs’ counsel waited until the last minute to start noticing depositions and seeking the additional discovery. Accordingly, the court finds Plaintiffs were dilatory in seeking discovery from Defendants and failed to explain why they waited to seek terminating sanctions until the eve of trial.

Accordingly, the court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion.

Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint

Plaintiffs move for leave to file a First Amended Complaint (FAC) that adds a cause of action for civil conspiracy. For the following reasons, the court DENIES their motion.

Legal Standard

“A court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) The court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendments of the pleadings. (Nestle v. City of Santa Monica (1972) 6 Cal.3d 920, 939.) “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)  The motion must also be supported by a declaration that specifies the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)

If the party seeking the amendment has needlessly delayed, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.  (See Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.)  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.  (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)

Discussion

Plaintiffs seek to add a cause of action for civil conspiracy that alleges Zhang and the Lius worked in agreement to entice Ma into making an investment through Chen. The declaration of Plaintiffs’ counsel, however, fails to specify why this amendment is necessary and proper as required by California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b).

This amendment is not proper since conspiracy is not a separate cause of action. (Applied Equipment Corp. v. Litton Saudi Arabia Ltd. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 503, 510-511.) Nor is this amendment necessary as the operative Complaint already alleges Defendants conspired to defraud Plaintiffs of their investment. (Complaint, ¶ 60, 74.)

Accordingly, the court DENIES their motion.

Motion to Permit Belated Service of Expert Information and to Permit Expert Testimony at Trial

Plaintiffs move for an order permitting the testimony of their expert witnesses notwithstanding their failure to timely serve expert witness disclosures. For the following reasons, the court finds Plaintiffs’ motion is moot.

Legal Standard

“On motion of any party who has failed to submit expert witness information on the date specified in a demand for that exchange, the court may grant leave to submit that information at a later date.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.710, subd. (a).) The motion “shall be made a sufficient time in advance of the time limit for the completion of discovery under Chapter 8 (commencing with Section 2024.010) to permit the deposition of any expert to whom the motion relates to be taken within that time limit. Under exceptional circumstances, the court may permit the motion to be made at a later time.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.710, subd. (b).) The motion must include a meet-and-confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.710, subd. (c).)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.720, “[t]he court shall grant leave to submit tardy expert witness information only if all of the following conditions are satisfied:

“(a) The court has taken into account the extent to which the opposing party has relied on the absence of a list of expert witnesses.

(b) The court has determined that any party opposing the motion will not be prejudiced in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.

(c) The court has determined that the moving party did all of the following:

(1) Failed to submit the information as the result of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.

(2) Sought leave to submit the information promptly after learning of the mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.

(3) Promptly thereafter served a copy of the proposed expert witness information described in Section 2034.260 on all other parties who have appeared in the action.

(d) The order is conditioned on the moving party making the expert available immediately for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410), and on any other terms as may be just, including, but not limited to, leave to any party opposing the motion to designate additional expert witnesses or to elicit additional opinions from those previously designated, a continuance of the trial for a reasonable period of time, and the awarding of costs and litigation expenses to any party opposing the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.720, subd. (a)-(d).)

Discussion

As an initial matter, Plaintiffs and Defendants dispute whether Defendants’ demand for the exchange of expert witness information and reports was timely. (Motion, p. 11:11-18; Sunny Way Opp., p. 3:14-19.) A party “may make a demand for an exchange of information concerning expert trial witnesses without leave of court. . . . no later than the 10th day after the initial trial date has been set, or 70 days before that trial date, whichever is closer to the trial date.” (Emphasis added; Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.220.)

On April 5, 2024, Sunny Way served a demand for expert exchange on Plaintiffs’ counsel with a response date of April 25, 2024. (Motion, p. 11:11-12; Morrow Decl., ¶ 7-9.) Plaintiffs argue this demand was untimely because it was not served between April 11, 2024, and April 21, 2024. Plaintiffs’ calculation, however, is inaccurate as the deadline is not calculated from the time the current trial or most recent trial was set. The date is calculated from the initial or first trial date, which was set for March 23, 2021, via minute order on February 25, 2020. (02/25/2020 Minute Order.)

Thus, Sunny Way’s demand was untimely. While Sunny Way argues the court has wide discretion to grant a party leave to serve discovery requests after the discovery cutoff, Sunny Way failed to move for such relief. Further, the court notes that the Discovery Act is silent as to the grounds upon which a party may seek relief for a late demand.  At a minimum, Sunny Way could have sought relief under Code Civ. Proc., §473(b).  It did not.  

Because there was no timely demand for expert exchange by Sunny Way, the court finds Sunny Way has no proper grounds upon which to object to Plaintiffs’ compliance with that demand.

Accordingly, the court deems MOOT Plaintiffs’ motion.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the court makes the following rulings:

Plaintiffs’ motion for terminating or issue sanctions is DENIED.

Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to amend is DENIED.

Plaintiffs’ motion for an order permitting belatedly served expert information at trial is DEEMED MOOT.
