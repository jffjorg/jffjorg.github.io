DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.   

Case Number: 21STCV45417    Hearing Date: October 21, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

BLANCA CABRERA, 

 

         vs.

 

NISSAN NORTH AMERICA, INC.

 Case No.:  21STCV45417

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 21, 2024

 

Plaintiff Blanca Cabrera’s motion for reconsideration is denied.

The Court sua sponte sets a hearing to reconsider Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration for Wednesday, December 11, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  Parties are to submit simultaneous briefs on November 15, 2024, regarding Kielar v. Superior Court of Placer County (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, Yeh v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 264, Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, Montemayor v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th, and Davis v. Nissan North America Inc. (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 825.

 

Plaintiff Blanca Cabrera (“Cabrera”) (“Plaintiff”) moves for this Court to reconsider, reverse, or vacate its March 6, 2023, order (“3/6/23 Ruling”) granting Defendant Nissa North America, Inc.’s (“Nissan”) (“Defendant”) motion to compel arbitration and stay proceedings.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2; C.C.P. §1008(c).) 

 

Procedural Background

          On December 14, 2021, Plaintiff filed her operative complaint against Defendant alleging five causes of action: (1) violation of Civil Code §§1793.2(D); (2) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(B); (3) violation of Civil Code §1793.2(A)(3); (4) breach of implied warranty of merchantability (Civ. Code §§1791.1, 1794, 1795.5); and (5) fraudulent inducement- concealment, arising from Plaintiff’s purchase of a 2019 Nissan Altima (“Subject Vehicle”).

          On March 6, 2023, this Court granted Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims.  (3/6/23 Ruling.)

          Plaintiff filed the instant motion on July 11, 2024.  Defendant filed its opposition on October 8, 2024.  Plaintiff filed her reply on October 14, 2024. 

 

Motion for Reconsideration

C.C.P. §1008(a) provides, as follows:  “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”  (C.C.P. §1008(a), emphasis added.)

Defendant’s objection to Plaintiff’s motion as untimely because it was not filed within 10 days of notice of entry of the 3/6/23 Ruling is unavailing.  In Malek v. Blue Cross of California (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 44, the Court of Appeal determined that a change in the law required the trial court to reconsider its prior ruling compelling arbitration.  (Id. at pgs. 59-60.)  The Malek Court stated, “the progress of the arbitration is not material when considering a change in the law affecting whether the arbitral forum was a correct one.”  (Id. at pg. 60.) 

“If a court believes one of its prior interim orders was erroneous, it should be able to correct that error no matter how it came to acquire that belief. For example, nothing would prevent the losing party from asking the court at a status conference to reconsider a ruling.  (See Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial, supra, § 9:327.8, p. 9(1)–107.)  But a party may not file a written motion to reconsider that has procedural significance if it does not satisfy the requirements of section 437c, subdivision (f)(2), or 1008. The court need not rule on any suggestion that it should reconsider a previous ruling and, without more, another party would not be expected to respond to such a suggestion.”  (Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1108, as modified (June 10, 2005).) 

          Plaintiff requests this Court reconsider its 3/6/23 Ruling in light of Kielar v. Superior Court of Placer County (2023) 94 Cal.App.5th 614, Yeh v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 264, Ford Motor Warranty Cases (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324, Montemayor v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th, and Davis v. Nissan North America Inc. (2024) 100 Cal.App.5th 825, which distinguish and disagree with Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486.

Here, the Court has been put on notice of new case law that disagrees with Felisilda, the case on which this Court relied to grant Defendant’s 3/6/23 Ruling.  Accordingly, the Court will sua sponte reconsider its Order.

Parties are to submit simultaneous briefs on November 15, 2023, regarding the new case law that disagrees with Felisilda.  The parties are also to address any prejudice caused by the timing of this motion. 

Accordingly, the Court sua sponte sets a hearing to reconsider Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration for December 11, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied.

The Court sua sponte sets a hearing to reconsider Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration for Wednesday, December 11, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  Parties are to submit simultaneous briefs on November 15, 2024.

1.     Plaintiff’s Motion to Disqualify Latham & Watkins

Background

Plaintiff filed the instant motion on July 8, 2024.  Defendant filed its opposition on October 8, 2024.  Plaintiff filed his reply on October 14, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

“Before an attorney may be disqualified from representing a party in litigation because his representation of that party is adverse to the interest of a current or former client, it must first be established that the party seeking the attorney’s disqualification was or is ‘represented’ by the attorney in a manner giving rise to an attorney-client relationship.”  (Shen v. Miller (2012) 212 Cal.App.4th 48, 56, citing Koo v. Rubio’s Restaurants, Inc. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 719, 729; id. n.3 [“Generally, before the disqualification of an attorney is proper, the complaining party must have or have had an attorney-client relationship with that attorney.”].)  Plaintiff bears the burden to establish that an attorney-client relationship existed.  (See Koo, 109 Cal.App.4th at pg. 729.)

The general rule is that an attorney’s client is the corporate entity, and individuals within an organization “cannot presume that corporate counsel is protecting their interests.”  (Sprengel v. Zbylut (2019) 40 Cal.App.5th 1028, 1042, citing La Jolla Cove Motel & Hotel Apartments, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 773, 784.)

“An attorney-client relationship is not created by the unilateral declaration of one party to the relationship . . .. Rather, the relationship can only be created by contract, express or implied.”  (Koo, 109 Cal.App.4th at pg. 729, citations omitted.)  “An implied contract is one, the existence and terms of which are manifested by conduct.”  (Responsible Citizens v. Superior Court (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 1717, 1732, quoting Civ. Code §1621).  Courts will thus consider the “totality of the circumstances” in determining whether an attorney-client relationship exists.  (Koo, 109 Cal.App.4th at pg. 732.)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff’s motion to disqualify Latham is denied.

Plaintiff fails to meet his burden to establish that the has ever had an attorney-client relationship with Latham.  First, Plaintiff fails to demonstrate that he had an express, written agreement with Latham.  (See Decl. of Ellison ¶7; Bus. & Prof. Code §6148(a).) 

Second, Plaintiff fails to demonstrate he had an implied contract with Latham for legal services.  In re Marriage of Zimmerman (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 556, 560, an appellant claimed she had spoken for about twenty minutes with an attorney during which she purportedly “‘outlined’ the case for [the attorney] by providing him with all ‘pertinent’ information.”  (Zimmerman, 16 Cal.App.4th at pg. 565.)  After the attorney joined a firm that was adverse to appellant in a pending lawsuit, the appellant moved to disqualify the attorney. The Zimmerman Court affirmed that the attorney’s brief conversation with the appellant was insufficient to disqualify the attorney or his new firm.  (Id. at pgs. 564-565 [“While [the attorney] may have offered appellant his initial impressions of the case, he obviously was not called upon to formulate a legal strategy and, by the very limited nature of his contact with appellant, could not have gained detailed knowledge of the pertinent facts and legal principles]”.)

Here, like in Zimmerman, Plaintiff’s email correspondence with Joe Calabrese (“Calabrese”) on March 18, 2021, to inquire about suing various outlets for defamation is insufficient to establish an attorney-client relationship.  (See Decl. of Asi ¶3, Exh. 3.)  In his response to Plaintiff’s email, Calabrese explicitly states, “Copying in Rob and Marvin, who are best suited to answer your question on litigation.”  (Decl. of Asi ¶3, Exh. 3.)  Calabrese’s response to Plaintiff contains no legal advice on defamation or litigation and merely offers sympathy to Plaintiff’s predicament.  (Id. [“From a purely human-standpoint, I am shocked at the tone.”].)  Plaintiff’s declaration that he followed up his email conversation with Calabrese on an unspecified date, wherein Calabrese advised Plaintiff that there was no legal remedy to protect him from the defamatory information about him in the press, and that Ralph Ellison (“Ellison”) would reach out to him for further advice is not sufficient evidence alone to support an implied attorney-client relationship.  (See Decl. of Asi ¶4.)  Plaintiff’s evidence in support his argument only demonstrates his communications, like in Zimmerman, were “of a preliminary and peripheral nature.”  (Zimmerman, 16 Cal.App.4th at pg. 565.)  

Third, Plaintiff’s argument that Ellison’s electronic invite to Plaintiff on or about March 30, 2022, and his conversation with Ellison along with other members of the Hollywood Foreign Press Association obviates a reasonable belief of the formation of an attorney-client relationship because of the presence of other members on the invitation and in the group meeting.  (See Decl. of Asi ¶5.)  Further, Plaintiff’s argument is contradicted by Ellison’s declaration stating that he provided the participants with a warning, expressly advising them that Latham represented Defendant.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶5.)

Finally, Plaintiff’s declaration that during his first meet and confer with Ellison on May 2, 2024, Ellison told him he should be suing The Wrap and The LA Times rather than Defendant does not constitute legal advice.  Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet his burden that Latham previously represented him, necessitating Latham’s disqualification as Defendant’s counsel.

          Plaintiff’s argument that Latham’s possession and use of his privileged emails further demonstrates the need for Latham’s disqualification is also unavailing.

          When an attorney comes into possession of what appears to be another

party’s potentially privileged communications, the attorney is permitted to review only as much as is necessary to make that determination and must then to cease review and notify opposing counsel immediately.  (See State Compensation Insurance Fund v. WPS, Inc. (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 644, 656-657.)

          Here, after the HFPA was able to recover the approximately 31,000 emails that were deleted from Plaintiff’s HFPA account, Latham reviewed a metadata report of the emails.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶9-10.)  In reviewing the metadata, Latham observed that some of the recovered emails were to or from Plaintiff’s former counsel.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶9-10.)  Latham refrained from reviewing any of these communications, sequestered them from review, and immediately informed Plaintiff’s then-counsel.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶10, Exh. E.)

          Moreover, on October 11, 2022, Plaintiff’s former counsel produced to Latham a full back up of Plaintiff’s HFPA email account, and the backup contained additional communications that Defendant did not recover with the initial 31,000 emails.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶14.)

          As a matter of law, any of the emails Plaintiff sent using his HFPA email account waived Plaintiff’s protections afforded by the attorney-client privilege.  (See Holmes v. Petrovich Development Co. (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1068-1071 [holding, in a subsequent lawsuit against her employer, that employee waived privilege over emails sent from her employer’s email account to her attorneys because she did not have reasonable expectation of privacy].)  Plaintiff’s knowledge that Defendant controls his HFPA email account does not create a reasonable expectation that Plaintiff’s emails would not be readily accessible to the Defendant.  (See id. [concluding that emails sent via employer’s email account were “akin to consulting her lawyer in her employer’s conference room, in a loud voice, with the door open, so that any reasonable person would expect that their discussion of her complaints about her employer would be overheard by him”].)  Therefore, Defendant’s conduct related to Plaintiff’s email communications with his former attorney is not grounds for Latham’s disqualification.

          Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is denied.

 

Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion to disqualify Latham as Defendant’s counsel is denied.

Legal Standard

“The party claiming the privilege has the burden of establishing the preliminary facts necessary to support its exercise, i.e., a communication made in the course of an attorney-client relationship.”  (Costco Wholesale Corp. v. Superior Court (2009) 219 Cal.4th 725, 733.)  This is done by providing a privilege log, which contains sufficient information for judicial evaluation of a claim of privilege.  (Best Products, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1188.)  “Once that party establishes facts necessary to support a prima facie claim of privilege, the communication is presumed to have been made in confidence and the opponent of the claim of privilege has the burden of proof to establish the communication was not confidential or that the privilege does not for other reasons apply.”  (Costco Wholesale Corp., 219 Cal.4th at pg. 733.)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses from Defendant as to RFP 1 is denied.  Defendant produced a detailed privilege log, which demonstrates that the documents withheld on the basis of privilege are communications made in the course of an attorney-client relationship, either with outside counsel or the Defendant’s COO and General Counsel, Greg Goeckner.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶7, 9, Exh. 5.)

Here, Defendant’s privilege log states the withheld documents.  The privilege log denotes the individuals who are attorneys with an asterisk on the log itself.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶7, 9, Exh. 5.)  Each entry in the “Description” field explains that the communication is one which involves “providing legal advice,” “requesting legal advice,” “memorializing legal advice,” or otherwise provides an even more specific description of the attorney function being performed in that specific communication.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶7, 9, Exh. 5.)  Defendant’s privilege log provides sufficient factual information for Plaintiff and this Court to evaluate the merits of that claim.  (Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1123 [“Even a minimal statement . . . is sufficient.”].)

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses is denied.

 

          Sanctions

          In light of the Court’s ruling on the instant motion, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied.

 

Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to his RFP 1 is denied.

Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is denied.

6.     Defendant’s Motion to Quash

On July 24, 2024, Plaintiff initially moved to quash or stay the deposition of Gabriel Lerman (“Lerman”) and for an order to bar Plaintiff from directly contacting or communicating with Defendant or any of its Board Members, including Lerman, while this action is pending.  (Notice Quash, pg. 2; C.C.P. §128(a), 2019.020, 2025.410, 2025.420; RPC, Rule 4.2; Rappleyea v. Campbell (1994) 8 Cal.4th 975, 985; Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543.)

On October 7, 2024, Defendant filed a notice of partial withdrawal of its motion pertaining to its request to quash or stay Lerman’s deposition.  (Notice Partial Withdrawal, pg. 2.)  Defendant’s notice states it does not withdraw its request for an order barring Plaintiff from directly contacting or communicating with Defendant or any of its Board Members, including Lerman, while this action is pending.  (Notice Partial Withdrawal, pg. 2.)

 

Discussion

C.C.P. §128(a)(5) provides that the Court has the power to control in the furtherance of justice the conduct of all persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto. In an ordinary action where all parties are represented by counsel, an attorney cannot contact a represented person about the action directly or indirectly without the consent of the opposing attorney.  (RPC, Rule 4.2.)  This rule exists to preserve the attorney-client relations from an opposing attorney’s (and party’s) intrusion and interference.  (Jackson v. Ingersoll-Rand Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1163, 1167 [discussing Rule 2-100, which is a precursor to Rule 4.2].)

To effectuate this rule, courts commonly issue orders that prohibit communications with represented parties.  (See, e.g., Hernandez v. Vitamin Shoppe Industries, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1454-1460 [affirming order issued pursuant to C.C.P. §128 that prohibited communications with represented parties].) Similarly, where the represented person is an employee or agent of a corporation, it is settled that a court can issue a protective order barring such contact when the person is privy to privileged information and there is a real danger that such information might be disclosed if the person is contacted ex parte (i.e., without his or her counsel).  (See Bobele v. Superior Court (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 708, 714-715.)

The Court of Appeal has held that the Rules of Professional Conduct should apply to parties who elect to proceed pro per in litigation:

Pro. per. litigants are held to the same standards as attorneys . . . We see no reason why the rules of professional conduct, at least as those rules govern conduct in the actual course of litigation, should not apply to pro per litigants in the very cases where pro per litigants elect to act as their own attorneys. To say otherwise would be to give pro per litigants (and particularly vexatious litigants representing themselves) an unfair advantage over parties represented by attorneys bound by that code.

 

(Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543.)

Here, Plaintiff has attempted to contact Lerman directly.  (See Supp.-Decl. of Asi ¶1 [“On July 26, 2024, I called Mr. Gabriel Lerman via WhatsApp.”]; id. at ¶2 [“On August 28, 2024, following the Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) where the court ordered me to disclose Mr. Lerman’s identity as my witness and directed the HFPA to withdraw their motion to quash his deposition, I sent Mr. Lerman a message via WhatsApp.”]; id. at ¶3 [“On October 4, 2024, I spoke with Attorney Neville Johnson, who confirmed that he was representing Mr. Lerman in a potential class action lawsuit against the HFPA until his clients’ retainer ran out. Later that day, I sent another message to Mr. Lerman via WhatsApp, informing him of the content of my conversation with Mr. Johnson.”].) 

Plaintiff is subject to the same standards of conduct as any attorney in this action.  Therefore, Plaintiff is prohibited from communicating with Defendant’s represented members without the consent of Defendant’s counsel.  (RPC, Rule 4.2.)

Accordingly, Defendant’s partial motion to prohibit Plaintiff from contacting Defendant’s represented members without the consent of their counsel is granted.

 

Conclusion

Defendant’s motion to prohibit Plaintiff from contacting Defendant’s represented members without the consent of their counsel is granted.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

7.     Defendant’s Motion for Protective Order

Meet and Confer

          C.C.P. §2031.060 provides, in part:

When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

 

(C.C.P. §2031.060(a), emphasis added.)

Defendant’s counsel declares that on May 21, 2024, he and Plaintiff met and conferred, and Plaintiff stated that he did not wish to sign a protective order despite the fact that it would provide him with his requested documents at an earlier date because he has “nothing to lose” from continued opposition to the entry of a protective order.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶15.)  The Court finds Defendant’s counsel’s declaration states facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the matter outside of court.  (C.C.P. §§2016.040, 2031.060(a).)  Therefore, the Court will consider the instant motion.

 

Legal Standard

C.C.P. §2031.060(b) provides that “[t]he court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.”  (C.C.P. §2031.060(b).)

The decision upon whether to enter a protective order lies within the sound discretion of the court.  (Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 588, 591; Meritplan Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (1981) 124 Cal.App.3d 237, 242.)  Moving parties have the burden to show good cause for protective order.  (Emerson Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 16 Cal.4th 1101, 1110.)   

 

          Discussion

Defendant’s motion for protective order is granted.  Defendant requests this Court impose a protective order related to the investigative report Plaintiff seeks in his RFPs that concerns allegations of sexual harassment and sexual misconduct. 

“The state Constitution expressly grants Californians a right of privacy. (Cal. Const. art. 1, §1.)  Protection of informational privacy is the provision’s central concern.  (Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1, 35.)  In Hill, the California Supreme Court established a framework for evaluating potential invasions of privacy.  The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.”  (Id. at pgs. 35-37.)

“The Hill test, conceived in the context of a pleaded cause of action for invasion of privacy, has been applied more broadly, including to circumstances where litigation requires a court to reconcile asserted privacy interests with competing claims for access to third party [personal] information.”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 532.)

“Assuming that a claimant has met the foregoing Hill criteria for invasion of a privacy interest, that interest must be measured against other competing or countervailing interests in a ‘balancing test.’”  (Pioneer Electronics (USA), Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 40 Cal.4th 360, 371.)  “Protective measures, safeguards and other alternatives may minimize the privacy intrusion. ‘For example, if intrusion is limited and confidential information is carefully shielded from disclosure except to those who have a legitimate need to know, privacy concerns are assuaged.’”  (Id., citing Hill, 7 Cal.4th at pg. 38.)

The Los Angeles Superior Court developed a Model Protective Order specifically for this purpose.  (See Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles Model Stipulation and Protective order – Confidential Designation Only, available at https://www.lacourt.org/division/civil/pdf/formprotectiveorder1confidential_1.pdf.)

          First, good cause exists to enter the proposed, modified version of the LASC Model Protective Order offered by Defendant.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶6-7, Exhs. 6-7.)  Defendant seeks to safeguard a legally protected privacy interest, namely Defendant’s 86-page investigative report (“Investigative Report”) conducted by Ramsey & Ehrlich LLP into allegations of sexual harassment.  Exhibits 3 and 4 to the Investigative Report includes private text messages between two third parties obtained by the investigators.  Defendant also seeks to safeguard Defendant’s internal communications related to the investigation and the accusers, Defendant’s internal communications related to PR from the investigation, and Defendant’s internal communications regarding Plaintiff and Defendant’s policies.  These documents implicate the legally protected information privacy interests of third parties who have alleged sexual misconduct by Plaintiff.  (Hill, 7 Cal.4th at pg. 37.)

          Second, the third parties have a reasonable expectation of privacy in their reports of misconduct to Defendant.  Defendant’s Whistleblower Policy, Code of Conduct, and Bylaws all expressly promise confidentiality.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶¶16-18, Exhs. 14-16.)  As the Court of Appeal specifically noted in its decision affirming in part and reversing in part the Defendant’s special motion to strike Plaintiff’s complaint, Plaintiff’s accusers are Covered Persons under Defendant’s Whistleblower Policy. The Whistleblower Policy’s express terms state that the identity of such Covered Persons is to be concealed “to the greatest extent practicable given legal requirements, consistent with the need to conduct a fair and adequate investigation and take necessary corrective action.”  (Decl. of Ellison ¶14, Exh. 14 at §5.)

          Defendant’s Code of Conduct also contains a section entitled “Confidential Reporting and Non-Retaliation,” which explains that “[a]lthough individuals are encouraged to identify themselves while making reports so that any necessary follow-up can be undertaken, the hotline accepts anonymous reports. In any case, the HFPA will protect the reporter’s confidentiality to the fullest extent possible (although information may need to be shared to facilitate an investigation and response to a report.)”  (Decl. of Ellison ¶15, Exh. 15 at pg. 5.)

          Defendant’s Bylaws also enshrine these protections: “Information disclosed at any meeting of the Members that is not generally known to the public, including Association financial information, business plans, salaries, awards, legal matters and/or intellectual property shall[] not be disclosed by Members to third parties at any time without approval of the Chief Executive Officer or the Board.”  (Decl. of Ellison ¶16, Exh. 16 at §4.16.)

          Here, the accusers had a reasonable expectation of privacy with respect to their participation in Defendant’s investigation.  The Court of Appeal determined that Defendant’s Whistleblower Policy, Code of Conduct, and Bylaws all applied to Plaintiff’s accusers.  (Asi v. Hollywood Foreign Press Association (Cal. Ct. App., Oct. 13, 2023, No. B327002) 2023 WL 6783094, at *18-20.)[1]

          Finally, not designating the Investigative Report and associated materials as confidential under the proposed protective order poses serious threats of privacy intrusions to third parties.  Plaintiff’s past conduct of using confidential materials against other HFPA members, including those who have accused him of misconduct, warrants the protection sought in this motion.  (See Decl. of Ellison ¶¶2-6, 19-20, Exhs. 1-5, 17-18.)

          Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for the proposed protective order is granted.

 

Conclusion

Defendant’s motion for a protective order is granted.
