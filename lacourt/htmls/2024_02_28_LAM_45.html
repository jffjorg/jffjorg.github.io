DEPARTMENT 45 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All rulings shown here are TENTATIVE ONLY, and thus oral argument WILL be heard. All Counsel are still required to attend.

Case Number: 19STCV30463    Hearing Date: February 28, 2024    Dept: 45

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

 

BEN MYERSON CANDY CO., INC. dba WINE WAREHOUSE,

 

                             Plaintiff,

 

                              vs.

DEVOTO-WADE LLC dba GOLDEN STATE CIDER, and DOES 1-10, inclusive,

 

                              Defendants.
Case No.:  19STCV30463

DEPARTMENT 45

 

 

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING

 

 

 

Action Filed:  08/27/19

2nd Amended Complaint Filed:   01/13/23

Trial Date:  None set.

 

Hearing date:  February 28, 2024

Moving Party:  Defendant Devoto-Wade LLC dba Golden State Cider

Responding Party:  None.

Motion to Seal

The Court considered the moving papers.

The motion is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Background

            On January 13, 2023, Plaintiff Ben Myerson Candy Co., Inc. dba Wine Warehouse (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) against Defendant Devoto-Wade LLC dba Golden State Cider (“Defendant”), and DOES 1-10, inclusive for: (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Common Count in Quantum Meruit.

            On January 2, 2024, Defendant filed this instant Motion to Seal. No opposition has been filed.

 

 

 

Legal Standard

            Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.551, “A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record. The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.” (CRC, rule 2.551, subd. (b)(1).) “A copy of the motion or application must be served on all parties that have appeared in the case. Unless the court orders otherwise, any party that already has access to the records to be placed under seal must be served with a complete, unredacted version of all papers as well as a redacted version.” (CRC, rule 2.551, subd. (b)(2).)

Finally, “[t]he party requesting that a record be filed under seal must lodge it with the court under (d) when the motion or application is made, unless good cause exists for not lodging it or the record has previously been lodged under (3)(A)(i). Pending the determination of the motion or application, the lodged record will be conditionally under seal.” (CRC, rule 2.551, subd. (b)(4).)

 

Discussion

            Defendant Devoto-Wade LLC dba Golden State Cider (“Defendant”) moves for an order sealing certain materials submitted by Plaintiff Ben Myerson Candy Co., Inc. dba Wine Warehouse (“Plaintiff”) in connection with Plaintiff’s opposition to Defendant’s motion in limine K and Plaintiff’s motion for issue and monetary sanctions. Defendant argues the information sought to be sealed consists of information reflecting or deriving from Defendant’s documents produced in discovery and the testimony of Defendant’s witnesses that were designated as “Confidential” or “Highly Confidential – Attorneys’ Eyes Only” pursuant to the parties’ Protective Order and/or provided to Defendant with an expectation that Defendant would seek to seal the information. Furthermore, Defendant contends it has an overriding interest in the sealing of this information to protect it and its members’ private and competitively sensitive information and that of relevant third-parties, which overrides the public interest in access. Moreover, Defendant asserts the documents were also previously ordered sealed or contain information similar in nature to documents and information that the Court ordered sealed. (Min. Order 4-20-23; see also Order re: Mtn. to Seal and Disqualify Counsel 5-30-23.)

Specifically, Defendant seeks to seal information contained within five exhibits: (1) Exhibit R to the Declaration of Oren Bitan in Support of Wine Warehouse’s Motion for Issue and Monetary Sanctions under seal; (2) Exhibit M to the Declaration of Oren Bitan in Support of Wine Warehouse’s Opposition to Golden State Cider’s Motion in Limine K under seal; (3) emails attached as Exhibit S to the Declaration of Oren Bitan in Support of Wine Warehouse’s Motion for Issue and Monetary Sanctions under seal; (4) Excerpts from Defendant’s proposed trial demonstratives; and (5) Excerpts from a confidential deposition of Defendant’s former CEO.

Additionally, Defendant argues the documents and deposition testimony it seeks to seal contain confidential information and negotiation strategies that were exchanged between the members and partners of Defendant, and confidential information requested and exchanged between counsel for Defendant and counsel for Sonoma Craft for purposes of completing the Golden State Cider Transaction. Defendant also argues revealing such confidential and sensitive materials to the public would put Defendant at a competitive disadvantage if the company sought to acquire or invest in a new business venture, because the information is not something Defendant would otherwise reveal during the typical negotiation process. Likewise, Defendant contends it takes extensive steps to protect its confidential information, including by restricting access to specific individuals and ensuring the company has adequate cybersecurity. Lastly, Defendant asserts the sealing motion is narrowly tailored and reasonable.

The Court finds that Defendant’s motion is narrowly tailored and reasonable. Defendant only seeks to seal information that have either already been subject to seal and/or are similarly in nature. Furthermore, Defendant only seeks to seal five exhibits, which is a relatively low quantity of documents. Defendant also demonstrates the information it seeks to seal contain confidential or highly confidential information and that revealing such information to the public would put it at a disadvantage against its competitors. Finally, Defendant shows that the information it seeks to protect under seal is not readily shared with competitors, business partners, or even certain individuals that work for Defendant. However, Defendant’s motion does not include proof of service as required by California Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1). Also, Plaintiff did not file an opposition. As such, the Court is unable to confirm whether or not Plaintiff had notice of this instant motion.

Therefore, the motion to seal is DENIED without prejudice.

Background

On June 28, 2023, Kamath filed her opposition to the motion to compel further regarding Tuggle’s demand for production of documents. On July 5, 2023, Tuggle filed his reply to this opposition. Thereafter, on July 10, 2023, Kamath filed her opposition to the motion to compel further regarding Tuggle’s form interrogatory no. 15.1. On July 13, 2023, Tuggle filed his reply to this opposition

On July 11, 2023, Tuggle and Conrad (collectively “Cross-Defendants”) filed the instant special motion to strike under Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16 (“anti-SLAPP Motion”), seeking to strike the first through fifth causes of action raised in the cross-complaint.

On July 14, 2023, the court continued the hearings of Tuggle’s motions to compel further to February 28, 2024.

In lieu of an opposition to the anti-SLAPP Motion, Kamath filed a request for continuance on February 23, 2024. This request also sought to continue the discovery motions. On February 26, 2024, Cross-Defendants filed their oppositions to Kamath’s continuance request.

Legal Standard

  1. Anti-SLAPP

The anti-SLAPP statute is “a mechanism through which complaints that arise from the exercise of free speech rights can be evaluated at an early stage of the litigation process and resolved expeditiously.” (Simmons v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2001) 92 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1073 [internal quotations omitted].) Courts use a two-step process for determining whether an action is a strategic lawsuit against public participation, or a SLAPP. First, the court determines whether the defendant has established that the challenged claim arises from protected speech. (Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.) If such a showing has been made, the court “determines whether the plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim.” (Ibid.)

  1. Compel Further

A motion for an order compelling further responses shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b), 2031.310(b)(2)), and notice of the motion shall be given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or any specific later date to which the parties have agreed in writing, or the party requesting the order waives any right to compel further responses (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c)).

Interrogatories

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that (1) an answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete, (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate, or (3) an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(a).) The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections thereto. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-21.)

Request for Production of Documents

On receipt of a response to a request for production of documents, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete, (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive, or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(a).) 

Motions to compel further responses to requests for production of documents must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the request. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b).) To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact. (Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224 disapproved on other grounds by Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531; see also Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98 (characterizing good cause as “a fact-specific showing of relevance”).) If good cause is shown by the moving party, the burden shifts to the responding party to justify any objections made to disclosure of the documents. (Kirkland, supra, 95 Cal.App.4th at 98.)

Discussion

A.    Continuance Request

Considering an opposition has not been filed in response to the anti-SLAPP Motion, it is understandable as to why Kamath seeks a continuance. However, Kamath has failed to establish relief for her continuance request. Within her request, Kamath first states that the notice of motions now between the court were improper and there lacked a meet and confer. (Request for Continuance filed on February 23, 2024 at pg. 2.) To the extent that this argument is directed at Tuggle’s motions to compel further, the court shall address any purported deficiencies further below. Now, as directed at the anti-SLAPP Motion, the court finds these initial arguments to be unpersuasive. First, the enclosed proof of service within the anti-SLAPP motion indicates that Kamath was served via email on July 11, 2023. The court record shows that Kamath has manifested her consent through her own conduct by using electronic service for her filings. (See Proof of Service, dated July 10, 2023 [stating only electronic mail service was used]; Code Civ. Proc. § 1010.6(c)(3).) Kamath fails to present any evidence, let alone a declaration, to support her claim of improper service.

Second, Kamath claims that the parties did not meet and confer. However, there is no requirement for the parties to meet and confer before proceeding with a motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16. (Trinity Risk Management, LLC v. Simplified Labor Staffing Solutions, Inc. (2021) 59 Cal. App. 5th 995, 1008.)

Third, Kamath argues that a continuance may be provided because Kamath has not had an opportunity to receive any discovery responses before the anti-SLAPP Motion was filed. (Request for Continuance filed on February 23, 2024 at pp. 2-3.) “To establish good cause, the plaintiff must file a noticed motion identifying the specific discovery sought and showing this discovery is needed ... to establish a prima facie case and tailored to that end. [Citation Omitted].” (Murray v. Tran (2020) 55 Cal.App.5th 10, 37 [internal quotations omitted].) Notably, Kamath’s request is devoid of the necessary information that the court would need to find that there is good cause to grant the continuance request.  Therefore, considering Kamath has been on notice since July 11, 2023 of the anti-SLAPP Motion and she has failed to identify the specific discovery sought that would establish a prima facie case for her claims, the court declines to exercise its discretionary authority in continuing the anti-SLAPP Motion.

Lastly, Kamath seemingly seeks to request sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure § 128.5 on the basis that the anti-SLAPP Motion is frivolous. (Request for Continuance filed on February 23, 2024 at pg. 4, relying on Changsha Metro Grp. v. Peng Xufeng (2020) 57 Cal.App.5th 173.) However, Kamath’s reliance on Changsha Metro Grp. is misplaced because that case is distinguishable from the case at hand. In Changsha, the Court of Appeal determined that is was appropriate for the non-movant to seek sanctions under Section 128.5 within its opposition to an anti-SLAPP motion because it reasoned that strictly adhering to the safe-harbor provision of Section 128.5 would contradict the purpose of the anti-SLAPP Act. (Id., supra, 57 Cal.App.5th at 181.) Normally, under Section 425.16, subdivision (f), a hearing for an anti-SLAPP motion must be held no more than 30 days after the service of an anti-SLAPP motion in order to quickly dispose of SLAPP suits. (Ibid.) If the nonmovant in Changsha sought strict adherence to the 21-day safe harbor rule under Section 128.5 without risking expenses associated with drafting an opposition to the anti-SLAPP motion, then he would have to request a continuance of the anti-SLAPP hearing, which would not result in the quick disposition of a possible SLAPP suit. (Ibid.) Unlike in Changsha, the hearing on the anti-SLAPP Motion was not set within 30 days of service, and this was most likely because of docket conditions that necessitated a later hearing. Considering the anti-SLAPP Motion was reserved for February 28, 2024, Kamath had nearly seven months to abide by Section 128.5’s safe harbor provision. Moreover, Kamath’s request for a continuance without good cause would contradict the purpose of the anti-SLAPP Act.

Accordingly, the court denies Kamath’s continuance request.

B.    Anti-SLAPP

                                     i.          Prong One: Protected Activity

“[T]he only thing the defendant needs to establish to invoke the protection of the SLAPP statute is that the challenged lawsuit arose from an act on the part of the defendant in furtherance of her right of petition or free speech.” (Fox Searchlight Pictures, Inc. v. Paladino (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 294, 307.)

An act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech includes the following:

(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e).)

In determining whether a cause of action arises from protected conduct, the court focuses on “the allegedly wrongful and injury-producing conduct that provides the foundation for the claims.” (Castleman v. Sagaser (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 481, 490-491.) “[T]he critical consideration is whether the cause of action is based on the defendant’s protected free speech or petitioning activity.” (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89 [emphasis in original].) In making this determination, the court considers “the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (Ibid.)

As alleged in the cross-complaint, Kamath claims that Cross-Defendants have initiated a defamatory lawsuit against her and that she has been injured as a result. (Cross-Compl. ¶¶ 6, 88-90.) In connection with the first through third causes of action for defamation, Kamath describes the defamatory statement as “financial elder abuse” and that it was to mean that Kamath engaged in financial elder abuse. (Id. at ¶ 95, 98-99, 105.) It is further alleged that this allegation of engaging in financial elder abuse has caused personal and professional damages to Kamath’s reputation and image. (Id. at 88.)

With regard to the fourth cause of action for malicious prosecution, Kamath alleges that the Cross-Defendants initiated this underlying complaint against Kamath without probable cause and asserts that the cause of action for financial elder abuse therein is without merit. (Id. at ¶¶ 113-114.)

Lastly, as to the fifth cause of action for abuse of process, Kamath alleges that the Cross-Defendants have abused the court’s process and that they had ulterior motives in using the legal process. (Id. at ¶ 125.) In particular, Kamath’s allegations suggest that the Cross-Defendants were not forthcoming in what was alleged in the underlying complaint. For instance, Kamath asserts that Tuggle was not responsive to Kamath’s communications during her representation (id. at ¶ 126), failed to provide appropriate information during the representation (ibid.), desired to delay foreclosure for as long as possible (id. at ¶ 133), and could not make necessary payments to Kamath (id. at ¶ 136). As for Conrad, Kamath alleges that he was responsible for failing to substitute as an attorney in the federal action but maintained improper communications with the opposing counsel (id. ¶¶ 127-130, 141, 143), insistent that Kamath should file a dismissal in the federal action (id. at ¶ 141), provide unsolicited legal advice to Kamath (id. at ¶ 138), and backdated Kamath’s consent on a substitution of attorney form in a separate matter (id. at ¶ 144.)

Here, Cross-Defendants contend that the first five causes of action raised in the cross-complaint arise out of protected activity because they all relate to statements made in connection with litigation. First, with regard to the defamation claims, Cross-Defendants highlight that the cross-complaint does not identify who made the defamatory remark or what was exactly stated. (Anti-SLAPP Motion at pg. 10.) Rather, based on the allegations, it is evident that the source of the defamation is the underlying complaint. (Cross-Compl. ¶¶ 88-89.) Thus, they reason that any statements regarding financial elder abuse would have been made in connection with the pending litigation. (Anti-SLAPP Motion at pg. 10.) Cross-Defendants further point out that, based on initial discovery, Kamath is unable to identify any statements beyond what is alleged in the underlying complaint. (Ibid., Exh. 3.) Thus, Cross-Defendants contend that the alleged conduct falls under the anti-SLAPP statute.

Under the circumstances, the court finds that the conduct alleged under the first through third causes of action are protected activities because it is alleged that the complaint itself is the source of the defamation. Therefore, it qualifies as litigation related conduct. (Kashian v. Harriman (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 892, 909 [asserting that litigation-related activity is viewed broadly by the courts].)

Second, Cross-Defendants argue that the conduct alleged in support of the fourth cause of action for malicious prosecution constitutes litigation-related activity. (Anti-SLAPP Motion at pg. 11.) The court agrees. “A complaint for malicious prosecution is necessarily based on protected speech and petitioning activity.” (Pasternack v McCullough (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 1347, 1355.)

Third, Cross-Defendants assert that the fifth cause of action for abuse of process falls within a protected activity under the Anti-SLAPP Statue because the alleged conduct stems from communication made in connection with issues under consideration by a judicial body. (Anti-SLAPP Motion at pg. 12.) The court is persuaded by this argument because “[a]buse of process claims are subject to a special motion to strike.” (Booker v. Roundtree (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1366, 1370.)

Accordingly, the court finds that Cross-Defendants have met their burden in satisfying the first prong has it relates to the first through fifth causes of action.

                                   ii.          Prong Two: Probability of Prevailing

To establish the likelihood of prevailing, a “plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.” (Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys., Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Assn. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 464, 476 [internal quotations omitted].) In making the prong two determination, “the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(2).) “The court does not, however, weigh [defendant’s] evidence against the plaintiff’s, in terms of either credibility or persuasiveness. Rather, the defendant’s evidence is considered with a view toward whether it defeats the plaintiff’s showing as a matter of law, such as by establishing a defense or the absence of a necessary element.” (1-800 Contacts, Inc. v. Steinberg (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 568, 585.) 

            Because Kamath has failed to file an opposition to the anti-SLAPP Motion, the Court finds that Kamath has failed to meet her burden of demonstrating probability of prevailing on her defamation claims, malicious prosecution claim, and abuse of process claim.

            Accordingly, the Court grants the anti-SLAPP Motion in its entirety.

                                 iii.          Attorney Fees

A prevailing defendant as to a special motion to strike is entitled to mandatory, reasonable attorney fees and costs.  (Civ. Proc. Code § 425.16(c)(1); Ketchum v. Moses¿(2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1141 -1142; Rylaarsdam, et al., Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2012) ¶7:1120.) The attorneys’ fees and costs recoverable are only those incurred as to the special motion to strike, and not as to the entire litigation.  (Lafayette Morehouse, Inc. v. Chronicle Pub. Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1383.)

Although detailed time records are not required, California Courts have expressed a preference for contemporaneous billing and an explanation of work. (Raining Data Corp. v. Barrenechea (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1363, 1375.) “Of course, the attorney's testimony must be based on the attorney's personal knowledge of the time spent and fees incurred. (Evid. Code, § 702 (a) [‘the testimony of a witness concerning a particular matter is inadmissible unless he has personal knowledge of the matter’].) Still, precise calculations are not required; fair approximations based on personal knowledge will suffice.” (Mardirossian & Associates, Inc. v. Ersoff (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 257, 269.)

Here, Cross-Defendants seek $12,187.75 in attorney fees and costs against Kamath. (Teitelbaum Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.) This amount consists of the following: (1) 18 hours in preparing the instant motion at an hourly rate of $550; (2) 2 anticipated hours in reviewing the opposition at an hourly rate of $550; (3) 1 hour in preparing a reply at an hourly rate of $550; (4) 1 hour in attending the hearing; and (5) $87.75 in costs. However, under the circumstances, the amount is excessive. First, because no opposition or reply was ever file, the three anticipated hours associated with those tasks were never incurred. Second, because appearances will likely be done remotely, it is unlikely that the hearing would last an hour. Third, because Cross-Defendant’s counsel has failed to provide a billing record, it is unclear what tasks were actual spent on drafting the anti-SLAPP Motion. (Lafayette Morehouse, Inc., surpa, 39 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1383.) Considering half of the motion consists of a summary of the allegations and the legal arguments presented are uncomplicated, it is unlikely that it took Counsel Teitelbaum 18 hours to draft the instant motion. Therefore, a reduction in fees is warranted.

Accordingly, the Court awards Cross-Defendants’ request for attorney fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(c) in the reduced amount of $4,762.75, consisting of 8 hours preparing the instant motion at a hourly rate of $550, .5 hours in attending the hearing at an hourly rate of $550, and 87.75 in costs.

C.    Motions to Compel Further

Here, Tuggle seeks to compel further responses from Kamath in connection with his First Set of Demands for Production of Documents and Second Set of Form Interrogatories. The pertinent discovery was propounded on January 23, 2023 and Kamath provided her responses on February 28, 2023. (Mots., Conrad Decls. ¶ 3.) Based on the perception that these responses were inadequate and improperly included objections, Counsel Conrad sent a meet and confer letter to Kamath, but she did not respond. (Id. at ¶ 4.) Thereafter, Tuggle attempted to secure a hearing date for an informal discovery conference on March 10, 2023, but the court, with Judge Ronald S. Draper presiding, would not consider the request April 7, 2023, until Kamath’s motion for reconsideration of her peremptory challenge had been determined. (Mots., Conrad Decl. ¶ 4.)

Under the circumstances, the court finds that these discovery motions are defective for Tuggle’s failure to meet and confer in good faith before seeking the court’s intervention. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.310(b), 2031.310(b)(2), a motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040 to show that the moving party has met and conferred in good faith. It is evident that meet and confer declarations submitted in support of both motions do not “state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.) 

Based on the declarations submitted within each motion, Counsel Conrad attests that he merely sent a single meet and confer letters to Kamath; this was the extent of Petitioner’s meet and confer attempts. While Tuggle attempted to request an informal discovery conference, this was only done in compliance with Judge Draper’s standing order. Also, it is evident by the reply papers, Tuggle has not taken a meaningful steps to meet and confer in good faith in order to resolve the discovery issues since filing the instant motions. A reasonable and good faith attempt means “a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution,” in other words, counsel must “attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.”  (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1294.)  The mere delivery of the meet and confer letter fails to actually show that the parties discussed or negotiated the purported deficiencies of the responses. Moreover, “[t]he Discovery Act makes no exception [to the meet and confer requirement] based upon one’s speculation that the prospect of informal resolution may be bleak.”  (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1438.)  

Consequently, Tuggle’s request for sanctions are denied. With regard to Kamath’s requests for sanctions, the court finds the request improper because she is self-represented and cannot recover attorney fees. (See Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp., (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 95.)

Accordingly, the court denies Tuggle’s motion to compel further responses to his Second Set

Conclusion

            Based on the foregoing, as to Defendant/Cross-Complainant Reshma Kamath’s request for continuance, the court DENIES the request because good cause has not been established to warrant the requested relief.

Also, the court GRANTS Cross-Defendants James H. Tuggle, individually and as trustee of the James Tuggle Family Revocable Living Trust and Robert Conrad’s Special Motion to Strike under Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16. Thus, the first through fifth causes of action raised in the cross-complaint are deemed stricken. The request for attorney fees is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $4,762.75. Cross-Complainant Reshma Kamath is ordered to tender payment to the Cross-Defendants’ counsel of record within 45 days of this order.

Lastly, the court DENIES Plaintiff James H. Tuggle’s Motion to Compel Defendant Reshma Kamath to Provide Further Responses to Interrogatory No. 15.1 of Plaintiff’s Second Set of Form Interrogatories and Motion to Compel Defendant Reshma Kamath to Provide Further Responses to Plaintiff’s First Set of Demands for Production of Documents and to Produce Documents. The requests for sanctions are DENIED.

Background

On February 6, 2023, Plaintiff Althea Richardson, for herself and as guardian ad litem of minors: Jordyn Richardson, Jyair Thomas, Jeremiah Walker, and Jayden Jones; Guadalupe Herrera; Glennzale Hunter Ervin; Antonio Jose Gilberto Chavez; Deborah Erwin, for herself and as guardian ad litem of minor: Faith Ervin; Glen Erwin, for himself and as guardian ad litem of minors: Inspiration Ervin, Elijah Ervin, Trinity Ervin and Cherish Ervin; Anthony Cooper; Amia Little, for herself and as guardian ad litem of minor: Treasure Anderson; Andre Cooper, for himself and as guardian ad litem of minor: Benjamin Cooper; Nazjah Burt; Dejoun Stuart; Angelo Little; Anthonette Crosby, for herself and as guardian ad litem of minors: Pocahuntes Riley, Timothy Riley, Sincere Riley and Janelle Age; Britney Rouse, for herself and as guardian ad litem of minor: Sarai Crenshaw; Imani Asiyah Fattaah, for herself and as guardian ad litem of minors: Kaden Zion Rivers, DeAnder LeAnthony Brown Jr. (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) commenced this action.

Plaintiffs filed a Complaint for damages against Defendant Desert View Apartments, LP, alleging causes of action for (1) Breach of the Warranty of Habitability; (2) Breach of the warranty of Quiet Enjoyment; (3) Nuisance; (4) Violation of Civil Code S. 1941.1, et seq; (5) Negligence; and (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

            On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff Amia Little (“Petitioner”) filed this instant Petition to Approve Compromise of Disputed Claim (Treasure Anderson, Age: 1). The Petition is unopposed.

 

Legal Standard

“A parent having the right to compromise the disputed claim of the minor under this section may execute a full release and satisfaction, or execute a covenant not to sue on or a covenant not to enforce judgment on the disputed claim, after the money or other property to be paid or delivered has been paid or delivered as provided in subdivision (c). If the court orders that all or any part of the money to be paid under the compromise or covenant be deposited in an insured account in a financial institution in this state, or in a single-premium deferred annuity, the release and satisfaction or covenant is not effective for any purpose until the money has been deposited as directed in the order of the court.” (Prob. Code, § 3500(d).) 

            “The court shall schedule a hearing on a petition for compromise of a minor’s disputed claim pursuant to Section 3500 within 30 days from the date of filing. If the petition is unopposed, the court shall issue a decision on the petition at the conclusion of the hearing.” (Prob. Code, § 3505.) 

            “The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that reasonable expenses, medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator, costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or person with a disability.” (Prob. Code, § 3601(a).) 

 

Discussion

Claimant Treasure Anderson (“Claimant”), a minor, by and through their Guardian Ad Litem, Amia Little (“Petitioner”), has agreed to settle their claims against Defendant Desert View Apartments, LP in exchange for $4,000.00.  If approved, $1,000.00 will be used for attorney’s fees, leaving a balance of $3,000.00 for Claimant to be deposited into a blocked account, subject to withdrawal only upon authorization of the court.

The Court has reviewed the proposed settlement and finds that it is fair and reasonable.  Further, the requested attorney’s fees, which amounts to approximately 25% of the total settlement, is fair and reasonable.

            Therefore, the Petition to Approve Compromise of Disputed Claim (Treasure Anderson, Age: 1) is GRANTED.

 

The Court sets an OSC for ________________ ____, 2024 for proof of deposit.  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 7.953(a).)  If an acknowledgement of receipt by the financial institution is filed before that date, no appearance will be required. 

 

 

            It is so ordered.
