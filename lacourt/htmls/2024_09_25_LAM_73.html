DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV08196    Hearing Date: September 25, 2024    Dept: 73

9/25/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

LAURA PADILLA v. YOUTH POLICY INSTITUTE, INC., et al. (19STCV08196) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Laura Padilla (Pro Per) 

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Lyne Richardson (Olgetree, Deakins, Nash, Smoak & Stewart, P.C.) 

 

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL (filed 8/16/2023) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On March 11, 2019, Plaintiff Lauren Padilla (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Youth Policy Institute, Inc. (“Youth Policy”) and Enrique Rodriguez (“Rodriguez”).

 

Plaintiff is a former employee of Defendant Youth Policy.  Youth Policy is in the business of transforming Los Angeles neighborhoods to reduce poverty, including the Youth Policy on Western Avenue, where all events alleged in the complaint occurred.  In June 2016, Defendant Youth Policy hired Plaintiff as a receptionist.  Plaintiff was later promoted to Program Assistant/Promotor.  Plaintiff alleges that, in March 2017, she was diagnosed with chronic migraines.  Because of her disability, Plaintiff was given special accommodations (i.e., more time to complete paperwork, more rest breaks, and not forced to do any physical activities).  In July 2017, Defendant Rodriguez began working for Youth Policy.  Rodriguez allegedly began a campaign of harassment against Plaintiff by: (1) taking away Plaintiff’s accommodations, (2) singling Plaintiff out to do tasks that she could not do in attempt to humiliate and degrade her (e.g., moving heavy boxes and throwing out heavy bags of garbage); and (3) yelling at, berating, and embarrassing Plaintiff by making certain derogative comments as well as pushing her (see Compl., ¶¶ 19-25).  On February 28, 2019, Plaintiff’s employment was terminated.   

 

The Complaint alleges causes of action for:  

 

C/A 1: Against Youth Policy for Disability Discrimination 

C/A 2: Against Youth Policy for Wrongful Termination  

C/A 3: Against Youth Policy for Retaliation (GC § 12940(h)) 

C/A 4: Against Defendants for IIED 

C/A 5: Against Defendants for Harassment 

 

On August 16, 2023, counsel for Defendants filed a Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel as to Youth Policy and Rodriguez each. They also filed supporting declarations and proposed orders.  

 

No opposition or other response has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

California Rules¿of Court,¿rule 3.1362 (Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel) requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel—Civil¿form (MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil¿form (MC-052)); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil¿form (MC-053)).¿ 

 

The¿court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted¿provided that¿there is no prejudice to the client, and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.¿¿(See¿Ramirez v.¿Sturdevant¿(1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.)¿ 

 

B.      Discussion

Here, Defendants’ counsel, Lyne Richardson and Brittney Turner, seek relief under Code of Civil Procedure, Section 284(2). Counsel correctly completed and filed the notice (Form MC-051) and declaration (MC-052). (Code Civ. Proc., § 284(2); Cal. Rules Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a) & (c).) In the declaration, counsel sufficiently explains that this motion is being brought under Section 284(2) instead of 284(1) because Defendants Rodriguez and Youth Policy have breached a material term of their Attorney Retainer Agreement concerning fees. Defendants were provided with reasonable warning after the breach that undersigned counsel would withdraw unless Defendants performed their obligations. However, Defendants have not cured the breach.

Counsel served the notice (MC-051), the declaration (MC-052), and the proposed order (MC-053) on Defendants and filed a Proof of Service with the Court.

The Court finds all the requirements for relief have been completed. Additionally, trial has not been scheduled in this case and Defendants have a reasonable amount of time to obtain new counsel, such that they should not be prejudiced by the withdrawal.

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is granted.


Case Number: 23STCV19508    Hearing Date: September 25, 2024    Dept: 73

RIVAS v. LOU SOBH CERRITOS SATURN, INC. (23STCV19508)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff:  Brandon Brouillette (Crosner Legal, PC)

Counsel for Defendant:  Craig Roeb (Chapman Glucksman)

 

(1)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed 03/01/2024)

(2)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed 03/01/2024)

(3)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE, AND REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS (filed 06/03/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $3,060.00 is GRANTED, to be paid within 30 days. 

Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One within 20 days.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 15, 2023, Plaintiff Wilber Jimenez Rivas, on behalf of the State of California, and others similarly situated and aggrieved, filed this action against Defendant Lou Sobh Cerritos Saturn, Inc. for violation of the Private Attorneys General Act, Cal. Labor Code Sections 2698, et seq.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff is a former employee of Defendant. Plaintiff worked for Defendant as a non-exempt employee with a job title of automotive technician, automotive service technician, piece-rate technician or a similar title from in or around May 2022 through in or around at least July 17, 2022 or later. This is a representative action filed by Plaintiff on behalf of Plaintiff, all Aggrieved Employees and the State of California against Defendant, pursuant to California’s Private Attorney General Act, Labor Code section 2698 et. seq. (“PAGA”), to recover civil penalties (75% payable to the Labor and Workforce Development Agency and 25% payable to Aggrieved Employees) for Defendant’s violations of the California Labor Code

 

On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and request for monetary sanctions. Plaintiff argues that at the time of filing this motion, Defendant has failed to timely serve Code-Compliant responses. No opposition was filed.

 

 On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and request for monetary sanctions. Plaintiff argues that at the time of filing this motion, Defendant has failed to timely serve Code-Compliant responses. No opposition was filed.

 

On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and request for monetary sanctions, arguing:

·       Defendant served the Supplemental Responses at issue, on April 15, 2024

·       Defendant responded to the Requests with boilerplate objections and evasive responses that fail to satisfy Code requirements. Plaintiff therefore seeks an order striking Defendant’s objections and compelling further responses and the production of all responsive documents, including electronically stored information

·       Defendant failed to serve timely Code-compliant responses, and Plaintiff submits that this failure constitutes the waiver of any objection.

·       As discussed in detail in Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, Defendant’s objections are almost uniformly without merit.

o   For example, Defendant objects that “Plaintiff lacks standing to pursue a representative PAGA claim as he was not an aggrieved employee for Defendant while employed with the company…[and that Defendant] does not and did not during any relevant time that Plaintiff was employed have any aggrieved employee as defined by the California labor code.” (Wallace Decl. ¶ 25) However, Defendant’s contention is an affirmative defense, not a valid discovery objection.

·       Good cause exists for compelling further responses.

·       Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,360

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that:

·       Plaintiff waived his right to compel a further response to the demand by not timely moving to compel production of additional responses nor obtain an extension to the 45-day deadline set forth in California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.310. Therefore the instant motion to compel should not have been filed

·       Nevertheless, Defendant provided code compliant responses

·       Sanctions are not warranted and should be denied

In reply, Plaintiff maintains that the Court should compel Defendant to provide responses/further responses.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer

 

Before bringing a motion to compel further responses to any discovery request, the moving party is required to make efforts to meet and confer in good faith and must submit a declaration attesting to those efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310(b)(2), 2030.300(b), 2033.290(b).) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating that she sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding the supplemental responses. The parties also attended an Informal Discovery Conference. (Wallace Decl. ¶¶ 26-31.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement.

 

A.      Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One

 

Plaintiff is seeking responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

Plaintiff propounded Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, on Defendant on September 14, 2023. (Wallace Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.) Responses to the requests were due on October 16, 2023, and Defendant’s counsel served objections without substantive responses on the ground, inter alia, that the discovery requests were premature, given that “Responding Party’s motion to compel arbitration and stay proceedings is pending (Wallace Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. C.) Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration was subsequently withdrawn. (Wallace Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. D.) On January 4, 2024, Defendant agreed to supplement its objections-only responses to Plaintiff’s written discovery. (Wallace Decl. ¶ 8.) However, at the time of the filing of the instant motion to compel, Plaintiff asserts that Defendant has not provided its supplemental responses. (Wallace Decl. ¶ 8.)

 

First, the Court notes that after the filing of Plaintiff’s motion to compel on March 1, 2024, Defendant subsequently served supplemental responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One on April 15, 2024. Therefore, the instant motion to compel responses is moot. When respondents serve untimely discovery responses after parties have filed motions to compel initial responses, courts have broad discretion as to the ruling and may hold that the motion is moot. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., v. Klugman (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 409.) 

 

However, the Court also notes that although Plaintiff’s motions are styled as motions to compel initial responses, they would in fact be motions to compel further responses because Defendant had served responses to the initial discovery requests. Motions to compel further responses must be accompanied by a separate statement. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(a)(3).) A properly-prepared Separate Statement enables both the parties and the Court to accurately analyze relevance and objections. The substantial rights of the parties are affected when there is no separate statement. Here, Plaintiff did not file separate statements.

 

Due to these procedural deficiencies, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

 

 

A.      Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One

 

                                                               i.      Legal Standard

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) 

 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1) (Information is discoverable if it is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and it is not unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive.); Lipton v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611-1612 (noting a party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence).) 

  

If a timely motion to compel has been filed, the¿burden is on the responding party¿to justify any objection or failure fully to answer.¿(Coy v. Superior Court¿(1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221 [addressing a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories]; see also¿Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court¿(2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

 

                                                             ii.      Timeliness

 

First, Defendant opposes this motion to compel further responses to Plaintiff’s First Set of Request for Production of Documents on the grounds that procedurally Plaintiff waived his right to compel a further response to the demand by not timely moving to compel production of additional responses.

 

The notice of motion must be served within 45 days after service of the responses in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1013)); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(c), 2031.310(c), 2033.290(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal.App.4th 736, 745.) The 45day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.) However, the parties can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(c), 2033.290(c).) 

 

Plaintiff propounded its First Set of Request for Production of Documents on October 16, 2023. Defendant asserts that December 2, 2023 was the statutory deadline for Plaintiff to compel production of further responses.

 

However, the Court disagrees. Section 2031.310 (c) states: “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified responses, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.” (emphasis added.) Therefore, Plaintiff can move to compel within 45 days of the service of any supplemental responses. Here, Defendant served its supplemental responses on April 15, 2024. Plaintiff timely brought this motion within 45 days of the service of the supplemental responses, on June 3, 2024.

 

Therefore, the Court does not deny Plaintiff’s motion on this ground.

 

                                                           iii.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to his Request for Production of Documents, Set One, nos. 1-28. Plaintiff asserts that Defendant’s supplemental responses are not code compliant and that Defendant had waived objections.

 

First, it appears Defendant failed to provide verified responses by the parties agreed upon deadline of February 18, 2024, to serve supplemental responses and thus, waived objections. (Wallace Decl., Ex. H.) Additionally upon review of Plaintiff’s requests and Defendant’s supplemental responses, the Court finds that further response is warranted.

 

Defendant’s supplemental responses fail to provide a statement of compliance, indicating whether production is in full or in part, as required by Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.220. Even where a responding party cannot comply with a request for production, a “representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand.” Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.230. The statement shall include the reason why the responding party is unable to comply and “set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Ibid.)

 

Further, to the extent that Defendant claims to withhold documents based on privilege, the responding party must produce a “privilege log that adequately identifies each document [that party] claims is privileged and the factual basis for the privilege claim.” (Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Sup. Ct. (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1127; Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) (a claim of privilege in a discovery response “shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”). Here, Defendant has not provided a privilege log.

 

In addition, Defendant’s objection based on lack of standing does not appear to be a valid discovery objection. Defendant asserts “that Plaintiff lacks standing to pursue a representative PAGA claim as he was not an aggrieved employee for Defendant while employed with the company. Moreover, Responding Party asserts that it does not and did not during any relevant time that Plaintiff was employed have any aggrieved employee as defined by the California labor code.” However, “the way to raise lack of standing is to plead it as an affirmative defense, and thereafter to bring a motion for summary adjudication or summary judgment, not to resist discovery until a plaintiff proves he or she has standing.” (Williams v. Superior Ct. (2017) 3 Cal. 5th 531, 558–59.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

                                                           iv.      Sanctions

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(h), monetary sanctions shall be imposed where a party unsuccessfully opposes a motion to compel further responses unless the Court finds the party acted with substantial justification or other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Defendant. Plaintiff’s counsel states she spent at least 8 hours on this Motion and the filing fee for this motion is $60.00. (Wallace Decl. ¶ 33.) Plaintiff submits that sanctions should be awarded to Plaintiff against Defendant and Defense Counsel of record in the amount of $3,060.00

 

Since Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses is granted, the Court finds sanctions are warranted. Thus, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $3,060.00 is GRANTED.  

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One and Motion to Compel Defendant’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s further responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions in the amount of $3,060.00 is GRANTED, to be paid within 30 days. 

Defendant shall provide code-compliant responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One within 20 days.

CALDERON, et al. v. FCA US LLC (23STCV21142)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/moving party:  Nadia Yashar (Yashar Law, APLC)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Gregory Brezovec (Clark Hill LLP)

 

PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION, SET ONE, ADMITTED AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT FC US LLC (filed 06/17/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Defendant FC US LLC is DENIED.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant FC US LLC and counsel of record, in the reduced amount of $981.65. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 31, 2023, Plaintiffs Oscar Calderon and Pabell Emilio de Leon Altun filed the instant action against Defendant FC US LLC alleging the following causes of action:

1.      Violation of the Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Express Warranty

2.      Violation of the Song-Beverly Act -Breach of Implied Warranty

3.      Violation of the Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2

4.      Violation of the Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.22-Tanner Consumer Protection Act

Plaintiffs allege this action arises out of the warranty obligations of Defendant in connection with a vehicle purchased by Plaintiffs and for which Defendant issued a written warranty. (Complaint, ¶ 4.)

On June 17, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted against Defendant, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves the Court for an Order that the truth of each matter specified in Plaintiffs’ Requests for Admission, Set One to Defendant, served on November 9, 2023, be deemed admitted and established for all purposes in this action. The responses to the Request for Admissions were due on or about December 14, 2023. The Defendant did not provide any responses and did not request an extension.

·       Plaintiffs request sanctions against Defendant and its counsel in the amount of $1,681.65.

 

Defendant filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Despite an inadvertent delay, verified responses to Plaintiffs’ Request for Admissions, Set One, were properly served on July 16, 2024. Thus, Defendant requests the Court deny Plaintiffs’ motion.

·       The oversight was due to FCA US counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, and excusable neglect, which allows FCA US to seek relief. FCA US has substantially complied and provided responses to the discovery including appropriate objections. Plaintiffs have not been prejudiced by any delay, especially since the responses have now been served in substantial compliance with Code.

·       Sanctions should not be granted given good faith efforts to address its mistake by counsel.

 

Plaintiffs did not file a reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

If a party fails to timely respond to requests for admission, the propounding party may move for a Court order deeming the Requests for Admission admitted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280.) An untimely responding party waives all objections, including privilege, unless they subsequently serve responses in substantial compliance with the Civil Discovery Act and demonstrate that their failure is the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); § 2033.280, subd. (a).) The Court shall impose monetary sanctions for failure to timely respond to requests for admission unless the party acted with substantial justification or the circumstances render imposition of sanctions unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) For untimely responses to Requests for Admission, the Court shall deem the Requests for Admission admitted unless the responding party serves a code compliant response prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).) The Court must impose a monetary sanction on the party or attorney whose failure to serve timely Requests for Admission responses necessitated the motion. (Ibid.) 

 

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiffs served Request for Admissions, Set One on Defendant on November 9, 2023. (Yashar Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. 1.) Defendant’s response was due on or before December 14, 2023, however, at the time of the filing of Plaintiffs’ instant motion, no response has been served. (Id., ¶¶ 3-4.)  Plaintiff requests that the Court order the genuineness of any document and the truth of any matter specified in Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions, Set One be deemed admitted.

 

According to Defendant’s opposition, Defendant’s counsel did not serve timely responses to the Requests for Admission due to a mistake regarding the status of the discovery responses. (Brezovec Decl., ¶ 4.) Defendant’s counsel states that amidst the exponential increase in Song-Beverly matters, a substantial increase in emails contributed to the oversight and as a result, the error in failing to provide timely responses went unnoticed and the deadline lapsed. (Ibid.) Courts recognize the need to grant relief where the requesting party shows reasonable efforts to resolve the error and a reasonable basis for the error. (Mannino v. Super (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 776) Courts have defined “inadvertence” as the “lack of heedfulness, or attentiveness, inattention, fault from negligence.” (Alderman v. Jacobs (1954) 128 Cal.App.2d 273, 276; Baratti v. Baratti (1952) 109 Cal.App.2d 917, 921). “Excusable neglect” is defined as “neglect which might have been an act of a reasonably prudent person under the same circumstances.” (Id.) Here, Defendant’s mistake falls within the scope of “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” Defendant served its untimely responses on July 16, 2024. (Brezovec Decl., ¶ 3.) 

Defendant’s responses are in substantial compliance with the Code of Civil Procedure. (Brezovec Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.)  The substantial compliance doctrine is “commonly understood to mean ‘compliance with the substantial or essential requirements of something (as a statute or contract) that satisfies its purpose or objective even though its formal requirements are not complied with.’” (In Re A.V. (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 697, 709.) After review of Defendant’s responses, the Court finds that Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One, are in substantial compliance with Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.290 et seq. (Brezovec Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.) 

As such, the Court DENIES Plaintiffs’ motion for an order that the truth of the requests for admission be deemed admitted.  

C.      Sanctions

Plaintiffs have requested that this Court impose monetary sanctions against Defendant and its counsel.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd.(d).)¿¿Sanctions are mandatory for a party making or opposing a motion, except when the party making or opposing the motion is determined by the Court to have been acting with substantial justification, or that other circumstances would render the imposition of sanctions unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) Under the Civil Discovery Act, the Court is only entitled to impose monetary sanctions in the amount of “reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of” the misuse of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (a).) The purpose of discovery sanctions is “not to provide a weapon for punishment, forfeiture and the avoidance of a trial on the merits, but to prevent abuse of the discovery process and correct the problem presented.” (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer U.S., Inc. (2007) 149 Cal. App. 4th 285, 301.) Consequently, “[t]he trial court cannot impose sanctions for misuse of the discovery process as a punishment.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal. App. 4th 967, 992.)¿ 

Here, Plaintiffs request sanctions against Defendant and Defendant’s counsel in the sum of $1,681.65. Plaintiffs assert that this amount is comprised of counsel’s time filing this motion, anticipated time to review the opposition, file a reply, appear at the hearing for this motion and filing costs. Plaintiff’s counsels’ hourly rate is $350 an hour.

The Court finds sanctions are warranted. However, since no reply was filed, the Court reduces the total award by $700.00, which represents two hours of Plaintiff counsel’s hourly rate that Plaintiff had anticipated counsel would spend on reviewing the opposition and drafting a reply. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the total amount of $981.65.

 

DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Defendant FC US LLC is DENIED.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant FC US LLC and counsel of record, in the reduced amount of $981.65. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order.

AISPURO, et al. v. BEVERLY COMMUNITY HOSPITAL ASSOCIATION (24STCV13872)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Stephen Garcia (Garcia & Artigliere)

Counsel for Defendant/moving party: Rima Badawiya (Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith, LLP)

 

Demurrer AND MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF THE complaint (filed on 08/14/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant’s demurrer to the Complaint’s first cause of action is OVERRULED. Defendant’s motion to strike portions of the Complaint is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiffs Gloria Aispuro, by and through her successor in interest, Jesse Aispuro; Jesse Aispuro; Peter Aispuro; Martha Aispuro and Priscilla Ortiz filed this action against Defendant Beverly Community Hospital Association (“Hospital”) and Does 1-250.

 

The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Elder Abuse

2.      Negligence

3.      Wrongful Death

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendant Hospital neglected decedent Gloria Aispuro while she was a patient in Defendant’s care and she suffered from an avoidable aspiration event when provided solid foods in direct contravention of physician’s orders.

 

On August 14, 2024, Defendant Hospital filed the instant Demurrer and Motion to Strike portions of the Complaint, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Statutory Elder Abuse/Neglect of a Dependent Adult (Wel. & Instit. Code §15600 et seq.) fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant.

·       Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action for Statutory Elder Abuse/Neglect of a Dependent Adult (Wel. & Instit. Code §15600 et seq.) is uncertain as alleged Defendant.

·       Plaintiff Gloria Aispuro, by and through her Successor in Interest, Jesse Aispuro’s cause of action for Elder Abuse as written in the Complaint filed on June 4, 2024 conflicts with the terms of the Stipulation entered into between Plaintiffs’ counsel and the Bankruptcy Trustee on April 17, 2024, with regard to the grant of relief from the bankruptcy stay.

·       Defendant moves to strike portions Plaintiffs’ prayer for damages regarding, “3.… punitive and exemplary damages (as to First Cause of Action only); 4. … treble damages pursuant to Civil Code §3345 (as to First Cause of Action only).” Defendant asserts it conflicts with the terms of the Stipulation.

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Restrictions on the enforcement of any final judgment by the Bankruptcy Court has no bearing on the sufficiency of pleading causes of action no does it render the pleadings uncertain in the matter at hand

·       The entirety of this Demurrer is based upon the following provisions set forth in a Stipulation filed in a Bankruptcy Court proceeding which acted to lift the stay for Plaintiff to pursue this civil matter.

·       The Stipulation permits Plaintiff to proceed in the civil forum to final judgment, and appeal if necessary, in accordance with the applicable civil laws with no restrictions placed thereon and then, if there is a judgment, any collection thereof is restricted to any available insurance of the Defendant. No remedies available thereunder were modified whatsoever. All that was modified and restricted by the Bankruptcy Court is the means of collecting said remedies, if and when they are awarded.

·       Regardless of orders pertaining to post-judgment matters, facts were set forth in the Complaint sufficient to support a cause for an elder abuse action.

·       Defendant completely failed to address its claims of uncertainty in its Demurrer. The Order in the Bankruptcy Court does not impair Defendant’s ability to understand the allegations against it and does not prevent Defendant from responding to same.

·       Defendant’s motion to strike should be denied. Plaintiffs’ claims for enhanced remedies and treble damages in this matter does not exceed the terms of a Stipulation filed with a Bankruptcy Court in a separate proceeding.

Defendants filed a reply, maintaining that:

·       Plaintiffs’ must abide by the terms of the Stipulation and the Complaint as it stands now violates the agreed upon terms of the Stipulation. Here, the remedies regularly available for an elder abuse/neglect cause of action have been voluntarily limited via stipulation between Plaintiffs and the Bankruptcy Trustee in exchange for relief from the automatic bankruptcy stay.

·       The Stipulation on its face does not contemplate punitive damages, exemplary damages, treble damages per Civil Code section 3345, and attorney’s fees for Elder Abuse/Neglect, as such damages are usually not covered in a standard insurance policy.

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendant submitted a declaration stating the parties sent multiple meet and confer letters regarding the issues discussed instant motions. (Aziz Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) While the parties did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, the failure to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4); 435.5 subd. (a)(4).) Thus, the Court proceeds on the merits.

 

C.      Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant requests the Court take judicial notice of the following:

D.     First Cause of Action: Elder Abuse/Neglect of Dependent Adult

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs’ First Cause of Action for Statutory Elder Abuse/Neglect of a Dependent Adult (Wel. & Instit. Code §15600 et seq.) fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant and is uncertain, as per the terms of the Stipulation entered into between Plaintiffs’ counsel and the Bankruptcy Trustee on April 17, 2024.

 

To state a cause of action for elder abuse and neglect, Plaintiff must allege “[p]hysical abuse, neglect, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering.”  (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15610.07(a)(1).)  Acts which are prohibited under the Elder Abuse Act provisions of the Welfare and Institute Code “do not include acts of simple professional negligence[] but refer to forms of abuse or neglect performed with some state of culpability greater than mere negligence.”  (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 781.)  To constitute abuse and neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act, thereby triggering the enhanced remedies available under the Act, a plaintiff “must demonstrate by clear and convincing evidence that defendant is guilty of something more than negligence; he or she must show reckless, oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious conduct,” with recklessness referring “to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a “deliberate disregard” of the “high degree of probability” that an injury will occur,” and “[o]ppression, fraud and malice [involving] intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature.”  (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 88-89, cleaned up.)

 

On April 19, 2023, Defendant filed for Chapter 11 Bankruptcy (USBC, Case #: 2:23-bk-12359-SK; Def’s RJN, Exh. 1). Defendant has been in Chapter 11 Bankruptcy protection as of April 19, 2023 and the case is still being actively litigated. (Aziz Decl., ¶ 2.) On August 4, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the 2023 Complaint in superior court against Defendant alleging Elder Abuse, Negligence, and Wrongful Death, which was dismissed without prejudice as it violated the bankruptcy stay. Thereafter, Plaintiffs entered into a stipulation with the Bankruptcy Trustee on April 17, 2024, wherein the Trustee granted relief from the bankruptcy stay contingent on Plaintiffs agreeing to, “prosecute the action pursuant for the purpose of collecting upon available insurance if any in accordance with applicable non-bankruptcy law.” (Def’s RJN, Exh. 2.) Plaintiff subsequently filed the operative Complaint on June 4, 2024.

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ cause of action for Elder Abuse/ Neglect in the operative Complaint violates the Stipulation. In support, Defendant relies on the terms of the Stipulation. The Stipulation states, in relevant part:

“Movants are granted relief from stay to re-file their complaint as a new action, and to prosecute the action pursuant for the purpose of collecting upon available insurance if any in accordance with applicable non-bankruptcy law.

Movant may proceed in the non-bankruptcy forum with respect to the matter described in the Motion to final judgment (including any appeals) in accordance with applicable non-bankruptcy law. Movant is permitted to enforce its final judgment only by collecting upon any available insurance in accordance with applicable non-bankruptcy law.”

(Def’s RJN, Exh. 2, page 4, §1, b and c [emphasis added.])

The Court disagrees that Plaintiffs’ first cause of action violates the Stipulation. The terms of the Stipulation appear to only restrict how any potential judgment obtained in the matter will be collected. The Stipulation goes on to state, in relevant part:

 

“5. Limitations on Enforcement of Judgment: Movant may proceed in the nonbankruptcy forum to final judgment (including any appeals) in accordance with applicable nonbankruptcy law. Movant is permitted to enforce its final judgment only by (specify all that apply):

 

a. ¿Collecting upon any available insurance in accordance with applicable nonbankruptcy law. b. ¿Proceeding against the Debtor as to property or earnings that are not property of this bankruptcy estate.”

 

(Pltfs’ RJN, Exh. 1.) Therefore, based on the terms of the Stipulation, Plaintiffs are permitted to proceed in the civil forum to final judgment in accordance with the applicable civil laws with no restrictions placed thereon and then, if there is a judgment, any collection thereof is restricted to any available insurance of the Defendant. The Court does not find Defendant’s argument that the Stipulation modified the statutory and regularly available remedies persuasive, nor does Defendant provide any authority in support.

 

While Defendant also asserts in the Notice of Motion that Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is uncertain, Defendant fails to present any argument in support in the motion. CCP section 430.10(f) provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous and unintelligible. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.” (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.) “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.) Here, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is uncertain.

 

The Court notes that Defendant does not argue in its Motion that Plaintiffs fail to allege the requisite elements of its Statutory Elder Abuse/Neglect of a Dependent Adult cause of action. Therefore, the Court will not address this. Instead, the basis of Defendant’s demurrer is that Plaintiffs’ first cause of action conflicts with the terms of the Stipulation entered into between Plaintiffs’ counsel and the Bankruptcy Trustee. As discussed above, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ first cause of action conflicts with the terms of the Stipulation.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ first cause of action is OVERRULED.   

E.      Motion to Strike

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike a pleading or any part thereof.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)¿ The court¿may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)¿ The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)¿ An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., 431.10, subd. (b).)¿ The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)¿¿¿ 

 

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff Gloria Aispuro, by and through her Successor in Interest, Jesse Aispuro’s prayer for damages as to the cause of action for Elder Abuse, specifically portions of Plaintiffs’ prayer for damages regarding, “3.… punitive and exemplary damages (as to First Cause of Action only); 4. … treble damages pursuant to Civil Code §3345 (as to First Cause of Action only).” (Complaint, p. 14.)

 

Defendant argues the prayer for damages as to the cause of action for Elder Abuse as written in the Complaint filed on June 4, 2024 conflicts with the terms of the Stipulation entered into between Plaintiffs’ counsel and the Bankruptcy Trustee on April 17, 2024, with regard to the grant of relief from the bankruptcy stay.

 

However, as discussed above, the Court does not find Plaintiffs’ first cause of action conflicts with the terms of the Stipulation. The terms of the Stipulation appear to only restrict how any potential judgment obtained in the matter will be collected and does not modify the statutory and regularly available remedies.

Based on the foregoing, the motion to strike is DENIED.

 

F.       Conclusion

 

Defendant’s demurrer to the Complaint’s first cause of action is OVERRULED.  Defendant’s motion to strike portions of the Complaint is DENIED.
