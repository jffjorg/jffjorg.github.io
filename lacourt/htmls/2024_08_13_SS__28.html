DEPARTMENT 28 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SSCDEPT28@lacourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.     If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect.

                                       Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.  This does not excuse a moving party's need to do one of the following: appear; submit; or take a matter off calendar by canceling the motion in the case reservation system before issuance of the tentative ruling if the matter moving party does not intend to proceed.    
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  


Case Number: 21STCV03830    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On January 29, 2021, Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”), a minor, by and through her guardian ad litem, Renee Noye, filed this action against Defendants Long Beach Unified School District, Jane Does 1-15, and Does 16-100 for negligence, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. 

On May 31, 2022, the Court appointed Renee Noye to serve as Plaintiff’s guardian ad litem. 

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Long Beach Unified School District, Jane Does 1-15, and Does 16-100 for negligent supervision of students, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, and negligence. 

On October 8, 2022, Defendant Long Beach Unified School District (“Defendant”) filed an answer. 

On February 2, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication.  The motion was set for hearing on April 19, 2024.  On April 3, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application and continued the hearing to June 18, 2024 under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (h).  On May 30, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiff’s second ex parte application to continue the hearing.  On June 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition.  On June 13, 2024, Defendant filed a reply. The Court continued the hearing to July 30, 2024. 

On July 30, 2024, the Court heard argument and continued the hearing to August 13, 2024. 

Trial is currently scheduled for September 24, 2024. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Defendant asks the Court to grant summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication. 

Plaintiff asks the Court to deny the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

“‘[F]rom commencement to conclusion, the party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.’”  (LAOSD Asbestos Cases (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 949, 945, quoting Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (Aguilar)) “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 850.) 

When the moving party is a defendant, it must show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action.  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 853.) “The defendant has shown that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Id. at p. 854.) The defendant must “present evidence, and not simply point out that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Ibid.) Thus, “the defendant must ‘support[ ]’ the ‘motion’ with evidence including ‘affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice’ must or may ‘be taken.’ [Citation.] The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff’s cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.”  (Id. at p. 855, original emphasis.) 

“Supporting and opposing affidavits or declarations . . . shall set forth admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (d).) “Matters which would be excluded under the rules of evidence if proffered by a witness in a trial as hearsay, conclusions or impermissible opinions, must be disregarded in supporting affidavits.”  (Hayman v. Block (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 629, 639.) 

In addition, a party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must support the motion with “a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts which the moving party contends are undisputed. Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.” (Parkview Villas Assn. v. State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. (2006) 133 Cal.App.4th 1197, 1209 (Parkview Villas), quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(1).) The party opposing the motion must file with the opposition papers “a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating whether the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed. The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts that the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (b)(3).)  If either party fails to comply with the applicable separate statement requirement, that failure may in the court’s discretion constitute a sufficient ground to decide the motion adversely to the offending party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (b)(1), (3).)  

In ruling on the motion, the court must consider all the evidence and all the inferences reasonably drawn from it and must view such evidence and inferences in the light most favorable to the opposing party.  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 843.)  

DEFENDANT’S REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE 

          Denied. 

DEFENDANT’S EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS 

          Overruled. 

DISCUSSION 

A.   The first amended complaint 

The first amended complaint alleges the following: 

During the 2019-2020 school year, while Plaintiff was fifteen and sixteen years old and a student at Lakewood High School, several of her female classmates bullied her for approximately one year.  The bullying included taunting Plaintiff, ridiculing her, and calling her names.  The bullying occurred during class time, snack time, lunchtime, and at other times and locations on campus.  Plaintiff, her parents, and other students advised the school’s staff and administration of the bullying but the school failed to take any action to prevent or stop it. 

On an unspecified date, ten to fifteen tenth grade girls followed Plaintiff as she exited the school and walked to Pan American Park.  At the park, the girls physically attacked and brutalized Plaintiff. Plaintiff sustained a chronic subdural hematoma that required brain surgery, a fractured jaw, and brain bleed. 

Before the incident, the bullying was reported to the Vice-Principal.  The Vice-Principal failed to intervene. 

Prior to the attack, school security followed the girls to the park but failed to protect Plaintiff or prevent the altercation. 

The school minimized and covered up the bullying, leading Plaintiff’s parents to believe that the issues were minor and had been resolved.  The school’s staff and administration represented to Plaintiff's parents that the bullying had been resolved, that the bullying never really happened, and that if any bullying ever happened, it was much less severe than Plaintiff reported.  The school’s representations to Plaintiff’s parents were false.  Plaintiff’s parents relied on the representations.  The school administration falsely told Plaintiff that she was not being bullied and that she had never been bullied at the school. 

The District and Doe defendants breached their duty to properly and adequately supervise Plaintiff and the other students and to take the necessary steps to prevent further bullying, causing Plaintiff’s injury. 

The first amended complaint asserts a claim against the District for negligent supervision of students under Government Code sections 815.2, 815.6, and 820 (first cause of action). 

B.   The motion for summary judgment or adjudication 

1.    Defendant has carried its initial burden on summary judgment 

Defendant argues the Court should grant summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication because Plaintiff allegedly was attacked after school at Pan American Park, which was not part of the school premises.  Defendant relies on Education Code section 44808, which provides: 

“Notwithstanding any other provision of this code, no school district, city or county board of education, county superintendent of schools, or any officer or employee of such district or board shall be responsible or in any way liable for the conduct or safety of any pupil of the public schools at any time when such pupil is not on school property, unless such district, board, or person has undertaken to provide transportation for such pupil to and from the school premises, has undertaken a school-sponsored activity off the premises of such school, has otherwise specifically assumed such responsibility or liability or has failed to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances. 

“In the event of such a specific undertaking, the district, board, or person shall be liable or responsible for the conduct or safety of any pupil only while such pupil is or should be under the immediate and direct supervision of an employee of such district or board.”

(Educ. Code, § 44808.) 

“A school district ‘owes a duty of care to its students because a special relationship exists between the students and the district.’ ” (Brinsmead v. Elk Grove Unified School Dist. (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 583, 588, quoting Guerrero v. South Bay Union School Dist. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 264, 268.)  “But that special relationship does not create liability on its own.”  (Ibid.) “ ‘ “The liability of public entities is entirely statutory.” ’ ” (Ibid., quoting Bassett v. Lakeside Inn, Inc. (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 863, 869.)  “A school district's liability is limited by [Education Code] section 44808.”  (Ibid.)  “Under section 44808, a district is immune from liability ‘for the conduct or safety of any pupil of the public schools at any time when [the] pupil is not on school property.’ ” (Ibid., quoting Educ. Code, § 44808; see Cerna v. City of Oakland (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 1340, 1357 [under Education Code section 44808, “school districts are not responsible for the safety of students outside school property absent a specific undertaking by the school district and direct supervision by a district employee”].) 

Defendant has presented evidence that Plaintiff was not on school property when other students allegedly attacked and injured her after school had ended for the day.  Based on this evidence, Defendant has carried its initial burden on summary judgment of proving it is entitled to immunity under Education Code section 88404, shifting the burden to Plaintiff. 

2.    Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue of fact 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant is responsible for her injuries because Defendant’s employees failed to take steps, while Plaintiff and her attackers were on school grounds, to stop the bullying and prevent it from escalating. 

Plaintiff relies on Hoyem v. Manhattan Beach Sch. Dist. (1978) 22 Cal.3d 508 (Hoyem), where a student left the school premises before the end of scheduled classes and was struck by a motorcycle at a public intersection.  (Hoyem, supra, 22 Cal.3d at p. 512.)  The student sued the school district, alleging the school district’s negligent supervision caused his injuries.  (Ibid.)  The trial court sustained the school district’s demurrer and dismissed the case.  (Ibid.) 

The Supreme Court reversed, holding that the complaint stated a cause of action because it alleged the school district failed to exercise ordinary care in supervising the student while he was on school premises, causing the injury.  (Hoyem, supra, 22 Cal.3d at pp. 513-514.)  The district's duty to supervise included a duty to enforce the regulation forbidding students from leaving school premises “ ‘at any other time before the regular hour for closing school . . . .’ ”  (Id. at p. 514, quoting Title 5, Cal. Admin. Code, § 303.) 

Addressing Education Code section 44808, the Court stated: “Although the initial portion of the statute provides that ‘no school district shall be responsible . . . for the conduct or safety of any pupil . . . at any time when such pupil is not on school property,’ the section goes on explicitly to withdraw this grant of immunity whenever the school district, inter alia, ‘has failed to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances.’”  (Hoyem, supra, 22 Cal.3d at p. 517.)  Because Plaintiff’s complaint alleged that the school district failed to exercise reasonable care, the statute did not bar liability at the demurrer stage.  (Ibid.) 

The Court of Appeal addressed the scope of Hoyem’s holding in LeRoy v. Yarboi (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 737 (LeRoy), where a student who had been bullied at school committed suicide at home.  (LeRoy, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 739.) In LeRoy, the student's parents sued the school district, alleging it was liable for their son’s suicide because of its inadequate response to his complaints of bullying by his classmates. (Ibid.)  The trial court granted summary judgment for the district.  (Ibid.)

On appeal, the parents relied on Hoyem to support their argument that Education Code section 44808 did not immunize the school district because it “ ‘failed to exercise reasonable care under the circumstances.’ ”  (LeRoy, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 743, quoting Educ. Code, § 44808.)  The Court of Appeal rejected the argument, stating: 

“We acknowledge that some language from Hoyem suggests that school districts and their employees may be liable for injuries a student suffers off-campus and after school. [Citation.] But [Education Code] section 44808 has been interpreted as imposing liability on schools districts for a student’s off-campus injury only when ‘the student is involved in activities supervised or undertaken by the school.’  [Citation.]  In fact, the ‘consensus of decisions from the Court of Appeal is that “[Education Code] section 44808 limits the liability of schools for after-hours, off-campus activity, absent a specific undertaking” ’ ” by the school during which a student is injured.  [Citation.]  Thus, with one exception, every court since Hoyem, including this court, ‘has interpreted [Education Code] section 44808 to provide that school districts are not responsible for the safety of students outside school property absent a specific undertaking by the school district and direct supervision by a district employee.’  [Citations.] 

“We agree with the great weight of authority that [Education Code] section 44808 “ ‘ “grants a district [and its employees] immunity [for a student’s injuries] unless [the] student was (or should have been) directly supervised during a specified undertaking.” ’ [Citation.]”  [Citations.]  We in turn agree that ‘[t]he portion of [Education Code] section 44808 that refers to failing to exercise reasonable care’ that the [plaintiffs] rely on ‘does not create a common law form of general negligence; it refers to the failure to exercise reasonable care during one of the [three] mentioned undertakings’ in [Education Code] section 44808.  [Citations.]” 

(LeRoy, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at pp. 743-744.) 

          The Court of Appeal concluded that the student committed suicide off-campus during summer break at his home while he was not and should not have been supervised by the school district or its employees.  (LeRoy, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 744.)  Therefore, the district was immune from liability under Education Code section 44808.  (Ibid.)

Plaintiff argues that the Vice Principal's promise to "get to the bottom" of the problem is evidence of a specific undertaking that creates an exception to immunity under Education Code section 44808.  The statutory phrase "otherwise specifically assumed such responsibility or liability," on which Plaintiff relies, necessarily refers back to the statutory provision, "shall be responsible or in any way liable for the conduct or safety of any pupil of the public schools at any time when such pupil is not on school property . . . ."  (Educ. Code, § 44808.)  Plaintiff has presented no evidence that the Vice Principal's promise to "get to the bottom" of the problem amounted to an undertaking to be responsible or liable for the conduct or safety of pupils when they were not on school property.      

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue of fact concerning immunity under Education Code section 88404.  Therefore, the Court grants Defendant’s motion for summary judgment. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Long Beach Unified School District’s motion for summary judgment.

Moving party is ordered to file the proof of service of this ruling within five days.

Case Number: 21STCV19791    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving, opposition, reply, and supplemental papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

A.   Prior proceedings 

On May 26, 2021, Plaintiff Sedigheh Hajizadeh (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Magic Mountain, LLC (“Defendant”) and Does 1-50 for general negligence and premises liability. 

On June 21, 2021, Defendant filed an answer.

On January 30, 2024, the Court granted in part and denied in part Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication of Defendant’s affirmative defenses.  The Court summarily adjudicated Defendant’s statute of limitations affirmative defense in Plaintiff's favor.  The Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication of (1) Defendant’s assumption of risk affirmative defense to the extent the affirmative defense involves the doctrine of secondary assumption of risk (which is merged into Defendant’s comparative negligence affirmative defense), (2) Defendant’s comparative negligence affirmative defense, (3) Defendant’s fault of others affirmative defense, and (4) Defendant’s Proposition 51 affirmative defense. 

On May 9, 2024, Department 1 of this Court (“Department 1”) denied Defendant’s motion to transfer and consolidate two cases filed in Santa Clara County Superior Court with this case (Los Angeles Superior Court case number 21STCV1971).  Department 1 stated that Plaintiff's complaint in the first Santa Clara action (21CV376599) raises the same causes of action as this case and has been dismissed.  In the second Santa Clara action (24CV433621), Plaintiff “seeks declaratory relief as a collateral attack on a judgment.” 

Department 1 found that this case (“the Los Angeles action”) did not share predominating and significant common questions of law or fact with the two Santa Clara actions:  “The Los Angeles action is a personal injury action, which seeks to adjudicate Defendant’s liability for Plaintiff’s injuries. The initial Santa Clara action, 21CV376599, has been reduced to judgment with the only pending issue whether Plaintiff is entitled to change the voluntary dismissal from one with prejudice to one without. [Citation.] Thus, Plaintiff has no intention of continuing to litigate the personal injury action in Santa Clara. In the second Santa Clara action, 24CV433621, Plaintiff seeks to collaterally attack the dismissal entered in 21CV376599 as void through an action for declaratory relief. [Citation.] Thus, the merits of Plaintiff’s personal injury claims will not be at issue in either Santa Clara case. Defendant’s asserted res judicata defenses in the Los Angeles case, based upon the dismissal in 21CV376599, are not a predominating common issue of fact or law with the other cases. Rather, it is a potential legal result based upon the status of the judgment entered in the first action.” 

In addition, Department 1 observed that “Defendant’s pending motion and defense in the Los Angeles action is based upon the outcome of Plaintiff’s efforts to alter the judgment of dismissal entered in 21CV376599 . . . .”  Accordingly, “any potential risk of [inconsistent] rulings arising from Defendant’s assertions of the res judicata defenses in the Los Angeles case is more efficiently resolved by a stay or continuance to allow the Santa Clara court to address the issues raised regarding its judgment in 21CV376599.” 

On August 7, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for leave to amend its answer to add the following affirmative defenses: estoppel (tenth affirmative defense), res judicata and collateral estoppel (eleventh affirmative defense), and waiver (twelfth affirmative defense). 

B.   This motion 

On March 11, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings and request for judicial notice.  The hearing was set for April 5, 2024.  On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition, request for judicial notice, and objection.  On March 28, 2024, Defendant filed a reply and reply to Plaintiff’s objection.  On June 28, 2024, Defendant filed a supplemental memorandum and request for judicial notice.  The Court continued the hearing to August 13, 2024. 

No trial date is currently scheduled. 

PARTIES’ REQUESTS 

Defendant asks the Court to grant its motion for judgment on the pleadings. 

Plaintiff asks the Court to deny the motion. 

LEGAL STANDARD         

A.   Judgment on the pleadings 

Code of Civil Procedure section 438 provides in part: 

“(b) (1) A party may move for judgment on the pleadings. 

* * *

 “(c) (1) The motion provided for in this section may only be made on one of the following grounds: 

* * *

 “(B) If the moving party is a defendant, that either of the following conditions exist: 

“(i) The court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint. 

“(ii) The complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant. 

“(2) The motion provided for in this section may be made as to either of the following: 

“(A) The entire complaint or cross-complaint or as to any of the causes of action stated therein. 

* * *

 “(d) The grounds for motion provided for in this section shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. Where the motion is based on a matter of which the court may take judicial notice pursuant to Section 452 or 453 of the Evidence Code, the matter shall be specified in the notice of motion, or in the supporting points and authorities, except as the court may otherwise permit. 

“(e) No motion may be made pursuant to this section if a pretrial conference order has been entered pursuant to Section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits. 

* * *

 “(h) (1) The motion provided for in this section may be granted with or without leave to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be. 

“(2) Where a motion is granted pursuant to this section with leave to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be, then the court shall grant 30 days to the party against whom the motion was granted to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be. 

“(3) If the motion is granted with respect to the entire complaint or answer without leave to file an amended complaint or answer, as the case may be, then judgment shall be entered forthwith in accordance with the motion granting judgment to the moving party. . . .” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subds. (b), (c), (d), (e), (h).) 

‘‘A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and [thus] attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. [Citations.]’ [Citation.]’ [Citation.]” (Pointe San Diego Residential Community, L.P. v. Procopio, Cory, Hargreaves & Savitch, LLP (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 265, 274.) “In reviewing the motion, [the Court] deem[s] true all properly pleaded material facts, but not contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law, and [the court] may also consider judicially noticed matters. [Citation.]” (Bear Creek Master Assn. v. Southern California Investors, Inc. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 809, 817.) 

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings lies where the court can take judicial notice of a prior action between the parties as the basis for collateral estoppel (or res judicata) in the present action.”  (L. Edmon and C. Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter 2024) ¶ 7:294, pp. 7(l)-95 to 7(l)-96, citing Barker v. Hull (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 221, 227 & Bucur v. Ahmad (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 175, 186-187 (Bucur).) 

B.   Res judicata 

“ “The doctrine of res judicata bars a party and persons in privity with that party from relitigating a claim following a final judgment on the merits of the claim.” (Roberson v. City of Rialto (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1499, 1510.) “The prerequisite elements of res judicata in its claim preclusion form are (1) the claim in the present action must be identical to a claim litigated or that could have been litigated in a prior proceeding; (2) the prior proceeding resulted in a final judgment on the merits; and (3) the party against whom the doctrine is being asserted was a party or in privity with a party to the prior proceeding. [Citations.]” (Bucur, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at p. 185.) 

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE         

          The Court grants the parties’ requests for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).) 

PLAINTIFF’S OBJECTIONS 

A.   Inadequate meet and confer effort 

Overruled.  “A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to grant or deny the motion for judgment on the pleadings.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 439, subd. (a)(4).) 

B.   Timeliness 

Overruled.  No trial date is currently set.  The Court finds that the motion is timely under Code of Civil Procedure section 438, subdivision (e). 

DISCUSSION 

Defendant asks the Court to grant judgment on the pleadings on Plaintiff’s claims for general negligence and premises liability because the Santa Clara County Superior Court dismissed another case, in which Plaintiff alleged the same facts and claims against Defendant, with prejudice at Plaintiff’s request.  According to Defendant, the doctrine of res judicata prevents Plaintiff from asserting these claims in this subsequently-filed case.   (See Motion pp. 4-5, citing Alpha Mechanical, Heating & Air Conditioning, Inc. v. Travelers Casualty & Surety Co. of America (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1319, 1326-1331; Roybal v. Univ. Ford (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 1080, 1085 [“Roybal's voluntary dismissal with prejudice constituted a determination on the merits and was res judicata”].) 

Plaintiff opposes the motion, first arguing that a statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings cannot be based on res judicata.  However, at least two Court of Appeal decisions have assumed that res judicata principles can, if applicable, support a statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings (although neither decision directly addressed the issue).  (See State Compensation Insurance Fund v. ReadyLink Healthcare, Inc. (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 422, 445-465 [reversing judgment on the pleadings because res judicata did not apply]; Bucur, supra, 244 Cal.App.4th at pp. 186-187 [affirming order granting statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings based on res judicata, judicial admissions, and judicial estoppel].)  Plaintiff has cited no case authority to the contrary.  The Court declines to adopt the analysis contained in the treatise Plaintiff cites, which asserts that Code of Civil Procedure section 438 “apparently” precludes use of res judicata as the basis for a statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings. 

Plaintiff also argues that the two actions assert different injuries and therefore are not identical for res judicata purposes.  Plaintiff’s form complaints in both the Santa Clara and Los Angeles actions checked a box alleging that Plaintiff’s injuries occurred within the respective Courts’ jurisdictions.  However, as Defendant points out, both complaints also alleged that Plaintiff was injured on August 19, 2019, at 26101 Magic Mountain Parkway, Valencia, CA, 91355.  Therefore, the alleged injuries are the same for purposes of res judicata. 

Plaintiff’s third argument – that the dismissal in the first action is void because Plaintiff did not authorize it – was the subject of Plaintiff’s motion asking the Santa Clara Superior Court to vacate or otherwise set aside the dismissal and enter a new order dismissing the case without prejudice.  On June 20, 2024, the Court denied the motion, concluding the dismissal was voidable rather than void and that Plaintiff did not seek timely relief. 

In addition to the motion to vacate the dismissal, Plaintiff filed a declaratory relief action in the Santa Clara Superior Court to set aside the dismissal as unauthorized and therefore void as a matter of law.  According to Defendant, Defendant’s demurrer in the declaratory relief action is set for hearing on August 22, 2024. 

As Department 1 observed on May 9, 2024, “Defendant’s pending motion and defense in the Los Angeles action is based upon the outcome of Plaintiff’s efforts to alter the judgment of dismissal entered in 21CV376599 . . . .”  Thus, “any potential risk of [inconsistent] rulings arising from Defendant’s assertions of the res judicata defenses in the Los Angeles case is more efficiently resolved by a stay or continuance to allow the Santa Clara court to address the issues raised regarding its judgment in 21CV376599.”   

Although the Santa Clara court addressed these issues in its June 20, 2024 order, the Court cannot predict how the Santa Clara Court will address these issues in the pending declaratory relief action.  The Court therefore continues the hearing on Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings to a date to be provided at the August 13, 2024 hearing. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court CONTINUES the hearing on Defendant Magic Mountain, LLC’s motion for judgment on the pleadings to a date to be provided at the August 13, 2024 hearing.

BACKGROUND 

On August 26, 2021, Plaintiff Malvina Mkchyan (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Margaret Mary Lewis (“Defendant”) and Does 1-50 for motor vehicle tort and general negligence. 

On May 15, 2023, Defendant filed an answer.

 On July 17, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to compel Plaintiff’s attendance and testimony at a deposition and for sanctions.  Plaintiff has not filed an opposition. 

Trial is currently scheduled for February 19, 2025. 

PARTY’S REQUESTS 

Defendant asks the Court to compel Plaintiff’s attendance and testimony at a deposition and to award sanctions. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450 provides in part: 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. 

“(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following: 

“(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. 

“(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. 

* * *

 “(g) (1) If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. . . .” 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subds. (a), (b), (g)(1).)

Plaintiff did not attend properly noticed depositions on May 10, 2024 and July 2, 2024 and did not serve timely objections to the deposition notices.  Defendant’s counsel attempted to meet and confer after the July 2, 2024 non-appearance, without success. 

The Court grants the motion and orders Plaintiff to attend a deposition within 30 days of the date of the hearing on this motion. 

Defendant requests $1,191.65 in sanctions based on two hours of attorney time at a rate of $250.00 per hour, one $61.65 filing fee, $265.00 in court reporter’s fees for the May 10, 2024 late cancellation, and $365.00 in court reporter’s fees for the July 2, 2024 late cancellation. 

The Court awards $1,191.65 in sanctions. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Margaret Mary Lewis’s motion to compel Plaintiff Malvina Mkchyan’s deposition.  The Court orders Plaintiff Malvina Mkchyan to appear and testify at a deposition within 30 days of the hearing on this motion. 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Margaret Mary Lewis’s request for sanctions and orders Plaintiff Malvina Mkchyan and her counsel to pay Defendant Margaret Mary Lewis $1,191.65 within 30 days of the hearing on this motion.

Case Number: 23STCV30306    Hearing Date: August 13, 2024    Dept: 28

Having considered the moving papers, the Court rules as follows. 

BACKGROUND 

On December 12, 2023, Plaintiffs Briana Barr (“Briana Barr”), Denise Corbin (“Corbin”), and Kairos Barr (“Kairos Barr”), a minor, by and through her guardian ad litem Briana Barr filed this action against Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc. (“Uber”), Jiajia Doe, and Does 1-100 for assault, battery, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligence, and negligent hiring, retention, and supervision. 

On December 14, 2023, the Court appointed Briana Barr to serve as Kairos Barr’s guardian ad litem. 

On March 4, 2024, Uber filed an answer. 

On June 5, 2024, Uber filed a motion to compel arbitration. The motion was set for hearing on July 2, 2024.  The Court continued the hearing to August 13, 2024.  Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition. 

Trial is currently set for June 10, 2025. 

PARTY’S REQUESTS 

Uber asks the Court (1) to compel Plaintiffs to submit their claims to arbitration and (2) to stay the case pending completion of arbitration. 

LEGAL STANDARD 

“A written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable, and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the rescission of any contract.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 provides in part: 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: 

“(a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or 

“(b) Grounds exist for rescission of the agreement." 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) 

“Arbitration is a favored procedure. An ‘ “ ‘arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.’ ” ’ ” (Salgado v. Carrows Restaurants, Inc. (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 356, 360, quoting Cruise v. Kroger Co. (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 390, 397.) “Doubts about the applicability of the arbitration clause to the dispute should be resolved ‘in favor of sending the parties to arbitration.’ ” (Ibid., quoting Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 625, 642.) 

“[N]o single test delineates the nature of the conduct that will constitute a waiver of arbitration.”   (St. Agnes Medical Center v. PacifiCare of California (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1187, 1195 (St. Agnes).) “ ‘ “In the past, California courts have found a waiver of the right to demand arbitration in a variety of contexts, ranging from situations in which the party seeking to compel arbitration has previously taken steps inconsistent with an intent to invoke arbitration [citations] to instances in which the petitioning party has unreasonably delayed in undertaking the procedure. [Citations.] The decisions likewise hold that the “bad faith” or “wilful misconduct” of a party may constitute a waiver and thus justify a refusal to compel arbitration. [Citations.]” ’ ” (Id. at p. 1196, quoting Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 983.) 

In St. Agnes, the Supreme Court agreed that the following factors are relevant and properly considered in assessing waiver claims: “(1) whether the party's actions are inconsistent with the right to arbitrate; (2) whether ‘the litigation machinery has been substantially invoked’ and the parties ‘were well into preparation of a lawsuit’ before the party notified the opposing party of an intent to arbitrate; (3) whether a party either requested arbitration enforcement close to the trial date or delayed for a long period before seeking a stay; (4) whether a defendant seeking arbitration filed a counterclaim without asking for a stay of the proceedings; (5) ‘whether important intervening steps [e.g., taking advantage of judicial discovery procedures not available in arbitration] had taken place’; and (6) whether the delay ‘affected, misled, or prejudiced’ the opposing party.” ’ ” (St. Agnes, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 1196, quoting Sobremonte v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 980, 992.) 

DISCUSSION         

Uber has provided evidence that Corbin expressly agreed to arbitrate her claims against Uber by accepting Uber’s Terms of Use.  According to Uber, Corbin violated her agreement by filing this action in state court.  As a result, Uber argues, the Court should compel Corbin to arbitrate her claims against Uber. 

In addition, Uber asserts that the Terms of Use required Briana Barr and Kairos Barr, as Corbin's guest riders, to arbitrate their claims as well.  (See Memorandum of Points and Authorities (“MPA”) p. 7.) 

“ ‘Both the federal government and California have strong public policies “ ‘in favor of arbitration as an expeditious and cost-effective way of resolving disputes.” ’ ” (In re Uber Technologies Wage & Hour Cases (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 1297, 1304, cert. petition pending, quoting People v. Maplebear Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 923, 930 (Maplebear).)  “But ‘[e]ven though the “ ‘law favors contracts for arbitration of disputes between parties’ [citation], ‘ “there is no policy compelling persons to accept arbitration of controversies which they have not agreed to arbitrate ....’ ” ’ ” ’ ” (Id. at pp. 1304-1305, quoting Maplebear, supra, 81 Cal.App.5th at p. 931.) 

With limited exceptions, only parties to an arbitration agreement can enforce it or be required to arbitrate. (See Westra v. Marcus & Millichap Real Estate Investment Brokerage Co., Inc. (2005) 129 Cal.App.4th 759, 763.)  But nonsignatories of arbitration agreements may be bound under ordinary contract and agency principles.  (See Philadelphia Indemnity Ins. Co. v. SMG Holdings, Inc. (2019) 44 Cal.App.5th 834, 840-841 (Philadelphia).) 

According to Uber, Briana Barr and Kairos Barr – who did not enter into an arbitration agreement with Uber – “are bound to the arbitration agreement [between Uber and Corbin] because they were an intended third-party beneficiary under both contract and common law, because of the principles of estoppel.”  (MPA p. 15.)

“Whether a nonsignatory is an intended third party beneficiary to the contract is determined from the parties’ intent, as gleaned from the contract as a whole and the circumstances under which it arose.”  (Philadelphia, supra, 44 Cal.App.5th at p. 841, quoting Epitech, Inc. v. Kann (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 1365, 1371–1372.) “ “ ‘The test for determining whether a contract was made for the benefit of a third person is whether an intent to benefit a third person appears from the terms of the contract.” ” ” ” (Pillar Project AG v. Payward Ventures, Inc. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 671, 677 (Pillar Project); see id. at p. 671 [the “ “ ‘mere fact that a contract results in benefits to a third party does not render that party a ‘third party beneficiary” ” ”].)  In Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817 (Goonewardene), the Supreme Court clarified that “the contracting parties must have a motivating purpose to benefit the third party, and not simply knowledge that a benefit to the third party may follow from the contract.”  (Goonewardene, supra, 6 Cal.5th at p. 830.) 

“Under the equitable estoppel theory, a nonsignatory ‘ “is estopped from avoiding arbitration if it knowingly seeks the benefits of the contract containing the arbitration clause.” ’ ” (Philadelphia, supra, 44 Cal.App.5th at p. 841, quoting Crowley Maritime Corp. v. Boston Old Colony Ins. Co. (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1061, 1070.)  “Equitable estoppel, thus, ‘ “precludes a party from claiming the benefits of a contract while simultaneously attempting to avoid the burdens that contract imposes.” ’ ” (Ibid., quoting Comer v. Micor, Inc. (9th Cir. 2006) 436 F.3d 1098, 1101.)  A nonsignatory plaintiff may be estopped from refusing to arbitrate when he or she asserts claims that are dependent upon, or inextricably intertwined with the underlying contractual obligations of the agreement containing the arbitration clause.  (Pillar Project, supra, 64 Cal.App.5th at p. 677.) 

Uber has not carried its burden of proving that Briana Barr and Kairos Barr are third-party beneficiaries of the agreement between Uber and Corbin.  Although Uber argues that the Terms of Use “specifically identified” Briana Barr and Kairos Barr, Uber has not shown that Corbin and Uber had “a motivating purpose to benefit [Briana Barr and Kairos Barr], and not simply knowledge that a benefit to the third party may follow from the contract.”  (Goonewardene, supra, 6 Cal.5th at p. 830.) 

Uber also argues that Briana Barr and Kairos Barr are equitably estopped from avoiding arbitration because their action is based on their use of Uber’s services, “which arose out of the use of Plaintiff Corbin’s Rider App and Account.”  (MPA p. 17.)  But Uber has not shown that Briana Barr and Kairos Barr “knowingly” sought the benefits of an agreement containing an arbitration clause.  (See Philadelphia, supra, 44 Cal.App.5th at p. 841.)  Uber has presented no evidence that Briana Barr and Kairos Barr knew about the Terms of Use or the arbitration clause. 

The Court grants the motion to compel arbitration of Corbin’s claims against Uber.  The Court denies Uber’s motion to compel arbitration of Briana Barr’s and Kairos Barr’s claims against Uber. 

CONCLUSION 

The Court GRANTS Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.’s motion to compel Plaintiff Denise Corbin to arbitrate her claims against Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc. 

The Court DENIES Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.’s motion to compel Plaintiff Briana Barr to arbitrate her claims against Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc. 

The Court DENIES Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.’s motion to compel Plaintiff Kairos Barr to arbitrate her claims against Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc. 

The Court STAYS proceedings on Plaintiff Denise Corbin’s claims against Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.  In all other respects, the Court DENIES Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.’s request for a stay.
