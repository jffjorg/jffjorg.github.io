     CASE NO.: 22VECV02302

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF KEVORK VORPERIAN, M.D.

 

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This is a personal injury case between Plaintiff Zina Huseynova (“Plaintiff”), and Defendants Saade Nowyhed (“Saade”) and Yasmin Nowyhed (“Yasmin”)(collectively, “Defendants”). Plaintiff alleges Defendants negligently drove their vehicle and injured Plaintiff because of their negligence and reckless driving, which thereby caused the collision. (See Complaint, pp. 4-5, ¶¶ MV-1-2, GN-1.)

 Defendants now seek to compel the deposition of Kevork Vorperian, M.D., (“Dr. Vorperian”) and monetary sanctions in the amount of $2,086.65 against Dr. Vorperian in a filed motion.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On December 9, 2022, Plaintiff filed a Complaint, alleging (1) Motor Vehicle; and (2) General Negligence.

On April 4, 2023, Defendants filed their Answer.

On October 15, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel (“Motion”) the Deposition of Dr. Vorperian.[1]

Plaintiff has not filed any Opposition.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a)[2] provides that, “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action… without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it…, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.410(a), provides that, “[a]ny party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with [the notice requirements in Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2025.210 through 2025.290] waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.”

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(b)(2) requires the motion to compel deposition be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition, by a declaration stating the moving party has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.290, which provides for a limit of seven hours of total testimony of a witness, does not apply to the deposition of a person designated as the person most qualified to be deposed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.290(b)(5).) 

IV.          MEET AND CONFER

A motion set forth under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450 must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration or, when the deponent does not attend the deposition, a “declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).)

Here, Defendants’ counsel has noted Dr. Vorperian’s failure to meet and confer, which has resulted in delays in relevant discovery completion and necessary law and motion work. (See Motion, pg. 6:14-16.)

Defendants’ counsel filed a meet and confer declaration, reflecting their meet and confer efforts. (See Declaration of Alan P. Trafton filed October 15, 2024 (“Trafton Decl.”), ¶ 10, Ex. G.) Defendants’ counsel details the inadequacy of record receival from Dr. Vorperian following the subpoena for production of records. (See Trafton Decl., ¶¶ 6-7.) Defendants’ counsel also further details the attempt to retrieve Plaintiff’s full file from Dr. Vorperian’s office. (See Trafton Decl., ¶ 8.) Unsuccessful, Defendants’ counsel served notice of deposition and request for production of documents from Dr. Vorperian. (See Trafton Decl., ¶ 9.) Demonstrating their efforts, Defendants’ counsel also detailed their mailed and emailed correspondence reminding Dr. Vorperian of such deposition and even provided Dr. Vorperian with an option of rescheduling at a mutually convenient date. (See Trafton Decl., pg. 10, ¶ 10.) Defendants’ counsel even informed Dr. Vorperian of the consequences if he failed to attend his deposition. (See Trafton Decl., pg. 10, ¶ 11.)

The Court finds this meet and confer effort inadequate. A handful of emails and letters reflects a disingenuous and nominal effort to avoid this Court’s involvement. Defendants’ counsel could have made further attempts by contacting Plaintiff’s counsel. The Court will discuss this failure to properly meet and confer in its sanctions section, below.

V.             ANALYSIS

          Defendants argue they first noticed Dr. Vorperian’s deposition on September 9, 2024, for a deposition of October 4, 2024. (See Trafton Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. F.)

Defendants also mailed and emailed correspondence to Dr. Vorperian to remind him of his deposition and further offered to reschedule if needed to a date that is mutually convenient. (See id., ¶ 10, Ex. G.) Further, Defendants ensured they informed Dr. Vorperian of the consequences, that being, filing of Motion to Compel with sanctions, of his failure to appear for the deposition. (See id., ¶ 11.)

          Dr. Vorperian did not respond to the letter. (See id.) Further, Dr. Vorperian did not serve written objections to the deposition, or the documents requested either. (See id., ¶ 12.) Dr. Vorperian failed to appear for his deposition leading to a Non-Appearance. (See id., ¶ 13, Ex. H.) Defendant then brought the instant Motion, which is unopposed.

          Defendant is entitled to an order compelling Dr. Vorperian’s deposition. It is undisputed that Dr. Vorperian was served with a valid deposition subpoena compelling his deposition attendance and production of requested documents. (See Motion, p. 5:22-25.) Similarly, there is no dispute that Dr. Vorperian never objected to the deposition or the requested documents. (See Motion, p. 5:25-27.) Since the deposition would lead to discoverable information regarding plaintiff’s claimed injuries and Dr. Vorperian has failed to identify any irregularity or defect in the subpoena, there is no justification for his refusal to respond or appear for the deposition.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion to Compel Dr. Vorperian’s deposition. The Court orders Dr. Vorperian to comply with Defendants’ subpoena on or before December 12, 2024.

VI.          SANCTIONS

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(g)(1) provides: “[i]f a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

Defendants seek $2,086.65 in sanctions against Dr. Vorperian, accounting for

Defendants’ Preparation of Motion, declaration, and assembling of relevant exhibits (5 hrs) and anticipation of Appearance for Hearing on Motion (1 hr). (See Trafton Decl., pg. 11, ¶ 15.) Defendants counsel hourly rate is $225 with the cost of filing this Motion $61.65. In total, Defendants seek $2,086.65 in sanctions, which the Court finds reasonable under the totality of the circumstances.

          Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

          The Court agrees with Defendants. Defendants made attempts to retrieve the production of records from Dr. Vorperian prior to serving the notice of deposition. (See Trafton Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.) Only after being unsuccessful and receiving partial records did Defendants resort to serving notice of the deposition. (See Trafton Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.) Acting in good faith to maintain cordial communication with Dr. Vorperian, Defendants did their efforts in trying to ensure Dr. Vorperian’s appearance by reminding him of the scheduled deposition and stating their willingness to accommodate the date. In response, Dr. Vorperian has maintained complete radio silence, which is unacceptable.

Accordingly, the Court orders Dr. Vorperian to pay Defendants’ counsel reasonable attorneys fees in the amount of $2,086.65 as a discovery sanction on or before December 12, 2024.

VII.    CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendants Motions to Compel Dr. Vorperian’s deposition. The Court orders Dr. Vorperian to comply with Defendants’ subpoena on or before December 12, 2024. Similarly, the Court orders Dr. Vorperian to pay Defendants’ counsel their reasonable attorneys fees of $2,086.65 as a discovery sanction on or before December 12, 2024.

          IT IS SO ORDERED.

I.               INTRODUCTION

This case stems from an incident where Plaintiff Rolando Morados (“Rolando”) allegedly sustained bodily injuries. On or about August 02, 2024, while Rolando was entering the Toyota Sienna (“Vehicle”) owned and operated by Defendant Arnel Gacuson (“Gacuson”), the strap affixed to the Vehicle, which Rolando was holding for support, broke, resulting in injuries to him. (See generally Complaint). At the time of the incident, Gacuson was functioning as a common carrier, and acting as an agent, employee or contractor for Defendant East Valley ADHC, Inc. (See id.) On April 16, 2024, Rolando and Plaintiff Evelyn Morados (“Evelyn”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed the complaint in this instant matter, alleging negligence. (See id.) Plaintiffs further contend that, as a result of the incident, Evelyn has suffered a loss of consortium, including deprivation of sexual relations, affection, companionship, and other forms of support from Morados. (See id.)     

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On April 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint alleging (1) Motor Vehicle Negligence; (2) General Negligence.

On June 28, 2024, Defendants filed their Answer to the Complaint.

On October 9, 2024, Defendants filed this instant motion

Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition to this motion.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

As a general rule, “if a party against whom a complaint has been filed and served fails to allege in a cross-complaint any related cause of action which (at the time of serving his answer to the complaint) he has against the plaintiff,” that party is barred from asserting against the plaintiff “the related cause of action not pleaded.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 426.30(a)[1].) If a party fails to plead a cause of action when otherwise required to do so, “whether through oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause, [they] may apply to the court for leave to amend his pleading, or to file a cross-complaint, to assert such cause at any time during the course of the action.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 426.50.) So long as the adverse party has received notice and the party who failed to plead the cause “acted in good faith,” “the court shall grant … leave to amend the pleading, or to file the cross-complaint …. This subdivision shall be liberally construed to avoid forfeiture of causes of action. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 426.50.) Parties must file their cross-complaints “against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 428.50(a).) “Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 428.50(b).) Leave of court is required for any cross-complaint except one filed within the time specified in subdivision (a) or (b). (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 428.50(c).) In addition, “[l]eave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.” (Id.)

A cross-complaint is compulsory if it is transactionally related to the subject matter of the complaint. (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 426.10.) “To be considered a compulsory cross-complaint, a related cause of action must have existed at the time of the service of [the] answer to [the] complaint.” (Crocker National Bank v. Emerald (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d 852, 864.) The late filing of a motion for leave to file a compulsory cross-complaint “absent some evidence of bad faith is insufficient evidence to support denial of the motion.” (Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 101.) “Permission to file a permissive cross-complaint is solely within the trial court’s discretion.” (Crocker, 221 Cal.App.3d at 864.) Finally, under the Rules of Court “[a] failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion.” (Rules of Ct., rule 8.54 (c).)

IV.     ANALYSIS

A.             Defendants’ Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint is Granted

          Defendants’ proposed verified Cross-Complaint is a compulsory cross-complaint. (Motion, Ex. A, ¶¶ 1-27.) The proposed Cross-Complaint stems from the same “transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences” as the causes of action in Plaintiffs’ complaint, specifically relating to the circumstances under which Rolando sustained injuries due to the detachment of the strap affixed to the Vehicle. (Id.; see also Code of Civ. Proc. § 426.10.) In the proposed cross-complaint, Defendants assert the following causes of action against Toyota Motor North America, Inc., Toyota Motor Engineering & Manufacturing North America, Inc., and Toyota Motor Sales, U.S.A., Inc. (“Cross-Defendants”): (1) Equitable Indemnification and Contribution; (2) Apportionment of Fault; (3) Negligence; (4) Products Liability – Design and Manufacture; (5) Products Liability –Failure to Warn; (6) Declaratory Relief. (Motion, Ex. A, ¶¶ 1-27.) Defendants allege Cross-Defendants were responsible for designing and manufacturing the Vehicle in its entirety and failed to adequately warn consumers of the risks and/or dangers associated with its use. (See id., ¶¶ 19-22.) Furthermore, Defendants contend that, in the event they are found liable for Rolando’s injuries, Defendants seek indemnification, either wholly or partially, from Cross-Defendants. Defendants also require Cross-Defendants bear a proportionate share of any judgment rendered against Defendants, consistent with the degree of comparative negligence attributed to Cross-Defendants. (See id., ¶¶ 1-16.)

          Here, Defendants did not file a cross-complaint at the time they filed their Answer on June 28, 2024. As such, in accordance with Code of Civ. Proc. § 426.30(a), Defendants may be precluded from asserting any claims that should have been filed with their initial Answer. However, since Defendants are not seeking to file their Cross-Complaint against Plaintiffs, pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. § 428.50(b), they are permitted to file their Cross-Complaint at any time before the trial date is set. As no trial date has been set and the case remains in its early stages, the Court is inclined to grant Defendants leave to file their Cross-Complaint.

Furthermore, Defendants have demonstrated their intent to file the Cross-Complaint in good faith. As detailed in Defense counsel’s Declaration and supported by email communications attached as Exhibit B to the Motion, Plaintiffs were notified of Defendants’ intention to file the Cross-Complaint. (Declaration ¶5.) The Court finds this proactive communication to be evidence of Defendants’ diligence and good faith efforts in this matter.

Additionally, as Plaintiffs have not filed an opposition to Defendants’ Motion, the Court finds no basis to deny the request for leave to file the Cross-Complaint. Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 8.54(c), and given the absence of prejudice to Plaintiffs, the Court finds Defendants’ motion well taken.

IV.     CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing analysis, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion for Leave to file a cross-complaint. Defendant shall file their Cross-Complaint on or before December 2, 2024.
