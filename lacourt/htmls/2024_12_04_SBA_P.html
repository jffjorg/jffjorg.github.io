DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV00583    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

Moving Parties: Defendant Godlewski

Responding Party: Plaintiff McCaslin

RULING

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply. Moving Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On February 20, 2024, Plaintiff Stefanie Kohl (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Leslie Corrigan (“Corrigan”); Nationwide Life and Annuity Insurance Company (“Nationwide”); Liliana Gaspar (“Liliana”); Laszlo C. Gaspar (“Laszlo Jr.”); and DOES 1-20, inclusive alleging

causes of action for (1) Intentional Misrepresentation; (2) Negligent Misrepresentation; (3) Breach of Insurance Contract; (4) Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (5) Declaratory Relief; (6) Equitable Conversion; and (7) Restitution.

On October 3, 2024, Defendants Corrigan and Nationwide (collectively, “Moving Defendants”) filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike. Plaintiff filed an opposition on November 26, 2024. On December 2, 2024, Moving Defendants filed a reply.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiff is the surviving spouse of decedent Laszlo, who passed away on February 19, 2022. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) Plaintiff and Laszlo had children from prior marriages. (Complaint, ¶ 11.)

Before Plaintiff and Laszlo married, Laszlo purchased two life insurance policies and named his two children, Liliana and Laszlo Jr., as beneficiaries. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Laszlo was the named insured, but the marital community made payments on the policies. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff also had a life insurance policy naming her daughter as the sole beneficiary. (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

Plaintiff alleges that she and Laszlo had an agreement to change their respective policies and name each other as beneficiaries. (Complaint, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff changed her policy to name Laszlo as a beneficiary, but Laszlo forgot to change his. (Complaint, ¶ 15.)

On April 8, 2022, Plaintiff telephonically reported Laszlo’s death to Nationwide through its agent, Corrigan, and informed Corrigan that she believed she was one of the beneficiaries of Laszlo’s policies. (Complaint, ¶ 17.) Corrigan made written note of Plaintiff’s contact information and claim on the benefits. (Complaint, ¶ 19.)

Corrigan later called Plaintiff to inform her that she was not a beneficiary to either of Laszlo’s policies. (Complaint, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff informed Corrigan that she still believed she was entitled to a portion of the benefits. (Complaint, ¶ 22.) Corrigan told Plaintiff that she would file the claims for benefits with Nationwide. (Complaint, ¶ 23.)

Plaintiff alleges that Nationwide intentionally or negligently ignored Plaintiff’s claim for benefits and paid out the entire disputed funds to the named beneficiaries, without first conducting an investigation. (Complaint, ¶ 25.)

Plaintiff also alleges that Corrigan and Laszlo were involved in a romantic relationship, and Corrigan held personal dislike towards Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶¶ 26-27.)

Moving Defendants now demur to all causes of action on the grounds that Plaintiff’s claims are barred by Ins. Code section 10172 and Plaintiff has failed to allege sufficient facts.

LEGAL STANDARD

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147

Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.(Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.) 

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Argument

Plaintiff’s claims are all barred by Ins. Code section 10172, as Plaintiff never provided written notice to Nationwide’s home office of her claim to the benefits. Even if they were not barred, Plaintiff has failed to allege sufficient facts to state any cause of action. Further, Plaintiff is collaterally estopped from relitigating these issues as they were already argued in probate court.

Opposing Party’s Argument

The court notes that the opposition was not timely filed. However, the court exercises its discretion to consider late papers, pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1300, subd. (d).

Moving Defendants have failed to meet their burden to establish collateral estoppel. Plaintiff also argues that it is premature to decide whether Ins. Code section 10172 applies because she has alleged that the payments were not made in accordance with the terms of the policy. Plaintiff contends that she has pleaded sufficient facts to support her first through fifth causes of action.

Reply Argument

Moving Defendants contend that the opposition is untimely and should not be considered. Plaintiff’s argument that the payments were not made in accordance with the terms of the policy are conclusory, and section 10172 still bars her complaint. Moving Defendants reiterate their arguments about collateral estoppel and Plaintiff’s failure to allege sufficient facts.

Request for Judicial Notice

Moving Defendants request judicial notice of seven documents, each of which were filed in probate court. Pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (d), the court may take notice of the records of any court of this state.

Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ requests for judicial notice are GRANTED.

Merits of the Motion

A. Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) A motion to strike is subject to a similar meet and confer requirement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).)

Defense counsel declares that he sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel but received no response. The court finds that the parties have not satisfied the meet and confer requirements. However, the court will still discuss the merits of the motion.

B. Insurance Code Section 10172

Ins. Code section 10172 reads, “[n]otwithstanding Sections 751 and 1100 of the Family Code and Section 249.5 of the Probate Code, when the proceeds of, or payments under, a life insurance policy become payable and the insurer makes payment thereof in accordance with the terms of the policy, or in accordance with the terms of any written assignment thereof if the policy has been assigned, that payment shall fully discharge the insurer from all claims under the policy unless, before that payment is made, the insurer has received, at its home office, written notice by or on behalf of some other person that the other person claims to be entitled to that payment or some interest in the policy.”

Plaintiff’s own complaint alleges that the only named beneficiaries to Laszlo’s policies were his two children. Further, Plaintiff alleges that the only notice she provided to Nationwide of her claim was telephonic and directed at Corrigan. Plaintiff does not allege that she provided written notice to Nationwide’s home office. Accordingly, Moving Defendants were entitled to the statutory protections once Nationwide made payments in accordance with the terms of the policies.

Plaintiff argues that the statutory bar should not apply because she has alleged that Nationwide did not make payments in accordance with the terms of the policy. She contends that the policies required that Nationwide first record Proof of Death for the Insured and proof of the claimant’s interest in the proceeds before dispensing payment. However, the court cannot consider these allegations as they were not raised in the complaint, nor did Plaintiff request judicial notice of the policies’ terms.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not provided sufficient factual allegations to show that the statutory bar of Ins. Code section 10172 should not apply.

Accordingly, Moving Defendants are entitled to the statutory protections. Moving Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED as to all causes of action.

C. Collateral Estoppel

“In the case of issue preclusion, it must appear that the first matter presented some issue that is necessary to the later claim or defense, and that the issue was actually litigated and necessarily decided. Ordinarily these matters will not appear on the face of the pleading, but they may be ascertainable from materials subject to judicial notice.” (Fireside Bank Cases (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 1120, 1127.)

If a plaintiff loses the first action, any issues actually litigated will be binding on him or her in the later action even though a separate cause of action is involved. (See Perez v. City of San Bruno (1980) 27 Cal.3d 875, 883–884.) Thus, if a plaintiff files a limited negligence case for his or her property damage and the jury finds the defendant not negligent, the jury’s finding may bar plaintiff’s subsequent personal injury action against that defendant for the same incident. (See Pratt v. Vaughan (1934) 2 Cal.App.2d 722, 723–724.)

Moving Defendants argue that Plaintiff is collaterally estopped from relitigating the applicability of Ins. Code 10172 because the Probate Court made a final decision on the merits and determined that her claims were barred by the statute. Plaintiff argues in opposition that the litigated issues were not identical. Further, the petition was partially dismissed on jurisdictional grounds, and Moving Defendants cannot now assert that the Probate Court had jurisdiction to render a judgment on the merits. In reply, Moving Defendants point to RJN Exh. 4, p. 4, in which the Probate Court found that “Stefanie Kohl has not alleged a valid claim pursuant to Probate Code section 850(a)(2)(C) because she has not alleged that Decedent died in possession of, or holding title to, property claimed to belong to another. The Court also finds that Ms. Kohl has not alleged written notice “by or on behalf of” herself at Nationwide’s Home office, as required by Insurance Code section 10172.” Plaintiff did not revise such allegations, and the Probate Court sustained Nationwide’s demurrer without leave to amend. (RJN, Exh. 7.) Thus, Moving Defendants argue that the issue of Ins. Code section 10172 was already litigated, and the Probate Court determined that it did apply to Plaintiff’s claims.

The court finds that the Exhibits provided do not contain enough information to determine whether the Probate Court ruled that the demurrer should be sustained specifically because of Ins. Code section 10172 or pursuant to Probate Code section 850. Exhibit 7 merely states that the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

Thus, the court cannot determine that collateral estoppel prevents Plaintiff from relitigating the issue.

D. Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.) 

Moving Defendants seek to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages as she has not sufficiently pleaded fraud, malice, oppression, or conscious disregard for others’ safety.

As Moving Defendants’ demurrer to all causes of action were sustained, Plaintiff has no basis to seek punitive damages. Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED.

E. Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

Plaintiff alleges facts in her opposition which could support her assertion that her claims are not statutorily barred. Accordingly, the court GRANTS leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Moving Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

Moving Defendants ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 24TRCV01250    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: P

Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint

Moving Parties: Defendants Shane Goodman and Terry Goodman

Responding Party: Plaintiff Luis Arellano

RULING

The court considered the moving papers and rules as follows: the demurrer is OVERRULED and the motion to strike is GRANTED in its entirety.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On April 11, 2024, Plaintiff Luis Arellano (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Shane Goodman (“Shane”) and Terry Goodman (“Terry”) (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging causes of action for (1) Motor Vehicle and (2) General Negligence.

On October 15, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer. On October 16, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to strike.

As of December 3, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On April 22, 2022, Shane was driving under the influence. (Complaint, p. 5.) Shane struck a curb, crashed into two cars, and rear-ended Plaintiff. (Complaint, p. 5.)

Plaintiff alleges that he is entitled to punitive damages, as Shane was thirty-one years old and knew the potential consequences of drunk driving. (Complaint, p. 6.)

Defendants now seek to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages. Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s second cause of action.

LEGAL STANDARD

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f), is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.(Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.)

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Argument

Defendants demur to the negligence cause of action because it is fatally uncertain. Defendants seek to strike the request for punitive damages as Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to justify the request.

Merits of the Motion

A. Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)A motion to strike is subject to a similar meet and confer requirement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).)

Defense counsel declares that the parties met telephonically to discuss issues with the complaint, but they were unable to reach an agreement. The court finds that the parties have satisfied the meet and confer requirements.

B. Second Cause of Action for Negligence and Exemplary Damages

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

Defendants demur to this cause of action, as Plaintiff has not alleged any facts supporting an award of punitive damages. The court notes, however, that a demurrer may not be directed at a portion of a cause of action. (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682.) When a portion of a cause of action is defective, a defendant may challenge it through a motion to strike. (Id. at pp. 1682-1683.)

As Defendants’ demurrer is procedurally improper, Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.

C. Motion to Strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. §436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

Defendants seek to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint which seek or reference punitive damages. Defendants contend that the California Supreme Court has previously held that driving while intoxicated may warrant punitive damages only if there are additional facts supporting an inference that the driver acted in conscious disregard of another’s safety. (See Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890; Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82.)

The Taylor case is instructive with respect to punitive damages for driving under the influence. In Taylor, the court noted “we have concluded that the act of operating a motor vehicle while intoxicated may constitute an act of ‘malice’ under §3294 if performed under circumstances which disclose a conscious disregard of the probable dangerous consequences.” (Taylor, supra, 24

Cal.3d at p. 892.) Thus, the plaintiff’s allegations that the driver had a history of alcoholism and prior arrests for drunk driving created a reasonable inference that he acted in conscious disregard of others’ safety. (Id. at p. 896.) Notably, however, the Taylor court fell short of holding that punitive damages are always appropriate in cases where a defendant drives while intoxicated.

In a subsequently decided case, the Dawes court held that mere allegations of drunk driving, without additional exacerbating facts, are not sufficient to establish that a defendant acted in conscious disregard of others’ safety. (Dawes, supra, 111 Cal.App.3d at p. 323.) In Dawes, the petitioners alleged that the driver was zigzagging through lanes, driving in excess of 65 miles per hour in a designated 35 miles per hour zone in a busy public street. (Id. at p. 321.) Such allegations were sufficient to support an award of punitive damages. (Id. at p. 324.)

Notably, Taylor and Dawes were both decided before the 1987 amendment to §3294, which added the requirement of “despicable” conduct to support an award of punitive damages under a conscious disregard standard. Under those heightened standards, it seems clear that allegations of drunk driving, absent additional facts, are insufficient to justify punitive damages.

Here, the Complaint merely alleges that Shane, as a thirty-one-year-old, knew of the dangerous consequences of driving intoxicated. The Complaint lacks any factual allegations that rise to the level of conduct described in Taylor or Dawes. Without such exacerbating factual allegations, Plaintiff’s complaint does not warrant the imposition of punitive damages.

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED in its entirety.

D. Leave to Amend

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”] The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

Given the liberal policy surrounding amendments, the court will grant Plaintiff leave to amend his complaint.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED. Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED with leave to amend.

Defendants ordered to give notice of ruling.

Case Number: 24TRCV01293    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration

Moving Party: Defendant Tesla Motors, Inc.

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Brenden Dunn and Ashlee Dunn

RULING

Having considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply, the motion to compel binding arbitration and stay proceedings is GRANTED.

PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On April 16, 2024, Plaintiffs Brenden Dunn and Ashlee Dunn (“Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Tesla Motors, Inc. (“Defendant”) and DOES 1-10, inclusive alleging five causes of action for violations of Statutory Obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act Civ. Code sections 1790-1795.8.

On May 24, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion. On November 19, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition. Defendant filed a reply on November 25, 2024.

FACTUAL BACKGROUND

On September 21, 2023, Plaintiffs purchased a Tesla Model 3 (the “Vehicle”) manufactured by Defendant. (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

Plaintiffs received an express written warranty which stated that if a defect developed during the warranty period, Plaintiff could deliver the Vehicle to a repair shop to make repairs. (Complaint, ¶ 9.)

During the warranty period, several defects developed. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) However, Defendant failed to repair the Vehicle after several attempts. (Complaint, ¶ 14.)

LEGAL STANDARD

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) California law favors enforcement of valid arbitration agreements. (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 97.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-357.)

The Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”) applies to any contract evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce which contains an arbitration clause. (Wolls v. Superior Court (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) Section 2 of the FAA provides that arbitration provisions shall be enforced, save upon grounds as exist at law or in equity for the revocation of any contract. (Ibid.) A state court may refuse to enforce an arbitration clause on the basis of generally applicable contract defenses, such as fraud, duress, or unconscionability. (Ibid.) A state court, however, may not defeat an arbitration clause by applying state laws applicable only to arbitration provisions. (Ibid.)

For the FAA to apply, a contract must involve interstate commerce. (Ibid.) When it applies, the FAA preempts any state law rule that stands as an obstacle to the accomplishment of the FAA’s objectives. (Carbajal v. CWPSC, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 227, 238.) A party asserting FAA preemption bears the burden to present evidence establishing a contract with the arbitration provision affects interstate commerce, and the failure to do so renders the FAA inapplicable. (Ibid.) Evidence must be presented, in the form of declarations or other evidence, that establishes that the contract affects interstate commerce. (Ibid.) “[S]ince arbitration is a matter of contract, the FAA also applies if it is so stated in the agreement.” (Davis v. Shiekh Shoes, LLC (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 956, 963.)

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the

agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2, subds. (a), (b).) Thus, in assessing a motion to compel arbitration, the moving party “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the [motion] bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284, citation omitted.)

“If a court of competent jurisdiction . . . has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

DISCUSSION

Request for Judicial Notice

Defendant requests judicial notice of the complaint filed by Plaintiffs. The court may take notice of its own file, pursuant to Evid. Code section 452, subd. (d).

Defendant’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

Moving Party’s Argument

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs consented to the terms of an online Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”), which included an arbitration provision. Both the FAA and California law require Plaintiffs to arbitrate this dispute.

Opposing Party’s Argument

The arbitration agreement is procedurally unconscionable and unenforceable. Further, the terms of the MVOA were superseded by other written agreements.

Reply Arguments

Plaintiffs ignore the language of the MVOA, which states that any other arbitration agreements are overridden by the MVOA. Plaintiffs also cite to cases which are distinguishable from the instant case.

Merits of the Motion

A. Existence of an Arbitration Agreement

In support of the motion, Defendant submits the Declaration of Raymond Kim (“Kim”). Kim declares that when Plaintiffs purchased the Vehicle, they signed a Motor Vehicle Order Agreement (“MVOA”), thereby agreeing to be bound by its terms, which include an arbitration provision. (Kim Decl., ¶ 3.) Prior to placing the order, Plaintiffs had the ability to click on a hyperlink to the MVOA’s terms and conditions, which would have opened in a new window.

(Kim Decl., ¶ 4.) Further, once executed, the MVOA would be visible on the customers’ mytesla.com accounts. (Kim Decl., ¶ 4.)

Courts have enforced similar “clickwrap” agreements. “Clickwrap” is an agreement “in which an internet user accepts a website’s terms of use by clicking an ‘I agree’ or ‘I accept’ button, with a link to the agreement readily available.” (Sellers v. JustAnswer LLC (2021) 73 Cal. App. 5th 444, 463 (internal quotation marks omitted). “[C]ourts have routinely found clickwrap agreements enforceable.” (Berman v. Freedom Fin. Network, LLC (9th Cir. 2022) 30 F.4th 849, 856.)

As with any other contract, however, “clickwrap” agreements must be supported by mutual assent. (Sellers, 73 Cal. App. 5th at 461.) “[I]n order to establish mutual assent for the valid formation of an internet contract, a provider must first establish the contractual terms were presented to the consumer in a manner that made it apparent the consumer was assenting to those very terms when checking a box or clicking on a button.” (Id.)

The terms of the MVOA indicate that the parties agree to arbitrate any disputes relating to any aspect of the parties’ relationship. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.) The arbitration provision also provides an opt-out option and states that if it is not exercised, the MVOA’s arbitration provision becomes the controlling agreement between the parties, overriding any other arbitration agreements. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiffs did not exercise their right to opt-out. (Kim Decl., ¶ 5.)

The court finds that with the MVOA, Defendant has met its burden of proving that an arbitration agreement exists between the parties. Further, the language of the provision is broad enough that it covers the dispute at hand. Thus, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish any facts necessary to show that the agreement should not be enforced.

B. Grounds for Compelling Arbitration

1. Does the Sales Contract Supersede the MVOA

Plaintiffs argue that the Retail Installment Sale Contract (“RISC”) supersedes the MVOA because it was signed after the MVOA, and the MVOA contradicts the express written agreement between the parties. Defendant argues in opposition that the terms of the MVOA’s arbitration provision expressly state that it overrides any other arbitration agreements, including any arbitration agreement in a lease or finance contract. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.)

The court agrees with Defendant that the terms of the MVOA are clear that the arbitration terms within that document control. Thus, the relevant arbitration agreement is contained within the MVOA.

2. Unconscionability

“Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element, the former focusing on ‘oppression’ or ‘surprise’ due to unequal bargaining power, the latter on ‘overly harsh’ or ‘one-sided’ results. The prevailing view is that procedural and substantive unconscionability must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability. But they need not be present in the same degree. The more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice

versa.” ((Peng v. First Republic Bank (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469.) “Ordinary contracts of adhesion, although they are indispensable facts of modern life that are generally enforced, contain a degree of procedural unconscionability.” (Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1244.)

Plaintiffs argue the arbitration agreement is procedurally unconscionable because it is an adhesion contract. Moreover, Plaintiffs lacked any substantial bargaining power because they were forced to simply click through various prompts related to the purchase of the Vehicle.

The arbitration agreement at hand does contain an opt-out option, which alone would not insulate it from procedural unconscionability. (Gentry v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 443, 470 (arbitration agreement had a degree of procedural unconscionability even though there was a 30 day opt out provision).) However, any degree of procedural unconscionability would be minimal, as the MVOA is only five pages long, and the arbitration provision is clearly demarcated and distinct from the rest of the terms. (Kim Decl., Exh. 1, p. 3.) Further, there are no facts to indicate that there was a great disparity in bargaining power. In general, customers are free to negotiate with car sellers, even if those sellers use form contracts. (Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 931.) Customers also have the option of purchasing a vehicle with comparable or superior terms from another seller, and thus, “no presumption is warranted that Plaintiff had no choice or power…” (Ibid.) Here, Plaintiffs were not forced to purchase a car from Defendant. Plaintiffs had the option of purchasing a comparable car from another dealer or manufacturer without an arbitration agreement.

Even if Plaintiffs could establish that the MVOA was procedurally unconscionable, the MVOA is not so one-sided that it would be substantively unconscionable to enforce it. The MVOA provides that both parties have the right to compel arbitration. Further, arbitration is to be conducted under the AAA’s rules, which allow for the selection of a neutral arbiter. Defendant also agreed to pay arbitration fees for both parties. (Kim Decl., ¶ 7.)

Accordingly, the court finds slight procedural unconscionability but no substantive unconscionability.

3. Express Warranties

Plaintiffs argue that Defendant cannot enforce the arbitration provision as their claims are distinct from the MVOA. Plaintiff cites several cases that Defendant notes are not instructive, as those cases concern the ability of a manufacturer to enforce an arbitration agreement between purchaser and dealer. Defendant sells vehicles directly to customers, and any agreements signed are directly between Defendant and the buyer.

Thus, the cases cited by Plaintiffs are not persuasive.

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration.

B. Stay of Proceedings

Code Civ. Proc. section 1281.4 provides that if the court has ordered the arbitration of a controversy, it “shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or

proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” Pursuant to Section 1281.4, therefore, the Court stays this action pending conclusion of the arbitration proceedings.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel binding arbitration is GRANTED. The court STAYS this action pending conclusion of the arbitration process.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
