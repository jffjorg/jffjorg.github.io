DEPARTMENT 74 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 



 




Case Number: 21STCV35596    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: 74

Ernest J. Franceschi Jr. v. Christopher Baldwin, et al.

Motion to Tax Costs

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a dispute between an attorney and his former client.

            On September 28, 2021, Plaintiff Ernest J. Franceschi Jr. filed a complaint against Defendant Christopher Baldwin (Defendant), Gloria Baldwin, and Ross Perkal (Perkal).

            On March 25, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (FAC). In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: (1) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (2) tortious interference with contract, and (3) extortion.

            On February 1, 2024, the court granted Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

            On February 23, 2024, Defendant filed a memorandum of costs.

            On March 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to tax costs.

LEGAL STANDARD

            The prevailing party is entitled to recover costs, including attorneys’ fees, as a matter of right, except as otherwise expressly provided by statute. (¿¿See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1032, subd. (a)(4), 1032, subd. (b), 1033.5¿¿.)¿Costs recoverable under¿¿section 1032¿¿are restricted to those that are both reasonable in amount and reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation. (¿Code Civ. Proc.,¿§§ 1033.5, subd. (c)(2), (3)¿.)¿Costs “¿merely convenient or beneficial¿” to the preparation of a case are disallowed. (¿Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2)¿.) 

            “¿A ‘verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of [the] propriety’ of the items listed on it, and the burden is on the party challenging these costs to demonstrate that they were not reasonable or necessary.¿” (¿¿Adams v. Ford Motor Co. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1475, 1486-1487 [italics and brackets omitted]¿¿.)¿Costs otherwise allowable as a matter of right may be disallowed if the court determines they were not reasonably necessary, and the court has power to reduce the amount of any cost item to an amount that is reasonable. (See Perko’s Enterprises, Inc. v. RRNS Enterprises¿(1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 238, 245 [finding that¿“the intent and effect of section 1033.5, subdivision¿(c)(2) is to authorize a trial court to disallow recovery of costs, including filing fees, when it determines the costs were incurred unnecessarily”].)¿

DISCUSSION 

            In his motion, Plaintiff argues that Defendants did not reasonably incur the following costs:

            Deposition Costs - $7,020.25

            Plaintiff contends the court should tax this cost because Defendant and Perkal jointly conducted the deposition. According to Plaintiff, Defendant does not seek costs proportionate to the amount he incurred conducting the deposition. In opposition, though, Defendant offers evidence he paid for the entire deposition. (Leigh Decl., ¶ 5; Ex. B.) Plaintiff also argues the deposition was unnecessary. But Defendant relied on the deposition as evidence in his motion for summary judgment. Thus, the court will not tax this cost.

            Gloria Baldwin First Appearance Fee - $435

            Defendant has withdrawn his request for this cost. (Opposition, p. 7.)

            Filing Fees for Two Motions to Quash and One Motion for Protective Order - $180

            Plaintiff asserts Defendant improperly seeks to recover these filing fees because the motions are not sufficiently identified in the memorandum of costs. But in his opposition, Defendant offers evidence of the filing fees he incurred for two motions to quash and one protective order. (Leigh Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. A.)[1] Thus, the court will not tax this cost.

            Fees for Electronic Filing or Service - $798.88

            Plaintiff argues the court should tax this cost because Defendant has not substantiated this request by identifying the electronic filings. However, Defendant has provided receipts of the electronic filing fees he incurred. (Leigh Decl., ¶ 6; Ex. 6.) In addition, Plaintiff contends there is no court order requiring electronic filing or service of documents. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(14).) But Defendant has provided the relevant court order. (Request for Judicial Notice; Ex. 1.) Thus, the court will not tax this cost.

            Fees for Hosting Electronic Documents Through Electronic Filing Service Provider - $91.20

 

            Defendant used this storage service to comply with Plaintiff’s discovery requests that targeted numerous emails and electronically stored information. (Leigh Decl., ¶ 7; Ex. D; Ex. E.) Accordingly, the court will exercise its discretion and permit Defendant to recover this cost. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(4).)

            Fee for Rescheduling Motion for Summary Judgment - $20

            Plaintiff claims it would be unfair to shift this cost to him. However, Plaintiff has cited no authority in support. The court will not tax this cost, either.

CONCLUSION 

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to tax in part. The court taxes $435, which represents Gloria Baldwin’s first appearance fee.

LEGAL STANDARD 

            A propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response to interrogatories if the propounding party deems that an answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete, or that an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).) Such a motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b).)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff seeks further response to Special Interrogatory Nos. 88, 89, 95, 96, 97, 98, 103, 113, 114, 115, 116, 117, 118, 119, and 120. The court declines to specifically address and instead over rules Defendant’s boilerplate objections because such objections are legally inadequate.

            Special Interrogatory No. 88

            This interrogatory asks Defendant to identify who formed Sams Ventures. Based on the court’s prior ruling, Defendant objects in part that this information is not relevant or discoverable. The court disagrees. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant breached the parties’ contract by not sharing 25% of the proceeds Defendant received from the sale of Straight Smile. Defendant held stock in Straight Smile through Sams Ventures, and claims he only received sale proceeds in proportion to his ownership interest in Sams Ventures. However, if Sams Ventures is Defendant’s alter-ego, Defendant would have received a greater amount of sale proceeds. The identities of those who formed Sams Ventures will help Plaintiff determine this issue and assess the damages to which he may be entitled.

            Defendant also objects that this request violates his right to privacy in his financial affairs because Sams Ventures is his family’s LLC. (Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, 656.) But Plaintiff has a countervailing interest that outweighs the Defendant’s potential privacy interest. (County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 621, 656.) As noted above, Plaintiff needs this discovery to determine the amount of sale proceeds that Defendant allegedly withheld from Plaintiff in violation of the parties’ partnership agreement. Thus, further response is necessary.

Case Number: 22STCV35846    Hearing Date: April 30, 2024    Dept: 74

Queen Williams v. Corey Flournoy Williams

Defendant Corey Flournoy William’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Queen William’s Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One) and Request for Sanctions

 

BACKGROUND 

            This action arises from a property dispute.

            On November 10, 2022, Plaintiff Queen Williams (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against Defendant Corey Flournoy Williams (Defendant). In the complaint, Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: (1) financial elder abuse, (2) conversion, (3) unjust enrichment, (4) cancellation of instrument, (5) cancellation of instrument, (6) quiet title, (7) quiet title, (8) violation of Tom Bane Civil Rights Act, (9) violation of Civil Code Section 789.3, and (10) wrongful eviction.

            On October 27, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with special interrogatories (set one) by mail and email. (Chen Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s daughter acknowledged receipt of the discovery by email but told Defendant’s counsel that the email address did not belong to Plaintiff. (Chen Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff’s daughter later asked Defendant’s counsel to send all documents to her via the same email address. (Chen Decl., ¶ 12.)

            On December 22, 2023, Defendant’s counsel called Plaintiff about her failure to respond to the special interrogatories. Plaintiff confirmed her mailing address but claimed she had not received the special interrogatories. (Chen Decl., ¶ 9.)

            On January 19, 2024, Defendant had the special interrogatories personally delivered to Plaintiff’s address. (Chen Decl., ¶ 10; Ex. F.)

            On January 31, 2024, Defendant’s counsel sent a letter to Plaintiff regarding her failure to respond to the special interrogatories. (Chen Decl., ¶ 11.)

            To date, Plaintiff has not provided responses to the special interrogatories.

            On February 20, 2024, Defendant filed this motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to the special interrogatories.

LEGAL STANDARD

¿¿            If a party to whom interrogatories or an inspection demand were directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses without objections. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (b), 2031.300, subd. (b).) Moreover, failure to timely serve responses waives objections to the requests. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, subd. (a), 2031.300, subd. (a).) Failure to verify a response is equivalent to no response at all. (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.) 

DISCUSSION 

            The court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to the special interrogatories. Defendant has persuaded the court that Plaintiff has received the discovery at issue. On October 27, 2023, Defendant mailed the special interrogatories to Plaintiff’s confirmed mailing address. (Chen Decl., ¶¶ 4, 9.) That same day, Defendant emailed the special interrogatories to Plaintiff’s daughter, who has expressed that all documents should be emailed to her. (Chen Decl., ¶¶ 4, 6, 12.) In addition, Defendant had the special interrogatories personally delivered to Plaintiff’s confirmed address on January 19, 2024. (Chen Decl., ¶¶ 9, 10.) Moreover, Plaintiff has provided no evidence to the contrary in a timely opposition. Given Plaintiff’s failure to timely respond, the court grants Defendant’s motion.

            As the prevailing party, Defendant is entitled to sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).) Moreover, Plaintiff has not demonstrated she acted with substantial justification in a timely opposition. The court finds Defendant’s counsel’s proposed hourly rate reasonable, and he has substantiated his request for attorney fees with a breakdown of work performed. (Chen Decl., ¶ 14.) However, this motion largely duplicates Defendant’s other discovery motions. The court will adjust the award accordingly: $761.65 ($350/hr x 2 hours + $61.65).

CONCLUSION 

The court grants Defendant’s motion to compel responses to special interrogatories (set one) and request for sanctions. Plaintiff shall serve responsive documents and Code-compliant responses, without objections, within 10 days of this order. The court grants Defendant’s request for sanctions. Plaintiff shall pay Defendant’s counsel attorney fees in the sum of $761.65 within 30 days of this order.

Defendant shall give notice.
