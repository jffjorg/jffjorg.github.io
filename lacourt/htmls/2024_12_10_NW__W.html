DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01550    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: W

SYRUS PARVIZIAN v. WEST VALLEY UPHOLSTERY, INC., et al.

 

defendant’s motion to compel compliance with court’s order and for terminating sanctions

 

Date of Hearing:        December 10, 2024                          Trial Date:       April 21, 2025  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01550

 

Moving Party:            Defendants West Valley Upholstery, Inc. and Steven Whitefield

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Syrus Parvizian

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Syrus Parvizian alleges he tripped and fell over a lifted portion of the sidewalk outside Defendant Whitefield’s property, West Valley Upholstery. On April 3, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Whitefield, West Valley Upholstery Inc., City of Los Angeles, and County of Los Angeles asserting causes of action for negligence and premises liability. Upon sustaining the demurrer to the first amended complaint, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint on July 19, 2023.

 

On April 22, 2024, County of Los Angeles was dismissed.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Compliance with Court’s Order and for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED, IN PART.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant West Valley Upholstery, Inc. and Steven Whitefield seek an order compelling Plaintiff to comply with the court’s discovery order and for sanctions including terminating sanctions against Plaintiff to dismiss the action filed by Plaintiff. Defendants make the motion on the grounds Plaintiff failed to serve any discovery responses after this court issued an order compelling Plaintiff to do so.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . .  [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . . .”  Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 provides that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery. . . .” 

 

Defendant seeks to compel compliance with the court’s September 4, 2024, Order, which required Plaintiff to produce further discovery responses, without objection, within 30 days, i.e., October 4, 2024. At the time, the court denied Defendant’s request for terminating sanctions because the court believed terminating sanctions should be used sparingly and less severe alternatives should be made first. However, Plaintiffs have still failed to provide discovery responses and as a result, Defendant also seeks monetary and terminating sanctions. Plaintiff’s blatant refusal to comply with the court’s Order is a flagrant abuse of the discovery process and Defendant maintains anything less than a terminating sanction would improperly allow Plaintiff to benefit from his own stalling tactics, and is insufficient to address Plaintiff’s comprehensive misconduct in this case.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff claims they accidentally only served other counsel the discovery responses after the court’s Order and have now included their discovery responses. Counsel for Plaintiff, Mr. Moghtader, asserts he instructed his office to serve discovery responses pursuant to the court’s September 4, 2024 Order. (Moghtader Decl. ¶3.) However, upon receiving the instant motion, Counsel discovered this office served responses but neglected to serve Defense Counsel for West Valley Upholstery, Inc. and Steven Whitefield. (Moghtader Decl. ¶¶4-5.) Mr. Moghtader has now attached the discovery responses. (Moghtader Decl. ¶6, Exhs. 2, 3, 4, 5.)

 

However, the discovery responses are not compliant with this court’s Order. Plaintiff was ordered to produce responses without objection and all requests for admissions were deemed admitted. The RFAs contain denials and all of the discovery responses contain general objections. Accordingly, the motion to compel compliance with the September 4, 2024 Court Order is GRANTED.

 

As for Defendant’s request for terminating sanctions, the court finds they are not warranted at this time. The request for monetary sanctions is denied without prejudice, as defendants failed to provide notice of the desired sanction or to support it in the memorandum of points and authorities and declaration. 


Case Number: 22VECV02109    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: W

YVONNE JOHNSON, AKA YVONNE MANESH v. SAFEBUILT INSURANCE SERVICES, LLC, et al.

 

motion for leave to file third amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        August 14, 2024                    Trial Date:       N/A

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV02109

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Yvonne Johnson aka Yvonne Manesh

Responding Party:     Defendant United Specialty Insurance Company   

Meet and Confer:      Attempted. (Schmitthenner Decl. ¶¶3-4.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Yvonne Johnson brought a prior action (via a cross-complaint) against defendant United Specialty Insurance Company’s insured, Arina Builders, who she claimed had damaged her property. That action resulted in a judgment in plaintiff’s favor for $261,120. Plaintiff subsequently sought attorney’s fees against the insured, which were granted. The judgment and the fees were not paid. Plaintiff then brought the instant suit against the insurance company (United), and Golden State Claims Adjusters (GSCA), a third party claims administrator, seeking the full amount of the judgment and attorneys fees. The first six causes of action in the First Amended Complaint are against United only, for 1) Judgment Creditor’s action against the Insurer; 2) Failure to Pay a Covered Claim; 3) Unreasonable Failure to Defend; 4) Bad Faith Failure to Investigate a Claim; 5) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and 6) Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code Section 17200, et seq. Plaintiff also stated claims against GSCA and United for Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation (the Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action).

 

On November 14, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed United from the 7th and 8th causes of action and dissed GSCA from the 7th and 8th causes of action. Upon sustaining of a demurrer to the FAC, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint for 1) Judgment Creditor’s action against the Insurer; 2) Breach for Failure to Pay a Covered Claim; 3) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and 4) Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code Section 17200, et seq.

 

Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint on August 15, 2024, adding a cause of action for Bad Faith – Unreasonable Failure to Defend.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Third and Fourth Causes of Action of the Third Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant United Specialty Insurance Company (“USIC”) requests this court take judicial notice of the complaint filed by Glendale Acceptance, LLC in the action of Glendale Acceptance, LLC v. Yvonne Johnson et al., Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. BC624204 (Exh. A) and USIC policy number SII0503B10568, which was issued to Jeff Davani dba Arina Builders and effective February 22, 2015, to February 22, 2016 (Exh. B).

 

The court GRANTS the request for judicial notice. (See Scott v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal. App. 4th 743, 754.)

 

ANALYSIS

 

USIC demurs to the third and fourth causes of action in the Third Amended Complaint on the grounds they are both barred by the statute of limitations and even if they were not barred, Defendant had no duty to defendant Plaintiff as an additional insured or provide any benefits under the policy to Plaintiff as an additional insured.

 

Statute of Limitations

 

USIC first demurs to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiff’s two new bad faith causes of action are time barred. In California, tort remedies for a claim of breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing are governed under a two-year statute of limitations. (CCP § 339; Archdale v. American International Specialty Lines Insurance Co. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 449, 473.) USIC argues the TAC alleges Plaintiff tendered her defense to USIC on February 27, 2018, and April 11, 2018, but USIC failed to respond. (See TAC, ¶ 44, 50.) As a result, the two-year statute of limitations would have run by April of 2020 at the latest. Even if Plaintiff were asserting a contractual bad faith claim based on her “additional insured” theory, USIC contends the four-year statute of limitations on that claim ran in April of 2022. USIC further argues the new causes of action do not relate back because the new bad faith claims do not involve the same facts, same injury, or same instrumentality as the original claims. USIC claims Plaintiff’s new bad faith claims are premised on new facts, namely Plaintiff’s alleged status as an additional insured, and a new injury to an entirely different party.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the statute of limitations does not begin to run from the date of tender, rather “an insurer’s denial of a claim started the statute of limitations running.” (Neff v. New York Life Ins. Co., (1947) 30, Cal.2d 165.) As a result, the statute of limitations was tolled because USIC never issued a denial. Moreover, Plaintiff argues the relation back doctrine is applicable because Plaintiff has consistently stated, numerous times over, that Plaintiff was an additional insured under the policy. Moreover, the TAC only elaborates on what was already provided to USIC in the LETTERS referenced as Exhibits C & D to the TAC. No new facts have been alleged here, there are no new instrumentalities of injury, and therefore the relation-back doctrine applies.

 

The court agrees the new allegations relate back. As noted by Defendant, “[u]nder the relation-back doctrine, an amendment relates back to the original complaint if the amendment (1) rests on the same general set of facts; (2) involves the same injury; and (3) refers to the same instrumentality." (Pointe San Diego Residential Cmty., L.P. v. Procopio, Cory, Hargreaves & Savitch, LLP (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 265, 277.) However, the doctrine will not apply if “the plaintiff seeks by amendment to recover upon a set of facts entirely unrelated to those pleaded in the original complaint.” (Ibid.) “In determining whether the amended complaint alleges facts that are sufficiently similar to those alleged in the original complaint, the critical inquiry is whether the defendant had adequate notice of the claim based on the original pleading.” (Ibid.) The allegations in the complaint and TAC both allege Plaintiff USIC acted in bad faith when it failed to pay benefits due under the insurance policy and suffered a loss covered under the insurance policy. The TAC only clarifies that Plaintiff was an additional insured under that policy rather than the insured.

 

Moreover, the court agrees that “[t]he statute of limitations for actions on insurance claims is equitably tolled from the time the insured notifies the insurer of the claim until coverage is denied.” (Marselis v. Allstate Ins. Co. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 122, 124.)The denial must be unequivocal. (Migliore v. Mid-Century Ins. Co. (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 592, 604.) Here, however, the allegations are United never responded to the tender, Plaintiff obtained a judgment in in Glendale Acceptance LLC v. Yvonne Johnson et al., and in 2022, United offered to pay below the judgment amount. (TAC ¶¶17-24.) Although not barred on its face, it is uncertain whether the tolling of the statute of limitations ended or is still ongoing.

 

Failure to State a Claim

 

Defendant argues even if the third and fourth causes of action are not time barred, the claims would still fail because USIC had no duty to defend Plaintiff in the underlying action or provide benefits in the underlying action. Specifically, USIC argues the USIC policy only provides coverage for claims of bodily injury or property damage made against the party seeking coverage, and the claims made against Plaintiff in the underlying action were merely for unpaid money, not bodily injury or property damage.

 

The USIC policy provides: We will pay those sums that the insured becomes legally obligated to pay as “damages” for “bodily injury” or “property damage” to which this insurance applies. We will have the right and duty to defend you, the Named Insured, against any “suit” seeking “damages” to which this insurance applies . . . . (RJN, Exh. B.)  It goes on to define “Property damage” as “Physical injury to tangible property . . . .” (RJN, Exh. B.) USIC argues the original complaint in the underlying action by Glendale Acceptance, however, did not contain any allegations of bodily injury or property damage. As a result, USIC had no duty to defend Plaintiff. Moreover, the complaint does not clearly allege what benefits were allegedly withheld from Plaintiff in her capacity as an additional insured. USIC contends it has already demonstrated there was no duty to defend and therefore, no duty to provide any policy benefits.

 

Plaintiff does not address these contentions in opposition. The court agrees Plaintiff has not alleged facts sufficient to support a claim for the third or fourth causes of action. Under the USIC policy, USIC only had a duty to defend for bodily injury or property damage. As noted by the judicially noticed underlying action, there were no allegations of bodily injury or physical injury to tangible property. As a result, Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged any duty to defend or provide benefits in the underlying action.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.
