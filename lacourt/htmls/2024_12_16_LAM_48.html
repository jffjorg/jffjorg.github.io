[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING IN PART DEMURRER; GRANTING IN PART MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

December 16, 2024

 

On May 24, 2023, Plaintiff Maggie Parsons filed this action against Defendants Robert Ozeran and Law Offices of Robert Ozeran Inc.

On November 9, 2023, Robert Ozeran filed a cross-complaint, alleging (1) fraud – intentional misrepresentation; (2) fraud – concealment; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress (“IIED”); (4) negligence; (5) common law appropriation of name; and (6) statutory appropriation of name.

On November 27, 2023, Parsons filed a demurrer and motion to strike.

The Court’s August 8, 2024 order acknowledged Parsons’s argument that that res judicata and collateral estoppel bar some causes of action because the same issue was being decided through the Petition for Nullity in the Dissolution Action.  The Court ordered supplemental briefing on res judicata/collateral estoppel and continued the hearings.

The parties’ supplemental briefing stated that as the date of the briefs, there was not yet a final judgment in the Dissolution Action.  The Court again continued the hearings.

On November 1, 2024, Parsons filed a Request for Judicial Notice of the judgment entered in the Dissolution Action.

REQUESTS FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Parsons’s requests for judicial notice of the Petition for Nullity and a July 31, 2024 order in the family law case In re: Marriage of Ozeran and Parsons, Case No. 23CHFL00714 (“Dissolution Action”) are granted.

Parsons’s request for judicial notice of the September 3, 2024 Minute Order after Bifurcated Trial re: Nullity by Fraud is granted.

Parsons’s request for judicial notice of the October 31, 2024 Judgment on Bifurcated Trial re Annulment and Putative Spouse Status for Respondent is granted.

DEMURRER

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, accepting the alleged facts as true.  (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1406.)  “Because a demurrer challenges defects on the face of the complaint, it can only refer to matters outside the pleading that are subject to judicial notice.”  (Arce ex rel. Arce v. Kaiser Found. Health Plan, Inc. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 471, 556.)

A.        Issue Preclusion Bars the First Through Third Causes of Action.

Parsons argues that res judicata or collateral estoppel bar the first four causes of action for fraud, IIED, and negligence.  (Demurrer at pp. 5-7.)

A court may sustain a general demurrer if all facts necessary to show that an action is barred by res judicata are within the complaint or subject to judicial notice.  (Frommhagen v. Board of Supervisors (1987) 197 Cal.App.3d 1292, 1299.)  Res judicata is “an umbrella term, encompassing both the primary aspect of claim preclusion and the secondary aspect of issue preclusion.”  (Cal Sierra Development, Inc. v. George Reed, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 663, 671.)

“Claim preclusion arises if a second suit involves: (1) the same cause of action (2) between the same parties (3) after a final judgment on the merits in the first suit.”  (DKN Holdings LLC v. Faerber (2015) 61 Cal.4th 813, 824.)  “Issue preclusion prohibits the relitigation of issues argued and decided in a previous case, even if the second suit raises different causes of action.  [Citation.]  Under issue preclusion, the prior judgment conclusively resolves an issue actually litigated and determined in the first action.”  (Ibid.)

Parsons cites In re Marriage of Buckley (1982) 133 Cal.App.3d 927 (Buckley), where “[h]usband was collaterally estopped by a petition for nullity from relitigating the issue of wife’s alleged fraud.”  (Demurrer at p. 6.)  In that case, the husband sought to have the marriage declared a nullity on the ground that on the date of their wedding, the wife’s prior marriage had not yet been finally dissolved.  (Buckley, supra, 133 Cal.App.3d at p. 930.)  A stipulated judgment was later entered that declared the parties’ marriage a nullity.  (Ibid.)  Thereafter, the husband filed an action against the wife for fraud and concealment because she had falsely represented to him that she was single.  (Ibid.)  The Court of Appeal determined that the trial court properly granted judgment for the wife because the husband was collaterally estopped from relitigating the issue of wife’s alleged fraud due to the stipulated judgment of nullity.  (Ibid.)  The petition for nullity had put the wife’s good faith belief that their marriage was valid at issue.  (Id. at p. 936.)  Although “there was no explicit finding in the stipulated judgment that she was innocent of fraud . . . the award of both attorney’s fees and spousal support amounts to an implied finding in her favor on that issue.”  (Id. at p. 936.)

The Court finds that, similar to the situation in Buckley, the issue of Parsons’s fraudulent intent is precluded by the judgment in the Dissolution Action.

The Dissolution Action’s October 31, 2024 judgment incorporated the September 3, 2024 Statement of Decision.  (11/1/2024 RJN, Ex. A.)  Ozeran sought an annulment based on fraud because Parsons “made ‘fraudulent representations’ that [Ozeran] was the biological father of Roman, when in fact he was not the father, and this fact was ‘known by [Parsons] and concealed from [Ozeran] when the parties got married.”  (Statement of Decision at p. 2.)  The court concluded that there was insufficient evidence that Parsons had “an intent to defraud and continued the fraud scheme and deception before and during the marriage.”  (Id. at p. 9.)  The court therefore denied the request for nullity based on fraud.  (Id. at pp. 9-10.)

Here, the first cause of action for intentional misrepresentation alleges that Parsons falsely represented to Ozeran that he was Roman’s biological father, and Parsons knew that the representation was false when she made it.  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 41-42.)  The second cause of action for fraudulent concealment alleges that Parsons “intentionally failed” to disclose to Ozeran that Roman was not Ozeran’s biological son.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 51; see Cross-Complaint ¶ 53.)  The third cause of action for IIED alleges that Parsons’s outrageous conduct was “her falsely representing that baby Roman was Ozeran’s biological father to induce him to marry her and financially provide for her and baby Roman.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 60.)

These causes of action require Parsons to knowingly and intentionally misrepresent (or conceal facts about) the identity of Roman’s biological father.  The Dissolution Action decided the issue of whether Parsons had “an intent to defraud and continued the fraud scheme and deception before and during the marriage.”  (Statement of Decision at p. 9.)  Accordingly, Ozeran is precluded from relitigating the issue.

Ozeran argues that “the claims that she seeks to strike are ones that cannot, by law and policy, be filed in the same action . . . because, at the core, they are separate claims that must be filed in separate actions, subject to a different body of law, litigate different rights, and request different relief.”  (Suppl. Opposition at p. 2.)  According to Ozeran, “[t]he very nature of the fact that the claims alleged here and the annulment action cannot be brought in the same action or in the same court demonstrates the separateness and uniqueness of the claims.”  (Id. at p. 4.)

As explained by the Buckley court, this makes no difference for issue preclusion:  “[T]he fact that husband could not have responded to wife’s dissolution action with a tort cross-complaint is irrelevant.  The doctrine of collateral estoppel is a doctrine of ‘issue preclusion.’  [Citation.]  The issue of wife’s alleged fraud was necessarily determined by the consent judgment, and husband cannot now vex and harass wife by litigating that issue again in a new cause of action.”  (Buckley, supra, 133 Cal.App.3d at p. 936.)

Ozeran also argues that “there is no collateral estoppel and the issue is not identical where the issue is adjudged on a completely different standard of proof,” noting that fraud must be shown by clear and convincing evidence for an annulment, but the standard of proof in this action is preponderance of the evidence.  (Suppl. Opposition at p. 6.)  Ozeran provides no authority for his contention that the differing standards of proof affect the analysis for issue preclusion.  (See Interinsurance Exchange v. Collins (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 1445, 1448 [“[P]arties are required to include argument and citation to authority in their briefs, and the absence of these necessary elements allows this court to treat [an] issue as waived.”]; Dabney v. Dabney (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 379, 384 [“We need not consider an argument for which no authority is furnished.”].)

The demurrer to the first, second, and third causes of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

B.        Issue Preclusion Does Not Bar the Fourth of Action.

A cause of action for negligence requires (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care, (2) breach of that duty, (3) proximate causation, and (4) damages.  (Artiglio v. Corning Inc. (1998) 18 Cal.4th 604, 614; County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318 [citing Artiglio].)  “Negligence is an unintentional tort, a failure to exercise the degree of care in a given situation that a reasonable man under similar circumstances would exercise to protect others from harm.”  (Donnelly v. Southern Pac. Co. (1941) 18 Cal.2d 863, 869; see also City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal.4th 747, 753 [citing Donnelly].)

The fourth cause of action for negligence alleges that Parsons was negligent in representing to Ozeran that he was Roman’s biological father.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 67.)  This issue was not decided in the Dissolution Action, which considered and decided only the issue of Parsons’s intentional misrepresentations and intent to defraud.

The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is OVERRULED on this ground.

C.        The First Through Fourth Causes of Action Are Valid Causes of Action.

Parsons argues that the first four causes of action are not actionable because “[i]nfidelity or adultery while married is not a civil tort in the State of California,” and “there is no cause for alienation of affection, seduction of an adult, nor breach of a promise to marry.”  (Demurrer at p. 8.)

The causes of action for fraud, IIED, and negligence are based on Parsons’s allegedly false representation that Ozeran was Roman’s biological father.  (E.g., Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 40, 51, 60, 67.)  These are not “infidelity” causes of action that are barred by Civil Code section 43.5.

The demurrer to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action is OVERRULED on this ground.

D.        Ozeran Does Not Allege Appropriation of His Name.

The fifth cause of action alleges common law appropriation of name, and the sixth cause of action alleges appropriation of name under Civil Code section 3344.

“A common law cause of action for appropriation of name or likeness may be pleaded by alleging (1) the defendant’s use of the plaintiff’s identity; (2) the appropriation of plaintiff’s name or likeness to defendant’s advantage, commercially or otherwise; (3) lack of consent; and (4) resulting injury.  [Citation.]  In addition, to plead the statutory remedy provided in Civil Code section 3344, there must also be an allegation of a knowing use of the plaintiff’s name, photograph or likeness for purposes of advertising or solicitation of purchases.”  (Eastwood v. Superior Court (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 409, 417.)

Ozeran alleges that Parsons “has used, and continues to use, Ozeran’s name without his authority or consent, on social media, in the promotion, advertising, and/or marketing of herself, her brand, her business, and her career.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 72, 79.)  “In her use of Ozeran’s name, Parsons seeks to gain, and has gained, an economic or commercial advantage, capitalizing on Ozeran’s reputation.  Indeed, Parsons’ use of Ozeran’s name has been directly connected to Parsons’ commercial purpose.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 81; see Cross-Complaint ¶ 73.)

Upon their marriage, Parsons was permitted to adopt Ozeran’s last name or a combination of both last names without intent to defraud.  (Fam. Code, § 306.5; see Demurrer at pp. 8-9.)  Additionally, a person has a common law right in California to use a different name without the necessity of any legal proceeding as long as the name is not adopted to defraud or intentionally confuse.  (Lee v. Superior Court (1992) 9 Cal.App.4th 510, 514; Weathers v. Superior Court (1976) 54 Cal.App.3d 286, 288; see also Fam. Code, § 306.5, subd. (c) [“This section does not abrogate the common law right of a person to change their name . . . .”].)

The only alleged use of Ozeran’s name is that “Parsons began publicly identifying herself as ‘Parsons-Ozeran,’ including on social media on her two public Instagram accounts.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 37.)  This occurred for the first time in October 2023, “a mere few weeks” before Ozeran filed the Cross-Complaint on November 9, 2023.  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 37.)  There are no facts showing that Parsons adopted the name with an intent to defraud or intentionally confuse.  Parsons using the last name “Parsons-Ozeran” for herself is not Parsons using Ozeran’s identity of “Robert Ozeran.”

Although Ozeran alleges that Parsons “seeks to wrongfully capitalize on Ozeran’s name and reputation for her own economic and commercial gain” and uses his name “on social media, in the promotion, advertising, and/or marketing of herself, her brand, her business, and her career,” this is conclusory and does not allege how Parsons is capitalizing on his name.  (See Cross-Complaint ¶¶ 37, 72, 79.)  There are also no facts about Parsons using Ozeran’s identity “on or in products, merchandise, or goods, or for purposes of advertising or selling, or soliciting purchases of, products, merchandise, goods or services.”  (Civ. Code, § 3344, subd. (a).)

Ozeran also argues that Parsons is wrongfully capitalizing on his name and reputation for her own economic and commercial gain by falsely representing that she is currently employed by his law firm.  (Opposition at p. 9.)  Specifically, “on LinkedIn, Parsons mispresents she is currently employed, and has been employed, as a Senior Paralegal for the Law Offices of Robert Ozeran since January 2020.”  (Cross-Complaint ¶ 37.)  This is not a use of Ozeran’s identity or an appropriation of his name or likeness.

The demurrer to the fifth and sixth causes of action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

E.        Conclusion

The demurrer is SUSTAINED IN PART and OVERRULED IN PART as set forth above.

Ozeran has not shown that he can amend the Cross-Complaint to remedy the deficiencies.  Accordingly, no leave to amend is granted.

The Cross-Complaint is now limited to the fourth cause of action for negligence.

MOTION TO STRIKE

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

Parsons seeks to strike Paragraphs 22-24, 27, 29, 48, 58, 65, 77, and 85.  (Motion at pp. 7-8.)

Paragraphs 22-24 allege, in unnecessary detail, a sexual encounter between Parsons and another man that occurred after all events that are relevant to Ozeran’s claims.  Paragraph 29 alleges that Parsons threatened to bring a legal claim against Ozeran, which is not relevant to any cause of action.  These allegations are inflammatory, prejudicial, and improper.  Paragraphs 48, 58, 65, 77, and 85 are part of causes of action that are no longer at issue following the Court’s demurrer order.  The motion is granted for these paragraphs.

Paragraph 27 is partially relevant to Parsons’s state of mind and her representations about Roman’s parentage.  The motion is granted in part, and the Court orders the following irrelevant and improper language stricken:  “During the conversation, Parsons admitted to Ozeran that she did have physical contact with Jansen, stating that they ‘made out,’ and when pressed further, she admitted that they had oral sex.  During the conversation, Parsons also admitted for the first time that she previously worked as a stripper.”  The motion is denied for the remainder of Paragraph 27.

Parsons seeks to strike the prayer for punitive damages.  (Motion at p. 8.)  A plaintiff can recover punitive damages in tort cases where “the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.”  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  Punitive damages thus require more than the mere commission of a tort.  (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-895.)  Ozeran contends that Parsons’s “extensive fraud and nefarious actions surrounding such a sensitive topic as the paternity or parentage of one’s child certainly warrant punitive damages.”  (Opposition at p. 5.)  The Court sustains the demurrer to all causes of action involving fraud.  There are no factual allegations showing oppression, fraud, or malice for the remaining negligence claim.  The motion is granted for the prayer for punitive damages.

Parsons also seeks to strike the prayer for statutory damages and attorney fees.  (Motion at p. 9.)  Civil Code section 3344 (sixth cause of action) authorizes an award of statutory damages, attorney fees, and costs to a prevailing party.  (See Opposition at p. 6.)  However, the Court sustains the demurrer to that cause of action without leave to amend.  The motion is therefore granted for these items.

The motion to strike is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART with respect to Paragraph 27, as detailed above.  The remainder of the motion is GRANTED without leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer is SUSTAINED IN PART and OVERRULED IN PART without leave to amend.

The motion to strike is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART without leave to amend.

Parsons is ordered to file an answer to the remainder of the Cross-Complaint with 10 days.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1320(j).)

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT

Dept. 48
8:30 a.m.
December 16, 2024

On February 15, 2024, Plaintiff 10630 Wilkins LLC filed this action against Defendant Ed Ovasapyan for unpaid rent and related fees.  The Court entered default against Defendant on September 11, 2024.  On the same day, Plaintiff filed this request for entry of default judgment.
Plaintiff seeks a judgment of $33,686.77, consisting of $28,789.60 in damages, $1,824.44 in prejudgment interest, $862.73 in costs, and $2,210.00 in attorney fees.
“‘Plaintiffs in a default judgment proceeding must prove they are entitled to the damages claimed.’  [Citation].”  (Kim v. Westmoore Partners, Inc. (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 267, 288.)  “[T]he plaintiff must affirmatively establish his entitlement to the specific judgment requested.” (Id. at p. 287.)
Plaintiff’s Managing Member provides a copy of the itemized list of charges sent to Defendant, but no evidence of those individual itemized amounts, no evidence that those charges are part of the lease, and no evidence of the account of credits and debits for an open book account.  (Ourian Decl., Ex. 1.)
Plaintiff does not provide the required calculations for prejudgment interest.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(3).)
Plaintiff requests $2,210.00 in attorney fees.  Neither the complaint nor the request for default judgment include a copy of the lease, so Plaintiff has not proven that it may be awarded contractual attorney fees.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).)
The $862.73 in costs includes $497.78 in filing fees, $355.00 in process server fees, and $9.95 for postage.  Postage is not a recoverable cost.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (b)(3).)
Dor the Declaration of Nonmilitary Status, Plaintiff states, “Defendant’s military status is unknown.”  As stated on the same page of the Form CIV-100, “If the defendant/respondent is in the military service, or their military status is unknown, the defendant/respondent is entitled to certain rights and protections under federal and state law before a default judgment can be entered.”  Plaintiff must verify that Defendant is not in the U.S. military service.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(5).)
The request for entry of default judgment is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.
Any amended request for default judgment should use Form JUD-100 for the proposed judgment.
