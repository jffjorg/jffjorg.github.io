DEPARTMENT 14 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV07006    Hearing Date: November 30, 2023    Dept: 14

Instant Motion

 

            Defendant Kirk now moves this court for an order staying this case in its entirety, pending the resolution of the criminal case against Defendant Kirk.

 

Decision

 

            The motion is DENIED.

 

Governing Standards

 

Renewed Motions

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 1008(b) provides in relevant part as follows:

 

“(b) A party who originally made an application for an order which was refused in whole or part, or granted conditionally or on terms, may make a subsequent application for the same order upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown. For a failure to comply with this subdivision, any order made on a subsequent application may be revoked or set aside on ex parte motion.”

 

“The party seeking reconsideration must provide not just new evidence or different facts, but a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce it at an earlier time.” Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457. This requirement also applies to new or different law. Baldwin v. Home Sav. Of America (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 1192, 1200.  New law is authority arising after the court’s decision, and different law is a principle different than that originally produced. Id. at 1196-97.

 

Motions to Stay

           

The leading case on how to handle civil actions with parallel criminal proceedings is Fuller v. Superior Court (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 299, 307-308:

 

“Courts faced with a civil defendant who is exposed to a related criminal prosecution have responded with various procedural solutions designed to fairly balance the interests of the parties and the judicial system. Accommodation of the various interests, however, is usually made to a defendant in a civil action from the standpoint of fairness, not from any constitutional right. Courts that are confronted with a civil defendant who is exposed to criminal prosecution arising from the same facts weigh the parties' competing interests with a view toward accommodating the interests of both parties, if possible. Courts have broad discretion in controlling the course of discovery. Hence, in a discovery dispute, such as this one, the trial court must exercise its discretion in assessing and balancing the nature and substantiality of the injustices claimed on all sides.

 

Historically, courts have devised a number of procedures designed to accommodate the specific circumstances of the case. One accommodation is to stay the civil proceeding until disposition of the related criminal prosecution. Another possibility is to allow the civil defendant to invoke the privilege against self-incrimination, even if doing so may limit the defendant's ability to put on a defense. Other accommodations have included conferring an immunity on the party invoking the privilege, or precluding a litigant who claims the constitutional privilege against self-incrimination in discovery from waiving the privilege and testifying at trial to matters upon which the privilege had been asserted. Each of these procedural tools is devised based on the circumstances of the particular case. The alleviation of tension between constitutional rights has been treated as within the province of a court's discretion in seeking to assure the sound administration of justice.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted).

 

A stay of the case is generally appropriate where the criminal case arises out of the same transaction as the civil case and the applicable criminal statute of limitations is likely to run comparatively soon. See Pacers, Inc. v. Superior Court (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 686, 690 (case involving battery of federal officers stayed where federal statute would run out in just less than two years). A stay order may be less appropriate where the party invoking the privilege is not the only defendant and the length of the stay is unknown. See Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 886-887 (providing a list of generic factors to be analyzed).

 

Discussion

 

            On November 8, 2022, Defendant Kirk brought a motion before this court asking for the same relief he now seeks. The motion was granted in part and denied in part. This court ordered that discovery would be stayed to the extent that Defendant Kirk need not give testimony. (Declaration of Alec M. Rishwain Exhibit C). The court expressly left open disputes about the production of documents and things, as well as third-party discovery. (Id.).

 

            Since that time, the court and parties have been able to resolve the disputes regarding requests for production directed to Defendant Kirk. The court also denied a motion for summary judgment brought by Plaintiff, based on the provisions of Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(h). Defendant Kirk argues that these two events provide “new evidence and different facts” sufficient to support renewal of this motion. They do not.

 

            The animating reason for the stay request is Defendant Kirk’s concern that testimony given in this case might have negative consequences in the criminal case. However, as stated in Fuller and quoted above, a civil court’s obligation is to accommodate both parties’ interests “from a standpoint of fairness, not from any constitutional right.”[1] This court did so when it ruled on the motion initially, by staying any testimonial discovery from Defendant Kirk, but permitting the collection of evidence from third parties or from other witnesses who were not subject to Fifth Amendment concerns. This court also did so when it first continued,[2] then denied outright Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment, based on Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(h). The resolution of certain inter-party discovery disputes does not change the fundamental analysis, nor would it justify this court in overruling its previous decisions.

 

            Defendant Kirk expresses a fear about what may happen on a future motion for summary judgment. But no such motion has been filed. And such a motion may itself be subject to the strictures of Code of Civil Procedure § 1008. In any event, that is a concern to be addressed if and when it arises, not by way of advance speculation.

 

Conclusion

 

            Defendant Kirk remains, as he has always been, free to withhold his testimony. When this motion was brought the first time, the court balanced the interests of these parties and determined that while the parties could not seek testimony from Defendant Kirk, they could seek to gather certain physical evidence, as well as seek all sorts of discovery from third parties. No new facts or evidence have come to light which would allow this court to overrule its prior decision. The renewed motion is DENIED.


[1] The court has various recognized options for making that accommodation, though it is not strictly limited to the options in any given list: (1) a complete stay of the case, (2) allow the defendant to refuse discovery responses until trial, at which time the plaintiff may bring a motion in limine to exclude any testimony by the defendant, (3) obtain a grant of immunity for the defendant, or (4) set a deadline in advance of the trial date by which the defendant must make a final decision about whether he will provide discovery and testify on his own behalf, or not. See People ex rel. City of Dana Point v. Holistic Health (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 1016, 1032; In re Marriage of Hoffmeister (1984) 161 Cal.App.3d 1163, 1171.

[2] See Minute Orders of February 2 and 9, 2023, as well as the ex parte papers filed on January 31 and February 8, 2023.


Case Number: 22STCV19629    Hearing Date: November 30, 2023    Dept: 14

(1)        MTCF RFP

 

            Plaintiffs now move this court for an order compelling Defendant Regents to make further responses to their Requests for Production of Documents. Plaintiffs also seek sanctions in the amount of $4,255.00.

 

Defendant Regents, in turn, seek sanctions of $3,500.00.

 

Decision

 

            The motion is CONTINUED to December 12, 2023. Plaintiff may serve and file reply papers on or before December 6, 2023.

 

Governing Statute

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310 provides in relevant part as follows:

 

“(a) On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with each of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

…

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.

…

(h) …the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiffs filed a notice of non-opposition to this motion, indicating that they had not received an opposition from Defendant Regents, and that one had not been filed. Consequently, no reply papers were filed. However, Defendant Regents did file and serve opposition papers on August 15, 2023. The papers themselves, along with a proof of service, appear in the court’s file. Plaintiffs are entitled to an opportunity to review and respond to those papers.

 

Conclusion

 

            Because there was confusion over the service and filing of the opposition papers, and because someone needs to inquire about the non-party’s willingness to release their records, the motion is CONTINUED to December 12, 2023. Plaintiff may serve and file reply papers on or before December 6, 2023.

 

(2)        MTCF RFA

 

            Plaintiffs now move this court for an order compelling Defendant Regents to make further responses to their Requests for Admissions. Plaintiffs also seek sanctions in the amount of $1,322.50.

 

Defendant Regents, in turn, seek sanctions of $1,812.00.

 

Decision

 

            The motion is DENIED, without prejudice. No sanctions are awarded.

 

Governing Statute

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.290 provides in relevant part as follows:

 

(a) On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or both of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.

 

(b)(1) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

…

(c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.

 

(d) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

Discussion

 

            It is well-established that a plaintiff in a FEHA case may introduce evidence of events that happened to other, similarly situated employees, even if she did not witness and was not present in the workplace for those events. See Pantoja v. Anton (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 87, 109-122. Case law has long referred to this as “me too” evidence and permits it on the basis that it is relevant to show intent under Evidence Code § 1101(b). Id. Since this evidence is generally admissible, it is also generally discoverable.

 

            However, this does not necessarily mean that a prospective “me too” witness can be compelled to release their records, nor does it mean that the evidence surrounding a “me too” witness is a proper subject for a Request for Admission.

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.010 requires that a Request for Admission cover “a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” Gopi Gopaldas is not a party to this case. What happened to them is not necessarily in controversy in this case. Should this person be deposed as a “me too” witness, and should other witnesses be questioned about her, then a Request for Admission might be ripe. Until then, it is premature.

 

            No sanctions are appropriate on this record. The questions may ultimately be proper, they just aren’t proper yet. 

 

Conclusion

 

            It is premature to use requests for admissions to seek facts about a prospective “me too” witness before that witness has agreed to testify or been deposed. Therefore, the motion is DENIED, without prejudice. No sanctions are awarded.

Case Number: 23STCV10822    Hearing Date: November 30, 2023    Dept: 14

Instant Motion

 

Plaintiff now moves this court to issue a preliminary injunction, directing Defendant Warren to allow Plaintiff access to the storage unit, and forbidding Defendant from destroying or otherwise disposing of the contents of that unit.

 

Decision

 

            The motion is DENIED.

 

Governing Law

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 526 provides in relevant part as follows:

 

            “(a) An injunction may be granted in the following cases:

(1) When it appears by the complaint that the plaintiff is entitled to the relief demanded, and the relief, or any part thereof, consists in restraining the commission or continuance of the act complained of, either for a limited period or perpetually.

(2) When it appears by the complaint or affidavits that the commission or continuance of some act during the litigation would produce waste, or great or irreparable injury, to a party to the action.

(3) When it appears, during the litigation, that a party to the action is doing, or threatens, or is about to do, or is procuring or suffering to be done, some act in violation of the rights of another party to the action respecting the subject of the action, and tending to render the judgment ineffectual.

(4) When pecuniary compensation would not afford adequate relief.

(5) Where it would be extremely difficult to ascertain the amount of compensation which would afford adequate relief.

(6) Where the restraint is necessary to prevent a multiplicity of judicial proceedings.

(7) Where the obligation arises from a trust.

 

(b) An injunction cannot be granted in the following cases:

(1) To stay a judicial proceeding pending at the commencement of the action in which the injunction is demanded, unless the restraint is necessary to prevent a multiplicity of proceedings.

(2) To stay proceedings in a court of the United States.

(3) To stay proceedings in another state upon a judgment of a court of that state.

(4) To prevent the execution of a public statute by officers of the law for the public benefit.

(5) To prevent the breach of a contract the performance of which would not be specifically enforced, other than a contract in writing for the rendition of personal services from one to another where the promised service is of a special, unique, unusual, extraordinary, or intellectual character, which gives it peculiar value, the loss of which cannot be reasonably or adequately compensated in damages in an action at law…

(6) To prevent the exercise of a public or private office, in a lawful manner, by the person in possession.

(7) To prevent a legislative act by a municipal corporation.”

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 527 provides in relevant part as follows:

 

“(a) A preliminary injunction may be granted at any time before judgment upon a verified complaint, or upon affidavits if the complaint in the one case, or the affidavits in the other, show satisfactorily that sufficient grounds exist therefor. No preliminary injunction shall be granted without notice to the opposing party.”

 

“A trial court's decision on whether to grant a preliminary injunction rests on ‘”(i) the likelihood that the party seeking the injunction will ultimately prevail on the merits of his [or her] claim, and (ii) the balance of harm presented, i.e., the comparative consequences of the issuance and nonissuance of the injunction.”’ (Law School Admission Council, Inc. v. State of California (2014) 222 Cal.App.4th 1265, 1280, quoting Common Cause v. Board of Supervisors (1989) 49 Cal.3d 432, 441-442).” Saltonstall v. City of Sacramento (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 837, 856.

 

Discussion

 

            There is currently no operative complaint in this case. The court sustained a demurrer to the operative complaint on October 16, 2023. Plaintiff was given 30 days to amend his complaint. 30 days from October 16, 2023, is November 15, 2023. No amended complaint has been filed.

 

While there is a motion for reconsideration pending, the pendency of that motion does not excuse Plaintiff from his obligation to comply with the court’s orders. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 581(f)(2), Defendant Warren is entitled to a dismissal of the action should they choose to make that motion in the form called for by California Rules of Court Rule 3.1320(h). In the absence of an operative complaint and under these circumstances, Plaintiff has no probability of prevailing and cannot meet his burden on this motion.

 

However, even if there were an operative complaint, Plaintiff has not provided any admissible evidence on which the court could base a ruling in his favor. Code of Civil Procedure § 527(a) requires that an injunction either be supported by a verified complaint or sworn declarations. Plaintiff’s last complaint was not verified, and no declarations were submitted with his motion. Therefore, this court cannot rule in his favor.

 

Conclusion

 

            Because there is no operative complaint, and motion is not supported by any admissible evidence, it is DENIED.
