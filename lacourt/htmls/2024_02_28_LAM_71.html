DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties
concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon
resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an
agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if
you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by
notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDept71@LACourt.org.  Include
the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject
line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the
hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and
whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant,
cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.



           
Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear
at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue
the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the
argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties
in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to
argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words
"SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    
If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via
Court-Connect.



If
the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling,
the Court will take the  matter off calendar.

                       
  

            Note
that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent
authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative
ruling as the order of the court. 
 

 

            If you
submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or
thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the
court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.

CRC Violations

CRC, Rule 3.1113(d) provides, “[e]xcept in a summary judgment or summary adjudication motion, no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 15 pages. In a summary judgment or summary adjudication motion, no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 20 pages. No reply or closing memorandum may exceed 10 pages. The page limit does not include the caption page, the notice of motion and motion, exhibits, declarations, attachments, the table of contents, the table of authorities, or the proof of service.” 

CRC, Rule 3.1113(e) provides, “[a] party may apply to the court ex parte but with written notice of the application to the other parties, at least 24 hours before the memorandum is due, for permission to file a longer memorandum. The application must state reasons why the argument cannot be made within the stated limit.” 

CRC, Rule 3.1113(g) provides, “[a] memorandum that exceeds the page limits of these rules must be filed and considered in the same manner as a late-filed paper.” 

Petitioner’s and Respondents’ filings greatly exceed the 15-page maximum stated in CRC, Rule 3.1113(d).  Parties violated CRC, Rule 3.1113(e) by failing to apply to the court ex parte with written notice of the application to the other parties, at least 24 hours before the memorandum is due, for permission to file a longer memorandum stating the reasons why the argument cannot be made within the stated limit.  The Court, in its discretion, will consider parties’ briefings up to their applicable page limits as defined in CRC, Rule 3.1113(d).

 

Background

On or about August 30, 2021, Petitioner and Respondent entered into a written agreement to arbitrate (“Agreement”).  (Petition ¶4(a).)  Paragraph 6 of the Agreement provides for arbitration of disputes arising out of the Agreement.  (Petition ¶¶4(b)-(c), Attachment 4(b).)  The subject of arbitration is the meaning of ¶25 of two ground leases between Petitioner, as successor ground lessor, and Respondent, as successor ground lessee, and affirmative defenses.  (Petition ¶5.)

Hon. Elizabeth A. White (ret.) was appointed as arbitrator.  (Petition ¶6.)  The arbitration hearing took place from May 22, 2023, until May 31, 2023, at JAMS, 1925 Century Park E., Suite 1400, Los Angeles, CA 90067.  (Petition ¶7.)

The arbitration award (“Award”) was made on October 10, 2023.  (Petition ¶8(a).)  The Award requires Respondent to pay Petitioner $2,829,433.78.  (Petition ¶8(b)(1).)  The Award also declares the meaning of ¶25 of the ground leases, rejects Respondent’s affirmative defenses, and provides the amount of the Award.  (Petition ¶¶8(b)(4), (c), Attachment 8(c).)  Petitioner alleges that a signed copy of the Award was actually served on October 10, 2023.  (Petition ¶9(b).)

On October 25, 2023, Petitioner filed the instant petition requesting this Court confirm the award and enter judgment according to it.  (Petition ¶10(a).)  Petitioner requests this Court award Petitioner interest from October 10, 2023, at the statutory rate, award costs of suit according to proof, and award Petitioner attorney fees incurred in this action according to proof.  (Petition ¶¶10(d)(1), (e)(2), (f)(2).)

On November 3, 2023, Respondent filed its opposition to Petitioner’s petition as well as a cross-petition to vacate the Award (“Cross-Petition”).  Respondent’s Cross-Petition seeks to vacate the Award because the award was obtained by corruption, fraud, or other unfair means; the misconduct of a neutral arbitrator substantially prejudiced Respondent’s rights; the arbitrator exceeded his or her authority and the Award cannot be fairly corrected; and the arbitrator unfairly refused to postpone the hearing or to hear evidence useful to settle the dispute.  (Cross-Petition ¶¶10(c)(1)(a), (c)(1)(c)-(e).)  Further, Respondent does not request a new arbitration hearing.  (Cross-Petition ¶10(c)(3).)  Cross-Petitioner requests an award for costs of suit according to proof, attorney fees according to proof, and an order that should there ever be a further arbitration rehearing, it must proceed before a new arbitrator and that this Court issue a statement of decision on the instant Cross-Petition.  (Cross-Petition ¶¶10(e)(2), (f)(2), (g).)

On December 15, 2023, Petitioner filed its reply to the Petition and opposition to the Cross-Petition.  On January 26, 2024, Respondent filed its reply to the Cross-Petition.

 

Legal Standard

The party seeking judicial enforcement of a private arbitration award has the burden of proving the award as well as the existence of a valid arbitration agreement.  (Toal v. Tardif (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1208, 1223 [holding burden not met by submitting copy of contract with arbitration provision signed by party’s attorney rather than by party personally].)

The specific grounds upon which an arbitrator’s award may be vacated are

set forth in C.C.P. §1286.2.  Except for such grounds, arbitration awards are

immune from judicial review.  (See Moncharsh v. Heily & Blase (1992) 3 Cal.4th 1, 10-11 [limiting grounds for judicial review effectuates the parties’ agreement that the award be final and also reflects that arbitrators need not follow the law and may base their decisions on “broad principles of justice and equity . . . paths neither marked nor traceable by judicial review”].)  Generally, errors of law committed by the arbitrator are not grounds for challenging the arbitration award.  (Id. at pg. 11.)  The sufficiency of evidence of the evidence to support the award is immaterial and courts cannot review the same.  (See Morris v. Zuckerman (1968) 69 Cal.2d. 686, 691.)  Courts cannot pass upon the validity of the arbitrator’s reasoning and cannot substitute its judgment for that of the arbitrator.  (See Moncharsh, 3 Cal.4th at pg. 11.)

          Interpretation of the underlying contract is ordinarily a question of law for the arbitrator, not the court: “It is the arbitrator’s construction which was bargained for; and so far as the arbitrator’s decision concerns construction of the contract, the courts have no business overruling him because their interpretation of the contract is different from his.”  (United Steelworkers of America v. Enterprise Wheel & Car Corp. (1960) 363 US 593, 599.)

 

Discussion

Petitioner has met its burden to prove the arbitration award and the existence of the valid arbitration agreement.  (Petition ¶¶4(a), (c); Attachments 4(b) at ¶6; Petition ¶8(c), Attachment 8c.)

          Respondent’s objections to the confirmation of the Award are not well taken.  An award may not be attacked for purported wrong arbitrator determinations of law, rule, interpretation, evidence, relevance, materiality, credibility, agreement, etc.  A court may not substitute whatever determinations it might make for those made by the arbitrator.  (See, e.g., Morris, 69 Cal.2d at pg. 691; Moncharsh, 3 Cal.4th at pg. 11; Heimlich v. Shivji (2019) 7 Cal.5th 350, 370 [“When parties opt for the forum of arbitration they agree to be bound by the decision of that forum knowing that arbitrators, like judges, are fallible.”].)  Respondent was not prevented from putting on its case in arbitration.  (See Heimlich, 7 Cal.5th at 368; Royal Alliance Associates, Inc. (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 1092.)  Further, the arbitration comported with the JAMS Comprehensive rules, which govern the arbitration proceedings.  (Greenspan v. LADT, LLC (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1413, 1426, 1440 [affirming the primacy of JAMS Comprehensive Rules and “final” arbitrator authority].)

          Petitioner requests prejudgment interest, fees, and costs incurred on the cross-petitions before this Court.  The Court is not inclined to grant such fees on a motion to confirm arbitration award, and in response to the instant ruling, parties can properly argue and challenge attorneys’ fee requests and prejudgment interest.

 

Conclusion

Petitioner’s petition to confirm the arbitration award is granted.  Petitioner may separately move for a motion to grant prejudgment interest, fees, and costs.

Respondent’s cross-petition to vacate the arbitration award is denied.

Background

A.   Stanley’s Demurrer to SAC Lead

Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a), emphasis added.)  A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Stanley’s counsel declares on September 25, 2023, after having reviewed Plaintiff’s SAC, she drafted and sent a letter via email to Plaintiff outlining the insufficiencies in the Plaintiff’s SAC in an attempt to meet and confer regarding Defendant’s potential Demurrer specifically as to Cause of Action against this demurring Defendant for Breach of Warranty.  (Decl. of Bridwell ¶3, Exh. A.)  Stanley’s counsel declares that in the letter she asked Plaintiff to call her office.  (Decl. of Bridwell ¶3.)  Stanley’s counsel declares she attempted to call/set up a telephone call with Plaintiff to discuss the issues set forth in this demurrer, however, Plaintiff responded back that he addressed all issues brought up in Stanley’s meet and confer letter via his letter dated October 3, 2023.  (Decl. of Bridwell ¶4, Exh. C.)  Stanley’s counsel declares the parties were not able to resolve the issues now before this Court via the subject demurrer.  (Decl. of Bridwell ¶4.)  Stanley’s counsel’s declaration is sufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a).  Therefore, the Court will consider the instant demurrer.

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Discussion

Failure to State a Claim

Breach of Warranty (1st COA)

An express warranty “is a contractual promise from the seller that the goods conform to the promise. If they do not, the buyer is entitled to recover the difference between the value of the goods accepted by the buyer and the value of the goods had they been as warranted.”  (Daugherty v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 830; Com. Code §2313.)

“The essential elements of a cause of action under the California Uniform Commercial Code for breach of an express warranty to repair defects are (1) an express warranty to repair defects given in connection with the sale of goods; (2) the existence of a defect covered by the warranty; (3) the buyer’s notice to the seller of such a defect within a reasonable time after its discovery; (4) the seller’s failure to repair the defect in compliance with the warranty; and (5) resulting damages.”  (Orichian v. BMW of North America, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1322, 1333-1334, internal citations omitted.)

“Unlike express warranties, which are basically contractual in nature, the implied warranty of merchantability arises by operation of law. It does not ‘impose a general requirement that goods precisely fulfill the expectation of the buyer. Instead, it provides for a minimum level of quality.’”  (American Suzuki Motor Corp. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1291, 1295-1296, internal citations omitted.). “[I]n cases involving personal injuries resulting from defective products, the theory of strict liability in tort has virtually superseded the concept of implied warranties.”  (Grinnell v. Charles Pfizer & Co. (1969) 274 Cal.App.2d 424, 432.)

“Vertical privity is a prerequisite in California for recovery on a theory of breach of the implied warranties of fitness and merchantability.” (United States Roofing, Inc. v. Credit Alliance Corp. (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 1431, 1441, internal citations omitted.)

Plaintiff alleges on October 15, 2021, he went to Stanley’s dental office and placed himself in charge of Stanley for the purpose of having cosmetic dental work including ordering and fitting Plaintiff with Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Lead, pg. 23:10-13.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley agreed to provide the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture for a total compensation of $450.  (SAC Lead, pg. 23:14-15.)  Plaintiff alleges on October 15, 2022, Stanley subjected Plaintiff to 24 unnecessary X-Rays of which 18 are on file, so he can get paid by Denti-Cal for the denture.  (SAC Lead, pg. 23:16-18.) 

Plaintiff alleges Denti-Cal paid Stanley $350 for the denture instead of $250 as Stanley had told Plaintiff that Denti-Cal will pay him, and Stanley’s and Plaintiff’s agreement was that Plaintiff would pay him the balance on delivery day of the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture after Stanley fits the denture properly on Plaintiff and delivers it with Valplast Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number.  (SAC Lead, pg. 23:19-24.) 

Plaintiff alleges Stanley failed to follow the proper procedures for ordering Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Lead, pg. 24:3-4.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley failed to order the denture from Certified Valplast Laboratory or from dental laboratory that makes Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Lead, pg. 24:5-7.)  Plaintiff alleges during all the times Stanley was treating Plaintiff, Stanley both expressly and impliedly warranted to Plaintiff that everything necessary and proper was being done for Plaintiff to perform and take proper impressions, use of proper materials, use of proper tooth shade, proper instructions for ordering the denture, and use of proper dental laboratory to order from the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture, however Stanley breached those warranties.  (SAC Lead, pg. 25:11-17.)  

Plaintiff alleges as a direct and proximate result of Stanley’s negligence, and breach of warranty, Plaintiff sustained the following injuries and damages: Plaintiff was subjected to 24 X-Rays of which at least 18 unnecessary X-Rays, Plaintiff was subjected to the inability to have the denture done with another dentist; Plaintiff remains up to this time without Genuine Valplast Partial Denture; Plaintiff remains up to this time without a denture; past dental expenses; and future dental and medical expenses.  (SAC Lead, pg. 25:18-24.)

Plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for express warranty because, first, Plaintiff fails to allege the denture at issue had a manufacturing defect. Second, there are no allegations that Stanley manufactured the denture or expressly guaranteed the quality of the denture.  The gravamen of Plaintiff’s cause of action is that Stanley ordered a different denture than the one he agreed to order, the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.

Plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for breach of implied warranty of merchantability because he does not allege the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture does not “provide[] for a minimum level of quality.’”  (American Suzuki Motor Corp., 37 Cal.App.4th 1291, 1295-1296, internal citations omitted.)

Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s cause of action for breach of warranty is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s cause of action for breach of warranty is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

Discussion

Failure to State a Claim

Breach of Contract (1st COA)

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) 

Although a written contract is usually pleaded by alleging its making and attaching a copy which is incorporated by reference, a written contract can also be pleaded by alleging the making and the substance of the relevant terms. (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Insurance Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199; Perry v. Robertson (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 333, 341.)  “An oral contract may be pleaded generally as to its effect, because it is rarely possible to allege the exact words. [Citation.] A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. [Citations.]”  (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  That plaintiff could not allege with specificity the exact terms of the contract does not preclude statement of a cause of action.  (Id.)

Plaintiff alleges on October 15, 2021, and November 15, 2021, Plaintiff and Stanley, and on or about mid-December 2021, and mid-January 2022, Plaintiff and Stanley and Dental Lab owner and leading dental technician Gary Gordon, entered into an oral agreement, according to the terms of which defendants promised and agreed to deliver Genuine Valplast Partial Denture with Valplast Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number, in exchange for valuable consideration.  (Related SAC, pg. 20 at ¶2.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley promised to deliver the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture prior to Christmas 2021 holiday, then Defendants Stanley and Dental Lab delayed delivery to February 21, 2022.  (Related SAC, pg. 20 at ¶2.)  Plaintiff alleges on February 21, 2022, Defendants breached their promise and agreement by failing to deliver as agreed instead delivered Generic Resin Partial Denture that Plaintiff told them at the time they promised and agreed he does not want.  (Related SAC, pg. 20 at ¶2.) 

Plaintiff alleges Stanley told Plaintiff that X-Rays are not necessary for any of the dental services he will be performing on Plaintiff, but Denti-Cal will not pay him for any dental service except Tooth Cleaning unless he takes X-Rays.  (Related SAC, pg. 20 at ¶3.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley told Plaintiff Denti-Cal requires him to take Full Mouth 24 X-Rays to pay him for the denture.  (Related SAC, pg. 20 at ¶3.)  Plaintiff alleges he told Stanley his Radiologist advised him not to take any X-Rays unless it is necessary and demanded from Stanley his assurance and guarantee that if he consents to unnecessary X-Rays, he will deliver Genuine Valplast Partial Denture with Valplace Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number and not Generic Resin Partial Denture.  (Related SAC, pgs. 20-21 at ¶3.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley agreed and assured Plaintiff telling him, “If Valplast Denture is what you want, you will get it.”  (Related SAC, pg. 21 at ¶3.)  Plaintiff alleges in reliance on the promise made by defendant Stanley that he will deliver a Genuine Valplast Partial Denture with Valplace Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number and not a Generic Resin Partial Denture,  Plaintiff consented to Stanley taking X-Rays, and Stanley took 24 unnecessary X-Rays of which 18 are on file.  (Related SAC, pg. 21 at ¶3.)

Plaintiff alleges in reliance on the promise made by Gary Gordon owner and the leading dental technician of Dental Lab that he will make a Genuine Valplast Partial Denture and not Generic Resin Partial Denture and deliver it to Stanley with Valplast Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number, and, in reliance on Stanley telling Plaintiff to be patient to wait until Dental Lab reopens in mid-January 2022 to make the the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture, Plaintiff waited.  (Related SAC, pg. 21 at ¶4.)

Plaintiff alleges he performed all of his obligations under the terms of the agreement.  (Related SAC, pg. 21 at ¶5.)  Plaintiff alleges on February 21, 2022, Defendants materially breached their oral contract with Plaintiff in that, among other things, Defendants failed to perform as promised by not delivering Genuine Valplast Partial Denture with Valplast Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number, instead Defendants presented Plaintiff with Generic Resin Partial Denture.  (Related SAC, pg. 21 at ¶5.)

Plaintiff alleges as a result of the material breach of contract by Stanley, Plaintiff has sustained the following injuries and damages: Plaintiff was subjected to 24 unnecessary X-Rays of which 18 are on file, plaintiff was subjected to the inability to have the denture done with another dentist; and as a direct result of the material breach of contract by Stanley and Dental Lab owner and leading technician Gary Gordon, Plaintiff remains up to this time without Genuine Valplast Partial Denture, and without a denture, past dental expenses, and future dental and medical expenses.  (Related SAC, pgs. 21-22 at ¶5.)

Plaintiff sufficiently alleges a cause of action for breach of oral contract.  (Related SAC, pgs. 20-22 at ¶¶1-5.)

Accordingly, Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s Related SAC is overruled as to the cause of action for breach of contract.

 

Fraud (2nd COA)

“The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’”  (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974, internal quotation marks omitted.)

Fraud actions are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading.  (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216.)  Fraud must be pleaded with specificity rather than with general and conclusory allegations.  (Small v. Fritz Companies, Inc. (2003) 30 Cal.4th 167, 184.)  The specificity requirement means a plaintiff must allege facts showing how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were made, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, the plaintiff must allege the names of the persons who made the representations, their authority to speak on behalf of the corporation, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when the representation was made.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645; West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 793.)

Plaintiff alleges on October 15, 2021, Plaintiff employed Stanley and retained him for compensation to provide him with Genuine Valplast Partial Denture delivered with Valplast Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Unique Serial Number.  (SAC Related, pg. 22 at ¶2.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley agreed in return for a total compensation of $450 for the denture.  (SAC Related, pgs. 22-23 at ¶2.) 

Plaintiff alleges on October 15, 2021, on November 15, 2021, and in mid-December 2021 Stanley misrepresented to Plaintiff that Dental Lab in Brentwood that he does business with for many years makes Genuine Valplast Partial Dentures, and Stanley misrepresented to Plaintiff that Dental dental technician George Seeber that he does business with for many years told him that he will be doing the Genuine Valplast Partial Dentures himself.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23 at ¶3.)   Plaintiff alleges the information Stanley told Plaintiff turned out to be false.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23 at ¶3.)   

Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that Stanley deliberately made the misrepresentation to Plaintiff when he knew or should have known that Dental Lab in Brentwood does not make Genuine Valplast Partial Dentures, and Stanley deliberately made the misrepresentation to Plaintiff that Dental Lab dental technician George Seeber told him that he will be doing the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture when he knew or should have known that Dental Lab dental technician George Seeber does not make Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23 at ¶4.)

Plaintiff alleges Stanley made such misrepresentation to gain financial benefit to get the consent of Plaintiff to file a claim for denture with Denti-Cal to get paid $250 and thereby delivers Generic Resin Partial Denture and to attempt to defraud Plaintiff that it is Genuine Valplast Partial Denture and demands that Plaintiff to pay him additional $250, $50 more than was agreed on for the total compensation of $450 for Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23 at ¶5.)

Plaintiff alleges on February 21, 2022, Stanley attempted to defraud Plaintiff to get paid an additional $250 by misrepresenting to Plaintiff that the Generic Resin Partial Denture he presented that Denti-Cal had paid him its full cost and even $100 more, is Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23 at ¶6.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley knew or should have known it is a Generic Resin Partial Denture.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23 at ¶6.)  Plaintiff alleges Stanley attempted to induce Plaintiff to believe him telling him, “I pride myself of having 30 years’ experience as dentist to back me up” to pay him the additional $250 he demanded, which is $50 more than was agreed on for the total compensation of $450 for Genuine Valplast Partial Denture delivered with Valplast Certificate of Authenticity and Valplast Serial Number which Stanley did not deliver.  (SAC Related, pgs. 23-24 at ¶6.)

Plaintiff alleges Stanley intended to induce Plaintiff’s reliance on his misrepresentation.  (SAC Related, pg. 24 at ¶8.)  Plaintiff alleges he did rely on Stanley’s misrepresentation in that Plaintiff would have neither allowed Stanley to subject him to 24 unnecessary X-Rays of which 18 on file, nor to file a claim for the denture with Denti-Cal to get paid.  (SAC Related, pg. 24 at ¶8.) 

Plaintiff alleges his reliance on Stanley’s misrepresentations was justifiable because he had a complete dependence on and trust in Dr. Stanley for the information regarding Dental Lab because he said he does business with it for many years, and his assurance that Dental Lab in Brentwood makes Genuine Valplast Partial Denture, and his assurance that Dental Lab dental technician George Seeber told him he will be doing the Genuine Valplast Partial Denture.  (SAC Related, pg. 25 at ¶12.)

Plaintiff alleges as a direct result of the fraudulent misrepresentations of Stanley, Plaintiff has sustained the following injuries and damages: Plaintiff was subjected to 24 unnecessary X-Rays of which 18 are on file, plaintiff was subjected to the inability to have the denture done with another dentist; and as a direct result of the fraudulent misrepresentation of Dr. Stanley and Dental Lab owner and leading technician Gary Gordon plaintiff remains up to this time without Genuine Valplast Partial Denture, and plaintiff remains up to this time without a denture, past dental expenses, and future dental and medical expenses.  (SAC Related, pg. 25 at ¶13.)

Plaintiff sufficiently alleges a cause of action for fraud.

Accordingly, Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 2nd cause of action for fraud is overruled.

 

Fraudulent Misrepresentation (3rd COA)

“The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’”  (Engalla, 15 Cal.4th at pg. 974, internal quotation marks omitted.)

Plaintiff’s allegations in his cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation duplicate his cause of action for fraud.  Claims which merely duplicate other claims in a pleading are subject to demurrer.  (Shoemaker v. Myers (1990) 52 Cal.3d 1, 14.)  Moreover, a demurrer is properly sustained without leave to amend as to a cause of action which adds nothing to a complaint by way of fact or theory of recovery.  (Rodrigues v. Campbell Industries (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 494, 501; Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Association, Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)

Accordingly, Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 3rd cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 1st cause of action for breach of contract and 2nd cause of action fraud are overruled.

Stanley’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 3rd cause of action for intentional misrepresentation sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

D.   Stanley’s Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s Related SAC

Stanley moves to strike the following portions from Plaintiff’s Related SAC: (1) Page 20, Prayer at paragraph 5, stating “For cost of legal counsel”; (2) Page 20, Prayer at paragraph 7, stating “For exemplary damages and leave of court for punitive damages”; (3) Page 22, Prayer at paragraph 5, stating “For cost of legal counsel”; (4) Page 22, Prayer at paragraph 7, stating “For exemplary damages and leave of court for punitive damages”; (5) Page 26, Prayer at paragraph 5, stating “For cost of legal counsel”; (6) Page 26, Prayer at paragraph 7, stating “For exemplary damages and leave of court for punitive damages”; (7) Page 28, Prayer at paragraph 5, stating “For cost of legal counsel”; and (8) Page 28, Prayer at paragraph 7, stating “For exemplary damages and leave of court for punitive damages.”  (Notice MTS Related SAC, pg. 2.)

 

Legal Standard

C.C.P. §436 provides that the Court may, upon a motion made pursuant to C.C.P. §435, or at any time within its discretion and upon terms it deems proper, “strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading,” or any pleading or part thereof “not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  (C.C.P. §436.)

 

Punitive Damages

C.C.P. §425.13 provides, “(a) In any action for damages arising out of the professional negligence of a health care provider, no claim for punitive damages shall be included in a complaint or other pleading unless the court enters an order allowing an amended pleading that includes a claim for punitive damages to be filed.”

Plaintiff has not obtained leave of court to include punitive damages in his pleading.  Therefore, Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages is subject to a motion to strike.

Accordingly, Stanley’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s requests for punitive damages is granted with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Attorneys’ Fees

C.C.P. §1021 provides, “[e]xcept as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties; but parties to actions or proceedings are entitled to their costs, as hereinafter provided.”

Here, there are no facts pleaded showing that Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees under any contract, statute nor law.

Accordingly, Stanley’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s requests for attorneys’ fees is granted without leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Stanley’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages is granted with 20 days leave to amend.

Stanley’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees is granted without leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

E.    Dental Lab’s Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s Lead SAC

Meet and Confer

Dental Lab’s counsel declares on October 4, 2023, he telephoned the number on Plaintiff Ali Tamimi’s SAC (424) 465-4100), and a man answered his call and told me I have the wrong number and that no one named Ali Tamimi was at that number.  (Decl. of Warden ¶5.)  Dental Lab’s counsel declares on October 4, 2023, he emailed Plaintiff and informed him of the meet-and-confer requirement under the Code and invited him to further amend his complaint striking the punitive or “exemplary” damages prayer.  (Decl. of Warden ¶6, Exh. B.)  Dental Lab’s counsel declares on October 5, 2023, he sent Plaintiff a copy of this motion via email and again requested they discuss the merits of the motion telephonically.  (Decl. of Warden ¶7.)  Dental Lab’s counsel declares on October 5, 2023, he received an email from Plaintiff informing him that Plaintiff’s “pleadings are sufficient to sustain a claim for punitive damages against Defendants including Defendant Associated Dental Laboratory.”  (Decl. of Warden ¶8, Exh. C.)  Dental Lab’s counsel declares that upon receiving Plaintiff’s email, he again telephoned Plaintiff at (424) 465-4100) to discuss the matter and no one answered his call.  (Decl. of Warden ¶9.)  Dental Lab’s counsel’s declaration is sufficient to demonstrate a good faith effort to meet and confer with Plaintiff.  Therefore, the Court will consider Dental Lab’s motion.

 

Punitive Damages

A plaintiff must demonstrate the defendant is guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice to obtain punitive damages in an action for the breach of an obligation not rising from contract.  (Civ. Code §3294(a).)  Plaintiff has the burden of proving he is entitled to punitive damages by “clear and convincing evidence.”  (Id.)  Since California law disfavors awarding punitive damages, such claims are to be granted “with the greatest of caution and are only allowed in the clearest of cases.”  (Henderson v. Security National Bank (1977) 72 Cal.App.3d 764, 771.)  “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.”  (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, citations omitted.)

Civil Code §3294(c)(3) defines fraud as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”  Malice is defined as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (Civ. Code §3294(c)(1).)  Oppression is defined as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.”  (Civ. Code §3294(c)(2).)

Plaintiff fails to allege that Dental Lab made any “intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact.”  Further, Plaintiff fails to allege Dental Lab intended to cause Plaintiff injury or that Dental Lab engaged in despicable conduct.

Accordingly, Dental Lab’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages against Dental Lab is granted with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Dental Lab’s motion to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages against Dental Lab is granted with 20 days leave to amend.
