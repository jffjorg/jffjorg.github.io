Case Number: 21VECV00054    Hearing Date: March 26, 2024    Dept: W

MIKE DAVIDYAN v. MOHAMMAD MOAYERY, et al.

 

motion for attorney fees

 

Date of Hearing:        March 26, 2024                                  Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                         Case No.:        21VECV00054

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Mohammad Moayery

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a habitability action. Plaintiff alleges he was a resident and tenant of a certain property located in Sherman Oaks and defendants Mohammad Moayery and Parvaneh Moshir Afshar are the owners. Plaintiff alleges the habitability violations include mold. Plaintiff contends as a result of the toxic mold, plaintiff became ill and incurred substantial medical damages.

 

On May 3, 2021, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint asserting causes of action for negligence and intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On August 3, 2021, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend. Plaintiff appealed. The Court of Appeal affirmed the decision. Defendant now seeks attorney fees on appeal.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Mohammad Moayery’s Motion for Attorney Fees is GRANTED.

 

discussion

 

Defendant Mohammad Moayery moves for an order awarding him attorney fees on appeal against Plaintiff Mike Davidyan. Defendant makes the motion on the grounds Defendant prevailed on appeal.

 

“The right to recover costs on appeal is governed by California Rules of Court, rule 8.278 (rule 8.278).”  (Musaelian v. Adams¿(2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1251, 1259.)   “Rule 8.278(a) provides in part: ‘(1) Except as provided in this rule, the party prevailing in the Court of Appeal in a civil case other than a juvenile case is entitled to costs on appeal. [¶] ... [¶] (5) In the interests of justice, the Court of Appeal may also award or deny costs as it deems proper.’ The right to recover costs depends on four conditions: ‘(1) There must be a valid judgment awarding costs to the party claiming them; (2) the item must be one allowed by rule or statute; (3) the amount claimed must have been actually incurred; (4) the amount claimed must be reasonable.’”  (Ibid.)  Additionally, “[u]nless the court orders otherwise, an award of costs neither includes attorney's fees on appeal nor precludes a party from seeking them under rule 3.1702.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, 8.278(d)(2).) 

 

Defendant seeks $7,065.00 in costs for having to defend both the underlying judgment and the award of attorneys’ fees. First, the court notes Defendant prevailed on appeal and was entitled to recover costs. (See Remittitur 01/02/2024.) Next, utilizing a lodestar approach and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the court agrees $7,065.00 is reasonable amount of attorney’s fees incurred on appeal.

 

The motion is unopposed. Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s Motion for Attorney Fees is GRANTED.

BANI SHAHBAZ v. FORD MOTOR COMPANY, et al.

 

Demurrer to plaintiffs’ first complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        March 26, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV03921

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action which arises from the purchase of a 2022 Ford F150. On September 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Ford Motor Company and Sunnyvale Ford for Violation of Subdivision (d) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; Violation of Subdivision (b) of Civil Code Section 1793.2; Violation of Subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code Section; Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment; and Negligent Repair. On December 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint.

 

Plaintiff alleges their vehicle had a transmissions defect, which Defendant Ford knew about and concealed.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND and OVERRULED as to the Fifth Cause of Action.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Ford Motor Company demurs to the first amended complaint on the grounds the third cause of action for Violation of Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3) and the fifth cause of action Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment remain defective and fail to meet the requisite pleading requirements, despite Plaintiff having been afforded the opportunity to cure the defects identified in Ford’s first demurrer.

 

Third Cause of Action

 

Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3) requires every manufacturer of consumer good sold in this state and for which the manufacturer has made an express warranty to make available to authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period.  

 

Defendant Ford argues despite this court’s prior ruling sustaining Ford’s previous demurrer to this cause of action, Plaintiff’s FAC contains no new allegations to support this claim. (See FAC at ¶¶ 54-56.) Defendant contends, instead, the FAC once again states in a conclusory fashion that Ford failed to make available service literature or replacement parts during the express warranty period and nothing more. Moreover, there are no facts whatsoever to give Ford any indication of what portion of the Civil Code section specifically was violated or how it was violated nor are there facts alleged regarding damages.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues regarding literature or parts that Ford withheld from its authorized repair facilities, that would be a matter for discovery. (See Committee on Children's Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216-217, [when it appears from the nature of the allegations that defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy, even under strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons is that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party], superseded by statute on other grounds.) Moreover, the Legislature used the word "effect" rather than "attempt" in drafting the statute. Had the Legislature intended attempts at repair to satisfy the statute, it could have used the word "attempt," as it did elsewhere in the same Civil Code section. As a result, the pleadings sufficiently put Defendant on notice as to the claim and the general type of literature and/or replacement parts that were insufficient or not provided.

 

The court finds the allegations are still insufficient to support a claim under Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3). The new allegations include “Even after performance of repairs based upon Defendant’s service literature and parts, Defendant’s authorized repair facility still discovered diagnostic trouble code SSM 51310, and contacted Defendant for further instruction.” (FAC ¶26.) However, this does not support a contention that Ford failed to make available sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs. By Plaintiff’s own contentions, Ford did make available service literature; however, the literature and/or replacement parts were insufficient to resolve the issue. The court notes Plaintiff’s allegation “and contacted Defendant for further instruction.” However, it is not clear from the allegations that Ford then failed to provide any further instruction. Moreover, Plaintiff has not provided any law that Ford can violate Civil Code section 1793.2(a)(3) by failing to develop service literature or replacement parts to permanently fix a car.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fifth Cause of Action

 

The elements of a claim for fraudulent concealment are: “(1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose that fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damages as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.” (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc.¿(2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.) As a species of fraud and deceit, such claims must be plead with specificity. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645 (specificity means pleading the who, where, when, what, and how).) But this rule for fraud by concealment, unlike fraud by an affirmative misrepresentation, must be applied differently in the case of non-disclosure because it is difficult to allege “who” or “how” or “by what means” something was not disclosed or “when” or “where” a statement was not made. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Imp. System & Planning Ass’n, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  Because of the nature of such fraud and the fact such facts supporting concealment are more likely to be known by defendant, less particularity is required.  (Id.)

 

Defendant Ford argues Plaintiff has not pled facts supporting his fraudulent concealment claim with the requisite specificity. Specifically, the FAC does not state any facts to support a finding that Plaintiff relied on any communications with Ford about the transmission. Whether or not plaintiff interacted with the sales representative is immaterial as Plaintiff does not allege Ford sold him the vehicle or agency. Moreover, the FAC remains vague and does not explain how the alleged silence by Ford amounts to an affirmative act or representation about the transmission or quality of the Subject Vehicle. Ford argues to the contrary, Plaintiff’s claim fails because it includes an additional publicly available Technical Service Bulletin on top of the previously alleged Technical Service Bulletins regarding the purportedly defective 10R80 transmission. Lastly, Plaintiff’s complaint fails to identify any individual authorized to speak on Ford’s behalf during the sale.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the elements of a fraudulent inducement by concealment claim under Dhital. In particular, Plaintiff alleged he entered into a warranty contract with Ford around January 22, 2023 (FAC ¶¶ 9-10); Plaintiff identified the material facts Ford knew prior to his acquisition of the Subject Vehicle that Ford withheld from Plaintiff (FAC ¶29); Plaintiff alleged that Defendant had superior knowledge of the facts (FAC ¶¶ 30, 68, 71 a-c ); the safety risks posed by the Transmission Defect (FAC ¶¶ 29, 67); the materiality of that information (FAC ¶¶ 73-74); Plaintiffs reliance on the non-disclosure (FAC ¶¶ 73-74); and damages. (FAC ¶¶ 16, 23, 76-77.) Additionally, the TSBs issued by Ford to its repair facilities illustrate the depth of Ford’s knowledge regarding the Transmission Defect affecting its 10R80 transmission. And because TSBs contain limited information and do not show the full extent of FORD's knowledge, these documents do not defeat Ford’s exclusive knowledge. (In re MyFord Touch Consumer Litigation (N.D.Cal. 2014) 46 F.Supp.3d 936, 960.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff’s allegations sufficiently allege fraudulent concealment at this juncture. The FAC now alleges portions of Defendant’s marketing material for the vehicle in the FAC, identifying the material misrepresentations Defendant made prior to, and after, the sale of the vehicle to Plaintiff. (FAC ¶64.) This along with the remaining allegations are sufficient to show fraudulent concealment. The court notes that Plaintiff’s additional allegations including the TSBs does not defeat Plaintiff’s contention. In Falk v. General Motors Corp. (N.D. Cal. 2007) 496 F.Supp.2d 1088, the court noted that while prospective purchasers may conduct an internet search prior to purchasing a vehicle, the manufacturer is alleged to know about additional information. Again, at this juncture and for pleading purposes, it is concluded that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged Ford’s exclusive knowledge of the alleged defect.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action is OVERRULED.
