I.                    Background

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiff filed a FAC.

 

On November 13, 2023, Mohebbi filed an Answer to the FAC.

 

On November 13, 2023, Mohebbi filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, Diane Todd (“Todd”), Deluxe, and Roes 1-25.

 

On December 28, 2023, Mohebbi filed proof of service of the Cross-Complaint on Matthew Todd on behalf of Gregory Todd and Todd via electronic service.

 

Default was entered against Todd on June 17, 2024.

 

On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff and Todd filed an Answer to the Cross-Complaint.

 

On November 20, 2024, Todd filed the instant “Notice of Motion and Motion to Vacate Default and Default Judgment” as to Todd (the “Motion”). On December 26, 2024, Mohebbi filed an Opposition. On December 30, 2024, Todd filed a Reply.

 

On December 27, 2024, Mohebbi filed a Statement of the Status of Settlement Negotiations.

 

On December 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Proposed Joint Status Conference Statement per Court Order of 10/31/24.

 

On January 6, 2024, the Court held a status conference and granted Plaintiff’s oral motion to continue the hearing date on the Motion due to scheduling conflict. (1/6/25 Order.) Accordingly, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion from January 8, 2025 to January 15, 2025. The Court also continued the hearing on the Order to Show Cause Re: Status of Default on the Complaint as to Defendant Deluxe Escrow from January 6, 2025, to April 7, 2025. (1/6/25 Order.)

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

Before addressing the merits of the Motion, the Court notes Todd gave improper notice of this hearing. Specifically, the Notice of Motion incorrectly states that the Motion would be heard on December 12, 2024, rather than January 8, 2025.

 

However, given Mohebbi’s Opposition which includes the correct original hearing date, the Court exercises its discretion to hear the Motion on the merits.

 

Additionally, the Court notes Mohebbi’s Opposition is untimely and does not contain proof of service. However, given Todd’s timely Reply, the Court exercises its discretion to consider the Opposition’s arguments on the merits.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief. (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure § 473, subdivision (b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(b).) 

 

The general underlying purpose of Section 473, subdivision (b) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits.  (Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838-839.)  

 

IV.              Discussion

 

A.    Moving Party Position

 

Todd moves to set aside the entry of default and default judgment on the grounds of mandatory and discretionary attorney mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. Specifically, Todd contends Todd’s attorney, Matthew P. Todd, (“Counsel”) failed to timely respond to Mohebbi’s Cross-Complaint due to the deaths of three members of Counsel’s immediate family and the “chaos” and “absence from the office” that proceeded as a result. (Motion, Todd Decl., ¶ 18.) While Counsel had met and conferred extensively with Mohebbi prior to Todd being served with the Cross-Complaint on the grounds Todd is not a party to this action and then Counsel drafted a Demurrer in response to the Cross-Complaint once the Cross-Complaint was filed, Counsel’s family tragedies waylaid Counsel’s earlier efforts. (Motion, Todd Decl., ¶ 18.) In navigating his family deaths, Counsel failed to file the Demurrer on behalf of Todd – a fact he did not discover until after the entry of default – which thus caused Todd’s lack of response to the Cross-Complaint. (Motion, Todd Decl., ¶ 18.) Todd requests the Court grant the Motion and vacate the default entered against Todd and also requests attorney’s fees. (Motion, p. 10.)

 

B.     Opposition

 

In Opposition, Mohebbi states he and his counsel “sympathize with the unfortunate circumstances of opposing counsel” but state they sadly to not “overshadow the overwhelming and extraordinary efforts, counsel for Mohebbi made to beg, plead and prof Matthew Todd into accepting service, answering the Complaint, and preventing a default.” (Opp., ¶ 1, emphasis in original.) Mohebbi’s counsel tried three separate times in writing to contact Todd “to offer to stipulate to the set aside of the default if the hearing could be taken off calendar and if the cost of drafting and filing a response could be avoided” to no response. (Opp., ¶ 2, emphasis in original.) Mohebbi then states Todd is a mandatory necessary party to this lawsuit, and “[a]ll the fluff regarding her distance and health conditions are nothing more than a ploy to distract the Court with irrelevant information.” (Opp., ¶ 4.) Finally, Mohebbi argues Counsel’s failure to timely respond to the Cross-Complaint is part of a “continued and deliberate strategy to drag out this case and cause as much expenditure of fees as possible.” (Opp., ¶ 4.) Mohebbi also states it is mandatory for Counsel to pay the reasonably attorneys’ fees and costs of Mohebbi and his attorney and further requests the Court impose an additional monetary penalty against Todd. (Opp., ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

C.     Reply

 

In Reply, Todd argues the Opposition is not supported by case law; the Opposition is based on inconsistent, unsupported, and irrelevant evidence, and the stance of “ ‘go ahead and file the demurrer’ approach taken by Mohebbi in meet and confer discussions prior to the entry of default should “not be rewarded by denying the instant motion.” (Reply, pp. 2-10.) Additionally, Todd argues attorney fees should not be granted in favor of Mohebbi and highlights that Mohebbi’s Opposition is not indicative of “opposing any contention of the Moving Papers in a meaningful manner.” (Reply, pp. 10-11.)

 

D.    Analysis

 

Timeliness

 

As an initial matter, Todd’s Motion was filed on November 20, 2024, five months after default was entered against her on June 17, 2024.

 

Here, Todd raises a claim for relief due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect by an attorney. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)  Counsel submitted a declaration in support of the Motion admitting neglect by counsel in failing to timely act upon Mohebbi’s Cross-Complaint. (Motion, Todd Decl., ¶ 18.)

 

Since Counsel filed the Motion less than six months from the entry of default, the Motion is timely as it pertains to a request for either discretionary or mandatory relief from default. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Mandatory Relief from Default

 

            As an initial matter, Todd moves to set aside the default entered against her and default judgment. However, the Court notes that, to date, the Court’s docket reflects that only default has been entered against Todd. Accordingly, the Court will only address whether relief from default is warranted.

 

            Here, the Court finds there is sufficient evidence to warrant setting aside the default entered against Todd. Counsel’s declaration established the default was entered due to Counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, error, or neglect. (Motion, Todd Decl.) Specifically, Counsel declares he failed to timely respond to Mohebbi’s Cross-Complaint due to the deaths of three members of Counsel’s immediate family and the “chaos” and “absence from the office” that ensued as a result. (Motion, Todd Decl., ¶ 18.) Despite diligently meeting and conferring with Mohebbi regarding the potential of a Cross-Complaint and taking efforts to timely respond to the Cross-Complaint upon its filing, Counsel’s family tragedies waylaid Counsel’s efforts, and Counsel failed to file the Demurrer on behalf of Todd – a fact he did not discover until after the entry of default. (Motion, Todd Decl., ¶ 18.)

 

The Court finds Todd has satisfied her burden to demonstrate inadvertence or neglect by her Counsel, and Mohebbi presents no reason for the Court to find otherwise.  

 

Accordingly, the Court finds the entry of default was caused by Counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. And, having considered the moving papers and evidence, the Court finds this Motion to set aside default was timely, and the requisite responsive pleading (Answer) was filed. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

Thus, Todd’s Motion is GRANTED.

 

            Fees and Costs

 

“The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney's affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

“Whenever the court grants relief from a default, default judgment, or dismissal based on any of the provisions of this section, the court may do any of the following:

 

(A) Impose a penalty of no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) upon an offending attorney or party.

(B) Direct that an offending attorney pay an amount no greater than one thousand dollars ($1,000) to the State Bar Client Security Fund.

(C) Grant other relief as is appropriate.”

 

(Code of Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (c).)

 

Mohebbi’s Opposition seeks an award of attorney’s fees in the amount of $4,899.00, reflecting six hours billed at $525.00 per hour for work on the Opposition, review of the Reply, and travel to and attendance at the instant hearing. (Sandarian Decl., ¶ 13.) Mohebbi’s Opposition further requests $24.75 for service fees to file the Opposition, as well as $1,575.00 for time investigating and locating Todd out of state and corresponding with multiple process servers to effect personal service of Todd. (Sandarian Decl., ¶ 13.) Finally, Mohebbi’s Opposition seeks $150.00 in an otherwise unnecessary service of process server fee. (Sandarian Decl., ¶ 13, Exh. 1-7.)

 

Mohebbi is awarded the reasonable amount of $1,500 in attorney’s fees and $24.75 in costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

            Given the circumstances of the instant default and Motion, the Court declines to impose an additional monetary penalty pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (c).

Cross-Defendant Diane Todd’s Motion to Vacate Default and Default Judgment is GRANTED. The default entered on June 17, 2024 is hereby VACATED.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On February 3, 2023, Plaintiff Aspire General Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Jose Cruz (“Defendant”) alleging a single cause of action for Automobile Subrogation. (Compl., pp. 1, 3.)

 

Defendant, proceeding pro per, filed an Answer on March 29, 2023.

 

On December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Deem Admitted the First Set of Request for Admissions (“RFAs”) Served on Defendant Jose Cruz and Request for Sanctions (the “RFAs Motion”), and the instant Motion to Compel Answers to Form Interrogatories Served on Carl Nunn and Request for Sanctions (the “ROGs Motion”) (collectively, the “Motions”).

 

No Oppositions were filed.

 

On January 9, 2025, the Court continued the hearing on the RFAs Motion, on the Court’s own motion, from January 9, 2025 to January 15, 2025, due to the lack of appearance or communication by or for any Party on that date. (1/9/25 Order.)

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

The Court notes a discrepancy in the ROGs Motion – namely, the ROGs Motion’s title includes reference to a “Carl Nunn,” who is neither the Defendant nor another identified party to this action.

 

Given that the remainder of the ROGs Motion, the Proposed Order, and all accompanying documents refer to Defendant, the Court exercises its discretion to find the title of the ROGs Motion to be a typographical error and addresses the ROGs Motion on the merits as to Defendant.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020, subd. (a), 2030.290.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

A party must respond to requests for admissions within 30 days after service of such requests. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).)  “If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response…(a) [that party] waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 94 limits discovery in limited jurisdiction actions to any combination of 35 interrogatories with no subparts, demands to produce documents, and requests for admission with no subparts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 94, subd. (a).)

 

IV.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff contends that Defendant failed to timely respond to the Form Interrogatories and Request for Admissions, on or by the statutory deadline.

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Plaintiff propounded 13 Requests for Admission and 20 Form Interrogatories on Defendant, totaling 33 requests. Accordingly, the Court finds the discovery requests served on Defendant are in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 94.

 

Here, Plaintiff’s counsel’s office served Defendant by mail on August 13, 2024 with the RFAs and Form Interrogatories. (RFAs Motion, Tapper Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A; ROGs Motion, Tapper Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) By September 18, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel had received no response; on September 20, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a letter to Defendant requesting responses to the RFAs and Form Interrogatories and offering to grant Defendant an extension to avoid bringing a motion. (RFAs Motion, Tapper Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. B; ROGs Motion, Tapper Decl., ¶¶ 3-4, Exh. B.)

 

As of the date these Motions were filed, December 6, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel had not received any responses. (Motions, Tapper Decl., ¶ 5.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming RFAs admitted and compelling Defendant to provide responses without objections to the Form Interrogatories.

 

A.    Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the person, or the attorney whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted and the motion to compel responses to interrogatories. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].)

“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought. The notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040.)

 

Here, Defendant failed to serve timely responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs and Form Interrogatories by the statutory deadline. Thus, sanctions are mandatory against Defendant. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a).)

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s Notice of Motion for each motion complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.040.

 

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions of $2,330.00, based on $1,165.00 for each motion, reflecting 2 hours incurred on bringing each Motion and 3 hours incurred traveling to and attending the Motion hearing for each Motion at an hourly rate of $225.00 per hour, plus the $40.00 filing fee for each Motion. (Motions, Tapper Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Given the similarity of the motions to each other, the shared hearing date, the lack of Oppositions and Replies, and the straightforward nature of the Motions, Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant is GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $867.50, reflecting 3.5 hours incurred at $225.00 per hour plus $80.00 in filing fees.

Plaintiff Aspire General Insurance Company’s unopposed Motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted and unopposed Motion to Compel Answers to Form Interrogatories are GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff Aspire General Insurance Company’s unopposed requests for monetary sanctions against Defendant are GRANTED in the reduced total amount of $867.50 payable to Plaintiff within thirty (30) days of this Court’s Order.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

            On September 5, 2019, minor Plaintiff Khilyn Williams (“minor Plaintiff”) and her mother were involved in a motor vehicle accident with Defendants Jose Rudy Amaya and Plumbing and Industrial Supply Company. (Compl., pp. 1, 4, 5.)

 

            On October 24, 2023, Petitioner Erika Wright (“Petitioner”) and minor Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants and Does 1-100 alleging motor vehicle and general negligence causes of action.

 

            On November 2, 2023, Petitioner, who is minor Plaintiff’s mother, was appointed as minor Plaintiff’s guardian ad litem.

 

            On March 6, 2024, Petitioner filed an Expedited Petition for Approval of Compromise of a Disputed Claim of Minor Claimant, Khilyn Williams (the “Expedited Petition”).

 

            On March 15, 2024, the Court entered an order setting a hearing on the Expedited Petition for April 25, 2024. (03/15/24 Order, at p. 3.) The Court noted that “CRC 7.950.5(a)(8) provides that for a petition to be eligible for expedited approval, the total amount payable to the minor ‘and all other parties under the proposed compromise or settlement’ must be $50,000 or less.” (03/15/24 Order, at p. 3.) The Court noted that “the Petition indicates that the defendant has offered payment of $250,000 to Petitioner to settle claims arising out of the same incident or accident that resulted in minor’s injury.” (03/15/24 Order, at pp. 3-4.) As such, the Court set a hearing and ordered counsel for minor Plaintiff and Petitioner to be present so that the Court could “obtain further details regarding the apportionment of the total settlement amount between Petitioner and the minor.” (03/15/24 Order, at p. 4.) The Court ordered Petitioner to file supplemental papers addressing the deficiencies articulated in the Court’s March 15, 2024 order at least 16 court days before the scheduled hearing. (03/15/24 Order.)

 

            On April 3, 2024, counsel for Minor Plaintiff filed a declaration stating that counsel overlooked that an expedited petition could only be used if all settlements including those to the claimant and all other parties needed to be $50,000 or less and instead understood that only the settlement of the claimant needed to be $50,000 or less. (Perlstein Decl., ¶ 3.) Counsel apologized for the oversight. (Perlstein Decl., ¶ 3.) Counsel further stated that Petitioner’s case was settled and funds were fully disbursed, including attorney fees to claimant’s counsel, in June 2023 prior to the lawsuit being filed minor Plaintiff. (Perlstein Decl., ¶ 5.) There was no apportionment of settlement between Petitioner and minor Plaintiff. (Perlstein Decl., ¶ 6.) The settlements were calculated based on each party’s injuries and medical specials. (Perlstein Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

            On April 25, 2024, the Court denied the Expedited Petition without prejudice, finding counsel mistakenly filed a petition for expedited approval (see Perlstein Decl.), and the Expedited Petition contained deficiencies. (4/25/24 Order, p. 5.) For instance, “Counsel [did] not clarif[y] why the Petition states that Petitioner paid counsel $100,000.00 in connection with the claims giving rise to the Petition. (03/15/24 Minute Order, at p. 4; see Petition at ¶ 18(b).)” (04/25/24 Order, p. 5.)

III.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

            Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim. (Prob. Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; Code of Civ. Proc., § 372.) “ ‘[W]ithout trial court approval of the proposed compromise of the ward’s claim, the settlement cannot be valid.  [Citation.] [¶] Nor is the settlement binding [on the minor] until it is endorsed by the trial court.’ ” (Pearson v. Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1338.) A minor, like Claimant, “shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(1).) Alternatively, the petitioner may file a declaration demonstrating that he or she has a right to compromise the minor’s claim under California Probate Code section 3500.

 

            Regarding the substance of the Petition, to obtain court approval of the settlement of a minor’s claims, the petitioner must file a complete and “verified petition for approval of the settlement and must disclose ‘all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise.’ ” (Barnes v. Western Heritage Ins. Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 249, 256, italics added; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.) 

 

            The Court finds the following deficiencies with the instant Petition:

 

¶ 3: Petitioner indicates the current proceeding is a pending action at Stanley Mosk Courthouse. The instant action is being heard at Spring Street Courthouse.

 

¶ 10(c): Petitioner states the balance of the proceeds will “not be made available until Ivy Wang attains the age of 18 years old.” The Petition does not state who “Ivy Wang” is or her connection to minor Plaintiff and/or Petitioner. Further, the Proposed Order and Order to Deposit Funds in Blocked Account state the balance of the proceeds in the blocked account belong to minor Plaintiff, and withdrawal of the proceeds may only occur when minor Plaintiff reaches 18 years of age. (Proposed Order, ¶ 9(c)(2); Order to Deposit Funds, ¶ 5(b).) This appears erroneous and the Moving Party is ordered to file a corrected Petition prior to the next hearing.

 

As to the Proposed Order, ¶ 8(a)(5) does not have a marked box even though a value is listed.

 

No proof of service was filed demonstrating Defendant was served with this Petition, Proposed Order, Order to Deposit Funds in Blocked Account, or notice of hearing.

 

The Petition is CONTINUED due to the deficiencies listed.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On February 15, 2024, Plaintiff Dianne Smith (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Bertha Peraza aka Bertha Ramirez (“Ramirez”), Eileen Peraza (“Peraza”), and Andrea Ramos (“Ramos”) (collectively “Defendants”) and Does 1-10, alleging a single cause of action for Fraudulent Transfer of Real Property by Judgment Debtor. (Compl., p. 1.)

 

On February 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On March 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed proof of personal service of the FAC and Notice of Case Assignment on Ramirez on March 5, 2024 and on Peraza and Ramos on March 6, 2024.

 

On April 16, 2024, default was entered against Defendants. 

 

On July 18, 2024, a default judgment of $23,680.99 was entered against Defendants, reflecting damages, prejudgment interest at the annual rate of 10 percent, and costs. (7/18/24 Default Judgment.)

 

On December 3, 2024, Peraza and Ramos, in pro per, filed the instant “Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment and Stay Collection Proceedings” (the “Motion”) and Request for Judicial Notice.

 

On December 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition.

 

No Reply has been filed.

 

On January 9, 2025, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion from January 9, 2025 to January 15, 2025 due to the unavailability of a Spanish language interpreter to assist Ramos. (1/9/25 Order.)

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Peraza and Ramos gave Plaintiff improper notice of this hearing by failing to include the Court’s address in the Notice of Hearing.

 

Further, the proof of service for the Motion is defective. The proof of service filed on December 16, 2024 states service was completed on Plaintiff by a person “over 18 years of age and not a party to this action.” (12/16/24 Proof of Service, ¶ 1.) However, Ramos, a party to the action, declared to effecting service. (12/16/24 Proof of Service, p. 2.)

 

While service was improper, the Court exercises its discretion to hear the Motion on the merits given Plaintiff’s timely-filed Opposition and the appropriate Court address listed in the reservation receipt attached to the Motion.

 

III.              Request for Judicial Notice

 

In conjunction with the Motion, Peraza and Ramos request judicial notice of the following files: (1) the court order in Case No. 03K02991 confirming Gustavo Peraza did not hold personal ownership of the subject property; (2) correspondence dated “8/14/25” misleading Peraza and Ramos to believe they had until the 2025 to respond to the claims; (3) service documentation; and (4) a bank statement showing the removal of $1,700 from Peraza’s account following the Writ of Execution on August 12, 2024. (Judicial Notice Request, Exhs. A-D.)

 

After reviewing the moving papers, the Court GRANTS Peraza and Ramos’ request as to the Letter of Administration in Case No. 03K02991 and the 2020 Third Amended Standing Order (Judicial Notice Request, Exhs. A, B). The remainder of the request is DENIED.

 

IV.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.    Moving Party Position

 

Peraza and Ramos contend the default judgment is void pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) due to “a fundamental misunderstanding of property ownership.” (Motion, p. 6.) Namely, the property in dispute was “part of an estate being administered by Gustavo Peraza, not owned by him or the Defendants.” (Motion, p. 6.) Further, Peraza and Ramos challenge the 2013 service of process of Gustavo Peraza in Case No. 03K02991, which is case from which the instant dispute arose. (Motion, p. 5.) Peraza and Ramos contend excusable neglect also warrants relief – specifically, Peraza and Ramos’s inability to respond was due to “misinformation about deadlines and the significant challenges posed by Defendant Bertha Peraza’s terminal illness and subsequent death.” (Motion, p. 6.) Finally, Peraza and Ramos contends the default judgment violates due process. (Motion, p. 6.)

 

Additionally, Peraza and Ramos request that “all collection proceedings, including enforcement of the Writ of Execution, be stayed pending resolution of this motion” to prevent irreparable harm to Peraza, “whose funds have already been wrongfully removed, and to ensure fairness in the proceedings.” (Motion, p. 7, citing Code Civ. Proc., § 918.)

 

 

B.     Opposition

 

In Opposition, Plaintiff argues service of the FAC was done in conformance with code requirements. (Opp., p. 4.) Indeed, Peraza and Ramos were personally served in March 2024. (Opp., p. 4.) Plaintiff further states the “Affidavit of Non-Receipt of Documents” presented by Peraza and Ramos is meaningless as it does pertain to the service effected in the instant matter. (Opp., pp. 4-5.)

 

Turning to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), Plaintiff contends the Motion is time-barred under the statute because Peraza and Ramos seek relief from default judgment more than six months after default was entered against them. (Opp., p. 5.) Even if the Motion was not time-barred, Plaintiff asserts Peraza and Ramos fail to demonstrate excusable neglect on the grounds they misunderstood case deadlines because a “reasonably prudent person would seek legal assistance when served with a summons and complaint and there are numerous free legal aid services available for such assistance.” (Opp., p. 6.)

 

Regarding Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d), Plaintiff argues the Motion’s argument under this code section fail because “there is nothing in the record or on the face of the default judgment in this case that would render it void on its face, nor do the defendants argue it is void on its face.” (Opp., p. 6.) To the extent Peraza and Ramos challenge service on Gustavo Peraza in 2013, Plaintiff argues this is irrelevant as 2013 is merely when the judgment entered in 2003 was renewed. (Opp., p. 7.)

 

Finally, Plaintiff makes two requests of the Court in the event the Motion is granted: (1) the Court should require a bond from Peraza and Ramos, and (2) the Court should award $1,000 to Plaintiff for attorney fees incurred in preparing and submitting the default package and appearing and opposing the Motion. (Opp., pp. 7-9.)

 

Glenn A. Besnyl, Attorney for National Commercial Recovery, Inc., insurer for Plaintiff, refers to a Declaration in the Opposition; however, no declaration is attached substantiating an award of $1,000.

 

C.     Analysis

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d)

 

“The court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (d), emphasis added.)¿

 

An order is considered “void on its face only when the invalidity is apparent from an inspection of the judgment roll or court record without consideration of extrinsic evidence.” (Pittman v. Beck Park Apartments Ltd. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1009, 1021.)¿ “There is no time limit to attack a judgment void on its face.” (Ibid.) However, if “the invalidity is not apparent [by an inspection of the judgment or the judgment roll], it may be set aside by motion, if such motion is filed within a reasonable time, i. e., not longer than the time set forth in Code of Civil Procedure, section 473.” (City of Los Angeles v. Morgan (1951) 105 Cal.App.2d 726, 730.) Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d) contains no express time limit, and the California Supreme Court recently abrogated the rule that Section 473, subdivision (d) is subject to the two-year limit set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5. (California Capital Ins. Co. v. Hoehn (2024) 17 Cal.5th 207, 225-226 [“We hold that a section 473(d) motion to vacate a judgment that is void for lack of proper service is not subject to the judicially imposed two-year limitation”].)  

 

In the context of a default judgment, Code of Civil Procedure section 670 subdivision (a) defines the “judgment roll” as the following papers, without being attached together: “[T]he summons, with the affidavit or proof of service;¿the complaint;¿the request for entry of default with a memorandum indorsed thereon that the default of the defendant in not answering was entered, and a copy of the judgment.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 670, subd. (a).)

 

Here, the Court finds the Motion timely. However, the default judgment is not void on its face after a review of the judgment roll, and the Motion does not present any intelligible challenge to service of the summons and complaint or the face of the default judgment in this matter. The Motion’s sole arguments as to voidness of the judgment appear to be that the 2013 service of process on Gustavo Peraza was improper in Case No. 03K02991 and that Plaintiff’s claims rest on a “fundamental misunderstanding of property ownership.” (Motion, pp. 5, 6.)

 

The Court finds these contentions are either irrelevant to the instant proceedings or are challenges to subject matter jurisdiction that raise only general legal principles or defenses, and thus fail to allege grounds for vacating a default judgment.

 

Accordingly, Peraza and Ramos are not entitled to relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (d).

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b)

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief. (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure § 473, subdivision (b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

The Motion states that Peraza and Ramos mistakenly thought they did not need to take action on the instant matter until August 2025 due to a misinterpretation of a 2020 Third Amended Standing Order they received. (Motion, p. 6, Exh. B.) The Motion further states the Defendants believe they have a meritorious case because Peraza and Ramos neither hold any ownership interest of the property in dispute nor participated in the alleged fraudulent transfer. (Motion, p. 5.)

 

The general underlying purpose of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits.  (Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838-839.) 

 

Here, Peraza and Ramos seek relief under the discretionary provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) for mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect by a party. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Peraza and Ramos seek relief less than six months after entry of default judgment. Default judgment was entered on July 18, 2024, and the instant Motion was filed on December 3, 2024.

 

However, Peraza and Ramos failed to attach a copy of a proposed pleading.

 

Accordingly, Peraza and Ramos are entitled to relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). However, the Court cannot grant relief until Defendants have file a proposed responsive pleading.

 

Thus, the Court CONTINUES the matter to allow Peraza and Ramos an opportunity to file a proposed responsive pleading prior to the next hearing date.

By FEBRUARY 21, 2025, Defendants are to file and serve a proposed responsive pleading to Plaintiff’s Complaint.  Failure to do so may result in the Motion being denied or taken off calendar.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes the Motion is untimely. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005.) Further, in the Notice of Motion, Plaintiff provides two different hearing dates for the instant hearing on the Motion.

 

However, given the filing of the Opposition and the Opposition’s correct date and time listed in the caption, the Court exercises its discretion to hear the Motion on the merits.

III.              Legal Standard

 

Discovery Requests

 

A party must respond to requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., §2031.300, subd. (a).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 94 limits discovery in limited jurisdiction actions to any combination of 35 interrogatories with no subparts, demands to produce documents, and requests for admission with no subparts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 94, subd. (a).)

 

            Discovery Responses

 

Parties responding to requests for production “shall respond separately to each item or category of item by any of the following: (1) [a] statement that the party will comply with the particular demand … [;] (2) [a] representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand … [; or] (3) [a]n objection to the particular demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210, subd. (a).)

 

Motions to Compel Further Responses

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a), provides that “[o]n receipt of a response to a demand for inspection … the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:  (1) [a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] (2) [a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] (3) [a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.” 

 

Further, a motion to compel must comply with each of the following: “(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand. (2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (3) In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b).)

 

            Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of service of the verified response, otherwise, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

 

            Finally, California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3)).

 

IV.              Discussion

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes that Plaintiff timely moved to compel less than 45 days from service of the responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

 

Also, the Motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).) Plaintiff’s counsel submitted a declaration stating that Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Trans Union’s counsel identifying deficiencies in the responses to Plaintiff’s requests for production. (Motion, Robenzadeh Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court notes that while Plaintiff’s counsel also states a copy of this letter is attached as “Exhibit A” to the declaration, no such document is provided. However, Trans Union states it responded to Plaintiff’s meet and confer, provided additional explanations regarding its responses to the requests for production, and provided availability to “thoroughly discuss Plaintiff’s other issues raised in the December 12, 2024 meet and confer letter and requested Plaintiff’s counsel to propose alternative dates and times if Plaintiff’s counsel was not available at the suggested time.” (Motion, pp. 2-3, Exh. D.) Plaintiff did not set a time with Trans Union to further meet and confer. Additionally, Trans Union attaches a copy of the email meet and confer thread between Plaintiff and Trans Union. (Motion, Exh. D.) The Court finds this email exchange insufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. The parties failed to engage in a collaborative discussion of any kind.

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345 requires all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a).) Alternatively, “the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b).) The Motion is not accompanied by a separate statement, and the Court has not allowed Plaintiff to submit a concise outline of the dispute. Instead, the request, response, and purported basis for ordering a further response are included in the body of the Motion itself. Also, the Motion does not include a copy of the discovery requests or responses.

 

The parties are to meet and confer in good faith and submit a joint statement of the issues remaining regarding the documents produced by Trans Union in response to the Request for Production of Documents. All relevant exhibits are to be attached to the joint statement.
