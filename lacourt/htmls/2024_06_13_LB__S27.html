DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00043    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Leilani Lacusong and Anthony K. Turner filed 23LBCV02308 against Defendants, Milan V. Balejka, et al. on 12/05/23.  The case was ultimately consolidated with 22LBCV00432, resulting in this pleading challenge being filed in 22LBCV00432.  Lacusong and Turner’s complaint, in its caption, indicates it is for fraud – individually, fraud – concealment, breach of fiduciary duty, and breach of contract.  The body of the complaint, however, indicates it is for intentional infliction of emotional distress, constructive fraud, fraud – misrepresentation, and breach of contract. 

 

  1. Demurrers

a.     Procedural History

Defendants, Kiat Chan and Theresa Hertzog filed demurrers and motions to strike on 3/06/24.  Any opposition to the pleading challenges was due on or before 5/13/24.  Plaintiffs have not filed timely opposition to the pleading challenges, and the Court will not consider any late-filed opposition papers. 

 

b.     Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

c.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel, Ptere R. Haven, filed a declaration in support of the demurrer.  In his declaration, he indicates he attempted to meet and confer by sending a letter to Plaintiffs.  The Code requires meet and confer attempts in person or by phone.  Because Plaintiffs appear to have abandoned this litigation, the Court will rule on the pleading challenges on their merits. 

 

d.     Analysis

Defendants correctly note that the complaint is defective in numerous respects.  As indicated above, the causes of action in the caption do not match the causes of action in the body of the complaint.  Additionally, there are no meaningful factual allegations in the complaint.  Plaintiffs repeatedly state that they “incorporate by reference the allegations contained in paragraphs 1-8,” but there are no paragraph numbers in the complaint.  See, for example, page 3, lines 17-18, page 4, lines 3-4, and page 5, lines 9-10. 

 

Finally, and most importantly, the causes of action plead nothing more than mere conclusions.  By way of example, the first cause of action alleges Plaintiff was forced to endure great mental anguish, economic loss and emotional distress, but no facts detailing any of those allegations are included in the complaint.  Similarly, the second cause of action alleges the parties were in a fiduciary relationship and fraud was committed, but no factual allegations are included.  The third cause of action, at page 4, line 20, indicates Plaintiffs “incorporate by reference the Fraud.”  It is not clear what fraud is being incorporated by reference, as no fraud allegations appear anywhere in the complaint.  Finally, the fourth cause of action alleges various facts and the existence of a lease agreement, but does not allege the terms of the agreement or what term(s) of the agreement was breached.

Case Number: 23LBCV00427    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Michael Read filed this action against Defendant, General Motors, LLC for violations of the Song-Beverly Act, breach of implied warranty, and fraudulent inducement – concealment.  The operative complaint is the First Amended Complaint, which Plaintiff filed on 10/05/23.

 

  1. Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he “attempted to meet and confer telephonically with Plaintiff’s counsel…but…were unsuccessful in our attempts.”  The declaration is unclear concerning whether the meet and confer was unsuccessful because it never took place, or whether it took place but the attorneys were unable to agree.  The Court has noted this problem with Defense Counsel’s declaration previously in connection with other cases.  Defense Counsel MUST clarify this in future meet and confer declarations.

 

c.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant demurs to the fraud-based causes of action, contending they are barred by the statute of limitations, are not pled with the necessary specificity, and do not allege a transactional relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the complaint is timely, all essential elements are pled, and a transactional relationship is not required under California law to find a duty to disclose.    

 

d.     Statute of Limitations

CCP §338(d) provides for a three-year statute of limitations on a fraud claim.  Defendant argues ¶¶11, 20, and 21 of the FAC show the action is time-barred.  ¶11 of the FAC alleges the subject defects manifested themselves during the warranty period.  ¶20 does not appear to be relevant.  ¶21 alleges Plaintiff first brought her vehicle in for repairs, including electrical and brake concerns on 3/15/19, and that the authorized repair facility performed warranty repairs and represented that the vehicle had been repaired.  Notably, Plaintiff purchased the vehicle on 3/12/19, such that she began experiencing problems very quickly after purchase.

 

Plaintiff filed the complaint on 6/05/23, more than three years after it was purchased and first repaired.  Plaintiff argues the statute of limitations does not act as a bar because (a) Plaintiff had not discovered facts constituting fraud at that point, (b) the fraudulent inducement continued every time he brought the vehicle in for repairs and was told it had been repaired, (c) the delayed discovery rule applies, (d) Defendant’s ongoing concealment tolls the statute of limitations on the claim, and (e) the claim is tolled under the delayed repair doctrine. 

 

Defendant conflates a problem with a vehicle with knowledge of fraud.  The mere fact that a vehicle has been purchased and has a problem would not lead a reasonable person to suspect fraud.  Nothing on the complaint’s face leads to the conclusion that Plaintiff must have known, at least three years prior to filing suit, that Defendant had committed fraud.  Notably, pursuant to Union Carbide v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 15, 25, a demurrer on statute of limitations grounds will only lie if the defect is clearly disclosed on the face of the complaint.  Such is not the case here.

 

The demurrer on this ground is overruled.

 

  1. Specificity

Defendant’s second argument is that Plaintiff failed to plead fraud with specificity.  Ordinarily, fraud claims are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading which necessitate pleading facts showing “how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered." (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.)  However, the specificity rule is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations and not to concealment. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 89 Cal.Rptr.3d 659).  Notably, the idea that a plaintiff could allege who concealed something, where the thing was concealed, when it was concealed, etc., is nonsensical; the nature of the claim is a failure to speak, which cannot possibly include these details.

 

  1. Transactional Relationship

The elements of a fraud claim based on concealment are: “(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310–311). 

 

There are “four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.” (Id. at 311). If a fiduciary relationship does not exist, but the latter three circumstances are present, Plaintiff must still show “the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant from which a duty to disclose can arise.” (Id. at 311).

 

            A duty to disclose may arise as a result of a transaction between the parties. However, the transaction "must necessarily arise from direct dealings between the plaintiff and the defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large." (Bigler-Engler at 312 [noting that the duty of a manufacturer to warn consumers of a product’s hazards and faults applies in the context of strict products liability actions but does not apply in a suit for intentional misrepresentation.]).

 

            The Court is aware of the Court of Appeals’ decision in Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, wherein the plaintiffs sued Nissan North America, Inc. (Nissan), alleging a transmission defect.  The plaintiffs alleged claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and a common law fraud claim alleging that Nissan fraudulently concealed the defects and induced them to purchase a car. (Dhital at 838).

 

            The California Supreme Court granted review of Dhital on February 1, 2023, however, the request for depublication was denied. (Dhital v. Nissan North America (Cal. 2023) 304 Cal.Rptr.3d 82, 83). Since review is pending and the Supreme Court did not otherwise order, Dhital does not having binding effect, however, “it may be cited for potentially persuasive value only.” (Cal Rules of Court, Rule 8.1115 (e)(1).

 

            The Dhital court concluded that allegations were sufficient to support the existence of a buyer-seller relationship between the parties as “Plaintiffs alleged that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan's authorized dealerships are [the manufacturer’s] agents for purposes of the sale of Nissan vehicles to consumers. In light of these allegations, we decline to hold plaintiffs’ claim is barred on the ground there was no relationship requiring Nissan to disclose known defects." (Dhital at 845).

 

  1. Conclusion

The Court finds the complaint adequately states a claim for fraud by concealment.  The demurrer is overruled.

 

  1. Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages from the complaint.  The prayer for punitive damages is made in connection with the fraud cause of action only.  A well-pled cause of action for fraud supports a claim for punitive damages.  The motion to strike is therefore denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV00818    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Betty Jo Crawford filed this action against Defendant, Costco Wholesale Corporation for damages arising out of a trip and fall.  Plaintiff’s complaint, filed on 5/04/23, includes causes of action for negligence and premises liability. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

a.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant moves for summary judgment on the complaint.  It contends it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law because it had no actual or constructive notice of any dangerous condition on its property, and because acts by non-party, Parisa Faroutan were intervening and superceding causes of Plaintiff’s injury, such that Defendant cannot be held liable for the injuries.  In the alternative, it seeks adjudication of the cause of action for premises liability on the ground that there is no defect in the premises alleged, and instead the allegation at issue is based on conduct, which cannot be the subject of a claim for premises liability. 

 

b.     Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

 

c.     Evidentiary Objections

Defendant filed evidentiary objections with its reply papers.  The objections are directed at Plaintiff’s expert, Mark J. Burns’s declaration.  The objections are overruled.

 

d.     Undisputed Facts

The basic facts relating to the subject incident are not disputed.  The Court has used the reply separate statement of disputed and undisputed facts, filed on 6/07/24, to review the pertinent facts of the case.  Plaintiff was at Costco when she fell over a trellis in the cart of a patron named Ms. Faroutan.  Faroutan is not a party to this action.  Faroutan had been pushing the cart with the trellis positioned on the bottom of the cart for at least five minutes before the incident.  Notably, a helpful photo of the cart with the trellis is found on page 9 of Defendant’s reply papers. 

 

Foroutan had, prior to the incident, asked a Costco employee to place the trellis at the cash register, but the employee instead put the trellis on the bottom of Faroutan’s cart. 

 

e.     Initial Note

The parties, in the opposition and reply papers, make much of whether the trellis was dangerous or not.  Defendant’s notice of motion did not seek summary judgment on the ground that the trellis was not dangerous, and the moving points and authorities did not meaningfully address this contention.  As noted above, the motion was made on the grounds that Defendant lacked notice of the condition, that the condition was caused by a third party, and that the condition does not constitute premises liability.  The Court therefore need not determine whether the trellis was dangerous, and assumes it was for purposes of this motion. 

 

f.      Actual or Constructive Notice

Defendant’s first argument is that it cannot be held liable for Plaintiff’s injuries because it lacked actual or constructive notice of any dangerous condition on its property.  Defendant’s argument is misplaced, however, because Plaintiff provides evidence that a Costco employee is the one who placed the trellis on the bottom of the cart. 

 

Defendant’s moving papers defeat its own argument.  Defendant cites Getchell v. Rogers Jewelry (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 381, 385, which it admits stands for the position that notice is not necessary when the employee creates the dangerous condition.  Defendant argues Getchell is distinguishable because the Getchell Court considered a situation where the defendant had exclusive control of the dangerous condition, whereas, in this case, Faroutan had the ultimate control over her own cart. 

 

The evidence, however, is to the contrary.  The evidence is that Faroutan asked a Costco employee to place the trellis at the front of the store, and the employee refused to do so, instead placing the trellis on the bottom of her cart.  How, exactly, did Faroutan “retain control” of the trellis?  What were her reasonable options?  Should she have proceeded immediately and carefully to the register to pay for the trellis, taken it to her car, then come back into the store to continue her shopping? 

The Court finds lack of notice is irrelevant where, as here, there are triable issues of material fact concerning whether a Costco employee created the allegedly dangerous condition.

 

g.     Intervening and Superceding Cause

Defendant next argues that Faroutan’s conduct was an intervening and superceding cause of the incident, such that Costco is not liable for the incident.  Specifically, Defendant argues, “Ms. Faroutan inexplicably chose to stop her shopping cart directly at the corner of two Store aisles for an unspecified period of time in a crowded store – despite her stated concerns over how far the trellis was sticking out from the cart.” 

 

Again, however, this would, at most, be contributory negligence.  In Defendant’s cited case, Tarasoff v. Regents of the University of California (1976) 17 Cal.3d 425, 435, the Court held that a defendant is not liable for its own negligence where a “highly unusual or extraordinary” event breaks the chain of causation.  A reasonable juror could find that a Costco patron stopping her cart after being instructed to leave the trellis in the bottom of the cart is not highly unusual or extraordinary. 

 

h.     Conclusion

Defendant’s summary judgment motion is denied because Defendant failed to show there are no triable issues of material fact concerning notice and/or intervening/superceding cause. 

 

i.      Alternative Motion for Summary Adjudication

In the alternative, Defendant seeks summary adjudication of the second cause of action for premises liability.  Defendant contends there was no dangerous condition of the premises itself, as a trellis on a cart is not a part of the premises. 

 

Defendant relies on Juarez v. Boy Scouts of America, Inc. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 377, wherein the plaintiff sued the BSA and the church where he attended meetings for premises liability, contending they were liable for the sexual assaults he suffered as a minor.  The Court of Appeals held that there was nothing about the nature of the property itself that caused the abuse to occur, and therefore the premises liability cause of action could not survive summary judgment.

 

Plaintiff, in opposition, relies on various cases, all of which collectively stand for the position that a defendant store can be held liable, under a premises liability theory, for a temporal condition of their property, such as spilled milk, a misplaced cement box, liquid soap, wet toilet papers, etc. 

 

Defendant, in reply, argues that all of the cases cited involve falls on stationary, rather than moving, conditions of property.  Defendant is correct.  The Court, however, finds that a moving condition on the property is more similar to a stationary condition on the property than it is to sexual assault.  In light of the lack of cited applicable authority in the moving papers, the Court finds Defendant failed to meet its moving burden to show it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law on the premises liability cause of action because the condition was not stationary. 

 

The alternative motion for summary adjudication is denied.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Weston Spiro filed this action against Defendants, Jeremy and Bridget Matthews for damages arising out of the parties’ landlord-tenant relationship.  Plaintiff’s complaint includes causes of action for:

1.     Breach of Contract

2.     Breach of Warranty of Habitability

3.     Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

4.     Negligence

5.     Nuisance

6.     Constructive Eviction

7.     Wrongful Eviction

8.     Failure to Provide Requisite Notice for Eviction

9.     Failure to Pay Relocation Assistance

10.  Failure to Return Security Deposit

2.     Motion to Strike

Plaintiff’s complaint includes a prayer for punitive damages and related allegations, which Defendants move to strike.  The motion is denied.  The Court notes a dearth of authority presented by either side in the landlord-tenant context, and notes that it is not independently aware of any authority decided in this context. 

 

The Court finds a reasonable trier of fact, upon presentation of evidence that Defendants failed, despite repeated notice, to cure the numerous serious problems at the property, including black mold growth, defective and clogged plumbing, and infestation, and then told Plaintiff to fix the problems himself and/or move out (without proper notice, without paying relocation, and without return of or accounting for his security deposit) could conclude that Defendants acted despicably and with a conscious disregard of Plaintiffs’ health and safety, which is the definition of “malice” set forth in Civil Code §3294.  This is particularly true at the pleading stage, when all inferences must be in favor of Plaintiff. 

 

Defendants are ordered to file an answer to the complaint within ten days.
