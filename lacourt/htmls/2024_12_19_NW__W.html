DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV36192    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: W

doran-hernandez v. mcclaskey

 

MOTION to tax costs

 

Date of Hearing:          December 19, 2024                Trial Date:       Jury Verdict –10/7/24

Department:               W                                             Case No.:         19STCV36192

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Zoilo Doran-Hernandez

Responding Party:       Defendant Deborah Marie McClaskey

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 9, 2019, Plaintiff Zoilo Doran-Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant Deborah Marie McClaskey (“Defendant”) and DOES 1 through 25, inclusive for: Negligence. The Complaint alleges that on or about December 21, 2017, Defendant negligently drove her respective vehicle causing it to collide with Plaintiff’s vehicle. (Compl., ¶11.) As a result, Plaintiff suffered general and property damages, and loss of earnings. (Id. at ¶¶12-15.)  On November 8, 2019, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On October 9, 2024, the Court entered Judgment for Plaintiff in the amount of $6,000.00 in damages after the jury trial.

 

On October 22, 2024, Defendant filed a Memorandum of Costs (“MOC”) seeking $134,760.30 in costs against Plaintiff.

 

On October 23, 2024, Defendant filed a Notice of Intent to Move for New Trial. The motion was taken under submission on December 4, 2024. The Court denied the motion on December 16, 2024.

 

On November 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Tax Costs. On December 11, 2024, Defendant filed an Opposition. On December 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff Zoilo Doran-Hernandez’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED in part as to the expert witness fees and court reporter fees, and DENIED in part as to the service of process and models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1032, subd. (b).) “Allowable costs shall be reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).)  “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal. App. 4th 761, 774.) “On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ibid.)

 

“Any notice of motion to strike or to tax costs must be served and filed 15 days after service of the cost memorandum. If the cost memorandum was served by mail, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1013. If the cost memorandum was served electronically, the period is extended as provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(a)(4).” (Cal. Rules Ct., rule 3.1700, subd. (b)(1).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff Zoilo Doran-Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) moves to tax costs in the Memorandum of Costs (“MOC”) filled and served by Defendant Deborah Marie McClaskey (“Defendant”) on October 22, 2024 in its entirety on the grounds that the claimed costs pre-date the Code of Civil Procedure Section 998 Offer to Compromise and the Section 998 offer was not reasonable and made in good faith.

 

Section 998 Offer

 

“Not less than 10 days prior to commencement of trial or arbitration (as provided in Section 1281 or 1295) of a dispute to be resolved by arbitration, any party may serve an offer in writing upon any other party to the action to allow judgment to be taken or an award to be entered in accordance with the terms and conditions stated at that time. The written offer shall include a statement of the offer, containing the terms and conditions of the judgment or award, and a provision that allows the accepting party to indicate acceptance of the offer by signing a statement that the offer is accepted.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (b).)

 

“If an offer made by a defendant is not accepted and the plaintiff fails to obtain a more favorable judgment or award, the costs under this section, from the time of the offer, shall be deducted from any damages awarded in favor of the plaintiff. If the costs awarded under this section exceed the amount of the damages awarded to the plaintiff the net amount shall be awarded to the defendant and judgment or award shall be entered accordingly.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (e).)

 

“To determine whether a plaintiff has obtained a judgment or award more favorable than a defendant's offer under section 998, a trial court must calculate the ‘net judgment,’ considering the status of the litigation at the time the section 998 offer was outstanding.” (Oakes v. Progressive Transportation Services, Inc. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 486, 500.) “To do so, the court must add to the judgment or award costs incurred by the plaintiff prior to the offer [footnote omitted], including preoffer attorney fees in any case in which attorney fees are otherwise awardable as costs.” (Id.) “The court must then deduct from the judgment or award the defendant's postoffer costs allowable under section 1033.5 [Citation], and any postoffer expert witness fees assessed against the plaintiff in the court's discretion. [Citation.].” (Id.)

 

Here, Plaintiff does not argue whether he obtained a judgment or award more favorable that Defendant’s offer to compromise under Section 998. As such, a reasonable person could infer that Plaintiff concedes he did not obtain a more favorable judgment than Defendant’s Section 998 offer. Instead, Plaintiff contends the Section 998 offer was not reasonable at the time it was made.

 

A pretrial settlement offer under Section 998 must be valid, reasonable, and made in good faith. When a Section 998 offer is found to be valid, the burden shifts to the offeree to demonstrate the offer was unreasonable or was not made in good faith. (Smalley v. Subaru of America, Inc. (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 450, 458 (Smalley).) “If the actual judgment is more favorable to the offeror than was the offer, it is prima facie evidence of the offer's reasonableness.” (Id.) “Whether an offer is made in good faith is based on whether, at the time it was made, it carried a reasonable prospect of acceptance by the offeree.” (Id. at 459.) In determining whether a Section 998 offer had a reasonable prospect of acceptance, the Court must consider: (1) if the Section 998 offer was within the “range of possible results” at trial, considering all of the information the offeror knew or reasonably should have known and (2) if the offeror knew that the offeree had sufficient information, based on what the offeree knew or reasonably should have known, to assess whether the offer was reasonable such that the offeree had a fair opportunity to intelligently evaluate the Section 998 offer. (See Licudine v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2019) 30 Cal.App.5th 918, 924–925 (Licudine).)

 

Like in Smalley, the actual judgment in this case was more favorable to Defendant than her Section 998 offer. Defendant’s Section 998 Offer was for $60,000.00. After trial, the jury awarded Plaintiff  $6,000.00. This constitutes prima facie evidence that Defendant’s Section 998 offer was reasonable.

 

Thus, the burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show that the Section 998 offer was unreasonable. To meet this burden, Plaintiff must establish that he did not have sufficient information and time to evaluate the Section 998 offer when it was made. In Licudine, the court held that “In assessing whether the 998 offeror knew that the offeree had sufficient information to evaluate the offer (the second consideration), the offeree needs information bearing on the issue of liability as well as on the amount of damages because these are the issues upon which a verdict would rest and because the 998 offer, if accepted, would be in lieu of that verdict.” (Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at 925.) As a matter of public policy, “ ‘Section 998 must be strictly construed in favor of the party sought to be subjected to its operation.’ ” (Oakes v. Progressive Transportation Services, Inc. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 486, 501.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant’s Section 998 offer was reasonable for the following reasons:

 

The Section 998 Offer was made approximately thirteen (13) months after commencement of the lawsuit, so it was not a premature offer to compromise. Furthermore, the parties had already engaged in initial discovery as Plaintiff had already provided initial responses to discovery propounded by Defendant including information about his purported medical expenses/special damages. Similarly, Plaintiff had made his own Section 998 offer prior to receiving one from Defendant. Likewise, Plaintiff does not contend he had insufficient time to review the Section 998 offer submitted by Defendant.  Plaintiff herself made an offer to settle the case for $100,000, not significantly more than the offer which plaintiff now contends was unreasonable and made in bad faith. 

 

On the other hand, Plaintiff appears to argue that the Section 998 offer was less than his claimed medical expenses of $76,335.85, which he disclosed via his initial discovery responses. Moreover, Plaintiff asserts the section 998 offer was less than Defendant’s policy limit of $100,000.00, which he stated in his own Section 998 offer was reasonable due to him undergoing recommended surgery and ongoing complaints from the collision. Additionally, Plaintiff contends Defendant’s counsel withheld she was in possession of findings from her biomedical expert on September 24, 2020. Plaintiff asserts the findings from Defendant’s expert is pertinent information that would have given him pause to contemplate the Section 998 Offer. Nonetheless, Plaintiff should have known that Defendant would retain an expert in this case to address the issue of liability. Also, there is no indication that Plaintiff sought discovery responses from Defendant, which would have possibly lead to the discovery of Defendant’s expert witness findings. Finally, Plaintiff was at least aware that Defendant denied liability due to the filing of her Answer to the Complaint. 

 

Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to show that he lacked sufficient information to evaluate the issue of liability and amount of damages such that the Section 998 offer was unreasonable.

 

Entitlement to Costs

 

Alternatively, Plaintiff moves to tax the service of process fee ($4,010.45), expert fees ($110,139.00), court reporter fees as established by statute ($702.50), and models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits ($1,298.89) on the grounds that they are unauthorized and/or unreasonable or unnecessary.

 

Here, the service of process fees, court reporter fees as established by statute, and models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits are expressly authorized per Code of Civil Procedure Section 1033.5, subdivision (a)(4), (a)(11), (a)(13), respectively.  Likewise, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 1033.5, subdivision (c)(5)(A), expert fees are allowable for Section 998 offers. As such, the claimed costs appear to be proper and the burden is on Plaintiff to show they were not reasonably incurred.

 

As to the service of process fees, court reporter fees, and expert fees Plaintiff merely argues Defendant provides no documentation to show when these charges were incurred, their necessity, and/or reasonableness. However, a verified MOC is prima facie evidence that the claimed costs are reasonably incurred. (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1700(a)(1).) With respect to Expert Kenneth Solomon Plaintiff contends he was retained and much of his work completed prior to the Section 998 offer. Defendant did submit invoices showing all experts fees with the exception of Dr. Solomon was used after the Section 998 offer but reduces the amount requested to $17,767.00 due to incurring less than the amount requested in the MOC and acknowledging Dr. Solomon was used for services prior to the Section 998 offer. Similarly, Defendant provided invoices supporting its claim for costs of  models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits. By contrast, Defendant withdrew the request for court reporter fees seemingly acknowledging that it was seeking transcripts of the proceedings which were not ordered by the court as raised by Plaintiff’s motion.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff Zoilo Doran-Hernandez’s Motion to Tax Costs is GRANTED in part as to the expert witness fees and court reporter fees, and DENIED in part as to the service of process and models, enlargements, and photocopies of exhibits.

Judgment for Plaintiff             -$6,000.00

Case Number: 22VECV01849    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: W

Sonja Domazet, et al. v. west hills hospital & medical Center, et al.

 

leave to amend Complaint

 

Date of Hearing:          December 19, 2024                            Trial Date:       None set.

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         22VECV01849

 

Moving Parties:           Plaintiffs Sonja Domazet and Elijah Stiny, individually, and Leonardo Stiny, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem Sonja Domazet

Responding Parties:    Defendants West Hills Hospital & Medical Center; Imelda Tio, M.D.; Imelda Tio, M.D., APC; Lauren Hyman, M.d.; Lauren Hyman, M.D., APC; The Partnership For Women’s Health; Bahman Mehdizadeh, M.D.; And Pediatrix Medical Group of California, APC.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from the delivery of twins, one of whom died soon after delivery and the other of whom, Plaintiff Leonardo Stiny, developed serious complications. (Compl., ¶ 20.) Plaintiff Leonardo Stiny (Leonardo) was born with a high blood count (polycythemia) due to Twin-to-Twin Transfusion Syndrome (TTTS), or the transfer of blood from one twin to another while in utero. According to the complaint, all named defendants fell below the standard of care in their “misdiagnosis and lack of adequate assessment and treatment of Plaintiff Leonardo Stiny’s condition in utero, delay in performing a proper emergency cesarean section surgery to deliver Plaintiff Leonardo Stiny in sufficient time to avoid injury, actions and omissions during the course of delivery, and acts, omissions and delay in assessment and specialized treatment of Plaintiff Leonardo Stiny after delivery.” (Id. ¶ 21.)

 

On November 2, 2022, Plaintiffs Sonja Domazet and Elijah Stiny, individually, and Leonardo Stiny, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem Sonja Domazet (collectively Plaintiffs) filed a complaint against Defendants West Hills Hospital & Medical Center; Imelda Tio, M.D.; Imelda Tio, M.D., APC; Lauren Hyman, M.d.; Lauren Hyman, M.D., APC; The Partnership For Women’s Health; Bahman Mehdizadeh, M.D.; And Pediatrix Medical Group of California, APC (collectively Defendants). The complaint alleged causes of action for (1) professional negligence, (2) negligent infliction of emotional distress, and (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On October 24, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant  motion to amend the complaint to add a claim for punitive damages as to all Defendants.

 

On November 20, 2024, Defendants Bahman Mehdizadeh M.D. and Pedatrix Medical Group of California, APC filed an opposition.

 

On December 5, 2024, Defendants Imelda Tio, M.D., Imelda Tio, M.D. APC, Lauren Hyman M.D., Lauren Hyman, M.D. APC, and the Partnership for Women’s Health filed an opposition.

 

On December 6, 2024, Defendant West Hills Hospital and Medical Center filed an opposition.

 

On December 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a reply.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

1.      Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to amend their complaint is DENIED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure § 473(a) and § 576.  The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.  [Citation.]”  (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422.)  Notwithstanding the “policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . . .’ [citation].  A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].”  (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.) 

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a), (b).) 

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendant West Hills Hospital and Medical Center filed evidentiary objections to the entirety of the deposition testimonies of Plaintiffs Sonja Domazet and Elijah Stiny on the following bases: Lacks foundation; speculative; conclusory; hearsay; insufficient to qualify as expert testimony or declaration sufficient to render any criticisms or opinions on medical or nursing standard of care or causation issues.

 

Both of Defendant West Hills Hospital and Medical Center’s objections are overruled.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Here, Plaintiffs are seeking to amend their complaint to assert new language supporting a claim for punitive damages as well as a prayer for punitive damages against all named Defendants.

 

Timeliness

 

Leave of Court to plead punitive damages must be obtained by Motion filed within two years after the initial pleading was filed or nine months before the date first set for trial, whichever is earlier.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.13.)

 

Here, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion on October 24, 2024, and the complaint was filed on November 2, 2022. Because the instant motion was filed within two years of the filing of the complaint and a date for trial has not been set yet, the instant motion is timely.

 

Defendants Imelda Tio, M.D., Imelda Tio, M.D. APC, Lauren Hyman M.D., Lauren Hyman, M.D. APC, and the Partnership for Women’s Health in their opposition argue that the instant motion is untimely given the that the that the related case BC691696 was filed on January 31, 2018, approaching the seventh year of litigation. Defendants Imelda Tio, M.D., Imelda Tio, M.D. APC, Lauren Hyman M.D., Lauren Hyman, M.D. APC, and the Partnership for Women’s Health argue that because the two related cases re presently consolidated for discovery purposes, “there is a high possibility that they will be consolidated for trial purposes as well.” They contend that the present trial in the related case is currently set for April 21, 2025, which makes the instant motion untimely as it was filed after the ninth-month deadline in Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.13.

 

However, as of the date of the hearing of the instant motion, the present case and the related case have not been consolidated for trial purposes. Because trial has not been set for the present case, and the instant motion was filed within two years of the filing of the complaint, the instant motion is technically timely under Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.13.

 

Even though timely, however, the instant motion fails on its merits.

 

 

Punitive Damages

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.13(a) provides "[t]he court may allow the filing of an amended pleading claiming punitive damages on a motion by the party seeking the amended pleading and on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294 of the Civil Code." Civil Code Section 3294(a) in turn provides that '[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant."

 

“[A]bsent a statutory prohibition, punitive damages may be awarded on what is traditionally considered a negligence cause of action if the conduct amounts to despicable conduct carried on with conscious disregard of the safety of others. (Bommareddy v. Superior Ct. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1017, 1021.)

 

“The party seeking punitive damages must demonstrate the existence of sufficient evidence to establish a prima facie case for punitive damages in accordance with Civil Code section 3294 's higher standard of proof by clear and convincing evidence." (Little Co. of Mary Hospital v. Superior Court (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 261, 267.) Section 425.13 “operates like a demurrer or motion for summary judgment in “reverse.” Rather than requiring the defendant to defeat the plaintiff's pleading by showing it is legally or factually meritless, the motion requires the plaintiff to demonstrate that he possesses a legally sufficient claim which is “substantiated,” that is, supported by competent, admissible evidence.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 719.)

 

A plaintiff must make a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain the punitive damage claim,¿taking into account¿the higher “clear and convincing” standard of proof required for such claims under Code of Civil Procedure section 3294.¿¿(Looney v. Superior Court¿(1993)¿16 Cal.App.4th 521, 538–540.)¿¿The Court may not assess credibility or weigh conflicting evidence.¿¿(Id. at p. 539.)  The Court must not reject a well-pled and factually supported punitive damages claim simply because the Court believes the evidence is not strong enough for probable success before a jury.¿¿(College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 709; see also¿Pomona Valley Hospital Medical Center v. Superior Court¿(2013)¿213 Cal.App.4th 828, 837 [“Section¿425.13(a) does not contemplate a minitrial in which witness testimony is introduced—like a motion for summary judgment, is decided entirely on an ‘affidavit’ showing.”].)  The Court will deny the Motion only when the facts asserted in the proposed amended Complaint are legally insufficient to support a punitive damages claim or when the evidence provided in the supporting and opposing declarations either negates or fails to reveal the actual existence of a triable claim.  (Ibid.)¿¿ 

 

Here, Plaintiffs argue that an amendment to the complaint to add a claim for punitive damages is appropriate because “the conduct of Defendants Dr. Hyman, Dr. Tio, Dr. Mehdizadeh, and West Hills Hospital falls squarely within the standard for punitive damages under Civil Code §3294(c)(1)” and “the facts demonstrate despicable conduct carried out with a willful disregard for the Plaintiff’s safety and wellbeing.” (Motion, page 8.)

 

Defendants collectively argue that Plaintiff’s instant motion for leave to amend to add a claim for punitive damages fails, as the evidence proffered by Plaintiffs does not constitute “competent, admissible evidence” as Plaintiffs have not provided expert medical testimony to support their motion to add a claim for punitive damages. The Court agrees.

 

“[I]n light of the ‘affidavit’ requirement and by analogy to summary judgment practice, substantiation of a proposed punitive damages claim occurs only where the factual recitals are made under penalty of perjury and set forth competent admissible evidence within the personal knowledge of the declarant.” (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at pp. 719-20.) “Where a medical process or procedure is not a matter of common knowledge, expert testimony is necessary to determine whether a probability of negligence appears from the happening of an accident or untoward result. [Citation.]” (Folk v. Kilk (1975) 53 Cal.App.3d 176, 185.) “The standard of skill, knowledge and care prevailing in a medical community is ordinarily a matter within the knowledge of experts. [Citation.]” (Ibid.) “However, when the witness is a layperson, he or she may not testify on matters which are not proper subjects of lay opinion testimony.” (Jambazian v. Borden (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 836, 848–49.)

 

The evidentiary burden is on Plaintiffs to come forth with competent, admissible evidence and Plaintiffs have not even provided expert testimony or any other competent and admissible affidavit or declaration from a qualified medical or nursing expert establishing any breach of standard of care in the treatment of Leonardo. (See Kelley v. Trunk (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 519, 523 [Expert testimony is required to “prove or disprove that the defendant performed in accordance with the standard of care”].) Because Plaintiffs have not come forth with competent, admissible evidence to establish a breach of the standard of care, Plaintiffs necessarily have not proffered competent, admissible evidence regarding the higher standard for the imposition of punitive damages, specifically whether Defendants acted “with the intent to vex, injure, or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights. [Citations.]” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895.)

 

The deposition testimony of Plaintiffs Sonja Domazet and Elijah Stiny, as laypersons, cannot be used to satisfy their high burden in showing a substantial probability of prevailing on a punitive damages prayer by clear and convincing evidence. (Pomona Valley Hosp. Med. Ctr. v. Superior Ct. (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 828, 838

[Plaintiffs’ deposition testimony “is not based on any personal knowledge of conduct by the hospital regarding plaintiff, so it necessarily does not amount to clear and convincing proof of the elements of malice.”].) While Plaintiffs are correct that the “[t]he trial court is not required to make any factual determination or to become involved in any weighing process beyond that necessarily involved in deciding whether a prima facie case for punitive damages exists,” Plaintiffs have failed to provide the Court with any admissible evidence to support their evidentiary burden in showing a claim for punitive damages under the clear and convincing standard. (Looney v. Superior Court (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 521, 539.)

 

Furthermore, as stated above, “section 425.13(a) does not contemplate a mini-trial in which witness testimony is introduced. As we have seen, a section 425.13(a) motion, like a motion for summary judgment, is decided entirely on an ‘affidavit’ showing.” (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 717.) Plaintiffs provided the deposition testimony of Plaintiffs Sonja Domazet, Elijah Stiny, and some Defendants, rather than admissible affidavits or declarations. Plaintiffs’ contention that “a jury reasonably can conclude that Defendants engaged in wrongful conduct with a willful conscious disregard of their rights and safety based on the evidence Plaintiffs have presented” is also not appropriate at this stage. (Reply, page 5; College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 715 [“The ‘affidavit’ format described by the [425.13] statute is a truncated one, reminiscent of summary judgment procedure. It is not well suited to predicting how the jury would react to a full-blown case at trial.”].)

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing reasons, Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to amend the complaint to add language relating to a claim for punitive damages as well as a prayer for punitive damages is DENIED.

 


Case Number: 24VECV03103    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: W

WILLIAM FIKHMAN v. auction brothers, inc., et al.

 

Demurrer with motion to strike

 

Date of Hearing:          December 19, 2024                            Trial Date:       None set.

Department:               W                                                         Case No.:         24VECV03103

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Auction Brother, Inc.

Responding Party:       Plaintiff William Fikhman

 

BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from the alleged wrongful termination of Plaintiff William Fikhman’s employment with Defendant Auction Brothers, Inc. (Auction Brothers).

 

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Auction Brothers, alleging causes of action for (1) Retaliation, (2) Wrongful Discharge in Violation of Public Policy, (3) Unfair Competition and (4) Waiting Time Penalties.

 

On October 1, 2024, Auction Brothers filed the instant demurrer to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for Waiting Time Penalties, as well as a motion to strike with respect to Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages.

 

On December 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition to the instant demurrer and motion to strike.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

1.      Auction Brothers’ demurrer is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

2.      Auction Brothers’ motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

1.      Demurrer

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “‘treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law . . ..’” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) 

 

When a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.)

 

2.      Motion to Strike

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike a pleading or any part thereof.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)¿ The court¿may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)¿ The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)¿ An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., 431.10, subd. (b).)¿ The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)¿¿¿

 

Meet and Confer  

 

Before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the moving party is required to meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer or motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41; 435.5.)  

 

Here, counsel for Auction Brothers sent Plaintiff a meet and confer correspondence addressing the purported deficiencies with Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action. (Matthews Decl., ¶ 3, Exhibit A.) However, counsel for Auction Brothers has not declared that he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel in person or via telephone. The Court does not find merely sending an email sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement. The Court admonishes counsel for not properly meeting and conferring to informally resolve the objections raised in the instant demurrer and motion to strike.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Auction Brothers demurs to the fourth cause of action for Waiting Time Penalties of Plaintiff’s complaint on two bases: (1) the complaint fails sufficiently alleged that Plaintiff was entitled to paid time off pursuant to California Labor Code §§ 201-203, despite being an exempt employee, and (2) the complaint fails to plead the amount of wages accrued and unpaid, and the rate of compensation. (Oppenheimer v. Robinson (1957) 150 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1384.)

 

Auction Brothers also moves to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damage. Specifically, Auction Brothers moves to strike both Paragraph 35 and Paragraph 49 and their mention of the purported “egregious nature” of Auction Brothers’ alleged conduct and the coinciding request for punitive damages. (Compl., ¶¶ 35, 49.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has filed a notice of non-opposition to Auction Brothers’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s references to, and requests for, punitive damages, and Auction Brothers’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for Waiting Time Penalties. Plaintiff, in his notice of non-opposition, stated that he will file an amended complaint by December 27, 2024. Thus, the Court will not address the arguments contained in the instant demurrer and motion to strike.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing reasons, Auction Brothers’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action in the complaint for Waiting Time Penalties is sustained with 20 days leave to amend. Auction Brothers’ motion to strike is granted without leave to amend.
