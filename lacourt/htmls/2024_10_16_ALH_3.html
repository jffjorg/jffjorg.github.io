[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEFENDANT DILYS JONES’ MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S COMPLIANCE WITH COURT ORDER; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

Dept. 3

)

 

 

Defendant Dilys Jones (“Defendant”), as Trustee of the Louis Petrie Separate Property Trust, moves for an order compelling Plaintiff Café on Lemon, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) to comply with the Court’s August 13, 2024, Order (“August 13 Order”) and serve responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One. Defendant also seeks $1,900 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(c) provides that the Court may impose a monetary sanction if party fails to obey an answer compelling responses to interrogatories.

Plaintiff opposes the motion and argues that any failure to comply with the Court’s order was due to its counsel’s illness. Counsel Dieter Zacher declares that he was “deathly ill from sepsis and a bacterial infection and could not complete discovery or respond to the demurrers or discovery responses timely in this case.” (Opp., Zacher Decl., ¶ 2.)

In its reply brief, Defendant points out that counsel’s declaration is identical to the one filed three months ago on July 19, 2024. Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s counsel has had ample time since July 2024 for his condition to improve and if not, to hire other counsel to assist with the case. The Court agrees. Plaintiff fails to provide any justification for disobeying the Court’s August 13 Order.

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion for sanctions is GRANTED and the Court imposes sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,425 consisting of 3 hours at defense counsel’s hourly rate of $475, to be paid within 20 days of the date of this Order.

Dated this 16th day of October, 2024

 

 

 

 

       William A. Crowfoot

Judge of the Superior Court

I.      INTRODUCTION

         On May 10, 2023, plaintiff Elijah Carder (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendant KAN Investment LTD LLC (“Defendant”) (erroneously sued as “South Pasadena Heights Apartments”). On September 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), to which Defendant demurred; Defendant also moved to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages. On April 19, 2024, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC and granted Defendant’s motion to strike with 20 days’ leave to amend.

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) asserting causes of action for: (1) battery, (2) negligence, (3) nuisance, (4) intentional infliction of emotional distress (“IIED”), (5) breach of lease, and (6) breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment.

          Plaintiff alleges that he was a tenant at the premises located at 435 Garfield Avenue, Unit 307, South Pasadena, CA 91030 (the “Property”). The Property was leased to him by Defendant and had a collection antenna which [Defendant] placed on the outside wall of the premises at which [Plaintiff] was residing.” (SAC, ¶ 4.) Plaintiff alleges he was exposed to excessive radiation emitted from this antenna. (SAC, ¶ 4.)  

          On June 4, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer and motion to strike arguing that Plaintiff’s SAC failed to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and was uncertain, vague, and ambiguous. Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages.

II.     LEGAL STANDARDS

A.   Demurrer

A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings and will be sustained only where the pleading is defective on its face. (City of Atascadero v. Merrill Lynch, Pierce, Fenner & Smith, Inc. (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 445, 459.)

A demurrer may also be brought if a cause of action is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Where the complaint contains substantial factual allegations sufficiently apprising defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet, a demurrer for uncertainty will be overruled or plaintiff will be given leave to amend. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)

B.   Motion to Strike

          Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).) The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

III.    DISCUSSION

As a threshold matter, the Court addresses Defendant’s argument that the entire SAC is preempted by federal law because it is based on alleged health effects from radiofrequency levels that the Federal Communications Commission (“FCC”) has already determined to be safe. (Demurrer, p. 2.) As the Court observed in its previous minute order ruling on a demurrer brought by now-dismissed defendant T-Mobile USA, Inc. (“T-Mobile”), if Plaintiff is contending that the radiation emitted is “excessive” despite being within the FCC’s limits, then Plaintiff’s claims are preempted. (Bennett v. T-Mobile USA, Inc. (C.D. Cal. 2008) 597 F. Supp. 2d 1050, 1052 [FCC’s determination of “optimal level of safe [radiofrequency] emissions” and licenses cannot be declared unsafe by jury].) Therefore, the Court sustained T-Mobile's demurrer with leave to amend based on Plaintiff’s representation in his opposition brief that he was, in fact, claiming that the antenna’s emissions exceeded the FCC’s limit. (4/19/2024 Minute Order, p. 3.)

In opposition, Plaintiff only argues that Defendant is precluded from arguing that the matter is preempted because Defendant did not raise this ground as a basis for its previous demurrer. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (b).) However, whether a state action is preempted by federal law is an issue of subject matter jurisdiction, which may be raised at any time by any available procedure. (Boisclair v. Superior Court (1990) 51 C3d 1140, 1144, n. 1.) Therefore, although Defendant did not previously demur to Plaintiff’s Second and Third Causes of Action for Negligence and Nuisance, Defendant did not waive its argument that the Court lacks subject matter jurisdiction over this action. Furthermore, since Plaintiff has not amended his complaint to add that the alleged emissions exceeded the limits established by the FCC, Plaintiff’s claims are preempted.

Plaintiff was already given an opportunity to amend his complaint yet did not do so. Accordingly, the demurrer to the SAC is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. Since the Court sustains the demurrer to the entire SAC, the motion to strike is moot.

 V.     CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer to the SAC is SUSTAINED without leave to amend. The motion to strike is MOOT.
