Plaintiffs,

Defendants.

Case No.:

22STCV39547

September 9, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion to disqualify attorney shawn m. olson and olson law firm

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Eduardo Luna          

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc., and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc.

Motion to Disqualify Attorney Shawn M. Olson and Olson Law Firm

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Eduardo Luna (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order disqualifying Shawn M. Olson and the Olson Law Firm (“Counsel”) from representing any party in this action, including defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc., and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”).

“A trial court’s authority to disqualify an attorney derives from the power inherent in every court ‘[t]o control in furtherance of justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto.’”  (People ex rel. Dept. of Corporations v. SpeeDee Oil Change Systems, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1135, 1145 (“SpeeDee Oil”).)  “In evaluating alleged conflicts, a court first looks to whether the challenged representation is concurrent or successive.”  (Ontiveros v. Constable (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 686, 695.)  “The ‘primary value’ at issue in concurrent ‘or dual representation is the attorney’s duty—and the client’s legitimate expectation—of loyalty….’”  (Ibid.)  The value jeopardized by the successive representation of clients with potentially adverse interests “is that of client confidentiality.”  (Ibid.)  “The paramount concern [when resolving disqualification motions] must be to preserve public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar.”  (SpeeDee Oil, supra, 20 Cal.4th at p. 1145.)  

First, the court finds, and the parties do not appear to dispute, that Plaintiff has standing to bring this motion.

“To bring a motion to disqualify counsel, the moving party must have standing; that is, the invasion of a legally cognizable interest.  [Citation.]  Standing may arise from an attorney-client relationship between the moving party and targeted counsel or from a duty of confidentiality owed by the attorney to the moving party despite the absence of an attorney-client relationship.”  (Lynn v. George (2017) 15 Cal.App.5th 630, 636-637 [internal citation omitted].)

Here, Plaintiff has alleged claims on behalf of the Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association Inc. defendants (the “VPHOA Parties”) on the ground that Plaintiff is a member of the VPHOA Parties by virtue of his ownership of two units in the building that they govern.  (FAC ¶ 16.)  Plaintiff has submitted his declaration, in which he states that he is “an owner of two units under the auspices of the Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association.”  (Luna Decl., ¶ 2.)  While Plaintiff did not submit evidence showing that such ownership of those units establishes that he was a member of the VPHOA Parties, in their opposition papers, Defendants have asserted that the VPHOA Parties “ha[ve] 16 units and thus 16 shareholders.”  (Opp., p. 5:12.)  Moreover, in the First Amended Cross-Complaint, the VPHOA Parties, Dawn Kuykendall, and Rodney Owens have alleged that Plaintiff is “a member of” the VPHOA Parties.  (FACC ¶ 4.)  Thus, it appears that Defendants concede that Plaintiff is a member of VPHOA Parties. 

            The court therefore finds that Plaintiff has standing to bring this motion to disqualify Counsel because (1) Plaintiff has alleged derivative claims on behalf of the VPHOA Parties,     (2) Plaintiff is a member of the VPHOA Parties by virtue of his ownership of two of their condominium units, such that (3) VPHOA Parties’ standing has been vicariously imputed to Plaintiff.  (Blue Water Sunset, LLC v. Markowitz (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 477, 485 [finding that, because counsel had an attorney-client relationship with the limited liability companies that were sued as nominal defendants, of which the plaintiff was a 50 percent member, “their standing must be imputed vicariously to” the plaintiff]; Id. at p. 486 [“A rule of vicarious standing permits [the plaintiff] to enforce that uniliteral right [to waive or not waive a conflict of interest] and insist upon the disqualification relief available to the limited liability companies”].)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has shown that (1) there exists a conflict of interest between VPHOA Parties and the named defendants as a result of Counsel’s concurrent representation of those parties, and (2) therefore Counsel should be disqualified from representing VPHOA Parties in this action.

It is undisputed that Counsel represents VPHOA Parties.  (See, e.g., Demurrer to First Amended Complaint filed Aug. 2, 2024, p. 1:1-7; Opp. to Motion, p. 1:1-6; Olson Decl., p. 1:1-6.)  However, the first through fifth causes of action alleged in the First Amended Complaint assert that they are brought on behalf of the plaintiffs, in their individual capacity, and derivatively on behalf of the VPHOA Parties.  (FAC pp. 6:1-3, 8:20-22, 9:17-19, 10:19-22, 11:11-14.)  Thus, because VPHOA Parties are named as nominal defendants in a derivative action, VPHOA Parties are “actually plaintiffs in the eyes of the law.”  (Blue Water Sunset, LLC, supra, 192 Cal.App.4th at p. 489; Heshejin v. Rostami (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 984, 995 [“A derivative cause of action belongs to the corporation, and the corporation is the true plaintiff”].)  VPHOA Parties’ “status as the true plaintiff prevents an attorney from representing both the company and its insiders in a derivative lawsuit that alleges the insiders breached their duties owed to the company or otherwise injured the company.  Such representation would be an impermissible concurrent representation of clients with conflicting interests.”  (Beachcomber Management Crystal Cove, LLC v. Superior Court (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1105, 1118.)  

            Thus, Plaintiff has shown that Counsel is, in effect, representing both the plaintiffs (i.e., VPHOA Parties) and the defendants in this action.  (Blue Water Sunset, LLC, supra, 192 Cal.App.4th at p. 489.)  VPHOA Parties do not appear to meaningfully dispute this in their opposition papers.  The court therefore grants Plaintiff’s motion to disqualify Counsel from representing VPHOA Parties in this action.  (Id. at p. 487 [“If an attorney simultaneously represents two clients with adverse interests, automatic disqualification is the rule in all but a few instances”].)

            Third, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Counsel should be disqualified from representing individual defendants Dawn Kuykendall (“D. Kuykendall”) and Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall (“J. Kuykendall”) in this action.

            As a threshold matter, it appears that Plaintiff contends that Counsel should not represent these defendants in this action based on the “simultaneous representations” of the parties.  (Mot., p. 5:20-21, 5:25-26.)  However, the court has disqualified Counsel from representing VPHOA Parties for the reasons set forth above.  Thus, Counsel will no longer be concurrently representing parties with adverse interests, such that disqualification is not necessary.  (See, e.g., Blue Water Sunset, LLC, supra, 192 Cal.App.4th at p. 492 [disqualifying counsel from representing the nominal defendants but permitting counsel to continue to represent the named defendant].)

            Moreover, to the extent that Plaintiff contends that Counsel should be disqualified from representing defendants D. Kuykendall and J. Kuykendall under a successive representation theory, the court disagrees.  “Successive representation rules . . . generally do not prevent an attorney from continuing to represent insiders in a derivative lawsuit even though a substantial relationship exists between the attorney’s previous representation of the company and the attorney’s current representation of insiders in the company’s lawsuit against them.  [Citations.]  This separate rule derives from a recognition that insiders are the source of a closely held company’s confidential information.”  (Beachcomber Management Crystal Cove, LLC, supra, 13 Cal.App.5th at pp. 1118-1119.)  “[I]n this scenario, ‘[c]ounsel’s continued representation of the [insiders] poses no threat to [c]ounsel’s continuing duty of confidentiality to [the company]’ because the insiders already know all of the Company’s confidential information.”  (Id. at p. 1119.)

            Both Plaintiff and Defendants have submitted evidence showing that D. Kuykendall and J. Kuykendall, as insiders, could provide their new attorney with the same information that Counsel has obtained.  Specifically, Plaintiff has (1) stated, in his declaration, that (ii) D. Kuykendall has acted as the Chief Executive of VPHOA Parties for more than five years, (ii) J. Kuykendall has acted as the Secretary of VPHOA Parties “for most of that time[,]” and (iii) D. Kuykendall and R. Kuykendall “both have acted and continue to act as the sole board members[,]” and (2) submitted a copy of the Statement of Information for Villa Patrician Homeowner’s Association (Entity No. 0889948), which states that D. Kuykendall is the Chief Executive Officer.  (Luna Decl., ¶¶ 5, 7; Luna Decl., Ex. C, p. 1.)  Similarly, Defendants have submitted the declaration of Counsel, in which Counsel states that, of the 16 residential units comprising VPHOA Parties, D. Kuykendall owns seven units.  (Olson Decl., ¶ 2.)

            Thus, in light of the evidence submitted by the parties showing that D. Kuykendall is a member of the VPHOA Parties and that she and defendant J. Kuykendall are the sources of VPHOA Parties’ confidential information by virtue of their positions as Chief Executive Officer and Secretary, such that “it would be meaningless to apply the successive representation rules to prevent” Counsel from representing them in this action, the court finds that disqualification of Counsel would “serve no purpose[.]”  (Beachcomber Management Crystal Cove, LLC, supra, 13 Cal.App.5th at p. 1119; Id. at p. 1122 [application of this rule “turns on whether the insiders had access to the same information as the attorney who represented both the insiders and the company”].)  The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion to disqualify Counsel from representing D. Kuykendall and J. Kuykendall in this action.

            Fourth, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that Counsel should be disqualified from representing defendants Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, and BJD Services, LLC in this action.  

As to these defendants, Plaintiff bases his motion on the sole ground that Counsel cannot represent VPHOA Parties while concurrently representing the individual defendants on the ground that it would violate Counsel’s duty of loyalty.  (Mot., p. 6:5-23.)  The duty of loyalty is “at stake” when there is a concurrent representation of multiple clients resulting in a conflict of interest.  (M’Guinness v. Johnson (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 602, 613.)  However, for the reasons set forth above, the court has disqualified Counsel from representing VPHOA Parties in this action, such that Counsel will no longer represent parties with adverse interests.  To the extent that Plaintiff contends that the duty of confidentiality may be breached (which is put at issue with successive representation), Plaintiff did not present sufficient argument establishing that the court should disqualify Counsel on that ground.  (Ibid. [noting that in successive representation cases, the “‘chief fiduciary value jeopardized is that of client confidentiality‘”] [emphasis in original].)  The court therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion to disqualify Counsel from representing defendants Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, and BJD Services, LLC in this action.

ORDER

            The court grants in part plaintiff Eduardo Luna’s motion to disqualify attorney Shawn M. Olson and Olson Law Firm as follows.

            The court orders that Shawn M. Olson and Olson Law Firm are disqualified from representing in this action (1) Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association, Inc., and (2) Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association, Inc.

            The court denies all other relief requested in plaintiff Eduardo Luna’s motion to disqualify attorney Shawn M. Olson and Olson Law Firm.
