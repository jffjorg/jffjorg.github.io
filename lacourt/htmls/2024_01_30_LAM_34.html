BACKGROUND:

 

On July 28, 2021, Family Choice Private Duty Agency, Inc. (“FCPDA”) filed its Complaint against Kamerron Easton (“Easton”) and James P. Ashby (“Ashby”) on causes of action for breach of contract, breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing, and commons counts.

 

On October 15, 2021, Easton, who is an attorney litigating in propria persona, filed his Cross-Complaint against FCPDA and Shellydale Princess Gray (“Gray”).

 

On October 22, 2021, Easton filed his Answer to the Complaint.

 

On February 8, 2022, the Court sustained in part FCPDA and Gray’s Demurrer to the Cross-Complaint. Specifically, the Court sustained the Demurrer to the first and sixth causes of action in the Cross-Complaint, for breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing and intentional infliction of emotional distress, respectively.

Case Number: BC482598    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion Re Order to Show Cause Re Sanctions Previously Set

 

Moving Party: Judgment Creditor Samsum Shadanloo

Resp. Party:    Judgment Debtor Ehsan Bashi

                                   

 

 

The Motion re Sanctions is DENIED.

 

        Monetary sanctions are AWARDED in favor of Judgment Debtor and against Judgment Creditor and Judgment Creditor’s Counsel, jointly and severally, in the total amount of $550.00.

 

The Bench Warrant on Judgment Debtor is QUASHED.

 

         “Judgement Debtor Ehsan Bashi Concise Supplemental Briefing” (i.e., the Motion for Reconsideration of the December 18, 2023 Award of Monetary Sanctions Against Judgment Debtor and Judgment Debtor’s Counsel”) is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND:

 

I.          Pre-Judgment Background

 

On April 11, 2012, Plaintiff Samsum Shadanloo filed his Complaint against Defendant 6623 Hollywood Cherokee, LLC (erroneously sued as SBE Entertainment Group, LLC) on causes of action arising from an assault and battery against Plaintiff at Defendant’s nightclub.

 

On August 13, 2012, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (FAC).

 

On December 28, 2012, Plaintiff amended his Complaint to substitute Doe 1 with Ehsan Bashi.

 

On March 13, 2013, by request of Plaintiff, the Clerk’s Office dismissed with prejudice Defendant 6623 Hollywood Cherokee, LLC from the FAC.

 

On July 31, 2013, the Court granted 6623 Hollywood Cherokee, LLC’s application for determination of good faith settlement.

 

On May 1, 2014, the Court held a trial in this matter. The Court found in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant Ehsan Bashi on a cause of action for battery.

 

On May 23, 2014, the Court entered judgment in this matter. After an offset from the good faith settlement of 6623 Hollywood Cherokee, LLC, the Court awarded total damages of $27,099.00 in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant Ehsan Bashi, plus accrued interest at the legal rate.

 

II.       Post-Judgment Background

 

On multiple occasions since Judgment was entered in this matter, Plaintiff (now Judgment Creditor) has moved to have Defendant Ehsan Bashi (now Judgment Debtor) appear for examination.

 

Suffice it to say that Judgment Debtor has resisted and continues to resist examination. (See Minute Order dated August 3, 2023, p. 6 [“Defendant has for several months undertaken herculean efforts to resist being forced to sit for a debtor’s examination — including, but not limited to challenging service, moving to reconsider when the challenge to service was rejected, filing a separate action against Plaintiff, filing this motion to stay enforcement of the judgment in the Main Action, asking for a live evidentiary hearing on the motion to stay, and submitting numerous ex parte applications.”].)

 

On August 3, 2023, the Court denied Judgment Debtor’s Motion for Temporary Stay [on] Judgment Enforcement Pending Disposition of Pending Civil Matter (“Motion to Stay Judgment”), which was filed on July 3, 2023.

 

On December 18, 2023, the Court denied Judgment Debtor’s Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment. The Court awarded monetary sanctions in favor of Judgment Creditor and against Judgment Debtor and Judgment Debtor’s Counsel, jointly and severally, in the total amount of $1,500.00.

 

On December 21, 2023, Judgment Creditor filed Judicial Council Form EJ-100, Acknowledgment of Full Satisfaction of Judgment.

 

On January 8, 2024, Judgment Creditor filed Motion re Order to Show Cause Re Sanctions Previously Set by Judge Ellis (“Motion Re Sanctions”). In support of his Motion Re Sanctions, Judgment Creditor concurrently filed: (1) Memorandum of Points and Authorities; (2) Declaration of Evertt J. Gotfredson; and (3) Proof of Service.

 

On January 18, 2024, Judgment Debtor filed a document which included three items: (1) Opposition to Judgment Debtor’s Motion Re Sanctions; (2) Request for Monetary Sanctions for Frivolous Opposition; and (3) Supplemental Briefing Concerning the Continued Sanctions Issue from December 18, 2023 Tentative Ruling (“Opposition”). In support of his Opposition, Judgment Debtor concurrently filed: (1) Declaration of Paymon John Vafa; and (2) Declaration of Ehsan Bashi.

 

On January 22, 2024, Judgment Creditor filed his Reply in support of his Motion Re Sanctions.

 

On January 22, 2024, Judgment Debtor filed his Concise Supplemental Briefing Concerning C.C.P. § 128.5 Sanctions from December 18, 2024.

 

On January 24, 2024, Judgment Debtor filed his Status Conference Statement.

 

On January 25, 2024, Judgment Debtor filed his Exhibits.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Motion Re Sanctions

 

Judgment Creditor moves the Court for an order to show cause regarding sanctions, which had been set last year by the prior judicial officer hearing this matter because Judgment Debtor had not appeared at prior debtor’s examinations. (Motion Re Sanctions, p. 2:2–12.)

 

The issue of sanctions for failure to pay at debtor’s examinations is moot. Judgment Debtor has paid the Judgment in full, which Judgment Creditor has already acknowledged. Because the point of monetary sanctions for failure to appear at a debtor’s examination are ultimately to have a judgment debtor satisfy the judgment, there is no longer a reason to issue monetary sanctions for that purpose, much less for the Court to issue an order to show cause regarding sanctions.

 

        The Court agrees with Judgment Debtor that the Motion re Sanctions — which is barely more than one page in substance —is a frivolous motion. If Judgment Creditor sought attorney’s fees for work done to satisfy the Judgment, Judgment Creditor should have filed such a motion. (See Decl. Gotfredson, ¶ 3.) But seeking monetary sanctions against Judgment Debtor for failing to appear at debtor’s examinations after Judgment Debtor has satisfied the Judgment is not the appropriate procedural avenue for such relief.

 

        Judgment Debtor requests monetary sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5. (Opposition, p. 13:17–22.)

 

“A trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. This section also applies to judicial arbitration proceedings under Chapter 2.5 (commencing with Section 1141.10) of Title 3 of Part 3.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.5, subd. (a).)

 

        Given the frivolous nature of the Motion re Sanctions, the Court orders Judgment Creditor and Judgment Creditor’s Counsel to pay reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred “as a result of” the frivolous Motion re Sanctions.

 

        Judgment Debtor’s Counsel declares that they have spent 19.90 hours in responding to the Motion Re Sanctions. (Decl. Vafa, ¶ 53.) According to their time sheet, they charge $550.00 per hour. (Decl. Vafa, Exh. 15.)

 

        While the hourly rate requested is reasonable, the number of hours spent on the Opposition was not reasonable. Although the Opposition was long, most of it was irrelevant to the issues at hand. The Court will award fees for one hour of work at the hourly rate of $550.00.

       

        The Motion re Sanctions is DENIED.

 

        Monetary sanctions are AWARDED in favor of Judgment Debtor and against Judgment Creditor and Judgment Creditor’s Counsel, jointly and severally, in the total amount of $550.00.

 

II.       Remaining Items

 

Judgment Debtor filed items on January 22, 24, and 25, 2024. The latter two filings (“Status Conference Statement” and “Exhibits”) presumably have to do with the status conference regarding this case and the status of the bench warrant issued. As the Judgment has been fully satisfied, there is no reason to maintain the bench warrant.

 

The Bench Warrant on Judgment Debtor is QUASHED.

 

        The prior filing (“Concise Supplemental Briefing”) has to do with the Court’s award of monetary sanctions against Judgment Debtor as a result of his frivolous Renewed Motion to Stay Judgment. Contrary to what Judgment Debtor claims, the Court’s award is not tentative — it is final. No supplemental briefing was requested or necessary. At most, the ”supplemental briefing” is actually a motion for reconsideration. Both because there is no new or different information presented and, on the merits, the Court declines to reconsider the award of monetary sanctions against Judgment Debtor and Judgment Debtor’s Counsel on December 18, 2023.

 

        The “Concise Supplemental Briefing” (i.e., the Motion for Reconsideration of the December 18, 2023 Award of Monetary Sanctions Against Judgment Debtor and Judgment Debtor’s Counsel”) is DENIED.
