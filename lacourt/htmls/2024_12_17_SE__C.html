DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22NWCV01091    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: C

SIMMONS, ET AL. v. COLIMA PROFESSIONAL BUILDING, LLC, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV01091

HEARING:  December 17, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

ADD-ON

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Attorney Michelangelo Tatone’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

 

Counsel to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a breach of lease action. Attorney Michelangelo Tatone of Tatone Law, APC, presently counsel for Defendants Colima Professional Building and Victor Fahmy Khalil, now moves to be relieved as counsel. The motion is unopposed as of December 13, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

Good cause exists to grant a motion to be relieved as counsel based on any of the grounds under Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3-700(c). Rule 3-700(c) provides that an attorney may withdraw based on any of the following: (1) The client (a) insists upon presenting a claim or defense that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law, or (b) seeks to pursue an illegal course of conduct, or (c) insists that the member pursue a course of conduct that is illegal or that is prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (d) by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively, or (e) insists, in a matter not pending before a tribunal, that the member engage in conduct that is contrary to the judgment and advice of the member but not prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (f) breaches an agreement or obligation to the member as to expenses or fees; (2) The continued employment is likely to result in a violation of these rules or of the State Bar Act; (3) the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal; (4) The member's mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively; (5) The client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the employment; or (6) The member believes in good faith, in a proceeding pending before a tribunal, that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal. 

 

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-052)); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-053)). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (d), (e).) 

 

Discussion

 

Attorney Michelangelo Tatone filed a Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. (See Form MC-051.) Attorney Tatone also filed a proposed order. (See Form MC-053.) Attorney Tatone’s declaration demonstrates good cause for withdrawal based on a breakdown of communication and the clients’ failure to comply with contractual obligations required for representation. (Tatone Decl., ¶ 2.) Attorney Tatone states that the clients were served copies of the motion papers by mail at the clients’ last known addresses, which were confirmed by telephone in the past thirty (30) days. (Tatone Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Accordingly, the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

CARDOZA v. CITY OF COMPTON

CASE NO.: 23NWCV00990

HEARING:  December 17, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

1)   Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel the Deposition of Blake David Jr. is GRANTED.

 

2)   Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Isaac Jacob Galvan is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiffs to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a wrongful death action. On March 30, 2023, Plaintiffs Elida Cardoza, Julio Cesar Cardoza, and Isaac Isaiah Galvan, through his guardian ad litem, Michelle Cardoza ("Plaintiffs") against Defendants City of Compton and David Blake Jr., (“Defendants”) and Does 1 to 50. The Complaint alleges Defendant David Blake, Jr. shot and killed Decedent Julio Moises Cardoza (“Decedent”) while in the course and scope of his employment with Defendant City of Compton (“Incident”). The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Negligence (Wrongful Death), 2) Battery (Wrongful Death), 3) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and 4) Violation of The Bane Act (Civ. Code § 52.1).

 

On May 17, 203, Defendant City of Compton filed a cross-complaint against Defendant David Blake Jr.

 

Plaintiffs now move to compel the depositions of Isaac Jacob Galvan, party affiliate, and David Blake Jr., Defendant. The motions are unopposed as of December 13, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

“The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (CCP § 2025.280 (a).) A deposition subpoena is not required to compel the attendance and testimony of a person who, although not a named party, is either an officer, director, managing agent or employee of a party. (CCP § 2025.280(a).)  

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action. . .without having served a valid objection. . .fails to appear for examination. . .the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony. . .The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document or tangible thing described in the deposition notice [and]. . .shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (CCP § 2025.450.)   

 

Discussion

 

Motion to Compel Deposition of Isaac Galvan

 

Plaintiffs move to compel the deposition of Isaac Jacob Galvan (“Galvan”), an employee of Defendant City of Compton. Plaintiffs seek to discover evidence regarding the Incident and his employment with Defendant City of Compton at the time of the Incident.

 

On May 20, 2024, Plaintiffs personally served Galvan with a Notice of Deposition and Deposition Subpoena for deposition on June 20, 2024. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. 1.) A few days prior to the deposition, Galvan asked Plaintiffs’ counsel for a continuance. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiffs’ counsel attempted to contact Galvan multiple times to reschedule the deposition but was unable to reach him because he refused to provide his phone number, and the email he provided was incorrect. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiffs’ counsel against noticed the deposition for August 15, 2024. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. 2.) Galvan failed to appear for the deposition. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiffs have properly noticed the deposition and attempted to reach out to the deponent to no avail. Accordingly, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of Issac Galvan.

 

Motion to Compel Deposition of David Blake Jr.

 

Plaintiffs move to compel the deposition of David Blake Jr. (Blake), through which Plaintiffs seek to discover evidence regarding the claims and defenses in this case.

 

On July 11, 2023, Defendant City of Compton (“Defendant City”) first noticed Blake’s deposition set for August 1, 2023. Plaintiffs served a cross-notice of Blake’s deposition. (Contreras Decl., ¶  3, Exh. 1.) Defendant City advised that Blake was evading service, and the deposition did not take place. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 4.) On February 15, 2024, Plaintiffs noticed Blake’s deposition, set for February 29, 2024, which was served via personal service. (Contreras Decl., ¶  5, Exh. 2.) On February 29, 2024, Blake failed to appear for his deposition. (Contreras Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiffs have properly noticed the deposition and attempted to reach out to the deponent to no avail. Accordingly, the Court grants the Motion to Compel Deposition of David Blake Jr.


Case Number: 23NWCV02095    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: C

GARCIA v. AMERICAN HONDA CORPORATION, INC.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV02095

HEARING:  December 17, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#8

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff Sandra Garcia’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, is CONTINUED to April 8, 2025 in Department SE-C at 10:30 a.m.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. On July 7, 2023, Plaintiff Sandra Garcia (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant American Honda Corporation, Inc. (“Defendant”). Plaintiff contends that Defendant violated the California Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“Song-Beverly Act”) by failing to repair or replace Plaintiff’s 2022 Acura MDX (“Subject Vehicle”). During the warranty period, the Subject Vehicle contained or developed numerous defects that Defendant’s authorized repair facility was unable to conform to warranty. 

 

On December 26, 2023, Plaintiff propounded the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, Nos. 1 to 101, on Defendant (“Requests”). (Grigoryan Decl. ¶ 19, Exh. 3; Moody Decl. ¶ 7, Exh. C.) On January 30, 2024, Defendant served responses containing objections to Plaintiff’s Requests. (Grigoryan Decl. ¶  20, Exh 4; Moody Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. D.)

 

Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production, set Two. Defendant opposes the Motion. No reply has been filed as of December 13, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP Section 2031.310 allows a party who has requested production of documents and received unsatisfactory responses to move for an order compelling further responses. A motion under this section must (1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand and (2) be accompanied by a meet and confer showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

 

Discussion

 

Timeliness

 

“Unless notice of this motion [to compel further] is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (c).) Failure to make motion within the specified period constitutes waiver of right to compel a further response. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410 (finding a late filed motion to compel further must be denied even if omitted in opposition papers).)

 

This Motion was filed on April 15, 2024. The Court finds that the issue of whether the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production, set Two, Nos. 1 to 101, was timely filed depends on when or whether Defendant served verified supplemental responses. (Opp., p. 3, lines 7-10.) On January 30, 2024, Defendant served its initial responses to Plaintiff’s Requests. (Grigoryan Decl. ¶  20, Exh 4; Moody Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. D.) In the opposition, Defendant states it “served its document production” in response to the Requests on March 12, 2024, although it cites to paragraph 10 of the “Vault Declaration” where there is no such declaration. (Opp., p. 3, line 7.) The Court notes the attached “Moody Declaration” states that Defendant served its document production in response to the Requests on December 4, 2024. (Vault Decl., ¶ 9.) Plaintiff’s Motion did not mention any supplemental responses.

 

The Court notes that if Defendant served supplemental verified responses on March 12, 2024, this Motion to Compel Further would be timely—it would have been filed 35 days after responses were filed, within the 45-day deadline. If Defendant only served initial verified responses on January 30, 2024, the Motion to Compel further would be untimely—it would have been filed 77 days after responses were filed—and the Court must deny the motion. Nevertheless, the Court will continue to address the issue of meet and confer efforts.

 

 

          Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain ‘an informal resolution of each issue.’ [Citations.] This rule is designed ‘to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order. . .’ [Citations.] This, in turn, will lessen the burden on the court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by litigants through promotion of informal, extrajudicial resolution of discovery disputes. . .[T]he law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435, 1439.)

 

On February 7, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding Defendant’s responses. (Grigoryan Decl. ¶  22, Exh. 5.) The letter explained the relevance of each request and why good causes exists to produce the documents. (Ibid.) On or around March 7, 2024, after having received no response from Defendant, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a further meet and confer letter in attempt to informally resolve the discovery dispute. (Grigoryan Decl. ¶  23, Exh. 6.) Plaintiff’s counsel addressed each objection and limited the discovery requests to Defendant’s internal investigation and analysis of the same or similar defects that occurred in the Subject Vehicle or in other vehicles of the same year, make, and model. (Ibid.) As of the motion’s filing date on March 15, 2024, Defendant had not provided all of the documents requested. (Grigoryan Decl. ¶  25.) Defendant does not address its meet and confer efforts in its opposition. Defendant merely suggests that Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts were “illusory” and “disengaged.” (Mot. p. 1, line 5; p. 3, line 14.)

 

The Court is not persuaded that counsel have exhausted their meet and confer obligations pursuant to the Code. The Court finds that the meet and confer efforts, if any, have been insufficient considering the number of outstanding discovery requests—specifically, 101 requests—at issue in the instant motion. The Court is hopeful that the Stipulation and Protective Order, signed and granted on February 2, 2024, will facilitate freer exchange of discovery between the parties.

 

Therefore, counsel is ordered to make further efforts to resolve the issues presented. If, after exhausting those efforts, court intervention is needed, counsel may appear and argue the merits on the continued hearing date. If counsel is unable to informally resolve their discovery disputes, then counsel is instructed to submit a joint separate statement with a detailed outline of the remaining disputed issues for which a ruling is required. The hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, is CONTINUED to April 8, 2025 in Department SE-C at 10:30 a.m. The joint separate statement must be FILED on or before April 1, 2025.

FINANCIAL PARTNERS CREDIT UNION v. INCHIZU

CASE NO.: 23NWCV02728

JUDGE: RAUL A SAHAGUN

 

#2

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff Financial Partners Credit Union’s Motion to Deem Request for Admissions (Set One) Admitted is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a debt collection action. On August 30, 2023, Plaintiff Financial Partners Credit Union (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Augusto Javier Inchizu aka Augusto Javier Arvizu aka Arvizu Augusto (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 50. The Complaint alleges the parties entered into Loan and Security Agreements and a Disclosure Statement (“Agreement”) on December 13, 2021. On or around February 6, 2023, Defendant allegedly defaulted on the loan, in which there was a remaining balance of $28,420.22 due and payable. Plaintiff seeks the balance due, interest, and attorneys’ fees and costs as provided by the Agreement.

 

Plaintiff now moves to deem the Requests for Admissions (Set One) admitted. The motion is unopposed as of December 13, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

Where there has been no timely response to a request for admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (b).) The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”¿ (Code of Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)¿¿

 

Discussion

 

On November 17, 2023, Plaintiff served a Request for Admissions (Set One) on Defendant. (Elder Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. 1.) The responses were due on December 23, 2023. (Elder Decl., ¶ 6.) On January 3, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel set a meet and confer letter to Defendant regarding the overdue responses, but counsel received no response. (Elder Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. 2.)

 

Given that Defendant has not responded to the Request for Admissions, the truth of the matters in the Request are deemed admitted. The Court grants the Motion to Deem Request for Admissions (Set One) Admitted.


Case Number: 23NWCV03464    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: C

SOTO v. FORD MOTOR COMPANY ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV03464

HEARING:  December 17, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#3

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff Francisco Soto’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production, Set Two is CONTINUED to April 22, 2025 in Department SE-C at 10:30 a.m.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. On October 27, 2023, Plaintiff Francisco Soto (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Ford Motor Company (“Defendant”), Ken Grody Ford, and Does 1 to 10. On August 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (FAC). Plaintiff contends that Defendant violated the California Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“Song-Beverly Act”) by failing repair or replace Plaintiff’s 2019 Ford Ranger (“Subject Vehicle”). During the warranty period, the Subject Vehicle contained or developed defects, including transmission defects, that Defendant’s authorized repair facility was unable to conform to warranty. 

 

On December 12, 2023, Plaintiff propounded the Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, on Defendant (“Requests”). (Smith Decl., ¶ 17, Exh. 5; Moawad Decl., ¶ 2.) On January 16, 2024, Defendant served its responses and objections to the Requests. (Smith Decl., ¶ 20-21, Exh. 6; Moawad Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.)

 

Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production, Set Two.

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP Section 2031.310 allows a party who has requested production of documents and received unsatisfactory responses to move for an order compelling further responses. A motion under this section must (1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand and (2) be accompanied by a meet and confer showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court overrules Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to certain paragraphs contained in the Declarations of Jodi Schebel and Kevin Payne.

 

Discussion

 

          Timeliness

 

“Unless notice of this motion [to compel further] is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (c).) Failure to make motion within the specified period constitutes waiver of right to compel a further response. (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410 (finding a late filed motion to compel further must be denied even if omitted in opposition papers).)

 

The Court finds the Motion was timely filed. Defendant served its responses and objections to the Requests on January 16, 2024. (Smith Decl., ¶¶  20-21, Exh. 6; Moawad Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Defendant verified its responses on February 24, 2024. (Moawad Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) This Motion was filed on March 11, 2024. The 45-day clock runs only upon service of verified responses, and responses consisting of both factual responses and objections must be verified. (Golf & Tennis Pro Shop, Inc. v. Super. Ct. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 127, 136.) This Motion was filed 17 days after verifications were given, and thus, the Motion is timely.

 

Meet and Confer

 

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain ‘an informal resolution of each issue.’ [Citations.] This rule is designed ‘to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order. . .’ [Citations.] This, in turn, will lessen the burden on the court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by litigants through promotion of informal, extrajudicial resolution of discovery disputes. . .[T]he law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435, 1439.)

 

On December 12, 2024, Plaintiff attempted to meet and confer regarding establishing a protocol for the discovery of electronically stored information (ESI). (Smith Decl., ¶ 26, Exh. 7.) On January 16, 2024, Defendant responded to the letter indicating broad discovery of ESI was not appropriate or proportional in this case but offered to meet and confer further on the issue. (Smith Decl., ¶ 27, Exh. 8.) On January 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter regarding demanding Defendant supplement all 88 requests for production and multiple special interrogatories, and reiterating its position on ESI discovery. (Smith Decl., ¶ 28, Exh. 9; Moawad Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) On February 20, 2024, Defendant responded with its own meet and confer letter, reiterating its objections and agreeing to supplement six responses (Nos. 23, 27-30, and 32). (Smith Decl., ¶ 29, Exh. 10; Moawad Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. C.) On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff sent a meet and confer email, dismissing Defendant’s offer to supplement its six responses and again demanding responses to all requests for production. (Smith Decl., ¶ 30, Exh. 11; Moawad Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. D.) Plaintiff filed this Motion three days later.

 

The Court is not persuaded that counsel have exhausted their meet and confer obligations pursuant to the Code. The Court finds that the meet and confer efforts have been insufficient considering the number of outstanding discovery requests—specifically, 25 requests—at issue in the instant motion. Merely stating one’s position in a meet and confer letter does not equate to meaningful deliberation of issues.

 

Therefore, counsel is ordered to make further efforts to resolve the issues presented, including the issue of discovery of ESI. If, after exhausting those efforts, court intervention is needed, counsel may appear and argue the merits on the continued hearing date. If counsel is unable to informally resolve their discovery disputes, then counsel is instructed to submit a joint separate statement with a detailed outline of the remaining disputed issues for which a ruling is required. Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production, Set Two is CONTINUED to April 22, 2025 in Department SE-C at 10:30 a.m. The joint separate statement must be filed on or before April 15, 2025.

BERMUDEZ v. NORWALK ARTS AND SPORTS COMPLEX, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV03886

HEARING:  December 17, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#4

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Attorney Armineh Yousefian’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is GRANTED.

 

Counsel to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a premises liability action. Attorney Armineh Yousefian, Esq., presently Counsel for Plaintiff Juan Bermudez, now moves to be relieved as counsel. The motion is unopposed as of December 13, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

Good cause exists to grant a motion to be relieved as counsel based on any of the grounds under Rules of Professional Conduct Rule 3-700(c). Rule 3-700(c) provides that an attorney may withdraw based on any of the following: (1) The client (a) insists upon presenting a claim or defense that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law, or (b) seeks to pursue an illegal course of conduct, or (c) insists that the member pursue a course of conduct that is illegal or that is prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (d) by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively, or (e) insists, in a matter not pending before a tribunal, that the member engage in conduct that is contrary to the judgment and advice of the member but not prohibited under these rules or the State Bar Act, or (f) breaches an agreement or obligation to the member as to expenses or fees; (2) The continued employment is likely to result in a violation of these rules or of the State Bar Act; (3) the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal; (4) The member's mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the member to carry out the employment effectively; (5) The client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the employment; or (6) The member believes in good faith, in a proceeding pending before a tribunal, that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal. 

 

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362 requires that the following be submitted in support of an attorney’s motion to be relieved as counsel pursuant Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2): (1) a notice of motion and motion directed to the client (made on Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-051)); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1) (made on Declaration in Support of Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-052)); (3) a proof of service evidencing service of the notice of motion and motion, declaration, and proposed order on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) a proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on Order Granting Attorney’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (Judicial Council Form, MC-053)). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (d), (e).) 

 

Discussion

 

Attorney Armineh Yousefian filed a Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel. (See Form MC-051.) Attorney Yousefian also filed a proposed order. (See Form MC-053.) Attorney Yousefian’s declaration demonstrates good cause for withdrawal based on a breakdown of communication such that Plaintiff’s counsel can no longer continue representing Plaintiff. (Yousefian Decl., ¶ 2.) Attorney Yousefian states that the client was served the motion papers by mail at the client’s last known address, which was confirmed by telephone in the past thirty (30) days. (Yousefian Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

Accordingly, the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is granted.


Case Number: 24NWCV00395    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: C

WILLIS v. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR, INC.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00395

HEARING:  December 17, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s Demurrer to the fifth cause of action for violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq. is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s Motion to Strike is MOOT.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. On February 8, 2024, Plaintiff Kathryn Willis (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 50. Plaintiff contends that Defendant violated the California Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“Song-Beverly Act”) by failing to repair or replace Plaintiff’s 2022 Honda Accord (“Subject Vehicle”). The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act § 1793.2(d), (2) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act § 1793.2(b), (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty, (4) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act - Breach of Implied Warranty, and (5) Violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq.

 

Defendant now demurs to the fifth cause of action for violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq. Defendant also moves to strike Prayers for Relief Nos. 3 to 10.

 

Legal Standard

 

          Standard for Demurrer

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

  

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245) 

 

          Standard for Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), and section 435.5, subdivision (a), require meeting and conferring “in person or by telephone.” 

 

Discussion

 

Fifth Cause of Action - Violation of Business and Professions Code §17200

 

California’s Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”) prohibits unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts or practices. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.) Section 17200's “unlawful” prong “borrows violations of other laws. . .and makes those unlawful practices actionable under the UCL.” (Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1383.) “[V]irtually any law or regulation—federal or state, statutory or common law—can serve as [a] predicate for a. . .[section] 17200 ‘unlawful’ violation.’” (Id.) “A business practice is “fraudulent” within the meaning of section 17200 if it is “likely to deceive the public.” (Id. at 1380.) A business practice is “unfair” within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive, or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits. (Nolte v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1407–1408.)

 

In order to plead a claim under Business and Professions Code section 17200, there must be allegations showing an unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice.¿(Paulus v. Bob Lynch Ford, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal. App. 4th 659, 676-677.) To plead this statutory claim, the pleadings must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.¿(Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal. App. 4th 612, 619.)¿

 

Defendant first argues Plaintiff lacks standing because the Complaint fails to allege that Plaintiff suffered an economic injury as a result of Defendant’s unlawful, unfair, and fraudulent business practices. The Court disagrees. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff suffered an injury in fact—specifically, that Plaintiff relied on Defendant’s misrepresentations or omissions, and Plaintiff would not have purchased the vehicle if the Subject Vehicle’s defects were known. (Compl., ¶ 59.) The Court finds there is no issue with Plaintiff’s standing under the UCL.

 

Next, Defendant argues the UCL claim fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action. Defendant argues the Complaint fails to allege any unlawful, fraudulent, or unfair practice with specificity. As to the fraudulent prong, the Complaint does not meet the heightened pleading standard. Defendant argues the Complaint does not allege any specific misrepresentations or omissions Plaintiff relied upon or demonstrate how such representations would likely deceive a reasonable consumer. As to the unfair prong, Defendant argues the Complaint fails to allege any immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous, or substantially injurious practice to consumers.

 

Plaintiff’s opposition does not address why the Complaint’s allegations are sufficient to state a cause of action under the UCL. Plaintiff’s opposition merely argues that Plaintiff may seek cumulative remedies under the UCL and breach of warranty claims.

 

The Court finds that the Complaint fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action under the UCL. In support of its allegation that Defendant violated the “fraudulent” prong of the UCL, the Complaint states that “the misrepresentations and omissions regarding the safety and reliability of its vehicles as set forth in this Complaint were likely to deceive a reasonable consumer, and the information would be material to a reasonable consumer.” (Compl., ¶ 56.) The Court finds such bare allegations are insufficient where claims of fraudulent conduct generally must be pled with heightened specificity. (See Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384 (rule of specifically pleading how, when, where, to whom, and by what means, misrepresentations were communicated, applies to affirmative misrepresentations, but not to fraudulent concealment). Even if the cause of action is based on fraudulent concealment, the Complaint does not put Defendant on notice of its alleged “omissions regarding the safety and reliability of its vehicles” because the Complaint fails to state what the Subject Vehicle’s defects are. (See Compl., ¶ 6.)

 

Further, the Complaint fails to identify any particular unfair or unlawful business practice. The most specific allegation of unfair conduct is Defendant’s alleged “manufacture and sale of vehicles with similar defects, and defendant’s failure to adequately investigate, disclose and remedy. . .” (Compl. ¶ 57.) But again, the Complaint does not describe how Defendant failed to disclose such defects or explain what the allege defects are. Overall, the Court finds the allegations for the fifth cause of action are conclusory and fail to state a cause of action under the UCL.

 

Accordingly, the Court sustains Defendant’s Demurrer to the fifth cause of action for violation of Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq. with leave to amend.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike Prayers for Relief Nos. 3 to 10 to the extent they are made under the fifth cause of action. (Mot., p. 6, lines 15-17.)

 

The Court initially notes that demurrers and motions to strike should be noticed and filed separately pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(b) and Code of Civil Procedure section 435. The Court will rule on the Motion but advises against this practice in the future.

 

Because the Demurrer to the fifth cause of action was sustained, the Court finds the motion to strike is moot.


Case Number: 24NWCV00839    Hearing Date: December 17, 2024    Dept: C

1)   

CUEVAS-ROBLES, ET AL. v. UBER TECHNOLOGIES, INC. ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00839

HEARING: December 17, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE: RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#6

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

1)   Uber Technologies, Inc., Rasier-CA, LLC, and Rasier, LLC’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay Litigation is GRANTED.

 

2)   Defendant Curtis Johnson’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay Litigation is GRANTED.

 

Moving parties to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. On March 20, 2024, Plaintiffs Rigoberto Cuevas-Robles (“Mr. Cuevas”) and Maria Auxilio De Lira De Cuevas (Mrs. Cuevas”) (“Plaintiffs”) filed this action against Defendants Uber Technologies, Inc. (“Uber”), Rasier-CA, LLC, Rasier, LLC (collectively, “Uber Defendants”), Curtis Doe (later amended to Curtis Johnson)(“Johnson”), and Does 1 to 100. The Complaint alleges Mr. Cuevas requested a vehicle through Uber, a ride-sharing app, at 6713 Templeton 24 Street, Huntington Park, Los Angeles, CA 90255 on May 2, 2022. The Complaint alleges Johnson picked up Mr. Cuevas in the scope of his employment with Uber as a driver. When Mr. Cuevas was exiting the vehicle at the conclusion of the ride, Johnson allegedly pushed Mr. Cuevas out of the vehicle, causing him to fall onto the ground and sustain injuries (“Incident”). The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Assault, 2) Battery, 3) Negligence, and 4) Loss of Consortium.

 

On May 8, 2024, Uber Defendants filed an answer to the Complaint. On August 28, 2024, Johnson filed an answer to the Complaint.

 

Uber Defendants and Johnson now move separately to compel arbitration and request to stay the action.

 

Legal Standard

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (CCP § 1281.2; see also Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿ 

 

¿“If a court of competent jurisdiction. . .has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (CCP § 1281.4.)¿¿ 

 

Discussion

 

1)   Defendant Uber’s Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

The parties dispute 1) whether there is a valid agreement that applies to the Plaintiffs, 2) whether Plaintiffs’ battery claims are covered by the agreement, and 3) whether right to compel arbitration was waived. The Court will discuss each issue in turn.

 

          Valid Agreement

 

Uber Defendants argue there is a valid agreement to arbitrate that can be applied to non-signatory Plaintiffs. Uber Defendants argue that Nathaly Cuevas, Uber account holder and Plaintiffs’ daughter, ordered the vehicle for Mr. Cuevas that is the subject of the Incident. (Nourmohammadi Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiffs concede to this fact. (Yazdi Decl., ¶ 3.) Uber Defendants argue this case is wrongly brought because Plaintiffs assert claims arising from the Incident, which are subject to an arbitration agreement consented to via her use of her Uber account. (Yu Decl., ¶¶ 8-11, Exhs. A-E.)

 

Uber Defendants argue that Plaintiffs, as non-signatories, are bound by the arbitration agreement firstly because a non-signatory may be estopped from refusing to arbitrate where the claims asserted are intertwined with the contractual obligations of the agreement. Specifically, Plaintiffs claims are intertwined with their daughter’s use of the Uber account. Secondly, Mr. Cuevas is a third-party beneficiary of the agreement—Mr. Cuevas benefitted from the agreement when he agreed to ride in the Uber vehicle. Lastly, both Plaintiffs had a pre-existing parent-child relationship with a signatory to the agreement. Uber Defendants assert that where there is a pre-existing relationship between a non-signatory and a party to the arbitration agreement, the non-signatory may be compelled to arbitrate the claim.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that a valid arbitration agreement does not exist between Plaintiffs and Uber Defendants. (Yazdi Decl., ¶¶ 12-13.) Plaintiffs do not dispute that an agreement exists between Plaintiffs’ daughter and Uber. Plaintiffs argue they are not third-party beneficiaries because the terms of the contract evidence no intent to benefit Plaintiffs as third parties.

 

As to the issue of whether Plaintiffs are bound as non-signatories, the Court initially finds Plaintiffs are not bound as third-party beneficiaries because the agreement evidences no intent to benefit them. (See Prouty v. Gores Technology Group (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1232 (“The test for determining whether a contract was made for the benefit of a third person is whether an intent to benefit a third person appears from the terms of the contract.”).) Nevertheless, the Court finds Plaintiffs are bound through the principles of estoppel and pre-existing relationships.

 

Equitable estoppel applies here. “[A] party may be estopped from asserting that the lack of his signature on a written contract precludes enforcement of the contract's arbitration clause when he has consistently maintained that other provisions of the same contract should be enforced to benefit him. . .[¶] A nonsignatory is estopped from refusing to comply with an arbitration clause ‘when it receives a “direct benefit” from a contract containing an arbitration clause.’” (Boucher v. Alliance Title Co., Inc. (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 262, 269 (citing International Paper Co. v. Schwabedissen Maschinen & Anlagen GMBH (4th Cir. 2000) 206 F.3d 411, 418 (finding equitable estoppel applied to preclude nonsignatory Plaintiff from arguing it was not bound to arbitration agreement with a signatory defendant)).) The Court finds that Mr. Cuevas accepted the benefits of the agreement between Uber and his daughter when he rode in the vehicle. Mr. Cuevas would not have benefitted from Uber’s services but for the agreement between Uber and his daughter. The Court finds Mr. Cuevas is estopped from arguing he is not bound to the agreement.

 

The principle of pre-existing relationships also applies to bind both Plaintiffs as parents for the signatory. A nonsignatory may be bound to arbitrate where “a preexisting relationship exist[s] between the nonsignatory and one of the parties to the arbitration agreement, making it equitable to compel the nonsignatory to also be bound to arbitrate his or her claim.” (County of Contra Costa v. Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 237, 242.) The Court finds Plaintiffs have a preexisting relationship to the signatory, their daughter. (Nourmohammadi Decl., ¶ 3.) Their daughter ordered the Uber for the benefit for her father, Mr. Cuevas. (Ibid.) Considering the claims by both Plaintiffs are so closely tied to the agreement made by their daughter, the Court finds it equitable to bind Plaintiffs to the agreement.

 

Finding that Plaintiffs are be bound by the agreement, Court now turns to the validity of the agreement between Nathaly Cuevas and Uber.

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, Arbitration Clauses are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

 

“With respect to the moving party’s burden to provide evidence of the¿existence¿of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court. (See¿Condee v. Longwood Management Corp.¿(2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218, 105 Cal.Rptr.2d 597 (Condee¿); see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference”].) Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges. [Citation.]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC¿(2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.)

 

Here, Uber Defendants have incorporated the Terms of the arbitration agreement in the Motion and have attached the original and updated agreements, including evidence that Nathaly Cuevas agreed. (Yu Decl., ¶¶ 8-12; Exhs. A, C, E; Mot. p. 9, lines 16-26.) The Court finds a valid agreement exists.

 

          Claims Covered by the Agreement

 

Plaintiffs argue, in the case the Court finds a valid arbitration agreement, that the battery-related causes of action are not arbitrable. Plaintiffs did not intend to agree to arbitration all tort claims that arise from Uber’s services. In the reply, Uber Defendants argue that the terms of the agreement state that “incidents or accidents resulting in personal injury. . .that you allege occurred in connection with your use of the Services” are covered by the agreement. (Yu Decl., ¶ 12; Exh. E.)

 

The Court finds that the battery claims are expressly covered by the arbitration agreement. Further, the fact that that an action involves tort claims does not bar valid arbitration against nonsignatories. “‘[C]laims framed in tort are subject to contractual arbitration provisions when they arise out of the contractual relationship between the parties.’ [Citations.] The same principle applies in cases involving nonsignatories: ‘That the claims are cast in tort rather than contract does not avoid the arbitration clause.’” (Molecular Analytical Systems v. Ciphergen Biosystems, Inc. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 696, 715–716.)

 

          Waiver

 

Plaintiffs argue Defendants waived their right to arbitration because Defendants participated in litigation—Defendants filed an answer, participated in a Case Management Conference, requested a jury trial and mediation, and participated in written discovery. (Yazdi Decl., ¶ 8, 10-11, Exhs. 2, 4.) In the reply, Uber Defendants argue their counsel attempted to reach out to Plaintiffs’ counsel on two occasions to discuss arbitration, but Plaintiffs disregarded such requests. (Nourmohammadi Decl., ¶¶ 4, 6.) Further, Uber Defendants have not propounded any discovery, produced documents, or designated experts.

 

“[T]he court shall [compel arbitration]. . .unless it determines. . .[t]he right to compel arbitration has been waived.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) A party seeking to prove a waiver may do so by showing “(1) knowledge of an existing right to compel arbitration; (2) acts inconsistent with that existing right; and (3) prejudice to the party opposing arbitration resulting from such inconsistent acts.” (Richards v. Ernst & Young, LLP (9th Cir. 2013) 744 F3d 1072, 1074 (internal quotes omitted).) In determining waiver, courts consider: whether the party demanding arbitration has taken action inconsistent with the right to arbitrate, whether litigation machinery “has been substantially invoked” and the parties “were well into preparation of a lawsuit” before the party demanding arbitration notified the opposing party of an intent to arbitrate, whether the party demanded arbitration close to the trial date or delayed for a long period before seeking a stay, whether a defendant seeking arbitration filed a counterclaim without asking for a stay of the proceedings, whether important intervening steps have taken place (e.g., taking advantage of judicial discovery procedures not available in arbitration), and whether the delay “affected, misled, or prejudiced the opposing party.” (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.App.4th 19, 31 (internal quotes omitted).)

 

The Court finds that Uber Defendants have not waived their right to arbitrate. Plaintiffs have not proven that Uber Defendants’ delay in seeking arbitration would result in prejudice against them. Uber Defendants’ have engaged in no discovery, and a trial date has not yet been set. Further, Uber Defendants attempted to meet with Plaintiff regarding arbitration and included arbitration as an affirmative defense in the answer. (Nourmohammadi Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Therefore, the parties were not “well into preparation of a lawsuit” before Uber Defendants notified Plaintiff of their intent to arbitrate. (See Wagner, supra, 41 Cal.App.4th at p. 31.) The Court thus finds there is no waiver at this juncture.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant Uber’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and request to stay the litigation.

 

2)   Defendant Curtis Johnson’s Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

Johnson argues he may compel arbitration as a non-signatory under the principles of agency and equitable estoppel. Johnson argues Plaintiffs have alleged that he acted as an agent of Uber, allowing him to invoke agency principles to compel arbitration as a nonsignatory. Johnson further argues that equitable estoppel allows him to compel arbitration where Plaintiffs’ claims against him are based on the same facts as the arbitrable claims against Uber Defendants. All of Plaintiffs’ causes of action against Johnson are asserted against Uber on the basis of vicarious liability, and thus, are inseparable. If the Court denies Johnson’s Motion to Compel Arbitration, Johnson requests the Court to stay the proceedings pending Plaintiffs’ arbitration with Uber.

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs assert many of the same arguments against Johnson as they did against Uber Defendants. Plaintiffs assert 1) there is no arbitration agreement between Plaintiffs and Johnson, 2) the battery claims are not arbitrable, and 3) Johnson waived his right to arbitration by participating in litigation.

 

In reply, Johnson notes that Plaintiffs did not address his arguments regarding agency and equitable estoppel. Further, Johnson argues he did not waive his right to arbitrate—Johnson only filed an answer and a case management statement, which stated his intention to file a motion to compel arbitration. Johnson has not propounded any discovery to Plaintiffs or otherwise engaged in the litigation process.

 

The Court agrees with Johnson. The Court finds that the agency and equitable estoppel exceptions to the general rule that nonsignatories may not enforce arbitration agreement apply here.

The agency principle applies “when a plaintiff alleges a defendant acted as an agent of a party to an arbitration agreement.” (Garcia v. Pexco, LLC (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 782, 788.) Here, the Complaint alleges the Johnson acted as an agent of Uber. (Complaint ¶ 19.) Further, Plaintiffs claims against Uber Defendants are asserted on the basis of Uber Defendants’ vicarious liability for the actions of Johnson. (Complaint ¶  11.) The Court accepts such allegations as true for the purposes of this Motion, and thus, the agency exception applies here.

 

The equitable estoppel exception applies where “the causes of action against the nonsignatory are intimately founded in and intertwined with the underlying contract obligations” and “where the claims are based on the same facts and are inherently inseparable from the arbitrable claims against signatory defendants.” (Garcia, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th at p. 786 (internal citations omitted).) Here, as mentioned previously, Plaintiffs’ claims against Uber Defendants are asserted on the basis of Uber Defendants’ vicarious liability for the actions of Johnson—the claims are thus inseparable. (Complaint ¶  11.) The Court finds the claims against Johnson are so “intertwined” with Plaintiffs’ daughter’s contractual agreement with Uber, which binds Plaintiffs, so as to make it equitable to require Plaintiffs to arbitrate their claims against Johnson. (See id.)

 

In sum, the Court finds Johnson may bind Plaintiffs to arbitration as a nonsignatory through the principles of agency and equitable estoppel. Accordingly, the Court grants Johnson’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Request to Stay.
