DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AVCV00238    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: W

CRYSTAL CLAYTONIA KARGES, et al. v. JOENDELL G. ABNER

 

defendant’s MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFFS’ FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS (SET ONE)

 

Date of Hearing:        May 22, 2024                        Trial Date:       March 3, 2025

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23AVCV00238

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Joendell G. Abner

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Crystal Claytonia Karges, individually and as the administrator of the estate of Farris Emile Tarazi, Farris Emile Tarazi, Jr., and Nadia Nalani Sheridan

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Kerr Decl. ¶¶5-7.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 1, 2023, Plaintiffs Crystal Claytonia Karges, individually and as the administrator of the estate of Farris Emile Tarazi, Farris Emile Tarazi, Jr., and Nadia Nalani Sheridan filed a complaint against Defendant Joendell G. Abner for wrongful death – negligence and survivor claims. Plaintiffs allege Decedent Farris Emile Tarazi was making a left turn when Defendant Joendell G. Abner, going southbound and driving his vehicle upwards of 100 mph, caused his vehicle to collide with Decedent’s vehicle. Defendant was engaged in a street race with another vehicle immediately prior to the collision. The force of the collision fatally injured Decedent.   

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Joendell G. Abner’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents (Set One) is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Joendell G. Abner moves the court for an order compelling Plaintiffs to provide further responses to the requests for production, Set One. Defendant also moves the court for an order that Plaintiffs and their counsel pay Defendant the sum of $3,760.00 for reasonable attorney fees incurred in connection with making this motion.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, on receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.

 

Defendant requested Plaintiffs produce (1) all documents related to any communications between plaintiffs and Defendant’s insurance carrier regarding the allegations in plaintiffs’ complaint; and (2) all documents related to any communications between plaintiffs and Defendant’s insurance carrier regarding the incident.

 

RPD 31 & 33 to Plaintiffs Crystal Claytonia Karges, Farris Emile Tarazi, Jr., and Nadia Nalani Sheridan

 

Defendants request for production of documents No. 31: All DOCUMENTS related to any COMMUNICATIONS between YOU and defendant Joendell Abner's insurance carrier regarding the allegations in YOUR COMPLAINT.

 

Defendant request for production of documents No. 33: All DOCUMENTS related to any COMMUNICATIONS between YOU and defendant Joendell Abner's insurance carrier regarding the INCIDENT.

 

Defendant contends there is good cause to produce the requested documents as any communications between Plaintiffs’ and Defendant’s insurance carrier regarding Plaintiffs’ allegations would contain information regarding Plaintiffs’ claims, the incident, and/or Plaintiffs’ alleged damages. Moreover, Plaintiffs have not produced the alleged policy limit demand they contend they have sent. The alleged policy limit demand will also assist Defendant in evaluating the potential exposure in this case.

 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs Crystal Claytonia Karges, Farris Emile Tarazi, Jr., and Nadia Nalani Sheridan’s objections regarding relevancy and admissibility lack merit. This document request is not vague, ambiguous, overbroad, or compound, so all those objections are unmeritorious. The document request does not seek attorney-client communications or attorney work-product, does not call for speculation, and does not call for premature disclosure of expert opinion, so all those objections are unfounded too. In addition, there is no invasion of Plaintiffs’ privacy regarding such information because Plaintiffs’ communications with a third party waive any privacy rights.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues none of the communications Defendant now seeks are relevant to any of Plaintiffs’ claims or Defendant’s affirmative defenses. Instead, Defendant’s requests for documents pertaining to pre-litigation discussions made between Plaintiffs and USAA relate to a possible bad faith claim against USAA. The requested documents are also inadmissible at the time of trial. Moreover, Defendant insists the requested communications are relevant yet at the same time objects that the information in USAA’s claims file is irrelevant. Defendant has even refused to produce the actual insurance policy in this case on the basis that it is irrelevant. Defendant cannot have it both ways. Either way, Plaintiffs agreed to enter into a stipulation with Defendant in which Plaintiffs would produce all communications between Plaintiffs and USAA provided Defendant produces a complete copy of the USAA claims file up to the time the litigation was filed.

 

The court finds good cause for further responses to RPD Nos. 31 and 33. Plaintiffs’ communications with USAA are relevant as such documents may contain information regarding Plaintiff’s allegations, the incident, and/or Plaintiff’s alleged damages. For example, plaintiff might have sent photographs of the incident or their injuries to the insurance company, which are no longer available or have been withheld by plaintiff in this case.   The standard is not whether the requested documents are admissible at trial but whether the requested documents will lead to discovery of admissible evidence. If Plaintiff wishes Defendants to produce USAA’s entire claim file, Plaintiff must bring their own motion for further responses.  

 

Accordingly, the court grants Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to RPD Nos. 31 and 33 with the limitation that a true settlement proposal or counter between plaintiff and the insurance company need not be produced as such communications are confidential.  Documents with evidentiary value (e.g., photos, medical records, witness statements, statement of the plaintiff) attached to any such proposal or counter proposal must be produced. 

 

RPD 32 & 34 to Plaintiff Estate of Farris Emile Tarazi

 

Defendant request for production of documents No. 32: All DOCUMENTS related to any COMMUNICATIONS between YOU and defendant Joendell Abner's insurance carrier regarding the allegations in YOUR COMPLAINT.

 

Defendant request for production of documents No. 34: All DOCUMENTS related to any COMMUNICATIONS between YOU and defendant Joendell Abner's insurance carrier regarding the INCIDENT.

 

Both parties make the same arguments as RPD 31 and 33.

 

As a result, the court grants Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to RPD Nos. 32 and 34.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2031.310(h).)  

 

Defendant requests $3,760 in reasonable attorney fees against Plaintiffs and their counsel. The court declines to impose sanctions against Plaintiffs at this time as both parties’ positions were substantially justified. 


Case Number: 23VECV04833    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: W

J. A. vs BLUE CROSS OF CALIFORNIA DBA ANTHEM BLUE CROSS

 

motion to compel further responses to demand for discovery and production, set one and motion for protective order

 

Date of Hearing:        May 22, 2024                                    Trial Date:       None Set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04833

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff J.A., a minor by and through his Guardian ad Litem, and Michael Allen filed a complaint against Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross for 1. Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; 2. Breach of Contract; and 3. Violations of Business & Professions Code Section 17200. Plaintiff alleges this action arises out of Anthem’s repeated wrongful and bad faith denials of inpatient hospital admission so that J.A. can have medically necessary brachial plexus (nerve repair) surgery. Plaintiff alleges J.A.’s surgery was cancelled because Anthem denied the required inpatient hospital admission in the intensive care unit (ICU) required for his surgery. As of the date of this complaint, J.A. is still waiting to have surgery.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross’ for Protective Order Regarding Plaintiff’s Demands for Production of Documents, Sets Nos. 1 and 2 is GRANTED as to RPD Nos. 28-30.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Demand for Discovery and Production, Set One, From Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross is GRANTED, IN PART.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff submits evidentiary objections to the declaration of Jennifer Macasieb submitted in support of its Motion for Protective Order Regarding Plaintiff’s Demands for Production of Documents, Set Nos. 1 and 2 as well as submitted in support of Defendant’s opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Demand for Discovery and Production, Set One.

 

Plaintiff’s objections 1 – 6 are OVERRULED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    DEFENDANT BLUE CROSS OF CALIFORNIA DBA ANTHEM BLUE CROSS FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER REGARDING PLAINTIFF’S DEMANDS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SETS NOS. 1 AND 2

 

Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross moves this court for a protective order against demands set forth in Plaintiff J.A.’s Demands for Production of Documents, Sets 1 and 2. Specifically, Anthem Blue Cross seeks a protective order (1) limiting Demands Nos. 5-12, 21, 38-44, 46, 47, 49-51, 54-56 and 68-79 to those Anthem Blue Cross policies and procedures relied upon in connection with the utilization management review of the pre-service request for an inpatient admission based on the Current Procedure Terminology (“CPT”) codes provided by the treating physician (i.e., 64912, 64913, 64861, 64856 and 64901) and (2) not requiring the production of any documents responsive to Demands Nos. 20, 22-24 and 28-30 at all.

 

The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  The court may make this determination pursuant to¿a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.  This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040.  (CCP¿§ 2017.020(a).)  “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each¿issue presented by the motion.”  (CCP¿§ 2016.040.)¿¿

 

The court shall restrict the frequency or extent of use of a discovery method provided in if it determines either of the following: (1) The discovery sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive; or (2)¿ The selected method of discovery is unduly burdensome or expensive, taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.¿¿ (CCP § 2019.030(a).)¿

 

Defendant argues RPD Nos. 5-12, 21, 38-44, 46, 47, 49-51, 54-56 and 69-78 (“policies and procedures”) are grossly overbroad and unduly burdensome as they cover at least eight distinct operational areas of Anthem Blue Cross – including inpatient utilization management pre-service review, claims, customer service, retrospective review, medical director/peer-to-peer review, grievances and appeals, training and compliance/accreditation (as well as all documents and communications relating thereto) – but also for all possible medical procedures, across all product lines, for all types of requests and claims and for a period of over 3 to 4 years. Defendant contends given the extraordinarily broad scope of the 34 document demands, it would be extremely time-consuming to identify, collect, review and produce all of the policies, procedures and related documents sought. Defendant contends for all eight of these operational areas, it would involve approximately 20 people and take a total of 192 hours. As such, the requests should be limited to those Anthem Blue Cross policies and procedures relied upon in connection with the utilization management review of the pre-service request for an inpatient admission based on the CPT codes provided by the treating physician here (i.e., 64912, 64913, 64861, 64856 and 64901).

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the document requests at issue seek information directly relevant to this case. Plaintiff contends the discovery requests are designed to discover the policies, guidelines, and practices that were used, or should have been used, in the handling, processing and/or investigation that was done in connection with Plaintiff’s request for benefits for the brachial plexus repair surgery and post-surgery admission to the ICU and the medical necessity review purportedly necessitated by the latter. Moreover, Defendant’s contention that there was a “peer to peer” review between Dr. Amirnovin and Dr. Meisel on June 20, 2023, purportedly discussing the CPT codes directly puts at issue Anthem’s practices regarding peer to peer reviews and establishes that the requested documents are relevant. Plaintiff goes on to argue Defendant has not made the required showing to entitle it to a protective order and moreover, the time period sought for the RPDs is reasonable and justified.

 

The court finds Defendant has demonstrated good cause for limiting the scope of Demands Nos. 5-7, 9, 41, 42, 46, 47, 49-51, and 54-55, as they  are grossly overbroad.   Request No. 5, as just one example, requests all policy manuals and any documents that set forth “company practices or policies regarding the handling, processing and/or investigation of requests for services or authorization for services (collectively “Manuals”)” without limitation to any services at issue in this case.  Nos. 6 and 7 are equally vague and  overbroad, as are the other requests listed above. Defendant has established that the searches would require multiple personnel and consume more than 190 hours of time.  Given the overbreadth of these requests, there is no justification for requiring defendant to expend this amount of time in response to these requests.   

 

The court finds that Requests No. 8, 10, 11, 12, 21, 38, 39, 40, 43, 44, 56, and 68-79 are narrowly enough drawn and relate to the procedure at issue in this case, such that defendant can answer this request without undue effort or expense, provided that they are narrowed to only require production of policies and procedures in effect at the time of the requested procedure in this case.

 

As for RPD Nos. 20, 22-24, and 28-30, Defendant argues these documents are overbroad, irrelevant and impede Defendant’s employee’s privacy right interests. According to Defendant, Demand Nos. 20, 22-24, seek private documents relating to employees of Anthem Blue Cross including information about employment agreements, compensation packages, performance evaluations, and any statistics relating to “reviews” worked on. The court agrees that defendant has established that its employees’ privacy rights outweigh any vague allegations set forth by plaintiff to support this extraordinarily overbroad request for information.  Plaintiff can ask at deposition of the person most knowledgeable or other person with knowledge as to whether there are financial or other incentives for denial of coverage and how such incentives work.

 

Concerning RPD Nos. 28-30, Defendant argues there is no indication that the burden to Anthem Blue Cross in having to identify, collect, review and produce all of this advertising, marketing and promotional material is outweighed by any benefit that this information would provide to any of the parties in this litigation. Defendant notes there is not even any allegation that Plaintiff or his parents saw any Anthem Blue Cross advertising or promotional materials.

 

The court agrees. The portions of the complaint Plaintiff references in opposition address the contract between the parties, and not any advertising or promotional materials. As such, the court finds such request will not lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross for Protective Order Regarding Plaintiff’s Demands for Production of Documents, Sets Nos. 1 and 2 is GRANTED in part and denied in part.

 

II.                  PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO DEMAND FOR DISCOVERY AND PRODUCTION, SET ONE, FROM DEFENDANT BLUE CROSS OF CALIFORNIA DBA ANTHEM BLUE CROSS; AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS AGAINST ANTHEM AND ITS COUNSEL IN THE AMOUNT OF $1,000.00

 

Plaintiff moves the court for an order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300 that compels Defendant Blue Cross of California dba Anthem Blue Cross to provide further verified responses and production of documents to Plaintiff’s first set of demands for production. Specifically, Plaintiff seeks an order requiring Anthem to provide further responses which: (1) Remove the Preliminary Statement and General Objections as they are not provided for or authorized by the Code of Civil Procedure; (2) Remove all specific objections including but not limited to objections contending

that requests are vague and ambiguous, overly burdensome, and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence; and (3) Agree to produce all non-privileged responsive documents and information within Anthem’s possession, custody or control.

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) It is not necessary for the motion to show that the material sought will be admissible in evidence. “Good cause” may be found to justify discovery where specific facts show that the discovery is necessary for effective trial preparation or to prevent surprise at trial. (See Associated Brewers Dist. Co. v. Superior Court (1967) 65 Cal.2d 583, 586-588; see also CCP §§ 2017.010, 2019.030(a)(1).)

 

Plaintiff argues Anthem’s responses to Request Nos. 4-12, 19-24, 27-30, 34, 37-44, 46-51, 53-56, and 60-62 contain only boilerplate and general objections while the responses to Request Nos. 1-3, 13-18, 31- 33, 57-59, and 63-68 are not code-compliant.

 

RFP Nos. 1-4, 56

 

This category of documents seeks information specifically relating to plaintiff (RFP 1), communications about plaintiff, including those between Anthem and plaintiff’s parents and his providers (RFP 2), plaintiff’s policy and application/enrollment form (RFP 3 & 4), and representations to members with brachial plexus injuries about what treatments or services are covered for a brachial plexus injury (RFP 56).

 

Plaintiff argues these requests should be uncontroversial and are necessarily relevant. Particularly, given that in his declaration Dr. Amirnovin purports to have communicated with Dr. Stevanovic’s office on several occasions and he admits that plaintiff’s parents directly communicated with Anthem’s Blue Cross’ Solution Central.

 

Defendant argues it has already produced all non-privileged documents to RFP 1-3 and for RFP 4, Defendant argues Plaintiff has made no effort to explain why these documents are relevant to this case.

 

The court finds good cause to compel further responses. Defendant cannot unilaterally limit the production to Plaintiff’s request for preauthorization of an ICU admission following a “brachial plexus repair surgery”. Communications about Plaintiff as well as Plaintiff’s policy may lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, similarly with representations made about brachial plexus injuries.

 

RFP Nos. 5-17, 21, 38-51, 57-68

 

This category of documents seeks the manuals, policies and procedures used or which should have been used to process the request for the in hospital admission to the ICU, including revisions to the same (RFPs 5-8), documents and communications utilized in evaluating grievances or appeals (RFP 9), training materials and guidelines for the individuals involved in the review of plaintiff’s request for benefits (RFPs 10-12, 21), documents relied upon in reaching the conclusions presented in the denial letters at issue in this case (RFP 8, 13, 57, 60, 63, 66), as well as all screenshots reflecting actions done and entries made or viewed during the investigation resulting in said denial letters (RFPs 14-15, 58-59, 61-62, 64-65, 67-68), the file containing all work done and information considered in investigating and processing plaintiff’s request for benefits, including medical records and communications (RFPs 16-17), documents regarding evaluating requests for in hospital admission to the ICU immediately following surgery (RFPs 38-40) as well as for evaluating “medical necessity” (RFPs 49-51) and Anthem’s policies for coverage of in hospital admission to the ICU immediately following surgery (RFPs 41-44, 46-47) and its communications with regulators regarding the same (RFP 48).

 

Plaintiff argues it is long-established law that claims handling policies, procedures, and guidelines are relevant and discoverable in bad faith cases. Here, the requested documents are directly relevant to plaintiff’s allegations that Anthem has implemented and designed a utilization review system that prevents a full, fair, and thorough investigation before denying coverage, that Anthem’s failure to authorize the in hospital admission to the ICU breached the contract, and that Anthem’s business practices, including its utilization of software interfaces which prevent full, fair, and thorough investigations, constitute acts of unfair, unlawful, misleading, and fraudulent business practices as set forth in Business & Professions Code section 17200 et seq. Further, the requested documents are necessary to establish that Anthem’s mishandling of the request for benefits in this case was part of Anthem’s pattern and practice of failing to investigate and properly handle claims.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues these are extraordinarily overbroad and are pending Defendant’s motion for protective order. Moreover, Defendant has already produced all non-privileged, responsive documents reasonably believed to be relevant to the specific pre-service request at issue. As the court has denied the motion for protective order as to 8, 10, 11, 12, 21, 38-40, 43, 45, 68-79, but with the limitations set forth above, the motion to compel further responses is GRANTED as to those demands.

 

The court notes Plaintiff seeks further responses to additional demands (RFP Nos. 13-17, 48, and 57-68). There, Defendant contends it has provided a code-compliant statement that it will produce the documents requested, in part. Moreover, Anthem Blue Cross’s responses reflect a good faith effort to provide Plaintiff with the subset of non-privileged documents that specifically relate to the pre-service request at issue, while preserving its objections to the remainder of the overly broad demands. Specifically as to RFP No. 60 – 62, Defendant argues these demands seek documents relating specifically to a June 15, 2023 denial letter. However, there was no denial of coverage or a denial letter on that date.

 

In reply, Plaintiff contends Defendant’s production is incomplete or unilaterally narrows what it is producing. The parties must work together to determine what needs to be produced and if Defendant cannot fully produce the requested documents, Defendant must provide a code compliant response stating why it cannot. As noted above, documents outside of Plaintiff’s preservice request may lead to discovery of admissible evidence. As to RFP No. 60 – 62, if this letter has never been in Defendant’s possession, Defendant must comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230.

 

RFP Nos. 18-20, 22-24

 

This category of documents relate to Anthem’s employees and/or agents who were involved in the utilization review process and/or in making decisions on Anthem’s behalf regarding the request for in hospital admission to the ICU post-surgery.

 

Plaintiff argues the requested documents are directly relevant to plaintiff’s claims against Anthem for bad faith and violation of the UCL claim, as well as his claim for punitive damages.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues these are unequivocally personal information relating to non-parties. Applying the required balancing test, Plaintiff has utterly failed to demonstrate how the significant and undeniable privacy rights of Anthem Blue Cross’s employees are outweighed by any need. Specifically, as to RFP No. 19, Plaintiff has made no effort to explain why any written job descriptions for everyone who was involved in the handling of Plaintiff’s pre-service request for the inpatient admission at issue – from customer service representatives taking calls to the medical directors reviewing the request and making the ultimate coverage decision and everyone in between.

 

As previously noted, Defendant has adequately shown that Defendant’s employee’s privacy rights outweigh the importance of the requested discovery when it involves direct allegations by Plaintiff.   

 

The court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RFP Nos. 18-20, 22-24.

 

RFPs 27 & 37

 

This category of document requests letters denying coverage for in hospital ICU admission to other members diagnosed with brachial plexus injuries (RFP 37) and decisions by any regulators regarding insureds requests for in hospital admission to the ICU immediately following brachial plexus repair surgery (RFP 27) since Jan. 1, 2020.

 

The court agrees that provision of this information would invade the privacy rights of patients and violates HIPAA.   The request also is overly burdensome.  The court would allow discovery of the numbers of patients seeking pre-admission approval for brachial plexus injuries and the number approved or denied, but this would have to be presented through a different discovery vehicle. 

 

RFPs 53-55

 

This category of documents seeks information regarding Anthem’s policies and practices related to “peer to peer” reviews.

 

Plaintiff argues in this case, Anthem alleges that there was a “peer to peer” review between Dr. Amirnovin and Dr. Meisel on June 20, 2023. This allegation directly puts at issue Anthem’s policies and practices regarding peer to peer reviews and establishes that the requested documents are relevant or, at a minimum, reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

The court finds good cause to compel further responses. Defendant objects on the grounds the requests are irrelevant, overbroad, seek confidential information, and so forth. However, information about the peer to peer reviews are relevant and may lead to discovery of admissible evidence. Moreover, Anthem fails to articulate why compliance would be unduly burdensome, difficult, or expensive or what trade secrets or proprietary or sensitive financial information is purportedly at issue. Defendant may also designate the documents as confidential pursuant to the protective order.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further as to RFP Nos. 53 to 55 is GRANTED.

 

RFPs 28-30

 

These requests seek documents relating to Anthem marketing activities. As noted in the motion for protective order, Plaintiff has failed to show good cause for these requests. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RFPs 28-30 is DENIED.

 

RFPs 31-34

 

These requests seek documents relating to Anthem’s Answer including defenses pertaining to Anthem’s contentions of comparative fault and plaintiff’s claimed damages.

 

Defendant argues it has already produced all non-privileged, responsive documents reasonably believed to be relevant to the specific pre-service request at issue as to RFP 31 – 33 and as for RFP 34, Anthem Blue Cross has already produced all relevant documents and when ordered by this court, it will also provide a listing of trial exhibits. However, Defendant will not provide a response to a discovery request seeking to know which documents Anthem Blue Cross intends to use at trial.

 

As noted above, Defendant cannot choose to limit the responses to pre-service requests as Plaintiff has shown that documents outside the pre-service request may lead to discovery of admissible evidence. As for RFP No. 34, Defendant is not required to identify which documents it intends to use at trial, as that requests violates the attorney work product privilege at this stage.    

 

Objections

 

Plaintiff also seeks to overrule Defendant’s objections. The court has addressed many of Plaintiff’s requests above and will not address them here. As for the omnibus objections and preliminary statement and general objections, the court OVERRULES the objections.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) provides for mandatory monetary sanctions “against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (CCP § 2031.310(h).) 

 

Plaintiff requests $1,000 in sanctions against Anthem as Plaintiff provided Anthem with detailed authority on why its requests were proper but Anthem still refuses produce documents central to plaintiff’s bad faith case.

 

In opposition, Defendant also requests $1,000 in sanctions against Plaintiff on the grounds Plaintiff did not properly meet and confer at all regarding certain demands.

 

The court DENIES both requests for sanctions.   

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV05696    Hearing Date: May 22, 2024    Dept: W

ANA MARIA SERNA CHAVEZ, ET AL. VS CITY OF LOS ANGELES, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANT ALYAZEED MOAWYA DOBI’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT

 

Date of Hearing:        May 22, 2024                                                 Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        23VECV05696

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Alyazeed Moawya Dobi

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Ana Maria Serna Chavez and Estate of Noe Antonio Lopez

 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 26, 1023, Plaintiffs Ana Maria Serna Chavez and Estate of Noe Antonio Lopez filed a complaint against Defendant City of Los Angeles and Alyazeed Moawya Dobi. Plaintiff alleges Defendant Dobi collided with his vehicle into Decedent Noe Antonio Lopez, a pedestrian. After the collision, Defendant Dobi and other witnesses called for an ambulance. Plaintiff alleges LAPD and paramedics assumed the duty of providing reasonable assistance to the injured party, and also represented that they were investigating and looking for the injured party. Unfortunately, no investigation was done, no report was made, and no search was conducted by Defendant City of Los Angeles personnel. The following day, Decedent Noe Antonio Lopez was found dead near the location of the subject incident.

 

Request for entry of default against Defendant Alyazeed Moawya Dobi was entered April 5, 2024.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendant Alyazeed Moawya Dobi’s Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Alyazeed Moawya Dobi moves the court for an order requested that the default against him be set aside on the grounds of mistake and/or excusable neglect under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b).

 

Relief under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) is either discretionary or mandatory. A motion for mandatory relief must be made no more than six months after entry of judgment and be accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to the attorney’s “mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect.” (CCP § 473(b).) The attorney affidavit of fault must contain a “straight forward admission of fault.” (State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. v. Pietak¿(2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 600, 610.) But it need not contain an explanation of the reasons for the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence surprise or neglect. (Martin Potts & Assocs., Inc. v. Corsair, LLC (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 432, 438-441.) 

 

Relief must be granted “unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Ibid.) If mandatory relief is granted, the court must¿“direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs” to the opposing counsel or parties. (CCP § 473(b).) 

 

Where a party cannot obtain an attorney affidavit of fault, the party may seek discretionary relief under section 473(b) due to “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (CCP § 473(b).) A motion for discretionary relief must be made “within a reasonable time but in no instance exceeding six months after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (Id.) If discretionary relief is granted, the court may in its discretion order the moving party to pay the costs, including attorney fees, incurred in obtaining the default. (Rogalski v. Nabers Cadillac¿(1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 816, 823;¿Vanderkous v. Conley¿(2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 111, 118-119.) If the motion for discretionary relief is granted, the court may order the offending attorney to pay monetary sanctions up to $1,000 to opposing parties, or up to $1,000 to the State Bar Client Security Fund, or “[g]rant other relief as is appropriate.” (CCP § 473(c)(1)(A), (B), (C).) 

 

A motion for relief under section 473(b) “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted. . .” (CCP § 473(b).) However, this requirement is not jurisdictional; substantial compliance may suffice. (Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi¿(2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403 [finding substantial compliance where counsel offered proposed answer at motion¿hearing¿rather than serving it with moving papers].)¿ 

 

Defendant Dobi argues Defendant’s failure to timely file responsive pleadings resulted purely from Defense counsel’s failure to calendar a deadline, as opposed to Defendant’s desire to avoid or delay trial.  (Johnstone Decl. ¶9.) If not for the calendaring error, Defendant would have timely filed a responsive pleading. (Johnstone Decl. ¶9.) Defendant was served via substituted service on February 28, 2024. On March 10, 2024, Counsel was retained to defend Defendant. (Johnstone ¶4.) However, mistakenly, the deadline to file responsive pleadings was not calendared and thus, defense failed to timely respond, leading to Plaintiff’s request for entry of default against Defendant. (Johnstone Decl. ¶6.) Defense counsel was also calling Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss a possible settlement, and after engaging in settlement negotiations, Plaintiff informed Defense counsel they were still considering whether to accept the offer. (Johnstone Decl. ¶¶10, 11.) However, on April 8, 2024, Defendant informed that he was served Plaintiffs request for entry of default. (Johnstone Decl. ¶12.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant’s failure to timely file an answer is not an excusable mistake. Defendant's knowledge of the complaint and deadline to file an answer is evidenced by a call between Straus Meyers, LLP and Plaintiff on March 21, 2024. Plaintiff should not bear the consequences of Defendant’s oversight or delay in fulfilling this obligation, especially when such actions fall within the Defendant's sphere of responsibility. Moreover, Defendant failed to include with his notice to set aside the default a copy of the responsive pleading proposed to be filed in this action.

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to set aside default. Defendant seeks relief pursuant to the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b). Additionally, the motion is timely. The motion was made one week after request for entry of default was entered. Defendant did not provide a copy of the proposed answer; however, as noted above, substantial compliance may suffice. (Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi¿(2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403 [finding substantial compliance where counsel offered proposed answer at motion¿hearing¿rather than serving it with moving papers].)¿As such, Defendant is ordered to provide a copy of the proposed answer at the hearing on this motion.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED pursuant to Defendant’s production of the proposed answer.
