Case Number: 21STCV07948    Hearing Date: October 15, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL 

Hearing Date: 10/15/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV07948 GABRIELA SABET-BAKTASH vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL IS GRANTED

 

On March 1, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present trip and fall case. Trial is currently set for November 14, 2024. Plaintiff requests a short continuance to December 2, 2024, because she has a trial in Berlin, Germany, on November 13, 2024. The Berlin case, titled Sabet v. Holzmuller, is set for trial on November 13, 2024, at the Landgericht Potsdam Court in Berlin, and requires Plaintiff’s in-person testimony. Plaintiff attempted to reschedule the Berlin trial but was informed that the date is firm and will not be continued. After discussing this with opposing counsel, Plaintiff proposed a December 2, 2024, trial date in Los Angeles, but opposing counsel raised concerns about a conflict with her prepaid vacation at the end of December and requested a continuance to June 2025.

The Court finds good cause to grant a short continuance due to Plaintiff’s required attendance at the trial in Germany. However, given that the trial of this slip and fall case should not take more than a couple weeks to try, and the case has been pending since 2021, the Court declines the parties' request to continue the trial to June 2025, and instead sets trial for December 2, 2024.

Moreover, the only basis for the continuance is Plaintiff’s trial in Germany. The Court thus denies Plaintiff’s request to reopen discovery, as no justification was provided.

The new trial date is set for December 2, 2024, at 8:30 a.m. The Final Status Conference is continued to November __, 2024, at 10:00 a.m. All other case-related deadlines will not follow the new trial date.

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES AND TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES 

Hearing Date: 10/15/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV21084 GALINA KOPELEV vs CHURCHILL CONDOMINIUM ASSOCIATION et al.

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: THE COURT ADOPTS DISCOVERY REFEREE’S RECOMMENDATION

 

On August 14, 2024, the Court appointed Hon. Ann Kough (Ret.) as Discovery Referee under CCP 639 to address issues arising from each party’s initial discovery requests, which resulted in motions to compel filed by both parties. The Referee heard the parties’ arguments regarding the outstanding discovery and submits the following recommendations to the Court:

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Ruling: MOTION TO SEAL IS CONTINUED

 

Law re Sealing of Documents

 

Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open to the public, pursuant to a potent “open court” policy undergirded by the First Amendment and favoring the public nature of court proceedings. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c); see NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1199-10.) Consequently, pleadings, motions, discovery documents, and other papers may not be filed under seal merely by stipulation of the parties; filing under seal requires a court order.1 (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(a); see H.B. Fuller Co. v. Doe¿(2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 879, 888.)

 

A sealing order must be sought by means of a motion (or application) and accompanied by a memorandum of points and authorities, as well as evidence and testimony containing facts sufficient to justify the mandatory findings required to support a sealing order. (Cal. Rules of Court, rules 2.550(d) and 2.551(b).) The proponent of the sealing order must also conditionally lodge the unredacted matter to be sealed with the court. (Id., rule 2.551(b)(4).)

 

In order to issue a sealing order, the Court must expressly find that: (1) there exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) the overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) a substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) no less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest. (CRC 2.550(d)(1)-(4); CRC 2.550(e); McGuan v. Endovascular Techs., Inc. (2010) 182 Cal.App.4th 974, 988.) In determining whether to seal records, courts must weigh constitutional requirements for disclosure against such factors as privacy rights. (People v. Jackson (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1009, 1026-27.) The Court exercises its discretion in making a determination on a motion to seal. (Universal City Studios, Inc. v. Superior Court (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1273, 1285.)

 

Unless the court orders otherwise, any party that already has access to the records to be placed under seal must be served with a complete, unredacted version of all papers as well as a redacted version. Other parties must be served with only the public redacted version. If a party's attorney but not the party has access to the record, only the party's attorney may be served with the complete, unredacted version. CRC 2.551(b)(2)

 

Discussion

 

Although notice of the motion and redacted versions of the documents were served on opposing counsel, Plaintiff failed to serve the unredacted versions on opposing counsel as required under California Rules of Court, Rule 2.551(b)(2). Rule 2.551(b)(2) explicitly mandates that both redacted and unredacted versions of the documents be served on opposing counsel to allow a meaningful opportunity for counsel to assess the redactions.  Likewise, the Court needs such unredacted versions readily available to it. In this case, Plaintiff seeks to redact portions of 23 documents, and it is unreasonable to expect the Court or opposing counsel to conduct an exhaustive search through the record to identify exactly what was redacted.    

Plaintiff argues that she seeks to redact personal medical records, reproductive history, and mental health evaluations. The Court recognizes this as a legitimate, recognized basis for redaction.  In Oiye v. Fox (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1070, the court was confronted with a similar decision and the motion to seal was granted because the medical records at issue were tangentially related to the litigation. The plaintiff in Oiye sought to seal records in a sexual abuse case, including disputes with her health insurer and a personal diary about her treatment for anxiety, depression, and eating disorders. These records were not central to the case, as the main issue was whether the defendant had sexually molested the plaintiff. In contrast, here, Plaintiff’s medical condition and history are directly related to the claims and defenses in this medical malpractice action. As such, Plaintiff’s medical records are not tangentially related but are central to the litigation, making broad redactions likely inappropriate.  Thus, again, providing the Court with clear guidance on what Plaintiff seeks redacted is necessary. 

Furthermore, Plaintiff’s requests, on their face, do not appear to be narrowly tailored to seal only sensitive medical information. For example, Plaintiff seeks to redact the title for the second cause of action and, at times, entire pages of the complaint. Based on a brief review of the unredacted Complaint, the Court does not believe that the requested redactions of the Complaint are narrowly tailored. 

The documents Plaintiff seeks to redact, including the complaint, Plaintiff’s Notice of Motion and Motion to Compel Further Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set No. Two, the Informal Discovery Conference Form for Personal Injury Courts, and various attachments to Defendant's Motion for Summary Judgment, have all already been filed in the public domain. As the court in Hurvitz v. Hoefflin (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 1232, 1245, held, "there can be no privacy with respect to a matter which is already public or which has previously become part of the ‘public domain.’" Once the information has been released, it cannot be recaptured and sealed, unlike a physical object.

Nonetheless, the Court recognizes Plaintiff’s desire to keep her medical records and history out of the public domain, even if they have been part of that domain.  Accordingly, the Court is willing to make the appropriate findings and seal documents that warrant it.  For that purpose, the Court continues this motion to give Plaintiff an opportunity to ensure that her request is narrowly tailored and to provide an exhibit containing unredacted documents, which can be filed under seal.

MOTIONS FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

Hearing Date: 10/15/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV03287 KATHRYN LEWIS vs. ZOE DIMME-ALEXEFF, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Los Angeles County and Total Barricade Services 

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTIONS FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT ARE GRANTED 

 

Background

On September 26, 2021, Plaintiff was participating in the bicycle portion of the Triathlon on a designated racecourse along Pacific Coast Highway (PCH) in Malibu. Approximately 2000 feet southeast of the intersection of PCH and Guernsey Drive,  Defendant Alexeff made an illegal U-turn, colliding with Plaintiff, who was riding in the separated bike lane on the highway. Defendants Los Angeles County and Total Barricade Services were responsible for implementing the Traffic Control Plan to divert traffic and ensure the safety of triathlon participants. Both Defendants now move for summary judgment, arguing that Plaintiff signed waivers releasing them from any liability for negligence related to the event. In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants were grossly negligent, which is not covered by the waiver.

Legal Standard

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) 

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

·        Plaintiff was unable to avoid the collision with Defendant Alexeff’s vehicle, resulting in Plaintiff falling off her bicycle and allegedly sustaining injuries.

·        The Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department investigated the incident. Deputy Paul Ferreira’s body-worn camera recorded an interview with Alexeff at the scene.

·        Defendant Alexeff was cited for violations of Vehicle Code § 21651 (unlawful U-turn) and § 2818 (traveling over a cone pattern).

Total Barricade Services

·        Total Barricade Service, Inc. ("TBS") is a traffic control contractor that has provided services for the Malibu Triathlon since 2003, including for the 2021 Malibu Triathlon, organized by Malibu Triathlon Productions, LLC.

This provided cyclists with both a vehicle lane and a bike lane for use, while vehicles could continue using the number 1 lane unless otherwise noted.

·        Around May 18, 2021, Plaintiff registered for the Malibu Triathlon online.

·        Plaintiff, like all other participants, was required to and did fill out and electronically sign the USAT Membership Agreement and Waiver, as well as the Malibu Tri Agreement and Waiver, during registration for the 2021 Malibu Triathlon. These waivers included a clause where Plaintiff waived her negligence claim against Defendant.

Plaintiff saw Defendant Alexeff make the U-turn but was unable to avoid the collision. (Exhibit “B,” Traffic Collision Report (TCR), p. 4; Exhibit “D,” LASD Body Cam Video; Decl.   • Defendant Alexeff admitted to knowingly making the illegal U-turn, disregarding two sets of double yellow lines and a cone pattern, which were designed to prevent her from entering the racecourse. (Exhibit "B," TCR p. 4; Exhibit "D," LASD Body Cam Video; Decl. Scott Shean ¶ 13; Decl. Paul Ferreira ¶ 7.)

 

Event Organization and Traffic Control Plan:

 

  • The event organizers, Malibu Triathlon Productions, LLC, Generic Events, and USA Triathlon ("Triathlon Defendants"), contracted with Total Barricade Services, LLC to prepare and implement the Traffic Control Plan for the bike portion of the Triathlon. (Exhibit “E.”)
  • The Traffic Control Plan was approved by Caltrans, confirming that the measures were appropriate for the event. (Exhibit "F.")
  • The Traffic Control Plan was provided to Los Angeles Sheriff Detective Shean, who was responsible for ensuring deputies performed traffic control per the approved plan. (Decl. Shean ¶ 5.)
  • Detective Shean prepared an Event Action Plan (EAP), which placed Los Angeles Sheriff’s Deputies at 14 key Fixed Traffic Control Posts along the closed racecourse.
    (Decl. Shean ¶¶ 7-8; Exhibit “G” (EAP).)

 

Waivers Signed by Plaintiff:

 

  • Plaintiff electronically signed two waivers that expressly waived and released the County from liability, including for any negligence arising from the event. (Exhibit “I” (Waivers).)

Defendant has met its burden by demonstrating that

 

Defendant Alexeff made an illegal U-turn, which directly resulted in the collision with Plaintiff. Additionally, the Traffic Control Plan, approved by Caltrans, was properly implemented in accordance with the event's requirements. Furthermore, Plaintiff signed waivers explicitly releasing the County and other defendants from liability, including for any negligence arising from the event. Defendant’s evidence shows that Defendant adhered to the approved safety measures, the accident was caused by Defendant Alexeff and Plaintiff voluntarily waived her right to pursue claims of negligence, thereby establishing that Defendant is not liable for the incident.

Total Barricade Service, Inc

TBS has similarly met its burden by showing the following:

  • TBS obtained the necessary permits for the triathlon cycling course from both Caltrans and the City of Malibu.
    (Caltrans Permit, COE Ex. 4; City of Malibu Permit, COE Ex. 5.)
  • The permits were issued after Caltrans reviewed and approved the detailed Traffic Control Plan ("the Plan") submitted by TBS, which outlined the 2021 Triathlon's route. (Becerril Decl. ¶¶ 6-9.)
  • In accordance with the approved Traffic Control Plan, TBS placed orange cones along both sides of PCH, blocking off the outer lanes (usually the number 2 lane) nearest the designated bike lanes. This setup provided cyclists with both a vehicle lane and a bike lane to use. (Becerril Decl. ¶¶ 7-8.)
  • TBS also installed "No U-turn" signs and cones, and law enforcement officers were posted at key intersections to manage traffic and ensure safety. (Traffic Control Plan, COE Ex. 2; Becerril Decl. ¶ 8.)

Triable Issue of Fact

Plaintiff, in her opposition, does not dispute that she signed the waivers releasing both the County and TBS from liability for negligence arising out of the triathlon. However, Plaintiff argues that Defendants were grossly negligent, which is not covered by the waiver as a matter of public policy, thereby raising a triable issue of fact. (City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal.4th 747, 777.) Plaintiff essentially makes this exact argument and relies on the same evidence to oppose both Defendants' motions for summary judgment.

Plaintiff contends that Defendants were grossly negligent because only one out of the three required "No U-turn" signs was posted in the area of the incident. Plaintiff points to a still image from body camera footage (Plf’s Exhibit "1"; Dep. of Det. Shean, 21:22, Exhibit "6"). However, the image is blurry, and while there appears to be a sign in the circled area, the content is not discernible, making it unclear whether it is a "No U-turn" sign or something else. In any event, in the police report (Plf’s Exhibit 10), Defendant Alexeff admitted that she knew the U-turn was unsafe and had seen a "No U-turn" sign but chose to proceed regardless. This directly undermines Plaintiff’s argument that the presence of additional signs would have changed Defendant Alexeff’s actions.

Plaintiff argues that a police officer stationed closest to the accident was dismissed one hour prior to the scheduled end time, even though at least three cyclists, including Plaintiff, were still traveling through that location (Dep. of Det. Shean, 14:4, 15:1, Exhibit "6"). Plaintiff further claims that the presence of a police officer would have deterred Defendant Alexeff from making an illegal U-turn as Defendant allegedly passed by where the officer should have been stationed. However, no evidence has been provided to show that Defendant Alexeff actually drove past Police Post #5.

This argument is further undermined by the GPS coordinates of the impact (Long. 34.024060, Lat: -118.833578), which are undisputed by the parties and show that the accident occurred approximately 2000 feet southeast of Police Post #5. (Def’s Ex. H.) According to the drawing showing the locations of cones, flaggers, officers, and signs near the intersection of PCH and Guernsey Avenue (Plf’s Ex. 7), the officer stationed at Post #5 would have been located north of the intersection, far from the scene of the accident.

Additionally, evidence provided by Plaintiff contradicts this claim. Detective Shean testified in his deposition that Plaintiff would have already passed the Guernsey intersection before the collision occurred (Dep. of Det. Shean, 18:18, 31:19). Therefore, Defendant Alexeff, who was traveling westbound, would not have passed the officer stationed at Post #5 near the intersection to have been deterred by his/her presence.

Plaintiff’s expert’s declaration is also problematic because it fails to cite any evidence from the record or exhibits to support its conclusions. For example, expert Louis Rubenstein opines that the Traffic Control Plan prepared by Total Barricade Service and amended by Caltrans required three "No U-turn" signs to be posted near a driveway approximately 2000 feet south of Guernsey Avenue, and that no "No U-turn" sign was posted facing northbound traffic in that area. However, Rubenstein provides no evidence to support this assertion.

Rubenstein concludes that had the other two "No U-turn" signs been posted, they would have been visible to Defendant Alexeff when she made the illegal U-turn. However, Defendant Alexeff admitted in the police report (Exhibit 10) that she saw the "No U-turn" sign but chose to make the U-turn anyway. This undermines Rubenstein’s conclusion, as the presence or absence of additional signs would not necessarily have changed Defendant’s actions.

Rubenstein also asserts that there was no flagger stationed in front of the driveway approximately 2000 feet from Guernsey Avenue and that a flagger was required under the revisions to Total Barricade’s plan mandated by Caltrans. Again, no evidence is cited to support this claim. If Plaintiff is referring to Exhibit 7, the drawing that shows the placement of flaggers and signs near Guernsey Avenue, the drawing seems to indicate that a flagger was to be placed west of the intersection. However, the GPS coordinates of the incident (Long. 34.024060, Lat: -118.833578), undisputed by Plaintiff, show that the impact occurred approximately 2370 feet southeast of the intersection, meaning a flagger would not have been stationed near the accident site to deter Defendant from making the illegal U-turn. Furthermore, Plaintiff provided no clear evidence demonstrating that no flagger was present at the location at the time of the incident.

Similarly, the officer stationed at Post #5, as shown on the drawing/map (Plf’s Exhibit 7), was located north of the intersection of Guernsey Avenue and PCH, and would not have been in a position to prevent the accident, which occurred significantly southeast of the post’s location. Contrary to Plaintiff’s allegation, Defendant, who was traveling westbound, would not have passed the police post, and Rubenstein’s assertion that the presence of the officer would have deterred Defendant from making the illegal U-turn is unsupported by the facts.

Aside from the conclusory statement that Defendant was grossly negligent, Rubenstein’s declaration lacks any substantive reasoning to show how the alleged omissions—such as the failure to provide additional "No U-turn" signs, the early dismissal of the officer at Post 5, or the absence of a flagger—constitute gross negligence. Gross negligence requires a "want of even scant care" or an "extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct" (City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court, 41 Cal.4th at p. 754). Even assuming these omissions occurred, conduct that merely fails to guard against or warn of a potential danger typically amounts to ordinary negligence, not gross negligence (Anderson v. Fitness Int’l, LLC, 4 Cal.App.5th 867, 881). Plaintiff’s allegations, at best, describe ordinary negligence and do not meet the high standard required to establish gross negligence.

Plaintiff does not dispute that Defendants are not liable for ordinary negligence under the waiver Plaintiff signed. Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of fact as to gross negligence. Therefore, Defendants’ motions for summary judgment are granted.

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV18301 ALEXANDER KANAKIS vs ANSCHUTZ ENTERTAINMENT GROUP et al.

Ruling: MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL IS GRANTED

 

On June 3, 2022, Plaintiff filed this premises liability action arising from an incident at the Coachella Music Festival. Plaintiff alleges that on April 18, 2022, while working as a sound engineer, a large light fixture rolled off the stage and onto his lower back, causing him to suffer three fractured vertebrae. Plaintiff claims that the wheels on the light fixture were not properly locked. Trial is currently set for December 9, 2024.

 

There are 18 defendants named in the case, and so far 8 defendants have responded. Defendant SSA Enterprises, LLC, the moving party, only appeared in the case recently on July 24, 2024. As the case progresses, more defendants are expected to appear.

Defendant SSA now moves to continue the trial date to December 2025, citing the need for additional time to conduct discovery, including written discovery, depositions of Plaintiff, defendants, cross-defendants, and expert discovery. Additionally, SSA indicates that new parties are still being added, which will require more time to address discovery with respect to those parties. SSA also requests the continuance to accommodate a mediation that is scheduled to take place in July or August 2025.

 

Plaintiff opposes only the length of the requested continuance, suggesting that a trial continuance to June 2025 would provide sufficient time for discovery, mediation, and trial preparation. Plaintiff argues that much of the discovery is already underway, and additional discovery involving newly added parties can be completed by the end of the year.

While Plaintiff’s position is understandable, the Court must account for several additional factors. Defendant SSA only appeared in this case in July 2024, and discovery is still in its early stages for SSA. Moreover, new defendants are being added, and these newly added parties must be given adequate time to prepare their defenses, participate in discovery, and conduct their own investigations into the claims. The scope of this case, involving numerous defendants, justifies a more extended continuance than Plaintiff suggests.

 

Given the number of defendants and the expected addition of more new parties, the Court finds good cause to continue the trial for about one year, to October 2025. The new trial date is set for October ____, 2025, at 8:30 a.m. The Final Status Conference is continued to October ____, 2025, at 10:00 a.m. All case-related deadlines will follow the ed new trial date.

Ruling: MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENA AND MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT ARE GRANTED.¿ 

 

Legal Standard

 

“‘Service of process, under longstanding tradition in our system of justice, is fundamental to any procedural imposition on a named defendant.’ [Citation.]” (AO Alfa-Bank v. Yakovlev (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 189, 202.) “To establish personal jurisdiction, compliance with statutory procedures for service of process is essential.” (Kremerman v. White (2021). 71 Cal.App.5th 358, 371.) Defendant’s knowledge of the action does not dispense with statutory requirements for service of summons. (Kappel v. Bartlett (1988) 200 Cal.App.3d 1457, 1466.)

 

“When a defendant challenges the court’s personal jurisdiction on the ground of improper service of process ‘the burden is on the plaintiff to prove the existence of jurisdiction by proving, inter alia, the facts requisite to an effective service.’” (Summers v. McClanahan (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 403, 413.)¿¿ 

 

Filing of proof of service that complies with the applicable statutory requirements creates a rebuttable presumption of proper service. (Am. Express Centurion Bank v. Zara, (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390.) The moving party may then introduce evidence to rebut this presumption.

 

Discussion

 

On May 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present case against Defendant Melvin William Martinez Quijano. On June 9, 2024, Defendant was allegedly served by substitute service. On August 1, 2024, a default was entered against Defendant. Defendant now moves to quash service on the basis that service was improper.

 

The Proof of Service indicates that on June 9, 2024, at 1:58 p.m., Plaintiff served Defendant through substitute service by delivering the summons and complaint to “Alfredo Perez,” a purported co-occupant at 10435 Omelveny Ave, Pacoima, CA 91331-3712. While Plaintiff’s proof of service technically complies with statutory requirements and creates a rebuttable presumption of proper service (Am. Express Centurion Bank v. Zara (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 383, 390), Defendant has introduced sufficient evidence to rebut this presumption.

 

Defendant Quijano declares that although he resides at 10435 Omelveny Ave, Alfredo Perez does not live at that address, has never lived there, and is not a co-occupant. Furthermore, Perez has never been authorized to accept service on Defendant’s behalf. This challenges the validity of the substitute service, as CCP § 415.20(b) requires that the documents be served on a competent member of the household.

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition or provide any countervailing evidence to support the validity of service. The Court finds that Defendant has successfully demonstrated that Alfredo Perez was not a competent member of the household, which makes the substituted service flawed. As a result, Defendant's motion to quash service is GRANTED.

 

Additionally, because Defendant did not receive proper service of the summons and complaint, and the parties were engaged in communications regarding a potential pre-litigation settlement from April to August 2024, Defendant’s counsel was taken by surprise when Plaintiff took Defendant’s default. Defendant acted promptly after discovering the default by moving to quash service and set aside the default. Plaintiff did not file an opposition contesting Defendant’s contentions. Thus, Defendant’s motion to set aside default is also GRANTED.
