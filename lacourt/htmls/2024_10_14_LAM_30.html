DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20STCV47947    Hearing Date: October 14, 2024    Dept: 30

Dept. 30

Calendar No.

Golan vs. Encino Villas Condominium Association, Inc., et. al., Case No. 20STCV47947

 

Tentative Ruling re:  Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal

 

Amir Golan (Plaintiff) moves for an order to set aside the dismissal entered by the Court on July 29, 2024, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 2023.010 and 2023.030, subdivision (d).  The motion is denied.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b): “the court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”

 

            Mandatory relief from default or dismissal is available when fault of counsel is shown through affidavit: “the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any . . . resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

A dismissal implementing a terminating sanction for discovery abuse is a “dismissal entered” under section 473, subdivision (b). (Rodriguez v. Brill (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 715, 719.) An application for relief from a terminating discovery sanction is in proper form if “(1) verified discovery responses are delivered to opposing counsel before the hearing on the application for relief and (2) the content of those responses substantially complies with applicable requirements.” (Id. at p. 719–720.)

 

Plaintiff moves for relief from dismissal based on the fault of Plaintiff’s counsel. Encino Villas Condominium Association, Inc. (Defendant) opposes Plaintiff’s motion on the ground that Plaintiff’s failure to respond to discovery requests resulted from gross carelessness rather than excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. Since Plaintiff’s counsel has submitted an affidavit attesting to his fault, the Court must grant relief unless it finds that the dismissal was not in fact caused by counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

Defendant filed a motion to compel discovery responses on July 1, 2024. Plaintiff filed no opposition and did not appear at the July 29 hearing on that matter. The Court dismissed Plaintiff’s action that day pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (d)(3), issuing a terminating sanction in response to Plaintiff’s failure to respond to discovery requests.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel contends that a medical emergency is to blame for his failure to appear or oppose the motion. Counsel suffered a serious injury and stroke on April 26, 2024. (Rudd Decl. ¶ 3.) He did not realize the full extent of his injury until being admitted to the hospital on June 24. (Ibid.) After subsequently contracting the flu, counsel did not leave the hospital until July 4. (Id. ¶ 5.) He required physical therapy and is only now resuming his practice full time. (Id. ¶ 7.) Counsel claims that he could not file timely oppositions to the discovery motions or appear at the hearing because of his injury, impairment, and hospitalization. (Id. ¶ 8.)

 

Plaintiff needed to file his opposition by July 16, twelve days after counsel was released from the hospital. Counsel did not notify the Court or Defendant of his situation. An extension could have been granted with proper communication. Additionally, other counsel has assisted Plaintiff in this matter. (Id. ¶ 7.) Plaintiff is aware of counsel’s health issues. Thus, Plaintiff’s failure to attend the hearing or oppose the motions to compel is not due to the excusable neglect or mistake of counsel.

 

Additionally, to obtain relief from a terminating discovery sanction Plaintiff must provide verified responses which substantially comply with any applicable requirements before the hearing. (Rodriguez, supra, at p. 719–720.) Plaintiff has not provided these required responses.

 


Case Number: BC709376    Hearing Date: October 14, 2024    Dept: 30

Dept. 30

Calendar No.

Sadeghi vs. Pinscreen Inc., et. al., Case No. BC709376

 

Tentative Ruling re:  Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash Business Records Subpoena

 

            Dr. Iman Sadeghi (Plaintiff) moves for an order to quash or modify Dr. Hao Li’s (Defendant) deposition subpoena for production of business records issued to Google Inc., Plaintiff’s former employer. Alternatively, Plaintiff moves for a protective order limiting the scope of requests 1–15 in the subpoena.  The motion is granted in part.

 

            As a preliminary matter, Defendant contends that the present motion should be denied because it was not timely filed. The Court tendered an order reopening discovery for certain motions at the urging of Defendant on October 9, 2024. In the interest of equity, the Court will reopen discovery for the purpose of hearing Plaintiff’s motion to quash as well.

 

            Additionally, Plaintiff does not contest Defendant’s subpoena as it relates to requests 16–26 regarding Plaintiff’s compensation while at Google. Thus, the Court will not disturb the subpoena as to these documents.

 

A party may only discover matters, not privileged, that are relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action and that are either admissible evidence or “reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)  Broad discovery requests are not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224–225.) A court may make an order quashing a subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with terms and conditions such as protective orders. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1.)

 

Plaintiff argues that requests 1–15 infringe on Plaintiff’s right to privacy under the California Constitution. The California Constitution ensures a right to privacy in Article 1, Section 1. Information otherwise discoverable, even when highly relevant, may be shielded from discovery if its disclosure would violate a person’s constitutional privacy rights. (County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 621, 639.) It is the burden of the party asserting the privacy interest to “establish its extent and the seriousness of the prospective invasion, and against that showing must weigh the countervailing interests the opposing party identifies.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557.) “Obvious invasions of interests fundamental to personal autonomy must be supported by a compelling interest.” (Ibid.)

 

Defendants request performance and disciplinary records (requests 2–5), documents related to complaints made about Plaintiff (requests 9, 10, 12, and 13), documents related to complaints made by Plaintiff (requests 6–8, 11), documents related to Plaintiff’s separation from Google (requests 14 and 15), and Plaintiff’s personnel file and employee records (request 1). With respect to each category of records, the Court will first determine whether the information sought it relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action as required by section 2017.010. Next, the Court will consider the extent and seriousness of the potential privacy invasion and weigh the countervailing interests of the opposing party to determine if discovery should be ordered. (Williams, supra, at p. 557.)

 

Performance and Disciplinary Records

In requests 2–5, Defendant asks Google to produce documents related to Plaintiff’s performance and disciplinary records. (Davidson Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. A.) These include documents discussing Plaintiff’s job performance, documents related to any issued performance expectation plan, documents related to Plaintiff’s objections and key results, and documents related to whether Plaintiff received any CMEs. (Ibid.)

 

These documents are relevant to Plaintiff’s claim for fraudulent inducement. Defendant may defeat this claim by showing that Plaintiff did not “justifiably rely” on assertions made by Defendant. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) If Plaintiff’s disciplinary and performance records reflect that he faced imminent termination at the time he ended his employment with Google, then he may have sought other employment regardless of Defendant’s assertions. However, only records from around the time Plaintiff left Google are relevant because earlier disciplinary violations or negative performance reviews would not have induced Plaintiff to leave Google.

 

Plaintiff asserts that he has a privacy interest in these documents. The Court agrees. Disciplinary and performance records are deeply personal and could reflect poorly on Plaintiff if publicized. However, Defendant does have a significant countervailing interest in this information to determine whether Plaintiff left Google because of Defendant’s inducement, or because of uncertainty surrounding his future employment at Google. Thus, the Court will allow these requests, provided that the subpoena is limited to records within a year of Plaintiff leaving Google.

 

Documents Related to Complaints Made about Plaintiff

In requests 9, 10, 12, and 13, Defendant asks that Google produce documents related to any complaints made about Plaintiff. (Davidson Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. A.) These include documents involving complaints made by employees or third parties, documents involving investigations conducted in response to complaints, and documents related to investigation by Google. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant argues these documents are relevant to its defense of Plaintiff’s claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress. The Court disagrees. Defendant would be unable to justify its firing of Plaintiff based on coworkers complaining about Plaintiff at a previous job. Such character evidence is inadmissible to prove Plaintiff’s quality on a specific occasion. (Evid. Code, § 1104.) Further, Plaintiff has a strong privacy interest in these documents. Thus, the Court will not allow these requests.

 

Documents Related to Complaints Made by Plaintiff

In requests 6–8 and 11, Defendant asks Google to produce documents related to complaints made by Plaintiff while employed at Google. (Davidson Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. A.) These include documents relating to complaints made by Plaintiff regarding his employment, other employees, or any third party, and documents relating to any investigations conducted by Google on Plaintiff’s behalf. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant argues these documents are relevant to show a pattern of behavior by Plaintiff, reducing the credibility of his claims against Defendant. The Court disagrees. A pattern of making complaints does not constitute admissible habit evidence under Evidence Code section 1105. Additionally, Defendant argues that these complaints could show a history of Plaintiff engaging in interpersonal workplace conflicts. This evidence would likely not be relevant. Even if it were within the scope of discovery, Plaintiff has a privacy interest in private complaints he made at his previous workplace. Defendant has not demonstrated a countervailing interest sufficient to override Plaintiff’s right to privacy. Thus, the Court will not allow these requests.

 

Documents Relating to Plaintiff’s Separation from Google

In requests 14 and 15, Defendant asks Google to produce documents related to Plaintiff’s separation, including any documents provided to Plaintiff on the day of his separation. (Ibid.) These documents are relevant in the same way as disciplinary and performance records. They can show whether Plaintiff relied on Defendant’s assertions when leaving his previous job. While Plaintiff may have a privacy interest in the details surrounding his employment’s end at Google, Defendant’s countervailing interest is significant. Thus, the Court allows these requests.

 

Personnel File and Employee Records

In request 1, Defendant asks Google to produce Plaintiff’s personnel file, or any other employee file maintained by them. This request is overbroad. Such a request likely includes information that would be relevant to Defendant’s case. However, confidential personnel files at a person’s place of employment are in a well-established zone of privacy. (Board of Trustees of Leland Stanford Junior University v. Superior Court (Dong) (1981) 119 Cal.App.3d 516, 528–530.) Defendant does not demonstrate a significant countervailing interest. Defendant claims that information within Plaintiff’s file could rebut some of his claims regarding pre-existing medical issues. But medical records are the clear source of such information. The Court will not allow this overbroad request.
