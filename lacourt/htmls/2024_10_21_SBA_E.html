ANDREW LWIN,

Moving Parties:          Defendant Andrew Lwin

 

Responding Party:      None

 

HEARING: Renewed Motion for Terminating Sanctions

           

The Court considered the moving papers.

 

RULING

 

            The Court denies the Renewed Motion for Terminating Sanctions.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On March 25, 2020, Plaintiff Eunmin Kim filed the Complaint alleging causes of action for: 1. Retaliatory Eviction; 2. Breach of the Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; 3.

Constructive Eviction; 4. Battery; 5. Assault; 6. IIED; 7. NIED; 8. Violation of Gov. Code

12955(a); 9. Violation of Gov. Code 12955(f); 10. Violation of Civ. Code 51.9; 11. Violation of

California Code of Regulations 12120 against Defendant Andrew Lwin arising out of alleged sexual harassment and retaliation.

 

            On September 21, 2023, Defendant’s first motion for terminating sanctions was denied.

           

            On October 24, 2023, Defendant’s second motion for terminating sanctions was denied.

 

            On January 16, 2024, Defendant’s third motion for terminating sanctions was denied.

 

            On May 24, 2024, Defendant’s fourth motion for terminating sanctions was denied.

 

            On September 19, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Renewed Motion for Terminating Sanctions.

 

            No opposition has been filed.

 

            No trial date has been set.

 

            Defendant has been admonished by the Court multiple times for filing meritless motions and has been reminded of the definition of a “vexatious litigant.”

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            The court is authorized, after notice and opportunity for hearing, to impose the following sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process: monetary sanctions, issue sanctions, evidence sanctions, terminating sanctions, and contempt. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2023.030 subd. (a)-(e).) A terminating sanction may be imposed by an order dismissing the action against whom the sanction is imposed. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2023.030 subd. (d)(3).)  

 

Code of Civil Procedure, section 2023.010, subdivision (g), provides that a misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.”  

 

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc.¿(2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992; see J.W. v. Watchtower Bible and Tract Society of New York, Inc.¿(2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1142, 1169.) If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted. (Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.) Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed what is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery. (Ibid.) “But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Ibid., quoting Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280; Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar¿(2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 690, 702 (quoting same).)  

 

Because terminating sanctions are the most “drastic” penalty, they are typically a “last resort” to be “used sparingly”; however, they may still be appropriate “as a first measure” in “extreme cases” where a litigant violates a court order and “persists in the outright refusal to comply with [its] discovery obligations.” (Siry Investment, L.P. v. Farkhondehpour¿(2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 1098, 1118,¿as modified on denial of reh’g (Mar. 23, 2020).)

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

On August 17, 2023, Defendant filed the Motion for Terminating Sanctions on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff’s Attorney Jerry Udeochu forged the verification form and (2) misused the CCP 446 form in responding to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories Set One. The Court found that it cannot grant terminating sanctions based on “Defendant’s own self-serving belief that a verification was not genuine.” (August 21, 2023, Minute Order p. 3.)

 

            Defendant now argues that new evidence has arisen that shows that Defendant was not in the United States at the time of the verification of Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories Set One. Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s attorney signed the verification form stating that Plaintiff was out of the country, but Plaintiff also signed a separate verification form stating that Plaintiff was in Carson, California. (Lwin Decl. Exhibit A.) Furthermore, Plaintiff provides a FOIA request on Plaintiff's travel history that shows she was not in the country at the time of the verification. (Lwin Decl. Exhibit C.) While Defendant has shown that there is a discrepancy between the two forms and that Plaintiff was not in the country at the time of the verification, Defendant has not shown that this was anything more than a simple mistake, nor has he shown that terminating sanctions are warranted.

 

            Terminating sanctions are meant to be a last resort to only be used in extreme cases. (Siry Investment, L.P, supra, 45 Cal.App.5th 1098, 1118.) While Plaintiff may not have been in the country at the time of the verification, Plaintiff’s location is immaterial as Plaintiff’s attorney had already verified the documents on Plaintiff’s behalf. (Lwin Decl. Exhibit A.) Defendant contends that this is a serious forgery, equating it to spoliation or fabrication of evidence, that warrants terminating sanctions, but fails to explain how the discrepancy harmed the discovery process or benefited Defendant.

 

            Therefore, the Court denies the Renewed Motion for Terminating Sanctions.

 

Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).) In addition, a court shall impose monetary sanctions if the motion to compel a deposition is granted unless the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or other circumstances would make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code. Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) 

 

Defendant requests that the court “impose a monetary sanction of $357.93 and attorney’s fee of $2,500”. (Renewed Motion for Terminating Sanctions p. 6.) As Defendant is not the prevailing party, they are not entitled to sanctions.

           

            Therefore, the Court denies Defendant’s request for sanctions.

DISCUSSION

Proof of Service

 

            Summons may be served on a corporation by delivering a copy of the summons and the complaint “to the person designated as agent for service of process as provided by any provision in Section 202, 1502, 2105, or 2107 of the Corporations Code” or “to the president, chief executive officer, or other head of the corporation, a vice president, a secretary or assistant secretary, a treasurer or assistant treasurer, a controller or chief financial officer, a general manager, or a person authorized by the corporation to receive service of process.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 416.10 subd. (a-b).)

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff did not properly serve the summons. Plaintiff filed a Proof of Service form stating that they delivered the summons via FedEx to Defendant's registered agent on June 8, 2024, but Defendant contends that FedEx delivery does not meet the legal requirements for proper service under California law. (RJN Exhibit 1 p. 2.)

 

 Defendant asserts that the proper methods of service include personal service or service by mail, neither of which were checked on the Proof of Service form. Additionally, service by mail requires an acknowledgment of receipt, which was not provided, making service improper under section 415.30. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30.) Service by leaving the summons with a person over 18 at Defendant’s address was also not followed, as required by section 416.20. (Code Civ. Proc., § 416.20.)

 

            Plaintiff argues that Defendant's motion should be denied because Defendant was properly served. Plaintiff claims that the Proof of Service of summons, attached as Exhibit 1, clearly shows that Defendant was served with the summons and complaint via FedEx delivery at the registered agent's address. Code of Civil Procedure sections 416.30 and 416.20 allow the service of a summons on a corporation by delivering it to the registered agent or corporate officers like the president or vice president. (Code Civ. Proc., § 416.20 - 416.30.)

 

Plaintiff further asserts that Defendant's registered agent, Zeyad Elalami, who is also a co-defendant and Vice President of Defendant, was personally served with the summons and complaint on July 23, 2024. Since Elalami received the summons and is a designated agent for service of process, Plaintiff argues that substantial compliance with the service requirements under Code of Civil Procedure sections 416.10(a) and (b) should be considered sufficient. (Code Civ. Proc., § 416.10 subd. (a-b).)

 

Service by mail is only an acceptable form of service when Defendant signs an acknowledgment of receipt. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.30.) Therefore, because Defendant did not sign an acknowledgment of receipt, the service was improper. Plaintiff argues that the Court should find substantial compliance with service requirements, being that the registered agent of Defendant, Zeyad Elalami, is also a named party in the case who was personally served. However, strict compliance with service requirements is required under California law. (Bolkiah v. Superior Court (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 984, 1000.)  

Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Vacate Default.

RULING

The Court grants the Special Motion to strike as to the fifth, tenth, and eleventh causes of actions and denies as to all others.

BACKGROUND

            On June 3, 2024, Plaintiffs Not by Sight, LLC, Gladney Johnson, and Denise Johnson, filed the Complaint alleging causes of action for breach of contract, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, negligent misrepresentation, constructive fraud, fraudulent misrepresentation, promissory estoppel, quiet title, wrongful foreclosure and wrongful eviction, injunctive relief, declaratory relief, and preliminary and permanent injunctions arising from the foreclosure sale of the property.

            On October 9, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the Opposition.

 

            On October 14, 2024, Defendants filed the reply.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

The court engages in a two-step process in determining whether to grant or deny a Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 special motion to strike. (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University¿(2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1061.) First, the court must decide whether the moving party has met the threshold burden of showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the moving party’s constitutional rights of free speech or petition for redress of grievances. (See Ibid.) This burden may be met by showing the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff’s cause of action was an act that falls within one of the four categories of conduct outlined in Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (e): 

 

(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, 

(2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, 

(3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or 

(4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest. 

 

If the defendant meets this initial burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to prove the plaintiff has a legally sufficient claim and to prove with admissible evidence a probability of prevailing on the claim. (De Havilland v. FX Networks, LLC¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 845, 855.) The trial court considers the pleadings and evidence of both parties. (Ibid.) The plaintiff’s proof must be made upon competent admissible evidence. (Sweetwater Union High School Dist. v. Gilbane Building Co. (2019) 6 Cal.5th 931, 940.) The court “does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims.” (Ibid.) The court’s inquiry “is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment,” accepting the plaintiff’s evidence as true. (Ibid.) “The court evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law. [Citation.] ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’” (Ibid.; see Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89.)

 

 

DISCUSSION

Prong One: Protected Activity

 

“The statutory phrase ‘cause of action . . . arising from’ means simply that the defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.” (Id.)  “In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.” (Park v. Board of Trustees, (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1063.) If a motion is directed to mixed causes of action, a defendant or cross-defendant may identify the allegations of protected activity and how a claim is based on that activity. (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System, (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1010-1012 A complaint to a law enforcement body is a protected activity as a statement before an official proceeding. (ComputerXpress Inc. v. Jackson, (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 993, 1009.)

 

Defendants argue that the entire Complaint should be subject to an anti-SLAPP motion to strike under California's Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 because all eleven causes of action are based on protected activities. These activities include filing an unlawful detainer action, serving eviction notices, and communications made in connection with the proceedings, which have consistently been recognized by California courts as protected under the anti-SLAPP statute. Defendants also point out that Plaintiffs' allegations, including breach of contract, misrepresentation, and wrongful foreclosure, all arise from the unlawful detainer proceedings and the stipulated judgment. Therefore, since these activities are protected, Defendants contend that Plaintiffs' entire Complaint should be dismissed.

 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Plaintiffs argue that while the Complaint alleges the existence of a contract between Buy2Let and Gladney Johnson, it does not seek to enforce that contract. Instead, Plaintiffs seek to enforce oral promises made by Defendants during the unlawful detainer proceedings. They allege that Defendants, through their agent Elalami, made ongoing promises that Plaintiffs could repurchase the property, which induced them to enter into a stipulated judgment on April 2, 2024. Plaintiffs contend that they relied on these promises when agreeing to the stipulated judgment and subsequently made additional payments based on their belief that the purchase would be completed as promised.

 

Paragraph 26: "After a foreclosure on the Subject Property, BUY 2 LET

INVESTMENTS, LLC filed an unlawful detainer action against Plaintiff Gladney

Johnson... which resulted in a stipulated judgment on April 2, 2024."

Paragraph 27: “Plaintiffs and Defendants (Elalami claiming to act as agent of Buy 2 Let)

entered into stipulated judgment on April 2, 2024, in reliance on Elalami’s continued

promise that Plaintiffs could repurchase the Subject Property... Attached to this

Complaint as Exhibit D is a copy of April 2, 2024, Stipulated Judgment.”

Paragraph 28: "Plaintiffs would not have entered into the stipulated judgment but for

their justifiable belief that Elalami would complete the purchase of the Subject Property

as he promised, in order to induce the Plaintiffs to execute the stipulated judgment of the

Unlawful Detainer action on April 2, 2024."

Paragraph 31: "On reliance upon Elalami’s promises... made to Plaintiffs before, during,

and after execution of the Purchase Agreement, through the conduct of the Unlawful

Detainer action... Plaintiffs have suffered thousands of dollars in additional payments..."

(Complaint ¶¶ 26-28, 31.)

 

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants' claim that the breach of contract cause of action is subject to the anti-SLAPP statute is baseless. (Complaint ¶ 25.) They contend that their breach of contract claim seeks damages for Defendants' failure to complete the sale of the property, as outlined in the contract executed on July 24 and 26, 2023, and not for any protected activity such as filing an unlawful detainer action. Plaintiffs assert that a breach of contract alone does not constitute an exercise of Defendants' constitutional rights to free speech or petition and is, therefore, not a protected activity under the anti-SLAPP statute.

 

Plaintiffs further note that while the Complaint references the unlawful detainer action, those references merely provide context and are not the basis for their claim, which focuses solely on Defendants' breach of contract. Plaintiffs maintain that Defendants have failed to show that the breach of contract claim arises from protected activity or conduct.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs' breach of contract claim is based on alleged oral promises made during the unlawful detainer proceedings, specifically concerning Defendants' agreement to sell the property back to Plaintiffs. These promises purportedly led Plaintiffs to enter into a stipulated judgment in the unlawful detainer action, and the breach of the implied covenant claim similarly relies on conduct during those proceedings.

 

While Defendants argue that the breach of contract claim arises from the oral promises made during the unlawful detainer proceedings, The Complaint states that “Defendants materially breached the Purchase Agreement by failing to sell the Subject Property.” The Complaint does mention the stipulated judgment. However, allegations made regarding the breach of contract claim are focused on the Purchase agreement. (Complaint ¶¶ 33-37.) Defendants argue that Navellier states that claims based on statements or conduct in connection with litigation are subject to the anti-SLAPP statute. (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 92.) However, the breach of contract claim is not based on statements made during the unlawful detainer litigation but on the Purchase Agreement.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that cause of action one is not based on protected activity.

 

Second Cause of Action – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith And Fair Dealing

 

            Plaintiffs argue that “The Complaint alleges liability for noticing the unlawful detainer action, which constitutes protected activity.” (Motion to Dismiss p. 12.)

 

            Paragraph 42: "By noticing a void foreclosure sale, and an unlawful detainer action,

when without the power or right to notice such sale or unlawful detainer action,

Defendants and each of them including Does 1-5, intentionally damaged and sought to

deprive Plaintiffs of the benefits they were to obtain under the above agreements, all

to their damage and harm in excess of the jurisdictional limits of the Court.”

(Complaint ¶ 42.)

 

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendants' claim that their cause of action is based on a protected activity, merely because it references the unlawful detainer in the Complaint, is insufficient. They assert that just because a claim may have been triggered by protected activity, such as the service of unlawful detainer papers, does not mean it arose from that activity. (See, Ulkarim v. Westfield LLC (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 1266, 1275-1282.)

 

            Again, while the Complaint mentions the unlawful detainer action and the foreclosure sale, the cause of action does not arise out of them. (Complaint ¶ 42.) A cause of action for breach of the implied covenant must be tied to a contract. (Smith v. San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49.) Therefore, the alleged breach of the implied covenant must arise from the purchase agreement, not the unlawful detainer action or foreclosure.

 

            Therefore, the Court finds that the second cause of action does not arise out of protected activity.

 

 

Third Cause of Action – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiffs allege they were misled by fraudulent actions into entering agreements during the unlawful detainer proceedings. Communications made in

connection with judicial proceedings are protected under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Feldman v.

1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1483.)

 

Paragraph 52: "Had Plaintiffs known of the falsity of Defendants’ representations in

inducing Plaintiffs to enter into the Purchase Agreement and other agreements and

continue to perform under the Agreements, Plaintiffs would not have entered into the

Agreements... or continue providing additional payments."

 

            Plaintiffs argue that their negligent misrepresentation claim is based on statements made by Defendant Elalami concerning the purchase contract between the parties, as outlined in the Complaint. They emphasize that this claim is unrelated to Defendants' filing of the unlawful detainer action.

 

            The Complaint, in alleging facts to state a cause of action for negligent misrepresentation, makes many vague allegations of misrepresentation regarding the property and the Purchase Agreement. (Complaint ¶¶ 45-52.) However, none of these allegations mention the unlawful detainer proceedings.

           

            Therefore, the third cause of action for negligent misrepresentation does not arise out of protected activity.

 

Fourth Cause of Action – Constructive Fraud

 

            Defendants argue that the Complaint alleges fraudulent actions connected to the unlawful detainer proceedings.

 

Paragraph 67: “Plaintiffs reasonably and justifiably relied upon the representations of

the Defendants in agreeing to provide payment to Defendants according to the terms of

the Purchase Agreement and other agreements.”

(Complaint ¶ 67.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that similar to the third cause of action, the liability for this claim is based on statements made by Defendants regarding the purchase contract. (Complaint¶¶ 63, 64-72.)

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that every reference to an allegedly false statement is in regard to the unlawful detainer proceedings.

 

While Plaintiffs assert that every reference to a false statement is in regard to the unlawful detainer proceedings, they provide no reasoning to back up this statement. Furthermore, in alleging the facts for constructive fraud, no allegation makes mention of the unlawful detainer proceedings. (Complaint ¶¶ 63, 64-72.)

 

            Therefore, the Court finds that the fourth cause of action does not arise from protective activity.

 

            Fifth Cause of Action – Fraudulent Misrepresentation

 

            Similar to the previous claims, Defendants argue this cause of action arises from statements made during the unlawful detainer proceedings:

 

            Paragraph 81: "Defendants knowingly made unwarranted and false assertions intending

to induce Plaintiffs in entering into the Purchase Agreement and the stipulated judgment

agreement relating to the unlawful detainer action... with the prior intent not to sell the

Subject Property..."

Paragraph 83: "Elalami... made the false material misrepresentations... before execution

of the Purchase Agreement, before execution of the stipulated judgment relating to the

unlawful detainer action, to induce Plaintiffs to enter into the Purchase Agreement, the

stipulated judgment agreement, and other agreements..."

 

 

Similar to the previous claims, Plaintiffs argue, the liability for this claim is based on statements made by Defendants regarding the purchase contract. (Complaint ¶ 80.)

 

Here, unlike the other causes of action, the Complaint directly states that Defendant made false representations about the purchase agreement before executing the stipulation agreement in the unlawful detainer action.  (Complaint ¶ 83.) Communications made in connection with judicial proceedings are protected under the anti-SLAPP statute. (Feldman, supra, 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1483) Thus, because some of the alleged misrepresentations occurred during negotiations regarding judicial proceedings, those representations are protected activities.

 

            Therefore, the fifth cause of action does arise out of protected activity.

 

            Sixth Cause of Action – Promissory Estoppel

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff's reliance on promises made during the unlawful detainer action forms the basis of this claim.

 

            Paragraph 97: "Defendants breached the promise to sell the Subject Property to

Plaintiffs."

Paragraph 98: "Plaintiffs justifiably relied upon Elalami’s promise, as communicated,

and persuaded by Elalami."

Paragraph 99: "Plaintiffs’ reliance on Defendants’ promise was both reasonable and

foreseeable."

(Complaint ¶¶ 98-99.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that this cause of action is based on the multiple promises made by Defendants, through their agent Elalami, regarding the sale of the subject property. The fact that some of these promises were made during Defendants' unlawful detainer action does not, by itself, bring the claim under the scope of the anti-SLAPP statute.

 

Compliant paragraphs 98 and 99 make only vague references to Defendant’s promises. They do not mention what the promises were for or when they were made. (Complaint ¶¶ 98-99.)

Thus, the lack of specificity found in the allegations prevents the Court from finding that they are referring to protected activity.

 

            Therefore, the Court finds that the sixth cause of action does not arise out of protected activity.

 

            Seventh Cause of Action – Quiet Title

 

            Defendants argues that the quiet title claim challenges Defendants’ rights established through the unlawful detainer proceedings.

 

            Paragraph 106: "None of the Defendants has any power or right to proceed with any

eviction, lockout, foreclosure, or any other action, but must transfer the Subject Property

to the buyers... Any purported foreclosure sale... was irregular and void as a matter of

law..."

(Complaint ¶ 106.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that, contrary to Defendants' assertion, this cause of action challenges the title Defendants acquired through a foreclosure sale, which Plaintiffs contend was wrongful. Plaintiffs claim that the Trustee Deed conveyed to Defendants during the foreclosure sale is void. (Complaint ¶ 109.)

 

            Defendants argue that the cause of action cannot arise out of the alleged wrongful foreclosure because the foreclosing party has not named the trustee that conducted the foreclosure sale. However, while failing to name the party conducting the foreclosure sale does leave the Complaint vulnerable to a demurrer, it does not prove that the claim did not arise out of the alleged wrongful foreclosure. Especially given that Complaint also includes a cause of action for wrongful eviction and foreclosure. (Complaint ¶112-120.) Thus, Defendants have not met its burden of proof to show that the claim arose out of protected activity. (Okorie v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist., 14 Cal.App.5th 574 (2017)

 

            Therefore, the Court finds that the seventh cause of action did not arise out of protected activity.

 

            Eighth Cause of Action – Wrongful Foreclosure And Wrongful Eviction

 

            Plaintiffs argue that these claims are directly linked to the unlawful detainer action.

 

            Paragraph 119: "Due to Defendants actions, Plaintiffs have been damaged... Plaintiffs

demand... a permanent injunction against Defendants, to prevent them from being evicted

from their home."

(Complaint ¶ 119.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that, similar to their seventh cause of action, they are claiming that the April 20, 2023, foreclosure sale is void due to non-compliance with California Civil Code § 2923.5. Specifically, they contend that the trustee failed to contact them to explore options to avoid foreclosure. (Cal. Civ. Code § 2923.5; Complaint ¶114.)

 

The Complaint directly mentions California Civil Code § 2923. (Complaint ¶114.) Defendants again reply that Plaintiffs did not name the trustee that conducted the foreclosure sale. However, again, while failing to name the party conducting the foreclosure sale does leave the Complaint vulnerable to a demurrer, it does not prove that the claim did not arise out of an alleged wrongful foreclosure sale.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that the eighth cause of action does not arise out of a protected activity.

 

Ninth Cause of Action – Injunctive Relief, Disgorgement Under Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs seek relief based on actions taken during the unlawful detainer proceedings.

 

Paragraph 127: Plaintiffs further pray for an injunctive order enjoining and restraining

Defendants, and each of them from engaging in or performing any act to deprive

Plaintiffs of ownership or possession of their real property, including but not limited to,

prosecuting or maintaining foreclosure or sale proceedings on Plaintiffs’ real property, from recording any deeds or mortgages regarding the property, or from otherwise taking any steps whatsoever to deprive Plaintiffs of ownership in the Subject Property.

(Complaint ¶ 127.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that similar to the eighth cause of action, their claim for violation of the Unfair Competition Law is based on Defendants' wrongful foreclosure of the subject property. They emphasize that the claim is unrelated to Defendants' filing of an eviction lawsuit.

 

The Complaint states that  “defrauding not only Plaintiffs but similarly situated borrowers, by perfecting foreclosures on residential properties without any legal authority whatsoever to do so.” (Complaint ¶ 123.) Furthermore, paragraph 127 of the Complaint requests an injunctive order to enjoin Defendants from “prosecuting or maintaining foreclosure or sale proceedings on Plaintiffs’ real property, from recording any deeds or mortgages regarding the property.” (Complaint ¶ 127.) Thus, the Court finds that this cause of action arises out of the alleged wrongful foreclosure, not the unlawful detainer proceedings.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that the ninth cause of action does not arise out of protected activity.

 

Tenth Cause of Action – Declaratory Relief

 

 

Defendant argues that the tenth cause of action for declaratory relief arises from disputes over actions taken during the unlawful detainer.

 

Paragraph 129: "An actual controversy has arisen... Plaintiffs contend that Defendants...

induced them into their fraudulent scheme... then proceeding to foreclose on their home

and filing an unlawful detainer action."

(Complaint ¶ 129.)

           

Plaintiffs argue that, contrary to Defendants’ assertions, this claim seeks to have the court adjudicate the parties' rights under the contract for the sale of the subject property. (Complaint ¶132.)

In reply, Defendants argue that the claims for declaratory and injunctive relief aim to prevent them from enforcing the stipulated judgment from the unlawful detainer action, as referenced in the Complaint. (Complaint ¶¶ 129, 137.) These claims directly relate to Defendants' protected activities concerning the unlawful detainer proceedings. Defendants assert that the right of possession, which Plaintiffs seek to challenge, has already been adjudicated by the unlawful detainer court.

 

Here, the Complaint states that a controversy has arisen based on fraudulent activity leading to foreclosure and an unlawful detainer action.  (Complaint ¶ 129.) Filing an unlawful detainer suit and the communications regarding the suit are protected activities. (Sheley v. Harrop, (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 1147, 1166.)

 

Therefore, the court finds that the tenth cause of action arises out of protected activity.

 

 

Eleventh Cause of Action – Preliminary and Permanent Injunction

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs aim to prevent enforcement of the stipulated judgment from the unlawful detainer action.

 

Paragraph 137: "The threatened actions of Defendants... pursuant to the fraudulently

obtained stipulated judgment in the unlawful detainer action has exposed Plaintiffs to...

irreparable harm... the Court should issue preliminary and permanent injunctions barring

Defendants... from conducting an eviction... as to the Subject Property..."

(Complaint ¶137.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that similar to their seventh and eighth causes of action, they are seeking the court's injunction to stop any conduct by Defendants that resulted from the wrongful foreclosure of their home.

 

Here, the Complaint refers to a “fraudulently obtained stipulated judgment in the unlawful detainer action. (Complaint ¶137.) Filing an unlawful detainer suit and the communications regarding the suit are protected activities. (Sheley, supra, 9 Cal.App.5th 1147, 1166.)

Therefore, the court finds that the eleventh cause of action arises out of protected activity.

 

Prong Two: Probability of Prevailing

 

Defendants have met the burden of demonstrating that causes of action 5, 10, and 11 arise from protected activity. Thus, the burden now shifts to Plaintiffs to establish a probability of succeeding on the merits. (Kyle v. Carmon (1999) 71 Cal.App.4th 901, 907.)

 

Fifth Cause of Action – Fraudulent Misrepresentation

 

Plaintiffs argue that their cause of action for fraudulent misrepresentation has merit, as demonstrated by the attached declaration and verified complaint. They reference Salma v. Capon (2008), which holds that verified allegations based on the personal knowledge of the pleader can be considered in deciding a motion under CCP §425.16. (Salma v. Capon (2008) 161 CA 4th 1275, 1289.) Plaintiffs do not provide reasoning as to how these declarations show that their cause of action has merit.

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs' fraud claims must be pleaded with particularity, specifying the who, what, when, where, and how of the alleged misconduct. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Defendants contend that Plaintiffs' declarations lack the necessary specificity, failing to identify specific false statements made by Defendants or to demonstrate justifiable reliance on those statements.

 

The Declaration of Denise Johnson outlines the representations made to Plaintiffs by Defendant regarding the sale of the house. However, the declaration fails to explain how these statements were fraudulent, as required under Lazar. (Lazar, supra, 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not shown a probability of succeeding on the merits.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Special Motion to Strike as to the fifth cause of action.

 

Tenth and Eleventh Causes of Action – Declaratory Relief; Preliminary and Permanent Injunction

 

Plaintiffs do not provide individualized arguments for why causes of action ten and eleven have a probability of succeeding on the merits. Instead, they merely argue that similar to their other causes of action, the attached declarations and verified complaint demonstrate that their claims have merit.

 

Plaintiffs have the burden for proof of establishing a probability of succeeding on the merits. The Court does not find Plaintiffs argument convincing.

 

Therefore, the Court grants the Special Motion to Strike as to the tenth and eleventh cause of action.

 

Sanctions

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 subdivision (c)(1) provides that a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike is entitled to recover attorney's fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16) This entitlement is mandatory, not discretionary, as long as the defendant prevails on the motion. (Maughan v. Google Technology, Inc. (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1259.) However, when a defendant only partially succeeds on an Anti-SLAPP motion, the fees and costs awarded are typically limited to those incurred in connection with the successful portion of the motion. (Richmond Compassionate Care Collective v. 7 Stars Holistic Foundation (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 38, 45.)

 

Defendants request sanctions in the amount of $13,500 resulting from 36 hours of work at $375. However, since Defendants only succeeded in dismissing three of the eleven claims, the sanctions amount will be reduced by a ratio of 3/11.

 

Therefore, the Court grants sanctions in the amount of $3,681.

LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 

53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) 

 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to. (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

DISCUSSION

Defendants demur to the entire complaint.

 

Standing

 

            When a plaintiff lacks standing to bring the action, the complaint is subject to a demurrer, and the court may enter a judgment of dismissal with prejudice. (See Reynolds v. City of Calistoga (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 865, 876; Hudis v. Crawford (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 1586, 1592.)

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiffs lack standing to bring the action due to the ongoing bankruptcy proceedings of Plaintiff Gladney Johnson.  Defendants highlight that once a bankruptcy petition is filed, all legal and equitable interests in the debtor's property become part of the bankruptcy estate. (M & M Foods, Inc. v. Pacific American Fish Co., Inc. (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 554, 562.)

 

For Plaintiffs to regain standing, the bankruptcy trustee must explicitly abandon the claim. (M & M Foods, Inc, supra,196 Cal.App.4th 554, 563.) This requires affirmative action from the trustee and adherence to federal bankruptcy statutes, including notice and hearing requirements. Without this abandonment, Plaintiff's complaint is vulnerable to a demurrer because it fails to state a valid cause of action due to the jurisdictional defect of lacking standing. (Bostanian v. Liberty Savings Bank (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 1075, 1086-1087.)

 

Furthermore, Defendants argue that if the Court finds that Plaintiff Gladney Johnson does not have standing, then Plaintiffs Denise Johnson and Not By Sight LLC also lack standing as they are not parties to the contract or the unlawful detainer proceedings.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendants are incorrect in claiming that Gladney Johnson lacks standing to pursue the action based on her Chapter 11 bankruptcy. They point out that unlike in a Chapter 7 bankruptcy, where a trustee is appointed, in a Chapter 11 proceeding—such as in this case—no trustee has been appointed. Ms. Johnson, as the debtor in possession, retains control over her assets and is, therefore, the real party in interest. As a result, she has standing to prosecute the action. (See Bostanian v Liberty Sav. Bank (1997) 52 CA 4th 1075, 1078-1079.)

 

            Furthermore, Plaintiffs argue that Denise Johnson and Not By Sight have standing based on the Declaration of Denise Johnson to Defendant’s Anti-Slapp motion.

 

            In reply, Defendants argue that even in Chapter 11 bankruptcy, a debtor lacks standing to sue in a lawsuit that belongs to the bankruptcy estate. (Tarr v. Merco Constr. Engineers, Inc. (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 707; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 1004.)

 

            Furthermore, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs Denise Johnson and Not By Sight LLC lack standing to pursue the lawsuit despite Plaintiffs’ reliance on the Declaration of Denise Johnson. They contend that the declaration is irrelevant to the demurrer, as a demurrer only tests the sufficiency of the pleadings, not evidence or extrinsic matters. (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Defendants assert that declarations and extrinsic evidence cannot be considered in a demurrer hearing, and judicial notice of the declaration would be improper since it would turn the hearing into a contested evidentiary matter. (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 115.)

 

Defendants further argue that the Complaint fails to allege facts showing that Denise Johnson or Not By Sight LLC were parties to any contract with Defendants or had any legal interest in the subject property. Specifically, they note that neither Denise Johnson nor Not By Sight LLC were parties to the Residential Purchase Agreement or Seller Counter Offer, which expressly prohibits assignment without the seller’s written consent—consent that was never given. (Complaint, Exh. C, ¶ 23.)  As non-parties to the contract, they lack standing to sue for breach or assert related claims. (Hatchwell v. Blue Shield of California (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 1027, 1034.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff Gladney Johnson has filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy. (Request for Judicial Notice exhibit 1.)  The Court has held that a Chapter 11 debtor in possession may prosecute any action before any tribunal without the approval of the bankruptcy court. (Bostanian, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th 1075, 61; California Aviation, Inc. v. Leeds (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 724, 729; but see, Tarr, supra, 84 Cal.App.3d 707 [holding Chapter 11 debtor had no standing to sue].) Therefore, because Gladney Johnson filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy and is a debtor in possession, Plaintiffs have standing to bring this action. (See, Bostanian, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th 1075, 61.)

 

            Default

 

            Plaintiffs argue that Defendant Luxor Funding Group has no standing to demur as it is in default. However, the Court has vacated the default.

 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

            To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove “ the contract, the plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance,  the defendant's breach, and the resulting damage to the plaintiff." (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) A written contract must be pled verbatim in the body of the complaint, be attached to the complaint, and incorporated by reference, or be pled according to its legal effect. (Bowden v. Robinson (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 705, 718.)

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiffs' breach of contract claim is invalid because the alleged promises and agreements regarding the sale of the property do not meet legal requirements.  Defendants point out that while the Residential Purchase Agreement stipulates a 30-day escrow period, the Complaint fails to allege that this agreement was performed.

 

Additionally, Defendants highlight that Residential Purchase Agreement specifies time is of the essence and any changes, extensions, or modifications must be in writing and signed by both parties. The alleged oral promises to extend the agreement are insufficient because they do not comply with this written requirement. Furthermore, Denise Johnson and Not By Sight LLC cannot enforce the contract because the agreement prohibits assignment without the seller's written consent, which was not given, and Not By Sight LLC is not alleged to be solely owned by Gladney Johnson.

 

Defendants also argue that any alleged oral promises during the unlawful detainer proceedings are invalid since contracts for the sale of real property must be in writing. (Civ. Code § 1624 subd. (a)(3).) No written agreement to sell the property to Plaintiffs exists, and the Stipulated Judgment from the unlawful detainer case does not mention any such agreement.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that they have adequately alleged the necessary elements for their first cause of action, as outlined in the Complaint. (Complaint ¶¶21-31, 33-39.) Plaintiffs relied on Defendant Elalami’s promise to sell the property, which led them to make additional payments beyond the contractual amount, including payments for escrow requirements. These actions, Plaintiffs claim, satisfy the part performance exception to the statute of frauds.

 

            In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have failed to establish the existence of a valid contract for the sale of real property, which must be in writing and signed by the party to be charged, as required by the statute of frauds (Civ. Code § 1624 subd. (a)(3)). The written agreements referenced in the Complaint—the Residential Purchase Agreement and Seller CounterOffer—were between Buy2Let Investments, LLC and the "Gladney Johnson Trust," not Denise Johnson or Not By Sight LLC. (Complaint, Exhibits. C, D.)  Furthermore, Plaintiffs have not alleged that they performed under the contract within the required timeframe or that Defendants breached any obligations.

 

Defendants also reject Plaintiffs' attempt to invoke the doctrine of part performance, arguing that merely making payments or securing financing is not sufficient to bypass the statute of frauds. To invoke part performance, Plaintiffs must demonstrate actions unequivocally referable to the oral agreement, which would prevent unjust enrichment. (Sutton v. Warner (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 415, 422.)  However, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have not alleged facts showing that they made substantial improvements to the property or that Defendants accepted payments, and thus, the statute of frauds applies, rendering the alleged oral agreement unenforceable.

 

            In the Complaint, Plaintiffs allege that on July 6, 2023, Defendant Elalami, under his company Buy2Let Investments, LLC, issued a "Payoff Statement" demanding $450,000 for the purchase of the subject property. (Complaint ¶ 21.) On July 24, 2023, Gladney Johnson signed a Purchase Agreement with Elalami for the property, and on July 26, 2023, both parties signed a Seller Counteroffer. (Complaint ¶ 22-23.) Plaintiffs claim they fully complied with their contractual obligations, but Elalami breached the agreement by not completing the sale and demanding additional payments. (Complaint ¶¶ 24-25.)

 

The Complaint also details a foreclosure on the property and a subsequent unlawful detainer action filed by Buy2Let Investments, LLC, resulting in a stipulated judgment on April 2, 2024. (Complaint ¶¶ 25-26.) Plaintiffs allege that they entered into this judgment based on Elalami’s promise to sell the property to them for $492,000, which included a carry-back lien. (Complaint ¶ 27.)

 

However, despite these efforts, Defendants point out that Elalami failed to complete the sale and continued to demand further payments. Plaintiffs argue that Elalami’s actions caused them significant financial harm, including paying excessive fees, legal expenses, and escrow costs. They allege that Elalami misused the corporate identities of Buy2Let and Luxor for his personal benefit, resulting in direct and consequential damages to Plaintiffs. (Complaint ¶¶ 32-29.)

 

Plaintiff has attached the Purchase Agreement, which shows the purchase price and escrow date, and is signed by both parties. (Complaint Exhibit B.) Therefore, the Court finds that a valid contract has been pleaded. However, the Court finds that the alleged oral contracts made during the unlawful detainer proceedings are barred by the Statute of Frauds. (Civil Code § 1624 subd. (a)(3); Complaint ¶¶ 27-28, 31.) Defendant argues that the doctrine of part performance overcomes the statute of frauds. However, making payments and securing finances is insufficient to show part performance; one must also show possession or improvement of the property. (Sutton v. Warner (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 415, 422.) Here, Plaintiffs have not pleaded any facts showing possession or improvement of the property. Therefore, part performance does not apply, and the only contracts sufficiently pleaded in the Complaint are the Purchase Agreement and Seller Counteroffer. (Complaint Exhibits B-C.)

 

The Complaint states that Plaintiffs “have duly performed all promises, conditions, and agreements on their part to be performed, as modified, under the contract.” (Complaint ¶ 34.) However, Defendants contend that the Residential Purchase Agreement stated that the close of escrow would take place 30 days after acceptance. While the Complaint states that Plaintiff has performed all aspects of the agreement, Plaintiffs have not pleaded that they performed all aspects of the agreement before the close of escrow as required by the Residential Purchase Agreement. (Complaint Exhibit B ¶ 3.) Therefore, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently pleaded facts to show that they fully performed the contract. (See, Bowden, supra, 67 Cal.App.3d 705, 718.)

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the Complaint as to the first cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 2 – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

            A claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing requires (1) the existence of a contractual relationship, (2) implied duty, (3) breach, (4) and causation of damages. (Smith v. San Francisco (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49.)

 

            Defendants argue that this claim should fail because it depends on the existence of a valid contract, which they argue does not exist.

 

            Plaintiffs argue that a valid contract has been adequately pleaded.

 

            The Complaint asserts that an implicit duty of good faith and fair dealing existed in the Purchase Agreement and its modifications. Plaintiffs claim that Defendants had a duty to complete the sale of the subject property in a fair and transparent manner, particularly regarding escrow requirements and communication. Plaintiffs accuse Defendants of failing to meet these obligations by improperly initiating a foreclosure sale and unlawful detainer action without legal authority. Plaintiffs argue that these actions were intended to harm them and deprive them of the benefits of the agreements, resulting in significant damage.

 

            While a breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing does not necessarily require a breach of an express contract, the scope of conduct prohibited by the implied covenant is circumscribed by the purposes and express terms of the contract. (Avidity Partners, LLC v. State of California (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 1180.) The Court has found that all modifications of the Purchase Agreement outside of the attached Seller Counteroffer are barred by the Statute of Frauds. Therefore, the scope of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is limited to the express terms and purpose of the Purchase Agreement. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants improperly foreclosed on the property, which they argue breached the implied covenant. However, the Plaintiffs have pleaded no facts to show how or why the foreclosure was improper. Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not pleaded facts sufficient facts to state a cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

            Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the second cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 3 – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

            A claim for negligent misrepresentation requires an assertion of an untrue fact; believed by defendant to be true; lack of reasonable ground for the belief; defendant’s intent to induce plaintiff’s reliance upon the representation; plaintiff’s justifiable reliance upon the representation; and resulting damage. (Melican v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 168, 182.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have failed to specify any false representations made by Defendants that Plaintiffs justifiably relied upon, which is required for fraud-based claims. Defendants emphasize that fraud claims must be pleaded with particularity, including the "who, what, when, where, and how" of the alleged misrepresentations. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  Additionally, when fraud claims are made against corporate defendants like Luxor Inc. and Buy2Let, there is an even higher standard. Plaintiffs must provide details such as the names of the individuals who made the statements, their authority to speak, and the specific content and timing of the representations, as required by (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co., 2 Cal. App. 4th 153.) Defendants further argue that any reliance on alleged statements was unreasonable, particularly in the absence of a written agreement.

 

In Opposition, Plaintiffs argue that their reliance on Defendants promises are reasonable.

 

The Complaint does not state with specificity which facts were misrepresented or concealed, to whom the facts were misrepresented or concealed, or when the facts were misrepresented or concealed. Thus, The Court finds that Plaintiffs have not met the heightened pleading standard required for fraud-based claims.

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the demurrer to the third cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 4 – Constructive Fraud

 

 A claim for constructive fraud requires a fiduciary, confidential, or special relationship, followed by a breach such as a nondisclosure, with the intent to deceive, resulting in reliance by the other party, causing harm or damages. (Younan v. Equifax Inc. (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 498, 516, 517.)

 

Defendants argue that the claim of constructive fraud fails because it requires a fiduciary or confidential relationship, which does not exist between the parties. As purchasers at a foreclosure sale and subsequent property owners, Defendants do not owe Plaintiffs any fiduciary duty or have a confidential relationship with them. (Tyler v. Children's Home Society (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 511, 548.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have not pleaded sufficient facts to state a cause of action for constructive fraud because they have not pleaded any facts showing a specific fiduciary relationship between the parties. (See, Tyler, supra, 29 Cal.App.4th 511, 548.)

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the fourth cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 5 – Fraudulent misrepresentation

 

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Lazar, Supra, 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)

 

Defendants argue that, like the negligent misrepresentation claim, Plaintiffs fail to meet the heightened pleading standard required for fraud. Plaintiffs have not provided specific details about the false statements, including who made them and when they were made. (Lazar, Supra, 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Additionally, there is no evidence showing that Defendants intended to defraud Plaintiffs at the time of the alleged representations.

 

Plaintiffs argue that they have adequately alleged the necessary elements of fraud in their fifth cause of action. (Complaint ¶¶ 76-82, 87-92.) They assert that Defendant Elalami, acting as an agent of Buy2Let and under his authority as an officer of Luxor, made false material misrepresentations to Plaintiffs orally, via text message, and email before the execution of the purchase agreement. (Complaint ¶ 83.)

 

The Complaint alleges that Defendants knowingly made false representations to Plaintiffs regarding the amount of funds paid by Plaintiffs prior to the execution of the contract orally and through text. (Complaint ¶ 83.)

 

Fraud claims must be pleaded with particularity, including the "who, what, when, where, and how" of the alleged misrepresentations. (Lazar, supra, 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) Plaintiffs had failed to specify to whom the statements were made and when the statements were made. The Complaint merely states that the misrepresentations were made to Plaintiffs before the execution of the contract. They do not specify to which Plaintiff the statements were made nor the time or location of the alleged misrepresentation. Thus, these statements do not meet the Court’s heightened standard for fraud pleadings.

 

While the Court would sustain the Demurrer to the fifth cause of action with leave to amend, it is granting the motion to strike this cause of action under CCP sec. 425.16.

 

Cause of Action 6 – Promissory Estoppel

 

A claim for Promissory Estoppel requires A promise, which the promisor should reasonably expect to induce action or forbearance of a definite and substantial character on the part of the promisee, induces such action or forbearance, and injustice can only be avoided by enforcing the promise. (C & K Eng'g Contractors v. Amber Steel Co. (1978) 23 Cal.3d 1, 6.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs cannot rely on promissory estoppel to bypass the statute of frauds because they have not demonstrated a clear and unambiguous promise from Defendants that would justify reliance. Moreover, Plaintiffs did not alter their position based on any oral agreement. While part performance can allow enforcement of a contract without a written agreement, this requires both partial performance and a significant change in position due to reliance, such that applying the statute of frauds would result in an unjust or unconscionable loss. (In re Marriage of Benson (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1096, 1108.)  Defendants assert that Plaintiffs' alleged down payment to escrow is insufficient to constitute part performance, as payment of money alone does not take an oral agreement out of the statute of frauds, and legal remedies are available for such payments. (Anderson v. Stansbury (1952) 38 Cal.2d 707, 715.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that they have adequately alleged the elements for their sixth cause of action. They claim to have actually and detrimentally relied on Defendants' promises, resulting in injuries amounting to at least $200,000. Plaintiffs distinguish their case from Secrest v. Nat. Mortg. Loan Trust 2002-2 (2008), by stating that unlike in Secrest, they altered their position by taking steps to secure a loan for the purchase of the property in reliance on Elalami's repeated promises. (Secrest v. Nat. Mortg. Loan Trust 2002-2 (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 544, 555.)

 

In reply, Plaintiffs argue that attempting to secure financing does constitute the type of substantial change in position required to invoke promissory estoppel. (Secrest, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th 544, 555.)

 

While Plaintiffs argue that they altered their position by attempting to secure financing, the Complaint does not allege this fact. Furthermore, attempting to secure financing is not a substantial change in one's position. (See, Secrest, supra, 167 Cal.App.4th 544, 555.) Thus, the Complaint has not alleged facts sufficient to state a claim for Promissory Estoppel.

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the sixth cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 7 – Quiet Title

 

A claim for quiet title requires a description of the property, the plaintiff's title or interest and its basis, the defendant's adverse claim or antagonistic property interest, the date as of which the determination is sought, and a prayer for determination of title. (Lucas v. Sweet (1956) 47 Cal.2d 20, 22.)

 

Defendants argue that the court can take judicial notice of the recorded documents, such as the Trustee Deed Upon Sale, which shows that Defendant Buy2Let holds valid title to the property through a legitimate foreclosure sale. As a bona fide purchaser, Defendant Buy2Let acquired the property by paying valuable consideration of $375, subject to a senior lien, and had no notice of any competing claims to the property. (See First Bank v. East West Bank (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1309, 1314.) Defendants emphasize that under the law, a bona fide purchaser at a foreclosure sale cannot have their title challenged by the trustor based on alleged irregularities in the sale. (See, Moeller v. Lien (1994) 25 Cal.App 4th 822, 831-832.) Thus, Defendants argue that the foreclosure sale is valid and cannot be rescinded.

 

Plaintiffs argue that their quiet title claim is based on the assertion that the April 20, 2023, foreclosure conducted by the Foreclosure Trustee is void because it did not comply with statutory requirements for a valid foreclosure. Therefore, Plaintiffs maintain that their cause of action has been validly pled. Plaintiffs cite no authority.

 

Because Defendants are bona fide purchasers, whether or not the Foreclosure Trustee did not comply with foreclosure statutes is immaterial. (See Moeller, supra, 25 Cal.App 4th 822, 831-832.) Thus, the claim fails as a matter of law.

 

Therefore, the Demurrer to the seventh cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

Cause of Action 8 – Wrongful Foreclosure and Wrongful Eviction

 

A claim for wrongful foreclosure requires that the trustee or mortgagee caused an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale of real property under the power of sale in a mortgage or trust deed, the trustor, mortgagor, or another party attacking the sale was prejudiced or harmed, and the trustor or mortgagor tendered the amount of the secured indebtedness or was excused from doing so. (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 408.)

 

A claim for wrongful eviction requires that the action was commenced by or at the direction of the defendant, pursued to a legal termination favorable to the plaintiff, brought without probable cause, and initiated with malice. (Asell v. Rodrigues (1973) 32 Cal.App.3d 817, 824.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs cannot sue them for wrongful foreclosure because none of the Defendants conducted the foreclosure, as shown by the Trustee Deed Upon Sale, which has been judicially noticed. Plaintiffs have not demonstrated any irregularities in the foreclosure process carried out by National Default Servicing Corporation, nor have they named that entity as a Defendant. Furthermore, Plaintiffs did not make or allege tender of the secured indebtedness, a requirement to challenge foreclosure irregularities. (Abdallah v. United Savings Bank (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1101, 1109.)  Defendants also assert that Plaintiffs cannot pursue a wrongful eviction claim due to collateral estoppel, as the unlawful detainer judgment conclusively determined the right to possession, preventing Plaintiffs from relitigating the issue.

 

Plaintiffs argue that the foreclosure was wrongful due to the Foreclosure Trustee's failure to comply with foreclosure statutes. They assert that the burden is on Defendants to prove they were bona fide purchasers of the property at a valid foreclosure sale. Plaintiffs again cite no authority.

 

The Court finds that the claim fails as a matter of law because Defendants did not conduct the foreclosure sale.

 

Therefore, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the eighth cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Cause of action 9 – Injunctive Relief, Disgorgement under Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq.

 

A claim for an injunction requires a wrongful act stating a cause of action and a basis for equitable relief, such as a threat of irreparable harm or the inadequacy of a remedy at law. (Brownfield v. Daniel Freeman Marina Hosp. (2d Dist. 1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 405, 410.)

 

Plaintiffs argue that they have not established grounds for equitable relief, as Defendants lawfully obtained possession of the property. In considering whether to issue a preliminary injunction, courts assess two factors: the likelihood that Plaintiffs will prevail at trial and the balance of harm between the parties. Plaintiffs have not shown any ongoing conduct to enjoin or any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts by Defendants, as required under the Unfair Competition Law. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.)

 

Defendants argue that the Unfair Completion Law allows civil recovery for unlawful, unfair, deceptive, or misleading practices. To qualify as an unfair business practice, the public policy supporting the claim must be linked to specific constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provisions. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A fraudulent practice under the UCL only requires showing that members of the public are likely to be deceived. Defendants claim that Plaintiffs have clearly alleged a violation of the UCL based on unfair and fraudulent practices, as outlined in their complaint.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have not alleged any facts showing unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business acts by Defendants. While Plaintiffs acknowledge that their cause of action must be connected to specific constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provisions, they fail to identify any law that was violated.

 

The Complaint has not provided the specific constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provisions which Defendants have violated. Thus, the claim fails as a matter of law.

 

Therefore, the Demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.

 

Cause of action 10 – Declaratory relief

 

 

“The fundamental basis of declaratory relief is the existence of an actual, present controversy over a proper subject.” (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 79) Courts do not issue advisory opinions about the rights and duties of parties under agreements if no actual, justiciable controversy has yet developed. (Younger v. Superior Court (1978) 21 Cal.3d 102, 119–120; Otay Land Co. v. Royal Indemnity Co. (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 556, 562.)

 

Defendants argue that declaratory relief is unwarranted because there is no ongoing, present controversy between the parties. The rights and obligations regarding the property have already been adjudicated through the foreclosure and unlawful detainer proceedings, including six post-judgment proceedings, resolving the matter.

 

Plaintiffs argue that, contrary to Defendants' assertion, they have clearly alleged a present and ongoing controversy regarding the parties' rights under the contract. They assert that a complaint for declaratory relief is sufficient if it presents facts showing an actual controversy related to the legal rights and duties of the parties under a written agreement, requesting the court to adjudicate those rights.

 

The Court has found that Plaintiffs have not sufficiently pled that they performed the contract in the escrow period. Thus, because the contract was not performed, there is no current justiciable controversy. (Younger v. Superior Court (1978) 21 Cal.3d 102, 119–120.)

 

While the Court would sustain the Demurrer to the tenth cause of action with leave to amend, it is granting the motion to strike this cause of action under CCP sec. 425.16.

 

Cause of Action Eleven – Preliminary and Permanent Injunction

 

Injunctive relief is a remedy, not a cause of action. (Shell Oil Co. v. Richter (1942) 52 Cal.App.2d 164, 168.)

 

Here, since Plaintiffs’ actions have not been sufficiently pleaded, the request for injunction also failed.

 

While the Court would sustain the Demurrer to the eleventh cause of action with leave to amend, it is granting the motion to strike this cause of action under CCP sec. 425.16.
