DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 18STCV05377    Hearing Date: August 2, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

freddy m., et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

joe oroÑoz, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

18STCV05377

Hearing Date:

August 2, 2024

Hearing Time:   2:00 p.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANT PI PROPERTIES NO. 79, LLC’S MOTION TO DISMISS FOR FAILURE TO BRING THE CASE TO TRIAL WITHIN THE PERIOD PRESCRIBED BY STATUTE

AND RELATED CROSS-ACTION

 

           

Background

Plaintiff Freddy M., by and through his guardian ad litem, Paola Mejia, Noe Reyes Lopez, and Paola Mejia (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this action on November 16, 2018 against Defendants Joe Oroñoz and Sarah Oroñoz. On February 13, 2019, Plaintiffs filed an amendment to the Complaint naming PI Properties No. 79, LLC (“PI Properties”) in place of “Doe 21.”

Plaintiffs filed the operative Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on June 26, 2020, alleging causes of action for (1) breach of implied warranty of habitability, (2) tortious breach of implied warranty of habitability, (3) negligence, (4) private nuisance, and (5) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200. This action concerns alleged uninhabitable conditions at 6429 ½ S. Victoria Avenue, Los Angeles, California 90043 (the “Subject Property”), where Plaintiffs reside. (TAC, ¶¶ 1, 16.) Plaintiffs allege, inter alia, that deteriorated lead-based paint and other lead hazards at the Subject Property resulted in Freddy M. contracting lead poisoning and suffering permanent neurological, cognitive, and behavioral harm… (TAC,

¶ 70.)

On June 29, 2020, PI Properties filed a Cross-Complaint against a number of cross-defendants, alleging causes of action for (1) declaratory relief, (2) implied equitable indemnity, and (3) negligence.    

PI Properties now moves “for an order to dismiss the above-entitled action for failure to bring the case to trial within the period prescribed by the statute, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 583.310 and 583.360(a).” Plaintiffs oppose.

Discussion

Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310 provides, “[a]n action shall be brought to trial within five years after the action is commenced against the defendant.” Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.360, “(a) An action shall be dismissed by the court on its own motion or on motion of the defendant, after notice to the parties, if the action is not brought to trial within the time prescribed in this article. (b) The requirements of this article are mandatory and are not subject to extension, excuse, or exception except as expressly provided by statute.”

“In computing the time within which an action must be brought to trial pursuant to this article, there shall be excluded the time during which any of the following conditions existed: (a) The jurisdiction of the court to try the action was suspended. (b) Prosecution or trial of the action was stayed or enjoined. (c) Bringing the action to trial, for any other reason, was impossible, impracticable, or futile.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.340.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 583.330, “[t]he parties may extend the time within which an action must be brought to trial pursuant to this article by…written stipulation” and “[b]y oral agreement made in open court, if entered in the minutes of the court or a transcript is made.”

In addition, pursuant to Emergency Rule 10(a), “[n]otwithstanding any other law, including Code of Civil Procedure section 583.310, for all civil actions filed on or before April 6, 2020, the time in which to bring the action to trial is extended by six months for a total time of five years and six months.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Appen. 1, Emergency Rule 10(a).)

As set forth above, the instant action was filed on November 16, 2018. In her supporting declaration, PI Properties’ counsel states that “[o]n April 6, 2020, the Judicial Council of California enacted emergency rules due to the Covid-19 pandemic, which extended the time frame to bring this lawsuit to trial by six months, for a total of five years and six months. Hence, the statutory deadline to bring this action to trial was May 16, 2024.” (Ciminelli Decl., ¶ 4.) Trial in this matter is currently set for August 7, 2024.

 PI Properties asserts that “[a]s of the date of filing this Motion, the lawsuit has yet to be brought to trial and Plaintiffs have not shown an extension, excuse, or exception to justify their failure. Therefore, dismissal is mandatory…” (Mot. at p. 11:24-26.)

In the opposition, Plaintiffs assert that “court congestion prevented this case from going to trial and tolled the ‘five-year statute’…” (Opp’n at p. 4:11-12.) As set forth above, “[i]n computing the time within which an action must be brought to trial pursuant to this article, there shall be excluded the time during which any of the following conditions existed:…(c) Bringing the action to trial, for any other reason, was impossible, impracticable, or futile.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.340, subd. (c).) Plaintiffs cite to Hattersley v. American Nucleonics Corp. (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 397, 401, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[i]n Chin v. Meier, supra, 235 Cal.App.3d 1373, we held that section 583.340, subdivision (c) tolling includes the aggregate time a case is continued because of courtroom unavailability.” The Hattersley Court noted that “[c]ourt congestion is not a valid basis for denying a litigant his or her day in court.” (Id. at p. 402.)

In his declaration in support of the opposition, Plaintiffs’ counsel states that “[o]n January 26, 2024, the court heard the Final Status Conference (‘FSC’) on this matter…As of this date, a jury trial was set to begin on February 14, 2024.” (Victoria Decl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that he “appeared at the FSC prepared to participate in the FSC and start trial on February 14, 2024.” (Victoria Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiffs’ counsel states that “[c]ounsel for Defendant, Mr. Caliguri, requested the court continue the trial date due to his unavailability, as trial counsel, due to his wife’s pregnancy and impending delivery date and his expected paternity leave, which coincided with the February 14, 2024 trial date. Plaintiffs’ counsel did not object to defense counsel’s request given the basis of the request but did raise the issue of the five-year statute at the FSC.” (Victoria Decl., ¶ 10.)

The Court’s January 26, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]his case came from the PI Hub and the Court intends to send the case for trial to the trial courts if available as previously represented to the parties. Pursuant to the request of defendant, the Final Status Conference scheduled for 01/26/2024 is continued to 03/14/24 at 02:00 PM in Department 50 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse…Pursuant to the request of defendant, the Jury Trial scheduled for 02/14/2024 is continued to 04/17/24 at 08:30 AM in Department 50 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse.”

Plaintiffs’ counsel states that on March 14, 2024 he attended the FSC, and “appeared at the FSC prepared to participate in the FSC and start trial on April 17, 2024.” (Victoria Decl., ¶¶ 12, 13.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[a]t the FSC, the court informed the parties that Department 1 may not be able to take the case to trial as scheduled on April 17, 2024…As such, the parties agreed to have the court act as the trial court in order to get a firm trial date of August 7, 2024. This was the first available trial date the court had for the parties. As such, the court set the current August 7, 2024 trial date pursuant to stipulation of both parties (Plaintiffs and Defendant).” (Victoria Decl., ¶¶ 14-15.)

The Court’s March 14, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Court informs the parties that Department 1 may not be able to take this case. The parties agree as does the Court that the case is now assigned to Department 50 and the time to file a 170.6 runs from today’s date. Pursuant to the discussion with Counsel re: available trial dates the trial is continued as follows:…Final Status Conference is continued to 06/28/24 at 02:00 PM in Department 50 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse. Jury Trial is continued to 08/07/24 at 09:30 AM in Department 50 at Stanley Mosk Courthouse.” Plaintiffs assert that “due to court congestion, this matter has been ‘tolling’ since the inability of this court to take this case to trial on April 17, 2024.” (Opp’n at p. 6:15-16.)

In the motion and in the reply, PI Properties cites to Gaines v. Fidelity National Title Ins. Co. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1081, 1100, where the California Supreme Court noted that “[a] circumstance that does not qualify for automatic tolling under section 583.340(b) may nonetheless be excludable from the five-year period if the circumstance makes it ‘impossible, impracticable, or futile’ to bring the action to trial. (§ 583.340(c); accord, Bruns, supra, 51 Cal.4th at p. 726.) Gaines invokes this provision. In deciding whether these exceptions are met, the court must consider ‘all the circumstances in the individual case, including the acts and conduct of the parties and the nature of the proceedings themselves…The critical factor in applying these exceptions to a given factual situation is whether the plaintiff exercised reasonable diligence in prosecuting his or her case.’”

PI Properties argues that here, “Plaintiffs failed to timely commence trial due to their lack of due reasonable diligence throughout the lifetime of the case.” (Reply at p. 3:13-14.) As set forth above, Plaintiffs’ counsel states that he raised the issue of the five-year statute at the January 26, 2024 FSC. (Victoria Decl., ¶ 10.) PI Properties asserts that “[t]here was plenty of time and opportunity before and after the January 26, 2024, FSC to seek an order to advance the trial date, preferentially set the trial[1], or obtain a stipulation to waive the five-year statute.” (Reply at p. 3:24-26.) However, as set forth above, Plaintiffs’ counsel states that he appeared at the January 26, 2024 FSC “prepared to participate in the FSC and start trial on February 14, 2024,” and that he appeared at the March 14, 2024 FSC “prepared to participate in the FSC and start trial on April 17, 2024.” (Victoria Decl., ¶¶ 8, 13.) In addition, as discussed, Plaintiffs’ counsel states that he raised the issue of the five-year statute at the January 26, 2024 FSC. (Victoria Decl., ¶ 10.) The Court finds that reasonable diligence has been sufficiently shown.

PI Properties also asserts that “the period between April 17, 2024, and August 7, 2024 is not tolled.” (Reply at p. 4:17.) PI Properties cites to Oswald v. Landmark Builders, Inc. (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 240, 248, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[t]he unavailability of courtrooms for trial does not automatically lead to a finding of an impossible or impractical circumstance under section 583.340(c). Rather, the trial court is tasked with determining the extent to which the unavailability of courtrooms for trial interfered with a plaintiff’s ability to mov[e] the case to trial during the relevant period[s]. We conclude the trial court here acted well within its discretion in determining that the courtroom closure and trial continuance at issue did not make it illegally impossible or impractical for plaintiffs to commence trial in a timely fashion. Rather, plaintiffs’ failure to timely commence trial was due to a lack of reasonable diligence, i.e., they were never ready for trial during the relevant periods.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.) But here, as discussed, Plaintiffs’ counsel states that he was prepared to start trial on February 14, 2024 and on April 17, 2024.” (Victoria Decl., ¶¶ 8, 13.)[2]

PI Properties also asserts that “the March 14, 2024, Minute Order says nothing about courtroom congestion…Nothing in the Order indicates that Department 50 could not take this case to trial before the statutory deadline.” (Reply at p. 5:15-19.) As discussed, the March 14, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Court informs the parties that Department 1 may not be able to take this case. The parties agree as does the Court that the case is now assigned to Department 50…Pursuant to the discussion with Counsel re: available trial dates the trial is continued as follows:…Jury Trial is continued to 08/07/24…” However, as discussed, Plaintiffs’ counsel states in his declaration that “[a]t the FSC, the court informed the parties that Department 1 may not be able to take the case to trial as scheduled on April 17, 2024…As such the parties agreed to have the court act as the trial court in order to get a firm trial date of August 7, 2024. This was the first available trial date the court had for the parties. As such, the court set the current August 7, 2024 trial date pursuant to stipulation of both parties…” (Victoria Decl., ¶¶ 14-15, emphasis added.)

In light of the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have shown that Code of Civil Procedure section 583.340, subdivision (c) tolling applies under the circumstances here. As set forth above, “section 583.340, subdivision (c) tolling includes the aggregate time a case is continued because of courtroom unavailability.” ((Hattersley v. American Nucleonics Corp., supra, 3 Cal.App.4th at p. 401.) Thus, the Court does not find that PI Properties has shown that the instant action must be dismissed because it was “not brought to trial within the time prescribed in [Code of Civil Procedure sections 583.310-583.360].” (Code Civ. Proc., § 583.360, subd. (a).)

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies PI Properties’ motion to dismiss.

The Court orders Plaintiffs to give notice of this ruling.

MATTHEW PORPORA v. HORRIGAN COLE ENTERPRISES, INC. DBA COLE VOCATIONAL SERVICES, CO.,

ET AL. (20STCV27361)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: David R. Denis, Armando M. Galvan (Law Offices of David R. Denis, P.C.)

Counsel for NM Defendants/opposing parties: Bradley E. Schwan, James Payer (Littler Mendelson, P.C.)

 

PLAINTIFF MATTHEW PORPORA’S AMENDED MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION OF ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION (filed 07/12/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s motion is denied.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 21, 2020, Plaintiff Matthew Porpora (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against a number of defendants. Plaintiff’s Complaint alleges causes of action for (1) violation of Health and Safety Code section 1432, (2) violation of Health and Safety Code section 1278.5, (3) violation of Labor Code section 1102.5, and (4) wrongful termination in violation of public policy.  

 

On April 7, 2022, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the Complaint naming National Mentor Healthcare, LLC in place of “Doe 1.” On November 2, 2022, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the Complaint naming National Mentor Holdings, LLC in place of “Doe 2.” On November 2, 2022, Plaintiff also filed an amendment to the Complaint naming National Mentor Holdings, Inc. in place of “Doe 3.”

 

Defendants National Mentor Healthcare, LLC, National Mentor Holdings, Inc., and National Mentor Holdings, LLC (collectively, the “NM Defendants”) previously moved for summary adjudication on the first and second causes of action of the Complaint, and on claims for punitive damages and attorney fees. Plaintiff opposed.

 

On November 9, 2023, the Court issued a minute order providing, inter alia, that “the NM Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication is granted as to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action, Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages, and Plaintiff’s claim for attorney’s fees in connection with his first, second, and third causes of action. The NM Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication of Plaintiff’s claim for attorney’s fees in connection with the fourth cause of action is denied. The Clerk is to provide notice to counsel for Plaintiff, who is to give notice to all parties.”

 

On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant amended motion “for order of reconsideration of, and to withdraw the Court’s November 9, 2023 Order…denying, in part, and granting, in part, the motion of the NM Defendants, for summary adjudication…” Plaintiff’s motion argues:

·       “Reconsideration of the adjudication order is warranted with respect to the first and second causes of action because it is based on the erroneous assumption of fact, induced by misleading defense argument, that ‘Plaintiff’ ‘Solely’ Relied ‘on Evidence that NMH Provided Support Services’.” (Mot. at p. 10:1-3.)

 

·       “Reconsideration is warranted as to the punitive damages claim because the order was based on erroneous assumptions of fact and ‘bad law’ induced by misleading defense argument, which the Court can now rectify via a reconsideration order.” (Mot. at p. 12:6-9.)

 

·       “Reconsideration of the adjudication order is warranted as to the claim of attorney fees pertaining to Labor Code section 1102.5 because it is based on the erroneous assumption, that it has an impermissible retroactive effect when applied to this case, and recent controlling case law justify reconsideration.” (Mot. at p. 15:14-18.)

 

·       “Plaintiff Porpora’s motion for reconsideration of the extraordinary order of November 9, 2023 granting summary adjudication is timely and procedurally proper.” (Mot. at p. 17:3-4.)

On July 24, 2024, the NM Defendants filed their opposition, arguing:

·       “Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is late on multiple grounds and should be denied.” (Opp’n at p. 9:8.)

 

·       “Plaintiff’s motion also fails to identify any new law or facts to support his motion.” (Opp’n at p. 11:15-16.)  

 

·       Plaintiff’s attorney affidavit does not meet the requirements of Section 1008.” (Opp’n at p. 13:1.)

 

·       Plaintiff’s motion asking the Court to reconsider its ruling based on its inherent authority is procedurally improper.” (Opp’n at p. 13:15-16.)

On July 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed his reply, arguing:

·       “No authority cited by Defendant refutes that Plaintiff had ‘ten days from service of’ his June 19, 2024 notice of entry of order to file his motion for reconsideration; Porpora did just that, thereby rendering his statutory motion timely.” (Reply at p. 4:3-5.)

 

·       “Reconsideration of the adjudication order is warranted given the erroneous assumption of facts, induced by misleading defense argument, that led to the order.” (Reply at p. 5:15-17.)

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

The Court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice.

 

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a)¿ provides as follows: 

 

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (e), “[t]his section specifies the court’s jurisdiction with regard to applications for reconsideration of its orders and renewals of previous motions, and applies to all applications to reconsider any order of a judge or court, or for the renewal of a previous motion, whether the order deciding the previous matter or motion is interim or final. No application to reconsider any order or for the renewal of a previous motion may be considered by any judge or court unless made according to this section.”

 

The legislative intent was to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered, and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (¿¿Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500¿¿; ¿¿Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342 [“[T]he party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.”]¿¿; ¿New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-213 [“The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.”]¿.)

 

B.      Procedural Issues

 

Timeliness

 

As an initial matter, the NM Defendants assert that Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is late on multiple grounds.

 

As noted by the NM Defendants, the Court’s November 9, 2023 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Clerk is to provide notice to counsel for Plaintiff, who is to give notice to all parties. Certificate of Mailing is attached.”

 

A Certificate of Mailing was filed on November 9, 2023 providing, inter alia, that “I, the below-named Executive Officer/Clerk of the above-entitled court, do hereby certify that I am not a party to the cause herein, and that on this date I served the Minute Order (Hearing on Motion for Summary Adjudication continued from 8/1...) of 11/09/2023 upon each party or counsel named below by placing the  document for collection and mailing so as to cause it to be deposited in the United States mail at the courthouse in Los Angeles, California, one copy of the original filed/entered herein in a separate sealed envelope to each address as shown below with the postage thereon fully prepaid, in accordance with  standard court practices.” “David Robert Denis” is listed on the Certificate of Mailing, which is dated November 9, 2023.

 

The NM Defendants assert that “[a]ccordingly, the clerk’s service of the Order triggered the 10-day period, which, after accounting for service via mail, would have expired on November 24, 2023.” (Opp’n. at p. 9:24-25.) 15 days after November 9, 2023 is November 24, 2023.

 

In the motion, Plaintiff asserts that “no written notice of entry of the order of November 9, 2023, granting NM Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication issued and was served until June 19, 2024. The Court ordered that notice of the order be given; a Notice of Entry of that Order was served electronically on June 19, 2024, making tenth day from ‘service of’ that notice, Tuesday, July 2, 2024. Hence, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is timely.” (Mot. at p. 17:19-23.)

 

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Entry of Order Denying in Part and Granting in Part Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication. The proof of service attached to this notice indicates that the notice was served on June 19, 2024 by email. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, subdivision (a)(3)(B), “[a]ny period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days…”

 

With respect to the 10-day time limit set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a), ten days after June 19, 2024 is June 29, 2024. Two court days after June 29, 2024 is July 2, 2024.  

 

However, the instant amended motion for reconsideration was filed on July 12, 2024. The docket for this case also shows that Plaintiff filed an initial motion for reconsideration on July 5, 2024. The docket only shows that Plaintiff’s “Request for Judicial Notice in Support of Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration of Order Granting Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of Issues” (Parts 1 and 2) were filed on July 2, 2024.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court does not find that Plaintiff has shown that his motion for reconsideration is timely under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a).  

 

Service and Filing Requirements

 

In addition, as noted by the NM Defendants, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b), “[u]nless otherwise ordered or specifically provided by law, all moving and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least 16 court days before the hearing.”

 

Here, 16 court days prior to the August 2, 2024 hearing on the instant motion is July 11, 2024. However, the instant motion was filed on July 12, 2024.

 

In addition, the proof of service attached to the motion indicates that it was served by email on July 11, 2024. However, as set forth above, “[a]ny period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(B). 16 court days prior to August 2, 2024 is July 11, 2024, and two court days prior to July 11, 2024 (for electronic service) is July 9, 2024.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court agrees with the NM Defendants that the instant motion was untimely filed and served.

 

New or Different Facts, Circumstances, or Law

 

In addition, as noted by the NM Defendants, Plaintiff does not appear to explain in the motion what “new or different facts, circumstances, or law” are purportedly applicable here under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a).

 

Further, as set forth above, “[t]he party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).) Here, the Declaration of Armando M. Galvan in support of the motion does not state “what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Ibid.)

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff filed second declaration in support of the reply. However, “¿[t]he general rule of motion practice…is that new evidence is not permitted with reply papers.¿”¿(¿Jay¿v. Mahaffey

 (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537.) Moreover, the Declaration of David R. Denis in support of the reply still does not appear to clearly state what “new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 128, subdivision (a)(8)

 

Plaintiff also moves for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 128, subdivision (a)(8), which provides that “[e]very court shall have the power to do all of the following:…(8) To amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice.” The NM Defendants assert that “Plaintiff’s motion asking the Court to reconsider its ruling based on its inherent authority is procedurally improper.” (Opp’n at p. 13:15-16.)

 

In Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1108, cited by both parties, the California Supreme Court noted that “[w]e cannot prevent a party from communicating the view to a court that it should reconsider a prior ruling (although any such communication should never be ex parte). We agree that it should not matter whether the judge has an unprovoked flash of understanding in the middle of the night or acts in response to a party’s suggestion. If a court believes one of its prior interim orders was erroneous, it should be able to correct that error no matter how it came to acquire that belief. For example, nothing would prevent the losing party from asking the court at a status conference to reconsider a ruling. But a party may not file a written motion to reconsider that has procedural significance if it does not satisfy the requirements of section 437c, subdivision (f)(2), or 1008.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted, italics in original, underline added.)

 

C.      Conclusion

 

In light of the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied.

DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

           

Background

Plaintiff Meir Levy (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on May 17, 2022 against Defendants Tova Fagan and Yael Sima Wintner.

On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) extortion, (2) conspiracy to commit fraud, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

On July 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Amendment to Complaint naming Pinchas (Paul) Wintner in place of Doe 1.

Tova Fagan and Yael Sima Wintner demurred to each of the causes of action of the FAC, and moved to strike portions of the FAC. On February 16, 2024, the Court issued an Order sustaining Tova Fagan and Yael Sima Wintner’s demurrer to the first and second causes of action of the FAC, with leave to amend. The demurrer to the fourth cause of action of the FAC was sustained, without leave to amend. The demurrer to the third cause of action of the FAC was overruled. In addition, the Court denied the motion to strike paragraph 29 of the FAC as moot, and denied the motion to strike paragraph 47 of the FAC and paragraph 3 of the Prayer for Relief. (See February 16, 2024 Order.)

On March 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) extortion, (2) conspiracy to commit fraud and/or extortion, and (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Tova Fagan, Yael Sima Wintner, and Pinchas (Paul) Wintner (collectively, “Defendants”) now demur to the first and second causes of action of the SAC. Plaintiff opposes.

Discussion

A.    Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. ((Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” ((C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. ((Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” ((Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)

B.     Allegations of the SAC

In the SAC, Plaintiff alleges that he and the Defendants’ mother Sarah Wintner were married on December 25, 1983. (SAC, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants Tova Fagan, Yael Wintner and Paul Wintner are the stepchildren of Plaintiff Meir Levy and the biological children of Decedent, Sarah Wintner.” (SAC, ¶ 16.)

Plaintiff alleges that “[o]n or about December 12, 2018, Defendants discovered Plaintiff and Sarah Wintner were preparing a new trust. The Defendants discovered they were not going to inherit the maternal home and other assets.” (SAC, ¶ 17.) “On or about July 2, 2019, Plaintiff and Decedent Sarah Wintner executed The Meir Levy and Sarah C. Wintner 2019 Trust (‘2019 Trust.’).” (SAC, ¶ 19.) “When the Defendants discovered the contents of the 2019 Trust, they became irate, irrational, volatile and angry. They began to harass Plaintiff and their dying mother Sarah.” (SAC, ¶ 20.)

Plaintiff alleges that “Defendant Yael Wintner became increasingly angry, threatening to buy a gun and shoot Plaintiff Meir Levy in the head. She also threatened to kill David Levy, the son of Plaintiff and decedent Sarah Wintner, and his then girlfriend.” (SAC, ¶ 23.) Plaintiff alleges that “[i]n or around December, 2019, after discovering the distribution of assets per the 2019 Trust, the Wintner Defendants threatened Plaintiff Meir Levy with criminal accusations of childhood rape if Plaintiff Levy did not change the 2019 Trust to give the Wintner Defendants more assets.” (SAC, ¶ 24.)

C.     First Cause of Action for Civil Extortion

Defendants assert that the first cause of action for civil extortion fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

Defendants cite to Fuhrman v. California Satellite Systems (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 408, 426, disapproved of on other grounds by Silberg v. Anderson (1990) 50 Cal.3d 205, 219. In Fuhrman, the Court of Appeal noted that “[h]owever denominated (e.g., extortion, menace, duress), our Supreme Court has recognized a cause of action for the recovery of money obtained by the wrongful threat of criminal or civil prosecution. It is essentially a cause of action for moneys obtained by duress, a form of fraud. To be actionable the threat of prosecution must be made with the knowledge of the falsity of the claim. In her first cause of action, plaintiff does not allege defendants knew their claims were false, other than a general statement that their conduct amounted to fraud. This can be cured by amendment. The fatal flaw in plaintiff’s action is that she apparently never paid the money defendants demanded in their letters. The only actual damages plaintiff alleges are her emotional distress and attorney fees.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted, emphasis in original.) Defendants assert that here, Plaintiff “has not, and cannot possibly, allege that he paid anything to any Defendant. Therefore, he has no cause of action for extortion…” (Demurrer at p. 6:18-19.)

Defendants also assert that “Plaintiff made an amendment to the SAC stating explicitly that he did not suffer any loss of money or property, and neither did the family Trust…” (Demurrer at p. 7:8-9.) Defendants point to paragraph 27 of the SAC, which alleges that “Defendants used threats of violence and criminal prosecution, and malicious fraudulent allegations in an effort to coerce Plaintiff Meir Levy to amend or alter the terms of the 2019 Trust to allow each Defendant to inherit an even larger portion of the 2019 Trust than was originally intended by their deceased Mother and Plaintiff Meir Levy, their Stepfather. They sought to have Plaintiff Meir Levy change the Trust to provide them more assets from both Plaintiff and their mother to the detriment of Plaintiff and resulting in him retaining less assets upon his wife’s death. Such actions as demanded by Defendants would have amounted to Plaintiff losing money, losing items of value and losing interests in real property that he and his wife owned or controlled and which they had placed into the Trust.” (SAC, ¶ 27.) Defendants emphasize that this paragraph alleges that “[s]uch actions as demanded by Defendants would have amounted to Plaintiff losing money…” (SAC, ¶ 27, emphasis added.)

In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that “Defendants argue that because Plaintiff did not pay anything to Defendants, there is no extortion. However, this argument is belied by the case law…” (Opp’n at p. 6:6-7.) Plaintiff cites to Flatley v. Mauro (2006) 39 Cal.4th 299, 305, where “Plaintiff Michael Flatley…sued defendant D. Dean Mauro, an attorney, for civil extortion, intentional infliction of emotional distress and wrongful interference with economic advantage. Flatley’s action was based on a demand letter Mauro sent to Flatley on behalf of Tyna Marie Robertson, a woman who claimed that Flatley had raped her, and on subsequent telephone calls Mauro made to Flatley’s attorneys, demanding a seven-figure payment to settle Robertson’s claims.” The Court of Appeal concluded that “Mauro’s communications constituted criminal extortion as a matter of law and, as such, were unprotected by constitutional guarantees of free speech or petition.” (Ibid.)

Plaintiff notes that the Flatley Court cited Penal Code section 523, which provides that “[e]very person who, with intent to extort any money or other property from another, sends or delivers to any person any letter or other writing, whether subscribed or not, expressing or implying, or adapted to imply, any threat such as is specified in Section 519, is punishable in the same manner as if such money or property were actually obtained by means of such threat.” (Flatley v. Mauro, supra, 39 Cal.4th at p. 326, citing Pen. Code, § 523.) Defendants did not file any reply in support of the demurrer and thus do not address the Flatley case or Penal Code section 523.

In their prior demurrer to the FAC, Tova Fagan and Yael Sima Wintner asserted that “it is unclear if any changes in the ‘2019 Trust’ would have amounted to Plaintiff losing any money or items of value to begin with. This was not an alleged threat for Plaintiff to pay anything out of his own pocket, and it was never alleged that Plaintiff himself will ever lose money or anything of value.” (February 16, 2024 Order at p. 4:21-24, citing Demurrer at p. 7:25-28.) Now, the SAC alleges that “Defendants used threats of violence and criminal prosecution, and malicious fraudulent allegations in an effort to coerce Plaintiff Meir Levy to amend or alter the terms of the 2019 Trust to allow each Defendant to inherit an even larger portion of the 2019 Trust than was originally intended by their deceased Mother and Plaintiff Meir Levy, their Stepfather. They sought to have Plaintiff Meir Levy change the Trust to provide them more assets from both Plaintiff and their mother to the detriment of Plaintiff and resulting in him retaining less assets upon his wife’s death. Such actions as demanded by Defendants would have amounted to Plaintiff losing money, losing items of value and losing interests in real property that he and his wife owned or controlled and which they had placed into the Trust.” (SAC, ¶ 27, emphasis added.)

In Flatley, the Court of Appeal noted that “[e]xtortion is the obtaining of property from another, with his consent…induced by a wrongful use of force or fear…Every person who, with intent to extort any money or other property from another, sends or delivers to any person any letter or other writing, whether subscribed or not, expressing or implying, or adapted to imply, any threat such as is specified in Section 519, is punishable in the same manner as if such money or property were actually obtained by means of such threat.” (Flatley v. Mauro, supra, 39 Cal.4th at p. 326 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

            Based on the foregoing, the Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action of the SAC.  

D.    Second Cause of Action for Conspiracy to Commit Fraud and/or Extortion

In the second cause of action for conspiracy to commit fraud and/or extortion, Plaintiff alleges, inter alia, that “[t]he mere fact that the Wintner Defendants would use fabricated lies, such as childhood rape, verbal threats of violence to Plaintiff and maliciously and intentionally threaten to kill Plaintiff if they (the Wintner Defendants) did not get exactly what they desired or deemed to be fair as their inheritance is a definitive example of Conspiracy to Commit Fraud and/or a Conspiracy to Commit Extortion.” (SAC, ¶ 35.)

The Court notes that in AREI II Cases (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1021-1022, the Court of Appeal found that “[c]onspiracy is not a cause of action, but a legal doctrine that imposes liability on persons who, although not actually committing a tort themselves, share with the immediate tortfeasors a common plan or design in its perpetration. A civil conspiracy must be activated by the commission of an actual tort. In this case, the underlying tort is common law fraud. The elements of common law fraud are: (1) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (or scienter); (3) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. Morgan Keegan does not dispute that the complaint adequately states a cause of action for fraud against AREI. Instead, Morgan Keegan argues that the conspiracy allegations are insufficient to establish that it should bear liability for the actions of AREI. To support a conspiracy claim, a plaintiff must allege the following elements: (1) the formation and operation of the conspiracy, (2) wrongful conduct in furtherance of the conspiracy, and (3) damages arising from the wrongful conduct.” (Internal quotations and citations omitted.)

In the demurrer, Defendants cite to Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645, where the California Supreme Court noted that “[i]n California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice…This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Internal quotations, citations, and emphasis omitted.) Defendants appear to assert that the SAC does not meet this standard, but do not provide analysis on this point. In addition, as discussed, Plaintiffs allege “[i]n or around December, 2019, after discovering the distribution of assets per the 2019 Trust, the Wintner Defendants threatened Plaintiff Meir Levy with criminal accusations of childhood rape if Plaintiff Levy did not change the 2019 Trust to give the Wintner Defendants more assets…Defendant Yael Wintner sent Plaintiff Meir Levy a text message stating that the Wintner Defendants were ‘entitled to the same as David!!!’ and ‘I will put u in jail for raping us and all the abuse before I allow this to happen.’” (SAC, ¶¶ 24-25).[1]

Defendants also assert that Plaintiff “makes zero allegation of detrimental reliance upon a misrepresentation.” (Demurrer at p. 8:6-7.) Plaintiff now alleges that “[a]lthough Plaintiff knew that the underlying allegations of childhood rape were false, he believed that the Defendants would carry out their threats of reporting him to authorities. Defendants made these threats to induce Plaintiff to change the terms of the Trust so they would receive a larger share to the detriment of Plaintiff. Plaintiff relied on the seriousness of the threats, and as a result, Plaintiff changed his estate planning and made defendants the beneficiaries under his term life insurance policy with his wife, who previously was the only beneficiary.” (SAC, ¶ 41.) Defendants assert that “a plaintiff cannot claim justifiable reliance on representations that he or she knows to be false.” (Demurrer at p. 8:21-22.) Defendants cite to Rochlis v. Walt Disney Co. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 201, disapproved of on other grounds by Turner v. Anheuser-Busch, Inc. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1238.

In Rochlis, “[t]he plaintiff and appellant, Jeffrey A. Rochlis (Rochlis) appeal[ed] from a summary judgment entered against him on his four-count first amended complaint. He had sued the defendants and respondents, The Walt Disney Company (Disney), Walt Disney Imagineering (WDI) and Martin A. Sklar…for damages arising out of his employment by Disney and WDI and the subsequent termination of that employment in 1989.” (Rochlis v. Walt Disney Co., supra, at p. 206.) The Rochlis Court noted that “Rochlis admittedly was under contract to Disney until April 1988. He went to WDI in October 1987 based on what he now claims were fraudulent misrepresentations concerning WDI’s condition and the status of its various pending projects. By January 1988, while he was still under his term contract, he had discovered the truth and had become familiar with all of the problems which WDI had and which he was being asked to solve. Yet, in spite of such knowledge, he stayed on for nine months beyond the expiration of his contractual commitment. Indeed, during this period he continued to press for, and ultimately obtained, the substantial bonus and salary increase previously described. Before he took the WDI position he had expressed the view that he was not interested in renewing my contract. Since, by April of 1988, he had become fully aware of all of the problems which he argues were previously not disclosed to him, but nonetheless decided to remain, we conclude, as did the trial court, that he did not detrimentally rely on any misrepresentations as a matter of law.” (Id. at p. 215 (internal quotations and emphasis omitted).)

In the opposition, Plaintiff asserts that here, “the detrimental reliance lies in believing the threat that Defendants would move forward with false criminal charges to get what they wanted…which resulted in Plaintiff changing beneficiaries on life insurance policies.” (Opp’n at p. 8:21-24.) As discussed, Plaintiff alleges that he “believed that the Defendants would carry out their threats of reporting him to authorities.” (SAC, ¶ 41.) Plaintiff alleges that he “relied on the seriousness of the threats, and as a result, Plaintiff changed his estate planning and made defendants the beneficiaries under his term life insurance policy with his wife, who previously was the only beneficiary.” (SAC, ¶ 41.)

Lastly, the Court notes that the second cause of action is for “conspiracy to commit fraud and/or extortion.” Defendants’ arguments concerning the second cause of action do not appear to challenge the claim for conspiracy to commit extortion. The Court notes that “¿¿a demurrer cannot rightfully be sustained to part of a cause of action or to a particular type of damage or remedy.¿¿” (Kong ¿v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redevelopment Agency (2002) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1047¿¿; ¿see also ¿PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682 [“A demurrer does not lie to a portion of a cause of action.”]¿¿.) “¿¿[W]hen a substantive defect is clear¿from the face of a complaint . . . a defendant may attack that portion of the cause of action by filing a motion to strike.¿¿” (PH II, Inc. v. ¿Superior Ct., supra¿, ¿¿33 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1682-1683¿¿.)¿¿¿

 Based on the foregoing, the Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action of the SAC.  

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer to the first and second causes of action of the SAC is overruled.

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

           

Plaintiff Timothy D. Reuben, Inc., a California professional corporation, d/b/a Reuben Raucher & Blum (“Plaintiff”) requests entry of default judgment against Defendants Michael Winter, Federal Street Holdings, LLC, Van Ness Partners I LLC, and RN Management, LLC. Plaintiff requests judgment in the total amount of $80,104.73, comprising $75,367.53 in damages, $855.00 in costs, and $3,882.20 in interest.   

The Court notes one defect with the submitted default judgment package. 

In the supporting Declaration of Stephen L. Raucher, Mr. Raucher states that “Defendants’ balance as of September 30, 2021 was $61,234.82.” (Raucher Decl., ¶ 28, emphasis added.) However, Mr. Raucher also states that “[i]n total, Defendants contributed only $47,399.01 in payments, while NAI paid $217,297.06. In total, $264,696.07 was paid out of the $340,063.60 owed to RRB, leaving a balance of $75,367.53 owing as of October 31, 2023.” (Raucher Decl., ¶ 29, emphasis added.) In addition, Plaintiff’s case summary asserts that “Defendants defaulted on their obligation to pay Plaintiff’s fees, leaving $61,234.82 of legal services rendered and costs advanced by RRB unpaid.” (Plaintiff’s Case Summary at p. 3:14-16.) Thus, it is unclear what amount of damages is owed. Although Plaintiff seeks $75,367.53 in damages in connection with the instant request, Plaintiff appears to state that only $61,234.82 is owed.

            In addition, Mr. Raucher states that “RRB ceased performing all legal services

by August 31, 2021,” and that “Defendants’ balance as of September 30, 2021 was $61,234.82.” (Raucher Decl., ¶¶ 28-29.) Thus, it is unclear why Plaintiff asserts that $75,367.53 is owed if Defendants’ balance as of September 30, 2021 was $61,234.82. (Ibid.)

Based on the foregoing, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for default judgment without prejudice. The Court will discuss with Plaintiff a schedule for resubmission of the default judgment package.
