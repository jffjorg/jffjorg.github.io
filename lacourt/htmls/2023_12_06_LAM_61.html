DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV22185    Hearing Date: December 6, 2023    Dept: 61

Defendant Marathon HVAC Service, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Further Response to Demands for Inspection and Destructive Testing is GRANTED, with the condition that Defendant is to pay all the costs of such testing. No sanctions are awarded.

 

Defendant to provide notice.

 

I.          MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER

A party may demand the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of “documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically stored information in the possession, custody, or control of any other party to the action.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.010, subd. (a).) The responding party must respond to the production demand either by complying, by representing that the party lacks the ability to comply, or by objecting to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210.) The demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete, (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive, or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).) “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand,” and “[t]he motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b).)

 

A motion to compel a further response to an inspection demand must set forth specific facts showing “good cause” justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1); Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) Unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance. (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 444.) Once the moving party demonstrates good cause for the discovery, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully respond to the inspection demand.  (Coy v Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220.)

 

Defendant Marathon HVAC Services Inc. (Defendant) moves for an order compelling further responses to Demands for Inspection No. 17–28. These inspection demands sought access to Plaintiff John N. Drooyan’s (Plaintiff) condominium (No. 17) to conduct destructive testing at various points in Plaintiff’s dwelling. The inspection demands contemplate removal of a fan-coil access panel in the master bedroom walk-in closet, the removal of drywall in the ceiling and wall of the same closet for the inspection of piping therein, the removal of drywall around windows and exterior doors for signs of water intrusion, the removal of wall and ceiling drywall to inspect fire sprinkler heads and piping, the disconnection of kitchen appliances and potentially the removal of drywall to inspect for water damage or its source, the disconnection of Plaintiff’s bathroom plumbing fixtures and the removal of drywall to inspect for water damage, and an inspection of the “remainder of interior of unit” for the same. (Motion Exh. A.) These requests state that the destructive testing (DT) crew is to restore any inspected areas “to pre-testing condition” after the inspection is concluded, but not all requests call for DT of Plaintiff’s dwelling. Request No. 20 calls for DT of a detached piece of copper piping located in Plaintiff’s closet, while Request No. 21 calls for a photo-inspection of the “condensate drains of the HVAC unit.” (Ibid.) Requests No. 22 and 23 likewise call for a mere “water test” in the condensate pan, to see where water in the pan goes, and photo-documentation thereof. (Ibid.) Plaintiff responded to these requests with objections, and did not allow the inspection sought to take place. (Ortega-Smith Decl. ¶¶ 4–10.)

 

Defendant supports the motion with the declaration of its counsel, Teresa Ortega-Smith, who states that Defendant has no other means to investigate Plaintiff’s claims than those specified in the requests, and that further inspection of the area is required to evaluate the condition of the unit and determine both the cause and the amount of Plaintiff’s damages. (Ortega-Smith Decl. ¶¶ 15–19.)

 

Plaintiff contends that no destructive testing is needed or useful in this matter, because the pipe-burst that is the subject of this action occurred in July 2019 and was fully remediated by six named vendors in November of that year. (Drooyan Decl. ¶ 6.) Plaintiff contends that the inspections sought are thus unlikely to uncover relevant information, and further represent a burdensome intrusion upon his privacy. (Opposition at pp. 7–8.)

A court may order destructive testing to take place, provided the party that seeks destructive discovery bears the costs thereof. (San Diego Unified Port Dist. v. Douglas E. Barnhart, Inc. (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 1400, 1404.)

Defendant has shown good cause for the discovery sought here. Plaintiff in interrogatories has claimed damages resulting from a burst HVAC pipe and remediation efforts for his hardwood floors on the first and second story of his unit, mold treatments, new walls, new bathroom and kitchen cabinets and countertops, and other matters. (Opposition Exh. 1, Interrogatory No. 7.1.) Defendant may seek to conduct testing upon these areas to evaluate Plaintiff’s alleged cause of damages and the damages themselves. Although Plaintiff claims that the damages have been remediated, this assertion does not foreclose the possibility that further inspection will uncover useful information. Damage to Plaintiff’s privacy and property is adequately safeguarded by the nature of the requests, which include as their element a restoration of Plaintiff’s property to pre-testing condition, and the requirement that Defendant bear all costs of the testing. (San Diego Unified Port Dist., supra, 95 Cal.app.4th at p.1404.)

An exception, however, shall be made for Demand No. 28, which identifies no particular aspect or area of Plaintiff’s property to be inspected, but states broadly that Defendant will “[i]nspect remainder of interior of unit for locations of water damage allegedly caused by HVAC fan coil leak, including all first-floor areas,” and further states that Defendant may mark out areas of the drywall and ceiling to remove and inspect potential water damage and leak pathways. (Motion Exh. A.) This request is overly broad and would permit potentially destructive testing of the entirety of the interior of Plaintiff’s unit, irrespective of Plaintiff’s allegations of particular damage.

 

Accordingly, the motion to compel further is GRANTED as to Inspection Demands No. 17–27, and DENIED as to Demand No. 28. Defendant is to bear the costs of testing.

 

II.       SANCTIONS

Statute provides that the court shall impose sanctions upon a party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, or requests for admission, absent substantial justification otherwise. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (d); 2031.310, subd. (h); 2033.290, subd. (d).)

 

Defendant seeks $2,260.00 in sanctions, representing apparently 10.47 hours of attorney work at $210 per hour plus a $60 filing fee. (Ortega-Smith Decl. ¶ 23.) No sanctions are awarded, as Plaintiff opposed the motion with substantial justification.

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. (“CCP”) section 284 states that “[t]he attorney in an action or special proceeding may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination” upon either consent of both client and attorney, or upon the order of the court under application of either the client of the attorney, after notice from one to the other. Cal. Rule of Court 3.1362 states the requirements for a motion to be relieved as counsel under CCP section 284. No memorandum is required, but the motion must be accompanied by (1) a declaration stating why a motion has been brought instead of filing a consent (without compromising attorney-client confidentiality), (2) proof of service of the motion, and (3) all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. (CRC Rule 3.1362, subd. (a), (c), (d).) Additionally, “[t]he proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel--Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers.” (CRC Rule 3.1362, subd. (e).)

 

The present motions pertain to Leonard G. Cruz of the firm Cruz & Del Valle, counsel for Plaintiff Worldwide ATM, Inc. The motion contains a declaration of counsel stating that the attorney-client relationship has broken down, and that Plaintiff has terminated the relationship as of July 2023, but no new counsel has been retained in this matter. The motion includes a proof of service, but not a proposed order prepared on form MC-053.

 

Leonard G. Cruz and Cruz & Del Valle’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff Worldwide ATM, Inc. is DENIED, pending submission of the order.
