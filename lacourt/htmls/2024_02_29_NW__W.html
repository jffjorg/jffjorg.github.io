DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV00728    Hearing Date: February 29, 2024    Dept: W

ALBERT BOYAJIAN V. SURGE GLOBAL BAKERIES PARTNERS GP, ET AL.

 

DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        February 29, 2024                             Trial Date:       None set   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00728

 

Moving Party:            Defendants McLarty Capital Partners SBIC II, LLC, Lauren Steel, and Parris Boyd

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Albert Boyajian  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 31, 2022, Plaintiff Albert Boyajian filed a complaint against Defendants Surge Global Bakeries Holdings LLC, Surge Global Bakeries Partners LP, Surge Global Bakeries Partners GP, LLC, Surge Private Equity LLC, Surge Private Equity, McLarty Capital Partners SBIC II, LP, The Firmament Group, Thomas Beauchamp, Lewis Sharp, Lauren Steel, Parris Boyd, Tobias Keller, Andy Burchfield, and Ashur Aghasi for (1) Limited Partner Derivative Suit; (2) Limited Partner Derivative Suit; and for three direct claims: (3) Fraudulent Concealment; (4) Breach of Contract; and (5) Breach of Contract.

 

This action involves shareholder derivative and direct claims by Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges he sold his profitable bakery for an equity position in a parent entity, a place on the board of directors of his former bakery (now a wholly-owned subsidiary), and a Consulting Agreement. However, Plaintiff alleges he did not know, when he sold his company, that the purchase was merely the first step of a lender’s scheme to exploit the company for the lender’s benefit, entirely contrary to the best interests of the equity holders. Plaintiff contends after the purchase, the company unwarrantedly acquired an additional two companies and took on additional dramatic debt, during periods of mismanagement by inept managers and the lender who controlled the board. Moreover, since the takeover, Plaintiff alleges the lender has not given plaintiff any information on the companies; and has not called a meeting of the board.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants McLarty Capital Partners SBIC II, LLC, Lauren Steel, and Parris Boyd’s Demurrer is OVERRULED in PART AND SUSTAINED IN PART. 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants McLarty Capital Partners SBIC II, LLC, Lauren Steel, and Parris Boyd (“Defendants”) demur to the first and second causes of action on the grounds Boyajian lacks standing, statute of limitations bars any breach of fiduciary duty, neither Delaware nor California recognize a cause of action for Interference with Corporate Governance, Plaintiff fails to adequately plead futility, and Plaintiff has failed to allege Defendants’ conduct breached any fiduciary duty.

 

The court briefly notes it may sustain the demurrer on alternative grounds including different governing laws. (See City of Morgan Hill v. Bay Area Air Quality Management Dist. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 861, 870.)

 

Interference with Corporate Governance

 

Defendants demur to the second cause of action (a derivative action interference with corporate governance) on the grounds neither Delaware nor California law recognizes a cause of action for interference with corporate governance. Defendants argue no decision in either state addresses such a cause of action and neither the Third Circuit Court of Appeals nor the District Court for the District of Delaware have recognized such a cause of action under Delaware law. As a result, Defendants contend this court should not recognize such a tort. According to Defendants, the Corporations Code carefully lays out matters of corporate governance, the duties owed by various persons involved in corporate governance and causes of action for breaches of those duties.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues California as well as other jurisdictions have long recognized that a lender may be liable for undue control of a business. (See Credit Managers Association of Southern California v. Superior Court (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 352; Central Laborers’ Pension Fund v. McAfee (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 292; State Nat’l Bank of El Paso v. Farah Mfg. Co., Inc. (Tex. Ct. App. 1984) 678 S.W.2d 661.)  

 

The court finds Plaintiff can state a claim for interference with corporate governance at this time. The cases by Defendants do not stand for the proposition Plaintiff cannot allege a claim for interference with corporate governance. Rather the cases cited show support for a claim of interference with corporate governance claim. The court OVERRULES the demurrer on this ground.

 

Statute of Limitations

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action (derivative action for breach of fiduciary duty) on the grounds Delaware’s three-year statute of limitations bars any breach of fiduciary claim based on conduct occurring prior to May 31, 2019.

 

“Under Delaware law, a three-year statute of limitations applies to claims for breach of contract or breach of fiduciary duty. 10 Del. C. § 8106.” (Fike v. Ruger (Del. Ch. 1999) 754 A.2d 254, 260.)

 

Defendants argue by May 2019 at the latest, Boyajian had actual notice of facts giving rise to the cause of action, and the limitations period for a claim based on these facts expired by the end of May 2022 at the absolute latest. Defendants contend Plaintiff, however, did not file his complaint until May 31, 2022, so it can only be supported by conduct occurring on or after May 31, 2019.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues allegations that events occurred “on or about” do not support sustaining a demurrer based on a statute of limitations; rather, it is enough that the claim may be timely. The court agrees. “[W]hen ‘a complaint asserts a cause of action that on its face accrued outside the statute of limitations,’ the plaintiffs have the burden to plead facts ‘leading to a reasonable inference that one of the tolling doctrines adopted by Delaware courts applies.’” (Commonwealth Land Title Insurance Company v. Funk (Del. Super. Ct., Dec. 22, 2014, No. CV N14C-04-199 PRW) 2014 WL 8623183, at *6.) Similarly in California, “ ‘ “[a] demurrer on the ground of the bar of the statute of limitations will not lie where an action may be, but is not necessarily barred.” It must appear affirmatively that, upon the face of the complaint, the right of action is necessarily barred.’” (Leasequip, Inc. v. Dapeer (2002) 103 Cal.App.4th 394, 400.) In order for a court to sustain a demurrer based on a statute of limitations defense, “ ‘the complaint [must allege] every fact which the defendant would be required to prove if he were to plead the bar of the applicable statute of limitation as an affirmative defense. [Citation.]’ ” (Ibid., citing Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 881.) 

 

Here, as noted by Plaintiff, some of the events alleged in the complaint may well prove to have occurred later than the “on or about” date as currently asserted. As such, Defendants have not sufficiently demonstrated on the face of the complaint that the events absolutely occurred outside the statute of limitations. Accordingly, the court OVERRULES the demurrer on the statute of limitations grounds.

 

Double Derivative

 

Defendants demur to the two derivative actions (first and second cause of action) on behalf of Holdings LLC on the grounds Boyajian lacks standing under California law because California law does not allow a double derivative suit. Defendants cite Corp. Code § 800; Kruss v. Booth (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 699; and Heshejin v. Rostami, 2018 WL 11226134, to support their contention.

 

“’[B]y its nature, a double derivative suit is one brought by a shareholder of a parent corporation to enforce a claim belonging to a subsidiary that is either wholly owned or majority controlled. Normally, such a claim is one that only the parent corporation, acting through its board of directors, is empowered to enforce. Cases may arise, however, where the parent corporation's board is shown to be incapable of making an impartial business judgment regarding whether to assert the subsidiary's claim. In those cases a shareholder of the parent will be permitted to enforce that claim on the parent corporation's behalf, that is, double derivatively.’ [Citation.]” (Villari v. Mozilo (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1470, 1478.)

 

Defendants argue Boyajian does not and cannot allege that he is a shareholder of Holdings LLC. Instead, he alleges only that he holds equity interests in Parent LP, which wholly-owns Holdings LLC. As such, he could only proceed as a double derivative suit, which is not allowed in California.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant’s citations are to state trial court decisions, with no appellate review. Such opinions are neither citable nor precedential. (See Aixtron, Inc. v. Veeco Instruments, Inc. (2020) 52 Cal.App.5th 360, 399, citing Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.1115(a).) Moreover, Plaintiff contends the Kruss court did not hold that “double-derivative” suits are not allowed in California. Similarly, in Heshejin, the appellate court affirmed a demurrer because the counterclaim plaintiffs failed to bring a compulsory counterclaim; it did not consider whether California recognizes double derivative suits.  

 

The court agrees with Defendant that the current law in California does not explicitly recognize a double derivative action. However, the Rutter Group California Practice Guide—Corporations section 6:612.3 states that, “it is unsettled in California whether a shareholder of a parent corporation has standing to bring a derivative suit on behalf of its subsidiary - i.e., to enforce the parents' right to sue derivatively for wrongs to the subsidiary. However, courts applying California law appear to look favorably upon the ‘double derivative’ suit concept. (See generally, Gaillard v. Natomas Co. (1985) 173 CA 3d 410, 419, fn 7 In re Imperial Corp. of America (9th Cir. 1996) 92 F3d 1503, 1510 and fn 10)).” Hashejian’s own holding leaves double derivative suits an open question in California. Accordingly, the court OVERRULES the demurrer on these grounds.

 

Standing on behalf of Parent LP

 

Defendants demur to the first two causes of action to the extent they are brought as derivative actions on behalf of Surge Global Bakeries Parent LP (“Parent LP”) because he is not a limited partner of Parent LP. Defendants argue although Plaintiff alleges that he holds equity interests in Parent LP, the Asset Purchase Agreement and Parent LP Agreement that Boyajian attached to the Complaint belie that claim as they both show that Global Bakeries, Inc. is the limited partner in Parent LP. Accordingly, only Global Bakeries, Inc. would arguably have standing to bring a derivative suit on behalf of Parent LP.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues derivative suits can be brought by persons owning “beneficial” shares of stock. (See, e.g., Pearce v. Superior Court (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 1058, 1067-1068 [trust beneficiary had standing to sue derivatively]; see also, at 1062-1063 [same for partners whose shares were held by partnership [dictum]]; Weingand v. Atlantic Sav. & Loan Assn. (1970) 1 Cal.3d 806, 818-819 [pledgee of security interest in stock had standing to assert derivative claims]; Reed v. Norman (1957) 48 Cal.2d 338, 341-342 [plaintiff whose stock was sold had standing, where stock sale was forced and buyer took on behalf of plaintiff].)

 

The court agrees Plaintiff does not have standing to bring a derivative action on behalf of Parent LP. Plaintiff does not dispute that Global Bakeries, Inc. is the limited partner in Parent LP. Moreover, having a beneficial ownership in an LP does not provide standing. As noted by Defendants, a derivative action may be maintained only by a person that is a partner at the time the action is commenced. (Corp. Code §15910.03.) Accordingly, the demurrer is SUSTAINED on this basis.  The court does not believe that amendment could cure this deficiency. 

 

Futility

 

Defendants demur to the complaint on the grounds Boyajian does not sufficiently plead demand futility.

 

A derivative suit plaintiff must allege that he made a demand on the corporation to bring

the lawsuit itself or that such demand would be futile. (See Cal. Corp. Code §§ 800(b)(2),

15910.04; Del. Chancery R. 23.1.)

 

Defendants argue in this case, Boyajian does not allege that he made a demand on Parent LP. Instead, he attempts to allege that such demand would have been futile, which fails both under Delaware and California law.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues these allegations are sufficient as they demonstrate futility in California and Delaware. In California, Plaintiff contends there is a reasonable doubt that the general partner or any director of Holdings LLC might be disinterested in and independent of the challenged transactions, rather than acting as agents for the lender; or that the challenged transaction was otherwise the product of a valid exercise of business judgment, since the sale made no attempt to recover the substantial equipment available or any money whatsoever. In Delaware, Plaintiff contends no general partner was acting for Parent LP and the only directors of Holdings LLC were principals and/or agents of McClarty, lacked independence from an entity which received material personal benefit, or who faced a substantial likelihood of liability.

 

Upon examination of the complaint the court finds that the allegations are sufficient to excuse a request upon the board. The complaint alleges “[a]t this point, plaintiff is informed and believes that there is no general partner from whom he could demand that suit be filed, rendering such a demand futile. Moreover, the prior equity and/or board members would be unlikely to grant such relief as it would require a vote to institute suit against themselves.” (Compl. ¶5.) The complaint further alleges Plaintiff did not make any effort to secure action from Parent LP’s general partner, GP LLC, in prosecuting this action since any such effort would have been futile in that: GP LLC no longer exists; Plaintiff, as a Board member of Holdings LLC, voted against the initial acquisitions and was outvoted, was prevented from reviewing key corporate records, and was not given any notice of the sale of Bubbles and Better, demonstrating that the Holdings LLC Board members were unwilling to bring this action; if GP LLC exists, it was dominated by Defendants; Defendants personally benefitted from the conduct alleged; and in order to bring this action, the Defendants would be required to make statements on the record which could result in their individual exposure. (Compl. ¶63.) This is sufficient. Accordingly, the court overrules the demurrer on these grounds.

 

Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

Defendants demur to the first cause of action on the grounds Boyajian fails to allege the Firmament Defendants’ conduct breached any fiduciary duty.    

 

Under Delaware and California law, a claim for breach of fiduciary duty requires showing “(1) that a fiduciary duty existed and (2) that the defendant breached that duty.” (Beard Research, Inc. v. Kates (Del. Ch. 2010) 8 A.3d 573, 601; see also Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff fails to allege SBIC owed a fiduciary duty to Holdings LLC and none of the allegations overcome the business judgment rule. In reply, Defendants concede Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged the existence of a fiduciary duty. However, Defendants remain steadfast that Boyajian does not sufficiently factually or legally allege a breach. Defendants argue Boyajian does not allege that the acquisition of either Bubbles or Better was beyond the “bounds of reason,” or plead facts making “the board’s decision … irrational, unsound, or unreasonable”. Moreover, Boyajian does not allege that he actually requested the documents or that any of the Defendants specifically took any steps to block his request and terminating officers is a classic business judgment. Lastly, Boyajian does not allege any conflict because Holdings LLC sold to a competitor, not an interested party or insider. Given his allegations that “Bubbles and Better continued to lose money,” it is within the bounds of reason for Holdings LLC to sell underperforming assets for nominal value.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the presumption fails because it may be rebutted depending on further factual development and Defendants’ cases do not establish that Defendants are entitled to a decision sustaining their demurrer. Plaintiff further argues the allegations in the complaint clearly show Defendants and its lenders irrationally overextending Holdings LLC’s borrowing; Plaintiff was not given information on key company business; McClarty, through Steel, Boyd, and Keller, ultimately took over and ran the business operations of Parent LP and Holdings LLC completely, fired personnel, and sold assets all without board action; and the lender and its agent did not tell the company’s director that these sales were to occur, let alone at a price of one dollar, despite the companies having substantial equipment or other assets.

 

The other way to excuse demand futility is by pleading that the challenged transaction was otherwise the product of valid corporate business judgment. (Bader v. Anderson (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 775, 797.) Under Rales v. Blasband (Del. 1993) 634 A.2d 927, 934, “a court must determine whether or not the particularized factual allegations of a derivative stockholder complaint create a reasonable doubt that, as of the time the complaint is filed, [a majority of] the board of directors could have properly exercised its independent and disinterested business judgment in responding to a demand. If the derivative plaintiff satisfies this burden, then demand will be excused as futile.”

 

The court finds Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a breach. Taking Plaintiff’s allegations as true, selling Bubbles and Better for $1 and preventing him from obtaining critical documents can show lack of due care.  Accordingly, the court OVERRULES the demurrer on these grounds.


Case Number: 22VECV02299    Hearing Date: February 29, 2024    Dept: W

KAREN SORONOW, et al. v. SUNSHINE'S PLACE II, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion for protective order

 

Date of Hearing:        February 29, 2024                 Trial Date:       December 9, 2024

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        22VECV02299

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Karen Soronow

Responding Party:     Defendant Sunshine’s Place II                     

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Karen Soronow filed a complaint on December 6, 2022 against Defendant Sunshine’s Place, II alleging (1) Wrongful Death – Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and/or Retention; (2) Survival – Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and/or Retention; (3) Wrongful Death – Negligence; (4) Survival – Negligence; and (5) Elder Abuse. Plaintiff alleges her mother, Joyce Soronow, was a resident of Sunshine’s Place, II. While under Sunshine’s care, Plaintiff alleges failed to provide for her basic needs, caused a lapse in her care, and inflicted terrible pain and suffering that caused Joyce’s fatal injuries.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff Soronow moves the court to enter a protective order directing that Plaintiff does not have to respond to Defendant Sunshine’s Place II’s third set of requests for admission. Plaintiff makes the motion on the grounds that Defendant has not justified discovery in excess of the presumptive limit of 35 RFAs and the RFAs are otherwise unduly burdensome.

 

Subject to the right of the responding party to seek a protective order, any party who attaches a supporting declaration as described in Section 2033.050 may request a greater number of admissions if the greater number is warranted by the complexity or the quantity of the existing and potential issues in the particular case. (CCP § 2033.040(a).)  If the responding party seeks a protective order on the ground that the number of requests for admission is unwarranted, the propounding party shall have the burden of justifying the number of requests for admission. (CCP § 2033.040(b).) When requests for admission have been made, the responding party may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP §2033.080(a).)

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant has failed to meet its burden to justify the excessive number of RFAs and moreover, a protective order is still necessary because the RFAs are unduly burdensome. Plaintiff contends the RFAs are of minimal probative value because they are largely cumulative and duplicative of one another as well as RFAs that Plaintiff has already responded to. Accordingly, Plaintiff contends Defendant indiscriminately seeks much of the same information from Plaintiff that it already has from its first and second sets of RFAs and corresponding FROGs. Responding to this set of RFAs would require Plaintiff to review, audit, and extract information from her prior responses and essentially repeat it for Defendant.

 

In opposition, Defendant argues Defendant's declaration is sufficient as it explains that the additional RFAs were necessary due to the complexity of this matter. This is more than sufficient to justify the additional RFAs given the nuances of the timeline of the decedent's care between November 22, 2020 and December 6, 2020. Defendant further contends that in efforts of acting in good faith and in the spirit of discovery, Defendant has provided code compliant responses agreeing that the matter is complex in nature yet Plaintiff is now claiming Defendant's reason that the matter is complex is not enough.

 

The court denies the motion for protective order. The declaration attached to the RFAs includes the boiler plate language set forth under Section 2033.050, but does not include an explanation of why the case is complex and why the complexity warrants the issuance of more than 35 RFAs. However, the opposition present facts that demonstrate that the complexity of the case warrants additional RFAs including Defendant’s care of the decedent during the height of COVID and Plaintiff’s repeated decision to not seek emergency care for her mother. Moreover, the court finds the RFAS are not unduly burdensome. Although the RFAs are akin to one another, they are not quite the same and do not burden Plaintiff by answering.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Protective Order is DENIED.

 

 


Case Number: 23VECV04745    Hearing Date: February 29, 2024    Dept: W

CARY JARRETT KIEVMAN v. WESTLAKE PROPERTIES INC., et al.

 

shady hatem hassan’s motion for leave to file cross-complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        February 29, 2024                 Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        23VECV04745

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Shady Hatem Hassan  

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On October 24, 2023, Plaintiff Carry Jarrett Kievman filed a complaint against Westlake Properties Inc.; Westlake Village Inn; The Terrace at Bogies; Anthony Gomez; and Shady Hatem Hassan for 1. General Negligence; 2. Premises Liability; 3. Providing Alcohol to Obviously Intoxicated Minors; 4. Negligent Hiring, Supervision, or Retention of Employee; and 5. Battery. Plaintiff alleges while at Bogies, Plaintiff’s body touched a nearby female patron either intentionally or accidentally. Thereafter, the female patron informed other unknown individuals that Plaintiff had touched her. Plaintiff alleges as Plaintiff was retreating to get away, he was violently pushed by another unknown patron and at some point, Defendants Gomez and Hassan rushed Plaintiff, knocked him to the ground, and began violently attacking him by punching him, kicking him, and stomping on him. Plaintiff alleges at least Defendants Gomez and Hassan were under the age of 21 at the time of the incident.

 

On December 28, 2023, Westlake Properties, Inc. doing business as Westlake Village Inn (erroneously named as The Terrace at Bogies) filed a cross-complaint against Roes 1 through 10 for indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.

 

On June 9, 2023, this action was related to LASC No. 23VECV02540 (Farid Mohandesi v. Bogies The Terrace, et al.)

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Shady Hatem Hassan’s Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Shady Hassan move the court for leave to file a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff Cary Jarrett Kievman and Roes 1 through 10.

 

Where a defendant wishes to assert a “related cause of action” against plaintiff, it must do so in a cross-complaint. Failure to plead it will bar defendant from asserting it in a later lawsuit. (CCP § 426.10 [“‘Related cause of action’ means a cause of action which arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause of action which the plaintiff alleges in his complaint.”].) 

 

A cross-complaint against plaintiff may be filed as a matter of right if it is filed before or at the same time as the answer. (CCP § 428.50(a).) Otherwise, leave of court must be obtained. (CCP § 428.50(c).) Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of¿the action.¿(Id., § 428.50(c).) Indeed, where a cause of action would otherwise be lost, leave to amend is appropriate even if the party was negligent in not moving for leave to amend earlier.¿ “The legislative mandate is clear.¿ A policy of liberal construction of section 426.50 to avoid forfeiture of causes of action is imposed on the trial court.”¿ Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98-99 (Silver Orgs).¿[“Factors such as oversight, inadvertence, neglect, mistake or other cause, are insufficient grounds to deny the motion unless accompanied by bad faith.”]; Sidney v. Superior Court (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 710, 718 [a “strong showing of bad faith” is required].)  

 

The court finds Defendant Hassan is entitled to file the proposed Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 426.50. Defendant Hassan attempted to file a Cross-Complaint on December 5, 2023 for assault and battery. (Hassan Decl. ¶4.) However, on December 8, 2023, Hassan was informed his Cross-Complaint was rejected due to a filing error, and therefore, his Cross-Complaint would not be timely filed and a court order was necessary. (Hassan Decl. ¶7.) The Cross-Complaint arises out of the incident that occurred at Westlake Village Inn. This motion is being made at the start of the lawsuit with no trial date set and no CMC yet to occur. Plaintiff filed a Notice of Non-Opposition to Defendant’s motion.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Shady Hatem Hassan’s Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint is GRANTED.
