DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV05601    Hearing Date: April 18, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

Pejman Kohansedgh,

 

 

         Plaintiff,

   v.

 

 

Gonzalo Lizama, et al.,

 

         Defendants.

   Case Number             Department

   23VECV05601        107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendants Demurrer To First Amended Complaint

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

 

I. BACKGROUND

This is a landlord-tenant dispute. On August 25, 2019, plaintiff Pejman Kohansedgh (“Plaintiff”), the landlord of a certain residential real property (the “Property”) entered a lease (the “Lease”) and a guaranty to the Lease with defendants Gonzalo Lizama (“Gonzalo”), Yasmina Monjaraz (“Yasmina”), Santiago Monjaraz (“Santiago”), and Yesenia Cayetano (“Yesenia” and, together with Gonzalo, Yasmina, and Santiago, “Defendants”). (Opposition p. 2.) In June 2023, Defendants ceased rent payments but remained in possession of the Property. (Ibid.)

 

On December 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed an unlawful detainer complaint (the “Complaint”) against Defendants. On January 12, 2024, Santiago filed a demurrer to the Complaint, and five days later, Yesenia did the same. On February 9, 2024, both demurrers were sustained with leave to amend.

 

On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended complaint (the “FAC”).

 

On March 27, 2024, Gonzalo and Yasmina filed a demurrer to the FAC (the “Gonzalo/Yasmina Demurrer”), and two days later, Santiago and Yesenia did the same (the “Santiago/Yesenia Demurrer” and, together with the Gonzalo/Yasmina Demurrer, the “Demurrers”).

 

On April 12, 2024, Plaintiff opposed both Demurrers.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer. Further,  

 

The demurring party shall file and serve with the demurrer a declaration stating either of the following: 

 

A.              The means by which the demurring party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer, and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the objections raised in the demurrer. 

 

B.               That the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith. 

 

(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿430.41, subd. (a)(3).) 

 

We note that Defendants do not include any declaration stating either that the meet and confer requirement has been met or that Parties were unable to meet this requirement, as required by California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a)(3). We therefore remind Defendants of their obligation to meet and confer with Plaintiff in good faith prior to the filing of a demurrer. 

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint.

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196 (disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176).)

 

The FAC attaches a proof of service indicating that Defendants were served with the FAC on February 12, 2024. Both the Gonzalo/Yasmina Demurrer and the Santiago/Yesenia Demurrer are therefore untimely as both were filed more than 30 days after February 12, 2024. Plaintiff’s opposition to both Demurrers is also untimely. We nonetheless consider both Demurrers and Plaintiff’s opposition thereto.

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A complaint is sufficient if it contains “[a] statement of the facts constituting the cause of action, in ordinary and concise language.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.10, subd. (a)(1).) Under this standard, a “complaint ordinarily is sufficient if it alleges ultimate rather than evidentiary facts.” (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

 

  1. The Gonzalo/Yasmina Demurrer

 

Gonzalo and Yasmina demur to the FAC on the grounds that (1) the FAC is uncertain as to whether Plaintiff is the “owner” or the “landlord and trustee with power of attorney” of the Property, (2) the FAC is uncertain as to whether this is a limited or unlimited jurisdiction case, and (3) the Lease limits recovery of attorneys’ fees to $500 but the FAC’s prayer for relief includes a request for “reasonable attorney fees.” (Demurrer p. 1.)

 

Demurrers “for uncertainty are disfavored, and are granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.” (Mahan v. Charles W. Chan Ins. Agency, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 841, 848, fn. 3, internal quotations omitted; accord Lickiss v. Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1125, 1135.) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Chen v. Berenjian (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822, quoting Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) A¿demurrer¿on the ground that the complaint is ambiguous,¿uncertain, and¿unintelligible, cannot be sustained unless all of these imperfections exist. (Kraner v. Halsey (1889) 82 Cal. 209, 211–12.) Not “every ambiguity in a complaint will justify a trial court in sustaining a special¿demurrer.” (Merlino v. West Coast Macaroni Mfg. Co. (1949) 90 Cal.App.2d 106, 108.) “A special¿demurrer¿should not be sustained if the allegations are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues that must be met, even if the allegations of the complaint may not be as clear and as detailed as might be desired. Moreover, a¿demurrer¿for uncertainty will not lie as to even¿uncertain¿and ambiguous allegations, if such allegations refer to immaterial matters.” (Ibid.) 

 

Gonzalo and Yasmina argue that Plaintiff’s status as either “owner” or “landlord and trustee with power of attorney” is important to their ability to determine if he has standing to sue. (Demurrer p. 3.) If Plaintiff is a trustee and not the owner, they contend, then he must sue in his capacity as trustee. (Ibid.)

 

“As a general rule, the trustee is the real party in interest with standing to sue and defend on the trust's behalf.” (Estate of Bowles (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 684, 691.) When a party mistakenly identifies itself as a trustee but has standing only as an individual, courts will disregard the trustee label as merely descriptive. (Graham v. Lenzi (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 248, 254.) We apply the same principle here and find that the absence of the descriptive label of trustee does not deprive a party of standing.

 

Plaintiff contends that he “wears several ‘hats’ vis-á-vis the [Property]” and that he is owner, landlord, and trustee with power of attorney but that this is “of trifling concern.” (Opposition p. 7.) Because Plaintiff’s failure to label himself a trustee does not deprive him of standing, we agree that he would have standing whether he were owner or landlord and trustee with power of attorney. Thus, we find that this uncertainty refers to immaterial matters and should not be the basis of a demurrer.

 

Gonzalo and Yasmina additionally argue that the FAC’s reclassification of the instant action as a limited case creates uncertainty because it remains in an unlimited court. (Demurrer pp. 3–4.) However, the classification of the action as either limited or unlimited does not affect the allegations of the FAC, which remain sufficient to “apprise the defendant of the issues that must be met” and so should not be the basis for sustaining a demurrer for uncertainty.

 

Finally, Gonzalo and Yasmina argue that the FAC’s checkmark in the box indicating that Plaintiff requests “reasonable attorney fees” contradicts the Lease, which limits such recovery to $500. We do not find these statements to be contradictory, as reasonable attorneys’ fees in this case may be $500, and additionally find that any uncertainty this does create refers to immaterial maters that should not be the basis of a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Gonzalo/Yasmina Demurrer.

 

  1. The Santiago/Yesenia Demurrer

 

Santiago and Yesenia demur to the FAC on the grounds that because they are guarantors of the Lease and not tenants under the Lease, the copy of the Lease attached to the FAC contradicts the FAC and because possession of the Property is not at issue in their case, Plaintiff cannot prevail on an unlawful detainer action against them. (Demurrer pp. 1–3.) Plaintiff disputes that Santiago and Yesenia are not tenants. (Opposition p. 9.)

 

We agree with Santiago and Yesenia that the Lease indicates they are not tenants entitled to possession of the Property as the Lease includes only Gonzalo and Yasmina in its definition of “Tenants.” (FAC p. 5.) The guaranty attached to the Lease appears to have been mistakenly signed by Defendants on the wrong lines but clearly indicates that two of Defendants are guaranteeing the other two Defendants’ performance under the Lease and that only two of the Defendants, not all of them, are tenants entitled to possession of the Property. (FAC p. 9.)

 

“Unlawful detainer is a remedy available to a landlord against a tenant who breaches a lease and is intended and designed to provide an expeditious remedy for the recovery of possession of real property.” (Castaic Studios, LLC v. Wonderland Studios LLC (2023) 97 Cal.App.5th 209, 215.) “The remedy is available in only three situations: to a lessor against a lessee for unlawfully holding over or for breach of a lease; to an owner against an employee, agent, or licensee whose relationship has terminated; and to a purchaser at an execution sale, a sale by foreclosure, or a sale under a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust against the former owner and possessor.” (Greene v. Municipal Court (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 446, 450.) “[U]nlawful detainer actions are limited to right of possession and not other claims, even if related to the property.” (Castaic Studios, LLC v. Wonderland Studios LLC, supra at 216, describing Larson v. City and County of San Francisco (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1297.)

 

The FAC fails to state a cause of action in unlawful detainer against Santiago and Yesenia because it does not allege a situation to which the remedy of unlawful detainer applies. Santiago and Yesenia, being guarantors of the Lease and not tenants have no claim to possession of the Property. They are not lessees under the Lease.

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) “If there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) “If the plaintiff has not had an opportunity to amend the complaint in response to the demurrer, leave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.” (City of Stockton v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 730, 747.)

 

We do not see any reasonable possibility that Plaintiff could make an amendment that would cure Santiago’s and Yesenia’s status as non-tenants so that an unlawful detainer action could be maintained against them.

 

Accordingly, this court SUSTAINS the Santiago/Yesenia Demurrer without leave to amend.

COURT’S [Tentative] ORDER RE:       

Application for Writ of Attachment (CCP 484.040)

I. BACKGROUND

On July 11, 2023, defendant Rshtuni Consulting Services, Inc. (“Consulting”) applied for a business installment loan with plaintiff Citibank, N.A. (“Plaintiff”). ( Complaint p. 2.) Simultaneously, defendant Anna Rshtuni (“Anna” and, together with Consulting, “Defendants” and Defendants, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”) executed a guaranty (the “Guaranty”), guaranteeing Consulting’s performance under the Agreement (as defined below). (Id. at p. 4.) On September 1, 2023, Plaintiff approved Consulting for a $110,000 business installment loan at a fixed interest rate of 6.5% per annum and lent Consulting such money under an agreement thereby created (the “Agreement”). (Id. at pp. 2–3.) Pursuant to the Agreement, Defendants received a certain business deposit account (the “Account”) from Plaintiff, which they subsequently overdrew. (Id. at pp. 5–6.)

 

On October 30, 2023, Consulting defaulted under the Agreement by ceasing to make payments due thereunder, and Anna defaulted under the Guaranty by also failing to make payments due under the Agreement. (Id. at p. 3–4.) Plaintiff consequently accelerated the loan. (Ibid.) Plaintiff seeks the full balance of the loan principal, accrued interest, late charges, the amount overdrawn from the Account, and attorney’s fees from each Defendant. (Ibid.)

 

On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed an application to attach order and for an order for issuance of a writ of attachment against each Defendant. The instant application (the “Application”) against Consulting is being heard on April 18, 2024. The application against Anna is being heard on April 19, 2024. Both applications are unopposed.

 

On April 11, 2024, defaults were entered against both Defendants.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Attachment is an ancillary or provisional remedy to aid the collection of a money demand by seizure of property in advance of trial or judgment as security for satisfaction of a judgment for the attaching party.” (Park v. NMSI, Inc. (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 346, 353, quoting Burke v. Superior Court (1969) 71 Cal.2d 276, 279, fn. 3.)

 

“Upon the filing of the complaint or at any time thereafter, the plaintiff may apply pursuant to this article for a right to attach order and a writ of attachment by filing an application for the order and writ with the court in which the action is brought.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.010.) The application shall be executed under oath and must include (1) a statement showing that the attachment is sought to secure the recovery on a claim upon which an attachment may be issued; (2) a statement of the amount to be secured by the attachment; (3) a statement that the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based; (4) a statement that the applicant has no information or belief that the claim is discharged or that the prosecution of the action is stayed in a proceeding under the Bankruptcy Act (11 U.S.C. section 101, et seq.); and (5) a description of the property to be attached under the writ of attachment and a statement that the plaintiff is informed and believes that such property is subject to attachment. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.020.) 

   

A court shall consider the showing made by the parties, as well as the pleadings and other papers in the record. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.090(a), (d).) A court shall issue a right to attach order if it finds all of the following:

 

(1) the claim upon which the attachment is based is one upon which an attachment may be issued,

 

(2) the plaintiff has established the probable validity of the claim upon which the attachment is based,

 

(3) the attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based, and

 

(4) the amount to be secured by the attachment is greater than zero. 

             

(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.090(a)(1)–(a)(4).)  

  

“The Attachment Law statutes are subject to strict construction….” (Epstein v. Abrams (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 1159, 1168.)  

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff seeks a right to attach order and order for issuance of a writ of attachment in the total amount of $160,269.31 against Consulting on any property of Consulting.

 

  1. Procedural Requirements

 

The Application, having been filed on an approved Judicial Council form, contains the statements required by California Code of Civil Procedure section 484.020, which therefore satisfies those procedural requirements.

 

At the times prescribed by California Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b), the defendant must be served with a copy of the summons and complaint, notice of application and hearing, and a copy of the application and supporting affidavits. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.040.) 

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of service indicating that all required documents were personally served on Consulting on February 21, 2024. The service of notice requirement has been met.

 

  1. Attachability of Claim

 

To issue a right to attach order, the court must find, inter alia, that “[t]he claim upon which the attachment is based is one upon which an attachment may be issued.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.090, subd. (a)(1).)

 

The court may only issue an attachment in an action on a claim for money, based on a contract, totaling at least $500.00 exclusive of costs, interest, and attorneys’ fees. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 483.010, subd. (a).) Plaintiff makes a claim for money based on the Agreement, which is a contract, for at least $110,000, exclusive of costs, interest, and attorneys’ fees.

 

“An attachment may not be issued on a claim which is secured by any interest in real property….” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 483.010, subd. (b).) The Austin Declaration (as defined below) states that “Plaintiff has no collateral or other security for the payment of the obligation” and attaches copies of the documents making up the Agreement, which only provide for security in the form of Consulting’s personal property and fixtures, and all proceeds and products thereof, not any real property. (Austin Decl. p. 5, Exh. 1, Exh. 2, Exh. 3.)

 

Accordingly, we find that Plaintiff’s claim, upon which the attachment sought by the Application is based, is one upon which an attachment may be issued.

 

  1. Probable Validity

 

To issue a right to attach order, the court must find, inter alia, that “[t]he plaintiff has established the probable validity of the claim upon which the attachment is based.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.090, subd. (a)(2).)

 

“Attachment… requires a court to make a preliminary determination of the merits of a dispute.” (Lorber Industries v. Turbulence, Inc. (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 532, 535.) For its part, the creditor applying for attachment must “follow[] the statutory guidelines and establish[] a prima facie claim to have a debtor’s assets seized and held until final adjudication at trial.” (Ibid.) “The plaintiff has the burden of establishing the probable validity of the claim upon which the attachment is based.” (Rreef America Reit II Corp, YYYY v. Samsara Inc. (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 609, 617.) “Probable validity means ‘it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim.’” (Park v. NMSI, Inc., supra, 96 Cal.App.5th 346, 354, quoting Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 481.190.)

 

The application for a writ of attachment must be supported “by an affidavit showing that the plaintiff on the facts presented would be entitled to a judgment on the claim upon which the attachment is based.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.030.) “Except where matters are specifically permitted to be shown upon information and belief, each affidavit or declaration must show that the affiant or declarant, if sworn as a witness, can testify competently to the facts stated therein. [Citation.] This means that the affiant or declarant must show actual, personal knowledge of the relevant facts, rather than the ultimate facts commonly found in pleadings, and such evidence must be admissible and not objectionable.” (Lydig Construction, Inc. v. Martinez Steel Corp. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 937, 944.)

 

“Whenever, under any law of this state or under any rule, regulation, order or requirement made pursuant to the law of this state, any matter is required or permitted to be supported, evidenced, established, or proved by the sworn statement, declaration, verification, certificate, oath, or affidavit, in writing of the person making the same (other than a deposition, or an oath of office, or an oath required to be taken before a specified official other than a notary public), such matter may with like force and effect be supported, evidenced, established or proved by the unsworn statement, declaration, verification, or certificate, in writing of such person which recites that it is certified or declared by him or her to be true under penalty of perjury, is subscribed by him or her, and (1), if executed within this state, states the date and place of execution, or (2), if executed at any place, within or without this state, states the date of execution and that it is so certified or declared under the laws of the State of California.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2015.5.)

 

The standard elements of a breach of contract claim are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) damages to the plaintiff therefrom. (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) Plaintiff submits with the Application a declaration (the “Austin Declaration”) signed under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that states, based upon the declarant’s personal review of Plaintiff’s records of Consulting’s account, (1) Parties entered the Agreement, (2) Plaintiff performed under the Agreement or was excused for nonperformance, (3) Consulting defaulted by failing to make payments under the Agreement, and (4) Plaintiff is consequently owed but has not been paid money damages. (Austin Decl. pp. 2–6.) The Austin Declaration meets the requirements of an affidavit and states a prima facie case for breach of contract against Consulting.

 

Accordingly, we find that Plaintiff has established the probable validity of its claim for breach of contract upon which the attachment sought by the Application is based.

 

  1. Purpose of Attachment

 

To issue a right to attach order, the court must find, inter alia, that “[t]he attachment is not sought for a purpose other than the recovery on the claim upon which the attachment is based.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.090, subd. (a)(3).)

 

The Application contains the statement, “[a]ttachment is not sought for a purpose other than recovery on a claim upon which the attachment is based.” (Application p. 1.) The Application is signed by Scott Austin (“Austin”) under penalty of perjury. (Id. at p. 3.) As Austin has signed the Application under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California, it is equivalent to an affidavit. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2015.5.) Furthermore, the Judicial Council form permits the applicant to set forth facts in a verified complaint or an attached affidavit or in the body of the application itself. (See Application ¶ 7.) Therefore, Austin’s statement, under penalty of perjury, in support of the Application regarding the purpose for which Plaintiff seeks attachment is sufficient to satisfy the affidavit requirement contained in California Code of Civil Procedure section 484.030.

 

Accordingly, we find that Plaintiff does not seek attachment for any purpose other than the recovery on its claim upon which the sought attachment is based.

 

  1. Amount to Be Attached

 

An attachment order may be issued only if the claim upon which the attachment is based is, inter alia, for money based upon a contract that is of a “fixed or readily ascertainable amount.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 483.010, subd. (a).) “Attachment will lie in an action for breach of contract when it appears by reference to the contract, complaint, and affidavit for attachment that the computation of damages alleged is reasonable, definite, and readily ascertainable.” (Hayward Lumber & Inv. Co. v. Construction Products Corp. (1952) 110 Cal.App.2d 386, 387.) “Although damages need not be liquidated, they must be measurable by reference to the contract sued upon, and their basis must be reasonable and certain.” (Kemp Bros. Construction, Inc. v. Titan Electric Corp. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 1474, 1482, fn. 5; CIT Group/Equipment Financing, Inc. v. Super DVD, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 537, 541.)

 

Plaintiff attaches to the Austin Declaration copies of the documents making up the Agreement, a statement for the Account, and terms related to the Account. (Austin Decl. Exhs. 1–6.) Plaintiff’s claim for the full balance of the loan under the Agreement, interest thereon, and the amount overdrawn from the Account are therefore fixed and readily ascertainable by reference to the Agreement, the terms and statement of the Account, the Complaint, and the Austin Declaration.

 

In an action on contract, the award of attorneys’ fees is governed by California Civil Code section 1717, and is only awarded when the contract specifically provides for it. The Agreement provides for Consulting’s payment of attorneys’ fees and costs associated with Plaintiff’s collection of amounts due thereunder. (Austin Decl. Exh. 3.) Thus, the Agreement provides a basis for attorneys’ fees and costs. However, the amount of attorneys’ fees and costs is not explained in any affidavit and is therefore not fixed or readily ascertainable by reference to the contract, complaint, and affidavit for attachment.

 

To issue a right to attach order, the court must find, inter alia, that “[t]he amount to be secured by the attachment is greater than zero.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 484.090, subd. (a)(4).)

 

We find that the balance of the loan under the Agreement, interest thereon, and the amount overdrawn from the Account are fixed or readily ascertainable and may be the basis for attachment. These amounts sum to $157,589.31, which is greater than zero.

 

Accordingly, we find that the amount to be secured by the attachment sought by the Application is greater than zero.

 

IV. CONCLUSION

 

Thus, we find that (1) Plaintiff’s claim, upon which the attachment sought by the Application is based, is one upon which an attachment may be issued, (2) Plaintiff has established the probable validity of its claim for breach of contract upon which the attachment sought by the Application is based, (3) Plaintiff does not seek attachment for any purpose other than the recovery on its claim upon which the sought attachment is based, and (4) the amount to be secured by the attachment sought by the Application is greater than zero. Per California Code of Civil Procedure section 484.090, subdivision (a), this court therefore shall issue a right to attach order.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Application in the reduced amount of $157,589.31.
