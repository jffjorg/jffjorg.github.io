DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19PSCV00291    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: H

Cross-Complainant Lone Oak Fund’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Background

Plaintiffs Zengpeng Feng (“Zengpeng”), Jiaxiang Feng (“Jiaxiang”) and Yongxiang Feng (“Yongxiang”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

Plaintiffs are Chinese nationals with limited knowledge of English and limited knowledge of the California real estate markets. In or around April 2012, Zengpeng and non-party Zhengjun Wang (“Zhengjun”) traveled together to Las Vegas, where they won a significant sum of money gambling. While in Las Vegas, Zengpeng and Zhengjun met Fai Wong (“Wong”), who represented himself as a casino host. Wong told Zengpeng and Zhengjun that they could not legally return to China with their winnings or otherwise transfer the money to China and that they had to invest the money in the United States. Wong represented that he knew of properties in Las Vegas that could be purchased as an investment for approximately the amount of money Zengpeng and Zhengjun had won and that he could handle this transaction for them. Zengpeng provided Wong with $460,000.00 to invest on his behalf.

Wong promised to invest the money in properties in Las Vegas, and the first of the properties was purchased on or about May 7, 2012. These properties are currently involved in litigation in Case No. A-18-777837-C (the “Nevada Case”). Zengpeng, and his sons Jiaxiang and Yongxiang, agreed to acquire additional properties, including properties in Southern California, by and through Wong. Wong would recommend a particular property and then, on Wong’s instructions, Plaintiffs would transfer funds into accounts controlled by Wong in China.

Wong and his associates, including his wife Liming Jiang (“Jiang”), would use funds transferred into the account to purchase the recommended property through a now-defunct entity controlled by them (i.e., Golden Ocean Investment, LLC [“Golden Ocean”]) and then have Golden Ocean sell the property to Zengpeng or his sons at inflated prices.

In 2012, Golden Ocean purchased three properties, (1) 14730 Finisterra Place, Hacienda Heights, California (the “Finisterra Property”), (2) 8684 Via Santa Cruz, Whittier, California (the “Santa Cruz Property”) and (3) vacant land near Via Santa Cruz and La Cuarta Street, Whittier, California (the “Vacant Lot”) on behalf of Zengpeng, which were titled in Jiaxiang’s and Yongxiang’s names. Wong, Jiang and their associates took care of the Finisterra and Santa Cruz Properties and the Vacant Lot.

In August 2014, Wong told Zengpeng that Jiaxiang should sign a power of attorney so that he could hire a professional property management company to manage the Santa Cruz Property. Jiaxiang signed a power of attorney which was recorded January 29, 2015 (the “2014 Santa Cruz Power of Attorney”).

In 2015, Wong told Zengpeng that Yongxiang needed to sign a power of attorney so that he could lease the Finisterra Property out to a tenant. Yongxiang signed a power of attorney which was recorded on November 9, 2015 (the “2015 Finisterra Power of Attorney”).

On or about January 20, 2015, Jiang, as “attorney in fact” for Jiaxiang, signed a grant deed conveying the Santa Cruz Property from Jiaxiang to Global Panda Entertainment LLC (“Global Panda”), without Plaintiffs’ knowledge or consent. The grant deed was recorded January 29, 2015 (the “2015 Santa Cruz Grant Deed”). Wong, as manager of Global Panda, signed a Deed of Trust with Absolute Assignment of Leases and Rents, Security Agreement and Fixture Filing (First Priority—to be recorded in Los Angeles County) for the benefit of Lone Oak Fund, LLC (“Lone Oak”) to secure a loan for $3,660,000.00 (the “2015 Lone Oak Deed of Trust (LA)”), again without Plaintiffs’ knowledge or consent. The 2015 Lone Oak Deed of Trust (LA) encumbered the Santa Cruz Property as well as the Northview Property and the Las Palmas Property (the latter two properties which are the subject of other litigations) and was recorded on January 29, 2015.

Background

On April 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint”), asserting causes of action against Inland Valley Partners, LLC (“Inland Valley”), Renew Health Consulting Services, LLC (“Renew Health”) (together, “Defendants”) and Does 1-250 for (1) dependent adult abuse under the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (Welfare and Institutions Code §§ 15600, et seq.) and (2) negligence.

Pending before the Court are Plaintiff’s five motions to compel further responses and/or documents from either Renew Health or Inland Valley. Prior to filing the instant motions, Plaintiff and Defendants met and conferred as to each motion. Many of the motions were also previewed at an Informal Discovery Conference (IDC) held before Judge Hernandez.

 

Legal Standards

“[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010). “In the context of discovery, evidence is ‘relevant’ if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a settlement.” (Glenfed Development Corp. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117).

“On receipt of a response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that . . . (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive or incomplete [and/or that] (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290, subd. (a).)

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that . . . (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete[,] (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate[, and/or that] (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).) 

As to requests for admissions and interrogatories, the responding party has the burden of justifying the objections to the requests. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.)

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that. . . (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[,] (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive [and/or that] (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a).)  A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents must “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) If the moving party has shown good cause for the production of documents, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) 

All of the aforementioned discovery motions require a meet and confer declaration stating “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2033.290, subd. (b)(1), 2030.300, subd. (b)(1), 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).) “In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.290, subd. (b)(2), 2030.300, subd. (b)(2), 2031.310, subd. (b)(3).)

Notice of the motions must be provided “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response” or by “any specific later date” to which the parties have agreed in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (c); 2031.310, subd. (c).)

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response [for admissions, to interrogatories, or to a demand], unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2033.290, subd. (d), 2030.300, subd. (d), 2031.310, subd. (h).)

 

Discussion

Plaintiff brings six motions to compel further responses and/or documents from Defendants, each of which the Court addresses in turn.[FN1]

 

Motion 1: Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses from Renew Health to Request for Admissions

Plaintiff first moves the Court for an order compelling Renew Health to provide further verified responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions, Set No. One (i.e., No. 1). Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Renew Health and its attorneys of record, jointly and severally, in the amount of $3,610.00.

Request No. 1 reads as follows: “Please admit that you are aware of no documents which you contend evidence the responding party having been adequately capitalized during the residency of the plaintiff in the facility.” 

Renew Health provided an objections-only response. Renew Health asserts that the request is irrelevant, overbroad, unduly burdensome, violates its right to privacy, and violates Civil Code section 3295.[FN2]

As to relevance, the Court disagrees that the requested information is irrelevant. Plaintiff has alleged that he suffered stage II+ coccyx pressure sore and Stage III+ left heel pressure sore while under the facility’s care; that Renew Health is the facility’s “owner[], operator[], parent company, and/or management company . . . and actively participated and controlled the business of the FACILITY and thus provided long-term professional and custodial care as a 24-hour skilled nursing facility”; and that the facility’s misconduct, which led to Plaintiffs’ injuries, was the direct result and product of financial limitations imposed upon the facility. (See FAC ¶¶ 3, 8, 9, 11 and 132.) The Operative Complaint also includes allegations of alter ego and single enterprise. (Id., ¶¶ 12 and 13.) Inadequate capitalization may be considered as a factor in an alter ego analysis. (Automotriz Del Golfo De California S.A. De C.V. v. Resnick (1957) 47 Cal.2d 792, 797.) Accordingly, the requested information is relevant as it “might reasonably assist [Plaintiff] in evaluating [his] case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a settlement.” (Glenfed Development Corp., supra, 53 Cal.App.4th at 1117). 

The Court also overrules the objections based on undue burden and overbreadth. The request merely seeks a response of “admit” or “deny” and is expressly limited to the time period of Plaintiff’s residency at the facility.

As to Renew Health’s objection based on Civil Code section 3295, the Court disagrees that a response of “admit” or “deny,” without more, would somehow divulge evidence of profit or financial condition prohibited from disclosure by that section. More fundamentally, disclosure is warranted here given the nature of Plaintiff’s case. While “the party against whom the discovery is sought should be afforded the full benefit of Civil Code section 3295” where “the only reason for seeking financial information is to give a tactical edge” to the requesting party “by allowing that party the benefit of pressure in settlement negotiations by threat or implication of disclosure,” disclosure should “not be denied . . . so easily” where “the financial information goes to the heart of the cause of action itself.” (Rawnsley v. Superior Court (1986) 183 Cal.App.3d 86, 91 [“Unlike the situation in which a plaintiff seeks to discover a defendant’s financial status solely for the purpose of assessing a punitive damages claim, the documents sought by petitioner were fundamental to his case.”].) 

Here, Plaintiff does not seek the information for the purposes of punitive damages and the information sought “goes to the heart” of Plaintiff’s elder abuse and negligence causes of action. Plaintiff seeks financial information in an attempt to prove Defendants’ “policy to systematically withhold care from residents in favor of profit maximization,” which allegedly led to Plaintiff’s injuries. (FAC, ¶ 11.)

The Court also disagrees that a response of “admit” or “deny” would invade privacy “to the extent that the information sought constitute[d] disclosure of unintended third parties.”[FN3]

Accordingly, the motion is granted. Renew Health is directed to provide a further, Code-compliant response to Request No. 1 within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling. 

As to Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Renew Health and its attorneys of record, jointly and severally, in the amount of $3,610.00, the Court grants the request but orders that the amount be reduced to $935. Utilizing a Lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the pending motion is $935.00 [i.e., 1.5 hours at $150.00/hour, plus 1.5 hours at $350.00/hour, plus $60.00 filing fee, plus $125.00 court reporter fee ($750.00 divided by 6 motions)]. Sanctions are payable within 30 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

No. 45 asks Inland Valley to identify each resident of the facility during the time period(s) in which Plaintiff was a facility resident. 

No. 46 asks Inland Valley to identify the responsible party for each resident of the facility during the time period(s) in which Plaintiff was a facility resident. 

The Court agrees that disclosure of such information is prohibited by the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (“HIPAA”), which prohibits the disclosure of “protected health information.” (45 C.F.R. § 164.502(a).) “Protected health information” is defined as “individually identifiable health information” (Id. at § 160.103), which in turn is defined as “information that. . .[r]elates to the past, present, or future physical or mental health or condition of an individual; the provision of health care to an individual; or the past, present, or future payment for the provision of health care to an individual; and (i) That identifies the individual; or (ii) With respect to which there is a reasonable basis to believe the information can be used to identify the individual.” (Id.) 

“A covered entity may determine that health information is not individually identifiable health information only if: . . .

(2)(i) The following identifiers of the individual or of relatives, employers, or household members of the individual, are removed:

(A) Names;

(B) All geographic subdivisions smaller than a State, including street address, city, county, precinct, zip code. . .

(D) Telephone numbers;

(E) Fax numbers;

(F) Electronic mail addresses;

(G) Social security numbers . . .

(Q) Full face photographic images and any comparable images; and

(R) Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code, except as permitted by paragraph (c) of this section . . .”  (45 C.F.R. § 164.514). 

As Inland Valley points out, there is a reasonable basis to believe that responsible parties’ contact information can be used to identify residents, particularly where the responsible party is a relative of the resident, often with the same last name.

Accordingly, the Court denies the motion as to Requests Nos. 45 and 46 and will hear further from the parties as to the other requests at the hearing. The Court is not inclined to award sanctions as to this motion. 

 

Motion 6: Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses and Documents from Inland Valley for Request for Identification and Production of Documents 

Plaintiff moves the Court for an order compelling Inland Valley to provide further verified responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set No. One (i.e., Nos. 1, 22-30, 32, 38-40, 48, 56, 62-69, 78 and 79). Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Inland Valley and its attorneys of record, jointly and severally, in the amount of $4,110.00. 

At the outset, Inland Valley represents in opposition that it served Code-compliant responses to Nos. 64 and 65. It also represents that it served supplemental responses on August 27, 2024, as to Nos. 22-30, 32, 38-40, 62, 63, 66-69, 78 and 79, as well as a privilege log, and that the motion should now be moot except as to Nos. 1 and 56.  

Plaintiff agrees that the motion would be moot as to Nos. 38-40, 64, 65 and 69 if Inland Valley had provided verifications to its supplemental responses served on August 27, 2024, which it failed to do. At the hearing, counsel for Inland Valley should address whether or not verifications for the above requests have been provided.

Plaintiff disagrees that the motion is otherwise moot. Plaintiff complains that Inland Valley is standing on its objections as to Nos. 1, 62, and 63, that Inland Valley failed to produce a “good number of the promised documents,” and that as to Nos. 22-30, not only were the promised documents not produced but the privilege log is deficient. 

Based on the above representations, the following analysis is limited to Nos. 1, 22-30, 32, 48, 56, 62, 63, 66, 68, 78 and 79. 

The Court notes that No. 48, while identified in the notice of motion, is not addressed in Plaintiff’s California Rules of Court rule 3.1345 accompanying separate statement. The motion is thus summarily denied as to No. 48. 

Request No. 1 asks Inland Valley to provide all applicable insurance agreements and policies, which Inland Valley agreed to do. Inland Valley, however, produced a redacted copy of the policy, which includes redaction of all additionally named insureds. Inland Valley contends that Plaintiff’s request for an unredacted copy violates third party privacy rights; this argument, however, is not well-taken, given Inland Valley’s statement that “[i]nformation regarding any potential overlap in ownership of Defendant and another facility is publicly available information and reflected in the Long-Term Care Facility Integrated Disclosure and Medi-Cal Cost Report which was previously produced to Plaintiff.” (Opp. Sep State., 8:26-28). Production of an unredacted copy of the policy is warranted. 

Request No. 32 seeks the facility’s 24 Hour Communication Log pertaining to the facility’s operations during the time period Plaintiff was a resident there. Inland Valley agreed to produce this log but redacted acuity information. Inland Valley represents that it supplemented this request, such that the issue is now moot; however, Plaintiff advises that the only documents produced in Inland Valley’s supplemental response as to No. 32 was 23 pages of a document entitled “Punch Detail Audit Log,” rather than the 24 Hour Communication Log with the acuity information unredacted. A further response is warranted.

The Court has reviewed Inland Valley’s supplemental responses to Nos. 22-30. The Court agrees that these responses do not contain a statement of compliance as per Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220.[FN6] Further, it appears to the Court from the July 29, 2024 deposition excerpt of Inland Valley’s person most knowledgeable (“PMK”) attached as Exhibit 4 to Garcia’s moving declaration that internal trending information exists which was not produced and which would not appear to be protected from discovery pursuant to Evidence Code section 1157[FN7], inasmuch as the PMK testified that this information is given to staff outside of committees.

Further, the Court has reviewed Inland Valley’s privilege log concurrently produced with its supplemental responses for Nos. 22-30, 62 and 63. (Isfeld Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. B.) Admittedly, “[t]he precise information required for an adequate privilege log will vary from case to case based on the privileges asserted and the underlying circumstances.” (Catalina Island Yacht Club v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 1116, 1130.) That said, “a privilege log typically should provide the identity and capacity of all individuals who authored, sent, or received each allegedly privileged document, the document’s date, a brief description of the document and its contents or subject matter sufficient to determine whether the privilege applies, and the precise privilege or protection asserted.” (Id.) Here, Inland Valley’s privilege log is insufficient in terms of information for the Court and Plaintiff’s counsel to determine whether the “Quarterly Quality Assurance Performance Improvement Meeting Minutes” are privileged. Further responses are warranted, as is a further privilege log. 

Plaintiff represents that Inland Valley falsely stated that it provided supplemental responses to Nos. 66 and 67. Plaintiff is wrong; further responses were provided. Still, the further response to No. 66 does not comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220. A further response is warranted. The Court also takes issue with the apparent inconsistency in Inland Valley’s original and further responses to No. 67; Inland Valley initially responded that it was “unable to comply with this request as responsive documents have never existed,” but then subsequently responded that “[f]ollowing a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, no responsive emails have been located. A diligent search and reasonable inquiry is ongoing and if non-privileged, non-protected responsive emails are located they will be provided.” Further responses are warranted. 

It is evident to the Court that Inland Valley has not produced and/or identified all documents responsive to No. 56 in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220. (See also, Garcia Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 4, 29:15-31:12).[FN8] Inland Valley’s response to No. 68 also reflects non-compliance with this Code provision. Further responses are warranted. 

Inland Valley supplemented its response to No. 78. No further response is warranted. Inland Valley’s further response to No. 79 is deficient, in that it is not accompanied by a privilege log. A further response is warranted. 

The motion, then, appears to be moot as to Nos. 38-40, 64, 65 and 69, provided that Inland Valley provides verifications at or before the time of the hearing. The motion is denied as to Nos. 48 and 78 and granted as to Nos. 1, 22-30, 56, 62, 63, 66-68 and 79. Inland Valley is to provide further, Code-compliant responses to Request for Production of Documents Nos. 1, 22-30, 56, 62, 63, 66-68 and 79 and to provide a further privilege log with respect to any requests for which any responsive documents are being withheld on the basis of privilege within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling. 

Based on the totality of the circumstances, the Court is not inclined to award sanctions as to this motion.

Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, the Court rules on the motions as follows:

Motion 1: The Court grants the motion as to Request for Admission No. 1. Renew Health is directed to provide a further, Code-compliant response to Plaintiff’s Request No. 1. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $935.

Motion 2: The Court grants the motion as to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1 only. Renew Health is directed to provide a further, Code-Compliant response to No. 17.1. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $935.

Motion 3: The Court grants the motion as to Special Interrogatory No. 15. Renew Health is directed to provide a further, Code-Compliant response to No. 15. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $935.

Motion 4: The Court grants the motion as to Request for Production of Documents No. 86. Renew Health is directed to provide a further, Code-Compliant response to No. 86. Sanctions are awarded in the amount of $935.

Motion 5: The Court denies the motion as to Nos. 45 and 46. It will hear further from the parties at the hearing regarding the adequacy of the proposed Stipulation Re Production of Witnesses for Deposition and Trial. The Court is not inclined to award sanctions as to this motion.

Motion 6: The Court denies the motion as to Nos. 48 and 78, deems the motion moot as to Nos. 38-40, 64, 65, and 69 (provided that Inland Valley confirms it has provided verifications to its supplemental responses), and grants the motion as to Nos. 1, 22-30, 56, 62, 63, 66-68, and 70. Inland Valley is to provide further, Code-compliant responses to Request for Production of Documents Nos. 1, 22-30, 56, 62, 63, 66-68 and 79 and to provide a further privilege log with respect to any requests for which any responsive documents are being withheld on the basis of privilege. The Court declines to award sanctions as to this motion.

Case Number: 24PSCV00328    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: H

Plaintiff Gref GG Eastland Center, LP’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.


Background

This is an unlawful detainer action regarding the commercial property located at 2851 E. Eastland Center Drive, Suite #5, aka Suite #162, West Covina, CA 91791.

On January 30, 2024, Plaintiff Gref GG Eastland Center, LP (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint, asserting a cause of action against Defendant West Covina Fire Wings, Inc. dba Fire Wings (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10 for unlawful detainer.

On April 2, 2024, Defendant’s default was entered.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment. For the reasons stated herein, the Court denies that application without prejudice and directs Plaintiff to file a revised default judgment packet.

Discussion

In denying Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment, the Court notes the following defects: 

1.       On June 11, 2024, the Honorable Peter A. Hernandez adopted his tentative ruling denying Plaintiff’s application for default judgment, which stated, in pertinent part, as follows:
“The court notes that the lease agreement (attached as Exhibit 4c to the Declaration of James Sattler provides for a security deposit of $11,207.17 (see pg. ii). It is unclear to the court whether or not the security deposit has been applied to the amount outstanding or credited back to Defendant. The court requests that a supplemental declaration be provided in this regard.”

On July 25, 2024, attorney John P. Byrne (“Byrne”) filed a supplemental declaration purporting to address the Court’s query. However, it is unclear to this Court how Byrne would have personal knowledge of the facts set forth in Paragraphs 3 and 4 or of Exhibit B thereto.

2.       Judge Hernandez’s June 11, 2024 ruling also “request[ed] that Plaintiff explain how the $11,590.00 monthly rent was calculated.” Again, the Court is unclear how Byrne would have personal knowledge of the facts set forth in Paragraph 5 of his supplemental declaration filed July 25, 2024. The Court also requests that Plaintiff identify the specific provisions of the lease (in addition to Lease Article 4.2) that were used to calculate the retail insurance and prorated real estate taxes.

Accordingly, the Court denies Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment without prejudice and directs Plaintiff to filed a revised default judgment packet that addresses the aforementioned issues.
