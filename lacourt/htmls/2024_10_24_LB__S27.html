DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00884    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Debra Thompson Crowe filed this action against Defendants, Walmart, Inc., Wal-Mart Associates, Inc., Sam’s West, Inc., and Maria Varela for age discrimination, disability discrimination, failure to engage in the interactive process, failure to provide reasonable accommodation, harassment, retaliation, failure to prevent harassment/discrimination/retaliation, and wrongful termination. Plaintiff’s original complaint, filed on 11/30/22, is her operative complaint.

 

2.     Motion to Exclude Expert Testimony

  1. Legal Standard

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.280, “(a) Within 20 days after the exchange described in 2034.260, any party who engaged in the exchange may submit a supplemental expert witness list containing the name and address of any experts who will express an opinion on a subject to be covered by an expert designated by an adverse party to the exchange, if the party supplementing an expert witness list has not previously retained an expert to testify on that subject. [¶] (b) This supplemental list shall be accompanied by an expert witness declaration under subdivision (c) of Section 2034.260 concerning those additional experts, and by all discoverable reports and writings, if any, made by those additional experts. [¶] (c) The party shall also make those experts immediately available for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410), which deposition may be taken even though the time limit for discovery under Chapter 8 (commencing with 2024.010) has expired.” (Ibid.)

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.300, “on objection of any party who has made a complete and timely compliance with Section 2034.260, the trial court shall exclude from evidence the expert opinion of any witness that is offered by any party who has unreasonably failed to do any of the following: [¶] (a) List that witness as an expert under Section 2034.260. [¶] (b) Submit an expert witness declaration. [¶] (c) Produce reports and writings of expert witnesses under Section 2034.270. [¶] (d) Make that expert available for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410).” (Ibid.)

“Failure to comply with expert designation rules may be found to be “unreasonable” when a party's conduct gives the appearance of gamesmanship, such as undue rigidity in responding to expert scheduling issues.” (Staub v. Kiley (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1437, 1447.) “Failure to comply with expert designation rules may be found to be ‘unreasonable’ when a party's conduct gives the appearance of gamesmanship, such as undue rigidity in responding to expert scheduling issues. [Citation.] The operative inquiry is whether the conduct being evaluated will compromise these evident purposes of the discovery statutes: “to assist the parties and the trier of fact in ascertaining the truth; to encourage settlement by educating the parties as to the strengths of their claims and defenses; to expedite and facilitate preparation and trial; to prevent delay; and to safeguard against surprise.’ [Citation.]” (Ibid.)

Further, even where nonmonetary sanctions, such as the exclusion of evidence, “are called for, they should be appropriate to the dereliction…. The sanctions the court may impose are such as are suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks, but the court may not impose sanctions which are designed not to accomplish the objects of discovery but to impose punishment.” (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327 (cleaned up) (internal citations and quotations omitted); see also St. Mary v. Super. Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 783-84; Do it Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 35, superseded by statute on another ground as stated in Union Bank v. Super. Ct. (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 582-83.)

 

  1. Relevant Facts

The parties do not dispute the following facts.

Trial for this case was originally scheduled for August 12, 2024.

On June 4, 2024, Plaintiff served on Defendants a demand for the exchange of expert witness information, with a deadline set for June 24, 2024. On June 24, 2024, Defendants timely disclosed Dr. Behnush Mortimer, Ph.D., CRC, CVE as an expert witness. However, Plaintiff failed to comply with her own deadline and served her expert witness disclosure on June 26, 2024. (Levitskaia Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 4.)

On July 16, 2024, Defendants served their supplemental expert designation, identifying Kristopher M. McLaughlin as a rebuttal expert. (Myers Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. C.)

On July 17, 2024, Defendants served deposition notices for Plaintiff’s experts. After a pre-trial video conference on July 18, 2024, Defendants’ counsel inquired about the availability of Plaintiff’s experts. Plaintiff’s counsel sent an email later that day proposing an alternative date.

On July 23, 2024, Plaintiffs inquired about Defendants’ experts’ availability. Defendants advised that both Dr. Mortimer and Mr. McLaughlin were available for deposition during the first week of trial.

During this same week, and on July 26, 2024, Mr. Myers, counsel for Plaintiffs, and Ms. Wrighten, counsel for Defendants, engaged in multiple discussions surrounding expert witness availability and the potential for trial continuances. Due to Ms. Wrighten’s unavailability until August 16, 2024, Ms. Wrighten proposed continuing trial to a date in October, which would have allowed for deposition of Defendants’ experts during the week of August 12. But, due to Mr. Meyers’ unavailability in the months of September and October, Mr. Myers refused to stipulate to a trial extension longer than one week.

On July 28, 2024, the time limit for expert discovery closed.

On July 30, 2024, Defendants applied for an ex parte trial continuance.

On July 30, 2024, Mr. Myers again inquired about the availability of Defendants’ experts for deposition. Ms. Wrighten refused to make Defendants’ experts available for deposition on the basis that Plaintiff failed to timely issue deposition notices for Defendants’ experts prior to the close of discovery, stating that “[t]he onus was on Plaintiff to serve the notices before the close of discovery. You know that. If you failed to do so, Plaintiff is precluded from that discovery. Again, you know that.” (Myers Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. D.) And, further, that Defendants would not “volunteer [their] experts for deposition when Plaintiff failed to timely notice their depositions.” (Ibid.)

On August 5, 2024, at the hearing on Defendants’ ex parte application for a trial continuance, the Court granted a trial continuance to December 2, 2024. Plaintiff also raised an ex parte application to advance the hearing on Plaintiff’s instant motion, previously set for November 26, 2024, which the Court granted and advanced to October 24, 2024.

In issuing its ruling, the Court held that expert discovery deadlines would remain closed and that only deadlines for pre-trial submissions would be recalculated from the new trial date.

 

  1. Parties’ Arguments

                 i.             Plaintiff’s Moving Arguments

Plaintiff’s central argument is that Defendants have acted unreasonably by failing to make their experts available for deposition. As noted above, Defendants designated Dr. Mortimer as an expert on June 24, 2024, and designated Mr. McLaughlin as a supplemental expert on July 16, 2024. Accordingly, Defendants were required to make both Dr. Mortimer and Mr. McLaughlin available for deposition. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2034.280(c), 2034.300(d).) However, Plaintiff contends that Defendants effectively side-stepped this requirement by only offering Mr. McLaughlin as available during the first week of trial.

Plaintiff further contends that the Defendants improperly disguised their gamesmanship under purported good faith meet and confer efforts to schedule the depositions, only to later refuse to make the experts available for deposition and attempting to blame Plaintiff for not unilaterally noticing the depositions during the prior meet and confer scheduling discussions. For example, as stated in the facts section above, when Plaintiff inquired about the availability of Defendants’ expert witnesses Ms. Wrighten stated on July 30, 2024 8:53 pm that it was on the onus of the Plaintiff to serve the notices “before the close of discovery. You know that. If you failed to do so, Plaintiff is precluded from that discovery. Again, you know that.”

However, Plaintiff contends that such failure to present Defendants’ experts for deposition was either unreasonable or, further, an abuse of the discovery process. First, Plaintiff argues that, at least with regard to supplemental witnesses, the onus is on the Defendant to “immediately” make their proposed expert witness available, and the time limits to notice the deposition before the close of discovery explicitly do not apply for supplemental experts. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2034.280(c).) Accordingly, Ms. Wrighten’s statement demonstrates an unreasonable attempt to flip the burden of making Defendants’ supplemental expert “immediately available” onto Plaintiffs.

Second, Plaintiff notes that it is a misuse of the discovery process to fail to submit to an authorized method of discovery and to fail to make a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning discovery. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) Here, Plaintiffs contend that Defendants failed to produce Dr. Mortimer for deposition, and failed to act in a reasonable and good faith manner during the parties’ meet and confer discussions because their communications did not demonstrate “a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution.” (Townsend v. Super. Ct. (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1438.) By way of alternative explanation, Plaintiff contends that Defendants only started to engage in these unreasonable and bad faith actions after Mr. Myers refused to agree to a trial continuance beyond a one-week extension.

 

                ii.             Defendants’ Opposition

In opposition, Defendant contends that Plaintiff lacks standing to bring this motion based on her own failure to comply with the expert discovery requirements, and, even if the court finds standing, Defendants failure to comply with section 2034.300 was not unreasonable.

First, Defendant notes that Plaintiff lacks standing to bring this motion because she failed to timely comply with expert witness disclosure requirements. Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.300 explicitly states that “on objection of any party who has made a complete and timely compliance with Section 2034.260, the trial court shall exclude from evidence the expert opinion of any witness that is offered by any party who has unreasonably failed to … [¶] (d) [m]ake that expert available for a deposition under Article 3 (commencing with Section 2034.410).” (Id. (emphasis added).) Further, Plaintiff points to case law where court have ruled that a party’s failure to comply with Code of Civil Procedure § 2034.260 precludes such party from bringing a motion to exclude the opposing party’s experts under § 2034.300. (Staub, supra, (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 1446 (Holding that defendant lacked standing to exclude opponent’s expert testimony pursuant to § 2034.300 based on defendant’s failure to strictly comply with § 2034.260’s disclosure requirements).) Defendant notes that Plaintiff failed to timely comply with Section 2034.260 because she served her expert disclosures two days after the required deadline. (Myers Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A; Levitskaia Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. 1.)

Second, Defendants contend that they did not unreasonably fail to comply with section 2034.300. Defendants agreed to make their experts available for deposition during the first week of trial, based on their experts’ availability. (Levitskaia Decl., ¶ 6.) Even if these dates were not amicable, Defendants state that Plaintiff failed to notice the expert depositions for any date before the closure of expert discovery (Id., ¶¶ 6-9.) And Plaintiff also failed to request the Court for any extension to the discovery deadline for the purpose of compelling Defendants’ Experts’ deposition. (Id. at ¶ 11.)

Defendant further argues that Plaintiff’s actions demonstrate Plaintiff’s own gamesmanship of the discovery process, not Defendant’s. As previously argued, Plaintiff failed to comply with the expert witness disclosure deadlines and failed to notice their expert depositions. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s instant motion seems to be an attempt to shield Plaintiff from the consequences of such failures, and to punish Defendants for holding to the discovery cutoff date that the Court explicitly confirmed was held unchanged.

Finally, Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s actions demonstrate a lack of diligence, and such lack of diligence should not be rewarded by excluding Defendants’ experts. On the one hand, Defendants timely complied with expert witness disclosures and provided dates for the availability of their experts for deposition. (Levitskaia Decl., ¶¶ 3, 6.) Defendants even offered to stipulate to re-designation of experts with a reasonable trial continuance, but Plaintiff rejected Defendants’ proposal. (Id., ¶ 8.)

On the other hand, Plaintiff failed to notice Defendants’ experts’ depositions, but still represented that it was ready for trial to begin on August 12, 2024, and refusing to agree to any trial continuance. Defendants’ proposed trial continuances would have allowed Plaintiff the opportunity to depose Defendants’ experts during the previously proposed dates of availability. (Levitskaia Decl., ¶ 6.) Further, Defendants have not received any request from Plaintiff to depose Defendants’ experts before the new trial date of December 2, 2024. (Stanchfield v. Hamer Toyota, Inc. (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1503-04; Staub, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 1447-48 (“The behavior of the party seeking to exclude the expert testimony is relevant to the reasonableness inquiry. … Any unfairness arising from plaintiffs’ tardy expert disclosure was exacerbated by defendants’ refusal to depose plaintiffs’ experts.”) Accordingly, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s actions demonstrate an attempt to unfairly disadvantage and unduly prejudice Defendants.

 

  1. Plaintiff Lacks Standing to Bring This Motion

Here, the Court find that Plaintiff failed to timely comply with Section 2034.260. Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.230, subdivision (b), provides that “[t]he specified date of exchange [for expert witness information] shall be 50 days before the initial trial date, or 20 days after service of the demand, whichever is closer to the trial date…” unless the trial court has found good cause to modify the exchange date. (Ibid.) Pursuant to this statutory provision, the date for timely expert witness information disclosure was June 24, 2024, as explicitly referenced in both Defendants’ and Plaintiff’s demand for the exchange of expert witness information. (Myers Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A; Levitskaia Decl., ¶ 2, Exhs. 1, 2.)

However, Defendants state, and provide evidence demonstrating, that Plaintiff did not serve her expert witness disclosure until two days after the disclosure deadline, on June 26, 2024. (Levitskaia Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 4 (“Dated: June 26, 2024”).) Accordingly, Plaintiff’s own failure to strictly comply with the expert witness disclosure requirements of section 2034.260 precludes her from bringing this motion to exclude Defendants’ expert testimony based on their alleged unreasonable failure to comply with expert discovery. (See Staub, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 1446.)

 

  1. Defendants did not Unreasonably Fail to Comply with Section 2034.300

Even if the Court finds that Plaintiff may have standing to bring this motion, Plaintiff has failed to satisfy its burden of demonstrating that Defendants’ unreasonably failed to comply with the requirements of section 2034.300. The factual assertions of both parties demonstrate that Defendants actually provided dates for Plaintiff to depose their expert witnesses and, nonetheless, Plaintiff failed to submit notices for such expert depositions until after the close of discovery. Even if these dates were not amicable, as they were originally set for the first week of trial, the factual evidence Plaintiff provided fails to demonstrate a diligent effort to set a different date for the expert depositions prior to the discovery cut off date.

Plaintiff’s failure to set a reasonable date for the expert depositions is compounded by the Plaintiff’s refusal to stipulate to, and opposition to, trial continuances. Although Plaintiff reasonably contends that he could not have stipulated to Defendants’ proposed continuance to October based on his previously scheduled arbitrations. However, even the limited one-week continuance that Plaintiff proposed would have provided Plaintiff the opportunity to depose Defendants’ experts during the dates of availability that Defendants previously provided, i.e., the first week of trial. Further, Defendants affirmatively state that Plaintiff has failed to seek such expert depositions after this Court issued it trial continuance. Plaintiff’s lack of diligence is explicitly relevant to the Court’s inquiry in whether Defendants’ failure to comply was unreasonable. (Staub, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at p. 1447-48 (“The behavior of the party seeking to exclude the expert testimony is relevant to the reasonableness inquiry”); see also Stanchfield v. Hamer Toyota, Inc. (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1503-04.) Based on these facts, Plaintiff’s motion seems to be aimed at eliciting an evidentiary sanction that would punish Defendants, rather than a sanction to obtain the object of discovery they were not able to receive. (See Biles, supra, 124 Cal.App.4th at p. 1327.)

Accordingly, Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate that Defendants’ failure to comply with section 2034.300 was unreasonable. Plaintiff’s motion to exclude defendants’ expert testimony is DENIED.

 

3.     Conclusion

In all, Plaintiff’s motion to exclude defendants’ expert testimony is DENIED.

Case Number: 23LBCV01317    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

On July 13, 2023, Plaintiffs Heirs and Estate of Jerrell Lee Williams Jr. and Darlene McCarty filed this action against Defendants VeoRide, Inc. (Veo); City of Long Beach; County of Los Angeles; the City of Long Beach Police Department; the Bull Bar; Mlouks, LLC; the Good Bar; Nicholas Mlouk; and Does 1 through 200 for (1) general negligence, (2) products liability, (3) dangerous condition of public property, (4) wrongful death, and (5) survival.  On October 16, 2023, Plaintiffs filed an operative First Amended Complaint (FAC) against Veo and Does 1 through 200 for (1) general negligence, (2) products liability, (3) wrongful death, and (4) survival.

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

  1. Evidentiary Objections

In their opposition papers, Plaintiffs submit evidentiary objections to Veo’s evidence. The Court OVERRULES objection number (4) and SUSTAINS objections numbered (1), (2), and (3).

  1. Request for Judicial Notice

In their opposition papers, Plaintiffs request judicial notice of (1) the City of Long Beach’s 2024 Shared Micromobility Program Regulations, (2) the Long Beach Municipal Code, and (3) two reports issued by the City of Long Beach Department of Public Works.  Because the Court may take judicial notice of the regulations of public entities pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (b) and the reports of a municipal government’s executive department pursuant to Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (c), the Court GRANTS Plaintiffs’ request.  (See Geraghty v. Shalizi (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 593, 602, fn. 4 [taking judicial notice of report from city budget analysts].)

  1. Legal Standard for Summary Judgment or Adjudication

A motion for summary judgment or adjudication provides “courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  It must be granted “if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”¿ (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)  To establish a triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must produce substantial responsive evidence.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)  Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”¿ (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)¿ “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)¿

  1. Undisputed Material Facts

The following facts are undisputed by the parties. XDS Technology Limited manufactures the Veo Astro 3 scooter for Veo.  (Defendant’s Separate Statement (DSS), ¶ 1.)  On July 17, 2021, Williams left a bar in the early hours of the morning and accessed one of these scooters (the Veo scooter) without signing in on its mobile application.  (DSS, ¶ 2.)  At approximately 3:30 AM, Williams drove the Veo scooter at the intersection of 7th Street and Ohio Avenue in Long Beach when a vehicle struck and killed Williams.  (DSS, ¶ 3.)  When the collision occurred, the Veo scooter was not connected to Veo’s network.  (DSS, ¶ 9.)

  1. General Negligence

              i.        Defendant’s Moving Arguments

Veo argues Plaintiffs cannot establish a negligence clam against Veo because Veo did not owe a duty of care to Williams.  Veo contends they do not have a duty to control the conduct of third persons or warn those endangered by such conduct in absence of a special relationship to the third person or the victim.  Here, Veo maintains there was no special relationship between Veo and Williams or any involved third party.  While Williams utilized one of Veo’s scooters, Veo argues Williams cannot be considered a customer as Williams was an unauthorized user of a scooter that had been disconnected from Veo’s network.  And even if Veo was in a special relationship, Veo contends the factors in Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108 (Rowland) weigh against finding a duty.    

             ii.        Opposition Arguments

Plaintiffs argue Veo owed a duty to Williams and members of the public as Veo was required by city regulation to report missing scooters.  Plaintiffs also contend Veo owed a duty as a result of its misfeasance in failing to report the missing scooters which placed Williams and members of the public in greater danger. 

            iii.        Analysis

Essential to a cause of action for negligence is the legal determination that the defendant owed a duty of care.  (Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 213.)  “[G]enerally a person has no duty to control the conduct of a third person, nor to warn those endangered by such conduct, in the absence of a ‘special relationship’ either to the third person or to the victim.”  (Pamela L. v. Farmer (1980) 112 Cal.App.3d 206, 209.)  But this rule “has no application where the defendant, through his or her own action (misfeasance) has made the plaintiff’s position worse and has created a foreseeable risk of harm from the third person.  In such cases the question of duty is governed by the standards of ordinary care.”  (Ibid.)  Furthermore, “[u]nder the doctrine of negligence per se, compliance with the standard of conduct established by the relevant statute, ordinance, or regulation is adopted as the duty of care.”  (Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 1200, 1210.)  Negligence per se requires that “(1) the defendant violated a statute, ordinance, or regulation of a public entity; (2) the violation proximately caused death or injury to person or property; (3) the death or injury resulted from an occurrence of the nature of which the statute, ordinance, or regulation was designed to prevent; and (4) the person suffering the death or the injury to his person or property was one of the class of persons for whose protection the statute, ordinance, or regulation was adopted.”  (Galvez v. Frields (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 1410, 1420.)

In this case, there can be no doubt that Veo owed a duty of care in providing scooters to authorized users.  But to the extent Veo claims they do not owe a duty to Williams because Williams was an unauthorized user, the authorities they have cited do not support this proposition.  In Robinson v. Bird Rides, Inc. (N.D. Ga. May 5, 2020, No. 1:19-cv-05295) 2020 WL 2129241, a federal district court found a scooter company did not owe a duty under Georgia law to protect third-party cyclists from scooters that were abandoned in the street.  (Id., at p. * 2.)  In Woodhouse v. Bird Rides, Inc. (W.D. Tex. May 17, 2021, No. SA-20-CV-01113-XR) 2021 WL 1986427, a federal district court found a scooter company did not owe a duty to protect third-party pedestrians from scooter customers.  (Id., at p. *6.)  In Springsteen v. Bird Rides, Inc. (D. Utah 2023) 666 F.Supp.3d 1170, a federal district court found a scooter company did not owe a duty to protect a third-party pedestrian from the tripping hazards posed by scooters that unknown persons abandoned.  (Id., at p. 1175-1175.)  And in Hacala v. Bird Rides, Inc. (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 292 (Hacala), the Second District Court of Appeal held a scooter company owed a duty of care to a pedestrian who tripped over one of their scooters which arose from the scooter company’s duty to use ordinary care in the management of their property.  (Id., at p. 313.)

Unlike the above cases, the present action does not involve a third-party pedestrian or cyclist attempting to hold Veo liable for the improper placement or use of its scooters.  And to the extent Veo claims it only owes duties to authorized users and not unauthorized users of its scooters, Veo has failed to provide authority that supports this contention.  Furthermore, “[t]he general rule in California is that ‘[e]veryone is responsible . . . for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person....”  (Cabral v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2011) 51 Cal.4th 764, 771, quoting Civ. Code, § 1714, subd. (a).)  As in Hacala, this rule imposes a duty on Veo to act with ordinary care when managing their scooters.  Veo argues Hacala is distinguishable from the present case because Veo did not have possessory control over the Veo scooter as Veo could not locate it, monitor its location, or control its use.  But Veo’s separate statement fails to establish this was the case.  While it is undisputed that the Veo scooter was not connected to Veo’s network at the time of the accident (DSS, ¶ 9), the effects of this lack of connection are unclear.  Veo states that when a scooter is “vandalized,” it goes off network and Veo loses both control and information.  (DSS, ¶ 8.)  But Veo’s separate statement fails to address if the Veo scooter at issue was vandalized and why it lost connection to Veo’s network.  This is not an insignificant omission as it leaves open the possibility that the loss of connection to Veo’s network was an internal issue and goes directly to Veo’s duty to manage its scooters with ordinary care.  Furthermore, while Veo’s separate statement states Veo cannot confirm the functionality of a vehicle once it is off-network, it does not state if Veo loses the ability to locate or track the scooters.

Accordingly, because Veo has failed to establish it did not owe any duty to Williams, Veo is not entitled to summary judgment or adjudication of this cause of action.

  1. Products Liability

              i.        Defendant’s Moving Arguments

Veo argues Plaintiffs cannot establish a claim for products liability against Veo because they do not have evidentiary support of any defect in the Veo scooter’s design.  Veo also contends they do not have any evidence that a defect in the Veo scooter’s design played a role in Williams’s accident.     

             ii.        Opposition Arguments

Plaintiffs argue they can establish triable issues of fact with regards to defects including the claim that the Veo scooter lacked proper warnings and could be operated in an unsafe manner.

            iii.        Analysis

In a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, “a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: ‘The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (“disproving”) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.’”  (Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103, quoting Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.) 

Here, Veo does neither.  On the issue of alleged defects, Veo’s separate statement first states that Plaintiffs believed the Veo scooter’s defects included the fact that it was accessible at night without the proper lighting, reflectors, and safety constructs.  (DSS, ¶ 12.)  Veo’s separate statement then states that the Veo scooter’s headlight was on at the time of the collision.  (DSS, ¶ 13.)  But the only evidence offered in support of this fact is Veo’s own discovery responses in which Veo states a police report indicates that surveillance footage shows the headlights of the Veo scooter.  (Wackman Decl., Ex. 7, p. 3:17-20, 4:21-24, 5:11-14.)  Because Veo’s discovery response is based on police reports which constitute inadmissible hearsay (See People v. McVey (2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 405, 415), Veo’s evidence is inadmissible and this fact is unsupported.  Thus, triable issues of fact remain with regards to whether the Veo scooter’s headlights were operative. 

On the issue of causation, Veo argues there is no indication the design of the Veo scooter played any role in Williams’s accident as Williams was “struck by an intoxicated and speeding driver.”  But no material facts in Veo’s separate statement support this claim.  Instead, the only fact offered states the driver that hit Williams pled nolo contendere to vehicular homicide in violation of Penal Code section 192, subdivision (c)(1).  No facts state if the driver was intoxicated or speeding as suggested by Veo.  Thus, triable issues of fact also remain with regards to whether the Veo scooter’s alleged defects were the cause of Williams’s fatal accident.

Accordingly, because Veo has failed to establish Plaintiffs cannot prove the elements of their products liability claim, Veo is not entitled to summary judgment or adjudication of this cause of action.

  1. Wrongful Death and Survival Claims

Veo’s motion for summary judgment or adjudication does not specifically address Plaintiffs’ claims for wrongful death and survival.  Because these causes of action are derivative of Plaintiffs’ negligence and products liability claims, Veo is not entitled to summary judgment or adjudication of these causes of action for the same reasons outlined above.

  1. Conclusion

Veo has failed to carry their initial burden of demonstrating an absence of triable issues of material fact with regards to Plaintiffs’ causes of action.  Veo’s motion for summary judgment or adjudication is therefore denied.

Defendants are ordered to give notice.


Case Number: 23LBCV01651    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Daniel Kwan Haeng Lee (Plaintiff) filed this action against JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A., as Successor-in-Interest for WaMu Mortgage Pass-Through Certificates Series 2008-AR12 and Does 1-25 for quiet title, slander of title, cancellation and expungement of void instrument, and declaratory relief. Plaintiff filed the complaint on September 6, 2023.

On October 9, 2023, JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. and U.S. Bank NA (Defendants) filed a demurrer and motion to strike directed at the complaint. On March 6, 2024, prior to the hearing on the pleading challenge, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (FAC). The FAC includes causes of action for cancellation and expungement of void instruments and declaratory relief.

On September 11, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend.

2.     Motion for Reconsideration

On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for reconsideration of the ruling sustaining the demurrer without leave to amend. Plaintiff contends there are new or different facts, circumstances, or law that support reconsideration per Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 subd. (b). Specifically, Plaintiff contends he has discovered that his revocable family trust instrument shows explicit and mutual intent by all settlors to hold the property as a community property. Additionally, he contends the recent court case of Haggerty v. Thornton (2024) 15 Cal.5th 729 makes clear that the community property presumption overrides the title presumption.

            On September 17, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition to Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration. Defendants contend Plaintiff does not demonstrate new or different facts, circumstances, or law that were not available during the demurrer proceedings. Further, Defendants argue that the cases Plaintiff cites are not new authority.

            On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed his reply. Plaintiff restates his argument that his motion for reconsideration is based on newly discovered facts.

            On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff submitted an objection raising multiple arguments to the Court’s tentative ruling denying Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration. Plaintiff argues that he presented new evidence of Defendant’s fraudulent conduct in addition to new evidence from the trust documents. Plaintiff argues that res judicata does not bar Plaintiff’s FAC. Plaintiff further argues that the Court improperly dismissed the demurrer with prejudice. Lastly, Plaintiff raises due process concerns based on the Courts Minute Order from August 19, 2024, regarding Plaintiff’s disability and the structure of oral arguments.

a.     Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 governs motions for consideration. “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1008 subd. (a).)

A party seeking reconsideration of a prior order based on “new or different facts, circumstances or law” must provide a satisfactory explanation for failing to present the information at the first hearing; i.e., a showing of reasonable diligence. (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.) “The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 211-23.) A motion for reconsideration is properly denied where based on evidence that could have been presented in connection with the original motion. (Morris v. AGFA Corp. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1452, 1460.) A court acts in excess of jurisdiction when it grants a motion to reconsider that is not based upon “new or different facts, circumstances, or law.” (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1499.)

b.     Motion for Reconsideration

Plaintiff argues that new or different facts or circumstances were discovered regarding Plaintiff’s revocable family trust instrument. Specifically, his revocable family trust instrument shows explicit and mutual intent by all settlors to hold the property as a community property and there is no other explicit agreement stating otherwise.

Here, Plaintiff has not shown that his revocable family trust instrument was new or previously inaccessible with reasonable diligence. The trust instrument is not new. It was signed and dated by Plaintiff on July 28, 2006. (Motion for Reconsideration, Exh. B.) Plaintiff states that his discovery of the trust instrument was delayed because the trust instrument was “stored in a remote location.” (Lee Decl., ¶ 6.) However, Plaintiff was aware of the revocable family trust during the course of this action. Plaintiff references the family trust in his FAC and includes the corresponding grant deed. (FAC, ¶ 2 and Exh. 2.) Plaintiff does not explain why he could not access the trust instrument in the remote location earlier. Plaintiff does not meet his burden under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 subd. (a) of why this trust instrument could not be produced earlier. (See Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.)

            Plaintiff further objects to the Court’s tentative ruling based on the Court’s failure to consider new evidence regarding a notice of default (NOD), notice of trustee sale (NOTS), and fraudulent actions by Defendants. However, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration does not mention any facts regarding a NOD, NOTS, or new fraudulent actions. Plaintiff raises these for the first time in its reply to Defendants’ opposition. (Reply, p. 2.) The Court cannot consider new facts raised in Plaintiff’s reply. (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1538 [“[p]oints raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument.”].) Further, Plaintiff does not explain how this is new information and why the information was not previously accessible. Therefore, Plaintiff has not raised new information regarding a NOD, NOTS, or fraudulent actions.

            Next, Plaintiff argues that new case law constitutes a basis for reconsideration. Plaintiff provides three cases: (1) Haggerty v. Thornton (2024) 15 Cal.5th 729, (2) In Re Brace (2020) 9 Cal.5th 903, and (3) Trenk v. Soheili (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 1033. This case law is not new. All three of these cases were decided before Defendants filed their demurrer on April 5, 2024. Further, Trenk is distinguishable from the instant action. In Trenk, the court considered whether a married couple owned their residence as community property since they held the property as joint tenants. (Trenk, supra, 58 Cal.App.5th at 1044-45.) Here, Plaintiff’s wife held the title as her sole and separate property. Accordingly, Plaintiff does not provide new or different law warranting reconsideration of the demurrer.

            Lastly, Plaintiff’s objections regarding the structure of oral arguments based on his disability are not properly raised in a motion for reconsideration. The standard for a motion for reconsideration is that the Plaintiff raises “new or different facts, circumstances, or law.” (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1008 subd. (a).) In lieu of oral arguments, Plaintiff was permitted to provide the instant written opposition to the tentative rulings.

            Plaintiff has not raised new facts, circumstances, or law warranting reconsideration of the Court’s ruling on the demurrer. Accordingly, the Court denied Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration.

3.     Motion for Leave to Amend SAC

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for leave to file a Second Amended Complaint, setting the motion for hearing on October 1, 2024. The hearing was scheduled for after the Court ruled on the demurrer to the First Amended Complaint.

On September 17, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition to Plaintiff’s motion. Defendants oppose the motion on the grounds that the demurrer was sustained without leave to amend and that leave to amend would be futile. On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply arguing that there are new facts, and that Defendants have not shown prejudice.

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff submitted his objections to the Court’s tentative ruling on the motion for reconsideration and motion for leave to amend. In connection with the leave to amend, Plaintiff argues the Court’s timeline incorrectly stated that Plaintiff filed his motion for leave to amend after the Court’s ruling on the demurrer.

a.     Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1) states: “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms, as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”  

Judicial policy favors resolution of all disputed matters between the parties, and therefore, courts have held that “there is a strong policy in favor of liberal allowance of amendments.” (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 296-97; see also Ventura v. ABM Industries, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 258, 268) [“Trial courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial where the adverse party will not be prejudiced.”].) 

Pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), a motion to amend must: (1) include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered; and (2) state what allegations are proposed to be deleted from or added to the previous pleading and where such allegations are located. Rule 3.1324(b) requires a separate declaration that accompanies the motion, stating: “(1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier.” 

b.     Motion for Leave to Amend  

Plaintiff seeks leave to amend to (1) correct party names and add parties, (2) add causes of action for fraud and wrongful foreclosure, (3) remove a cause of action for declaratory relief, (4) fix clerical errors, and (5) add facts to the causes of action.

Firstly, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend meets the procedural requirements articulated in California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a). Plaintiff attaches the proposed SAC and states and locates the proposed allegations. (Motion for Leave to Amend, Exh. 1 and Appendix.) As for California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b), Plaintiff includes a declaration with the effect of the proposed amendments and why it is necessary and proper. (Lee Decl., ¶ 6.) Plaintiff states that he discovered new facts after April 24, 2024, but does not explain why the request for amendment was not made at that time. (Lee Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.)

Next, Plaintiff argues that the proposed amendments are warranted in furtherance of justice. However, Plaintiff fails to show how the proposed SAC would cure the defects detailed in the Court’s ruling sustaining the demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend. Plaintiff seeks to replace three Does with US Bank entities. However, these added named defendants would not cure the defects in the demurrer. Defendant US Bank was a moving party in the demurrer. The demurrer found that the FAC did not plead the existence of a void deed of trust because Plaintiff’s wife was the sole owner. (Minute Order, 8/8/24.) The addition of new US Bank defendants does not alter that finding.

Plaintiff’s amendments further do not cure the defects in the FAC regarding his standing to bring these claims. Plaintiff seeks to add two causes of action for fraud and wrongful foreclosure. However, these new causes of action still fail for the same defect with Plaintiff’s standing. Namely, that Plaintiff was not an owner of the property on the date of the encumbrance. These causes of action rely on the same facts and documents as the prior causes of action which the Court found Plaintiff did not have standing to bring. Plaintiff’s objection that dismissal without prejudice is extreme ignores the fact that Plaintiff’s new findings do not change the core defect regarding Plaintiff’s standing. Plaintiff’s proposed amendments do not provide facts supporting Plaintiff’s standing.

Plaintiff further objects to the Court’s tentative ruling on the ground that the Court misstated the timeline. However, while Plaintiff filed the motion before the ruling on the demurrer, the hearing on the motion was not scheduled until October 1, 2024. The motion was scheduled to be heard after the ruling on the demurrer.

Based on the foregoing, and because Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend to file a SAC is denied.

4.     Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is denied.

Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend is denied.

Case Number: 24LBCV00100    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Davida Wright (Plaintiff) filed this action against Ford Motor Company (Defendant) and Does 1 through 10 on January 17, 2024. The complaint alleges causes of action for failure to repurchase/replace, failure to complete repairs within 30 days, breach of express warranty, breach of implied warranty of merchantability, violation of the Magnusson Moss Warranty Act, violation of the Business and Professions Code, and violation of a service contract. The complaint alleges Plaintiff purchased a 2018 Lincoln MKC (the Vehicle) from Defendant and that the Vehicle began presenting defects and nonconformities during the warranty period. The complaint also alleges that Defendant violated the Song-Beverly Warranty Act by failing to promptly service, repair, or replace the vehicle.

Defendant filed the instant demurrer on March 29, 2024, challenging the sufficiency of Plaintiff’s sixth and seventh causes of action.

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Meet and Confer

On February 23, 2024, Defendant’s counsel, Douglas A. Proudfoot, sent Plaintiff’s counsel a meet and confer letter stating his intent to file a demurrer and outlining the legal arguments supporting the demurrer. (Declaration of Douglas A. Proudfoot [Proudfoot Decl.], ¶ 3.) Defendant did not attach the letter to the declaration. Plaintiff’s counsel did not reply to the correspondence. (Ibid.) Subsequently, Proudfoot attempted to reach Plaintiff’s counsel via phone to discuss the issues raised in the demurrer, but Plaintiff’s counsel has not responded. (Id., at ¶ 4.) Plaintiff does not oppose Proudfoot’s statements.

The declaration satisfies the meet and confer requirement.

 

b.     Legal Standard

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading. It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan 39 (1985) Cal.3d 311.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under Civil Procedure Code section 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within]. Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per section 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. Per section 430.10(a), a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).)

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. (Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn (1987) 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769.) And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

Finally, Civil Procedure Code section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).)

 

c.     Analysis

 

Plaintiff’s Sixth Cause of Action seeking injunctive relief and restitution fails to state facts establishing no remedy at law if available.

Generally, the Song-Beverly Act does not restrict remedies that are otherwise available. (Civ. Code, § 1790.4.) However, because Unfair Competition Law (UCL) claims are equitable in nature, a plaintiff seeking UCL remedies must establish that no adequate remedy at law is available. (Knox v. Phoenix Leasing, Inc. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1357, 1368; Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 24 Cal.4th 1134, 1144; see also Prudential Home Mortgage Company v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1236, 1249 [holding the UCL “is subject to fundamental equitable principles, including inadequacy of the legal remedy”].)

Here, Defendant argues that Plaintiff does not sufficiently plead the sixth cause of action because in order to seek injunctive relief under Business and Professions Code section 17200, Plaintiff must establish there is no adequate remedy at law available. Specifically, Defendant notes that Plaintiff seeks injunctive relief and restitution (Compl, ¶ 38.) even though other viable causes of action are available for breach of express and implied warranties under the Song-Beverly Act. Plaintiff does not oppose.

The court finds that Plaintiff does not plead sufficient facts to establish that no other remedies at law exist.

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained on this ground.

 

Plaintiff’s Seventh Cause of Action fails to state facts supporting a claim for service contract violations because Plaintiff does not plead facts establishing Defendant is subject to the service contract.

“Except as otherwise expressly provided in the service contract, every service contract shall obligate the service contractor to provide to the buyer of the product all of the services and functional parts that may be necessary to maintain proper operation of the entire product under normal operation and service for the duration of the service contract and without additional charge.” (Civ. Code, § 1794.4, subd. (b).) “Warranties and service contracts, though they may sometimes overlap, are intended by the Legislature to fall into distinct categories: a warranty specifies a product is fit and defect-free, whereas a service contract is an agreement to provide repair services, a kind of insurance . . . generally purchased for an additional cost. [Citation.]” (Dell, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 911, 915, quoting Gavaldon v. DaimlerChrysler Corp. (2004) 32 Cal.4th 1246, 1258, fn. Omitted; see Civ. Code, § 1794.4, subd. (a).)

Here, Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to establish a service contract claim against Defendant because Plaintiff does not allege facts establishing that Defendant was the service contractor under the service contract. The complaint alleges that Plaintiff purchased the service contract from Echo Park Automative (Compl., ¶ 40.), and thus, Echo Park Automotive is the service contractor. Plaintiff does not oppose.

The court finds that Plaintiff failed to allege facts establishing Defendant as the service contractor liable under the service contract.

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer on this ground.

 

3.     Conclusion

Because Plaintiff inadequately pleads causes of action for UCL violations and service contract violations, Defendant’s demurrer is sustained, without leave to amend.

On March 18, 2024, Plaintiffs Hannah Lanae Navarro and Ismael Navarro filed a complaint against Defendant Jose Miguel Lim Iv and Does 1 through 20 before this Court alleging negligence, gross negligence, recklessness, negligent entrustment, and negligent infliction of emotional distress.

1.     Motions to Compel Further

a.     Meet and Confer

            On July 8, 2024, the parties met and conferred pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2016.040. (Smith Decl. ¶ 16.)

b.     Legal Standard

 

            Compel Interrogatories and Requests for Production – Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.300 & 2031.310 provide for a party to bring a motion to compel further responses to interrogatories and requests for production where the responding party provides inadequate, incomplete, or evasive responses, or the objections are too general or without merit. The propounding party must submit a declaration under Code of Civ. Proc. §2016.040 stating facts demonstrating a good faith and reasonable effort to informally resolve each issue raised by the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §2030.300, subd. (b).) The motions must be brought within 45 days of service of the responses or supplemental responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (c) & 2031.310, subd. (c).) Sanctions are mandatory against the party or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further unless the party acted with substantial justification or the circumstances make imposition of sanctions unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300, subd. (d) & 2031.310, subd. (h).)    

            California Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(a) provides, in relevant part that a party “may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete. (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive. (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

            California Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310 (b)(1) requires a party seeking to compel production to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.” (Accord, Kirkland v. Superior Ct., 95 Cal. App. 4th 92, 98 (2002).) “Good cause” has been liberally construed by the courts when considering a request for production of documents. Good cause typically may be shown merely upon a showing of relevance. (See Associated Brewers Distributing Co. v. Superior Ct., 65 Cal. 2d 583, 586 (1967); TBG Insurance Services Corp. v. Superior Ct., 96 Cal. App. 4th 443, 448 (2002).)

Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280 provides, in relevant part:

            If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply: 

            (a) The party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010). . .  (b) The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). 

(c) The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (a), (b), (c).)  Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.250, a responding party must serve responses to a propounding party’s request for admission within thirty (30) days of service.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).) 

c.     Merits

            Plaintiff Hannah Lanae Navarro moves for an Order compelling Defendant to provide further responses to the four discovery requests because the responses were inadequate, incomplete, and evasive. (Not. at p.1.)

            Thus, the only dispute remaining is whether sanctions are warranted.

d.     Sanctions

Plaintiff seeks sanctions in the amount of $2,100.00 for each discovery motion for a total of $8,400.00. Defendant argues that sanctions are not warranted because Acker & Whipple acted with substantial justification in serving objections on Mr. Lim’s behalf for preservation purposes because counsel had a conflict. (Opposition pp.4-5.) Nevertheless, the Court finds sanctions are warranted in a reduced amount against Defendant’s counsel Acker & Whipple because the amount sought is excessive due to the simplicity of the motions.

Here, Acker & Whipple was assigned as defense counsel in relation to Hannah Navarro, et al. v. Jose Miguel Lim IV, et al. (the “Navarro Action”). (Smith Decl. ¶ 4.) In early June 2024, Acker & Whipple discovered that Mr. Lim had filed his own personal injury action against various government entities, Jose Miguel Lim IV v. City of Carson, et al., case no. 23CMCV01137 (the “Lim Action”), and that one of the named defendants to the Lim Action, the City of Los Angeles, is a client of Acker & Whipple, which created a conflict fundamentally precluding Acker & Whipple from assisting Mr. Lim with the preparation of his responses because its two existing clients had adverse interests in relation to the subject incident. Acker & Whipple requested from defendants’ insurance carrier that new defense counsel be assigned and informed Mr. Taheripour about these developments on June 17, 2024, and then again on June 20, 2024, when Michael Schonbuch of Schonbuch Hallissy LLP was assigned as the new defense counsel. (Smith Decl. ¶ 7.) Notably, no substitution of attorney was ever filed with the Court. Shortly thereafter, it was discovered that Schonbuch Hallissy LLP also had a conflict relative to the Lim Action as named defendant California Department of Transportation (“CalTrans”) was one of its clients. (Smith Decl. ¶ 8.)

On June 27, 2024, Acker & Whipple sent an email to Plaintiff’s Counsel advising him of the Lim action and how this lawsuit had created conflicts for both Acker & Whipple and Schonbuch Hallissy LLP. (Smith Decl. ¶ 10.) The firm also informed Plaintiff’s Counsel of Mr. Bloeser’s representation that the City of Los Angeles had been dismissed from the Lim Action, which would clear the conflict for Acker & Whipple, and allow for it to resume the handling of the defense in the Navarro Action. (Smith Decl. ¶ 10.)

Nevertheless, it is unclear how Acker & Whipple was unaware of the conflict between clients when Mr. Lim filed the Lim Action on July 24, 2023, before Acker & Whipple’s representation of Mr. Lim in the Navarro Action. Moreover, Acker & Whipple remained as Defendant’s counsel of record. Thus, sanctions are warranted against Defendant’s counsel, Acker & Whipple.

However, sanctions are reduced to $4,800.00 for 3.5 hours total drafting and preparing the motions, reply and 0.5 hour attending the hearing at a rate of $300.00 because the four motions are nearly identical and simple.

 

2.     Conclusion

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s discovery motions compelling Defendant’s further response as MOOT.

Sanctions are granted in the amount of $4,800.00 against Defendants counsel only. Acker & Whipple is ordered to pay Plaintiff $4,800.00 within 30 days of this order.

The Court further notes that there are no future hearing dates scheduled in this case.  The Court sets a Case Management Conference and Trial Setting Conference for December 18, 2024.

Moving Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.
