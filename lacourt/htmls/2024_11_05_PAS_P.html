DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV01269    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO SEAL


I.        INTRODUCTION

This action arises from claims of (1) sexual assault, (2) negligence, and (3) negligent failure to educate, train, or warn, brought by Plaintiffs JANE M.A.T. DOE and JANE M.I.G. DOE (“Plaintiffs”) against Defendants DEFENDANT DOE 1, DEFENDANT DOE 2, and DOES 3 through 100, inclusive. Plaintiffs initiated this action on December 29, 2022, alleging harm stemming from sexual abuse they endured as minors.

On October 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a motion to seal Exhibits 1 and 2 attached to the Declaration of Thomas B. Murtland in support of their Motion for Protective Order. Defendant DOE 1 (“Defendant”) filed an opposition to the motion to seal on October 23, 2024, arguing against the necessity and scope of the requested sealing order. Plaintiffs subsequently filed a reply to Defendant’s opposition on October 29, 2024, reaffirming their need for the sealing order to protect sensitive information related to the underlying allegations.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open to public review.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c).)  “The court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.550(d); see also NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1217-1218 (NBC).)  

“A record must not be filed under seal without a court order.  The court must not permit a record to be filed under seal based solely on the agreement or stipulation of the parties.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(a).)  “A party requesting that a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing the record.  The motion or application must be accompanied by a memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(1).)  “If necessary to prevent disclosure, any motion or application, any opposition, and any supporting documents must be filed in a public redacted version and lodged in a complete, unredacted version conditionally under seal.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 2.551(b)(5).)   

III.     ANALYSIS

          Plaintiffs request the Court seal Exhibits 1 and 2 attached to the Declaration of Thomas B. Murtland, arguing that these exhibits contain sensitive information that identifies Plaintiffs as victims of childhood sexual assault. Citing Rule 2.550 of the California Rules of Court, Plaintiffs contend that sealing is necessary to protect their privacy and prevent further harm and public exposure. (Motion, pg.3) They assert that an overriding interest—the privacy rights of Plaintiffs—justifies restricting public access. Specifically, Plaintiffs argue that without sealing, their identities would be disclosed, prejudicing their privacy rights.

Plaintiffs contend that the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored to redact only the identifying information, without sealing the entire record. (Motion, pg. 4) They also argue that no less restrictive alternative exists, as the redactions are limited to the specific information necessary to protect their identities. (Id.)

In Opposition, Defendant Doe 1 argues Plaintiff’s motion for protective order lacks clear scope and is impractical. (Opposition, pg. 2) Defendant also argues Plaintiffs have inconsistently expanded the scope of the protective order, seeking to label various information as confidential which could hinder the Defendant’s investigation. (Opposition pg. 3-4) Defendant further contends that Plaintiffs' claims of harassment are unsubstantiated, and that Plaintiffs have also disclosed Defendant’s true names. (Opposition pg. 5-6) Defendant argues the motion should be denied because it would unfairly restrict their right to investigate while permitting Plaintiffs broad discretion over what information is deemed confidential.

In their Reply, Plaintiffs contend that Defendant’s past approach to gathering information was excessive, asserting that Defendant did not need to disclose Plaintiffs' names to the broader alumni network to obtain relevant details (Reply, pg. 3). Plaintiffs clarify that their own outreach emails to individuals omitted any identifying information about themselves. They reiterate the necessity of a protective order to protect Plaintiffs privacy rights and prevent further exposure.

The Court agrees with Plaintiffs and finds that sealing the documents is necessary to protect them from further exposure. Pursuant to Rules 2.550 and 2.551, the Court determines that there is an overriding interest in safeguarding Plaintiffs' identities in this sexual assault case. The interest in protecting individuals involved in sensitive matters justifies sealing the records, as Plaintiffs could be prejudiced if their identities were disclosed. This case involves highly sensitive allegations of sexual assault and inappropriate treatment of minors. The Court further finds that the request to seal Exhibits 1 and 2 to Thomas B. Murtland’s Declaration and the Motion for Protective Order is narrowly tailored.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Seal Exhibits 1 and 2 to Thomas B. Murtland’s Declaration.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV01804    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO THE FAC and GRANTING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO STRIKE IN PART and DENYING IN PART

I.         INTRODUCTION

            This landlord-tenant action was filed by Plaintiffs Kim Carlton and Frederick Carlton (“Plaintiffs”) against Defendants Gladys Fung and James Tam (“Defendants”). Plaintiffs bring seven causes of action related to their rental property. Specifically, Plaintiffs assert claims for: (1) breach of contract; (2) nuisance under Civil Code § 3479; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (4) breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment under Civil Code § 1972; (5) breach of the implied warranty of habitability under Civil Code § 1942.4; (6) constructive eviction under Civil Code § 1946.2; and (7) negligence.

Plaintiffs allege that Defendants failed to maintain the rental property in a safe and habitable condition, despite repeated notifications of significant issues.

On June 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). On July 25, 2024, Defendants responded by filing a Demurrer with a Motion to Strike. On September 18, 2024, Plaintiffs opposed the Demurrer and on October 16, 2024, Defendants filed a Reply.

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).

Here, Defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter via email on July 17, 2024 (Mantovani Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A). The following day, both Plaintiffs’ counsel and Defense counsel spoke on the phone but were unable to resolve the issues (Mantovani Decl., ¶ 5). Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendants have satisfied the meet and confer requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41.

 

II.        DEMURRER

A.    Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. A demurrer admits, provisionally for purposes of testing the pleading, all material facts properly pleaded. Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1247. A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. 

 

B.    Discussion

Defendants demur to the second and third causes of action in Plaintiffs’ FAC—private nuisance and intentional infliction of emotional distress—arguing that Plaintiffs have failed to allege sufficient facts to support these claims and that the nuisance claim is duplicative. Defendants have not demurred to the first, fourth, fifth, sixth, or seventh causes of action.

Second Cause of Action: Nuisance

“The essence of a private nuisance is an interference with the use and enjoyment of land.” (Friends of H Street v. City of Sacramento (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 152, 160.) Thus, to allege a cause of action for private nuisance, the plaintiff must allege injury specific to the use and enjoyment of his land. (See Adams v. MHC Colony Park Limited Partnership (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 601, 610.) 

Defendants argue that the facts underlying the nuisance claim are identical to those in the negligence claim, rendering the second cause of action duplicative of the seventh cause of action. (Demurrer, pp. 8-9) Defendants contend that both claims rely on the same alleged conduct, specifically they arise out of Defendants’ alleged duty and alleged failure to remedy the claimed defects on the Property. (Demurrer p. 9)

Upon review of the FAC, the Court finds that the nuisance cause of action alleges that “Defendants negligently and intentionally caused to exist [a] nuisance” (FAC ¶ 28). A nuisance claim is treated as a negligence claim if both causes of action rely on the same underlying facts about a lack of due care (Melton v. Boustred (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 521, 542; El Escorial Owners’ Association v. DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1337, 1349).

Here, Plaintiffs’ claims that Defendants actions were both negligent and intentional are 1) internally inconsistent and 2) based on the same facts and alleged conduct as the negligence cause of action. The FAC does not provide specific factual allegations to support the conclusory assertion that Defendants intentionally created the nuisance. Without such factual support, the allegation of intentionality remains insufficient to sustain nuisance as a separate cause of action.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer as the second cause of action, with leave to amend.

Third Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress (IIED)

A cause of action for IIED exists when there is “(1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.”  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1050-51.)  A defendant's conduct is “outrageous” when it is so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.  (Id.)  With regard to the first element, IIED calls for intentional, or at least reckless conduct—conduct intended to inflict injury or engaged in with the realization that injury will result.  (Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal.3d 197, 210.)  

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute IIED, asserting that the FAC does not specify any extreme emotional distress suffered by Plaintiffs, which is an essential element for this claim (Demurrer, p. 5). Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ allegations are conclusory and lack specific details of “enduring” medical or psychological issues that would satisfy the standard of severe distress beyond what a reasonable person in a civilized society should be expected to bear (Demurrer, p. 6). They contend that the claims, at most, suggest negligence rather than intentional or outrageous conduct that could support IIED.

The FAC alleges that “the actions of the Defendants, as alleged herein, were extreme and outrageous and done with reckless disregard of the possibility that the Plaintiffs would suffer emotional distress” (FAC ¶ 34). Plaintiffs further claim they suffered “extreme emotional distress” as a result of Defendants’ reckless conduct, causing “mental, emotional, and physical distress, pain and suffering,” for which they seek exemplary and punitive damages (FAC ¶¶ 35-36).

The Court finds that Plaintiffs’ FAC primarily relies on conclusory statements rather than specific factual allegations to substantiate “extreme and outrageous” conduct or reckless intent. General statements, such as “Defendants acted with reckless disregard” (FAC ¶ 34), lack detail and fail to demonstrate how the conduct meets the standard of “outrageous,” as defined by California law. As Hughes v. Pair explains, extreme or outrageous conduct must “exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community” (46 Cal.4th at 1050-51).

Although Plaintiffs allege that they experienced physical and psychological symptoms, including “respiratory issues, brain fog, insomnia, congestion, labored breathing, fatigue, stress, extreme forgetfulness, and sleep apnea” (FAC ¶ 9), the FAC does not provide sufficient factual details to establish severe emotional distress that is “so enduring that no reasonable person in a civilized society should be expected to bear it” (Demurrer, p. 5) 

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the third cause of action, with leave to amend.

 

IV.       MOTION TO STRIKE

A.    Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. §436: “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:

(a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.

(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”

Code Civ. Proc. §431.10:

“(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense.

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:

(1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.

(2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.

(3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”             

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294 (a).) “Malice” is conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294 (c)(1).) “‘Punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.’ [Citation.]” (Lackner v. North, 135 Cal. App. 4th 1188, 1210 (2006).) “As amended to include [despicable], the [Civil Code section 3294] plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests. The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court, 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725 (1994).) The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.” (Id.) Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people. Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’” (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co., 25 Cal. App. 4th 1269, 1287 (1994).) 

Further, “[t]here must be evidence that defendant acted with knowledge of the probable dangerous consequences to plaintiff’s interests and deliberately failed to avoid these consequences.” (Flyer’s Body Shop Profit Sharing Plan v. Ticor Title Ins. Co., 185 Cal. App. 3d 1149, 1155 (1986); see also Angie M. v. Superior Court, 37 Cal. App. 4th 1217, 1228 (1995) [“Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probably dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences”].) 

In landlord-tenant cases, punitive damage awards have been upheld where there is evidence of intentionally created nuisance, wrongful eviction, and other similar circumstances. Fernandez v. Singh (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 932; Rich v. Schwab (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 803 (Civ. Code §1942.5 provides for an award of punitive damages); Stroiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903.

 

B.    Discussion

            Defendant moves to strike specific portions of the FAC that reference punitive damages, asserting that the FAC fails to allege facts sufficient to meet the requirements for punitive damages. Defendant argues that the allegations lack a clear and convincing factual showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, as required under California law (Motion to Strike, p. 2).

Specifically, Defendant seeks to strike the following portions from the FAC: paragraph 34, 36 (in part), 51 and 57 (in part), Prayer for relief in paragraph 3 under the fifth and sixth causes of action. 

The Court notes that Defendant’s motion to strike Paragraphs 34 and 36 of the FAC are moot and thus require no further consideration.

Paragraph 51

Paragraph 51 alleges that “the conduct of the Defendants alleged herein was carried on with a willful and conscious disregard to the health and safety of the Plaintiffs. By reason thereof, the Plaintiffs are entitled to punitive damages in an amount determined at trial.” Defendants argue that the FAC lacks specific factual allegations showing that Defendants acted with malice or conscious disregard, asserting that a mere failure to investigate tenant complaints does not meet the threshold for punitive damages as established in Lackner v. North (135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210 (2006)).

 

While Plaintiffs oppose the motion to strike and allege that Defendants disregarded numerous complaints of respiratory issues, mold, and water intrusion (FAC ¶¶ 9-14), the Court agrees that there is a lack of specific factual detail demonstrating that Defendants’ conduct was “so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome” as to be considered despicable (Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co., 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287 (1994)). The Court finds that, without more specific allegations indicating knowledge or intent to harm, Paragraph 51 does not substantiate punitive damages as defined under Civil Code section 3294.

Accordingly, Paragraph 51 is stricken.

 

Paragraph 57

Paragraph 57 alleges that “tenants in constructive eviction cases are entitled to damages, including punitive damages…” Defendants argue that this language improperly implies an entitlement to punitive damages without providing sufficient factual support for malice, oppression, or fraud.

The Court finds that this statement is a blanket assertion by Plaintiffs that tenants are entitled to punitive damages in constructive eviction cases. Plaintiffs imply an entitlement to punitive damages, but without specific factual allegations that meet the “despicable conduct” standard required to establish malice or oppression, Plaintiffs cannot sustain a claim for punitive damages.

Accordingly, Paragraph 57 is stricken.

 

Prayer for relief - paragraph 3 under fifth and sixth causes of action

The prayer for punitive and exemplary damages in Paragraph 3 of the fifth and sixth causes of action requests “punitive and exemplary damages according to proof at time of trial.” Defendants argue that these requests are unsupported by the factual allegations in the FAC, as the claims for punitive damages fail to meet the legal standard for despicable conduct or malice.

As discussed above, given the Court’s findings of insufficiency in the factual basis for malice or conscious disregard, the requests for punitive damages in the prayers for relief under the fifth and sixth causes of action similarly fail. 

Accordingly, the prayers for punitive and exemplary damages in paragraph 3 under the fifth and sixth causes of action are stricken. 

           

V.        CONCLUSION

The Court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer to the second and third causes of action of the FAC, with leave to amend.         

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion to strike paragraphs 51 and 57, as well as the prayer for relief under the fifth and sixth causes of action. The Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike paragraph 34 and 36.

Plaintiffs have 20 days to file amended pleading.

Defendants are to give notice.
