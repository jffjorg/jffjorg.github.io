DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SMCV01313    Hearing Date: January 17, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

January 17, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV01313

MOTIONS

Demurrer and Motion to Strike Portions of Second Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Pacific Source Insurance Agency and Antoinette Turner

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Glenn Vallecillos, M.D., Inc.

 

MOTIONS

 

This case stems from a dispute over the remodel of Plaintiff Glenn Vallecillos, M.D., Inc.’s (“Plaintiff”) surgical center.  Plaintiff’s Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleges sixteen causes of action: (1) breach of written contract; (2) unjust enrichment; (3) intentional misrepresentation; (4) negligent misrepresentation; (5) unfair business practices; (6) negligence; (7) declaratory relief; (8) disgorgement of money paid to unlicensed contractor; (9) recovery on contractors bond; (10) conversion; (11) violation of penal code § 496; (12) negligence – ins broker; (13) action on bond – ins broker; (14) intentional misrepresentation – ins broker; (15) negligent misrepresentation – ins broker; and (16) conspiracy to commit fraud.

 

As to moving Defendants Pacific Source Insurance Agency (“Pacific”) and Antoinette Turner (“Turner”), who is the Chief Executive Officer, Secretary, Chief Financial Officer, and Director of Pacific (collectively, “Defendants”), Plaintiff alleges only the second, twelfth, fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth causes of action.

 

Defendants demur to the fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth causes of action for intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, and conspiracy to commit fraud, respectively, for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.31, subdivision (e).  Defendants also move to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff opposes both the demurrer and motion and Defendants reply.

 

MEET AND CONFER REQUIREMENT

 

            Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a) requires that “Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” 

 

The statute further requires “As part of the meet and confer process, the demurring party shall identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and identify with legal support the basis of the deficiencies.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(1).)  “The party who filed the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer shall provide legal support for its position that the pleading is legally sufficient or, in the alternative, how the complaint, cross-complaint, or answer could be amended to cure any legal insufficiency.”  (Ibid.)

 

“The parties shall meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).)  “The demurring party shall file and serve with the demurrer a declaration stating either” the means by which the parties met and conferred, or that the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request.  (Id., subd. (a)(3).) 

 

Here, Defendants provided an attorney declaration indicating that Defendants’ counsel sent Plaintiff’s counsel an email on November 27, 2023 requesting a meet and confer, and that upon Plaintiff’s counsel’s request to put the arguments of the demurrer in writing, Defendants’  counsel sent the written arguments to Plaintiff’s counsel via email on December 8, 2023.  Plaintiff’s counsel responded on December 12, 2023, the same day Defendants filed the demurrer and motion to strike.  (Waldberg Decl. ¶¶ 2-6.)

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendants did not meet and confer in good faith, because Defendants filed the demurrer and motion to strike on the same day Defendants purportedly received Plaintiff’s written response, instead of further discussing the issues with Plaintiff.  Plaintiff also provided an attorney declaration, with copies of the written meet and confer correspondences.  (Glaubiger Decl. ¶¶ 2-5 and Exs. A-B.) The written correspondence demonstrates that the parties outlined their respective positions in detail prior to Defendants’ filing of the demurrer and motion. 

 

Defendants counter that Plaintiff refused to meet and confer telephonically.

 

Under the circumstances, the Court finds that further telephonic meet and confer efforts would not be fruitful. 

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Defendants request the Court to take judicial notice of the Court’s November 3, 2023 Minute Order on Defendants’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint.

 

Judicial notice may be taken of records of any court in this state.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d)(1).)  Because the November 3, 2023 Minute Order is part of the Court’s record for this case, the Court may take judicial notice of it.  (Ibid.)   However, “while courts are free to take judicial notice of the existence of each document in a court file, including the truth of results reached, they may not take judicial notice of the truth of hearsay statements in decisions and court files.  Courts may not take judicial notice of allegations in affidavits, declarations and probation reports in court records because such matters are reasonably subject to dispute and therefore require formal proof.”  (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 [cleaned up].) 

 

Accordingly, the Court takes judicial notice of the November 3, 2023 Minute Order, including the truth of results reached, but not the truth of any hearsay statements contained therein. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In testing the sufficiency of a cause of action, a court accepts “[a]s true all material facts properly pled and matters which may be judicially noticed but disregard contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  [A court also gives] the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (290 Division (EAT), LLC v. City & County of San Francisco (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 439, 450 [cleaned up]; Hacker v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 270, 280 [“in considering the merits of a demurrer, however, “the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

 

Further, in ruling on a demurrer, a court must “liberally construe” the allegations of the complaint “with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  

 

In summary, “[d]etermining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the rule is that if one consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated, or may be intermingled with a statement of other facts irrelevant to the cause of action shown, or although the plaintiff may demand relief to which he is not entitled under the facts alleged.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

A.    FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

                                                                    i.            Fourteenth Cause of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.” (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.)  

 

Defendants contend Plaintiff fails to plead the elements for a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation against them with the requisite particularity. Specifically, Defendants contend (1) the SAC only alleges representations Defendant Keyes made and fails to allege the moving Defendants made any representation to Plaintiff; (2) the SAC does not allege how Defendants knew at the time they issued the initial Certificate of Insurance (“COI”) that Keyes lacked workers’ compensation coverage; (3) Plaintiff fails to allege reliance and the SAC contradicts facts alleged in the FAC in contravention of the sham pleading doctrine; and (4) Plaintiff fails to allege damages caused by the alleged misrepresentation.  The Court disagrees. 

 

Misrepresentation, Knowledge, and Reliance

 

With respect to misrepresentations, knowledge, and reliance, the SAC alleges:

 

16.       Prior to agreeing to hire defendant Keyes, plaintiff provided defendant Keyes with a copy of the TIPG.  Defendant Keyes represented to plaintiff that he could and would comply with all of Welltower’s rules and regulations, e.g. provide proof of workers compensation (wc) insurance.  Defendant Keyes tendered the following representations:

 

1.      He was a properly licensed contractor.

 

2.      He had fully reviewed the remodel plans and specifications.

 

3.      He was fully capable of and would make all repairs set forth in the plans and specifications.

 

4.      He and his employees/workers were covered by policy of workers compensation insurance.

 

5.      He would add plaintiff as an additional insured on his workers compensation policy.

 

17.       Pursuant thereto, in order to ‘fake’ defendant Keyes’ compliance with the above pre-conditions and representations regarding workers compensation coverage, etc., defendants Keyes, Pacific and Turner entered into an agreement whereby defendants Pacific and Turner would create false insurance documentation stating defendant Keyes was covered by a policy of worker’s compensation insurance when, in reality, he had no such coverage.  Plaintiff is further informed and believes that, in return for falsifying such insurance documentation, defendant Keyes paid money to defendants Pacific and Turner.  Pursuant to this agreement, defendants Pacific and Turner “created” the below identified false Certificates of Insurance (“COI”).

 

18.       On June 17, 2021, defendants Turner and Pacific created a COI falsely representing that “Global Ocean and Land Construction” (defendant Keyes) carried worker’s compensation policy no. 9217479.  The Description of Operations section of the COI includes the exact language specified in Welltower’s TIPG:

 

“405 Bedford LP and Healthcare Property Managers of America, LLC and its affiliates, subsidiaries and corporations owned, controlled, or coming under its active management, are listed as additional insured for work performed at 9675 Brighton Way, Beverly Hills, CA 90210…”

 

Additionally, the certificate holder is exactly as specified in Welltower’s TIPG.”  Based on the language defendants Pacific and Turner chose to include in the COI, it is clear that defendants Pacific and Turner had a copy of Welltower’s TIPG prior to creating the COI.  A copy of this COI is attached hereto as Exhibit C.

 

19.       Plaintiff is informed and believes that defendants Turner and Pacific provided copies of the COI dated June 17, 2021 to Welltower.  Thereafter, Welltower informed Plaintiff that defendant Keyes had complied with Welltower’s rules and regulations and was approved to perform the plaintiff’s remodel, e.g. defendant Keyes provided the requisite proof of workers compensation insurance.  In reliance thereon, plaintiff agreed to hire defendant Keyes to perform the remodel.

 

20.       On or about June 25, 2021, plaintiff signed a construction contract with defendant Keyes calling for the remodel of the Subject Property.  Thereafter and continuing to believe defendant Keyes had truthfully qualified to perform the remodel, plaintiff agreed to, accepted and signed a series of change orders.  True and correct copies thereof are attached hereto as Exhibit D.

 

[…]

 

22.       On or about March 18, 2022, defendants Turner and Pacific created another COI again falsely representing that “Global Ocean and Land Construction” carried worker’s compensation policy no. 9217479.  The Description of Operations section of the COI includes the exact language specified in Welltower’s TIPG and identifies plaintiff:

 

“405 Bedford LP and Healthcare Property Managers of America, LLC and its affiliates, subsidiaries and corporations owned, controlled, or coming under its active management, are listed as additional insureds for work performed at 9675 Brighton Way, Beverly Hills, CA 90210, Project: Glenn Vallecillos MD Inc 9675 Brighton Way Beverly Hills, CA.”

 

Additionally, the certificate holder is also exactly as specified in Welltower’s TIPG.  Thereafter, Welltower informed Plaintiff that defendant Keyes had again complied with Welltower’s rules and regulations and was approved to continue performing the plaintiff’s remodel, e.g. defendant Keyes provided the requisite proof of workers compensation insurance.  In reliance thereon, plaintiff agreed to allow defendant Keyes to continue performing the plaintiff’s remodel.  A copy of the COI dated March 18, 2022 is attached hereto as Exhibit F.

 

23.       On or about April 1, 2022, defendants Turner and Pacific created another COI again falsely representing that “Global Ocean and Land Construction” carried worker’s compensation policy no. 9217479.  Additionally, the certificate holder is plaintiff.  A copy thereof is attached hereto as Exhibit G.

 

[…]

 

25.       During this period of time and under the mistaken belief that defendant Keyes carried workers compensation insurance and had qualified to perform the remodel per the TIPG, plaintiff paid defendant Keyes upwards of $258,000.  All of these monies were paid by plaintiff within the three years preceding the filing of this suit.  Proof of such payment is attached hereto as Exhibit H.

 

[…]

 

29.       On or about August 1, 2022, after plaintiff had paid defendant Keyes upwards of $250,000, defendants Turner and Pacific provided another COI to plaintiff.  This COI identified the insured as “Global Ocean and Land Construction” and identified plaintiff as an additional insured BUT omitted all mention of workers compensation insurance.  A copy thereof is attached hereto as Exhibit I.

 

30.       After filing the lawsuit, plaintiff obtained records from STATE FUND.  According to those records, STATE FUND policy no. 9217479 does exist.  However, this policy does not belong to defendant Keyes dba Global Ocean and Land Construction, nor is defendant Turner and/or defendant Pacific the broker of record.  In reality, the policy belongs to another company with a similar name, Global Ocean and Land Developers Instern. and the broker of record is Dickerson Insurance Services, 1918 Riverside Drive.  In effect, defendants Keyes, Turner and Pacific ‘hijacked’ an existing policy from a company with a similar name (clearly intending to use the name-similarities to mask the deception.)  Copies of the STATE FUND documents are attached hereto as Exhibit J.

 

31.       Plaintiff also learned that, according to CSLB records, defendant Keyes did not carry workers compensation insurance during the period of August 2003 through August 2022.  The period of construction was June 2021 through April 2022.  See attached Exhibit K.

 

(SAC ¶¶ 16-21, 22-23, 25, 29-31.)

 

            Therefore, the SAC adequately alleges Defendants made several misrepresentations on the COIs, which they knew to be false when made by virtue of the fact that the matching language suggests they had copies of the TIPG, outlining the worker’s compensation requirements, and Plaintiff relied upon these misrepresentations in determining Keyes was qualified to perform the remodel and ultimately hiring Keyes to do so.

 

            Sham Pleading

 

Plaintiff also contends that the SAC is a sham pleading because it contradicts the FAC in the following respects: (1) the FAC alleged Plaintiff reasonably relied on the April 1, 2022 COI that was issued nearly a year after Keyes was hired and months after Keyes allegedly abandoned the project in December 2021 (FAC ¶¶ 17, 93); (2) the SAC now alleges that non-party Welltower relied on a different certificate not initially included in the FAC; and (3) the SAC omits the prior allegations that Keyes abandoned the project in December 2021.

 

“Under the sham pleading doctrine, plaintiffs are precluded from amending complaints to omit harmful allegations, without explanation, from previous complaints to avoid attacks raised in demurrers or motions for summary judgment.”  (Deveny v. Entropin, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 408, 425 [emphasis added].)

 

Here, at the hearing on Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC, Plaintiff’s counsel explained that the December 2021 date was erroneous.  Therefore, the exclusion of the December 2021 date, which Plaintiff’s counsel has already explained was erroneous, does not make the SAC a sham pleading.  Moreover, the allegations of additional COIs serve to provide additional detail and further clarify Plaintiff’s allegations of fraud.  The inclusion of additional clarifying information does not inherently contradict a prior pleading that did not include those details.

 

Therefore, the SAC is not a sham pleading.

 

Causation and Damages

 

Defendants further argue that the damages Plaintiff alleges stem from construction defects, not from Defendants’ alleged misrepresentations.  The Court disagrees.

 

Plaintiff alleges that in reliance upon Defendant’s repeated knowing misrepresentations that Defendant Keyes complied with the requirements of the TIPG by, among other things, carrying worker’s compensation insurance, Defendant hired and paid Keyes $258,000, before Keyes ultimately abandoned the project, and the work Keyes did perform contained numerous construction defects, causing an additional $100,000 in remediation costs.  (See SAC ¶¶ 24-28.)

 

Therefore, the Court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to the fourteenth cause of action.

 

                                                                  ii.            Fifteenth Cause of Action – Negligent Misrepresentation

 

“The essential elements of a count for negligent misrepresentation are the same except that it does not require knowledge of falsity but instead requires a misrepresentation of fact by a person who has no reasonable grounds for believing it to be true.” (Chapman v. Skype Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 217, 230-231.) Like intentional misrepresentation, causes of action for negligent misrepresentation sound in fraud, and must also, therefore, be pleaded with particularity. (Ibid.)

 

Therefore, the same allegations supporting Plaintiff’s fourteenth cause of action for intentional misrepresentation also support Plaintiff’s fifteenth cause of action for negligent misrepresentation.

 

As such, the Court overrules Defendant’s demurrer to the fifteenth cause of action.

 

                                                                iii.            Sixteenth Cause of Action – Conspiracy to Commit Fraud

 

“The elements of an action for civil conspiracy are (1) formation and operation of the conspiracy and (2) damage resulting to plaintiff (3) from a wrongful act done in furtherance of the common design.” (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1136 (hereinafter Prakashpalan).) “There is no separate tort of civil conspiracy and no action for conspiracy to commit a tort unless the underlying tort is committed and damage results therefrom.” (Ibid.) “The significance of a conspiracy theory of liability is that each member may be held jointly liable as a tortfeasor, even though he or she may not have participated directly in the underlying tort.” (Ibid.)

 

Moreover, “[w]here fraud is alleged to be the object of the conspiracy, the claim must be pleaded with particularity.” (Prakashpalan, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at 1136.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action for conspiracy to commit fraud because (1) Plaintiffs fail to adequately allege the underlying fraud tort; (2) Plaintiff fails to allege a conspiracy with requisite specificity.

 

As discussed above, the Court finds that Plaintiff adequately alleged the underlying fraudulent and negligent misrepresentation torts.

 

With respect to conspiracy, the Court notes that many of Plaintiff’s conspiracy allegations are alleged on information and belief.  (See SAC ¶¶ 32-33; 126-128; 131.)  “[I]t is not sufficient to allege fraud or its elements upon information and belief unless the facts upon which the belief is founded are stated in the pleading.” (Dowling v. Spring Val. Water Co. (1917) 174 Cal.218, 221.)

 

However, as discussed above, Plaintiff has provided factual detail suggesting that Keyes provided Defendants with a copy of the TIPG, suggesting at least one meeting occurred prior to Defendants’ issuance of the COIs. 

 

Moreover, “[o]ne of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.” (Ibid.)

 

The details of meetings between and among the defendants in furtherance of a conspiracy would naturally lie more within their knowledge than Plaintiff’s.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff adequately alleges a conspiracy to commit fraud.

 

2.      MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)  Here, Chan moves to strike from the complaint, references to and claims for punitive damages.    

 

In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294.  (College Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.)  Per Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)   As set forth in the Civil Code,

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.  (2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.  (3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

(Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1)-(3), emphasis added.) 

 

Further, a plaintiff must assert facts with specificity to support a conclusion that a defendant acted with oppression, fraud or malice.  To wit, there is a heightened pleading requirement regarding a claim for punitive damages.  (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1041-1042.)  “When nondeliberate injury is charged, allegations that the defendant’s conduct was wrongful, willful, wanton, reckless or unlawful do not support a claim for exemplary damages; such allegations do not charge malice.  When a defendant must produce evidence in defense of an exemplary damage claim, fairness demands that he receive adequate notice of the kind of conduct charged against him.” (G. D. Searle & Co. v. Superior Court (1975) 49 Cal.App.3d 22, 29 [cleaned up].)  In Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. v. Snepp, the Court of Appeal noted that the plaintiffs’ assertions related to their claim for punitive damages were “insufficient to meet the specific pleading requirement.”  (Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. v. Snepp (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 598, 643 [plaintiffs alleged “the conduct of Defendants was intentional, and done willfully, maliciously, with ill will towards Plaintiffs, and with conscious disregard for Plaintiff's rights. Plaintiff's injuries were exacerbated by the malicious conduct of Defendants. Defendants' conduct justifies an award of exemplary and punitive damages”]; see also Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 [“The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.  Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud, or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim”].) 

           

Here, as discussed above, Plaintiff has adequately pleaded the fraud-based causes of action.  As such, Plaintiff’s allegations adequately support a claim for punitive damages. 

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Defendants’ Demurrers to the Fourteenth, Fifteenth, and Sixteenth Causes of Action in their entirety. 

 

The Court also denies Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages from the second amended complaint.    

 

Further, the Court orders Defendants to file and serve an Answer to the Second Amended Complaint on or before February 2, 2024.

 

Defendants shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file a proof of service regarding the same.

MOTION

Motion for a Protective Order

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Anita Pourshalimy

none

 

MOTION

 

Plaintiff Anita Pourshalimy (“Plaintiff”) seeks a protective order preventing a non-party to the action, Markel Insurance Company (“Markel”), which is a respondent in an arbitration proceeding pertaining to the same motor vehicle incident, “from conducting additional Defense Medical Examinations without leave of Court, as additional Defense Medical Examinations beyond the eight that have been conducted to date and/or a second Mental Defense Examination would be harassing and oppressive to Plaintiff.”  Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions. 

 

Markel filed an untimely opposition.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      Procedural Issues

 

The proof of service for the Motion indicates that Markel was served electronically by email.  However, Markel is a non-party to the action which has not yet appeared in or made any filing in this action.  Moreover, there is no consent to electronic service on record with the Court.  Therefore, service of the Motion on Markel was not proper.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b) provides, “All papers opposing a motion […] shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days, and all reply papers at least five court days before the hearing.”  The court has discretion whether to consider late-filed papers.  (California Rules of Court, rule 3.1300(d).)  

 

Because the Motion was not properly served on Markel, the Court exercises its discretion and considers Markel’s untimely opposition.

 

2.      Substance of Motion

 

The California Arbitration Act grants arbitrators authority of discovery in arbitration proceedings.  (McConnell v. Advantest America, Inc. (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 596, 608.) 

 

Here, the underlying dispute between Plaintiff and Markel is currently in arbitration proceedings, and the Court notes that the arbitration was not ordered as part of the underlying action.  Moreover, Markel is not a party to this action.  As such, Plaintiff’s request, which seeks a protective order from discovery arising from the arbitration proceeding, is not justiciable before this Court and should be brought before the arbitrator.[1]

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Accordingly, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for a protective order.
