Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Jaime Espindola v. Demarco Smith, et al.

            23TRCV00882

1.      USAA Casualty Insurance Company’s Motion for Leave to File Complaint-in-Intervention

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            USAA Casualty Insurance Company’s (“USAA”) Motion for Leave to File Complaint-in-Intervention is granted.

 

            Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on March 24, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff was involved in a motor vehicle accident with Defendant.

 

Motion for Leave to File Complaint in Intervention

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 387(b) states: “An intervention takes place when a nonparty, deemed an intervenor, becomes a party to an action or proceeding between other persons by doing any of the following: (1) Joining a plaintiff in claiming what is sought by the complaint. (2) Uniting with a defendant in resisting the claims of a plaintiff. (3) Demanding anything adverse to both a plaintiff and a defendant.”

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 387(d)(1) states: “The court shall, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if either of the following conditions is satisfied: (A) A provision of law confers an unconditional right to intervene.  (B) The person seeking intervention claims an interest relating to the property or transaction that is the subject of the action and that person is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede that person's ability to protect that interest, unless that person’s interest is adequately represented by one or more of the existing parties.”

 

Code of Civ. Proc., 387(d)(2) states: “The court may, upon timely application, permit a nonparty to intervene in the action or proceeding if the person has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of either of the parties, or an interest against both.”

 

“When leave [to intervene] is granted, the intervenor becomes a party with the same rights and remedies of the original parties.”  4 Witkin, Cal. Proc., Sec. 226 (5th Ed. 2012) (citing People v. Perris Irr. Dist. (1901) 132 Cal. 289, 290).  “A party seeking intervention must ordinarily show an interest under the existing pleadings and issues, and will not be allowed to come in on a claim that enlarges the issues and changes the nature of the main proceedings.  4 Witkin, Cal. Proc., Sec. 227 (5th Ed. 2012) (citing Muller v. Robinson (1959) 174 Cal.App.2d 511, 515.)

 

USAA moves for an order permitting it to file a Complaint-in-Intervention pursuant to CCP § 387 to defend this action and assert defenses on behalf of its insured Defendant Demarco Smith. Moving party states that its insured Demarco Smith cannot be located by USAA, at this time.

 

USAA has established that the motion is timely. The motion was filed only a few months after service upon Smith of the summons and complaint. There is no trial date yet and the case is not at issue. The motion is considered timely if brought within a reasonable time. See, Sanders v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (1975) 53 Cal. App.3d 661, 668-69.  Here, no party has challenged the motion based on untimeliness.

 

USAA has established that it has an interest in this litigation that warrants intervention in this proceeding. USAA is the insurer of Defendant and Defendant has yet to be located.  Therefore, USAA has established that it has an interest relating to the subject matter of the action and that it is so situated that the disposition of the action may impair or impede its ability to protect that interest, and/or that it has an interest in the matter in litigation, or in the success of the parties.  In addition, there is no showing that granting leave to intervene will enlarge the issues or change the nature of the proceedings.

 

Thus, USAA’s Motion for Leave to File Complaint-in-Intervention is granted.

 

USAA is ordered to file and serve the proposed Complaint-in-Intervention within five days of this date.

 

USAA is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Epps & Coulson, LLP v. Stuart Lee Zubrick, et al.    

23TRCV01317

1.      Epps & Coulson, LLP’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings    

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING


Epps & Coulson, LLP’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is denied.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on April 26, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Defendant owes Plaintiff for legal services rendered. On July 13, 2023, Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint. Cross-Complainant alleged the following causes of action based on Cross-Defendant’s representation of Cross-Complainant in the prior legal action: 1. Fraud; 2. Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 3. Breach of Contract; 4. Slander of Title; 5. Cancellation/Expungement of Lien and Quiet Title/Injunctive Relief.

 

Meet and Confer

 

Cross-Defendant filed a meet and confer declaration in sufficient compliance with CCP § 439. (Decl., Harold C. Trimmer, ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Cross-Defendant’s request for judicial notice is granted pursuant to Evidence Code § 452(d).


Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer but is made after the time for demurrer has expired.  Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).  Except as provided by statute, the rules governing demurrers apply.  Civic Partners Stockton, LLC v. Youssefi (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1005, 1012.  “Judgment on the pleadings is proper when the complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against the defendant.”  Rolfe v. Cal. Transp. Comm’n (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 239, 242; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(3)(B)(ii).  “Like a demurrer, the grounds for the motion [for judgment on the pleadings] must appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.”  Civic Partners Stockton, LLC, supra, 218 Cal.App.4th at p. 1013.  In ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “[a]ll allegations in the complaint and matters upon which judicial notice may be taken are assumed to be true.”  Rippon v. Bowen (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1313.

 

Cross-Defendant moves for judgment on the pleadings as to Cross-Complaint. “This motion is made on the grounds that the Cross-Complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action for fraud, breach of contract, and breach of fiduciary duty because those causes of action are barred by the statute of limitations. The Cross-Complaint also does not state a cause of action for slander of title, because it is barred by the litigation privilege.” (Notice of Motion, page 2, lines 6-9.)

 

First Cause of Action for Fraud

Second Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Third Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

           

            Cross-Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first through third causes of action is denied. Cross-Complainant states sufficient facts to state a cause of action.

 

            The motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first through third causes of action is based solely on the belief that the allegations of the first through third causes of action reveal a bar pursuant to the applicable statute of limitations which Cross-Defendant contends is CCP § 340.6(a).

 

            CCP § 340.6(a) states, in relevant part: “An action against an attorney for a wrongful act or omission, other than for actual fraud, arising in the performance of professional services shall be commenced within one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the facts constituting the wrongful act or omission, or four years from the date of the wrongful act or omission, whichever occurs first.”

 

            CCP § 340.6 has also been applied to tort-based claims such as breach of fiduciary duty and negligent misrepresentation, as well as contract-based claims that arise from the provision of professional legal services. Quintilliani v. Mannerino (1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 54, 69. “The principal purpose or “gravamen” of the action, rather than the form of action or the relief demanded, determines the applicable statute of limitations. The court should take into account matters such as context, the object in view, the evils to be remedied, the history of the times and of legislation upon the same subject, public policy, and contemporaneous construction.” Vafi v. McCloskey (2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 874, 880 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

            Initially, as to the first cause of action, by its very terms, CCP § 340.6 does not apply to “actual fraud.”  Thus, the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action can be quickly denied outright.

 

            Cross-Defendant attempts to argue that the allegations do not support a cause of action for “actual fraud,” but instead simply restates and repurposes the allegations of breach of contract and breach of fiduciary duty (or professional negligence). Cross-Defendant is incorrect. The allegations specifically are stated in the form of actual fraud – more specifically fraud in the inducement. Cross-Complainant contends that material misrepresentations were made that induced Cross-Complainant into entering into the legal services agreement with Cross-Defendant. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 20-27.) The allegations are not simply a clever way of restating the professional negligence and breach of contract claims but instead a separate distinct theory of fraud in the inducement.

 

            As to the second and third causes of action to which CCP § 340.6 would apply, there are no allegations on the face of the Cross-Complaint which would reveal a bar based on the statute of limitations. Cross-Defendant argues that “he admits that a malpractice claim, if asserted, would be barred. (See Cross-Complaint at ¶ 31.)” (Memorandum of Points and Authorities, page 7, lines 2-3.)  Paragraph 31 of the Cross-Complaint states as follows: “Cross-Complainant is informed and believes and thereon alleges that then Coulson kept working until a convenient time to bail out, then asserting liens against the client’s case, and often the client’s assets, waiting a year or more until after terminating representation to attempt to avoid the inevitable malpractice claim (by clients who could afford to pursue it), then suing the clients for outrageous, questionable, unreasonable and unfair fees for work which was either partial, incomplete, unnecessary, or combinations of the three.”  However, a review of the preceding paragraphs reveal that this allegation is not specific to the case at hand but instead a sweeping statement concerning Cross-Defendant’s pattern with unnamed “clients.” The allegation is not nearly specific enough to demonstrate that this Cross-Complainant discovered or reasonably should have discovered wrongdoing within one year. In fact, even assuming arguendo that the allegation was specifically directed to this case, the allegation is simply too vague to show that this specific Cross-Complainant reasonably should have discovered facts of wrongdoing within one year.

 

            Later, Cross-Defendant argues that “the cross-complaint admits that Zubrick had notice of any potential claim. (Cross-Complaint at ¶ 33.)” (Memorandum of Points and Authorities, page 9, lines 13-14.)  Paragraph 33 of the Cross-Complaint states as follows: “Cross-Complainant is informed and believes and thereon alleges that Cross-Defendants have obtained millions of dollars in fraudulent judgments and settlements with clients in the past eleven years, many of which were default judgments, against unsuspecting clients who simply could not afford the costs of legal defense.” Clearly, nothing within this allegation reveals a bar based on the one-year (discovery portion) statute of limitations of CCP § 340.6. In addition, a review of all the allegations of the Cross-Complaint and not merely the two paragraphs specifically identified by Cross-Defendant does not reveal such a bar.

 

            Instead, the only specific allegations that appear to touch upon the statute of limitations states as follows: “What Zubrick did not know, however, was that on or about April 30, 2021, Cross-Defendants falsely, fraudulently, and without legal justification, had also caused to be filed and publicly recorded a “Notice of Attorney’s Lien,” on the Subject Properties, true and correct copies of which are attached hereto as Exhibits C and D. As no notice was given by Coulson, this was not discovered until a year later. In November 2, 2022, demand was made on Coulson to explain the basis for the lien.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 46-48.)  The Complaint was filed in April 2023 and the Cross-Complaint in July 2023, both within one year of that date.

 

            The motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first through third causes of action is denied.

 

            Fourth Cause of Action for Slander of Title

 

            Cross-Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the fourth cause of action is denied. Cross-Complainant states facts sufficient to state a cause of action.

 

            The motion is based solely on the grounds that the fourth cause of action is barred by the litigation privilege.

 

            Civ. Code § 47(b) provides an absolute privilege for a publication made in a legislative, judicial, or official proceeding.  See Dove Audio, Inc. v. Rosenfeld, Meyer & Susman (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 777, 780.  “The usual formulation is that the privilege applies to any communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that have some connection or logical relation to the action. The principal purpose of [the litigation privilege] is to afford litigants and witnesses [citation] the utmost freedom of access to the courts without fear of being harassed subsequently by derivative tort actions. Or, as otherwise stated, it exists to protect citizens from the threat of litigation for communications to government agencies whose function it is to investigate and remedy wrongdoing. To achieve this end, the absolute privilege is interpreted broadly to apply to any communication, not just a publication, having ‘some relation’ to a judicial [or quasi-judicial] proceeding,” irrespective of the communication's maliciousness or untruthfulness. And judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings are defined broadly to include all kinds of truth-seeking proceedings, including administrative, legislative and other official proceedings. Further, the privilege is not limited to statements made during a trial or other proceedings, but may extend to steps taken prior thereto, or afterwards.” People ex rel. Gallegos v. Pacific Lumber Co. (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 950, 958 (internal citations and quotations omitted; emphasis in original).

 

            However, an exception to the general rule of the application of the litigation privilege exists as to a former client filing an action against his former attorney. “The litigation privilege, if applicable, would preclude essentially any action by a former client against an attorney for breach of professional duties arising from communicative conduct in litigation on behalf of that client. We believe that to allow litigation attorneys to breach their professional duties owed to their own clients with impunity from civil liability would undermine the attorney-client relationship and would not further the policies of affording free access to the courts and encouraging open channels of communication and zealous advocacy. We therefore hold that the litigation privilege is inapplicable in an action by a former client against an attorney for breach of professional duties[.]” Fremont Reorganizing Corp. v. Faigin (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1153, 1173–74.

 

            Cross-Defendant attempts to limit and distinguish this holding by arguing that the fourth cause of action is not based on a breach of professional duty. However, the fourth cause of action for Slander of Title is specifically based upon recordation of liens in March 2021 upon the subject property in the prior underlying lawsuit. The fourth cause of action directly relates to the series of events and actions that implicate Cross-Defendant’s alleged breach of professional duty which were previously outlined in depth in the first through third causes of action. The policy reason for the holding of Fremont would be undermined if the exception is not applied in this instance simply because the cause of action was titled “Slander of Title.” The alleged slander of title occurred during the course of Cross-Defendant’s alleged conduct constituting breach of professional duties.

 

            The motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the fourth cause of action is denied.

 

Thus, Cross-Defendant’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is denied.

 

Cross-Complainant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

            Martha Sofia Ayala Barrientos v. Mercury Air Cargo, LLC, et al.

            23TRCV01367

1.      Mercury Air Cargo, LLC, et al.’s Demurrer to Complaint  

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Mercury Air Cargo, LLC and Worldwide Flight Services, Inc.’s Demurrer to Complaint is overruled.

 

           

            Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on April 28, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff was injured by a pallet being carried by a forklift operated by Defendants on property owned or controlled by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action. 1. Negligence; 2. Negligent Hiring, Retention, and Supervision; 3. Premises Liability.  

 

Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.)  In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  The Court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law.  (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.App.4th 634, 638.) Because a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint, the plaintiff must show that the complaint alleges facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action.  (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.)  Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer.  (C.C.P., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.App.4th 1112, 1126.)

 

Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.)  "Whether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer."  (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)  Under Code Civil Procedure § 430.10(f), a demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.”  Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet.  (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

Defendants set forth a meet and confer declaration in sufficient compliance with CCP § 430.41. (Decl., Valeria Granata.)

 

Demurrer

 

Defendants demur to the third cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action and uncertainty.

 

Third Cause of Action for Premises Liability

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is overruled. Plaintiff states sufficient facts to state a cause of action and the cause of action is not uncertain.

 

“To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care, (2) the defendant breached that duty, and (3) the breach proximately caused the plaintiff's damages or injuries.” Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 62. “The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence. Accordingly, the plaintiff must prove, legal duty to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 1200, 1207 (internal citations and quotations omitted).

 

First, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to distinguish the purported conduct of each Defendant and instead simply group them together. However, in paragraph 30 of the Complaint, Plaintiff has alleged that both Defendants owned, possessed, and operated the subject property. Plaintiff is only required to allege general and not specific facts to state a cause of action for premises liability. The allegations are sufficiently stated in a manner to allow Defendants the opportunity to respond.

 

“Demurrers for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) (sic) are disfavored. A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures. A demurrer for uncertainty should be overruled when the facts as to which the complaint is uncertain are presumptively within the defendant's knowledge.” Chen v. Berenjian (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822 (internal citations and quotations omitted

 

Second, Defendants argue that the cause of action is duplicative of the first cause of action for Negligence because the same factual allegations of the first and third causes of action are utilized to state the elements of each of the causes of action - purported duty of care, breach of duty, and causation of damages. Plaintiff citing to Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132 argues that “premises liability is subject to specific defenses that do not necessarily apply to negligence. The most notable example is ownership and control of the subject property.” (Opposition, page 5, lines 11-13.)

 

Importantly, the Court notes that Plaintiff did not incorporate the factual allegations of paragraph 5 regarding ownership, management, and control of the subject property into the first cause of action for Negligence. Again, as noted above, while the elements of the causes of action are the same for both causes of action, by not incorporating those factual allegations into the first cause of action, Plaintiff has made clear that she is pursuing the first cause of action merely on the contention that Defendants breached a general duty of care of the ordinary prudent person to not operate a forklift to harm Plaintiff rather than a duty of care based on ownership or control of the property itself. As to the third cause of action, Plaintiff is pursuing a theory of negligence based on premises liability based on ownership, possession, and control of the subject property. (Complaint, ¶ 30.)

 

Thus, the demurrer to the third cause of action is overruled.  

 

Defendants are ordered to file and serve an Answer within ten days of this date.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
