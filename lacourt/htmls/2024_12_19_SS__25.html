Case Number: 22STLC06416    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: 25

PROCEEDINGS:     MOTION TO SET ASIDE DISMISSAL

 

MOVING PARTY:   Plaintiffs Manual and Berta Najera

RESP. PARTY:         None

MOTION TO SET ASIDE DISMISSAL

(CCP § 473(b))

 TENTATIVE RULING:

 Plaintiffs Manual and Berta Najera’s unopposed Motion to Set Aside the Dismissal (Without Prejudice) and Reinstate the Complaint is GRANTED. The dismissal entered on March 28, 2024 is hereby VACATED.

 ORDER TO SHOW CAUSE regarding failure to file proof of service is set for February 26, 2025 at 9:30 a.m., in Department 25 of the Spring Street Courthouse.

 Trial is scheduled for June 3, 2025 at 8:30 a.m., in Department 25 at Spring Street Courthouse.  Parties are ordered to comply with Third Amended Standing Order in Civil Limited Jurisdiction Cases, effective as of February 24, 2020.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On September 29, 2022, Plaintiffs Manual Najera and Berta Najera (“Plaintiffs”) initiated this action against Defendant Roosevelt Henderson, Jr. (“Defendant”) and Does 1-20, alleging causes of action for Motor Vehicle and General Negligence. (Compl., pp. 1, 3, ¶ 10.)

 

On March 28, 2024, the Court dismissed the action without prejudice pursuant to the provisions of Code of Civil Procedure section 581, subdivision (b)(3) following the parties’ failure to appear at trial. (3/28/24 Order of Dismissal; 3/28/24 Minute Order.)

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant Notice of Motion and Motion to Set Aside Dismissal (Without Prejudice) and Reinstate the Complaint Pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 473 (the “Motion”).

 

No Opposition was filed.

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

The Court notes Plaintiff failed to attach a copy of the Complaint to the Motion.

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief. (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

The second provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is mandatory, providing: “[T]he court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney’s affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties. However, this section shall not lengthen the time within which an action shall be brought to trial pursuant to Section 583.310.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

The general underlying purpose of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) is to promote the determination of actions on their merits. (Even Zohar Const. & Remodeling, Inc. v. Bellaire Townhouses, LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 830, 838-839.)  

 

IV.              Discussion

 

A.    Timeliness

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes the Motion was filed on September 30, 2024, which is six months after the dismissal without prejudice was ordered on March 28, 2024.

 

 Here, Plaintiffs raise a claim for relief due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect by an attorney, which is a form of mandatory relief that requires application for relief to be filed no later than six months from the date of dismissal. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

Since Plaintiffs filed the Motion at exactly six months from the dismissal, the Court finds the Motion to be timely.

 

B.     Motion to Set Aside Dismissal

 

            Plaintiffs move to set aside the dismissal on the grounds that Plaintiffs’ counsel, Maryetta C. Marks, (“Counsel”) failed to appear in court on March 28, 2024 due to Counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, error and/or excusable neglect. (Motion, p. 3.) In addition to moving for relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), Plaintiffs also move pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128, subdivision (a)(5) to have the “dismissal set aside, complaint reinstated, all pre-trial dates reset including and not limited to the trial date.” (Motion, Marks Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

Here, the Court finds there is sufficient evidence to warrant setting aside the dismissal against Plaintiffs. Counsel’s declaration established that the March 28, 2024 dismissal was entered due to Counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, error, and neglect in failing to appear in court on March 28, 2024. (Motion, Marks Decl.) While Counsel broadly declares that Counsel failed to appear in court on March 28, 2024 due to Counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise and/or excusable neglect without providing further detail, Counsel also states Plaintiffs are “innocent of any wrongdoing, fault, mistakes and or errors regarding the instant case. In fact, [Plaintiffs] have always and at all times diligently and consistently inquired about the status of their cases since the inception of their cases. [Plaintiffs] continue to be ready, willing, and able to go to trial in this matter.” (Marks Decl., ¶ 5.) Further, delay in seeking relief and inexcusable neglect are not valid grounds for denying a motion for mandatory relief (see Metropolitan Service Corp. v. Casa de Palms, Ltd. (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1481, 1487-1488), and the instant dismissal was not a mandatory dismissal. Therefore, pursuant to the mandatory provision of Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), the Court finds Plaintiffs have established a right to relief.

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ unopposed Motion is GRANTED.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

Plaintiffs Manual and Berta Najera’s unopposed Motion to Set Aside the Dismissal (Without Prejudice) and Reinstate the Complaint is GRANTED. The dismissal entered on March 28, 2024 is hereby VACATED.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff Western Funding Incorporated (“Plaintiff”) initiated this action against Defendant Herschel Edwards (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10, alleging causes of action for Breach of Contract and Common Counts. (Compl., pp. 1, ¶ 8.)

 

On August 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed proof of personal service of the Summons and Complaint and accompanying papers on Defendant on August 13, 2024. (8/15/24 Proof of Service, ¶¶ 2, 5.)

 

On September 18, 2024, default was entered against Defendant. (9/18/24 Request for Entry of Default.) The same day, Plaintiff also dismissed Does 1-10 without prejudice.

 

On September 23, 2024, Defendant filed an Answer.

 

On September 30, 2024, default judgment was entered against Defendant. (9/30/24 Request for Entry of Default Judgment.)

 

On October 14, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Notice of Motion and Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment (the “Motion”).

 

No Opposition was filed.

 

II.                 Procedural Issues

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes Defendant failed to file proof of service of the Motion on Plaintiff. The Court cannot act on the Motion until properly served.

 

Next, the Court notes Defendant failed to attach a reservation receipt for the Motion hearing.

 

Third, the Court notes Defendant states there are four exhibits attached to the Motion – namely, “(1) Notice of rejection of First Answer, (2) Acceptance by Clerk of Corrected Answer, (3) Proof of Service of Answer within 30 days of service of Complaint, [and] (4) Proof of Service documentation Defendant Received when Served.” (Edwards Decl., ¶ 4.) However, no such documents are attached – only the cover sheets identifying the exhibits are included with the Motion.

 

Finally, the Court notes Defendant failed to attach a copy of the proposed Answer or other responsive pleading to the Motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

III.              Legal Standard

 

The court has broad discretion to vacate the entry of default, default judgment, or a dismissal where the moving party timely establishes a proper ground for relief. (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) contains two distinct provisions for relief from default.  The first provision is discretionary and broad in scope, providing: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”  (Code Civ. IV.              Discussion

 

A.    Timeliness

 

As an initial matter, the Court notes the Motion was filed on October 14, 2024, which is less than six months after the entry of default entered on September 18, 2024.

 

 Here, Defendant raises a claim for relief due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect by a party, which is a form of discretionary relief that requires application for relief to be filed within a reasonable time and no later than six months from the time of entry of default. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) 

 

Since Defendant filed the Motion within six months from the entry of default, the Motion is timely.

 

B.     Motion to Set Aside Default Judgment and Default

 

            Defendant moves to set aside the default and default judgment on the grounds that Defendant was proceeding pro se and attempted to file an Answer on September 12, 2024 – within 30 days of receiving service of the Complaint on August 13, 2024 – but the Answer was rejected by the Court due to incomplete information. (Motion, pp. 1-2, 5; Edwards Decl.) Accordingly, Defendant contends the failure to timely respond to the Complaint was due to Defendant’s excusable inadvertence and mistake.

 

Here, the Court finds there is sufficient evidence to warrant setting aside the default judgment and default against Defendant. While the Court notes its record does not indicate Defendant attempted to file an Answer on September 12, 2024, Defendant submitted evidence of his own declaration, which states that this is his first breach of contract lawsuit; Defendant repeatedly attempted to file an Answer in this matter; Defendant struggled to navigate the procedural requirements for filing an Answer and scheduling a hearing for this Motion; Defendant eventually hired a lawyer to file an Answer; and Defendant intends to participate in this action by denying the Complaint, making discovery requests, and raising defenses to the causes of action alleged in the Complaint. (Edwards Decl., ¶¶ 1-3.) The Court also notes an Answer was filed by Defendant on September 23, 2024.

 

The Court finds there is sufficient evidence to suggest Defendant’s lack of timely response to the Complaint was due to Defendant’s inadvertence and excusable mistakes in navigating the legal system given Defendant’s unfamiliarity with the litigation process. (See Hopkins & Carley v. Gens (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1410 [defendant’s burden to demonstrate “that due to some mistake, either of fact or of law, of himself or of his counsel, or through some inadvertence, surprise or neglect which may properly considered excusable, the judgment or order from which he seeks relief should be reverse”] (emphasis in original).) 

 

However, Defendant failed to file proof of service and attach the requisite Answer or responsive pleading to the Motion (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b)). The Court cannot act on the Motion until properly served.

 

            Thus, the Motion is CONTINUED.
