DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV11917A    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 30, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV11917A

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Depositions

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiffs Krystal Tivis

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendants Khatchaturian & Zargarian DDS, Inc., Sanderson & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., Zaragarian & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., and Smile Dental Group

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Krystal Tivis (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel Defendants Khatchaturian & Zargarian DDS, Inc., Sanderson & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., Zaragarian & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., and Smile Dental Group (“Defendants”) to produce persons most knowledgeable to appear for deposition and to produce documents identified in the deposition notices.  Defendants oppose the motion.

ANALYSIS

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a), “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action  . . . without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document  . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent ‘s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.” 

According to Plaintiffs, their efforts to take the subject depositions began in October 2021.  Defendants are parties to this action.  As such, Defendants must produce their persons most knowledgeable to testify at deposition and produce documents set forth in the deposition notice.  With respect to the depositions that have been taken to date, there appear to be no indication those individuals were offered as persons most knowledgeable for any of the entity Defendants in this case and Defendants have not stated otherwise.  However, as Defendants point out in opposition to the motion, Plaintiff did not notice the deposition of Defendant Zaragarian & Khatchaturian Dental Corp.  The motion is, therefore, premature with respect to that Defendant, but it is granted as to Defendants Khatchaturian & Zargarian DDS, Inc., Sanderson & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., and Smile Dental Group.

In opposition, Defendants argue Plaintiff’s deposition notices are overbroad and seek material that is not discoverable. If Defendants sought to avoid producing persons most qualified or documents pertaining to specified topics, Defendants were required to seek a protective order so “[t]hat certain matters [would] not be inquired into.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b)(9).)  Defendants did not do so. The scope of the requested information is not an “error or irregularity” in the deposition notice to which Defendants could object. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subd. (a).)

Defendants also argue Plaintiff failed to adequately attempt to informally resolve this matter.  Plaintiff was not required to do so, as Defendants indicated they did not intend to appear for the noticed depositions, and they did not.  Instead, Plaintiff was required to “contact[] [Defendants] to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)  Plaintiff did so.  (Declaration of Susan R. Huerta in Support of Motion to Compel Defendants’ Person Most Knowledgeable Depositions, Exhibits 27-35.)

Plaintiff seeks sanctions.  In this regard, the e-mail exchange between the parties is revealing in that it appears Defendant simply stood on their objections to the depositions and did not meaningfully meet and confer regarding those objections so that they could take place.  Under these circumstances, the Court finds sanctions are warranted, given Defendants abuse of the discovery process.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030; Huerta Decl., Exhibits 27-35.)  The Court imposes sanctions against Defendants Khatchaturian & Zargarian DDS, Inc., Sanderson & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., and Smile Dental Group and their counsel of record, Ballard Rosenburg Golper & Savitt, LLP, in the amount of $2,660, which represents four hours of attorney time to prepare the motion and attend the hearing at $650 per hour, plus the filing fee. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendants Khatchaturian & Zargarian DDS, Inc., Sanderson & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., and Smile Dental Group to designate and produce their persons most qualified to testify on specified topics and to produce documents is granted.  Those Defendants are to produce their persons most qualified for deposition within 30 days of notice of this order, unless Plaintiff stipulates otherwise. The scope of the depositions is as set forth in Exhibit 7 attached to Huerta’s Declaration. But Defendants are not obligated to produce documents in response to those requests that are overly broad, duplicative, and/or not appropriately limited in time, such as Request Numbers 4, 8, 11, 15, 17, 18, 19, 20, and 22. 

Defendants Khatchaturian & Zargarian DDS, Inc., Sanderson & Khatchaturian Dental Corp., and Smile Dental Group and their counsel of record, Ballard Rosenburg Golper & Savitt, LLP, jointly and severally, are to pay $2,660 in sanctions to Plaintiff, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 21STCV11917B    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

September 30, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV11917B

MOTION: 

Motions to Compel Depositions

MOVING PARTY:

Plaintiff Krystal Tivis

OPPOSING PARTY:

Defendants Chris Khatchaturian, Albert Zargarian, and Vanessa Sanderson

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Krystal Tivis (“Plaintiff”) moves to compel Defendants Chris Khatchaturian, Albert Zargarian, and Vanessa Sanderson (“Defendants”) and Deponents Anthony Adams and Jasmine Triche (“Deponents”) to appear for deposition and to produce documents identified in the deposition notices.  Defendants oppose the motion.

ANALYSIS

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a), “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action  . . . without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document  . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent ‘s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.” 

Defendants are parties to this action.  As such, Defendants must testify at deposition and produce documents set forth in the deposition notices.  Deponents are employees of parties to this action.  As such, they must appear for deposition as well.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280, subd. (a) [service of deposition notice upon employee of party in action sufficient for employee’s attendance at deposition].)  The motion is granted.

In opposition, Defendants argue Plaintiff’s deposition notices are overbroad and seek material that is not discoverable. If Defendants sought to avoid producing these individuals for deposition or documents on specified topics, Defendants were required to seek a protective order so “[t]hat certain matters [would] not be inquired into.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b)(9).)  Defendants did not do so. The scope of the requested information is not an “error or irregularity” in the deposition notice, to which Defendants could object. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410.)

Defendants also argue Plaintiff failed to adequately attempt to informally resolve this matter.  Plaintiff was not required to do so, as Defendants did not appear for deposition. Instead, Plaintiff was required to “contact[] [Defendants] to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)  Plaintiff did so.  (Declaration of Susan R. Huerta in Support of Motion to Compel Depositions, Exhibit 34.)

Plaintiff seeks sanctions in connection with this motion.  However, in her reply brief, Plaintiff acknowledges, of the individuals noted above, only Albert Zargarian’s deposition remains to be scheduled.  Under the circumstances, the Court will not award sanctions. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant and Deponent Albert Zargarian to testify at deposition and to produce documents is granted.  Deponent is to appear for deposition within 30 days of notice of this order, unless Plaintiff stipulates otherwise. The scope of the deposition is as set forth in Exhibit 21 of the Huerta Declaration.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV03786    Hearing Date: September 30, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

September 30, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV03786

MOTIONS

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant United Convalescent Facilities, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiffs Edmund Negrete and Fernando Negrete

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Edmund Negrete and Fernando Negrete (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendant United Convalescent Facilities, Inc. (“Defendant”) based on injuries Decedent Adan Abram Negrete (“Decedent”) sustained while in Defendant’s care.  Defendant demurs to the second and third causes of action in Plaintiff’s operative first amended complaint.  Plaintiffs oppose the demurrer.

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322(b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Second Cause of Action:  Dependent Adult Abuse

The Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act prohibits “[a]buse of an elder or a dependent adult[,]” which is defined as “physical abuse, neglect, financial abuse, abandonment, isolation, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering[.]”  (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15610.07.)  Because it is a statutory claim, Plaintiffs must allege the cause of action for elder abuse with particularity. (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 790.) 

Plaintiffs allege Decedent was fully dependent on Defendant’s employees for assistance with all activities of daily living (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 63); because of Defendant’s lack of proper care for Decedent, Decedent developed multiple pressure ulcers (id., ¶¶ 83-88); and .  Defendant’s treatment of Decedent resulted in Decedent being physically harmed (id., ¶ __).   For pleading purposes, these allegations suffice, and Plaintiffs have adequately alleged a claim for dependent abuse. 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs fail to allege intentional abuse of Decedent.  Defendant is incorrect.  Plaintiffs allege Defendant knew of Decedent’s medical conditions, which placed him at an elevated risk of developing pressure ulcers and, nonetheless, Defendants failed to take adequate care of Decedent to avoid pressure ulcers.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 84-85.)  These allegations raise the inference Defendant intentionally mistreated Decedent.

Defendant also contend Plaintiffs have not alleged facts to state a claim for the heightened remedies for elder abuse under Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657.  Defendant cannot demur to a claim for remedies.  (See Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1150, 1167, disapproved of on other grounds Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 948, fn. 12 [    ].) 

Third Cause of Action: Willful Misconduct

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ third cause of action for willful misconduct.  Although it has been stated willful misconduct is not a separate cause of action, but rather a type of claim for negligence (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 526 (Berkley)), the California Supreme Court has held “it is generally recognized that willful or wanton misconduct is separate and distinct from negligence, involving different principles of liability and different defenses.” (Calvillo-Silva v. Home Grocery (1998) 19 Cal.4th 714, 729.)  Thus, there is a heightened pleading standard for willful misconduct above that of simple negligence in that “willful misconduct is not marked by a mere absence of care. Rather, it involves a more positive intent actually to harm another or to do an act with a positive, active and absolute disregard of its consequences.” (Ibid., internal quotes and citations omitted.)

Therefore, to establish willful misconduct, Plaintiff must prove the basic elements of a negligence cause of action (duty, breach of duty, causation, and damage) and “actual or constructive knowledge of the peril to be apprehended, actual or constructive knowledge injury is a probable, as opposed to a possible, result of the danger, and conscious failure to act to avoid the peril.”  (Berkley, supra, 152 Cal.App.4th at p. 528.)  In the instant case, Plaintiff does not satisfy the heightened pleading standard in that Plaintiff does not allege, for example,  Defendant’s positive intent to actually harm Decedent and Defendant committed acts with a positive, active, and absolute disregard of the consequences.  Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer to the third cause of action with leave to amend.

Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike the punitive damages allegations.  In ruling on a motion to strike punitive damages, “judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.) To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294. (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)  Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.  (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.)

Plaintiffs allege, “Defendant[], through the decisions of [its] officers, directors, employees and/or managing agents, acted in conscious disregard of the rights and safety of patients and residents, including DECEDENT, by failing to provide adequate staffing levels, among other failures noted above.  The facilities were not staffed as required by law and were understaffed during the time period that the DECEDENT was treated at the Facilities.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 164.)  Plaintiff further alleges, Defendant’s “officers, directors, employees and/or managing agents, were aware that staffing levels were inadequate to ensure adequate supervision in meeting the DECEDENT’s needs.  Defendant[] chose to take no corrective action.  The officers, directors, employees and/or managing agents, knew that inadequate staffing levels at the Facilities, and failure to adequately supervise the DECEDENT, would result in injury.”  (Id., ¶ 168.) 

Plaintiffs do not allege specific facts to support their conclusion Defendant acted with oppression, fraud, or malice such that an award of punitive damages is warranted.  While Plaintiffs allege Defendant failed to adequately staff the facility to care for Decedent, there are no specific allegations to show Defendant’s officers, directors, or managing agents made this staffing decision with oppression, fraud, or malice, nor which such individuals committed the acts.  Accordingly, the Court strikes the prayer for punitive damages with leave to amend.

Defendant also moves to strike the prayer for attorney fees, which Plaintiffs seek an in connection with their claim for elder abuse.  Welfare and Institutions Code section 15657 provides the statutory basis for an attorney fee award.  To that end, Plaintiffs must allege facts to show Plaintiffs are entitled to punitive damages in order to recover attorney fees.  (Welf. & Inst. Code, § 15657.)  As set forth above, Plaintiffs fail to allege specific facts demonstrating they are entitled to punitive damages and, therefore, they have not alleged facts supporting their entitlement to attorney fees.  Accordingly, the Court strikes the prayer for attorney fees with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s cause of action for elder abuse is overruled.  Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s cause of action for willful misconduct is sustained with leave to amend.  Defendant’s motion to strike the prayers for punitive damages and for attorney fees is granted with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint within 10 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
