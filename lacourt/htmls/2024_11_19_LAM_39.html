DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV13023    Hearing Date: November 19, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV13023

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant Van Nuys-L, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky

 

MOTION

            Defendant Van Nuys-L, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky (“Plaintiff”) to further respond to Requests for Production of Documents, set three (“RPD”).  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

If the propounding party deems responses to requests for production of documents unsatisfactory, the propounding party may move to compel further responses.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310.) 

In Plaintiff’s supplemental responses to the RPD, Plaintiff states, after asserting objections, “After making a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, Plaintiff has no responsive documents in his possession, custody, or control.  Discovery is continuing and Plaintiff reserves the right to supplement this response upon completion of further discovery.”  (See Separate Statement in Support of Motion to Compel Further Responses, p. 3.)  Plaintiff’s response is improper.  If a party cannot respond to an RPD, the responding party must state “a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand . . . ,” and the party must “specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.  The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)  Plaintiff fails to state why he cannot locate responsive documents and, to the extent such documents do exist, Plaintiff has not identified any party who may have them.  In addition, Plaintiff cannot unilaterally extend the time in which Plaintiff responds to discovery by stating discovery is ongoing.    

Defendant contends responsive documents do exist, and Plaintiff can access them. Ordinarily, Defendant cannot move to compel Plaintiff to further respond on the basis Defendant believes Plaintiff’s responses are not true. (Holguin v. Superior Court (1972) 22 Cal.App.3d 812, 820.)  Defendant’s remedy then is to offer Plaintiff’s allegedly incomplete responses as admissions or as prior inconsistent statements to impeach Plaintiff at trial, if Plaintiff seeks to introduce evidence it did not disclose in discovery.  (See Evid. Code, §§ 1220; 1235.)   

In any event, this issue may be premature.  As discussed above, Plaintiff must further respond to the RPD by stating why he cannot locate responsive documents before the Court can reach Defendant’s argument Plaintiff can, in fact, obtain responsive documents.  However, the Court notes in this instance in which Defendant seeks documents related to Plaintiff’s disability claim in this action, which themselves may be admissible or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence at trial, the Court agrees with Defendant such documents are relevant.  To the extent they contain privileged information, they should be listed on a privilege log, and such information can be redacted if the documents are to be used at trial.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues he testified at deposition the majority of the documents sought do not exist.  That is irrelevant.  Plaintiff’s duty to properly respond to the RPD is independent of Plaintiff’s deposition testimony.  Likewise, as alluded to above, Plaintiff’s argument responsive documents are privileged is irrelevant.  If Plaintiff has privileged documents in his possession, custody, or control, Plaintiff must so state and provide Defendant with a privilege log.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.240, subd. (c)(1).)  Plaintiff has not done so.

Under the circumstances of this matter in which Plaintiff’s supplemental response is incomplete, the Court exercises its discretion and concludes a sanctions award is not warranted. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to the RPD is granted.  Plaintiff is to serve further responses within 20 days of its receipt of notice of this order. 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV04376

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Appearance and Production of Documents at Trial

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant Spina Organics, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff Ellen Pittleman

 

MOTION

            Defendant Spina Organics, LLC (“Defendant”) moves to enforce a notice to appear at trial and to produce documents and things that Defendant served on Plaintiff Ellen Pittleman (“Plaintiff”).  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

A party may use a notice to attend to compel another party to attend trial and produce documents and things at trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987, subd. (b).)  A party may challenge a notice to attend trial by moving for an order “to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)  A party may also challenge a request to produce documents and things at trial by serving written objections to the request with a statement of grounds.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987, subd. (c).)  The service of written objections shifts the burden to the requesting party to file a noticed motion for production of the items sought, “accompanied by a showing of good cause and of materiality of the items to the issues” in the case.  (Ibid.)

Defendant served a notice to appear and produce documents at trial on Plaintiff on October 28, 2024.  Plaintiff objected to the notice on November 2, 2024 on the basis Defendant does not have a valid claim for punitive damages, which would support discovery of Plaintiff’s financial condition.  (Declaration of Steven P. Krakowsky, Exhibit 2.)  Plaintiff argues Defendant’s “discovery abuse should preclude any attempt to introduce evidence about [Plaintiff’s] financial condition.”  (Opposition to Motion to Compel Production of Documents at Trial, p. 3.)  That is not a valid objection.  Defendant’s right to compel Plaintiff’s attendance and production of documents at trial is independent of Defendant’s discovery obligations. 

A notice to produce documents and things at trial “requires a listing of the exact materials or things to be produced.”  (I-CA Enterprises, Inc. v. Palram Americas, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 257, 280.)  Accordingly, Defendant’s notice to Plaintiff to have the categories of documents available at trial for the purpose of establishing Plaintiff’s financial condition pursuant to Civil Code section 3295, subdivision (c) is proper.  (Soto v. BorgWarner Morse TEC Inc. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 165, 193.)  Such information, however, will only become relevant if and when Defendant has established an entitlement to punitive damages.  The Court, therefore, grants the motion to the extent it seeks to compel Plaintiff’s appearance and testimony at trial and for Plaintiff to have documents concerning her financial condition available.  As Plaintiff has not failed to do so at this point, the Court exercises its discretion and denies Defendant’s request for sanctions.

Defendant is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 23STCV21002    Hearing Date: November 19, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

November 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV21002

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Rolling Hills Covenant Church, Clyde LaGue, Sam Tabari, Craig Knickerbocker, Sam Evans, and John Thrill

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Daniel A. Burgoyne

 

MOTION

            Plaintiff Daniel A. Burgoyne (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Rolling Hills Covenant Church (“Rolling Hills”), Clyde LaGue, Sam Tabari, Craig Knickerbocker, Sam Evans, and John Thrill (collectively, “Defendants”) for wrongfully terminating Plaintiff’s employment as pastor at Rolling Hills.  Defendants move to compel arbitration with Plaintiff.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.  The Court previously considered this matter, and, in its order of March 15, 2024, continued the matter because the parties requested an evidentiary hearing.  (See March 15, 2024 Minute Order.)  The parties have since abandoned the request for an evidentiary hearing.  (See September 19, 2024 Minute Order.)  As such, the Court now rules on the motion.

ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842 (Ruiz), internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Defendants have presented in their papers an arbitration agreement purportedly between Plaintiff and Rolling Hills.  The document states, “the parties agree that any controversy or claim arising out of this agreement shall be resolved with the assistance of the Center for Conflict Resolution, a Christian Conciliation Service, through mediation, and if necessary, legally binding arbitration . . . .”  (Declaration of Sam Tabari, Exhibit B.)  Defendants attach the agreement to the declaration of Sam Tabari (“Tabari”), Rolling Hills’ executive director and an elder at the Church.  Tabari declares the arbitration agreement “was received by [Rolling Hills] from Plaintiff.”  (Tabari Decl., ¶ 10.)  Although Tabari adds he is aware Plaintiff signed the agreement, he does not provide the basis for such awareness, and Tabari does not state he has personal knowledge regarding the authenticity of the agreement.  As such, Defendants have not met their burden to prove the existence of a valid arbitration agreement.  (See Ruiz, supra, 232 Cal.App.4th at pp. 845-846.) 

Through its reply in support of the motion, Defendants advance the declaration of Robert H. Cubillos (“Cubillos”), who worked for Rolling Hills as its vice president and business administrator at the time Plaintiff was employed by Rolling Hills.  Cubillos declares he was at Rolling Hills when Plaintiff executed his onboarding paperwork, and Plaintiff must have had a complete set of paperwork when Plaintiff left, or he would have asked Plaintiff to complete any outstanding documents.  (Declaration of Robert H. Cubillos, ¶¶ 11-12.)  Again, however, in the face of Plaintiff’s insistence he never saw, let alone signed, the arbitration agreement, Cubillos does not state he witnessed Plaintiff signing the agreement.  Thus, Cubillos lacks personal knowledge concerning the existence of any arbitration agreement into which Plaintiff purportedly entered.

Defendants also advance a second declaration from Tabari while replying to Plaintiff’s opposition.  In that declaration, Tabari advances identical arbitration agreements between Rolling Hills and 196 of its employees.  (Declaration of Sam Tabari in Support of Defendants’ Reply, Exhibit A.)  However, none of these employees worked in the same position as Plaintiff.  Plaintiff counters with evidence multiple pastors for Rolling Hills did not sign the arbitration agreements.  (Declaration of Joy Burgoyne in Support of Sur-Reply, ¶¶ 13, 33.)  Further, many employees of Rolling Hills are not among the 196 employees who signed an arbitration agreement.  (Burgoyne Decl., ¶¶ 7, 12.)  Therefore, Defendants fail to show Plaintiff necessarily signed the arbitration agreement when he began working for Rolling Hills.

Defendants also filed a sur-reply brief in which they offer new arguments and advance new evidence.  The Court did not authorize Defendants to do so.  (See February 21, 2024 Minute Order.)  Defendants cannot present such evidence and arguments in an unauthorized sur-reply, as they are not entitled to unlimited opportunities to do so.  For that reason, the Court disregards Defendants’ sur-reply.

In his opposition, Plaintiff consistently argues the signature on the arbitration agreement is not Plaintiff’s authentic signature because Plaintiff did not see nor did he sign the arbitration agreement.  Defendants have not satisfied their burden of establishing otherwise.  Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration.[1] 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

The motion to compel arbitration is denied. 

Plaintiff is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


[1]Given the basis of the Court’s ruling, the Court does not reach issues the parties addressed after completing the briefing on this motion concerning whether there could have been a meeting of the minds regarding the arbitration agreement because Plaintiff alleges the entity which was designated in the agreement to conduct any arbitration had not done so since 1994.
