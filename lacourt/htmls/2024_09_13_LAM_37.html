DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV02390    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Friday, September 13, 2023

CASE NUMBER:                   23STCV02390

CASE NAME:                        Immigrant Rights Defense Co. v. Sonia I. Zaldivar

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Z Legal Services, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY:             Plaintiff Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC

TRIAL DATE:                        Not set.

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion for Summary Judgment, or Summary Adjudication

OPPOSITION:                        09 August 2024

REPLY:                                  24 May 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         On its own motion, the court reconsiders the November 15, 2023 Order denying Z Legal’s motion to quash and grants the motion. The motion for summary judgment is denied as moot. Plaintiff is given leave to amend the SAC to add a new defendant.

 

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

This is a dispute arising in connection with Defendant Sonia I. Zaldivar (“Zaldivar”) operating as an Immigration Consultant in California. Plaintiff, Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC (“Plaintiff”) alleges that Defendant has operated as an Immigration Consultant not in conformity with the Immigration Consultants Act of California (“ICA”).

Plaintiff brings this claim for an injunction on behalf of the general public as authorized by Business and Professions Code § 22446.5(a).¿ Plaintiff asserts that Defendant has violated multiple provisions of ICA and, as such, Plaintiff is entitled to obtain an injunction on behalf of the general public.¿¿

On June 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging a single cause of action for injunctive relief for violation of the ICA. Plaintiff filed a Doe amendment, adding Z Legal Services Inc. (“Z Legal”) as a defendant.

 

Defendant Z Legal now moves for summary judgment, or in the alternative, for summary adjudication. Plaintiff opposes the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

 

motion for summary judgment

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”¿ (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843 (Aguilar).) “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) Summary adjudication may be granted as to one or more causes of action within an action, or one or more claims for damages. (CCP, § 437c(f).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

Defeating summary judgment requires only a single disputed material fact. (See CCP, § 437c(c) [a motion for summary judgment “shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law”] [italics added].) Thus, any disputed material fact means the court must deny the motion – the court has no discretion to grant summary judgment. (Zavala v. Arce (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 915, 925, fn. 8; Saldana v. Globe-Weis Systems Co. (1991) 233 Cal.App.3d 1505, 1511-1512.)¿¿¿

 

II.        Request for Judicial Notice

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) “Taking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning.” (Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374.)

 

Defendant Z Legal Requests Judicial Notice of the Following:

 

Exhibit A: A true and correct copy of the summons.

 

Exhibit B: A true and correct copy of the original complaint filed on February 3, 2023.

 

Exhibit C: A true and correct copy of Amendment to Complaint.

Defendant Z Legal’s request for judicial notice is granted as to Exhibits A to L and L to P. The request for judicial notice as to Exhibit M is denied as to the Declaration of Sebastian Medvei, but is otherwise granted. (See Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 [“Courts may not take judicial notice of allegations in affidavits, declarations and probation reports in court records because such matters are reasonably subject to dispute and therefore require formal proof.”].)

 

III.      Plaintiff’s Objections

 

As Defendant Z Legal’s objections are not material to the court’s disposition of this Motion, the court declines to rule upon them. All objections not ruled upon are preserved for appeal. (CCP

§ 437c(q).)

 

IV.       Discussion

 

Plaintiff filed this action on February 3, 2023, against Zaldivar and Does 1 to 100 for violation of ICA. Plaintiff filed the operative SAC on June 14, 2023.

 

Plaintiff asserts that after Zaldivar was served with a copy of the original Complaint on July 9, 2023, she incorporated Defendant Z Legal. (See Proof of Service filed. 07/10/2023; Defendants’ Compendium of Evidence (“DCOE”) Ex. E.) On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Amendment to Complaint naming Defendant Z Legal as Doe 1. (Amendment filed 07/10/2023.) Defendant Z Legal moved to quash the service of the SAC on the ground that the Doe amendment was improper. On November 15, 2023, the court denied Z Legal’s motion to quash.

 

Defendant Z Legal now moves for summary judgment as to the SAC or summary adjudication of the first cause of action on the four following grounds as asserted in the Separate Statement:

1)     Issue 1: Plaintiff Cannot Prevail on Its SAC Because Defendant Z Legal Cannot Be Doe 1;

2)     Issue 2: Plaintiff Cannot Prevail on Its First Cause of Action for Statutory Violations of the ICA against Defendant Z Legal;

3)     Issue 3: Plaintiff Cannot Prevail on Its SAC Because Defendant Z Leal is Not Subject to the ICA;

4)     Issue 4: Plaintiff cannot Prevail on Its SAC Because Defendant Z Legal Could not have Committed the Acts Attributed to Doe 1 which Occurred Before Defendant’s Formation.

 

A.        Plaintiff’s Improper Doe Amendment

Defendant Z Legal argues that because it did not exist prior to the filing of the SAC, it cannot be Doe 1 based on the allegations in the pleadings. The SAC alleges that Plaintiff does not know the true names and capacities of the Doe Defendants but “Plaintiff will amend the complaint to show the true names and capacities of such DOES when they have been ascertained.” (SAC, ¶ 3.) “Plaintiff is informed and believes, and thereon alleges, that each of the fictitiously named defendants is responsible in some manner for the actions and occurrences herein alleged[.]” (SAC, ¶ 3.) Doe 1 is alleged to be an agent of the other Defendants. (SAC, ¶ 4.) Defendants were immigration consultants, “identified herein as DOES 1 through 5, employed at the business” who filed to “submit a background check to the Secretary of State.” (SAC, ¶ 20(f).)

 

At issue is whether Z Legal was properly identified as Doe 1 or if Plaintiff was required to file an amended complaint identifying Z Legal as a new Defendant rather than a Doe Defendant. “Code of Civil Procedure section 474 allows a plaintiff to name fictitious defendants, or ‘Does.’ ” (Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. v. Sparks Construction, Inc. (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1135, 1143.) “Provided these requirements are satisfied, the amendment is deemed to ‘relate[ ] back’ to the filing date of the original complaint for purposes of the statute of limitations. [Citation.]” (Ibid.) “Failure to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 474 does not prevent a plaintiff from filing an amendment adding a new defendant; however, it does prevent the amendment from relating back.” (Id., at p. 1144.)

 

The court agrees that it is not possible for Z Legal to be Doe 1 because its liability would relate back to the filing of the original Complaint filed on February 3, 2023, and Z Legal was not created until February 9, 2023. (DCOE Ex. E.) Accordingly, any alleged violations of ICA by Z Legal could only occur after February 9, 2023.  “[T]he relevant inquiry when the plaintiff seeks to substitute a real defendant for one sued fictitiously is what facts the plaintiff actually knew at the time the original complaint was filed.” (General Motors Corp. v. Superior Court (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 580, 588 [italics original].) As Z Legal did not exist until February 9, 2023, Plaintiff could not know that Doe 1 was “in the business of acting as immigration consultants.” (SAC, ¶ 7.)

As Defendant Z Legal did not exist at the time the original Complaint was filed, there is no factual basis for Plaintiff to believe Z Legal was liable for violating ICA.  Once Plaintiff learned about Z Legal’s creation and had reason to believe it was violating ICA, Plaintiff should have amended the pleadings to add Z Legal as the new defendant, rather than as a Doe defendant.

 

As the Doe Amendment was improper, the court should have granted Z Legal’s motion to quash. “Improper service of a defendant under section 474 may be attacked by a motion to quash. [Citations.]  ‘If the terms of ... section 474 have not been complied with, the purported defendant has not been named as such in the complaint. A service upon one not named in a complaint does not confer jurisdiction to proceed upon the complaint against him, and a motion to quash is proper.’ [Citation.]” (McClatchy v. Coblentz, Patch, Duffy & Bass, LLP (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 368, 375 [italics original].)

Therefore, the court reconsiders the November 15, 2023 Order denying Z Legal’s Motion to Quash.

            B.        Reconsideration of the November 15, 2023 Order

 

“[A] trial judge may reconsider and correct erroneous orders independent of the statutory limitations imposed on reconsideration motions.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 603.) “If a court believes one of its prior interim orders was erroneous, it should be able to correct that error no matter how it came to acquire that belief.” (Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1108.) “In our view, the California Constitution requires that in any case in which a trial judge reconsiders an erroneous order, and enters a new order that is substantively correct, the resulting ruling must be affirmed regardless of any procedural error committed along the way.” (In re Marriage of Barthold (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1301, 1313.)

 

Because the court now finds that Plaintiff’s Doe amendment was improper, the court reconsiders its November 15, 2023 Order denying Z Legal’s Motion to Quash and grants the motion.

 

Consequently, the court lacks jurisdiction over Defendant Z Legal and its motion for summary judgment is moot.

 

The court notes that should Defendant Z Legal wish to proceed on the merits of its motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, for summary adjudication, it failed to establish that there were no triable issues of material fact.

 

            C.        Plaintiff May Amend the SAC to Add Z Legal As a New Defendant

 

A complaint can be amended to add new facts which occurred after the filing of the initial complaint. (Honig v. Financial Corp. of America (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 960, 966.) A new defendant may be added provided that the statute of limitations has not passed. (McGee Street Productions v. Workers' Comp. Appeals Bd. (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 717, 724.)

 

Plaintiff may amend the SAC to add Z Legal as a new defendant because the statue to limitations for ICA violations has not passed.

 

In its motion for summary judgment, Defendant Z Legal asserts that Plaintiff’s ICA claims are barred because Plaintiff is not an “aggrieved party” capable of discovering ICA violations under Bus. & Prof. Code § 22448:

 

Any civil action to enforce any cause of action pursuant to this chapter shall be commenced within four years after the cause of action has accrued. The cause of action is not to be deemed to have accrued until the discovery, by the aggrieved party, of the facts constituting the violation.

 

(Bus. & Prof. Code, § 22448 [bold added].) In other words, because Plaintiff is not an aggrieved party, it cannot discover Z Legal’s violations of ICA.

 

Defendant Z Legal relies on Immigrant Rights Defense Council, LLC v. Hudson Ins. Co. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th in support of its proposition that Plaintiff is not an aggrieved party because “the Legislature has consistently declared that the bond funds are designated for those who have been actually injured and suffered damages from ICA violations committed by an immigration consultant.” (Id., at pp. 265-366.) “In short, the class of people the ICA is designed to protect is the population most vulnerable to fraud and deceit by unscrupulous consultants—their customers. The Legislature's decision to limit the ICA bond funds to victim compensation is therefore consistent with the ICA's purpose in protecting—and making whole—the vulnerable population it was designed to protect.” (Id., at p. 317.)

 

However, Immigrant Rights pertained to whether the plaintiff was an aggrieved party entitled to recover damages from bond or deposit under Bus. & Prof. Code § 22447[1]. Bus. & Prof. Code section 22446.5(b) expressly states:

 

Any other party who, upon information and belief, claims a violation of this chapter has been committed by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action for injunctive relief on behalf of the general public and, upon prevailing, shall recover reasonable attorneys' fees and costs.

 

Therefore, because Plaintiff is “[a]ny other party” who believes ICA has been violated, Plaintiff can discover ICA violations even if Plaintiff is not an aggrieved party who has been injured and suffered damages. Bus. & Prof. Code § 22446.5(a) That section specifically applies to “[a] person claiming to be aggrieved by a violation of this chapter by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action for injunctive relief or damages, or both” whereas section 22446.5(b) is reserved for “[a]ny other party who, upon information and belief, claims a violation of this chapter has been committed by an immigration consultant may bring a civil action for injunctive relief on behalf of the general public [.]” (Id., [italics added].)

 

The fact that Plaintiff is not an aggrieved party, does not mean that Plaintiff cannot discover ICA violations. To find otherwise and accept Z Legal’s interpretation, would produce absurd consequences because it would make Section 22446.5 subdivisions (b) and (c) effectively void by depriving “[a]ny other party” and “[t]he Attorney General, a district attorney, or a city attorney” of standing to bring an ICA action. When interpreting a statute, “‘[w]e do not examine that language in isolation, but in the context of the statutory framework as a whole in order to determine its scope and purpose and to harmonize the various parts of the enactment. If the language is clear, courts must generally follow its plain meaning unless a literal interpretation would result in absurd consequences the Legislature did not intend.’ ” (Meda v. Autozone, Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 366, 378 [internal citation and quotation marks omitted].)

 

Because Z Legal was incorporated on February 3, 2023, the four-year statute of limitations articulated in Bus. & Prof. Code § 22448 has not yet run, and Plaintiff may amend the SAC to add Z Legal as a new defendant.

 

            D.        Defendant Z Legal’s Motion for Summary Judgment Fails

 

Assuming, arguendo, that Defendant Z Legal accepts the court’s jurisdiction and wishes to proceed on the merits of its Motion, Defendant Z Legal fails to establish there are no triable issues of material fact and it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. Defendant Z Legal failed to cite any authority supporting the proposition that when the Legislature uses the word “person” and fails to provide a definition, the presumption is that person refers only to natural persons and not artificial persons. (See Wells v. One2One Learning Foundation (2006) 39 Cal.4th 1164, 1198.)

 

Furthermore, Defendant Z Legal failed to establish that it cannot be held vicariously liable for ICA violations.  Under the doctrine of respondeat superior, an employer may be held vicariously liable for torts committed by an employee within the scope of employment.” (Mary M. v. City of Los Angeles (1991) 54 Cal.3d 202, 208.) “A corporation can act only through its employees; thus, an employee acting on behalf of the employer cannot be acting in concert with the employer, as there is in law only a single actor.” (Fiol v. Doellstedt (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1318, 1326.)

 

Here, a business entity acting as an employer can be “any person” who “for compensation” engages “in the business or act[s] in the capacity of an immigration consultant[.]” (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 22440.) Moreover, a business entity can be a person who engages in the business or acts in the capacity of an immigration consultant when it gives advice or assistance related to:

 

(1) Completing a form provided by a federal or state agency but not advising a person as to their answers on those forms.

(2) Translating a person's answers to questions posed in those forms.

(3) Securing for a person supporting documents, such as birth certificates, which may be necessary to complete those forms.

(4) Submitting completed forms on a person's behalf and at their request to the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services.

(5) Making referrals to persons who could undertake legal representation activities for a person in an immigration matter.

(Bus. & Prof. Code, § 22441(a).)

 

Therefore, Defendant Z Legal fails to show that it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law because a business entity can be held vicariously liable for ICA violations.

 

Conclusion

 

On its own motion, the court reconsiders the November 15, 2023 Order denying Z Legal’s motion to quash and grants the motion. The motion for summary judgment is denied as moot. Plaintiff is given leave to amend the SAC to add a new defendant.


[1] Bus. & Prof. Code section 22447(a) states in part “A person who is awarded damages in an action or proceeding for injuries caused by the acts of a person engaged in the business of, or acting in the capacity of, an immigration consultant, in the performance of his or her duties as an immigration consultant, may recover damages from the bond required by Section 22443.1.”

Background

 

On October 10, 2023, Kevin Kim (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Bruce Haebong Lee (“Lee”); Seven Seung Woo Bae (“Bae”); Masters Realty Group, Inc. (“MRG”); Masters Realty & Investment (“MRI”) (a corporation); Masters Realty & Investment (a business); and Does 1 – 50.) This action stems from a dispute as to whether the Defendants failed in their obligation to maintain insurance that would have indemnified Plaintiff in an underlying action.

 

Defendants each demurred to the Complaint and the court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend. The operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), filed May 8, 2024, alleges three causes of action:

 

1)     Breach of Contract against all Defendants;

2)     Fraud against all Defendants, Except Defendants Bae and MRI; and

3)     Negligent Misrepresentation against all Defendants, Except Defendants Bae and MRI.

 

Defendants MRI and Bae now demurrer to the first and only cause of action alleged against them in the FAC. Plaintiff opposes the demurrer. The matter is now before the court.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

The court may take judicial notice of “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States,” “[r]ecords of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States,” and “[f]acts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (c), (d), and (h).) “Taking judicial notice of a document is not the same as accepting the truth of its contents or accepting a particular interpretation of its meaning.” (Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374.)

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of the following:

 

1)     Exhibit 1: A true and correct copy of the Articles of Incorporation filed by Masters Realty & Investments, Inc. with the Secretary of State on January 7, 2004.

 

2)     Exhibit 2: A true and correct copy of the Certificate of Amendment of Articles of Incorporation filed by Masters Realty & Investments, Inc. on April 7, 2005.

 

3)     Exhibit 3: A true and correct copy of the LoopNet webpage at https://www.loopnet.com/company/masters-realty-group/los-angeles-ca/7slrdpwl/, which containing information relating to “Masters Realty Group”.

 

Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is granted as to Exhibit 1 and Exhibit 2. The court denies the request as to Exhibit 3 as Plaintiff fails to show judicial notice of Exhibit 3 is proper and not subject to dispute and capable of immediate and accurate determination.

 

demurrer to first amended complaint[1]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters.¿ (CCP, § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) In evaluating a demurrer, the court accepts the complainant’s properly pled facts as true and ignores contentions, deductions, and conclusory statements. (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1976) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713; Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Moreover, the court does not consider whether a plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations or the possible difficulty in making such proof. (Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)¿ The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

 

III.      Discussion

 

A.        First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

The elements of a claim for breach of contract are: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal. 4th 811, 821.) In addition, the complaint must demonstrate damages proximately caused by the breach. (St. Paul Ins. v. American Dynasty (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1060.) Furthermore, “the complaint must [also] indicate on its face whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct.” (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458-59 citing CCP § 430.10(g).)

Defendants MRI and Bae assert that the breach of contract claim fails as alleged against them because the FAC does not connect MRI and Bae to the contract. Plaintiff does not dispute the allegation but instead asserts that MRI and Bae’s liability stems from alter ego liability.

“‘A claim against a defendant, based on the alter ego theory, is not itself a claim for substantive relief, e.g., breach of contract ..., but rather, procedural, i.e., to disregard the corporate entity as a distinct defendant and to hold the alter ego individuals liable on the obligations  of the corporation where the corporate form is being used by the individuals to escape personal liability, sanction a fraud, or promote injustice.’ ” (Greenspan v. LADT, LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 486, 516 citing Hennessey's Tavern, Inc. v. American Air Filter Co. (1988) 204 Cal.App.3d 1351, 1359.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant MRI and Bae may be held liable if they are the alter ego of Defendant MRG, the party to the contract. (FAC ¶ 29, Ex. 4.) “To succeed on their alter ego claim, plaintiffs must be able to show: (1) such a unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its equitable owner that no separation actually exists, and (2) an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.” (Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 417.) However, on demurrer, the court does not consider whether the plaintiff will be able to prove the allegations. (See Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610; Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 604.)  Instead, the court focuses on whether the complaint sets forth facts sufficient to put the defendant on notice about what the plaintiff is complaining about and what remedies are being sought. (Leek, at p. 415.)

 

As to the first prong for alter ego liability, the FAC alleges that Defendants MRI and Bae share the same address as Defendant MRG. (Id., ¶¶ 6, 7.) Defendant Bae is the son-in-law of Defendant Lee and Lee and MRG are the brokers responsible for Defendant Bae. (Id., ¶¶ 10, 13.) Defendants Lee and Bae operated the real estate brokerage business under the names MRG, MRI, and Master Realty & Investments and used the names interchangeably despite MRG being suspended by the California Secretary of State. (Id., ¶ 15.) Defendant Bae is also the shareholder, director and officer of MRI. (Id., Ex. 1, 2, 3.) Taking these allegations as true, Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts to show that a unity of interest exists between Defendants MRI and Bae and Defendant MRG, the signatory of the contract.

 

As to the second prong, the FAC further alleges that Defendants Lee and Bae incorporated MRI for the for the sole purpose of transferring assets out of MRG and to hinder, defraud, and/or delay MRG’s creditors, including Plaintiff. (FAC, ¶ 15.) Defendants Lee and Bae used MRG as a “mere shell, instrumentality, and conduit to carry out their business such that there was no separateness between them. (Id., ¶ 15.) Defendants Lee and Bae completely controlled, managed, and operated MRG and intermingled assets for their own convenience. (Id., ¶ 17.) To find that a separation exits between Defendants MRG and Bae and Lee would sanction fraud and promote injustice and allow Defendants Lee and Bae to escape liability. (Id., ¶ 18.) Therefore, the second prong of alter ego liability is adequately pled.

 

Plaintiff further asserts that successor liability may be imposed on Defendant MRI because it is the successor of MRG.  (FAC, ¶ 12.)

 

Under the theory of successor liability, “corporations cannot escape liability by a mere change of name or a shift of assets when and where it is shown that the new corporation is, in reality, but a continuation of the old. Especially is this well settled when actual fraud or the rights of creditors are involved, under which circumstances the courts uniformly hold the new corporation liable for the debts of the former corporation.’ ” (Cleveland v. Johnson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327 citing Blank v. Olcovich Shoe Corp. (1937) 20 Cal.App.2d 456, 461.)

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action is overruled.

 

motion for judgment on the pleading as to first amended complaint[2]

 

I.         Legal Standard

 

“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed.” (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1064.) “In deciding or reviewing a judgment on the pleadings, all properly pleaded material facts are deemed to be true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.” (Fire Ins. Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452.) When considering demurrers and judgment on the pleadings, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Wilson v. Transit Authority of City of Sacramento (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 716, 720-21.) A motion for judgment on the pleadings does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action. (Id.) “In the case of either a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings, leave to amend should be granted if there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action.” (Gami v. Mullikin Medical Ctr. (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 870, 876.) A non-statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings may be made any time before or during trial. (Stoops v. Abbassi (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 644, 650.)¿¿¿ 

 

III.      Discussion

 

Defendant Lee moves for judgment on the pleadings as to all three causes of action alleged in the  FAC. Plaintiff asserts that Defendant Lee may not bring a motion for judgment on the pleadings because Defendant Lee already demurred to the first cause of action and CCP § 438(g) only permits a MJOP  if “[t]moving party has already demurred to the complaint or answer, as the case may be, on the same grounds as is the basis for the motion provided for in this section and the demurrer has been overruled, provided that there has been a material change in applicable case law or statute since the ruling on the demurrer.” (Id.) On March 27, 2024, this court overruled the demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract. (3/27/2024 Order.)

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action focused on whether the E&O Insurance fee was for the benefit of Plaintiff to provide insurance coverage. (03/27/2024 Order, at p. 3.) Here, however the judgment on the pleadings focuses on the fact that Section 18 of the contract voids any prior verbal representations such that Plaintiff cannot allege that Defendant Lee made representations to him about the E&O policy. (FAC, ¶ 29, Ex. 4, ¶ 18.)

 

Therefore, the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action is made on “the same grounds” as the previous demurrer and section 438(g) does not apply. Moreover, the court retains the discretion to consider the motion for judgment made on the same basis as a prior demurrer because “a trial judge may reconsider and correct erroneous orders independent of the statutory limitations imposed on reconsideration motions.” (Thomson v. Canyon (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 594, 603; see also Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1106.) Therefore, the court addresses the request for judgment on the pleadings on its merits.

 

            A.        First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

As to the first cause of action, Defendant Lee’s liability for breach of contract is not based on the representations he made to Plaintiff but on allegations that he is the alter ego of MRG. (FAC, ¶¶ 7-12, 15 -18, 25-26.) Defendant Lee does not allege that the alter ego allegations against him are inadequately pled. Therefore, the motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the first cause of action is denied.

 

B.        Second Cause and Third Cause of Action – Negligent Misrepresenting and Fraud

 

The elements of a misrepresentation claim are: (1) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); (3) intent to defraud or induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) damages. (See Civ. Code, § 1709.) Fraud actions are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) A plaintiff must allege what was said, by whom, in what manner (i.e. oral or in writing), when, and, in the case of a corporate defendant, under what authority to bind the corporation. (See Goldrich v. Natural Y Surgical Specialties, Inc. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 772, 782.)

 

As to the second and third causes of action for fraud and negligence, the motion is made on the basis that the negligence and fraud causes of action are not specifically pled. This assertion is made on different grounds than Lee’s prior demurrer wherein Lee argued that the insurance fee was to cover Defendant’s insurance premium and not to provide insurance coverage to Plaintiff. (See 03/27/2024 Order, at p. 5.)

 

The FAC alleges that Defendant Lee represented to Plaintiff that Defendant MRG, as broker for Plaintiff, would obtain the E&O policy for Plaintiff and would charge plaintiff a fee of $90.00 for each transaction. (FAC, ¶ 30.) The $90.00 charge began on July 19, 2010 and later increased to $100.00 per transaction. (Id., ¶ 31.) On July 2, 2020, as part of the Montebello Transaction, Lee and MRG made a Commission Disbursement Request, wherein Lee and MRG charged $100.00 for the E&O policy. (Id., ¶ 33.) It was not until January 3, 2023, that Plaintiff learned via an email from Defendants that the MRG’s license had expired on June 30, 2021. (Id., ¶ 35, Ex. 7.) Despite charging for the E&O policy during the Montebello transaction, Defendants did not have the E&O Policy during the time that the transaction took place.

 

The court agrees that the negligent misrepresentation and fraud claims are not pled with the requisite specificity. Plaintiff fails to state when Lee made the representations about the E&O Policy, if the representations were verbal or written, and if and how Lee continued to make those representations after the MRG’s policy expired. Plaintiff also alleges in a conclusory manner that “Defendants” had no reasonable grounds to believe their representations but fails to allege specific facts as to why Plaintiff believes Defendant Lee had no basis to believe that Plaintiff had an E&O Policy. “These conclusionary allegations, which allege no specific acts, are insufficient to survive demurrer.” (Bagatti v. Department of Rehabilitation (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 344, 366, fn. 8.) Lastly, Plaintiff fails to allege that Lee was responsible or ratified the representation made in the Commission Disbursement Request, that the $100.00 fee was for the E&O policy.

 

Defendant Lee’s motion for judgment on the pleadings is granted at to the second and third causes of action with ten days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court OVERRULES the demurrers brought by Defendants MRI and Bae; DENIES

Defendant Lee’s MJOP as to the first cause of action and GRANTS the MJOP as to the second

and third causes of action with ten days leave to amend.  The Court sets a Non-Appearance OSC

re Amended Complaint for October 10, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  Plaintiff to give notice.
