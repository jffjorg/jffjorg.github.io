Case Number: 23TRCV00445    Hearing Date: June 26, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                 June 26, 2024

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                  23TRCV00445

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                        Gabriella Morgan v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc., et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Plaintiff, Gabriella Morgan

¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant, American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

 

TRIAL DATE:                        May 5, 2025

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                              (1) Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set One

                                                (2) Request for Sanctions

 

Tentative Rulings:                   (1) GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

                                                (2) Plaintiff’s Request for Sanctions is DENIED 

¿¿ 

¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

On February 15, 2023, Plaintiff, Gabriella Morgan (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendant, American Honda Motor Co., Inc., and DOES 1 through 10. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; and (2) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment.

 

On October 17, 2023, Plaintiff propounded her first set of discovery requests, including Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), to investigate and develop the case.  Per the moving papers, on November 30, 2023, Defendant served its responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests, but Plaintiff found them deficient.  Plaintiff notes that many of Defendant’s responses failed to either agree to produce the documents, agreed to produce certain document and failed to do so, or failed to properly object in a manner required by the Code of Civil Procedure. Plaintiff notes that on January 8, 2024, Plaintiff initiated a meet and confer letter to Defendant specifying the deficiencies in Defendant’s responses and requesting they be corrected, but claims that Defendant ignored all attempts at meeting and conferring to resolve.

 

As such, Plaintiffs bring this motion to compel further responses seeking an order from this Court to compel Defendant to produce proper, Code compliant, verified further responses Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, all documents, and award monetary sanctions.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

            On January 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. On March 1, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. On March 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply brief.

 

            This motion was originally heard on March 13, 2024, where this Court continued the hearing to April 26, 2024. Further, on April 26, 2024, the motion was continued again, with an order for the parties to meet and confer pursuant to guidelines provided by this Court.   On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended separate statement.

¿ ¿¿ 

¿II. MEET AND CONFER

 

            Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(b)(2) requires that a motion to compel a part deponent to appear or produce documents “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (C.C.P. §2025.450(b)(2).) The attempt to resolve informally may be made either by conferring “in person, by telephone, or by letter with an opposing party or attorney.”  (C.C.P. §2023.010(i), [failure to make such attempt constitutes “misuse of discovery process”].)

 

            Here, the Court notes the parties have not sufficiently met and conferred, but that Plaintiff made an effort to do so, and thus the hearing on the motion to compel will go forward.   

 

III. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

A.    Legal Standard

“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) For discovery purposes, information is regarded as relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof.” (City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 272, 288.)  

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.” If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Sup.Ct (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) ¿ 

"The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence." (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party's subjective intent to create burden or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to respond. (See West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, no such evidence is necessary where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face. (See Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)

B.     Discussion

 

Plaintiff seeks an order from this Court compelling AHM’s further responses to Requests for Production of Documents Nos. 25-38, 44, and 46-47. The Court discusses and rules on each of the requests as follows:

 

The Amended Separate Statement.  The Court continued the hearing in part to obtain a truncated, bare-bones statement limited to just the discovery request, discovery response, and any amended or supplemental response, i.e., without briefing, without argument as to reasons why a further response should be compelled, and without regurgitating arguments made in a memorandum or points and authorities.  Plaintiff did amend and reduce the size of the separate statement, but still included page after page after page after page of reasons why a further response should or should not be compelled.  It is still 94 pages long, set for a continued hearing on a calendar with 19 other cases to be heard.  That is not what the Court requested.  To avoid further delay, the Court is proceeding with the merits of the motion regardless.  But in future, when a judge wants JUST or ONLY a very limited separate statement, counsel should respect that there is a very good reason why a trial court might make such a request in continuing a hearing. 

 

RFP Nos. 24-26: These requests ask AHM to provide documents evidencing their goals to reduce warranty costs, reduce the number of vehicles repurchased or replaced, and to reduce the costs associated with repurchases or replacements from 2020 to present. In response to each of these requests, AHM objected to the requests as vague, ambiguous, overly broad, unduly burdensome, and irrelevant.  Ostensibly, Plaintiff seeks background evidence to support one of its civil penalty theories which is that AHM has a policy or goal to save costs by declining to repurchase defective vehicles.  Further, Plaintiff seeks evidence bearing on its fraudulent concealment claim, i.e., that AHM knew that other customers had a similar malfunction with one of the 6 included symptoms in the definition of “Honda Sensing System.”  But the 3 RFPs at issue contain much more than that seemingly straightforward discovery effort.  The “costs associated” language could reasonably be interpreted to include efforts by the defendant to negotiate the expenses of its litigation counsel in all 50 states.  The goals to reduce buybacks could reasonably be interpreted to include quality improvements among outside vendors that may have caused buy-backs caused by faulty components as to systems or features of other Honda models and complaints that Plaintiff never reported. 

 

            The Court agrees with Defendant and denies the motion to compel these requests.  Not only the time range, but also the breadth of documents sought are overbroad and unreasonably burdensome, “taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2019.030(a)(2).)    Without the parties having narrowed the scope of the requested discovery, despite prior hearings where the Court clearly indicated that it would not grant the motion in its entirety, RFP Nos. 24-26 are DENIED.  

 

RFP Nos. 27-28, 34: These requests ask AHM to provide documents evidencing or describing the statistics for the number of repurchases and replacements AHM has made in California in response to consumers’ personal requests as well as the statistics for the number of service contracts AHM gave consumers in California in response to their personal requests for buyback or replacement from 2020 to present. Also, RFP No. 34 seeks documents from 2020 to present that reflect the relative frequency of Honda Sensing System repairs for 2020 Honda Insight Vehicles including, but not limited to, all such documents that reflect any comparison with the frequency of repairs to comparable transmissions in vehicles manufactured or distributed by AHM’s competitors In response to each of these requests, AHM objected to the requests as vague, ambiguous, overly broad, unduly burdensome, and irrelevant.  

            Here, the Court agrees that the requests are overly broad and unduly burdensome. A trial court is empowered to restrict the use of discovery methods by “taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2019.030(a)(2).)  Plaintiff’s moving papers do not provide the Court with a compelling reason as to how statistics evidencing documents relating to all vehicles sold by AHM would be relevant to the case at bar. This is not a class action case. Instead, this is a single-vehicle warranty plus fraudulent concealment case. RFP Nos. 27 and 28 are not even limited to the make, model, or year of the subject vehicle and instead seek documents that likely have nothing to do with the subject vehicle or the alleged defects present in the subject vehicle. For example, matters concerning AHM’s offer of service contracts to other customers with unknown repair histories would, in the Court’s view, lead into additional discovery as to why a given customer other than plaintiff was offered something that this Plaintiff was not offered, an inquiry into matters this Court, on the limited record presented here, finds to be beyond what is “reasonably calculated” to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.)  As such, the Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP Nos. 27 and 28 are DENIED but without prejudice to a more tailored RFP or RFPs concerning the same make, model and model year as the plaintiff’s vehicle.

 

With respect to RFP No. 34 and the “Honda sensing system” expansively defined with 6 sub-parts, the Court believes that more narrowly tailored document requests that focus on the exact symptoms this plaintiff reported in the instant vehicle’s repair history could be fashioned and would be reasonably calculated, but such narrowing has not been accomplished by the parties in the months since the first meet and confer letter was mailed in January.  Did this plaintiff experience each of the 6 examples of a manifestation of the claimed defect?  If so, Plaintiff could reasonably revise its discovery request and ask AHM to produce 6 separate categories of document consistent with the Court’s Tentative Ruling as to this and other RFPs in this set.   The Court would also suggest defining “relative frequency” and eliminating the reference to competitors’ vehicle.   

 

            Given the plaintiff’s discovery requests seeking evidence including emails of AHM employees generally and ESI relating to other vehicles besides plaintiff’s with any of the constellation of possible repair complaints not only in California but in any other state, this set of Requests is unduly burdensome, taking into account the needs of this case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in this litigation. See Code of Civil Procedure § 2019.030(a)(2). Thus, the Motion to Compel Further Responses as to RFP No. 34 is also DENIED, but without prejudice to a future narrowly tailored request or requests, as discussed above.

 

RFP Nos. 29-31, 35-36: These requests seek documents evidencing complaints, surveys, reports, and summaries from other 2020 Honda Insight vehicles of any “Honda Sensing Defects” as well as the tracking of vehicle repurchases made by AHM of 2020 Honda Insight vehicles allegedly containing “Honda Sensing Defects.” Further, these requests seek the aggregate warranty repair costs relating to “Honda Sensing Defects” from 2020 to present date. In response, AHM objected to these requests as vague, ambiguous, overly broad, harassing, and irrelevant. AHM further objected to the definition of “Honda Sensing Defect” and its subparts as overly broad and failing to describe with reasonable particularity the documents or categories of documents being requested.

 

            Here, the Court agrees that the definition of “Honda Sensing Defects,” is overly broad and would require AHM to produce an overly burdensome amount of documents, especially with the inclusion of what appears to be a compound definition. Plaintiff’s definition includes: “quality, design, engineering, materials, software, programming, and/or manufacturing problems or defects that impede the Honda Sensing System’s ability to reliably and accurately detect and respond to conditions on the roadway and which result in symptoms and malfunctions including: (1) numerous warning messages intermittently appearing on the vehicle’s instrument cluster alerting drivers to a problem inappropriately or falsely, (2) autonomous fluctuation of the vehicle’s highway speed without warning when adaptive cruise control is set, (3) a vehicle alert instructing drivers to apply brakes immediately even though no obstruction is present, (4) the vehicle independently applying brakes even though no obstruction is present, (5) vehicle alerts inaccurately alerting drivers that the vehicle is not driving within road lane markings, (6) the vehicle autonomously steering itself outside the lane of travel, and other similar concerns.”

 

            As noted above, there is no indication that the six specific sub-definitions were actual symptoms experienced in the subject vehicle. However, evidence of other customers’ complaints is a topic that can be reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence, both bearing on the existence of a warranty-covered defect, whether a reasonable number of repair opportunities were provided by Plaintiff in this case, the civil penalty claimed, and bearing on the fraudulent concealment cause of action.  As noted above with respect to RFP No. 34, the Court believes that more narrowly tailored document requests that focus on the exact symptoms this plaintiff reported in the instant vehicle’s repair history could be fashioned and that the Court would be inclined to require AHM to provide such discovery.  But since the parties failed to achieve a reasonable narrowing of the scope of what was requested here, the Court will rule on the language used by Plaintiff in this set of RFPs. 

 

While the full Motion to Compel Further Responses as to RFP Nos. 29-31, and 35-36 are DENIED, the Court will GRANT the motion in tightened, narrowed, and focused part as follows.  As to RFPs No. 29 and 30, if AHM has a report in existence or can generate a report from its warranty claim database as to the 1 to 6 defined specific sensing system complaints that Plaintiff herself reported to an authorized Honda dealer in the subject vehicle’s repair history, as to California buyers or lessees of a 2020 Honda Insight, AHM is order to produce a single report in chart or spreadsheet form of such complaints reported to AHM by a dealer in a warranty reimbursement request or warranty claim.  The report shall not identify any other customers by name, address, email address or other PII, but the report shall contain the VIN of each such vehicle, the name of the servicing dealership, the date of the repair visit, and the dealer’s identified cause and correction, for the time period of January 1, 2020 through the February 15, 2023 filing of the lawsuit in this case.  As to RFP 31, AHM shall provide a single report for that same time period containing a list of the VINs of any and every 2020 Honda Insight vehicles repurchased, reacquired, or replaced in California where Honda included the 1 to 6 defined specific sensing system complaints (that Plaintiff herself reported to an authorized Honda dealer in the subject vehicle’s repair history) in the reason or reasons the other vehicles were reacquired.  To those extents, the motion compel as to RFPs  Nos. 29-31, and 35-36 are GRANTED.  The motion as to those RFPs are otherwise DENIED. 

 

RFP No. 32, 44: These requests seek AHM to produce documents evidencing or describing the numbers of owners of 2020 Honda Insight vehicles who have complained of any of the conditions, defects, or nonconformities for which the subject vehicle was presented as well as the complaints by owners of 2020 Honda Insight vehicles regarding any of the complaints for which the subject vehicle was present for repair. In response, AHM objected to this request as vague, ambiguous, overly broad, unduly burdensome, harassing, and irrelevant.  The Court makes the same order here as it did for RFPs No. 29 and 30, i.e., AHM is to provide the single report detailed as to RFPs 29 and 30, but RFPs 32 and 44 are otherwise denied as overly broad and not reasonably calculated, “taking into account the needs of the case, the amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2019.030(a)(2).)  

 

RFP No. 33: This request seeks AHM to produce all documents evidencing quality problems identified by AHM in the Honda Sensing System used in 2020 Honda Insight vehicles. The undefined term “quality problems” is vague and ambiguous, but the Court will attempt to fashion an order to address the thrust of what Plaintiff reasonably seeks.  The Court GRANTS the motion to compel RFP No. 33 by requiring AHM to produce its Technical Service Bulletins, work instructions, diagnostic procedures, and/or Recall Notices applicable to  model year 2020 Honda Insight vehicles purchased in California, as to the 1 to 6 defined specific sensing system complaints that Plaintiff herself reported to an authorized Honda dealer in the subject vehicle’s repair history.  Except as expressly granted, the Court DENIES the motion as it relates to RFP No. 33.

 

RFP Nos. 37-38: These requests seek AHM to produce all documents evidencing the agendas and/or minutes of AHM’s board of directors or committee meetings, at which any concerns of the automatic transmissions in 2020 Honda Insight vehicles were addressed or discussed or where AHM’s warranty spending or reacquired vehicle spending pertaining to 2020 Honda Insight vehicles was addressed or discussed.

 

The Court agrees that the requests are overly broad, irrelevant, and unduly burdensome. Plaintiff’s motion does not provide the Court with a compelling reason as to how agenda or minute documents from board of director or committee meetings addressing the automatic transmission or AHM’s warranty spending or reacquired vehicle spending as they relate to 2020 Honda Insights generally would be relevant to the case at bar. RFP Nos. 37 and 38 are not even limited to the alleged defects actually reported by Plaintiff as to the subject vehicle, and have the potential to include a multiply of documents not relevant at all to the case at bar. As such, the Motion to Compel Further Responses to RFP Nos. 37-38 is DENIED.

 

RFP No. 46: This request seeks to compel AHM to produce all documents it produced in connection with the Cadena, et al. v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc., United States District Court, Central District of California, Case No. 2018- cv-04007 lawsuit which, was filed on May 14, 2018. The Motion is DENIED as to RFP No. 46.

 

RFP No. 47: This request seeks to compel AHM to produce all documents showing that AHM has a third-party dispute resolution process in California that is certified by the California Department of Consumer Affairs.  The Court will take oral argument as to the discoverability of a California Lemon Arbitration program, including a discussion of whether Plaintiff contends this is a “presumption” case, whether Honda contends that documents relating to any then-existing Honda arbitration program were provided with the subject vehicle at the point of sale, and other reasons why Plaintiff contends that production of correspondence rather than the certification of the program itself is discoverable in this case.

III. ANALYSIS

 

A.    Legal Standard  

 

“The primary function of a pleading is to give the other party notice so that it may prepare its case [citation], and a defect in a pleading that otherwise properly notifies a party cannot be said to affect substantial rights.” (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240.) “A¿demurrer¿tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Ivanoff v. Bank of America, N.A.¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 719, 725.) The Court looks to whether “the complaint alleges facts sufficient to state a cause of action or discloses a complete defense.” (Id.) The Court does not “read passages from a complaint in isolation; in reviewing a ruling on a demurrer, we read the complaint ‘as a whole and its parts in their context.’ [Citation.]” (West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 804.) The Court “assume[s] the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations, facts that reasonably can be inferred from those expressly pleaded and matters of which judicial notice has been taken.” (Harris, supra, 56 Cal.4th p. 240.) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) 

 

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92, subd. (c).) 

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.)  The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)  

 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Preliminarily, this Court notes that this is the third demurrer hearing as to Co-Trustees demurrers. As such, in the interest of brevity, the Court finds no reason to regurgitate the same level of analysis as its previous tentative rulings. Thus, the Court will provide an abridged analysis for Co-Trustees’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ SAC.

 

Breach of Contract

 

            In this Court’s March 21, 2024 tentative ruling, it indicated that it was not persuaded by Plaintiffs’ arguments, and that it did not interpret the provisions of the pledge agreement to mean what Plaintiffs are interpreting it to mean. The Court previously reasoned that paragraph 1, section (b) does not, in the Court’s view, have anything to do with the “upon the earlier” clause in section (a). Instead, this Court noted that paragraph 1 indicates that it is an agreement that the Donors jointly and severally agreed and irrevocably promised to give to HAPE: (a) Annual Pledge; and (b) Testamentary Pledge. The Court noted that this does not mean that the termination of the pledge via written notice from a donor terminating the pledge after the last date of the calendar year in which the notice is delivered (paragraph 1(a)(ii)) invalidates the Testamentary pledge found in paragraph 1(b). However, this Court noted that such a scenario was not being raised by Plaintiffs in the FAC. Instead, this Court noted that the issue Plaintiffs raised in their FAC was a single issue for a breach of contract and seeks to recover damages for the annual pledges under the terms of the written contract. As such, because this Court found that the use of the phrase “upon the earlier” presents a clear and unambiguous meaning of paragraph 1(a) – which the Court views as meaning the earlier of the two options laid out in paragraph 1(a)(i) and 1(a)(ii).

 

            In Defendants’ Demurrer, they argue that the parol evidence rule bars Plaintiffs’ evidence of prior negotiations and prior contemporaneous oral argument. Defendants argue that Plaintiff is not arguing that the pledge agreement is ambiguous, and instead, claim that the extrinsic evidence shows that the contract means something other than what the plain language of the contract states. The parol evidence rule generally prohibits the introduction of either oral or written extrinsic evidence to vary, alter, or add to the term of an integrated written agreement. (Casa Herrera, Inc. Beydoun (2004) 32 Cal.4th 336, 343 (“Casa Herrera”).) However, extrinsic evidence introduced to explain the meaning of a written contract is admissible if the interpretation of the contract urged is consistent with the document. (Ibid.) Moreover, “as a matter of substantive law [evidence that contradicts an integrated written contract] cannot serve to create or alter the obligations under the instrument.” (Id. at 344.) Thus, a written agreement supersedes any prior or contemporaneous negotiations, either oral or written. (Alling v. Universal Manufacturing Corp. (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1412, 1434 (“Alling”))

 

            Plaintiffs included documents which allegedly support their contractual interpretation, despite this Court noting that it views the language of the contract as clear and unambiguous. Exhibit C is entitled “Areas of Discussion – Future of the Automobile Driving Museum – Meeting with Stanley Zimmerman, Principal Sponsor – July 15, 2019.” Despite this Court’s noted interpretation of the clause in issue in the Pledge Agreement, Plaintiffs have maintained that “it was specifically discussed that the delivery of notice per paragraph 2 of the Pledge could only be delivered upon the death of the last Donor.” (SAC, ¶ 18.) Plaintiffs contend that this agenda was created in collaboration with Defendant, Stanley Zimmerman and with the Plaintiffs, not solely by Plaintiffs, and is has introductory paragraphs noting that the board members are looking to create an understanding between the Board and Stan Zimmerman, as to ongoing operations and expectation for short-term and long-term support of the ADM, to properly execute the Boards’ duties. The introductory paragraph noted that the Board’s current understanding of financial stability did not enable to Board to properly plan strategy and execution for more than 6 months to 1 year, and was followed by six (6) questions.

 

            The Court is confused as to how the above is the evidence Plaintiffs claim proves their interpretation of the contract. Specifically, exhibit C, is disputed by the parties to be a “collaborative” document. At first blush, the Court notes that it appears to be questions listed by the Board (per the introductory paragraphs) to ask Stan Zimmerman prior to signing the Pledge Agreement. As noted above, written agreements supersede any prior or contemporaneous negotiations, either oral or written. (Alling v. Universal Manufacturing Corp. (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1412, 1434.) There is no indication that this document is anything different than what it appears to be, i.e., negotiation questions that the Board took into the meeting with Stan Zimmerman.  Exhibit C is not signed by defendants and does not indicate that it was drafted by or in collaboration with the defendants.  Thus, Exhibit C would be barred by the parol evidence rule. Further, Plaintiffs’ opposition brief discusses each of the questions and provides answers to them. However, the document itself does not represent any of what Plaintiffs’ believed answers to the questions stand for.

 

            Next, Exhibit B is a letter sent from Jay and Thomas Zimmerman, which is entitled “RE: Notice of Termination of Annual Pledge.”   Exhibit B states that Stanley Zimmerman had passed away in July of that year (2020), and further notes that Defendants are providing written notice terminating the pledge pursuant to paragraph 1. Plaintiffs’ opposition brief argues that this notice of termination impeaches Defendants’ argument that they could unilaterally terminate the Pledge Agreement. Plaintiffs argue that this exhibit is evidence that the defendants provided notice terminating the Pledge Agreement based upon the death of Stanley Zimmerman, not on the reading of a bilateral contract. Plaintiffs contend that Defendants’ Notice of Termination admits now that the death of Stanley was the precipitating factor to terminate the Pledge Agreement thus confirming Plaintiffs’ theory on interpreting the Pledge Agreement.

 

            Plaintiffs appear to have disregarded this Court’s interpretation of the language in the Pledge Agreement to be both clear and unambiguous.  The Court notes that the new arguments made in the SAC, as well as the arguments made in the opposition brief as to Exhibits B and C, seem to be unilateral interpretations of documents by Plaintiffs. For example, the Notice of Termination does not state that the pledge is being terminated because of Stanley Zimmerman’s death in reference to paragraph 1(a)(i). It certainly mentions that Stanley Zimmerman passed away, however, this was written notice of terminating the Pledge Agreement. Plaintiffs’ attempted use of Exhibit B would also violate the parol evidence rule as it does not seek to clarify an alleged ambiguity (where the Court has not found one), but instead seeks to speculate the purpose of Defendants’ sending of that notice. The Court is no persuaded by Plaintiff’s argument.

 

            As such, the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend. It is clear that Plaintiffs do not have any evidence to support their interpretation of a contract this Court has already found to be unambiguous, and interpreted differently than how Plaintiffs have asked this Court to interpret it.

As to the Second Cause of Action for Negligent Misrepresentation, this Court overruled the demurrer as to the harassing neighbor, but sustained the demurrer as to the parking and electrical/appliances.  As to the Third Cause of Action for Fraudulent Concealment, the Court sustained the demurrer for failure to sufficiently allege duty.   As to Sixth Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, this Court sustained the demurrer.   As to the Seventh Cause of Action for Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability, this Court sustained the demurrer.  Lastly, as to the Eighth Cause of Action for Unlawful Self-Help by Landlord in Violation of Civil Code § 789.3, this Court overruled the demurrer.

B. Procedural¿¿ 

¿ 

On May 23, 2024, Demurring Defendants filed their demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint. On June 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition brief. On June 18, 2024, Demurring Defendants filed a reply brief.

1.     This Court’s Minute Order for Defendants’ Demurrer to the Complaint, filed April 2, 2024. (Exhibit A); and

III. ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

A. Legal Standard

¿ 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer for uncertainty may lie if the failure to label the parties and claims renders the complaint so confusing defendant cannot tell what he or she is supposed to respond to.¿ (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.) However, “[a] demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)¿¿ 

 

B. Discussion

 

            Demurring Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s FAC on the grounds they argue her first, second, third, and seventh causes of action fail to state sufficient facts to state a cause of action against them and are uncertain. Further, Demurring Defendants seek an order dismissing the sixth cause of action against them because they argue Plaintiff agreed to dismiss it.

 

            Preliminarily, this Court notes that its previous minute order analyzes each cause of action in great detail. Because the Court has previously provided such great detail, in the interest of brevity, this Court notes that it will be abridging its analysis to determine whether Plaintiff’s FAC has cured the previously identified deficiencies.

 

Intentional Misrepresentation

Here, Demurring Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s cause of action for Intentional misrepresentation remains unrepaired as to the issues causing the Court to sustain the prior demurrer in part. Previously, this Court found that Plaintiff sufficiently pled intentional misrepresentation as to the harassing neighbor component of her pleading. However, the Court noted that for the alleged parking spot misrepresentation as well as the alleged electrical/appliance misrepresentation, Plaintiff had failed to sufficiently allege intentional misrepresentation. As such, the Court only analyzes the first cause of action in connection with the parking and electrical/appliance issues.  

Parking Misrepresentation

Demurring Defendants again ask the Court to take judicial notice of their cross-complaint which contained the lease. The lease indicates that in paragraph 7A, there is a 2-car garage, parking in the front of the home and side garage is in an easement, and that the easement can be utilized for temporary parking but needs to be readily accessible. In Plaintiff’s previous opposition brief, she argued that the fact that the lease said that there was an easement did not explain why Pinky continued to state that Plaintiff would be able to park the third vehicle in the driveway as the Complaint had alleged. However, this Court noted that the complaint failed to contain allegations of continuing misrepresentation about the parking sport after the signing of the lease. Further, this Court also noted that although Plaintiff was alleging that oral representations were made, contrary to the express language of the lease, Plaintiff nonetheless signed the lease. Lastly, with Plaintiff’s allegation that the nearest street parking was a considerable distance away, this Court noted that the complaint did not contain any allegation that any defendant promised the street parking would be immediately in front of the home or that the street parking was available within a block or similar claim. 

Plaintiff’s FAC now alleges that prior to signing the lease, she made it clear that she would not be able to move into the Subject Property, under any conditions, unless there was space to park three vehicles. (FAC, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff alleges that both Pinky and Kawata assuaged Plaintiff’s concerns by claiming that although the driveway in front of the subject property is on an easement, Plaintiff and her family would be able to park the third vehicle “on the side of the Subject Property next to where the trash cans are.” (FAC, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff suggests that this representation was made by both Kawata and Pinky and was made by them both on June 19, 2023, when Plaintiff signed the lease, as well as after plaintiff signed the lease, and even after Plaintiff and her family began to occupy the subject property on or around July 3, 2023. (FAC, ¶ 21.)

Regardless of the fact that the lease clearly warned about the existence of the easement, continuing assurances may still amount to intentional misrepresentation.  In allegedly making the false representations, plaintiffs must allege the allegedly misrepresenting persons’  authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written. (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.) As such, Plaintiff may not merely add in an allegation that the misrepresentations continued from both Pinky and Kawata after signing the lease without more particularity. The only time frame Plaintiff provides is “after July 3, 2023.”   Plaintiff’s FAC states that she was told by Pinky, “through Searock”, on August 14, 2023, that she was not allowed to park the vehicle on the Subject Property. As such, the purported misrepresentations about 3 parking spots must have occurred between July 3 and August 14, 2023.  While not quoting verbatim what Pinky or Kawata said in those five weeks, the Court is satisfied at the pleading stage that sufficient specificity has been alleged. 

Electrical Issues

            Previously, this Court found that although Plaintiff had technically satisfied each of the elements of a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation, she did so in a conclusory way. Plaintiff’s complaint suggested that on July 1, 2023, representations were made to her regarding the electricity at the apartment and that such electricity and appliances were in good working order. However, this Court warned that Plaintiff would need to plead much more specific facts in order to state a cause of action for intentional misrepresentation, i.e., whether Plaintiff inquired as to the electrical and appliances? What question were asked by Plaintiff regarding the electrical and appliances, and what were the answers? This Court noted that these inquiries would be necessary and required to be pled in order for Plaintiff to maintain this cause of action as it relates to the electrical and appliances.      

            In Plaintiff’s FAC, she now alleges that before signing the lease, she “specifically asked the agent before she moved about the electrical system and the health of the appliances, specifically if she should expect any issues with the appliances upon moving into the Subject Property.” (FAC, ¶ 22.) Plaintiff notes that she had concerns because she was experiencing both refrigerator and stove top issues at her previous residence. (FAC, ¶ 22.)  Because of this, Plaintiff alleges that she “asked the agent Kawata if everything was in working order, meaning all of the appliances,” and that Kawata told her that she spoke to the owners [Searock, Pinky, and Ahmad], and the owners told her that, “everything was in working order,” and “she should have no issues,” and “if there were any issues, that they would fix it immediately.” (FAC, ¶ 22.)

            Demurring Defendants argue that these additional allegations are still entirely conclusory and did not cure the defects identified in this Court’s previous minute order nor answer the Court’s questions. The Court disagrees. Plaintiff was asked to identify questions she asked prior to moving in and the answers provided by Demurring Defendants. Plaintiff has done so here. A demurrer tests the sufficiency of the pleadings on their face. As pled, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged intentional misrepresentation as it relates to the electrical and appliances.  Thus, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first cause of action for intentional misrepresentations as to the parking spaces and electrical/appliance issues.

Negligent Misrepresentation

Here, this Court’s ruling as to this cause of action, and the first cause of action is the same, for the same reasons despite the slightly different elements. As such, the demurrer is OVERRULED.

Fraudulent Concealment

In the FAC, Plaintiff has added the allegations that Searock, Pinky, and Ahmad had a duty to disclose the following material facts to Plaintiff: (1) the danger Murat would pose to Plaintiff and her family; (2) the parking and electrical system because Searock, Pinky, and Ahmad are the owners of the Subject Property, and the material facts concealed from Plaintiff were not known to Plaintiff nor could they be reasonably ascertained by Plaintiff, and these facts were only known to Searock, Pinky, Amad, and Kawata. (FAC, ¶ 32.) Further, Plaintiff alleges that Kawata had a duty to disclose because her office was acting as a dual agent, and was supposed to act in the best interest of Plaintiff as well, but did not. (FAC, ¶ 32.) Plaintiff alleges that Kawata had a fiduciary relationship with plaintiff, meaning she was under a duty to fully disclose all material facts that were concealed from plaintiff. Plaintiff further alleges the following material facts which were allegedly concealed: (1) the dangerous, violent, and harassing propensities of the neighbor Murat; (2) the faulty and dangerous electrical wiring that resulting in a power outage for three days, with continued issues; (3) that the electrical wiring does not conform to code; and (4) that the fact that there is no functioning oven on the premises, making it nearly impossible for Plaintiff and her family to enjoy homecooked meals. (FAC, ¶ 56.)

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged fraudulent concealment.  Plaintiff cured the previously identified deficiencies.  The Demurrer to the third cause of action is thus OVERRULED.

Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

Previously, this Court ruled that the original complaint failed to allege how the electrical issue in this case is a breach of the implied warranty of habitability. The Court gave the example of Plaintiff not describing the consequence or effect of the habitability concerns. Had Plaintiff been unable to keep food refrigerated? Had Plaintiff been unable to use cooking amenities? Had Plaintiff’s air conditioning or heating gone out? Without more, this Court noted that Plaintiff could not maintain a cause of action for Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability.

Plaintiff has now added that Demurring Defendants breached the implied warranty of habitability because the oven did not function, and electricity in most areas of the home was non-existent. (FAC, ¶ 25.) For example, Plaintiff notes the electric/appliance issues she was: (1) unable to use any cooking amenities, which were all located downstairs, meaning her family was unable to cook; (2) unable to use the front and back patio lights which presented a safety concern because of Murat’s alleged relentless harassment, torment, and nuisance; (3) that she and her family were unable to go to the restroom downstairs without a flashlight, and were forced to walk around the subject property without flashlights at night; (4) that plaintiff had to purchase a 120 foot extension cord, plug it in upstairs in an area where the electricity was actually working, and take the power from upstairs to the downstairs; (5) that she and her family could not shower at night because of the lack of electricity; (6) that eventually the lights upstairs failed to work as well; and (7) that Plaintiff was forced to discard hundreds of dollars’ worth of perishable groceries when the electricity completely went out on the subject property. (FAC, ¶¶ 25, 97.)

            Again, Demurring Defendants argue that Plaintiff has again alleged such allegations in a conclusory way and failed to answer the Court’s questions, however, the Court disagrees. Plaintiff has provided allegations the Court noted would cure the deficiencies of her previous pleading.  Whether Plaintiff can actually prove these allegations is a question better decided on a Motion for Summary Judgment. Thus, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the sixth cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability.

III. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.  Defendants are to file an Answer to the FAC within 20 days.

An appeal bond or undertaking by defendant’s liability insurer to stay the part of a judgment within the policy limits is effective to stay enforcement of that part of the judgment.  There is no other stay of enforcement besides to the extent of the undertaking

 

                                                 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On March 1, 2021, Plaintiffs, Jesse Franklin Esphorst, an individual, and as successor-in-interest to The Estate of Jesse Eric Esphorst, deceased (Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants, Tung Ming, Kwan Cheung, Teddyland, LLC, and DOES 1 through 50. On October 16, 2018, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Wrongful Death – Negligence; (2) Negligence Per Se; and (3) Dangerous Condition of Public Property (Government Code § 835).

 

On January 26, 2024, this Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiffs and against Defendant, Tung Ming, in the amount of $58,762,500 plus costs and interest. Post-trial, this Court reduced the non-economic damages by 20%. Accordingly, on April 26, 2024, the Court entered an Amended Judgment against Defendant, Tung Ming, in the amount of $47,410,000 plus costs and interest.

 

On May 3, 2024, Defendant, Tung Ming filed a Notice of Appeal. On May 14, 2024, Defendant, Tung Ming and insurer served a document entitled “undertaking on Appeal” which purports to be an undertaking in the amount of $60,000. (Plaintiff’s Moving Papers, Exhibit 1.) Because of this, Plaintiff, Jesse Franklin Esphorst has filed this objection to the undertaking of $60,000 given by Defendant Tung Ming and insurer, State Farm Fire and Casualty Company on May 14, 2024, as insufficient to stay enforcement of the judgment because it is less than one and a half times the amount of the Judgment (Code Civ. Proc., §917.1) Jesse Franklin Esphorst notes that this Court has entered judgment against Tung Ming in the amount of $47,410,000 plus costs and interest. As such, Jesse Franklin Esphorst argues that an amount that would be sufficient is estimated to be $71,115,000.

 

B. Procedural¿¿ 

 

On May 17, 2023, Plaintiff, Jesse Franklin Esphorst filed this Objection to Defendant Tung Ming’s Undertaking and Motion for an Order that Sufficient Bond be Given. On May 21, 2024, Plaintiff, Julie A. Esphorst filed a joinder to this motion.

On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff, Jesse Franklin Esphorst filed a rely brief. On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff, Julie A. Esphorst filed a joiner to that reply brief.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.    Legal Standard 

 

An appeal of a trial court order involving the payment of money does not stay enforcement of the order unless the appellant posts an undertaking.  (Code Civ. Proc., §917.1(a)(1).)  Costs awarded by the trial court under Code of Civil procedure section 1021 et seq. shall be included in the order.  (Code Civ. Proc. §917.1(d).)  If the order is affirmed or the appeal withdrawn or dismissed, the party ordered to pay shall pay the amount of the order, together with any interest which may have accrued pending the appeal and costs which may be awarded against the appellant on appeal.  (Code Civ. Proc. §917.1(b).)  The undertaking posted shall be for double the amount of the order unless given by an admitted surety in which event it shall be for one and one-half times the amount of the order. (Code Civ. Proc. §917.1(b).)The liability may be enforced if the party ordered to pay does not do so within 30 days after the remittitur is filed in the lower court. (Code Civ. Proc. §917.1(b).) 

 

An objection to a bond must be made within ten days after service of a copy of the bond on the beneficiary.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.930(b).)  “If no objection is made within the time required by statute, the beneficiary is deemed to have waived all objections except upon a showing of good cause for failure to make the objection within the time required by statute or of changed circumstances.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §995.930(c).)

 

Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 996.010(a) provides: “If a bond is given in an action or proceeding, the court may determine that the bond is or has from any cause become insufficient because the sureties are insufficient or because the amount of the bond is insufficient.”  Motions challenging the sufficiency of a bond are deemed objections to the bond.  (Code Civ. Proc. §996.010(b).)  If the court determines the bond to be insufficient, it shall order that a new, additional, or supplemental bond be given within a reasonable time not less than five days.  (Code Civ. Proc. §996.010(c).) Should a bond become insufficient during the case, Code of Civil Procedure section 996.010 enables a court to make that determination and order a sufficient new or supplemental bond.  (Grant v. Superior Court (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 929, 938.) The statute reflects a common sense view that the sufficiency of the undertaking should be assessed in light of those sums which it may be required to satisfy, not in light of the formula for initially setting the amount of the undertaking.  (Ibid.)  The statute’s purpose is to guard against a bond or undertaking failing to serve its original purpose. (Ibid.) 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Here, the Court notes that Plaintiff timely objected in writing to the $60,000 undertaking as insufficient to stay the enforcement of judgment during Defendant Tung Ming’s appeal. However, the case of Merritt v. J.A. Stafford Co. (1968) 68 Cal.2d 619 (“Merritt”), relied upon by Defendant, Tung Ming and Intervenor, State Farm, and distinguished by Plaintiffs’ reply brief is the crux of the issue here.

 

In Merritt, plaintiff had recovered against defendant, the J.A. Staffing Company, a corporation, and one of its employees for $434,441.68 for personal injuries suffered in a truck accident, and Merritt’s employer recovered judgment for $21,255.93 against Stafford and its employee for property damage. (Merritt, supra, 68 Cal.2d at 621.) Subsequently, Stafford and its employee filed a notice of appeal, and on May 22, 1964, Stuyvesant Insurance Company filed an “undertaking for Appeal Bond.” (Ibid.) The filing indicated that Stuyvesant was obliged itself ‘under said statutory obligations’ in the sum of $181,883.90. The judgement was affirmed, and the remittitur was filed on October 13, 1965. (Ibid.) Stuyvesant and Reserve Insurance Company filed an action for declaratory relief alleging that Reserve had issued to Stafford a liability insurance policy providing that the limit for injury to any one person was $100,000 and that the company shall pay ‘all costs and all interests accruing after entry of judgment until the company has paid or tendered or deposited in court such part od such judgment as does not exceed the limit of the company’s liability thereon. (Ibid.) The trial court then issued a temporary restraining order in the declaratory relief action enjoining Merritt from seeking an ex parte judgment against Stuyvesant but providing that upon notice Merritt could move for judgment on the appeal bond. (Id. at 622.)

 

Subsequently, Merritt noticed a motion for judgment on the appeal bond, alleging that no  part of the judgment in the personal injury action had been paid. (Ibid.) The motion for judgment on the appeal bond was denied and was followed by an appeal to the California Supreme Court. (Ibid.) The Supreme Court held “that a bond furnished by a liability insurer to stay the part of a judgment within the policy limits is effective to stay enforcement of that part of the judgment.” (Id. at 625.) The Merritt Court reasoned that “[p]rotection of the right of appeal of insurers, and consideration of the rights of insureds and of judgment creditor require such a result. Liability insurance is ordinarily written with limits, and when a judgment is obtained in excess of those limits, an insurer should not be faced with the alternatives of either posting a bond for the entire judgment or refusing to pose a bond at all.” (Id. at 626.)

 

Here, the declaration of Ming’s attorney, Allison L. Grandy, indicates that the bond is being furnished by Ming’s insurer and reflects the coverage limits of Ming’s policy. (Declaration of Allison L. Grandy (“Grandy Decl.”), ¶ 2.) As such, pursuant to the Merritt decision, State Farm is allowed, in the interest of public policy, to post a bond reflecting the limits of Ming’s insurance policy, and which is in turn allowed to stay the part of the judgment within the policy limits as to that part of the judgment.

 

The Merritt Court further reasoned that an “insurer cannot be required to post a bond for the entire judgment when its liability does not extend to the entire judgment. Fairness to the insurer and the insured requires that the insurer be permitted to fulfill its covenant of good faith and fair dealing by filing an appeal bond in an amount sufficient to cover part of the judgment for which it is liable and that the respondent be denied his right to seek execution with regard to such part of the judgment. Such a rule does not harm to the respondent. As to the excess part of the judgment he may seek execution or enter into an agreement to stay execution with the insured, and as to the part of the judgment within the policy limits he will be protected by the bond.” (Merritt, supra, 68 Cal.2d at 626.) “In other words, where there is a judgment in excess of the policy limits, the insurer and the insured have separate and differing interests; the insurer may furnish a bond for the portion of the judgment within the policy limits, and the bond will be given effect pending appeal to stay execution on that portion of the judgment.” (Ibid.)

 

Thus, State Farm is allowed to post a bond for $60,000 of its potential liability, and would stay that portion of the judgment, not the entirety of the awarded judgment to Plaintiff. As such, like the plaintiff/respondent in Merritt, Plaintiffs here will not be burdened by a stay on appeal for the entirety of the judgment for which they were awarded. Plaintiff’s arguments on why Merritt is distinguishable enough from the case at bar are not persuasive as to this Court’s tentative ruling that only $60,000 of Plaintiff, Jesse Esphorst’s judgment amount will be stayed pending appeal, not the rest of the $47,350,000.

 

            As such, this Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to object to the undertaking amount and motion for order that sufficient bond be given.

           

IV. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.
