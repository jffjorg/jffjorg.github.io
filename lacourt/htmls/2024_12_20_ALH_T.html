DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV23135    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: T

Demurrer and Motion to Strike

Rogelio Cerna v. George Warthan, et al.
Case No. 21STCV23135
Moving Party: Defendants George Allen Warthan; Allen Warthan; Horizontal Grading & Paving; And Horizontal Paving
Responding Party: Plaintiff Rogelio Cerna
Tentative Ruling: SUSTAINED/GRANTED

BACKGROUND 

This is a three-vehicle collision case arising from a July 18, 2019 accident. Plaintiff alleges causes of action for (1) Motor Vehicle Negligence; (2) Negligence per se; and (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress and Further Physical Damage, violation of Vehicle Code § 20001 (Hit and Run) against the named Defendants. (Opposition Memorandum of Points and Authorities, p. 1.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendants argue Plaintiff's exemplary damage claim in the First-Amended Complaint (“FAC”) fails as a matter of law. (Defendants’ Memorandum of Points and Authorities, p. 3.) Defendants also argue the complaint fails to set forth facts to constitute a cause of action, specifically with respect to Plaintiff’s third cause of action is for intentional tort. (Demurrer, p. 5.)

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff argues sufficient facts have been alleged to support punitive damages. (Opposition Memorandum of Points and Authorities, p. 3.) Plaintiff additionally argues he alleged facts sufficient to support the third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress. (Opposition, p. 8.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Demurrer and Motion to Strike

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) 

Plaintiff has not alleged facts sufficient to support the third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress against the corporate entities.  Plaintiff fails to allege that any of the individual drivers were employed by or were agents of the corporate entity defendants or that they were acting within the scope of that employment or agency.  (Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons, Inc. (1986) 41 Cal.3d 962, 967.)  Plaintiff also fails to allege that the corporate entity defendants knew or should have known that their employees/agents were unfit or were likely to engage in the intentional torts alleged herein.  (Evan F. v. Hughson United Methodist Church (1992) 8 Cal.App.4th 828, 836.)  As such, the demurrer is sustained as to the corporate entity defendants.

California law generally allows plaintiffs to plead inconsistent causes of action in separate counts when the exact nature of the facts or the legal rights are in doubt. (Batta v. Hunt (2024) 106 Cal.App.5th 295.) This practice accommodates uncertainties about what actually occurred or what can be established by the evidence. (Id.) However, there are limitations. A plaintiff may not avoid a demurrer by pleading facts or positions in an amended complaint that contradict the facts pleaded in the original complaint or by suppressing facts that prove the pleaded facts false (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp. (1992) 4 Cal.App.4th 857; McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457.) “Under the sham pleading doctrine, allegations in an original pleading that rendered it vulnerable to demurrer or other attack cannot simply be omitted without explanation.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 751.) “The purpose of the doctrine is to enable the courts to prevent an abuse of process.” (Id.) “The doctrine is not intended to prevent honest complainants from correcting erroneous allegations or to prevent the correction of ambiguous facts.” (Id.)

The Court finds the allegations in the FAC to be sufficiently contradictory to require sustaining the demurrer to the third cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  This finding relies, in part, on the allegations in Plaintiff’s original complaint and the FAC which both allege that defendant failed “to immediately stop his vehicle” and that it “was evident that defendant had no intention of stopping and identifying himself.”  [Complaint and FAC, Para. 20.]  However, while the Complaint originally alleged that “[a]fter the crash, Defendants failed to stop at the location” [Complaint, Para. 21], the FAC now alleges that “[a]fter the crash, Defendants stopped at the location” and “Defendant realized plaintiff was injured and needed medical assistance and despite same, defendant, got back in his car and drove away from scene [sic].”  [FAC, Para. 21]

Plaintiff’s FAC is clearly internally inconsistent, for Defendant can’t both have failed to stop and stopped.  Additionally, the allegations in the FAC directly conflict with the allegations in the Complaint.  Further, it appears that Plaintiff is now alleging physical injuries from following the hit-and-run driver in an attempt to conform his claims with the prior ruling of this Court. [See the January 23, 2024 Minute Order discussing the need to plead “additional damages above and beyond the damages caused by the accident that precedes the hit and run.”]  Based on these inconsistencies and what appears to be a sham amendment designed solely to comport with this Court’s prior order, the Court sustains the demurrer as to all defendants.
    
Because this ruling eliminates the intentional tort on which punitive damages are alleged, the Court also grants the Motion to Strike the punitive damages claims.  

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer as to Plaintiff’s third cause of action is SUSTAINED.  Absent a declaration under penalty of perjury by Plaintiff’s counsel establishing a good faith belief that there is a factual basis for the amendments, the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

Defendants’ motion to strike Plaintiff’s claims for punitive damages is GRANTED.

Case Number: 21STCV37991    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Further Responses

Zenya Dennis v. Adam and Lilith, Business Entity Unknown

Moving Party: Defendant Green Cosmos Inc.
Responding Party: Plaintiff Zenya Dennis
Tentative Ruling: GRANTED

BACKGROUND

This is a premises liability dispute in which Plaintiff allegedly went into a store intending to purchase an item and was stabbed by Defendant’s employee. (Complaint, p. 4.)

On August 7, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff a first set of Form Interrogatories and first and second set of Special Interrogatories via email. On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff served responses to the first set of Form Interrogatories, the first set of Special Interrogatories and the second set of Special Interrogatories. On October 28, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff an email seeking to meet and confer regarding Plaintiff’s perceived failure to fully respond to specific Form and Special Interrogatories — specifically Form Interrogatory No. 10.2 and Special Interrogatory No. 11 — and demanded full and complete responses. (See Motion to Compel Further, Exhibit A.) On November 12, 2024, Defendant emailed to follow up on the October 28th letter, and again requesting an extension for filing a motion to compel to further meet and confer on the issues. (See id., Exhibit B.) On November 15, 2024, Defendant called to meet and confer and/or seek an extension of the motion to compel filing deadline. 

As of the filing of this motion, Defendant has not received additional responses to its propounded Form Interrogatories and first set of Special Interrogatories, nor a response to Defendant’s meet and confer communications.
 
MOVING PARTY POSITION

Defendant argues that ordering Plaintiff to provide further responses to its propounded Form Interrogatories and first set of Special Interrogatories is appropriate. (See Motion to Compel Further, p. 4.)

Defendant also requests sanctions for the fees and costs of having to prepare, file and attend the hearing for this motion in the amount of $1,480. (Id. at 5.)

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues Defendant did not meet and confer in good faith. (Opposition, p. 3.) Additionally, Plaintiff argues the propounded responses to Defendant’s Interrogatories was sufficient. (Id. at 4.) 

Plaintiff further asserts Defendant’s misconduct should lead to sanctions. (Id. at 6.)

REPLY

No reply has been filed.

ANALYSIS

I. Motion to Compel Further Responses

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290; 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) The propounding party may file motions to compel further responses to requests for production if it believes the responses received are (1) evasive, (2) incomplete, or (3) if the objections raised are meritless or overly general. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(a).) In response to a motion to compel further responses, the respondent bears the burden of justifying any objections. (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

A. Meet and Confer

Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.300 requires that prior to filing a motion to compel the moving party must follow the meet and confer requirements of CCP § 2016.040. “The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.) This means that the parties must attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate. (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1294.) The purpose of the meet and confer requirement is to bridge the gap between the parties – to force lawyers to reexamine their positions, and to narrow their discovery disputes to the irreducible minimum, before calling upon the court to resolve the matter. (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.)

In this case, the Court finds Defendant has made sufficient good faith efforts to meet and confer prior to raising the instant motion. On October 28, 2024, Defendant sent Plaintiff an email seeking to meet and confer regarding Plaintiff’s perceived failure to fully respond to specific Form and Special Interrogatories and demanded full and complete responses. (See Motion to Compel Further, Exhibit A.) Defendant emailed once again on November 12, 2024 raising these concerns. (See id., Exhibit B.) Finally, Defendant called to meet and confer on November 15, 2024. However, these attempts by Defendant to meet and confer were unavailing, and reflect Defendant’s attempts to informally resolve the instant discovery dispute prior to seeking the Court’s intervention. On this record, the Court finds Defendant “made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution” to the discovery disputes at issue here.  (Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431, 1435.) The Court therefore concludes the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied, and considers the motion on the merits.

B. Good Cause

A “party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2031.010. For the purposes of discovery, information is “relevant to the subject matter” if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof. (Gonzalez v. Super. Ct. (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546; Lipton v. Super. Ct. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1599, 1611.)

Admissibility does not control the scope of discovery. (Id.) Instead, the “scope of discovery extends to any information that reasonably might lead to other evidence that would be admissible at trial.” (Id. at 1612.) Any doubts as to relevance should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Colonial Life & Acc. Ins. Co. v. Super. Ct. (1982) 31 Cal.3d 785, 790.) Objections based on relevancy and materiality to the issues of the case “cannot be used to deny discovery.” (Coy v. Super. Ct. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 217.) 

Form Interrogatory No. 10.2 seeks all physical, mental, and emotional disabilities Plaintiff had immediately prior to the incident. Plaintiff’s response expressed that Plaintiff “experienced emotional injury but not more than any other person that was assaulted and stabbed and almost died would experience.” Defendant argues that, because Plaintiff is seeking to recover for emotional injury or distress as a result of the incident, Defendant is entitled to discovery on information about her mental and emotional state prior to the incident. (Motion to Compel Further, Exhibit A.) The Court here agrees with Defendant that Plaintiff’s physical, mental, and emotional health is at issue. While Plaintiff claims not to seek damages “more than any other person that was assaulted and stabbed and almost died would experience,” the nature of Plaintiff’s alleged assault and near-death injury inherently alleges damages beyond that of a garden variety emotional or physical injury. (See, generally, Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833 and Doyle v. Superior Court (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1878.) Defendant is accordingly entitled to discovery on this issue.

Special Interrogatory No. 11 seeks all health care providers who has treated Plaintiff during the past 10 years, including the dates and type of treatment received. Plaintiff objected on the bases of relevance and invasion of privacy. Defendant asserts that “[e]ven if… [Plaintiff’s] emotional injury is ‘not more than any other person that was assaulted and stabbed and almost died would experience,’” Defendant is still entitled to medical records of her past treatment relating to emotional injuries. (Motion to Compel Further, Exhibit A.) The Court agrees in part.  Defendant requests Plaintiff’s ten-year medical history - a vast amount of information about Plaintiff’s medical conditions that likely no relation to the injury sued upon. Such a broad request lies beyond the scope of discovery. While the right to privacy is not absolute, (Palay v. Superior Court (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 919, 933) Plaintiff is entitled to retain the confidentiality of unrelated medical treatment. (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 864.)  Accordingly, The Court grants the motion as to Special Interrogatory No. 11, but limits the scope to the five year period prior to the injury to the current date.  

Accordingly, the Court finds Defendant has established good cause with respect to Request No. 10.2 for physical, mental, and emotional disabilities Plaintiff had immediately prior to the incident. This information directly pertains and is relevant to evaluating Plaintiff’s damages as a result of the alleged attack and injuries, which go beyond the garden variety. The Court finds Defendant’s Special Interrogatory No. 11 is overbroad and therefore grants the motion but limits the period to five years before the incident to the current date.  

II. Sanctions

The court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party on the motion to compel unless it finds that party acted “with substantial justification” or other circumstances render sanctions “unjust.” CCP §2030.290(c). 

The Court here declines to grant sanctions against either party. While the Court concludes it is appropriate to grant Defendant’s motion to compel further responses, Plaintiff here had substantial justification to object to Defendant’s motion. Namely, the privacy interests at stake in this case, and the validity of Plaintiff’s objection to the overreaching elements of Defendant’s discovery requests reflect Plaintiff was substantially justified to object, despite being required to produce further responses. The Court is therefore inclined not to grant sanctions in this case, as doing so would be unjust.

RULING

Defendant’s motion to compel further is GRANTED as to Form Interrogatory No. 10.2 and Defendant’s motion to compel further is GRANTED as to Special Interrogatory No. 11 but limits the period to five years before the incident to the current date.  Supplemental responses to be served within 30 days.  

Defendant and Plaintiff’s requests for sanctions are DENIED. 



Case Number: 24NNCV02431    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: T

Demurrer

Carson Goring v. Masis Khodaverdian

Moving Party: Defendant Masis Khodaverdian
Responding Party: Plaintiff Carson Goring
Tentative Ruling: SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE

BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Carson Goring was a former tenant of a property owned by Defendant. (See Declaration of Christine D. Luong-Pham (“Luong-Pham Dec.”) ¿ 2.) On August 14, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant, case number 23BBCV01856, in the Los Angeles Superior Court. (See Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice (“Req. for Jud. Ntc.”) ¿ 1, Ex. A.) Plaintiff subsequently filed an Amended Complaint on September 18, 2023. (Id. at ¿ 2, Ex. B.) The Amended Complaint alleges general negligence, premises liability, and harassment violating Civil Code section 1940.2. (Id.) Plaintiff also alleged wage loss; loss of use of property; hospital and medical expenses; general damage; loss of earning capacity; loss of peace, severe and ongoing illness, and emotional damage. (Id.) Additionally, the Amended Complaint stated “30 counts of punishable harassment, trauma suffered, and loss of voucher amount due to inhabitability.” (Id.)


MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s complaint is precluded by res judicata and collateral estoppel. (Demurrer, p. 5.) Additionally, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s complaint fails to meet the pleading requirements under the code of civil procedure. (Id. at 6.) 

OPPOSITION 

Plaintiff, represented pro per, asserts the previous complaint was dismissed due to Plaintiff’s unfamiliarity, as he was “unsure how to write a legal claim,” and “begs lenience with learning the law” given his personal circumstances, including homelessness. (Opposition, p. 1.) 

Plaintiff argues the instant action is a separate legal action from his prior case against Defendant, and that the instant case alleges distinct causes of action. Plaintiff asserts this action alleges ongoing fraud and seeks to recover for Plaintiff’s ongoing homelessness, effects of carbon monoxide poisoning, and emotional suffering from Defendant’s constant threats. (Opposition, p. 20.)

REPLY

Defendant argues Plaintiff admits the previous action was dismissed, and that Plaintiff should be barred from proceeding with the current complaint, as Plaintiff seeks to “do over” her prior lawsuit. (Reply, p. 2.) Defendant also asserts that Plaintiff’s opposition is unresponsive to the issues raised in the demurrer. (Id.)

ANALYSIS 

I. Request for Judicial Notice

The Court takes judicial notice of the following:

• Plaintiff’s prior complaint against Defendant, case number 23BBCV01856. (Req. for Jud. Ntc., ¿ 1, Ex. A.)
• Plaintiff’s September 18, 2023 amended complaint. (Id. at Ex. B.)
• Defendant’s January 16, 2024 demurrer. (Id. at Ex. C.)
• Court ruling sustaining Defendant's demurrer. (Id. at Ex. D.)
• Court’s April 15, 2024 Order. (Id. at Ex. E.)
• Court’s July 2, 2024 judgement. (Id. at Ex. F.)
• July 11, 2024, Notice of Entry of Judgement. (Id. at Ex. G.)

II. Demurrer

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  When considering demurrers, courts assume the truth of the facts alleged in the pleading and read the allegations liberally and in context.  (Schifando v. City of Los Angeles (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1081.) A demurrer must be denied where the complaint states a cause of action "under any possible legal theory." (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) The court must “liberally” construe the complaint’s allegations "with a view to substantial justice." (Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer for sufficiency attacks a pleading on the ground that it does not contain facts sufficient to state a cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10 (e); Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  Parties may also demur to causes of action individually.  In either case, the procedure is also called a ‘demurrer for sufficiency,’ and it tests the pleading by itself, alone and disconnected from extraneous matters.  (Hahn at p. 747.) Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e) permits the filing of a demurrer when a pleading “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” Additionally, when a party cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are being directed against it, a demurrer for uncertainty lies. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f); Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

A. Estoppel


This is not the first case Plaintiff has brought against Defendant; this case is brought following the Burbank Court’s April 15, 2024 order dismissing Plaintiff’s case with prejudice after Plaintiff failed amend her previous complaint within the prescribed 20-day period. (Case No. 23BBCV01856, April 15 Minute Order.) 

The central issue in this case is whether Plaintiff’s present case against Defendant is estopped by Plaintiff’s prior first unsuccessful attempt to sue Defendant. The Court here declines to find that Plaintiff is estopped by the first action on the basis that the complaint in this case is insufficiently specific as to the causes of actions being alleged; as a result, the Court cannot meaningfully evaluate whether the causes of action in this complaint have already been adjudicated. The Court notes that the instant complaint here alleging a cause of action for fraud was not alleged in Plaintiff’s prior case against Defendant alleging harassment and “loss of voucher amount due to [un]inhabitability [sic].” (Req. for Jud. Ntc., Ex. B.) The instant case for fraud against Defendant may not necessarily arise from the same factual circumstances. While Plaintiff’s first case alleged harassment and uninhabitable conditions at Defendant’s property —  and thus concern the factual circumstances arising during Plaintiff’s tenancy — the instant complaint for fraud may concern Defendant’s conduct leading up to Plaintiff’s tenancy. Plaintiff here suggests the complaint arises from a breach of contract in which the “necessary elements to establish liability” include “duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (See Opposition, p. 22.) 

Liberally construing Plaintiff’s complaint’s allegations “with a view to substantial justice” (Code Civ. Proc. § 452), the Court cannot ascertain whether Plaintiff’s complaint might state a cause of action under a possible legal theory for fraud, nor whether Plaintiff’s cause of action relates to circumstances prior to or during the tenancy. Because Plaintiff’s cause of action here is impossible to discern, the Court cannot determine whether the complaint states a cause of action under a possible legal theory of liability. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.) As a result of the vague nature of Plaintiff’s complaint, the Court declines to sustain Defendant’s demurrer based on collateral estoppel.

However, Plaintiff’s complaint here is nevertheless insufficient, because it fails to plead sufficient facts. Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e) permits the filing of a demurrer when a pleading “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” When a party cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what claims are being directed against it, a demurrer for uncertainty lies. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f); Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) Here, Plaintiff’s complaint fails to meet the pleading requirements dictated by the Code of Civil Procedure. While Plaintiff does allege fraud against Defendant, Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to notify Defendant of what actions or representations Defendant made that constitute fraud. Because the allegation of fraud is too vague, the Court finds Defendant’s demurrer should be sustained—and Plaintiff afforded an opportunity to amend the complaint to provide specific allegations regarding the cause of action in this case. Based on Plaintiff’s additional allegations, the Court, in turn, can revisit the issue of whether Plaintiff is estopped.

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED. Plaintiff is granted 20 days leave to amend the complaint.

Case Number: 24NNCV03292    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: T

Demurrer

Terri Milner, et al. v. Los Angeles Unified School District

Moving Party: Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District
Responding Party: N/A
Tentative Ruling: SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE

BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Terri Milner attempts to bring this lawsuit for two incidents involving her daughter, minor Taya Lynn Jones, but Terri Milner does not have a license to practice law. (See Demurrer, Exhibit A.) 

MOVING PARTY POSITION 

Defendant argues the complaint is subject to demurrer and barred because Plaintiff Terri Milner, who is pro se on behalf of minor Taya Lynn Jones, is not an attorney, and prosecuting this case on her daughter’s behalf would constitute unauthorized practice of law. (Demurrer, p. 3.) 

Defendant also argues the causes of action are time-barred and lacks any statutory authority for liability. (Id. at 7-8.)

OPPOSITION 

No opposition has been filed.

ANALYSIS 

I. Demurrer

A demurrer is a procedure to object to a pleading set forth under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, et seq.  Because a demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters, a demurrer analysis considers only those defects which appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.  (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.)  A demurrer is proper if “[t]he person who filed the pleading does not have the legal capacity to sue.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(b).)

Terri Milner brings this lawsuit “Pro Se” and on behalf of Taya Lynn Jones, her daughter. In doing so, Terri Milner is engaging in the unauthorized practice of law, as she is attempting to represent her minor daughter as her attorney. Terri Milner lacks the legal capacity to sue on her daughter’s behalf; the complaint is barred for that reason. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(b).) In J.W. v. Superior Court ((1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 958, 967) the court held that a non-attorney appointed as a guardian ad litem for a minor cannot act “in pro per” as doing so would constitute the unauthorized practice of law. The court stated: “We hold that ... a nonattorney mother cannot represent her minor son as guardian ad litem in propria persona, even though she cannot afford counsel and her son is a necessary party to the proceeding.” (Id. at 961-962.) We reach this conclusion here. Plaintiff is a nonattorney who is attempting to represent another person in court proceedings — violating the prohibition against unauthorized practice of law, as she may not function as her attorney. (Id. at 961-962.) Defendant’s demurrer is appropriate on these grounds. (Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 6125, 6126; Code Civ. Proc. § 436(b).) 

Because there is no attorney representing minor Taya Lynn Jones, the Court declines to consider the merits of Defendant’s other arguments to sustain Defendant’s demurrer without leave to amend. The complaint is best addressed and considered on the merits after Plaintiff appoints a guardian ad litem on behalf of her daughter and the minor is represented by counsel.  

RULING 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend. 

Case Number: 24NNCV03704    Hearing Date: December 20, 2024    Dept: T

Motion To Deem RFAs Admitted

Bruce Wasserman v. Erez Karni, et al.

Moving Party: Plaintiff Bruce Wasserman
Responding Party: N/A
Tentative Ruling: GRANT

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff filed this case against Defendant Erez Kami (“Defendant”) on August 20, 2024. There is no answer filed to Plaintiff’s complaint. On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with Requests for Admission (alternatively, “RFAs”) via U.S. mail, and has yet to receive responses. (Declaration of Robert M. Ross, Exhibit A.)  On November 6, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a letter to Defendant inquiring into Defendant’s lack of discovery responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission. (Id. at Exhibit B.) Plaintiff received no response to this letter, nor any response to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission. (Declaration of Robert M. Ross, ¶ 5.)  

MOVING PARTY POSITION

Plaintiff argues the requested discovery is relevant to issues raised in Plaintiff's complaint. (Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions as Admitted, p. 3.) Plaintiff additionally requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,460.00 based on 2 hours of attorney work at a $700.00 hourly rate, in addition to a $60.00 filing fee. (Id. at 5.)

OPPOSITION

No opposition has been filed.

ANALYSIS

I. Motion to Deem Truth of Matters in RFAs as Admitted 

If a party to whom requests for admission have been directed fails to serve a timely response, the requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(b)). The court shall make the requested order for deemed admissions, unless it finds that before the hearing on the motion the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with CCP §§ 2033.210–2033.230. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)

Here, Plaintiff propounded RFAs on October 1, 2024. (Declaration of Robert M. Ross, Exhibit A.)   Plaintiff’s responses were due 30 days after service, plus an additional 5 days because service was via U.S. mail; Plaintiff’s responses were accordingly due on November 4, 2024. (California Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.250; California Code of Civil Procedure § 1013.) Defendant, in turn, failed to provide timely responses, and has yet to provide any responses to Plaintiff’s RFAs or otherwise contact Plaintiff to request an extension. Thus, it is appropriate to grant Plaintiff’s motion to deem the truth of the matters specified in Plaintiff’s RFAs to Defendant as admitted.

II. Sanctions

California Code of Civil Procedure § 2033.280 allows a party moving for an order that the truth of any matters specified in requests for admission be deemed admitted to also seek monetary sanctions commencing with California Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.010. The court must impose a CCP § 2023.030 monetary sanction on the party whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated the motion for deemed admissions. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).) California Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030 permits the court to “impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process…pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” “Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery[]” is a misuse of the discovery process. (California Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.010.)

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,460.00 based on 2 hours of attorney work at a $700.00 hourly rate, in addition to a $60.00 filing fee. (Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions as Admitted, p. 5; Declaration of Robert M. Ross, ¶ 6.) The Court finds Plaintiff’s number of hours worked on the instant motion is reasonable, and that Plaintiff may also recover for the $60.00 filing fee. However, the Court here finds Plaintiff’s hourly rate is excessive in this instance, particularly in light of the simple nature of the instant, unopposed motion. Accordingly, the Court reduces Plaintiff’s hourly rate to $400.00 in calculating sanctions for this motion. The Court thus finds Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions in the amount of $860.00 for the total costs arising from the instant motion.

RULING

Plaintiff’s motion Deeming Facts Admitted as to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One, is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $860.00.  Sanctions are payable within 30 days of Plaintiff giving notice of this order.
