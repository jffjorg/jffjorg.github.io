Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

Larry A. Rothstein seeks to be relieved as counsel of record for defendant Vision Construction Group, Inc. on grounds that there has been an irreconcilable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship. Absent a showing of resulting prejudice, an attorney’s request for withdrawal should be granted. (People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 406.) The Court notes that trial in this matter is scheduled for March 10, 2025, and finds that no prejudice will result from granting this motion.

The Court has continued the hearing twice already to allow Mr. Rothstein the opportunity to file a proposed order. On December 10, 2024, a proposed order on the correct form, Judicial Council Form MC-053, was indeed filed. However, Item 6 is incomplete because it does not include Defendant’s phone number. The Court therefore affords Mr. Rothstein one more opportunity to file a completed proposed order and continues the hearing to ___________ at 8:30 a.m. in Department 3 of the Alhambra Courthouse unless Mr. Rothstein can provide this telephone number in court so that it can be inserted and this matter dealt with; this kind of oversight wastes everyone’s time. The proposed order must be filed no later than 5 court days before the hearing.

Moving party to give notice.

Dated this 18th day of December, 2024

 

 

 

 

William A. Crowfoot

Judge of the Superior Court

I.            INTRODUCTION 

On November 22, 2024, plaintiff Mario Garcia (“Plaintiff”) filed an ex parte application for leave to file a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). The application was denied but the Court ordered Plaintiff to file a noticed motion and specially set the motion for hearing on December 18, 2024. Plaintiff filed said motion on November 25, 2024.

On December 3, 2024, defendants Joanna Esquivel (“Esquivel”), Juan Molina (“Molina”), and Maria De Jesus Meza Hernandez (“Hernandez”) filed an opposition brief. On December 5, 2024, defendant Jennifer Soyohn Suh (“Suh”) filed an opposition brief.

No reply brief is on file.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

The court may, in its discretion and after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading, including adding or striking out the name of any party, or correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) “Public policy dictates that leave to amend be liberally granted.” (Centex Homes v. St. Paul Fire & Marine Ins. Co. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 23, 32.) “Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial . . . this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party.’ [Citation]. A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation.]” (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

A motion to amend a pleading must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments and must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted or added, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the allegations are located.  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324(a).) The motion shall also be accompanied by a declaration attesting to the effect of the amendment, why the amendment is necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 1.324(b).)

III.        DISCUSSION

Plaintiff’s proposed SAC includes causes of action for: (1) breach of a settlement agreement entered into between Plaintiff and Suh, (2) promissory estoppel, and (3) fraud. Plaintiff also seeks to add United Financial Casualty Company, Progressive Commercial Casualty Company, and Progressive Group of Insurance as defendants. Plaintiff argues that the motion is timely because up through November 18, 2024, Plaintiff and counsel “held a good faith, reasonable belief that a settlement agreement had been reached between Defendants and [Plaintiff].” (Motion, p. 8; Shtofman Decl., ¶¶ 9-10.) Plaintiff argues that the SAC is necessary to enforce his settlement agreement with Suh.

Esquivel, Molina, Hernandez, and Suh (collectively, “Defendants”) oppose the motion for leave to amend on the same substantive grounds, albeit in separate briefs. First, Defendants argue that the new defendants and additional causes of action will cause significant delay to trial, currently scheduled for January 13, 2025. The defendants also point out that the proposed pleading is prejudicial because the scope of this case will be expanded to include claims and parties that are completely unrelated to the underlying motor vehicle accident. They further observe that the purported conflict precipitating Plaintiff’s belated request for amendment appears to stem from Plaintiff’s refusal to acknowledge the rights of statutory lienholders and dismiss Suh from the action. These arguments are all well-taken.

Plaintiff did not file a brief responding to any of the arguments raised in the two opposition briefs and the Court construes Plaintiff’s silence as conceding the merits of Defendants’ arguments. denies the motion for leave to file the SAC.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED.

I.            INTRODUCTION

On November 12, 2024, defendant Jennifer S. Suh (“Defendant”) filed this unopposed motion to interplead settlement funds of $100,000 after reaching a settlement agreement with plaintiff Mario Garcia (“Plaintiff”). This motion also seeks to: (1) discharge Defendant from further liability to plaintiff Mario Garcia (“Plaintiff”, Robert S. Shtofman, Paratus Partners LLC (“Paratus”), and Department of Health Care Services (“DHC”), and (2) dismiss her from this action. The motion is made pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 386 and 386.5.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

“A defendant, against whom an action is pending upon a contract, or for specific personal property, may, at any time before answer, upon affidavit that a person not a party to the action makes against him, and without any collusion with him, a demand upon such contract, or for such property, upon notice to such person and the adverse party, apply to the court for an order to substitute such person in his place, and discharge him from liability to either party, on his depositing in court the amount claimed on the contract, or delivering the property or its value to such person as the court may direct; and the court may, in its discretion, make the order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (a).) “Where the only relief sought against one of the defendants is the payment of a stated amount of money alleged to be wrongfully withheld, such defendant may, upon affidavit that he is a mere stakeholder with no interest in the amount or any portion thereof and that conflicting demands have been made upon him for the amount by parties to the action, upon notice to such parties, apply to the court for an order discharging him from liability and dismissing him from the action on his depositing with the clerk of the court the amount in dispute and the court may, in its discretion, make such order. (Code Civ. Proc., § 386.5.) Alternatively, a defendant may file a verified cross-complaint in interpleader or an action in interpleader, whenever conflicting claims are or may be made upon him, to compel those claimants to litigate their several claims. (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (a)-(b).)

III.        DISCUSSION

Defendant states, and no other party disputes, that she and Plaintiff have entered into a written settlement agreement in which Plaintiff released all claims against Defendant’s insurer, in exchange for $100,000 minus any statutory liens. (Motion, p. 3.) The settlement agreement specifies that the amount made payable will be “less the amount of [any] liens” and that no drafts will be issued until the written amounts of statutory liens have been received. (Hill Decl., Ex. A.) The settlement agreement also required Plaintiff to dismiss Defendant with prejudice and sign a request for dismissal of the complaint. (Ibid.) Defendant also settled with co-plaintiff, Leonardo Garcia, who filed a request for dismissal on July 31, 2024.

Defendant states that Plaintiff has not provided written notice of statutory liens to Defendant and that it has become aware that Plaintiff’s lienholders, Paratus and DHCS, are asserting liens for $146,165.90 and $30,138, respectively, exceeding the settlement funds of $100,000. Defendant also claims that Plaintiff’s counsel, Mr. Shtofman, has demanded that $40,000 of the settlement be paid directly to him. As a result, there are four competing claims to the settlement funds, namely, from Plaintiff, Paratus, DHCS, and Mr. Shtofman.

Interpleader would typically be appropriate in these factual circumstances. However, Defendant’s motion is a procedural oddity not authorized by sections 386 or 386.5 because the action pending against Defendant is not one “upon a contract” for the settlement funds, but for Plaintiff’s personal injuries; therefore, the potential claimants cannot be “substituted in” for Defendant in this action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (a).) The relief sought against Defendant in this action is not the payment of a stated amount of money alleged to be wrongfully withheld. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 386.5.) Additionally, even if Defendant’s motion were procedurally allowed, Defendant did not give notice to Paratus of this motion and its intent to interplead the settlement funds. If Defendant feels the need to continue with an interpleader action, Defendant may file a verified cross-complaint in this action against all the claimants, or an original complaint in interpleader naming the claimants, and then move for an order depositing the settlement funds and discharging it from liability.

IV.        CONCLUSION

Defendant’s motion is DENIED.
