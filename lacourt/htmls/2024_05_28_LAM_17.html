DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV39600    Hearing Date: May 28, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

WHITNEY ROLLAND REED II, an individual, by his guardian ad litem,

Joanna Jones Reed

 

         vs.

 

County of Los Angeles, et al.

 

 Case No.:  20STCV39600 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 28, 2024

 

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to his RFPs is DENIED. Accordingly, the Court declines to award sanctions at this time. 

 

On 10/15/2020, Plaintiff Whitney Rolland Reed II (Plaintiff or Mr. Reed) filed suit against the County of Los Angeles, Los Angeles County Sherriff’s Department, Sheriff Alex Villanueva, Sergeant Rodriguez, Sergeant L. Raymond, Deputy D. Auner, Deputy Hussainian, Deputy M. Salomon, and Deputy J. Stern (collectively, Defendants). On 5/26/2023, Plaintiff filed a second amended complaint (SAC) alleging: (1) breach of mandatory duties; (2) failure to summon medical care; (3) negligence; and (4) violation of the Bane Act.

 

            On 4/23/2024, Plaintiff moved to compel further responses from County of Los Angeles and the Los Angeles County Sherriff’s Department (collectively, Defendants) to his Requests for Production (RFPs). Plaintiff seeks $7,200 in monetary sanctions.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to his RFP Nos. 18-20. The RFP in question are as follow:

 

Request No. 18: Please provide all surveillance video of Title 15 safety checks conducted at the cell holding Plaintiff on September 20, 2019, between 7:00 p.m. and 8:00 p.m.

 

Request No. 19: Please provide all surveillance video of Title 15 safety checks conducted at the cell holding Plaintiff on September 27, 2019, between 1:00 p.m. and 3:00 p.m.

 

Request No. 20: Please provide all surveillance video of Title 15 safety checks conducted at the cell holding Plaintiff on October 7, 2019, between 9 a.m. and 10 a.m.

 

            In its responses, Defendant County of Los Angeles (COLA) maintains that it cannot produce CCTV footage because it has been replaced and overwritten.

 

            Plaintiff argues that this representation must be false because of COLA documents which clearly demonstrate the requested materials were independently set aside and saved for purposes of a criminal investigation into the assaults onto Plaintiff:

 

The requested materials are not random videos that would be overwritten in the course of retention because they were separately set aside for review by the County itself from the dates of the assaults on Mr. Reed: (1) September 20, 2019; (2) September 27, 2019; (3) October 7, 2019.

 

A LASD Incident Report dated September 20, 2019, acknowledges that DVTEL video of the September 20, 2019, incident was reviewed and booked into evidence: “I reviewed DVTEDL video footage to confirm V/Reed’s statement. After reviewing DVTEL video footage, suspect in question was Inmate Tucker…A compact disc (EV-1) containing video footage of the physical altercation that took place between S/Tucker, and V/Reed was booked into evidence by Deputy Hussainian #648655. Sergeant Johnson #52031 was notified of the incident.” See Plaintiff’s Exhibit C, BATES 87.

 

A LASD Incident Report dated September 27, 2019, acknowledges that surveillance of the September 27, 2019, incident was also booked into custody: “CDs/Video – (1) envelope containing DVD-CD/R of photographs of the victims and suspects from the indicated incident.” (BATES 124) Furthermore, defendant Deputy Salomon “formed the opinion that S/Espinoza and S/Raygoza were in violation of 242 P.C. (Battery). V/Reed Stated he was desirous of prosecution against both.” See Plaintiff’s Exhibit D, BATES 125.

 

LASD deputies also acknowledged booking surveillance evidence of the October 7, 2019, incident into custody: a “compact disk containing photographs of: victim Reed’s injuries” was booked into evidence by Ryker Convento (#639804) on October 7, 2019,” as well as a “Compact disk containing video surveillance footage of the incident.” See Plaintiff’s Exhibit E, at BATES 104-105. Surveillance video of the October 7th incident was reviewed and saved onto a compact disc: “Based on the statements given by V/Reed and the CCTV footage, I formed the opinion that S1/Reyes, S2/Don Cruz, S3/Lozano, S4Perez and S5/Garcia are in violation of 245(a)(1) P.C. The CCTV footage of the incident was saved to a second DVD-R compact disc (EV-2) and booked into the Men’s Central Jail evidence locker by Deputy Convento. Id. at Bates 107.

 

            (Motion., 4:11-5:14.)

 

            In opposition, Defendants argues that Plaintiff is conflating two distinct issues relating to the video surveillance footage: “While it is undisputed that some video surveillance footage depicting the actual physical altercations was booked into evidence as a part of a criminal investigations, there is absolutely no evidence to suggest that any surveillance footage depicting the Title 15 safety checks before each incident was booked into evidence.” (Opp., 3:16-19.)

 

            As such, Defendant contends that there is nothing contradictory or evasive about its responses indicating that after a reasonable and diligent search for responsive materials, Responding Party has been unable to locate any responsive documents within its possession, custody, and control as to RFP Nos. 18-20.

 

            Moreover, as for electronic media that was previously booked into evidence, it is unclear to the Court whether Defendant’s have maintained a copy.  Defendant’s responses indicated that the evidence had been subsequently submitted to the District Attorney for filing consideration, and specifically advised Plaintiff to subpoena the District Attorney to obtain copies of the material. (See Opp., Exhibit 9)

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ statement of an inability to comply is Code-Compliant. The Court cannot compel Defendants to produce materials which it contends do not exist. If Plaintiff believes that there was intentional destruction or spoilation of evidence, the proper motion would be a motion for sanctions, rather than a motion to compel further.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to his RFPs is denied. Accordingly, the Court declines to award sanctions at this time. 

 

           

 It is so ordered.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

ANDREW J. CHIN

                          

         vs.

 

DOORDASH, ICN.

 

 

 Case No.:  24STCV03751

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 28, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED. This matter is ordered stayed pending the completion of arbitration proceedings.

 

            On 2/14/2024, Plaintiff Andrew J. Chin (Plaintiff) filed suit against Doordash, Inc. (Defendant), alleging: (1) retaliation; (2) interference with taking leave; (3) whistleblower retaliation; (4) wrongful termination; and (5) unfair competition.

 

            On 3/12/2024, Defendant moved to compel arbitration, and stay these proceedings.

 

Legal Standard

 

Where the Court has determined that an agreement to arbitrate a controversy exists, the Court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy …unless it determines that…  grounds exist for rescission of the agreement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2.) Among the grounds which can support rescission are fraud, duress, and unconscionability. (Tiri v. Lucky Chances, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 231, 239.) The Court may also decline to compel arbitration wherein there is possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2 (c).)

 

Discussion

 

The party moving to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving [the] existence [of an arbitration agreement] by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.) The moving party also bears the burden of demonstrating that the claims fall within the scope of the arbitration agreement. (Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

 

A.    Existing Agreement

 

Defendant submitted evidence that on 8/25/2020, Plaintiff entered into an agreement to arbitrate (the Arbitration Agreement) with DoorDash when he accepted his Offer of Employment as a Senior Manager. (Gil Decl. at ¶ 6.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant has not met its burden because the attached Arbitration Agreement includes only an electronic signature, and Roma Gill does not have personal knowledge to authenticate the document.

 

The Court finds the agreement to properly authenticated, and sufficient to meet Defendant’s initial burden. (See Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th at pp. 218-219 [a party moving to compel arbitration need do no more than attach a signed copy of the agreement to the motion to satisfy the authentication requirement].) 

 

Roma Gill submitted a declaration stating that she has access to Defendant’s business records, including business files, that Defendant’s business records reflect that Plaintiff signed the Arbitration Agreement the same day he received an offer letter, and there is no record of Plaintiff opting out of the Agreement. (See Gill Decl.)

 

Plaintiff has not submitted any persuasive evidence which would suggest that this Agreement was forged or not found within Defendant’s records as contended.

 

Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant arise out of the employment agreement, and thus fall within the scope of the arbitration agreement. 

 

Given that Defendant has established by a preponderance of the evidence that an arbitration agreement exists, and that Plaintiff’s claims are covered by that agreement, the burden shifts to the Plaintiff to establish that the arbitration clause should not be enforced. (Pinnacle Museum Tower Assn. v. Pinnacle Market Development (US), LLC (2012) 55 Cal.4th 223, 236. (Pinnacle).)

 

II.               Plaintiff’s Burden 

 

The party opposing arbitration bears the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence any defense, such as unconscionability or duress. (Pinnacle, supra, 55 Cal.4th at p. 236.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff did not advance any defense to enforceability. As such, he has not met his burden to show that the arbitration agreement should be not be enforced.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel arbitration is granted. This matter is ordered stayed pending the completion of arbitration proceedings.

 

It is so ordered.
