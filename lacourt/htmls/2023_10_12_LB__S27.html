DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21LBCV00497    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: S27

Defendants propounded RPDs, set two on Plaintiff on 7/11/23.  To date, despite an attempt to meet and confer, Plaintiff has not served responses.  Defendants therefore seek an order compelling Plaintiff to respond, without objections, to the outstanding discovery and to pay sanctions.

 

As an initial note, Defendants reserved the hearing on the motion as a “motion to compel further responses.”  The motion is a motion to compel initial, not further, responses.  The Court asks Counsel to ensure motions are reserved using the correct category in the future in connection with this and other actions.

 

Defendants’ motion is granted.  Plaintiff is ordered to serve verified responses to RPDs, set two, without objections, within ten days.  CCP §§2031.300(a),(b). 

 

Sanctions are mandatory.  §2031.300(c).  Defendants seek sanctions in the amount of $1172.50 against Plaintiff and his attorney of record, jointly and severally.  Defense Counsel bills at the rate of $250/hour.  The Court awards one hour to prepare this form discovery motion.  No opposition was filed and therefore no time to file a reply is awarded.  The Court awards one hour for the remote appearance in connection with the motion.  The Court therefore awards a total of two hours of attorney time at the rate of $250/hour, or $500, in attorneys’ fees.  The Court also awards the filing fee of $60.  

 

Sanctions are sought and imposed against Plaintiff and his attorney of record, jointly and severally.  They are ordered to pay sanctions to Defendants, by and through their attorney of record, in the total amount of $560, within twenty days.     

 

Defendants are ordered to give notice.

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. 


Case Number: 21STCV01257    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Matthew Hernandez filed this action against Defendants, Silverstar Express, Inc., Direct Chassislink, Inc., and Apollo Global Management, LLC for personal injury damages.  Plaintiff alleges he was injured when a Utility Tractor Rig and chassis he was driving overturned.  He was using the UTR to move a reel of steel cable.  The reel was stowed on a flatrack container on the chassis.  Plaintiff alleges the subject chassis was not equipped to handle this type of cargo, and DCLI, the owner of the chassis, failed to warn the necessary persons and entities that the chassis should not be loaded with this type of cargo.  Plaintiff has added additional doe defendants to the action. 

 

2.     Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement

The parties have participated in settlement discussions, and the negotiations resulted in a settlement between Plaintiff and Defendant, Silverstar Express, Inc..  Pursuant to the agreement, Silverstar will pay Plaintiffs $1,000,000 in exchange for a release of all claims against it.  Notably, this is the limit on Silverstar’s insurance policy covering Plaintiff’s claims.  Silverstar seeks a determination that its settlement with Plaintiff is in good faith.  Silverstar timely and properly served all parties to the action with its moving papers.  Any opposition to the motion was due on or before 9/29/23.  No party has filed opposition to the motion.  CCP §877.6(d) places the burden on a motion for determination of good faith settlement on the party who opposes the motion.  In light of the lack of opposition, the motion is granted.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Claudia and Josue Morales filed this action against Defendants, Long Beach Memorial Medical Center and Ali Khoynezhad, M.D., PhD for negligence, hospital negligence, and loss of consortium.  The complaint arises out of Defendants’ care and treatment of Claudia Morales for cardiothoracic surgery. 

 

On 2/07/23, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to amend.  On 2/27/23, they filed their operative FAC, which includes causes of action for:

Negligence

Hospital Negligence

Loss of Consortium

Corporate Negligence

Intentional Misrepresentation

Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Willful and Wanton Misconduct

Fraud

Battery

 

2.     Demurrer to FAC

a.     Parties’ Positions

Defendants demur to the complaint, contending (a) the fourth cause of action is duplicative of the second, (b) wanton and willful misconduct is not a separate cause of action, (c) the fraud-based causes of action are not sufficiently pled, and (d) the battery cause of action is not sufficiently pled. 

 

Plaintiffs oppose the demurrer.  They argue the fourth and second cause of action are not duplicative, wanton and willful misconduct is properly pled, and the fraud-based claims are properly pled.

 

Defendants, in reply, contend Plaintiffs’ cited cases do not support a separate cause of action for willful and wanton misconduct, fraud is not properly pled, and the demurrer to the battery cause of action should be sustained without leave to amend in light of Plaintiffs’ failure to address the issue.

 

b.     Initial Note

Plaintiffs, with their opposition, present their attorney’s declaration, an LA Times article, and Dr. Khoynezhad’s deposition testimony.  Defendants, in reply, object to consideration of the material, contending it is not subject to judicial notice and cannot be considered on demurrer.  The Court will not consider the evidence in determining whether the FAC states a cause of action, which can be decided based solely on the face of the FAC; the Court will, however, consider the declaration and evidence, to the extent necessary, to determine whether leave to amend should be granted in the event the demurrer is sustained.

 

Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading. It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within]. Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond. CCP § 430.10(f).

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

d.         Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel submitted a declaration in compliance with the meet and confer requirements.  The issue is therefore ripe for determination on its merits. 

 

c.     Initial Note re: Sixth Cause of Action

Defendants’ demurrer, at page 3, line 21 through page 4, line 1 purports to challenge Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action.  Plaintiffs’ sixth cause of action is for breach of fiduciary duty.  Defendants’ points and authorities do not mention this cause of action or articulate any basis for their pleading challenge.  To the extent there is a demurrer to this cause of action, it is overruled as unsupported. 

 

d.     Second and Fourth Causes of Action

Defendants demur to the fourth cause of action for corporate negligence, contending it is duplicative of the second cause of action for hospital negligence.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, explain that the second cause of action for hospital liability is pled against LBMMC, but their fourth cause of action for corporate liability is pled against MHS, and is therefore not duplicative. 

 

Defendants do not address this issue in reply.  The demurrer to the fourth cause of action is therefore overruled.

 

e.     Seventh Cause of Action

Defendants demur to the seventh cause of action for willful and wanton misconduct, contending it is not a separate cause of action and is duplicative of the negligence cause of action.  Defendants rely on Berkly v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 527 to support their position.  Berkly, however, set forth the elements of a cause of action for willful misconduct, noting that they are more strict than the elements of mere negligence.  If there are elements of a cause of action, then the cause of action exists.  While it may be only a “heightened” form of negligence, if Plaintiffs were to merely plead a negligence claim, Defendants would inevitably argue that they could not recover on a willful and wanton misconduct theory at trial. 

 

The demurrer to the seventh cause of action is overruled.

 

f.      Fifth and Eighth Causes of Action

i.              Parties’ Arguments

Defendants demur to the fifth (intentional misrepresentation) and eighth (fraud) causes of action, contending the claim is based on an alleged failure to disclose that the subject medical device was not FDA-approved for Plaintiff’s use, which is not a recognized basis for a fraud claim.

Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, argue their fraud claims are based both on the theory that the hospital defendants knew Dr. Khoynezhad was dangerous but granted him privileges despite this knowledge, and also on the theory that Dr. Khoynezhad improperly used an experimental medical device on Plaintiff. 

 

Defendants, in reply, contend the claims based on the medical device fail for the reasons stated in the demurrer, and the claims based on granting privileges sound in negligence, not in fraud. 

 

ii.             Allegations of the FAC

Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action is entitled “intentional misrepresentation – fraud by concealment.”  As an initial note, while not discussed by the parties, this is confusing, as an intentional misrepresentation is, by its very nature, false speech, whereas concealment is, by its very nature, failure to speak. 

 

The cause of action, at ¶105(a) through (j), alleges Defendants concealed various facts from Plaintiffs relating to implantation of the non-approved stent.  At ¶105(k) through (m), Plaintiffs allege Defendants concealed facts relating to Dr. Khoynezhad’s privileges, specifically, that the medical executive committee had recommended the hospital NOT grant him privileges because of his history of poor judgment and inappropriate care and management of patients resulting in serious and sometimes fatal complications. 

 

Plaintiffs’ eight cause of action is entitled “fraud.”  The caption of the cause of action does not indicate which species of fraud (intentional misrepresentation, negligent misrepresentation, or concealment) Plaintiffs are alleging.  At ¶168, Plaintiffs refer to the cause of action as one for “constructive fraud.”  Pursuant to Assilzadeh v. California Federal Bank (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 399, “’constructive fraud’ is a unique species of fraud applicable only to a fiduciary or confidential relationship; generally, constructive fraud comprises any act, omission or concealment involving a breach of legal or equitable duty, trust or confidence which results in damage to another even though the conduct is not otherwise fraudulent.”  The cause of action alleges Defendants engaged in constructive fraud by suppressing evidence that the subject stent was not approved for Plaintiff’s use and also that Dr. Khoynezhad should not have been permitted to practice in the facility.  Thus, the fifth cause of action and the eighth cause of action are pled on the same basic set of facts, and are both pled based on concealment theories. 

 

iii.            Analysis

Defendants, in their demurrer, failed to address the arguments that they suppressed the fact that the hospital review committee recommended not granting Dr. Khoynezhad credentials because he is unsafe as a physician.  Defendants attempt to address these arguments in reply, but the Court will not consider an argument raised for the first time in reply, as Plaintiffs do not have the opportunity to address the argument.

 

Additionally, the FAC alleges facts far over and above merely using a stent not approved by the FDA for implementation under Plaintiff’s circumstances.  It alleges, at ¶105(d) that the FDA had expressly warned Defendants NOT to use the stent in the way they did with Plaintiff, at ¶105(g) that Dr. Khoynezhad was a paid consultant for Medtronic, which manufactured the subject stent graft system, and, at ¶115, that the hospital defendants were aware of Dr. Khoynezhad’s financial relationship with Medtronic.  These facts, among others pled in the FAC, render the FAC different in kind from Daum v. SpineCare Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 1285, 1313, relied upon heavily by Defendants in their moving papers.  There were no allegations of financial motivation in Daum. 

 

The demurrer to the fraud causes of action is therefore overruled.

g.     Ninth Cause of Action

Plaintiffs’ ninth cause of action is for battery.  Defendants contend that, pursuant to Cobbs v. Grant (1972) 8 Cal.3d 229, 240, when a patient consents to a surgery, and the surgery is performed, but complications arise, the claim does not sound in battery.  Plaintiffs do not oppose the demurrer to the battery cause of action.  The demurrer is therefore sustained without leave to amend. 

 

h.     Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer to the ninth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  Defendants’ demurrer is otherwise overruled.  Defendants are ordered to file an answer to the FAC, with the ninth cause of action deemed stricken, within ten days.

1.     Expert Exchange

2.     9/29/23 Ex Parte Application

On 9/29/23, the Court heard and granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to shorten time for the hearing on this motion.  Both parties appeared at the hearing, and the parties waived notice of the order.  Defendant was ordered to file any opposition to the motion on or before 10/05/23.  Defendant has not filed timely opposition to the motion. 

 

3.     Analysis

a.     Law Governing Late Designations

A party who failed to exchange under CCP § 2034.260 on time (normally 50 days before trial) may seek leave to submit the required material on a later date.  (CCP § 2034.710.)

 

            Absent “exceptional circumstances,” the motion for permission to file a tardy list must be made early enough to permit deposing the experts involved before the 15–day cut-off on expert witness depositions.  (CCP §§ 2034.710(b), 2024.030.) 

 

            The court may permit tardy submission of expert witness lists and information if the following matters are shown (CCP § 2034.720):

 

(a) Excuse: The failure to exchange expert witness information on time resulted from “mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.” (CCP §2034.720(c)(1).) 

 

(b) Relief sought promptly: The motion for relief is being made promptly after learning of such excuse;

 

(c) Information belatedly served on all other parties: The moving party has already served a copy of the proposed expert witness list and declarations on all other parties to the action;

 

(d) Availability for deposition: The moving party will make the witness available immediately for a deposition if leave is granted;

 

(e) No prejudice to opposing party: The court must “take into account” the extent to which the opposing party has relied on the absence of an expert witness list from the moving party. Before granting leave to submit the tardy list, the court must determine that it will not prejudice the opposing party's ability to maintain its action or defense on the merits; and

 

(f) Attempt to resolve informally: The usual declarations are required stating a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally each issue presented by the motion. (CCP § 2034.710(c).)

 

In ruling on the motion, the court must consider all of the § 2034.720 factors enumerated above.  (Plunkett v. Spaulding, supra, 52 Cal.App.4th at pp. 135–136—denial based solely on timing of motion was abuse of discretion.)  If relief is granted, it must be conditioned on the moving party making the expert available for deposition “immediately.”  (CCP § 2034.720(d).)  

 

b.     Analysis

Plaintiff made the required showing that it failed to designate experts due to a calendaring error, that it promptly sought relief, and that it has provided the expert designation to Defendant.  The Court finds Plaintiff and Defendant could have met and conferred additionally in good faith to resolve these issues, as there was no meaningful “meet and confer,” only a series of accusations about whether the designation was timely or not.  The Court will rule on the motion despite the lack of meaningful meet and confer, but asks Counsel to work together in good faith in the future in connection with this and other actions. 

Defendant has not opposed the motion, and has therefore not shown any grounds for denial of the motion.  The case is set for trial on 11/13/23, making time of the essence, but also providing sufficient time for deposition of the experts.  The motion is granted.  Plaintiff must make its experts available for deposition immediately.  Counsel must meet and confer to schedule the expert depositions.

Case Number: 23LBCV00046    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Julio Contreras filed this action against Defendant, City of Long Beach for damages arising out of an automobile accident that occurred on 7/10/22.  Plaintiff filed the complaint on 1/09/23. 

 

2.     Motion for Leave to Amend

a.     Initial Note

While Plaintiff’s proof of service shows he served his motion on Defendant in August, Plaintiff did not actually file his motion until 10/03/23, only eight days prior to the hearing on the motion, in violation of CCP §1005(b).  This led to confusion about whether the motion was on calendar, and the motion was added to the calendar at a very late date.  The Court asks Plaintiff’s attorney to ensure papers are timely filed, as well as served, in the future in connection with this and other actions.  The Court has read and considered the moving papers despite this defect. 

 

b.     Relief Sought

Plaintiff, when he served Defendant with his government tort claim, also included the claims of two third parties, Luis and Marco Bonilla, both of whom were also involved in the subject accident.  When Plaintiff filed his complaint, however, he erroneously failed to include the Bonillas in the action.  Plaintiff concedes the statute of limitations has run as to the Bonillas’ claims, but seeks leave to add them to his complaint because the failure to include them was the result of his attorney’s mistake or neglect.  

 

Plaintiff does not cite any on point authority permitting him to add two previously un-named plaintiffs whose claims are barred by the statute of limitations to the complaint.  The Court has considered the various possible mechanisms to name the new plaintiffs, and finds Plaintiff cannot do so.

 

c.     Relation Back

The claims clearly do not relate back to the named plaintiff’s claims.  Amendments naming a new party plaintiff are permissible if there is no change in the claim being asserted: e.g., amendments to reflect an assignment of the claim; or, on plaintiff's death, to substitute their executor or administrator.  Plaintiffs who lack standing to sue on a claim or cause of action may seek leave to amend their complaint to substitute a plaintiff with standing to sue (the “real party in interest”). Such an amendment is authorized under CCP § 473(a)(1), which allows amendments “by adding or striking out the name of any party.” See Branick v. Downey Sav. &Loan Ass'n (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 243.  However, where the amendment adds a plaintiff whose claim is independent of the claims previously asserted, the amendment does not “relate back” to the original complaint. I.e., the plaintiff proposed to be substituted may not “state facts which give rise to a wholly distinct and different legal obligation against the defendant.”  Id. at 243.

Plaintiff seeks to add wholly distinct claims by other plaintiffs, and not to merely substitute the new plaintiffs’ claims for his own.  The proposed amended complaint, therefore, does not relate back to the original complaint, and amendment is not proper under §473(a).

 

d.     Neglect or Mistake

While Plaintiff does not expressly mention §473(b), he does contend he should be granted leave to amend because his attorney made a mistake or neglected to name the other two plaintiffs when he filed the complaint.  §473(b) governs relief from attorney errors.  Pursuant to Life Sav. Bank v. Wilhelm (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 174, 178, §473(b) does not provide a mechanism for relief when the attorney error was failure to comply with the applicable statute of limitations. 

 

e.     Gov Code §946.6

Plaintiff also argues relief should be granted pursuant to Gov Code §946.6.  §946.6 permits a plaintiff whose application for leave to file a late claim was denied to then file a petition, with the Superior Court, for relief from failure to file a timely claim.  §946.6 does not have any application to this case, because the proposed new plaintiffs did not file any application for leave to file a late claim, and did not initiate this action by way of a petition for relief.  If the proposed new plaintiffs have any procedural mechanism to assert their claims, such as a petition for relief, they are free to file any such petition.  They have failed, however, to show that §946.6 permits them to proceed in this action by way of a motion for leave to amend to add claims that are barred by the statute of limitations. 

 

f.      Conclusion

Plaintiff failed to cite any authority for his position that it would be appropriate to add plaintiffs whose claims are barred by the statute of limitations as parties to this action.  The motion for leave to amend is therefore denied.

Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

d.         Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel submitted a declaration in compliance with the meet and confer requirements.  The issue is therefore ripe for determination on its merits. 

 

e.         Workers’ Compensation and Equitable Tolling

Plaintiff sustained his injury on 6/06/19.  Complaint, ¶12.  Plaintiff filed a workers’ compensation claim on 12/11/19.  ¶33.  The workers’ compensation judge approved the parties’ stipulation to dismiss the claim on 10/26/22.  Plaintiffs filed their complaint on 1/17/23. 

 

Defendant argues, in the demurrer, that the statute of limitations bars the complaint, and Plaintiff has not pled tolling because (a) the exhibits mentioned in the complaint are not attached, (b) Plaintiff alleges mere conclusions re: equitable tolling, and (c) a workers’ compensation claim would not toll the loss of consortium claim, which is separate and distinct. 

 

Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, cites numerous cases holding that the timely filing of a workers’ compensation claim equitably tolls the statute of limitations on a civil action arising out of the same events.  Plaintiff also attaches the exhibits that were missing from the complaint to the opposition.  It is unclear how anything other than “mere conclusions” could be pled concerning the facts that give rise to equitable tolling.

 

Defendant, in reply, does not revisit the arguments made in the demurrer.  Defendant, instead, contends there was a thirty-day time period during which Plaintiffs were required to file their civil complaint after the dismissal of the workers’ compensation claim, and Plaintiffs failed to file within thirty days.  Defendant relies on Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 409 to support his position.  In Kolani, the Court considered the issue of whether the plaintiff’s filing of a federal action equitably tolled the time for her to file those claims in state court.  It held that 28 USC §1367(d) requires any such action to be filed within thirty days after the federal claims are dismissed, and failure to do so mandates dismissal of the claims.

 

The Court rejects this argument for two reasons.  First, Defendant raised the argument for the first time in reply, and Plaintiffs had no opportunity to address the argument.  Second, Defendant failed to show that 28 USC §1367(d), which expressly deals with a situation where a plaintiff attaches pendent state claims to a federal action, also applies in the workers’ compensation context.

 

f.          Negligence

Defendant argues Plaintiffs have alleged mere conclusions concerning negligence, and have not alleged any facts showing he was negligent.  Plaintiffs, however, allege Defendant failed to act to ensure trimming the fifteen-foot tree, which requires a license Plaintiff did not have, was a safe activity.  ¶22.  Plaintiffs allege there was no policy, condition, or procedure for the trimming.  ¶22.  It has long been the law in California that a plaintiff can plead negligence in general terms.  Rannard v. Lockheed Aircraft Corp. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 149, 154-155.  The Court finds the allegations sufficient, at the pleading stage, to allege negligence. 

Notably, Plaintiffs allege Plaintiff was Defendant’s employee at the time of the fall, because trimming trees requires a license, and Plaintiff lacks such license.  ¶¶13-16.  Defendant, in his reply papers, cites various cases holding workers are not entitled to workers’ compensation benefits under the circumstances of this case.  None of those cases, however, hold that this is so because the plaintiff is not the defendant’s employee; the cases so hold because the plaintiff is not the defendant’s “qualified” employee.  The word “qualified” is the key distinction, as a person can be an employee without qualifying to workers’ compensation benefits. 

 

Labor Code §2750.5, cited by Plaintiff in the complaint and not challenged by Defendant in the demurrer or reply, makes a person a legal employee when the person is performing work for which a license is required but lacks that license. 

 

The Court finds Defendant failed to meet his burden to show Plaintiff was not his employee and/or to negate the claim for negligence.  The demurrer to the negligence cause of action is therefore overruled.

 

g.         Premises Liability

Defendant argues the premises liability cause of action fails to state a cause of action because it fails to allege Defendant had actual or constructive notice of a dangerous condition on his property.  Plaintiffs allege Defendant hired Plaintiff to trim a tree, but failed to ensure he had a license and failed to provide him with necessary safety equipment.  Plaintiffs therefore allege Defendant created the safety condition, which is sufficient at the pleading stage.  The demurrer is overruled. 

 

h.         Loss of Consortium

Defendant argues, in the moving papers, that the loss of consortium cause of action is barred by the statute of limitations because it is an independent claim.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, show that a loss of consortium claim is precluded per the workers’ compensation exclusivity doctrine, and therefore it could not be filed while the workers’ compensation proceedings were pending.  Defendant does not address this argument in reply.  The demurrer to the loss of consortium cause of action is therefore also overruled.

 

i.              Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer to the complaint within ten days.

Case Number: 23LBCV00268    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Raul Field Escandon filed this action against Defendants, Universal Iron Works, Appleby Property Management, Olive Crest Homeowners Association, Ara Agopian, and HOA Board of Directors on 2/21/23.  Plaintiff’s complaint includes various causes of action and spans 73 pages.  Plaintiff seeks an injunction providing for various forms of relief, and also seeks compensatory damages. 

 

2.     Motion for Preliminary Injunction

a.     Procedural History of Motion

On 5/24/23, Plaintiff filed a motion for preliminary injunction, emergency injunctive relief, and restraining/protective order, setting it for hearing on 9/14/23.  On 6/21/23, the Court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to advance the hearing on the motion, and rescheduled the hearing for 7/27/23.  The Court indicated Plaintiff could not combine motions, and limited Plaintiff to hearing on a motion for protective order.  On 7/14/23, Defendants filed opposition papers.  On 7/18/23, Plaintiff filed reply papers.

 

On 7/27/23, the Court ruled on the motion.  The Court noted various procedural defects, and then ultimately concluded:

Plaintiff attempts to clarify, in his reply papers, exactly the relief he seeks.  He indicates the relief sought is as follows:

The Defendants including Olive Crest HOA Directors Agopian, Huerta, and  Garcia are ordered to refrain and hereby restrained and enjoined from taking any adverse acts, directly or indirectly, by themselves or by others on their behalf against Plaintiff, and/or against other Owners acting in support of this litigation's objectives, for matters related to this Case, including acts of retaliation intimidation, harassment, or harm to their persons and/or property for which said  Defendants or HOA Directors may be held civilly and/or criminally liable.

 

The motion is denied for several reasons.  First, the procedural defects detailed above each warrant denial of the motion.  Second, despite the prior order limiting Plaintiff to seeking a protective order, the order sought is clearly for injunctive relief.  Third, and most importantly, pursuant to Evans v. Evans (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1157, 1169, the Court cannot issue an injunction that is overbroad, vague, or generally phrased, because there is no available remedy if there is a violation of the injunction.  Plaintiff’s requested injunction is so broad and subjective that the Court cannot possibly enforce it if it is entered. 

 

3.     Motion for Reconsideration

On 8/09/23, Plaintiff filed this motion for reconsideration.  Plaintiff contends he has fixed all the procedural errors in the original motion, and seeks a protective order restraining Defendants in the manner detailed in the reply papers in connection with the original motion.  Plaintiff clarifies the fact that he is seeking relief pursuant to CCP §527.6, which permits the Court to grant a restraining order where it finds the plaintiff has suffered harassment due to the defendants’ acts. 

 

The Court will assume, without deciding, that Plaintiff has shown new facts, circumstances, or law, such that reconsidering its prior order is justified under CCP §1008.  Even if that is the case, the motion for reconsideration must be denied. 

 

As an initial note, Plaintiff has confused the proceedings by referring to his requested relief as a “protective order,” when §527.6 refers to the relief available as a “restraining order.”  In reviewing §527.6, it is clear it does not govern the relationship between Plaintiff and Defendants herein.  The types of acts Plaintiff contends constitute harassment include bolting a fence and fumigating (see motion, pages 9-11)  This is not the type of harassment contemplated by §527.6, which defines harassment as follows:

(b) For purposes of this section, the following terms have the following meanings:

(1) “Course of conduct” is a pattern of conduct composed of a series of acts over a period of time, however short, evidencing a continuity of purpose, including following or stalking an individual, making harassing telephone calls to an individual, or sending harassing correspondence to an individual by any means, including, but not limited to, the use of public or private mails, interoffice mail, facsimile, or email. Constitutionally protected activity is not included within the meaning of “course of conduct.”

(2) “Credible threat of violence” is a knowing and willful statement or course of conduct that would place a reasonable person in fear for the person's safety or the safety of the person's immediate family, and that serves no legitimate purpose.

(3) “Harassment” is unlawful violence, a credible threat of violence, or a knowing and willful course of conduct directed at a specific person that seriously alarms, annoys, or harasses the person, and that serves no legitimate purpose. The course of conduct must be that which would cause a reasonable person to suffer substantial emotional distress, and must actually cause substantial emotional distress to the petitioner.

 

Plaintiff’s described conduct does not constitute an act of violence, a threat of violence, or a course of conduct as defined by §527.6(b)(1).  Plaintiff’s only contention that even comes close to meeting the definition is found at page 10, lines 10-12, wherein Plaintiff says another owner asked him if he was afraid to be on the premises.  It appears, however, that the owner was asking because the property was being fumigated, and it is not safe to be on the property when it is being fumigated.  The question, therefore, does not appear to be a credible threat of violence.  Even if it were, any restraining order would be appropriate against the owner, Diekmann, only, and Plaintiff’s proposed order is against Agopian, Huerta, and Garcia. 

 

Plaintiff has therefore failed to show he is entitled to the relief he seeks, and the motion for reconsideration is denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV00347    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: S27

1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Kemechukwu Agu filed this action against Defendants, Win Chevrolet, Inc. and Hyundai Motor Financial, alleging a sole cause of action for breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges Defendants breached the parties’ contract by overcharging him in connection with the purchase of an automobile, including by charging a sale price in excess of the stipulated maximum loan amount in the unsolicited letter of offer, creating and multiplying fictitious fees that were not part of the parties’ sale contract, etc.

Plaintiff filed the complaint on 3/03/23. Defendants filed answers to the complaint on 4/03/23 (Win) and 4/19/23 (Hyundai). Win filed this motion to compel arbitration on 7/18/23, and Hyundai filed a timely joinder on 9/11/23.

2. Motion to Compel Arbitration

Defendant moves to compel arbitration pursuant to the parties’ written retail installment sales contract agreement, which is attached to the complaint as Exhibit 2. The contract is between Win and Plaintiff. The contract contains a provision, at the bottom of page one, requiring the parties to arbitration any dispute arising under the contract.

Notably, any opposition to the motion was due on or before 9/29/23, and the Court has not received timely opposition to the motion.

CCP §1281.2 obligates the Court to grant a motion to compel arbitration if it finds a binding contract providing for arbitration exists, unless the party opposing arbitration provides evidence establishing grounds for refusal to enforce the agreement. In light of the lack of opposition, Plaintiff necessarily failed to meet his burden to establish any such grounds, and the motion to compel arbitration is granted.

CCP §1281.4 requires the Court to stay the pending action pursuant to completion of arbitration proceedings. Defendant seeks an order staying this action. The request is granted. This action is stayed pending completion of arbitration proceedings.

3. Joinder

Honda joins in the motion. It supports its joinder with the Declaration of Nayeli Saldivar, a manager with knowledge of the facts that support the joinder. Saldivar declares, at ¶9 of the declaration, that Plaintiff agreed to an assignment of the sales contract from Win to Honda. Saldivar declares Exhibit A, page 1, shows this agreement. The Court has reviewed Exhibit A, page 1, and cannot locate the assignment clause. The points and authorities in support of the joinder explain that the subject clause is

located at the bottom of page 5 of the agreement. The Court located the clause at the bottom of page 5. The Court finds Plaintiff agreed to Win’s assignment of the contract to Honda, such that the arbitration clause governs Plaintif’s claims against Honda also.

As with the motion, there is no timely opposition to the joinder. The joinder is granted for the reasons detailed above in connection with the motion to compel.
