I.               BACKGROUND 

Plaintiff Benjamin Devinney (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant CR&R Incorporated (“CR&R”) for damages Plaintiff sustained after inspecting a container owned by Defendant. Plaintiff alleges the subject container’s ladder was broken, so Plaintiff climbed on the corner of the container. Plaintiff stabilized himself with one foot on the container and the other on the container’s contents. As Plaintiff pushed the container’s contents down, the contents shifted and caused Plaintiff to fall backward off the container.

On June 12, 2023, CR&R filed its Cross-Complaint against Cross-Defendant Performance Team LLC (“Performance”).

Plaintiff now moves for sanctions against CR&R or, in the alternative, for an order compelling CR&R comply with his demand for inspection. Performance also moves to compel the physical examination of Plaintiff.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 5, 2021, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Negligence; and (2) Negligent Hiring, Training, Supervisions, and Retention.

On November 30, 2021, CR&R filed its Answer.

On July 7, 2022, the court granted in part Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Request for Admission Admitted.

On November 9, 2022, the court deemed Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel CR&R’s discovery responses moot and declined to award Plaintiff sanctions.

On June 12, 2023, CR&R filed its Cross-Complaint against Performance, alleging (1) Implied Indemnity; and (2) Express Indemnity.

On August 28, 2023, Performance filed its Answer.

On January 19, 2024, Travelers Property Casualty Company of America (non-party) filed a Notice of Lien.

On July 3, 2024, Performance filed the instant Motion to Compel Physical Examination of Plaintiff (“Motion to Compel”).

On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Terminating Sanctions or, in the Alternative, Motion to Comply with Plaintiff’s Demand for Inspection against CR&R (“Motion for Sanctions”).

On July 18, 2024, CR&R filed a Notice of Joinder to the Motion to Compel.

On July 19, 2024, this Court granted Performance’s Ex Parte Application to Continue Trial.

On September 19, 2024, CR&R filed its Opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions.

On September 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition to Performance’s Motion to Compel.

On September 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Reply to CR&R’s Opposition to his Motion for Sanctions.

On September 25, 2024, Performance filed its Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to its Motion to Compel.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

A.             Terminating, Issue, Evidentiary, and/or Sanctions

On motion, if a party “fails to obey an order compelling attendance, testimony, and production, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction” as provided in Section 2030.010, et seq. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(h).)[1] The “court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after an opportunity for hearing, may impose” monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions “against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process[.]” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a)-(d).) Failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery are misuses of the discovery process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)   

B.             Motion to Compel Physical Examination

The Civil Discovery Act authorizes a defendant to demand one physical examination of a plaintiff who is seeking recovery for personal injuries. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220.) The examination may not include “any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive” and must be conducted “within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(a).)

The defendant may demand the physical examination of the plaintiff under this statute without leave of court. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(b).) The demand for such an exam shall identify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the physician who will perform the examination. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(c).) The physical examination should be scheduled for a date at least 30 days after service of the demand. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(d).)

The plaintiff to whom a demand for a physical examination has been served should respond to the demand within 20 days after service with a written statement that he or she will comply with the demand as stated, will comply with the demand as specifically modified by the plaintiff, or will refuse. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.230.) If the plaintiff fails to respond, unjustifiably refuses the demand, or specifies a modification that the defendant finds unwarranted, the defendant may move for an order compelling compliance with the demand. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2032.240(b), 2032.250(a).) This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.250(a).)

IV.          ANALYSIS

There are two motions before this Court. The Court will first address Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions and will next address Performance’s Motion to Compel.

A.             Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions or, in the Alternative, Motion to Comply with Plaintiff’s Demand for Inspection is DENIED.

Plaintiff requests this Court impose terminating sanctions against CR&R or, if the Court finds terminating sanctions inappropriate, issue and evidentiary sanctions. (See Motion for Sanctions, pp. 3-4.) Plaintiff requests this Court impose such sanctions because CR&R has previously been sanctioned for untimely discovery responses and because CR&R ignored “Plaintiff’s repeated requests for dates to inspect CRR’s facility” and served “a blatantly false objection.” (Id. at 3.) In the alternative, Plaintiff requests this Court order CR&R to permit inspection of the container at issue and its facility pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310. (See id. at 7).

In opposition, CR&R argues sanctions are inappropriate because Plaintiff unilaterally served a demand for inspection on a date that didn’t work for CR&R, its counsel, or Performance. (See Opposition, pg. 3.) CR&R also argues “it was approximately one year before CR&R was placed on notice of the incident. The location of the exact container and precise alleged defect contributing to the subject incident are therefore impossible to locate, inspect and/or replicate at this point. CR&R has instead offered Plaintiff the opportunity to inspect an exemplar container and intends to do so upon identification of an agreeable date and time.” (Id.)

In reply, Plaintiff reiterates that lesser sanctions are not appropriate as they “would permit CRR and Mr. Worthe to continue to benefit from their stalling tactics” and argues CR&R has provided no actual proof the container at issue cannot be found, nor has it ever stated it conducted a reasonable and diligent search but the container cannot be found. (Reply, pg. 2, fn. 3.)

The Court finds Plaintiff is not entitled to an order granting terminating, issue, or evidentiary sanctions. First, Plaintiff’s allusion to prior discovery sanctions against CR&R is unpersuasive. On July 7, 2022, a previously assigned judge awarded Plaintiff discovery sanctions for CR&R’s failure to timely serve responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission. (See Minute Order dated July 7, 2022.) On November 9, 2022, the court deemed Plaintiff’s remaining discovery motions moot due to CR&R’s filing of supplemental responses and declined to award discovery sanctions, finding they were unwarranted in light of CR&R’s counsel’s mistake and willingness to cooperate with Plaintiff’s counsel. (See Minute Order dated November 9, 2022.) Plaintiff does not refer to any other previous discovery issues that would lead this Court to believe there is a pattern of misconduct.

The only other basis for Plaintiff’s sanctions request is CR&R’s alleged refusal to comply with Plaintiff’s inspection demand. But CR&R did not refuse to provide dates; rather, CR&R and Performance objected to Plaintiff’s rushed demands to set an inspection date. (See Declaration of Hani Ganji filed July 15, 2024 (“Ganji Decl.”), ¶ 4, Ex. B.) Plaintiff first emailed CR&R and Performance about an inspection on May 6, 2024, and by May 29, 2024, Plaintiff had served a demand to inspect, even though the parties had not agreed upon a date for the inspection. (See Ganji Decl., ¶¶ 4-5, Exs. B-C.) The Court can understand why Plaintiff was in a hurry—the trial date was, at that time, set for August 15, 2024. (See Ganji Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff’s decision to wait over two and half years after he filed his Complaint to issue an inspection demand, however, is a crisis of his own making and the Court doesn’t find CR&R’s objection based on unavailability unreasonable. Moreover, Plaintiff’s Reply is silent as to what efforts, if any, he made after the Court continued the trial date or how the continued trial date might impact Plaintiff’s position. Whatever sense of urgency Plaintiff might have felt at the time he filed his Motion for Sanctions should surely have dissipated after the trial continuance. Similarly, Plaintiff is also silent as to whether they find CR&R’s compromise offer of inspecting an exemplar satisfactory. At the same time, Plaintiff hasn’t asked for an order compelling CR&R to make a further response formally stating they have conducted a diligent search and reasonable inquiry. Instead, Plaintiff has only used CR&R’s failure to do so as the basis for seeking the other specific relief in their motion.

CR&R therefore did not disobey an order of this Court, nor did it fail to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. If it has, Plaintiff did not include such information in its Motion for Sanctions. As a result, Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions is DENIED. For the same reasons, Plaintiff’s alternative request for an order compelling CR&R to permit inspection of the container at issue and its facility is also DENIED.

In denying Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions, of course, Plaintiff is not precluded from exploring other forms of relief to address the issue in the event the parties are unable to resolve it. Furthermore, the Court remains ready and willing to offer its assistance through an Informal Discovery Conference should the parties so desire.

1.               Sanctions

 “The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying¿a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)

As an alternative to his terminating, issue, or evidentiary sanctions request, Plaintiff requests $4,060.00 in discovery sanctions. (See Motion for Sanctions, pg. 8.) Because Plaintiff was not the prevailing party, Plaintiff’s request is denied.

CR&R also requests $6,750.00 in discovery sanctions. Although CR&R prevailed, the Court finds Plaintiff acted with substantial justification in bringing its Motion for Sanctions in light of the totality of the circumstances. Accordingly, the Court denies CR&R’s request for sanctions as well.

B.             Performance’s Motion to Compel the Physical Examination of Plaintiff is DENIED

Performance moves to compel Plaintiff submit to a physical examination pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2032.250(a) and 2032.220(a). Performance argues it has complied with its meet and confer requirements but that Plaintiff has continually, since November 17, 2023, refused to submit to an examination on the basis that the notice improperly omits the specific tests and procedures to be performed. (See Motion to Compel, pp. 3-6; Declaration of April Cristal filed July 3, 2024 (“Cristal Decl.”), ¶¶ 4-13, Exs. B-E.) Performance argues Plaintiff’s refusal is misplaced, as notices for mental examinations require a list of diagnostic tests but notices for physical examinations do not. (See Motion to Compel, pg. 9.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues “to conduct one physical exam without a court order, a defendant must ‘specify the . . . manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination[.]’” (Opposition, pg. 4 (quoting Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(c)).) Plaintiff also argues Performance’s Motion to Compel is untimely pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.310(c), which requires “a motion to compel a defense medical examination…be made within 45 days of a response.” (Opposition, pg. 4.)

In reply, Performance again argues its notice is code compliant and notes, though not at issue, Performance’s notice is for an exam within 75 miles of the residence of Plaintiff. (See Reply, pg. 2, fn. 1.) Performance also argues its Motion to Compel is timely as it is brought under Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.250, not § 2031.310, and § 2032.250 does not have the same timing requirements as § 2031.310. (See Reply, pg. 3.)

The Court finds Performance is not entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff submit to a physical examination for failure to properly meet and confer pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2016.040. As an initial matter, Performance’s Motion to Compel is not untimely for the reasons it listed. However, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure 2032.220(c), requiring a notice “identify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination,” it is reasonable for Plaintiff to demand more information besides Performance’s general notice that “[t]he examination to be conducted of Plaintiff consists of an orthopedic examination of Plaintiff's affected bodily areas as Plaintiff has claimed in this case, which are the subject of this litigation.” (Cristal Decl., ¶ 5, Ex. C.) In the correspondence provided to this Court it appears Performance’s counsel has refused (since November 2023) to further detail what tests their chosen medical provider will perform. (See Cristal Decl. ¶¶ 4-13, Exs. B-E.) Indeed, Performance does not further outline what testing will take place even in the instant Motion to Compel. Such failures indicate a lack of true meeting and conferring because meeting and conferring in good faith involves coming up with collaborative and creative solutions to try and resolve the dispute at hand. The correspondence provided to the Court reflects Performance’s counsel made no such effort. Instead, Performance’s counsel simply dug in and refused to budge from a position that does not have any credible legal support.

The Court DENIES Performance’s Motion to Compel.

1.               Sanctions

 “The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).) Misuse of the discovery process includes “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying¿a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)

Because Performance was not the prevailing party, Performance’s request for sanctions is denied.

Plaintiff also requests $3,000.00 in discovery sanctions, which accounts for (1) hour to meet and confer, (3) hours to draft the instant Opposition, and (2) hours to review Performance’s Reply and prepare for/appear at the hearing, at a billing rate of $500.00/hr.  The Court finds Performance’s counsel has misused the discovery process as defined by Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(i) because she failed to properly meet and confer in good faith prior to filing its Motion to Compel.

The Court finds Plaintiff’s counsel’s hourly rate reasonable given the prevailing rates in the Greater Los Angeles area for attorneys with his experience. However, the Court finds the number of hours requested to be inflated. Thus, the Court finds it appropriate to reduce Plaintiff’s counsel’s requested sanctions to $2,060.00, which accounts for (1) hour to meet and confer, (2) hours to draft the instant Opposition, and (1) hour to prepare for and attend the hearing, plus the $60.00 filing fee.

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Performance’s Motion to Compel and orders Performance’s counsel to pay Plaintiff’s counsel $1,560.00 in discovery sanctions on or before October 31, 2024.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion for Sanctions and denies both Plaintiff’s and CR&R’s requests for monetary sanctions in connection with it.  

The Court also DENIES Performance’s Motion to Compel. However, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in connection with it and orders Performance’s counsel to pay Plaintiff’s counsel $2,060.00 in discovery sanctions on or before October 31, 2024.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
