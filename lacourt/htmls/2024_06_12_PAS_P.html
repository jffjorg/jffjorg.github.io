DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21GDCV00974    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER ON PLAINTIFFS’ MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION, AND REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

The motion is granted in its entirety, and sanctions imposed in the amount of $1,710.00.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          Plaintiffs Futang Ji, Fan-Ling Wen, Yi-Ching Wu, Mingde Zheng, and Yong Zong sued defendants Punta Gorda Acquisitions, LLC and various affiliated entities on July 27, 2021. Plaintiffs filed their operative third amended complaint (TAC) on April 20, 2023, against Punta Gorda Acquisitions (PG Acquisitions), Punta Gorda Senior Living, LP dba The Palms of Punta Gorda Assisted Living (PG Senior Living), Madison Realty Equities, LLC (MR Equities), GR Madison Realty, LLC (GR Madison), Gary Langendoen (Langendoen), and Does 1-50.

The TAC alleged nine causes of action. On August 9, 2023, the Court sustained a demurrer to the fourth and ninth causes of action without leave to amend. The following claims remain:

1.       Breach of Third-Party Beneficiary Contract (v. PG Acquisitions and
PG Senior Living);

2.       Intentional Misrepresentation (v. Langendoen, PG Acquisitions, and
MR Equities);

3.       Negligent Misrepresentation (v. same);

5.       Breach of Fiduciary Duty (v. same);

6.       Unfair Business Practices (v. same);

7.       Unjust Enrichment (v. all defendants); and

8.       Conspiracy to Commit Fraud (v. all defendants).

          Plaintiffs allege, in brief, that they are Chinese nationals who invested in a purported real estate development project as a means of obtaining permanent U.S. residency. They provided what they believed was a construction loan to Defendants for use building an assisting living facility in Florida. Defendants allegedly absconded with the money.

          As relevant here, Plaintiffs served requests for admission, special interrogatories, and requests for production of documents on defendant PG Senior Living on November 16, 2024. On January 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed motions to compel further responses to each.

          The first motion, directed to Plaintiffs’ request for admissions, now comes before the Court. The motion is unopposed.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          The party to whom requests for admission have been directed shall respond in writing, under oath, separately to each request. (§ 2033.210(a).) The response must either admit or deny the matter, or state that a reasonable inquiry was made and the party lacks sufficient information to admit the matter. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220.) If the response is an admission, the admission may “[a]dmit so much of the matter involved in the request as is true, either as expressed in the request itself or as reasonably and clearly qualified by the responding party.” (Id., § 2033.220(b)(1).)

If the responding party makes an objection that the propounding party considers to be “without merit or too general,” or an answer which the propounding party finds evasive or incomplete, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further response. (Id., § 2033.290 (a)(2).) The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration and a separate statement or concise outline. (Id., § 2033.290(b).)

          III. ANALYSIS

          A. Procedural Considerations

          A party receiving requests for admission has thirty (30) days to respond. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).) If they do not timely respond, the propounding party may move to have the matters referred to in its requests admitted. (Id., § 2033.280.) If – as here – a party responds, but the propounding party contends the responses are insufficient, then the propounding party must move to compel further responses within forty-five (45) days of the most recent response.

          Plaintiffs received their most recent response on November 16, 2024. (Gesund Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiffs’ last day to move to compel was January 30, 2024. They filed their motions on January 26, 2024. The motion is timely.

          Counsel also met and conferred regarding the deficient responses, as required; in fact, Plaintiffs provided several extensions for PG Senior Living to provide supplemental responses. It did not do so. (Id., ¶¶ 7-4 and Exhs. 4-6.)

          Plaintiffs satisfy all procedural requirements.

          B. Further Responses

          PG Senior Living provided the same boilerplate objections to all of Plaintiffs’ RFAs. It “incorporate[d] a Preliminary Statement” reserving its right to supplement, then objected and refused to respond, claiming (1) the RFAs were all defective because they contained definitions in a preface, (2) each RFA was compound, and (3) each was vague and ambiguous.

          As to the latter two objections: contrary to PG Senior Living’s contentions, Plaintiffs’ RFAs are uniformly straightforward and direct. The objections are meritless.

As for PG Senior Living’s “definitions in a preface” objection based on Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.060: inclusion of a definitional preface is standard practice in written discovery. It conserves time and resources for the parties and the Court. The objection relies on form, rather than substance.

Moreover, PG Senior Living cites no law to establish that failure to comply with section 2033.260(d) justifies a total refusal to respond to RFAs when the responding party is capable of providing a complete and straightforward response based on available information. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220 [response to be “as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available ... permits”].) Perhaps there is some remedy for a propounding party’s failure to comply with section 2033.260(d). The remedy is not a waiver of the propounding party’s right to responses.

          PG Senior Living’s objections are overruled, and Plaintiffs’ motion is granted in its entirety.

          C. Sanctions

          “The court shall impose a monetary sanction ... against any party, person or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.290(c).)

          PG Senior Living filed no opposition demonstrating substantial justification or other circumstances relevant to its failure to respond.

          Plaintiffs are entitled to sanctions.

          Plaintiffs’ counsel attests to a reasonably hourly billing rate of $275. (Gesund Decl., § 15.)

However, Counsel seeks excessive hours. She attests to “20 hours in pursuit of this matter” and “another 10 hours drafting a Reply ... and preparing for and appearing at the hearing”. (Ibid.) Counsel does not clarify whether “pursuit of this matter” means the entire discovery dispute or only the RFAs. Certainly twenty hours devoted to this straightforward motion is excessive. The Court awards four (4) hours for drafting and one (1) hour for meet-and-confer efforts. There was no opposition, so the Court declines to award hours for drafting a Reply, but awards one (1) hour to prepare for and attend the hearing.

The Court awards six (6) billable hours at a rate of $275.00, plus $60.00 in filing fees, for a total of $1,710.00.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          The motion is granted in its entirety. Defendant PG Senior Living is ordered to provide complete, Code-compliant responses within twenty (20) days of this order. PG Senior Living is ordered to pay sanctions of $1,710.00 to Plaintiffs and/or their counsel of record within twenty (20) days.

Case Number: 23AHV00301    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS

I.        INTRODUCTION

           This is a case for personal injury and other damages filed by Chris Szajner (Szajner) arising from an alleged bed bug infestation in his hotel room. He sues Defendants Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc., Park Hotels and Resorts Inc.,  Hilton Garden Inn Arcadia/Pasadena area, and individual Pedro Figueroa for these injuries, arising out of his stay at the Hilton Garden Inn in Arcadia in February-March of 2021. He alleges he awoke on February 12, 2021 with a rash and bug bites. He alleges he changed rooms but the problem persisted. On March 10, 2021, he sought medical treatment and was diagnosed with bedbug bites. The amended complaint contains seven causes of action: (1) Battery, (2) Negligence,  (3)Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, (4) Fraudulent Concealment, (5) Private Nuisance, (6) Public Nuisance, and (7) Breach of Contract.

           This complaint was filed on February 9, 2023. On December 12, 2023, Plaintiff Szajner indicated that he was still attempting to serve the defendants in this case. Szajner filed a proof of service as to Hilton on March 21, 2024, indicating that Hilton was served by personal service in Delaware. On April 2, 2024, Defendant Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. (Hilton) filed a motion to quash service of summons. Plaintiff filed an opposition on May 30, 2024. Hilton filed a reply on June 4, 2024.

           The Court grants Hilton’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons.

II.       LEGAL STANDARD

           Code Civ. Proc. §418.10(a)(1) provides that a defendant, on or before the last day to plead, may file a motion to quash service of the summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction. 

California’s “long-arm” statute provides for jurisdiction “on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.” Code Civ. Proc. §410.10. 

The possible bases for jurisdiction in this state are the following: 

- Physical presence in the forum when served, aka “gotcha” jurisdiction; Burnham v. Superior Court (1990) 495 U.S. 604, 612; 

- Domicile in the forum state; Milliken v. Meyer (1940) 311 U.S. 457, 462; 

- General appearance in the action; Code Civ. Proc. §410.50(a); 

- Contractual consent/forum selection clause; National Equipment Rental, Ltd. v. Szukhent (1964) 375 U.S. 311, 315-316; 

- Minimum contacts; International Shoe Co. v. State of Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316; Burger King v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475. 

See Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023), ¶3:131. 

           CCP Section 581 provides that an action may be dismissed without prejudice as to a defendant if the motion is made pursuant to CCP Section 418.10. (CCP § 581.)

 

III.     ANALYSIS

           a. General Jurisdiction

To exercise general jurisdiction over a non-resident defendant, there must be such minimum contacts between the defendant and the forum state that the exercise of personal jurisdiction is allowed. (International Shoe Co. v. State of Washington (1945) 326 U.S. 310, 316; Burger King v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 475.) Here, Hilton urges that this is not the case. In support, Hilton provides the declaration of its vice president and assistant secretary, James O. Smith. (Smith Declaration.) He declares that Hilton is a Delaware corporation with its principal place of business in Virginia. It does not and did not own, operate, lease, manage the Arcadia Hilton Garden Inn property. (Smith Declaration ¶ 4.) Additionally, Hilton is not a franchisor of the Property, does not employ persons at the subject property nor has responsibility for the property. (Id.) Lastly, he declares that Hilton does not conduct any continuous or systematic business activities in California. (Smith Declaration ¶ 4.)

           In his opposition, Plaintiff does not offer any evidence that this Court has jurisdiction over Hilton. Instead, Plaintiff urges that “Counsel for Defendant did not attempt to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the present matter.” (Opposition p. 1: 24-26.) However, there is no meet and confer requirement before filing a CCP Section 418.10 motion.

As Plaintiff does not offer any evidence to support the Court’s exercise of jurisdiction, and the Defendant has offered evidence to the contrary, the Court finds that it cannot exercise general jurisdiction over Hilton. (See Doe v. Roman Catholic Archbishop of Cashel & Emly (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 209, 217, referencing Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 449.)

b. Specific Jurisdiction

To exercise specific personal jurisdiction over a defendant, there must be a substantial connection with a business relationship defendant has purposefully formed with California. (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 434, 448.) Here, Hilton urges that this is not the case. Despite Plaintiff’s allegations that Hilton owns and operates the subject property, Hilton urges that it does not. In support, Hilton provides the declaration of its vice president and assistant secretary, as described above. Its attorney declares that Hilton does not own, lease, operate, manage, occupy, or control the Arcadia Hilton Garden Inn property. (Smith Decl. ¶ 4.) He further declares that Hilton has no business relationship with the property, its employees, or the state of California. (Id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff does not offer any evidence to demonstrate this Court has jurisdiction over Hilton. As Plaintiff does not offer any evidence to support the Court’s exercise of jurisdiction, and the Defendant has offered evidence that it is not subject to specific jurisdiction, the Court finds that it cannot exercise specific jurisdiction over Defendant Hilton.

In sum, Hilton is not subject to general or specific personal jurisdiction in California.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            Specially appearing entity Hilton Worldwide Holdings Inc. motion to quash service of summons is GRANTED. Counsel for Hilton is ordered to provide notice of this ruling.
