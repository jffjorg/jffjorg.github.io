DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01020    Hearing Date: June 28, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL THE DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT’S PERSON MOST KNOWLEDGEABLE AND DENYING PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is a lemon law case. In November 2020, Plaintiff Debra K. Robinson (“Plaintiff”) purchased a new 2019 Land Rover Range Rover Evoque VIN No. SALVR2RX2KH341534. She alleges that her vehicle developed defects and brought this action against the vehicle’s manufacturer, Jaguar Land Rover North America, LLC (Jaguar), and the dealership, Land Rover Pasadena (Pasadena), for not repairing or otherwise repurchasing the vehicle. 

Procedural Background

On May 5, 2023, Plaintiff filed its Complaint alleging negligent repair and violations under the Song-Beverly Act for breach of express warranty, breach of the implied warranty of merchantability and violation of its Section 1793.2.

On July 12, 2023, Defendant filed its Answer.

On October 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Further Responses to its Request for Production, Set One, which was taken off calendar when Defendant produced supplemental documents. (3/4/24 Minute Order)

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK Deposition”) and Request for Production of Documents concurrently with a proposed order, separate statement, and supporting declaration.

On June 16, 2024, Defendant filed an Opposition and supporting declaration.

On June 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Reply.

On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff Debra K. Robinson served Defendant Jaguar Land Rover North America LLC by electronic mail with:

1.     Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable containing 34 Matters for Examination requested for December 15, 2023, and

2.     Requests for Production of Documents containing 17 written requests for the PMK Deposition

On December 1, 2023, Defendant served objections to Plaintiff’s PMK Deposition. (Motion to Compel, p. 1.)

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff served the instant motion before the court to Defendant by electronic mail (Motion to Compel, p. 13.)

Trial for this case is set to begin on January 28, 2025. (Opp’n., p.1.)

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A.    Law Governing Depositions

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent. (CCP § 2025.230.)

Code Civ. Proc. Section 2025.450¿

(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:¿

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.¿

. . .¿

(g)(1) If a motion under subdivision (a) is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.¿

 

B.    Law Governing Requests for Production

“Any party may obtain discovery by inspecting, copying, testing, or sampling documents, tangible things, land or other property, and electronically stored information in the possession, custody, or control of any other party to the action.” (CCP § 2031.010.) “If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.” (CCP § 2025.480(a).)

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A.    Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

Code Civ. Proc. Section 2030.290 provides that when a party to whom interrogatories has been propounded fails to serve a timely response, that party waives any objection to the interrogatories; it further provides that the propounding party may move for an order compelling a response to the interrogatory. Subdivision (c) provides for monetary sanctions in connection with such motion. (CCP § 2030.290.)

B.    Motion to Compel Further Production of Documents

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, parties may move for a further response to requests for production of documents where a statement of compliance is incomplete or where a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.  A motion to compel further response to requests for production “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) 

The Code of Civil Procedure contemplates three forms of proper responses to a request for production:  a statement of compliance in full or in part (CCP § 2031.220); a statement of inability to comply (CCP § 2031.230); and a partial objection coupled with a statement of compliance or representation of inability to comply (CCP § 2031.240).   

A statement of compliance under section 2031.220 has two parts: (1) the responding party “shall state that the production, inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, and related activity demanded, will be allowed either in whole or in part,” and (2) “that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.” 

A representation of inability to comply under section 2031.230 has three parts: the statement must (1) affirm that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply, and (2) the statement shall specify whether the inability to comply is because “the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party.”  The third part comes into play if the responding party knows or believes someone else has possession of the documents:  if so, “[t]he statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” 

Finally, California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires that all motions or responses involving further discovery contain a separate statement with the text of each request, the response, and a statement of factual and legal reasons for compelling further responses.  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(3).)   

C.    Request for Admissions

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”  CCP § 2033.010.  “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . . .”  CCP § 2033.250(a).

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c).

D.    Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 is a general statute authorizing the Court to impose discovery sanctions for “misuse of the discovery process,” which includes (without limitation) a variety of conduct such as: making, without substantial justification, an unmeritorious objection to discovery; making an evasive response to discovery; and unsuccessfully and without substantial justification making or opposing a motion to compel or limit discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.) 

If sanctions are sought, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.040 requires that the notice specify the identity of the person against whom sanctions are sought and the type of sanction requested, that the motion be supported in the points and authorities, and the facts be set forth in a declaration supporting the amount of any monetary sanction.   

With regard to a motion to compel further responses to requests for production, Code of Civil Procedure Section 2031.310, subdivision (h) provides that sanctions shall be awarded against any party, person or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the Court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

II.             DEMURRER

A.    Legal Standard

Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(e) provides for a demurrer on the basis that a complaint fails to state a cause of action. A demurrer admits, provisionally for purposes of testing the pleading, all material facts properly pleaded. Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1247. A demurrer is treated as “admitting all material facts properly pleaded,” but not the truth of “contentions, deductions or conclusions of law.” Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.

The general rule on demurrer is that the pleadings are “deemed to be true, however improbable they may be.” Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.4th 593, 604.

Questions of plaintiff’s ability to prove unlikely allegations are of no concern. Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 213-214.

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.  A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb). Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc. §452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.

 

B.    Meet and Confer Requirement Met

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a).)  

Counsel for Cross-Defendants, Jonathan J. Elia, Esq., attempted to meet and confer with Cross-Complainant on March 14, 2024. (Elia Decl., ¶¶ 8-9; Exhs. A, B.)

The Court finds Defendant’s attempts to meet and confer are sufficient for purposes of the instant motion.

 

III.      ANALYSIS

A.    Uncertainty of the First Amended Cross Complaint

The FACC is uncertain because Cross-Complainants neglect to attach the referenced Exhibits A-D to the FACC.

Code Civ. Proc. §430.10(f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain.

Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.”  (Brea v.¿McGlashan¿(1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.)  “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.)  “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.”  (People v. Lim¿(1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)¿¿

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 C3d 311, 318, 216 CR 718, 721; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 CA4th 968, 994, 11 CR3d 45, 64 (citing text)]

 “Face of the complaint” includes matters shown in exhibits attached to the complaint and incorporated by reference, or in a superseded complaint in the same action. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 CA3d 91, 94, 234 CR 178, 179-180); Barnett v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (2001) 90 CA4th 500, 505, 108 CR2d 657, 659 — “we rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits and treat as surplusage the pleader's allegations as to the legal effect of the exhibits.”

Where a demurrer is made upon this ground, it must distinctly specify exactly how or why the pleading is uncertain, and where such uncertainty appears (by reference to page and line numbers of the complaint). (See Fenton v. Groveland Comm. Services Dist. (1982) 135 CA3d 797, 809, 185 CR 758, 765 (disapproved on other grounds by Katzberg v. Regents of Univ. of Calif. (2002) 29 C4th 300, 328, 127 CR2d 482, 504, fn. 30)]

Cross-Defendants’ demurrer is based on uncertainty because the FACC references Exhibits A through D but fails to attach the referenced exhibits to the pleading. (Demurrer, pp. 5-6.) Cross-Defendants recognize that Exhibits A-D were attached to the original Cross-Complaint filed January 17, 2024, but missing from the FACC. (Id.)  

Cross-Complainants argue that Cross-Defendants are familiar with the referenced exhibits because Cross-Defendants were previously provided with Exhibits A-D. (Opp’n., p. 7.) Cross-Complainants concede that if granted leave to file a Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SACC”), the relevant exhibits will be attached. (Id.)

            Cross-Defendants do not allege that the pleading is so incomprehensible that they cannot respond, indeed they did respond. Since there is a possibility of curing this defect, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer as to the entire FACC is sustained with leave to amend. While this ruling is dispositive of the entire complaint, in the interest of judicial economy, the Court will address the deficiencies in the other causes of action.

 

B.    First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

The FACC does not state a cause of action for breach of contract because the facts to support Cross-Defendants’ breach are insufficiently pled. To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

Cross-Complainants’ cause of action for breach of contract is based on Cross-Defendants’ alleged breach of the executed STA, dated May 2023. (FACC, ¶ 55.) Cross-Complainants’ allege section 2.1 of the STA was violated when the NAAC Lawsuit was filed because the pending suit subjected the Faith7 common stock shares to a lien.

2.1 Ownership of Shares. Seller is the sole record and beneficial owner of the Shares, and such Shares are to be sold pursuant to this Agreement. Except as set forth in the Prior Investment Agreements, which will terminate upon Closing, the Shares are not subject to any lien, charge, pledge, claim, restrictions on transfer, mortgage, security interest, or title defect or other encumbrance of any sort or to any rights of first refusal, and Seller has not granted any rights to purchase the Shares to any other person or entity. (emphasis added)

(FACC, ¶ 55.)

As of June 27, 2024, the NAAC Lawsuit is pending before Department 51 in the Stanley Mosk Courthouse with no judgment rendered and no writ of attachment filed. The Court finds no case law to support the argument that a pending lawsuit functions as a “lien” unless and until a final judgment is issued or a writ of attachment is filed.

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that Cross-Defendant Bautista “affirmatively represented that the shares to be sold to Cross-Complainants are not subject to any claim, title defect, or other encumbrance of any sort.” Because the FACC grounds its breach allegation on a lien theory, Cross-Complainants have not alleged sufficient facts to state a cause of action for breach of contract.

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action for breach of contract is sustained, with leave to amend.

 

C.    Second Cause of Action: Fraud

The FACC does not meet the elevated pleading standard for fraud.

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc. §452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Each element must be pleaded with particularity. (Glaski v. Bank of America (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1090; Heritage Pacific Financial, LLC v. Monroy (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 972, 989.) Fraud actions are subject to “strict requirements of particularity in pleading.  . . . Accordingly, the rule is followed everywhere that fraud must be specifically pleaded. The effect of this rule is twofold: (a) General pleading of the legal conclusion of ‘fraud’ is insufficient; the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged. (b) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner (i.e., factually and specifically), and the policy of liberal construction of the pleadings ... will not ordinarily be invoked to sustain a pleading defective in any material respect.” (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 216, superseded by statute on other grounds.)

A fraud cause of action must contain: “(1) a knowingly false representation by the defendant; (2) an intent to deceive or induce reliance; (3) justifiable reliance by the plaintiff; and (4) resulting damages.” (Service by Medallion, Inc. v. Clorox Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1807, 1816.)

Fraud based on concealment requires that “(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.”  (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310-311.)  Less specificity is required to plead fraud by concealment.  (Ibid.) “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.” (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1384.)

Cross-Complainants’ second cause of action is against all Cross-Defendants for fraud and deceit. (FACC, ¶ 60.) Cross-Complainants argue that Cross-Defendants Bautista and Tamashiro intentionally concealed a material fact, i.e., the filing of the NAAC Lawsuit and its negative impact on potential investors to induce Cross-Complainants to invest in Chime TV. (Id.) Cross-Complainants also identify Cross-Defendants as “fiduciaries” to Cross-Complainants, “under a duty to disclose to disclose the NAAC Lawsuit … and its impact on investors”. (FACC ¶ 61.)

Cross-Complainants failed to allege sufficient facts to support their fraud-based cause of action. Cross-Complainants appear to be proceeding on a theory of fraudulent concealment. The cause of action is not alleged with sufficient factual specificity to support fraud.

The allegations are made collectively as to all Cross-Defendants. Even in naming Bautista and Tamashiro, Cross-Complainants do not identify which individual intentionally suppressed material facts, when, and by what means. In addition, Cross-Complainants failed to allege facts supporting the existence of a fiduciary duty for purposes of the concealment cause of action. 

Accordingly, Cross-Complainant’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action for fraud is sustained, with leave to amend.

 

D.    Third Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty (Direct) And Fifth Cause of Action: Breach of Duty of Loyalty (Direct)

            The stockholder's individual suit is a suit to enforce a right against the corporation which the stockholder possesses as an individual. (Paclink Communications Internat. v. Superior Court, 90 Cal. App. 4th 958, 960.) Where conduct, including mismanagement by corporate officers, causes damage to the corporation, it is the entity that must bring suit; the individual shareholder may not bring an action for indirect personal losses (i.e., decrease in stock value) sustained as a result of the overall harm to the entity. (Schrage v. Schrage, 69 Cal. App. 5th 126, 126.)

 

                                                  i.     Third Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty (Direct)

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship, breach of fiduciary duty, and damages.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.)

A fiduciary duty is founded upon a special relationship imposed by law or when “confidence is reposed by persons in the integrity of others,” who voluntarily accept the confidence.  (Tri-Growth Centre City, Ltd. v. Silldorf, Burdman, Duignan & Eisenberg (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 1139, 1150; see CACI 4100, et seq.) “‘[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.’” (City of Hope Nat’l Med. Ctr. v. Genentech (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386.)

“[B]ecause of ‘[t]he vagueness of the common law definition of the confidential relation that gives rise to a fiduciary duty, and the range of the relationships that can potentially be characterized as fiduciary,’ the ‘essential elements’ have been distilled as follows: ‘1) The vulnerability of one party to the other which 2) results in the empowerment of the stronger party by the weaker which 3) empowerment has been solicited or accepted by the stronger party and 4) prevents the weaker party from effectively protecting itself.’ (Richelle L. v. Roman Catholic Archbishop (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 257, 272 (“Richelle”).)  In short, vulnerability ‘is the necessary predicate of a confidential relation,’ and ‘the law treats [it] as “absolutely essential”’…” (Persson v. Smart Inventions (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 1141, 1161.)

            Cross-Complainants’ breach of fiduciary duty cause of action is based on the following allegations: (1) Cross-Defendants were “directors and officers of Chime TV (f/k/a Faith 7) … individuals tasked with overseeing its operations”, (2) Cross-Defendants owed fiduciary duties to Cross-Complainants, (3) Cross-Defendants breached that duty by misappropriating Chime TV’s assets for “personal and/or non-business-related expenses”, (4) harming Cross-Complainants, and (5) entitling them to a punitive damage award. (FACC ¶¶ 67-73.)

            Cross-Complainants fail to allege sufficient facts to support the breach of fiduciary duty cause of action. Cross-Complainants fail to allege facts suggesting Cross-Defendants owed Cross-Complainants fiduciary duties based on the transaction. Cross-Complainants bring this claim against all Cross-Defendants and do not allege how each Cross-Defendant owed them a duty, beyond stating their roles as directors and officers of Chime TV.

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty (direct) is sustained, with leave to amend.

 

                                                ii.     Fifth Cause of Action: Breach of Duty of Loyalty (Direct)

The elements of a cause of action for breach of the duty of loyalty of persons employed in positions of trust, by analogy to a claim for breach of fiduciary duty, are (1) the existence of a relationship giving rise to a duty of loyalty, (2) one or more breaches of that duty, and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach. Huong Que, Inc. v. Luu, 150 Cal. App. 4th 400, 58 (2007)

To state a cause of action for a breach of the duty of loyalty, the plaintiff must plead: (1) The existence of a relationship giving rise to a duty of loyalty; (2) one or more breaches of that duty; and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach. (Huong Que, Inc. v. Luu (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 400, 410.) The duty of loyalty does not depend upon contract, but the nature of the parties’ relationship. Such a relationship includes agent-principal, attorney-client, employer-employee, trustee-trustor, director-shareholder, partners-partnership. (See CACI 4102.)

 

Cross-Complainants’ breach of duty of loyalty claim is based on the following allegations: (1) as directors and officers of Chime TV, Cross-Defendants owed Cross-Complainants a duty of loyalty, obligating Cross-Defendants to refrain from self-dealing, (2) Cross-Defendants misappropriated Chime TV’s funds for personal use and acted “intentionally and/ or recklessly”, (4) causing damage to Cross-Complainants. (FACC, ¶¶ 83-87.)

Cross-Complainants have not sufficiently stated a cause of action for breach of the duty of loyalty because they have not alleged facts supporting the existence of a fiduciary duty between themselves and Cross-Defendants. Cross-Complainants do not allege any facts to show that they held a special relationship of director-shareholder or partners to invoke such duties.  

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action for breach of the duty of loyalty (direct) is sustained, with leave to amend.

 

E.    Fourth Cause of Action: Breach of Fiduciary Duty (Derivative) And Sixth Cause of Action: Breach of Duty of Loyalty (Derivative)

Under California law, ‘a shareholder cannot bring a direct action for damages against management on the theory their alleged wrongdoing decreased the value of his or her stock (e.g., by reducing corporate assets and net worth). The corporation itself must bring such an action, or a derivative suit may be brought on the corporation's behalf.’ [Citation.] A different rule would ‘authorize multitudinous litigation and ignore the corporate entity.’” (Schuster v. Gardner (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 305, 312 [25 Cal. Rptr. 3d 468]; accord, Grosset v. Wenaas, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 1108.)

“An action is deemed derivative ‘“if the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, or to the whole body of its stock and property without any severance or distribution among individual holders, or it seeks to recover assets for the corporation or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.” (Heshejin v. Rostami, 54 Cal. App. 5th 984, 994.)

A breach of fiduciary duty cause of action based on mismanagement of the corporation resulting in the diminution of value of the corporation is a derivative claim. (Schrage v. Schrage, (2021) 69 Cal.App.5th 155,156.)

To bring or maintain a derivative action, Corporations Code section 800, subdivision (b)(1), 19 requires the plaintiff to own stock at the time of the challenged action—subject to an exception referred to as “the continuing wrong doctrine.” (Kruss v. Booth (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 699, 724–725) as well as when the action is filed and throughout the duration of the litigation. (See Grosset v. Wenaas (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1100, 1119.) (Favila v. Katten Muchin Rosenman LLP, 188 Cal. App. 4th 189, 214.)

Because a derivative claim does not belong to the stockholder asserting it, standing to maintain such a claim is justified only by the stockholder relationship and the indirect benefits made possible thereby, which furnish the stockholder with an interest and incentive to seek redress for injury to the corporation. [Citations.] Once this relationship ceases to exist, the derivative plaintiff lacks standing because he or she ‘no longer has a financial interest in any recovery pursued for the benefit of the corporation.’ [Citations.] As one court put it, allowing a plaintiff to retain standing despite the loss of stock ownership would produce ‘the anomalous result that a plaintiff with absolutely no “dog in the hunt” is permitted to pursue a right of action that belongs solely to the corporation.’(Favila, at 214.)

Cross-Complainants derivative cause of action for breach of duty of loyalty and breach of fiduciary duty arise from the following allegations: (1) as directors and officers of Chime TV Cross-Defendant Bautista and Tamashiro owed Cross-Complainants duties, (2) Cross-Defendants breached those duties by misappropriating funds belonging to Chime TV by using Chime TV’s assets and accounts to pay for hundreds of thousands of Cross-Defendants’ non-business-related expenses without shareholder knowledge, and failing to ensure timely payment of debts to vendors, (3) causing harm to Cross-Complainants. (FACC, ¶¶ 74-81, 90-93).

Cross-Defendants demur to the derivative claims under the premise that Cross-Complainants lack standing. Because Cross-Complainants alleged that Cross-Defendant breached their fiduciary duties by misappropriating the funds of Chime TV, any recovery would be awarded to Chime TV, not to Cross-Complainants.

Based on the alleged harm to Chime TV, Cross-Complainants were required to satisfy the pre-suit requirements set forth in Corporations Code § 800(b)(1) due to a premature relationship and § 800(b)(2) for failure to state with specificity their efforts to inform the Board of Directors to bring this cross complaint.

Cross-Complainants lack derivative standing because the relationship between them and the corporation had not yet formed since Cross-Complainants did not become shareholders of Chime TV until the May 30, 2023 STA and CSPA became effective. On January 2, 2024, Cross-Complainants made a demand on Chime TV’s Board of Directors to take legal action against Cross-Defendants, which the Board of Directors denied. (FACC, ¶¶ 10-12.)

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty (derivative) is sustained with leave to amend, and;

Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer to the sixth cause of action for breach of duty of loyalty (derivative) is sustained with leave to amend.

 

F.    Seventh Cause of Action: Violation of Penal Code § 496

Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment in a county jail for not more than one year, or imprisonment pursuant to subdivision (h) of Section 1170. However, if the value of the property does not exceed nine hundred fifty dollars ($950), the offense shall be a misdemeanor, punishable only by imprisonment in a county jail not exceeding one year, if such person has no prior convictions for an offense specified in clause (iv) of subparagraph (C) of paragraph (2) of subdivision (e) of Section 667 or for an offense requiring registration pursuant to subdivision (c) of Section 290. (Cal. Pen. Code § 496)

            Pursuant to the plain language of section 496, an intangible ownership interest in a company does not constitute "property." (See In re Ethan C. (2012) 54 Cal. 4th 610, 627 (statutory language should be given its usual and ordinary meaning, and ordinarily the plain meaning of the statute governs). This situation should be contrasted from Siry Investment, LP. v. Farkhondehpour (2022) 13 Cal.5th 333, which involved the fraudulent diversion of tangible partnership funds.

The basis of Cross-Complainants’ allegations is that (1) Cross-Defendants knowingly received, possessed, concealed, and/or withheld monies belonging to Chime TV, (2) that Chime TV sustained damages as a result; and (3)Chime TV and its shareholders are entitled to treble damages and attorneys’ fees. (FACC, ¶¶ 95-97.)

The above allegations are uncertain and insufficient to allege a violation of Penal Code section 496 because Cross-Complainants fail to plead sufficient facts of tangible property. Specifically, Cross-Complainants allege that Cross-Defendants Faith Bautista and Roes 1 through 11 “stole Chime TV’s assets and monies to pay for hundreds of thousands of dollars of Cross-Defendants’ personal and/or non-business-related expenses without shareholder knowledge or consent”. (FACC, ¶ 95.)

Notably, the FACC does not allege which assets or monies belonged to Chime TV, nor how Cross-Defendant Bautista personally obtained possession of the funds.

Instead, the allegations against Cross-Defendant Bautista rely entirely on a possible agency relationship with Chime TV, rooted in an intangible ownership interest – the common stock shares of Chime TV (f/k/a Faith7) which are intangible and possess an unascertainable value.

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the seventh cause of action for violation of Penal Code § 496 is sustained without leave to amend.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER           

Cross-Defendants’ Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to all causes of action, except the 7th cause of action, for which the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend.

Cross-Complainants have 20 days to file a second amended complaint.

Cross-Defendant to give notice.
