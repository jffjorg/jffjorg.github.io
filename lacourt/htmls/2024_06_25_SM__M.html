DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV01204    Hearing Date: June 25, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Zarabi, et al., v. Perez, et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCV01204

MOTION:                  Motion for Terminating Sanctions against Defendant John Perez

HEARING DATE:   6/25/2024

 

Legal Standard

           

If a party fails to obey a court order compelling it to provide a discovery response, “the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction . . . In lieu of or in addition to this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction . . ..” (CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2030.300(e), 2031.300(c), 2031.320(c).) Misuse of the discovery process, which includes disobeying a court order to provide discovery, is conduct subject to sanctions. (CCP § 2023.010(g).) Possible sanctions are:

 

(a) [A] monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct….

 

(b) [A]n issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.

 

(c) [A]n evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.

 

(d) [A] terminating sanction by one of the following orders:

 

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

 

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

 

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

 

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.

 

(e) [A] contempt sanction by an order treating the misuse of the discovery process as a contempt of court.

 

(CCP § 2023.030.)

 

The party seeking to impose sanctions need only show the failure to obey earlier discovery orders. (Puritan Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 171 Cal.App.3d 877, 884 [interpreting former statute dealing with “refusal” to comply].) However, numerous cases hold that severe sanctions (i.e., terminating or evidentiary sanctions) for failure to comply with a court order are allowed only where the failure was willful. (R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495; Vallbona v. Springer (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 1525, 1545; Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) The burden of proof then shifts to the party seeking to avoid sanctions to establish a satisfactory excuse for his or her conduct. (Corns v. Miller (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 195, 201.)

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiffs Daniel Zarabi, Shirin Nourollah, and Parvin Nourollah move for terminating sanctions against Defendant John M. Perez. Plaintiffs argue that this sanction is justified because Defendant refused and failed to provide any substantive responses to any of Plaintiffs’ discovery requests and has refused to cooperate with the Court’s order compelling Defendant to provide further discovery responses and to appear for his deposition. Defendant opposes the motion asserting that the requested terminating sanctions would be improper because Defendant’s insurance agent has joined the action and is actively defending against the liability and damages claims.

 

The record demonstrates the need for greater sanctions against Defendant. On December 8, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ motions to compel further responses against Defendant. On January 5, 2024, the Court ordered Defendant to provide further responses to Plaintiffs’ discovery requests within 20 days and to appear for his deposition within 10 days at the noticed location, at a mutually agreeable time. (Ames Decl. ¶8, Ex. 2.) Defendant did not provide any further responses by the deadline of January 25, 2024. In response to meet and confer efforts, Defense counsel confirmed that they could not reach Defendant. (Ames Decl. ¶¶10-13; Exs. 3, 4.) The Court notes that lesser sanctions, such as monetary sanctions, have been ineffective to compel defendant to answer discovery. The Court further finds that Defendant’s disobedience is willful. (See Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co., (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 250 [a party’s failure to keep his attorney informed as to his whereabouts may be deemed “willful” discovery abuse, as to hold otherwise “would permit a litigant to disappear and not be obliged to comply with our discovery statutes”].) Given Defendant’s willful refusal to obey court orders and participate in this action, greater sanctions are certainly warranted.

 

            The Court finds the case of Thomas v. Luong instructive. (Thomas v. Luong (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 76, 81-82.) In Thomas, the court found that the trial court abused its discretion in striking the defendant’s answer and entering default under similar circumstances. There, defendant Luong initially answered a first set of written discovery without issue, but later failed to appear for his noticed deposition or substantively answer any subsequent interrogatories. (Id., at 78-79.) Luong’s counsel had lost contact with Luong. (Id. at 79.) After Luong failed to appear or respond to discovery, the plaintiff moved for terminating sanctions against Luong. Meanwhile, Luong’s counsel advised their prospective arbitrator that Luong would be admitting liability, and that they would only contest damages. (Id.) Despite this offer to stipulate to liability, the trial court granted plaintiffs’ motions for terminating sanctions and entered a default judgment.  (Id. at 80.)

 

The Thomas court held that full terminating sanctions were improper in such an instance, because such a sanction exceeded what was reasonably required to protect the plaintiff’s interest and constituted a forfeiture of Defendant’s rights. (Id. at 82.) Critically, a lesser sanction was available: striking defendant’s answer contesting liability and causation. The court reasoned that if liability was not at issue, then there was no need for Luong’s deposition or any new answers to interrogatories. The court explained that “had the trial court exercised the lesser sanction of striking the defendant's pleadings insofar as they contested liability or causation, the deposition of the defendant would have been superfluous. If plaintiff required further protection, the additional lesser sanction of precluding defendant from testifying at trial (or arbitration) could have been imposed.” (Ibid.) Further, if liability is presumed, “much of the information sought by the form interrogatories becomes irrelevant and, if additional protection were required, the court could have imposed a further lesser sanction of denying introduction of evidence contradicting or amending the prior answers.” (Id.) In light of the availability of alternative lesser sanctions, the Thomas court reversed the trial court’s order striking the answer. (Id. at 83.)

 

            Here, the same lesser sanction is available against Defendant. The Court may strike Defendant’s answer only insofar as his denial of liability and causation or grant an issue sanction establishing Defendant’s liability and causation. Either way, the Court would establish liability against Defendant, but leave open the question of damages. Such a sanction is more narrowly tailored to fit the discovery abuse at bar than entering default against Defendant. Defendant’s discovery abuse has unjustifiably hindered Plaintiffs’ ability to prove liability. However, as with Thomas, Plaintiffs would not need discovery from Defendant to prove their damages. Therefore, the sanctions should not extend to establish damages.

 

Accordingly, the motion for sanctions is GRANTED in part. The Court strikes Defendant Perez’s answer insofar as it denies the complaint’s allegations of liability and causation. (Ans. ¶ 1.)

CASE NAME:           La Verne Rambla LLC v. Jayco Premium Finance of CA Inc., et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCV03468

MOTION:                  Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

HEARING DATE:   6/25/2024

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (CCP § 284(2).) The attorney seeking to withdraw must take “reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the rights of the client, including giving due notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel.” (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, 3-700(A)(2). See, e.g., Vann v. Shilleh (1975) [holding withdrawal prejudicial where attorney withdraw from the representation of defendant on the Friday before trial began the following Monday].) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.) 

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (CRC 3.1362(a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (CRC, 3.1362(d).) When a client is served by mail, the attorney’s declaration must indicate that the client’s address was confirmed within the last 30 days and how it was confirmed. (Id.) If the attorney is unable to confirm the client’s current address, the declaration must state the reasonable efforts made within the last 30 days to obtain the client’s current address. (Id.)

 

Additionally, the declaration “must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship why” a motion is brought instead of filing a substitution of attorney. (CRC, 3.1362(c).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Dirk O. Julander and M. Adam Tate move to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff La Verne Rambla LLC. Counsel submits all the mandatory forms. Counsel states that an irreparable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship has occurred due to their client’s refusal to pay incurred legal fees. Counsel served his client by mail at his last known address, which was confirmed within the past 30 days by several methods, including via email, Secretary of State records, and Westlaw records. No trial is set, and no dispositive motions are on calendar. Therefore, the client will likely not be prejudiced by the withdrawal.

 

Accordingly, the motion is tentatively GRANTED. Counsel must note any new hearing dates on the order (MC-053). The order is effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the order on the client. Until then, counsel remains counsel of record.
