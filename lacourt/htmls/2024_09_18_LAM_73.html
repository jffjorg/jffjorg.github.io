DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV10614    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: 73

09/18/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

WEAVER, et al. v. LANCE JAY ROBBINS PALOMA PARTNERSHIP, et al. (23STCV10614)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:   James Fazio III (Gordon Rees Scully Mansukhani, LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:           Lisa Salisbury (Salisbury Group, Inc.)

 

MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS (filed 04/10/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 20, 2023, Plaintiffs H. Duane Weaver (“Weaver”) and Negative Asset Limited Partnership filed this action against Defendants Lance Jay Robbins Paloma Partnership, 7th St. Associates, Inc. and Lance Jay Robbins (“Robbins”).

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; (2) Breach of Contract; (3) Abuse of Control; (4) Appointment of Receiver; (5) Declaratory Relief; (6) Accounting; (7) Violation of Inspection Rights; (8) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and/or Retention; and (9) Negligence.

 

The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff Weaver and Defendant Robbins formed a partnership in the 1980s to acquire, own and operate a 58-unit apartment building located at 15 Paloma Avenue, Venice, California 90291. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed in their duty to properly manage and operate the subject property pursuant to the partnership agreement. Defendants had hired Concord Real Estate Service, Inc. (“Concord”) to manage the subject property.

 

On April 10, 2024, Defendants filed its instant motion for judgment on the pleadings, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs lack standing to bring any claim for direct injury, and the claims for direct injury alleged in the first, second, and fourth through seventh causes of action must be dismissed.

o   The alleged mismanagement of the Property is an injury to the Partnership, not to individual limited partners.

o   In their complaint, Plaintiffs originally pled alleged nine causes of action, but the third cause of action for breach of control was dismissed by this Court on or about September 28, 2023. Of the remaining eight causes of action, the first cause of action (breach of fiduciary duty), the second cause of action (breach of contract), and the fourth cause of action (appointment of receiver) all purport to allege both direct and derivative claims. The fifth, sixth and seventh causes of action (for declaratory relief, an accounting and for an inspection of the Partnership’s books and records) are all styled as direct claims arising from the alleged mismanagement of the Partnership. The eighth cause of action (negligent hiring, supervision, and/or retention) and the ninth cause of action (negligence) are styled as derivative claims only and are not the subject of this motion. Plaintiffs lack standing to bring direct claims.

 

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The motion is improper as to the first, second and fourth causes of action because it would only partially resolve those claims

·       A limited partner may maintain direct claims against a managing partner and/or a general partner

·       Should the Court be inclined to grant the motion, Plaintiffs request leave to amend

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiffs lack standing to bring direct claims for the mismanagement of the partnership.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

It is well established in California that either prior to trial, but after the time to answer or demur has passed, or at the trial the plaintiff or the defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings and that the appropriate ground for such a motion is the same as that arguable by general demurrer, namely, the failure to state a cause of action or defense.  (Dobbins v. Hardister (1966) 242 Cal.App.2d 787, 791; See also Sofias v. Bank of America (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 583, 586 [The non statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings can be made at any time, even during trial, since the grounds for a general demurrer are never waived.], see also Code of Civ. Proc. §438(f).)   

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings or by matters that can be judicially noticed. (See, e.g., Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (TRG 1998) §§ 7:275, 7:322; Lance Camper Manufacturing Corp. v. Republic Indemnity Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 194, 198.) Presentation of extrinsic evidence is therefore not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Id.; Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999.)  Both a demurrer and a motion for judgment on the pleadings accept as true all material factual allegations of the challenged pleading, unless contrary to law or to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.  (Mechanical Contractors Assn. v. Greater Bay Area Assn. (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 672, 677; Edwards v. Centex Real Estate Corp, (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 15, 27.) 

 

In the case of either a demurrer or a motion for judgment on the pleadings, leave to amend should be granted if there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action. (Virginia G. v. ABC Unified School Dist. (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1852.)  

 

B.      Meet and Confer

 

Before addressing the merits, the Court notes that the Motion is accompanied by a meet and confer declaration as required by Code of Civil Procedure Section 439(a). Defendants’ counsel states he met via videoconference with Plaintiff’s counsel on April 1, 2024 regarding the issues raised in the instant motion, but the parties were unable to reach a resolution. (Davisson Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court finds the moving party has satisfied its meet and confer obligation.

 

C.      Discussion

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs lack standing to bring any claim for direct injury, and the claims for direct injury alleged in the first, second, and fourth through seventh causes of action must be dismissed.

 

a.       First, Second and Fourth causes of action

 

First, Defendants move for judgment on the pleadings on the first, second and fourth causes of action only “to the extent that those claims purport to allege direct claims against the Defendants.” (Motion, p. 8.)

 

Plaintiffs’ first cause of action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty, second cause of action for Breach of Contract, and fourth cause of action for Appointment of a Receiver are brought by Plaintiffs directly in their own names and derivatively pursuant to California Corporations Code § 15702.

 

However, the Court does not find that Defendants can move for judgment on only the direct claims and not the derivative claims of these causes of action. Judgment on the pleadings, like a general demurrer, does not lie as to a portion of a cause of action and, if any part of a cause of action is properly pleaded, the motion will be denied. (Fire Ins. Exch. v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal. App. 4th 446, 452.) Therefore, Defendants cannot move for judgment on only a portion of these causes of action.

 

Based on the foregoing, judgment on Plaintiffs’ first, second and fourth causes of action are DENIED.

 

b.      Fifth, Sixth and Seventh causes of action

 

Next, Defendants move for judgment on the pleadings on the fifth cause of action for declaratory relief, sixth cause of action for accounting and seventh cause of action for violation of inspection rights arguing that Plaintiffs lack standing to bring these direct claims.

 

The California Supreme Court has clearly laid out the difference between individual and derivative causes of action.  (Jones v. H.F. Ahmanson & Co. (1969) 1 Cal.3d 93, 106–107.)  An individual action is “ ‘a suit to enforce a right against the corporation which the stockholder possesses as an individual.’ ”  (Id. at 107.)  An action is derivative if “ ‘the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, or to the whole body of its stock and property without any severance or distribution among individual holders, or it seeks to recover assets for the corporation or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.’  ”  (Id. at 106.)   

 

In other words, whether a direct or derivative action is most appropriate turns on whether the shareholder is alleging that they have been harmed due to the corporation being harmed, in which case the corporation is the proper party to bring the action; or whether they are alleging that they, specifically, have been harmed.  (See Jones, supra, 1 Cal.3d 93 [upholding right of minority shareholder to bring action against majority shareholders for specific diminution of the value of her personal stock, rather than requiring that she bring suit on behalf of the corporation for the general diminution of the value of all stock]; Jara, Sr. v. Suprema Meats, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 1238 [permitting a minority shareholder to bring a breach of fiduciary duty claim against majority shareholders whose decisions had benefited the corporation, but harmed the value of her individual shares].) 

 

Defendants argue that here, Plaintiffs can only bring derivative, and not direct claims, because Plaintiffs alleged gross mismanagement that impacted all of the partners and thus caused injury to the entire Partnership. Plaintiffs allege that Defendants 1) failed to maintain the Property and/or to timely make repairs to the Property, which led the issuance of fire and safety violations against the Property, to litigation against the Partnership, and to the inability of the Partnership to make profit distributions to the limited partners; 2) improperly attempted to convert the Property into a hotel, which led to litigation by the City of Los Angeles; 3) failed to refinance the debt on the Property into an interest only loan and thus reduce the Partnership’s loan payments; and 3) failed to supervise their professional property manager (Concord Property Management). Thus, Defendants argue that the gravamen of this case is injury to the entire partnership.

 

However, in opposition Plaintiffs argue that they have been specifically harmed. According to the Complaint:

 

“Section 6.7 of the Partnership Agreement governs the ‘Allocations of Net Profits, Net Gains and Distributions to Weaver and NALP.’ Section 6.7 pertinently provides as follows: ‘For each calendar month of 2015, commencing January 1, 2015, and thereafter subject to adjustments as set forth below, the Partnership shall pay to Weaver and amount equal to the fraction of 12.5 divided by 52.5, multiplied by seventeen thousand dollars ($17,000) and to NALP an amount equal to the fraction of 40.0 divided by 52.5, multiplied by seventeen thousand dollars ($17,000) by the tenth (10th) day of each succeeding calendar month." (Compl., ¶ 28.)

 

“Under Section 6.7 of the Partnership Agreement, after appropriate adjustments were made in accordance with that section, Plaintiffs were historically paid $15,694.54 per month. These payments continued until Robbins stated that there was insufficient cashflow from the Paloma Partnership to make the required monthly payments.” (Compl. ¶ 29.)

 

“Plaintiffs are informed and believe, and thereon allege, that there currently is and that there always has been sufficient income generated by the Paloma Partnership to make the required monthly payments to Plaintiffs pursuant to Section 6.7 of the Partnership Agreement.” (Compl. ¶ 30.)

 

Therefore, if Plaintiffs prevail on their claims related to Section 6.7, it is Plaintiffs, not the Partnership, that would be entitled to relief.  Plaintiffs’ fifth, sixth and seventh causes of action are all related to Section 6.7. The fifth cause of action seeks declaratory relief to resolve the controversy between Plaintiffs and Defendants, including the failure “to make distributions to Plaintiffs in accordance with Section 6.7 of the Partnership Agreement and applicable rules and accounting practices” and a determination of the rights between Plaintiffs and Defendants under the Agreement. (Compl. ¶ 57.) The sixth cause of action for accounting seeks a “full, complete, and decipherable accounting” which is “necessary in order to determine the amount(s) that should have been distributed to Plaintiffs pursuant to Section 6.7 of the Agreement.” (Compl. ¶¶ 61-62.) The seventh cause of action for violation of inspection rights is brought pursuant to Section 13.4 of the Agreement, which gives Plaintiffs the right to inspect the PARTNERSHIP’s books and records to certify the accuracy of the amounts referenced in Section 6.7. (Compl. ¶ 65.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged standing to bring its direct claims. The Court notes that Defendants argue that the alleged failure to make distributions to the Plaintiffs’ is still a derivative claim because it involves injury to the Partnership. Defendants argue the alleged mismanagement led to the lack of profitability of the Partnership, which in turn led to the inability of the Partnership to make distributions to Plaintiffs. However, Plaintiffs allege in the Complaint that the Partnership did have the income to make the distributions, but refused to do so. As stated in Everest Investors v. McNeil Partners (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 411, a limited partner can bring direct claims against managing partners when the limited partners suffer a specific and distinct injury not suffered by the partnership. Here, Plaintiff is asserting that the Partnership failed to make required distributions to Plaintiffs. The Court finds that here, Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged injury separate from the Partnership.

 

Accordingly, judgment on Plaintiffs’ fifth, sixth and seventh causes of action are DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is DENIED.

demurrer and motion to strike portions of the SECOND AMENDED complaint (filed 7/17/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint’s sixth cause of action for harassment is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 9, 2023, Plaintiff Lester Jimenez (“Plaintiff”) filed this employment action against Defendants Whole Foods Market, Inc.; Whole Foods Market California, Inc.; Mrs. Gooch’s Natural Food Markets, Inc.; Wild Oats Marketing, LLC; and John Kirkpatrick (collectively “Defendants.”)

 

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) disability discrimination in violation of Government Code § 12940(a); (2) failure to accommodate disability in violation of Government Code § 12940(m); (3) failure to engage in an interactive process in violation of Government Code § 12940(n); (4) failure to prevent discrimination in violation of Government Code § 12940(k); (5) retaliation in violation of FEHA; (6) harassment in violation of Government Code § 12940(j); and (7) wrongful termination of employment in violation of public policy.

 

The SAC alleges the following. Plaintiff began working for Defendants on or about April 7, 2005. (SAC, ¶ 14.) On or about April 17, 2020, Plaintiff was injured. (Id., ¶ 18.) Plaintiff’s physician informed Defendants that Plaintiff was placed on disability and would not be able to return to work for the time being. (Id., ¶ 19.) On or about September 17, 2020, Plaintiff received a notice from Defendants’ insurance company stating that Defendants had approved Plaintiff’s medical leave from August 3, 2020, to April 18, 2021. (Id., ¶ 20.) In March 2021, Plaintiff’s physician extended Plaintiff’s restriction from working to April 18, 2022. (Id., ¶ 21.) On or about March 4, 2021, Defendants’ manager contacted Plaintiff and notified him that he would lose his health coverage as of May 1, 2021. (Id., ¶ 22.) On or about April 16, 2021, Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment and falsely labeled his termination as a voluntary resignation for Plaintiff’s failure to return from his leave of absence. (Id., ¶¶ 25-26.)

 

On July 17, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike, arguing:

·       The SAC’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination is time barred by the applicable statute of limitations.

·       The SAC’s sixth cause of action for harassment fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

·       Defendants filed a motion to strike the SAC’s references to punitive damages because Plaintiff fails to plead facts to demonstrate that Defendants acted with malice, oppression or fraud.

·       Leave to amend would be futile. Plaintiff should not be permitted to amend his Complaint for a third time.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The SAC alleges sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for harassment.

·       Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action is moot because Plaintiff will dismiss that cause of action.

·       Plaintiff has pled that Defendants acted maliciously. Specifically, Defendants failed to remedy their or their employees’ treatment of Plaintiff and accommodate Plaintiff’s disability, and instead authorized Plaintiff’s manager and Defendant Kirkpatrick’s decision to terminate Plaintiff.

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff failed to plead facts to constitute harassment and that Plaintiff has not pled facts warranting punitive damages.  

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating she discussed the grounds of the Demurrer and Motion to Strike with Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone on July 12, 2024. (Leon Decl., ¶ 8.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

B.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.      Sixth Cause of Action: Harassment

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action for harassment based on his disability.

 

The elements for a hostile work environment harassment are: (1) plaintiff belongs to a protected group; (2) plaintiff was subject to unwelcome harassment; (3) the harassment complained of was based on membership in the protected class, (4) the harassment complained of was sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment; and (5) respondeat superior.  Further, for hostile work environment harassment to be actionable, it must be sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment… (Fisher v. Penninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 608.)   

 

In determining what constitutes sufficiently pervasive harassment, the courts have held that acts of harassment cannot be occasional, isolated, sporadic, or trivial, rather the plaintiff must show a concerted pattern of harassment of a repeated, routine or a generalized nature.  (Fisher, supra, 214 Cal.App.3d at pp. 610-611.) 

 

The SAC alleges the following conduct by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that he sustained an injury and notified Defendants of said injury. (SAC ¶¶ 18-19.) Plaintiff underwent surgeries and his work restrictions were extended to April 18, 2022. (SAC ¶ 19-20.) Plaintiff informed Defendants of his continuing restrictions, and shortly thereafter, Defendants’ manager contacted Plaintiff to notify him that he would lose his health coverage, and eventually terminated Plaintiff. (SAC ¶¶ 19-22.) The SAC further alleges “near the end of Plaintiff’s employment and after requesting to return to work, Plaintiff’s supervisor, Defendant Kirkpatrick, commented that Defendants cannot have an employee working in a wheelchair. Plaintiff was also not considered for any other positions.” (SAC ¶ 23.)

 

The Court does not find these allegations sufficient to show harassment by Defendants. In Plaintiff’s opposition, he argues that Defendants’ conduct constituted harassment because Plaintiff was prevented from doing his job and Defendants’ failed to accommodate him by refusing Plaintiff’s request to work an alternate position. However, the Court does not find that this constitutes severe or pervasive harassing conduct. Further, the allegation pertaining to the singular comment made by Defendant Kirkpatrick does not appear to rise to the level of actionable harassment. Plaintiff argues that a single discriminatory comment can be sufficient to constitute harassment. For example, in Dee v. Vintage Petroleum, Inc., 106 Cal.App.4th 30, 33, the court found a discriminatory comment made by a supervisor about the plaintiff’s ethnicity, “it is your Filipino understanding versus mine,” as sufficient. On the other hand, here, the comment made by Defendant Kirkpatrick does not include “epithets, derogatory comments or slurs.” (Id. at 35.) In addition, Plaintiff does not provide the context for the comment and whether it was made in relation to the evaluation of Plaintiff’s performance of his job duties. Plaintiff also does not specify to whom Defendant Kirkpatrick made the wheelchair comment to and whether it was made directly to Plaintiff. Thus, the Court finds the SAC has not alleged enough facts to state a cause of action for harassment against Defendants based on Plaintiff’s disability.

 

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer to the SAC’s sixth cause of action for harassment.

 

D.     Seventh Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination of Employment in Violation of Public Policy

 

Defendants demur to the SAC’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination arguing that it is time barred by the applicable statute of limitations. On September 11, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed his seventh cause of action for wrongful termination. Thus, this issue is moot and the Court does not discuss Defendants’ argument.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the SAC’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination is MOOT.

 

E.      Motion to Strike

 

A motion to strike lies only where the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Civ. Proc. Code § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

 

Defendants request the Court to strike all allegations of punitive damages as follows:

1.      SAC, paragraph 35;

2.      SAC, paragraph 42;

3.      SAC, paragraph 53;

4.      SAC, paragraph 63;

5.      SAC, paragraph 71;

6.      SAC, paragraph 78;

7.      SAC’s Prayer for Relief page 15, line 10: “For exemplary and punitive damages”.

 

The adequacy of a claim for punitive damages is properly tested by a motion to strike. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (a) authorizes the recovery of punitive damages where the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied. Malice means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1).) Oppression is despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Id., subd. (c)(2).) Fraud means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the party of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury. (Id., subd. (c)(3).) 

 

Pursuant to Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b), an entity employer cannot be liable for punitive damages based on the acts of tis employee unless an officer, director or managing agent of the corporation “was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,” or “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety or others or authorized or ratified the [unfit employee’s] wrongful conduct.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294(b); see also Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721, 726.) A managing agent is not merely a supervisory employee, but rather is limited to those “who exercise[] substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal. 4th 563, 577.) Corporate policies are “the general principles which guide a corporation, or rules intended to be followed consistently over time in corporate operations” and a managing agent is “one with substantial authority over decisions that set these general principles and rules.” (Cruz v. Homebase (2000) 83 Cal. App. 4th 160, 167-68.)

 

Plaintiff alleges “[T]he wrongful acts against Plaintiff were carried out, authorized or ratified by Defendants’ directors, officers and/or managing agents, acting with malice, oppression or fraud, or deliberate, willful and conscious disregard of the probability of causing injury to Plaintiff and with the intent to deprive Plaintiff of his employment rights. The conduct of Defendants was also malicious and oppressive in that Plaintiff was terminated in violation of Government Code §12940, et seq. On that basis, Plaintiff seeks punitive and exemplary damages against Defendants as a means of punishing Defendants and by way of example to deter such behavior in the future as being contrary to sound public policy.” (SAC, ¶¶ 35, 42, 53, 63, 71). Further, Plaintiff alleges that conduct “through their agents…was malicious, fraudulent, and oppressive and/or done with knowledge that they were acting in violation of federal and state law, and/or with a willful and conscious disregard for Plaintiff’s rights and for the deleterious consequences of their actions.” (SAC, ¶ 78).

 

The Court does not find these conclusory allegations sufficient to warrant punitive damages. Further, Plaintiff has not specifically pled conduct by Defendants that constitute malice, oppression or fraud. In Plaintiff’s opposition, he argues that he has pled that Defendants’ acted with malice and cites to Colucci v. T-Mobile USA, Inc. (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 442 in support. In Colucci, the plaintiff was terminated when he complained that he was being discriminated against based on his medical disability. (Id., at 454-55.) The court found that the defendant willfully and consciously retaliated against the plaintiff when the defendant terminated him. (Id. at 456.) The court found these facts to be sufficient evidence of malicious and oppressive behavior by the defendant. (Ibid.) However, the Court does not find this case persuasive since here, Plaintiff has not alleged that he complained he was being discriminated against based on his medical disability and then was terminated by Defendants. Moreover, Plaintiff has not pled specific facts showing oppression, fraud or malice by an officer, director or managing agent for Defendants. Plaintiff does not allege that Defendant Kirkpatrick is an officer, director or managing agent. Thus, Plaintiff has failed to set forth allegations that would warrant punitive damages.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages is GRANTED.

 

F.       Leave to Amend

 

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) 

 

Here, there was no indication in the Opposition Plaintiff would be able to cure the defects of the SAC. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Service (2000) 81 Cal. App. 4th 39, 43, 96 Cal.Rptr.2d 354, 356). Therefore, no amendment of the Complaint should be permitted. There is no basis in law to grant leave to amend where, “the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Casterson v. Sup. Ct. (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 177, 190). Thus, the Court denies leave to amend.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint’s sixth cause of action for harassment is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination is MOOT.

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED.

LESTER JIMENEZ v. WHOLE FOODS MARKET, INC., et al. (23STCV27537)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Jack Bazerkanian (C & B Law Group LLP)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Michael Kibbe (Littler Mendelson, APC)

 

demurrer and motion to strike portions of the SECOND AMENDED complaint (filed 7/17/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint’s sixth cause of action for harassment is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination is MOOT.

 

Defendants’ motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 9, 2023, Plaintiff Lester Jimenez (“Plaintiff”) filed this employment action against Defendants Whole Foods Market, Inc.; Whole Foods Market California, Inc.; Mrs. Gooch’s Natural Food Markets, Inc.; Wild Oats Marketing, LLC; and John Kirkpatrick (collectively “Defendants.”)

 

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) disability discrimination in violation of Government Code § 12940(a); (2) failure to accommodate disability in violation of Government Code § 12940(m); (3) failure to engage in an interactive process in violation of Government Code § 12940(n); (4) failure to prevent discrimination in violation of Government Code § 12940(k); (5) retaliation in violation of FEHA; (6) harassment in violation of Government Code § 12940(j); and (7) wrongful termination of employment in violation of public policy.

 

The SAC alleges the following. Plaintiff began working for Defendants on or about April 7, 2005. (SAC, ¶ 14.) On or about April 17, 2020, Plaintiff was injured. (Id., ¶ 18.) Plaintiff’s physician informed Defendants that Plaintiff was placed on disability and would not be able to return to work for the time being. (Id., ¶ 19.) On or about September 17, 2020, Plaintiff received a notice from Defendants’ insurance company stating that Defendants had approved Plaintiff’s medical leave from August 3, 2020, to April 18, 2021. (Id., ¶ 20.) In March 2021, Plaintiff’s physician extended Plaintiff’s restriction from working to April 18, 2022. (Id., ¶ 21.) On or about March 4, 2021, Defendants’ manager contacted Plaintiff and notified him that he would lose his health coverage as of May 1, 2021. (Id., ¶ 22.) On or about April 16, 2021, Defendants terminated Plaintiff’s employment and falsely labeled his termination as a voluntary resignation for Plaintiff’s failure to return from his leave of absence. (Id., ¶¶ 25-26.)

 

On July 17, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike, arguing:

·       The SAC’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination is time barred by the applicable statute of limitations.

·       The SAC’s sixth cause of action for harassment fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

·       Defendants filed a motion to strike the SAC’s references to punitive damages because Plaintiff fails to plead facts to demonstrate that Defendants acted with malice, oppression or fraud.

·       Leave to amend would be futile. Plaintiff should not be permitted to amend his Complaint for a third time.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The SAC alleges sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for harassment.

·       Defendants’ demurrer to the seventh cause of action is moot because Plaintiff will dismiss that cause of action.

·       Plaintiff has pled that Defendants acted maliciously. Specifically, Defendants failed to remedy their or their employees’ treatment of Plaintiff and accommodate Plaintiff’s disability, and instead authorized Plaintiff’s manager and Defendant Kirkpatrick’s decision to terminate Plaintiff.

 

Defendants filed a reply maintaining that Plaintiff failed to plead facts to constitute harassment and that Plaintiff has not pled facts warranting punitive damages.  

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Defendants submitted a declaration stating she discussed the grounds of the Demurrer and Motion to Strike with Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone on July 12, 2024. (Leon Decl., ¶ 8.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

B.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.     Sixth Cause of Action: Harassment

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff fails to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action for harassment based on his disability.

 

The elements for a hostile work environment harassment are: (1) plaintiff belongs to a protected group; (2) plaintiff was subject to unwelcome harassment; (3) the harassment complained of was based on membership in the protected class, (4) the harassment complained of was sufficiently pervasive so as to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment; and (5) respondeat superior.  Further, for hostile work environment harassment to be actionable, it must be sufficiently severe or pervasive to alter the conditions of the victim's employment and create an abusive working environment… (Fisher v. Penninsula Hospital (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 608.)   

 

In determining what constitutes sufficiently pervasive harassment, the courts have held that acts of harassment cannot be occasional, isolated, sporadic, or trivial, rather the plaintiff must show a concerted pattern of harassment of a repeated, routine or a generalized nature.  (Fisher, supra, 214 Cal.App.3d at pp. 610-611.) 

 

The SAC alleges the following conduct by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that he sustained an injury and notified Defendants of said injury. (SAC ¶¶ 18-19.) Plaintiff underwent surgeries and his work restrictions were extended to April 18, 2022. (SAC ¶ 19-20.) Plaintiff informed Defendants of his continuing restrictions, and shortly thereafter, Defendants’ manager contacted Plaintiff to notify him that he would lose his health coverage, and eventually terminated Plaintiff. (SAC ¶¶ 19-22.) The SAC further alleges “near the end of Plaintiff’s employment and after requesting to return to work, Plaintiff’s supervisor, Defendant Kirkpatrick, commented that Defendants cannot have an employee working in a wheelchair. Plaintiff was also not considered for any other positions.” (SAC ¶ 23.)

 

The Court does not find these allegations sufficient to show harassment by Defendants. In Plaintiff’s opposition, he argues that Defendants’ conduct constituted harassment because Plaintiff was prevented from doing his job and Defendants’ failed to accommodate him by refusing Plaintiff’s request to work an alternate position. However, the Court does not find that this constitutes severe or pervasive harassing conduct. Further, the allegation pertaining to the singular comment made by Defendant Kirkpatrick does not appear to rise to the level of actionable harassment. Plaintiff argues that a single discriminatory comment can be sufficient to constitute harassment. For example, in Dee v. Vintage Petroleum, Inc., 106 Cal.App.4th 30, 33, the court found a discriminatory comment made by a supervisor about the plaintiff’s ethnicity, “it is your Filipino understanding versus mine,” as sufficient. On the other hand, here, the comment made by Defendant Kirkpatrick does not include “epithets, derogatory comments or slurs.” (Id. at 35.) In addition, Plaintiff does not provide the context for the comment and whether it was made in relation to the evaluation of Plaintiff’s performance of his job duties. Plaintiff also does not specify to whom Defendant Kirkpatrick made the wheelchair comment to and whether it was made directly to Plaintiff. Thus, the Court finds the SAC has not alleged enough facts to state a cause of action for harassment against Defendants based on Plaintiff’s disability.

 

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer to the SAC’s sixth cause of action for harassment.

 

D.     Seventh Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination of Employment in Violation of Public Policy

 

Defendants demur to the SAC’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination arguing that it is time barred by the applicable statute of limitations. On September 11, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed his seventh cause of action for wrongful termination. Thus, this issue is moot and the Court does not discuss Defendants’ argument.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to the SAC’s seventh cause of action for wrongful termination is MOOT.

Defendants request the Court to strike all allegations of punitive damages as follows:

2.     SAC, paragraph 42;

3.     SAC, paragraph 53;

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to strike punitive damages is GRANTED.

 

F.      Leave to Amend

The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.)

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants’ demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint’s sixth cause of action for harassment is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell’s Motions to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted are GRANTED. The truth of the matters specified in the Requests for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

Miller and Annie Boxell’s requests for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $580.00. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to Defendants’ counsel as to each Defendant within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff Claire Donahue brought this action against Defendants 427 S Mariposa Partners, LP; LA Urban Living LLC; Jeclim LLC; Jeremy Miller; Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff was an occupant of real property located at 427 S Mariposa, Unit 511 Los Angeles, CA (the “Property”) as a lessee/tenant under a written lease with Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that on April 6, 2020, Plaintiff reported an active leak and water damage in the bedroom ceiling to management, who acknowledged the issue but failed to conduct repairs despite multiple follow-ups. On January 25, 2022, Plaintiff discovered additional mold growth in the bedroom after heavy rains. Plaintiff informed the building manager and requested repairs. During Plaintiff’s tenancy, Defendants failed to maintain the Property in a safe and habitable condition. Plaintiff was eventually forced to vacate the Property due to health issues while living in the Property. Defendants constructively evicted Plaintiff from the Property—having refused to make the required repairs to the Property and ensure the Property be made safe and habitable, and after Plaintiff told Defendants that Plaintiff suspected that the fungal growth at the Property was making Plaintiff sick. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action:

1.      Breach of the implied warranty of habitability;

2.      Nuisance;

3.      Breach of contract;

4.      Negligence;

5.      Negligent misrepresentation;

6.      Constructive eviction;

7.      Premises liability;

8.      Fraud; and

9.      Intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On July 10, 2024, Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell each filed Motions to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted and Request for Sanctions. No opposition was filed.

 

On August 15, 2024, the Court held the hearing on the Motions to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Defendants Jeclim LLC and LA Urban Living LLC. The Court continued the hearing to allow any opposition to the motion to be filed no later than August 28, 2024. No opposition has been filed. Defendants filed notices of non-opposition.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

 When a party fails to serve a timely response to a request for admission, the party propounding the request for admission may move for an order to deem the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., §¿2033.280, subd. (b).) A party who fails to provide a timely response waives any objection, including one based on privilege or work product. (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction… on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Id., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

On March 22, 2024, Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell’s served Plaintiff electronically with Requests for Admissions, Set One. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A). Plaintiff’s responses were due on April 24, 2024, 30 days following service, plus an additional two court days for electronic service. (Cal. Code Civ. Pro. §§ 2030.260, subd. (a) and 1013, subd. (a).) However, Plaintiff failed to provide responses by April 24, 2024. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 3.)

 

The parties met and conferred regarding Plaintiff’s failure to respond. On April 25, 2024, Defendants sent an email to Plaintiff’s counsel to confer about Plaintiff’s failure to respond to Defendants’ written discovery requests. Defendants allowed Plaintiff until May 3, 2024 to serve verified answers without objections to Defendants’ discovery requests. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 4.) On April 25, 2024, in response to Defendants’ meet and confer correspondence, Plaintiff’s counsel claimed that Defendants failed to properly serve their written discovery requests in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6(b)(3). (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 5.) On April 25, 2024, Defendants offered to grant Plaintiff an extension of time to respond as another sign of good faith. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 6.) On April 29, 2024, after receiving no response, Defendants followed up with Plaintiff’s counsel to determine how long Plaintiff will need to provide Defendants with her responses. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 7.) On April 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel stated that he would accept service electronically from April 30, 2024, making Plaintiff’s responses due on or before June 4, 2024. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 8.) On June 7, 2024, after still not receiving Plaintiff’s responses, Defendants sent Plaintiff’s counsel a second email to meet and confer, extending Plaintiff until June 14, 2024 to provide verified answers, without objection. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 9.) As of the date of the filing of the instant motion, Defendants have not received any communication from Plaintiff’s counsel regarding Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Request for Admissions, nor have Defendants received Plaintiff’s Response to Defendant’s Request for Admissions. (Ghattas Decl., ¶ 10.)

 

On August 15, 2024, the Court held the hearing on the Motions to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Defendants Jeclim LLC and LA Urban Living LLC. The Court continued the hearing to allow any opposition to the motions to be filed no later than August 28, 2024. However, the Court notes that Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

Thus, it appears Defendants offered Plaintiff multiple extensions to provide Defendants with her responses. However, Plaintiff has failed to serve responses. Therefore, the motion is granted. The truth of the matters specified in Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

Defendants request monetary sanctions for counsel’s work on each of its Motion to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted against Plaintiff. Defendants request $1,360.00 for each motion, representing 5 hours of attorney work at an hourly rate of $260, plus $60 for the filing fee. (Id. ¶¶ 12-13.)

 

The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Plaintiff has failed to respond. However, since the motions are unopposed, the Court will not award Defendants’ anticipated fees for preparing a Reply. Further, each of the motions will be heard on the same day, thus, the Court will reduce the amount Defendants request to attend the hearing. The Court awards each Defendant attorney’s fees for two hours of attorney work and for the filing fee. Thus, monetary sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $580 as to each Defendant.

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS each of Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell’s requests for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $580.00 as to each Defendant.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell’s Motions to Deem Request for Admissions, Set One, Admitted are GRANTED. The truth of the matters specified in the Requests for Admissions, Set One, are hereby deemed admitted.

 

The Court further GRANTS each of Defendants Jeclim LLC, LA Urban Living LLC, Jeremy Miller, Philip G. Miller and Annie Boxell’s requests for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff in the reduced amount of $580.00.
