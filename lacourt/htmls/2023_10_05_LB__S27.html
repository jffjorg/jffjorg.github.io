[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

8:30 a.m.

October 5, 2023

 

Moving Party:               Defendant, CHLB, LLC dba College Medical Center

Opposing Party:           None filed

Notice:                         OK

 

Plaintiffs, Brian and Frida Bundi filed this action against Defendants, CHLB, LLC and Serap Sobnosky, M.D. for medical malpractice. 

 

Plaintiffs filed their complaint on 7/19/22.  The Court held a CMC on 12/19/22 and scheduled trial for 12/04/23.  Plaintiff added Christina Tran, M.D. as a doe defendant on 2/27/23.  She filed an answer the same day.  Plaintiff recently dismissed Sobnosky from the action. 

 

CHLB moves to continue the trial for 90-120 days.  It seeks relief because (a) Tran has only recently appeared in the action, (b) the parties have been engaged in ongoing mediation, and have a second session of mediation scheduled for 8/07/23, and (c) substantial discovery remains outstanding because Frida Bundi has been unable to answer questions about her husband’s finances, health, etc., and needs more time to provide the necessary information. 

 

The Court finds the foregoing constitutes good cause to continue the trial date.  The Court finds the requested 90-120 days reasonable.  The Court advances the 12/04/23 trial date to today’s date and continues it to Thursday, 4/04/24 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27.  The Court advances the 12/01/23 FSC date to today’s date and continues it to Monday, 4/01/24 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27. 

 

Moving Defendant is ordered to give notice. 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.   

 

DATED:  October 5, 2023                       _____________________________________

Plaintiff, R.A. filed this action against Defendants, Doe 1, Doe 2, and Does 3-60.  Plaintiff alleges Doe 1 is a CA nonprofit that provided after-school programs, overnight camps, recreational services, and other services to minors in LA County and surrounding areas; Doe 1 is a partnership operated, run, controlled, managed, directed, supervised, and/or operated by Doe 2.  Plaintiff alleges Doe 2 is a national nonprofit corporation headquartered in the State of Illinois, which managed, ran, controlled, oversaw, supervised and/or operated Doe 1. 

 

Plaintiff alleges Robert “Bob” Felder was an adult male employee of Does 1 and 2, and specifically was the general manager of Doe 1.  Plaintiff alleges Felder died in 1986 and is not a party to the lawsuit.  Plaintiff alleges he sustained childhood sexual assault at the hands of Felder in the 1970s. 

Plaintiff’s complaint includes causes of action for negligent hiring, supervision, and retention against all defendants and negligence against Does 31-50.  Plaintiff has amended his complaint to name Young Men’s Christian Association of Greater Long Beach as Doe 1 and National Council of YMCAs of the USA as Doe 2. 

 

  1. Motion to Compel Further Responses
  1. Prior Hearing

The Court was originally scheduled to hear this motion on 9/05/23.  At the conclusion of the hearing, the Court continued the matter to require the parties to provide a joint statement of items in dispute.  On 9/26/23, the parties filed their joint statement.  The RPDs that remain at issue are RPDs 38 and 39.  These RPDs seek production of:

RPD 38:            All documents or writings that relate to your discussions pertaining to the termination of your (former) employee, Brian Bost, by Felder

RPD 39:            All documents or writings that identify your employees who were present at meetings and/or discussions regarding the termination of your (former) employee, Brian Bost, by Felder

 

Plaintiff’s position is that he needs to review these documents to show Defendant had knowledge of Felder’s prior abuse at the time Felder abused Plaintiff.  Specifically, Plaintiff contends he confronted Bost, when he was an adult, and told Bost about his childhood abuse.  Shortly thereafter, Felder terminated Bost’s employment.  Bost sued for wrongful termination, and apparently had his employment reinstated, because documents show he worked for Defendant after the subject time period.  Plaintiff’s thesis is that documents from the termination and wrongful termination lawsuit could include information showing Bost, or possibly others, knew of Felder’s abuse prior to the time Felder abused Plaintiff, which knowledge would be imputed to Defendant. 

The burden to justify an objection to a discovery request is on the party asserting the objection.  See Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.  Defendant does not, in its opposing portion of the joint statement, address many of the objections asserted.  Those objections are therefore not justified and overruled. 

 

Defendant makes numerous arguments in the joint statement.  First, it contends none of the documents that are responsive to the RPDs mention anything about Felder having committed any type of physical or sexual abuse, or about Brian Bost having been terminated for confronting Robert Felder.  A party’s assertion that documents do not say something is not the same as permitting the opposing party to review the documents and make that determination for himself.  Notably, the documents not containing the information the propounding party hopes to uncover is not a ground for “objection” to the categories. 

 

Defendant’s second argument is that the documents at issue were created in and around 1987, at the time Felder terminated Bost’s employment, more than a decade after Plaintiff alleges Felder abused him.  The Court understands Plaintiff’s requests are a bit of a fishing expedition.  However, for discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.  Admissibility is not the test and information, unless privileged, is discoverable if it might reasonably lead to admissible evidence.  These rules are applied liberally in favor of discovery, and (contrary to popular belief), fishing expeditions are permissible in some cases. (Cruz v. Superior Court (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 646, 653-654; Gonzalez v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546; see also Garamendi v. Golden Eagle Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 694, 712, fn. 8 [citations, quotations omitted].)  The Court is inclined to find, in light of the above, that the documents are sufficiently potentially relevant to overcome this relevance objection. 

 

Defendant’s third argument is that the subject documents implicate the attorney-client privilege and work product protection.  The problem with this argument is that Plaintiff correctly notes, in the joint statement, that the documents listed in the privilege log are not listed with sufficient specificity to enable Plaintiff (or the Court) to analyze whether the privilege/protection applies.  Notably, Defendant asks the Court to appoint a discovery referee to review the documents to determine whether the privilege and/or protection applies.  The Court is inclined to do so.

 

Defendant’s final argument is that the rights to privacy of the individuals on the board, the deceased individuals, and Defendant itself all outweigh any relevance of the documents.  This inquiry is also difficult without viewing the documents themselves.  The documents could include highly sensitive information that has nothing to do with knowledge of prior abuse, in which case the need for the documents would not outweigh the right to privacy.  However, if the documents contain anything at all showing Bose or others knew of Felder’s abuse prior to his abuse of Plaintiff, the relevance of the documents would highly outweigh any right to privacy.  Again, the Court is inclined, at Defendant’s request, to appoint a referee to review the documents in order to balance the right of privacy against the relevance of the documents.

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Edison Manrique filed this action against Defendant, General Motors, LLC on 10/13/22.  Defendant demurred to the complaint and moved to strike portions of the complaint, and Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint prior to the hearing.  The FAC includes causes of action for violation of Song-Beverly and fraudulent inducement/concealment. 

 

  1. Demurrer to FAC

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he attempted to meet and confer telephonically with Plaintiff prior to filing the demurrer, but was unsuccessful in his attempt.  He does not declare whether meet and confer was unsuccessful because the attorneys never actually spoke, or because they did speak and did not resolve their issues.  In the future in this and other actions, Counsel must specify.    

 

c.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant demurs to the fourth cause of action for fraudulent concealment, contending it is not pled with the requisite specificity and Plaintiff does not allege a transactional relationship between himself and GM giving rise to a duty to disclose. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the fourth cause of action adequately pleads a transactional relationship and is pled with the necessary specificity in light of the fact that the claim is for concealment, rather than intentional misrepresentation. 

 

To date, the Court has not received reply papers.  The Court will not consider late-filed reply papers. 

d.     Duty to Disclose

The elements of a fraud claim based on concealment are: “(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310–311). 

 

There are “four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.” (Id. at 311). If a fiduciary relationship does not exist, but the latter three circumstances are present, Plaintiff must still show “the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant from which a duty to disclose can arise.” (Id. at 311).

 

            A duty to disclose may arise as a result of a transaction between the parties. However, the transaction "must necessarily arise from direct dealings between the plaintiff and the defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large." (Bigler-Engler at 312 [noting that the duty of a manufacturer to warn consumers of a product’s hazards and faults applies in the context of strict products liability actions but does not apply in a suit for intentional misrepresentation.]).

            Plaintiff cites Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, wherein the Plaintiffs sued Nissan North America, Inc. (Nissan), alleging a transmission defect. Plaintiffs alleged claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and a common law fraud claim alleging that Nissan fraudulently concealed the defects and induced them to purchase a car. (Dhital at 838).

 

            The California Supreme Court granted review of Dhital on February 1, 2023, however, the request for depublication was denied. (Dhital v. Nissan North America (Cal. 2023) 304 Cal.Rptr.3d 82, 83). Since review is pending and the Supreme Court did not otherwise order, Dhital does not having binding effect, however, “it may be cited for potentially persuasive value only.” (Cal Rules of Court, Rule 8.1115 (e)(1).

 

            The Dhital court concluded that allegations were sufficient to support the existence of a buyer-seller relationship between the parties as “Plaintiffs alleged that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan's authorized dealerships are [the manufacturer’s] agents for purposes of the sale of Nissan vehicles to consumers. In light of these allegations, we decline to hold plaintiffs’ claim is barred on the ground there was no relationship requiring Nissan to disclose known defects." (Dhital at 845). Plaintiff’s amended allegations conform with the allegations found by the Court of Appeal to establish a relationship with the manufacturer sufficient to withstand demurrer. (FAC ¶4-8).

 

e.     Specificity

Ordinarily, fraud claims are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading which necessitate pleading facts showing “how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered." (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.)  However, the specificity rule is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations and not to concealment. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 89 Cal.Rptr.3d 659).  Notably, the idea that a plaintiff could allege who concealed something, where the thing was concealed, when it was concealed, etc., is nonsensical; the nature of the claim is a failure to speak, which cannot possibly include these details.

 

Accordingly, the allegation that Defendant had exclusive knowledge of the transmission defects and the alleged facts supporting the existence of a direct transactional relationship with Defendant’s agent are sufficient to support a duty not to conceal material facts.

 

f.      Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer is overruled.

 

  1. Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages from the FAC.  The motion to strike rises and falls with the demurrer, as a well-pled claim for fraud necessarily supports a prayer for punitive damages.  See Civil Code §3294, which permits imposition of punitive damages in cases involving malice, oppression, or fraud.  The motion to strike is therefore denied.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jane M.C. Doe, et al. are a group of eleven plaintiffs who have collectively filed suit against Defendants, LAUSD, Dennis Jenkins, and Jennifer Mak for damages arising out of Jenkins’s alleged sexual abuse of Plaintiffs while they were fifth grade students at 15th St. Elementary School during 2013 and 2014. 

 

On 5/11/23, the Court heard and sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the FAC with leave to amend.  The crux of the Court’s holding on demurrer was that the FAC lacked specificity and improperly combined multiple theories into a single cause of action. 

 

On 5/30/23, Plaintiffs filed their operative Second Amended Complaint.  The SAC includes causes of action for:

·         Negligence – Supervision of a Minor

·         Negligence per se – breach of duty to report failure to report

·         Negligence – breach of duty to use reasonable care

·         Negligence – breach of mandatory duty

·         Sexual Assault

·         Sexual Battery

·         False Imprisonment

 

The first through third causes of action are pled against LAUSD and the principal at the relevant time period, Jennifer Mak.  The fourth cause of action is pled against LAUSD.  The fifth through seventh causes of action are pled against Jenkins. 

 

2.     Demurrer

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel, Megan Asrani, declares the attorneys met and conferred before Defendants filed this pleading challenge, but were unable to resolve the issues presented.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for ruling on its merits.

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

Defendants, LAUSD and Mak demur to the first through fourth causes of action.  Plaintiffs oppose.

 

Defendants contend (a) the causes of action pled lack a statutory basis, (b) Plaintiffs failed to plead facts showing LAUSD/Mak knew or should have known of the subject abuse, (c) as to Plaintiff K.J., she does not and cannot plead any facts showing prior knowledge, (d) the other plaintiffs’ claims fail because knowledge of potential harm to K.J. does not support knowledge of potential harm to others, and (e) the two John Does cannot support their claims because they are mere bystanders and do not allege the elements required to state a claim for bystander liability. 

 

Plaintiffs oppose the demurrer.  They argue K.J. has alleged notice, and the remaining plaintiffs have also alleged notice.  They argue the John Doe claims are not bystander claims, but instead are direct liability claims. 

 

  1. Statutory Authority

Defendants’ first contention is that none of the claims asserted has a statutory basis.  Defendants go on, however, to cite cases that discuss whether and when a defendant school district has a duty to protect students from the type of harm at issue in this case, including C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 851, 870.  If there is no duty to protect the students, then it is unclear why there would be a case considering whether and when that duty is met and when it is breached.  

 

Defendants cite C.A, supra, at 866-67 to establish that a student can only sue for negligent supervision if the student shows LAUSD knew or should have known of Jenkins’ dangerous and exploitative propensities before the student was injured.  Defendants cite Doe v. Lawndale Elementary School Dist. (2021) 72 Cal.App.5th 113, 139-140 to establish a student can only sue for failure to report suspected child abuse if the student shows it was objectively reasonable for a mandated reporter to suspect abuse based on the facts the reporter actually knew, not based on facts the reporter reasonably could have discovered. 

 

These claims could not have elements if they did not exist.  The Court therefore finds the claims against the district and Mak can be pled, and the issue is whether sufficient facts are pled to sustain the claims. 

 

  1. Students Other than K.J.

Plaintiffs allege that, in 2013, K.J. reported her own abuse to Mak, and Mak ignored her report and allowed the abuse to continue.  Defendants argue K.J. failed to state any particular facts concerning Jenkins’s conduct, or how that conduct would have put LAUSD or Mak on notice of Jenkins’s inappropriate sexual behavior. 

 

At ¶24, Plaintiffs allege exactly what happened to K.J., which was despicable and inappropriate, and also that K.J. reported this to Mak during the 2013 school year.  No further specificity is required.  Notably, the other students all allege they were abused during the following school year.

 

Defendants also argue that, at most, this put them on notice that Jenkins was a danger to K.J., but not to other students.  Defendants cite no authority for this position, and it is nonsensical.  A teacher who abuses a student is likely to abuse other students.  The Court finds the students, other than K.J., have sufficiently alleged knowledge of actual prior abuse, such that the “knowledge” element of each cause of action pled is satisfied.  The demurrer to their claims is overruled.

 

  1. K.J. 

Defendants argue that, even if the other plaintiffs have alleged prior knowledge, K.J. has not, as she is the only student who alleges she personally told Defendants what was happening to her.  K.J. does not specifically allege that, after she told Mak what was happening, it happened again; it is unclear whether she was abused again after she reported the behavior.  Clearly, if she was, this would be sufficient. 

 

She appears, instead, to rely on allegations that she is informed and believes that other students in 2013 and before reported similar abuse and molestation to Mak and LAUSD.  This is the type of allegation the Court, in ruling on the demurrer to the FAC, found to lack sufficient specificity.  The Court again finds the allegations lack sufficient specificity. 

 

The demurrer to K.J.’s claims is sustained with leave to amend.  K.J. must either allege specific facts showing Defendants had notice of abuse prior to her abuse, and/or facts showing her abuse continued after she reported Jenkins to Mak if she wishes to pursue her claims against them. 

  1. John Doe Plaintiffs

Defendants contend the John Doe plaintiffs do not allege they were personally touched by Jenkins, but rather that they sustained damages as mere bystanders to the abuse of the female students in their class.  Defendants cite numerous cases all decided in the personal injury context, which cases hold that a person who witnesses another person’s injury can only sue for NIED if the person personally witnesses the injury AND has a close personal relationship with the injured person that gives rise to emotional distress beyond what would be anticipated if he were merely a disinterested witness. 

 

Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, correctly note that their claims are not bystander NIED claims.  They are direct claims for their own damages, because making a male student witness sexual abuse against female students can damage the male student as well.  Absent any on point authority, the demurrer to their claims is overruled. 

 

3.     Motion to Strike

a.     Allegations Concerning Statutory Duties

Defendants move to strike various allegations concerning statutory duties, contending the allegations do not actually give rise to statutory duties.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the motion, do not argue that any specific statutory duty applies, but instead argue that there is no prejudice to Defendants as a result of the allegations remaining in the SAC.  The Court agrees.  The cases, detailed above, appear to govern the duties a school district and its principal owe to students.  Regardless of which particular statute does or does not govern those duties, it will be up to the plaintiffs to show, at trial, that Mak and/or the District failed to comply with its duties.  The motion to strike is denied.

 

b.     Attorneys’ Fees 

Plaintiffs seek to recover their attorneys’ fees expended in this action pursuant to Civil Code §1021.5.  Defendants contend the prayer for attorneys’ fees is inappropriate and should be stricken.  Plaintiffs contend it is appropriate and should remain. 

 

The Court considered this issue in connection with Defendants’ motion to strike, directed at the FAC.  The Court held:

Plaintiffs seek to recover attorneys’ fees pursuant to CCP §1021.5.  Notably, Plaintiffs do not currently have a properly pled claim against Defendants, and therefore the motion to strike is moot.  The Court will, however, consider the substance of the arguments raised in an attempt to assist the parties in resolving any issues raised by way of the SAC.

 

Pursuant to Community Youth Athletic Center v. City of National City (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1385, 1447, attorneys’ fees are appropriate under §1021.5 if (1) plaintiffs' action “has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest,” (2) “a significant benefit, whether pecuniary or nonpecuniary has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons” and (3) “the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make the award appropriate.” 

 

Defendants argue §1021.5 does not apply because this case does not seek enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest or the conveyance of a significant benefit upon the general public or a large class of persons.  Plaintiffs, in opposition, contend Jenkins continues to teach at 15th St Elementary, and therefore this action, if successful, will confer a benefit on a large class of persons (themselves and future students of Jenkins).  Defendants, in reply, contend attorneys’ fees are not appropriate because the complaint is not brought on behalf of the general public or as a class action. 

Defendants fail to cite authority for the position that §1021.5 fees are only available in PAGA or class action suits, and the Court knows of no such authority.  By way of example, in Conservatorship of Whitley (2010) 50 Cal.4th 1206, the Supreme Court held that §1021.5 attorneys’ fees were appropriate whether the plaintiff successfully created a procedural precedent that conferred a benefit on persons with developmental disabilities.  The Supreme Court held that the fact that the litigant had a personal interest in the outcome of the litigation did not foreclose the ability to obtain fees.  Notably, the Court did specify that a litigant who has a financial interest in the litigation may be disqualified from obtaining such fees when expected or realized financial gains offset litigation costs.  In this case, the Court cannot determine, at the pleading stage, whether Plaintiffs’ expected financial gains outweigh their litigation costs; in light of the allegation that Jenkins continues to work for LAUSD, the Court finds it is possible, if Plaintiffs’ claims are proven, that they will meet the test under §1021.5.  At the pleading stage, to the extent the motion is not moot, the motion would be denied. 

 

Defendants do not raise additional arguments in their motion directed at the SAC, and the motion is denied.

 

4.     Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff K.J.’s claims is sustained with leave to amend.  Defendants’ demurrer is otherwise overruled.  Defendants’ motion to strike is denied.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jane M.M. Doe and John J.C. Doe have filed suit against Defendants, LAUSD, Dennis Jenkins, and Jennifer Mak for damages arising out of Jenkins’s alleged sexual abuse of Plaintiffs while they were fifth grade students at 15th St. Elementary School during 2013 and 2014. 

 

This case is related to 22STCV16136, a case wherein eleven plaintiffs have sued the same defendants for the same or similar acts.

 

2.     Demurrer

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel, Megan Asrani, declares the attorneys met and conferred before Defendants filed this pleading challenge, but were unable to resolve the issues presented.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for ruling on its merits.

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

Defendants demur to the FAC, contending M.M.’s claims are inappropriate because she has also asserted claims in the earlier-filed case 22STCV16136, and also arguing J.C.’s claims are inappropriate because he is a mere bystander and does not allege the elements required to state a claim for bystander liability. 

 

Plaintiffs oppose the demurrer.  They argue the John Doe claims are not bystander claims, but instead are direct liability claims. 

  1. M.M.

Defendants’ first contention is M.M. is a plaintiff in 22STCV16136 and cannot assert the same claims in this case.  Defendants’ RJN in this regard is granted.  Plaintiffs do not address this argument in opposition to the demurrer.  The demurrer to M.M.’s claims is sustained without leave to amend. 

 

  1. John Doe Plaintiff J.C.

Defendants contend John Doe J.C. does not allege he was personally touched by Jenkins, but rather that he sustained damages as a mere bystander to the abuse of the female students in his class.  Defendants cite numerous cases all decided in the personal injury context, which cases hold that a person who witnesses another person’s injury can only sue for NIED if the person personally witnesses the injury AND has a close personal relationship with the injured person that gives rise to emotional distress beyond what would be anticipated if he were merely a disinterested witness. 

 

Plaintiffs, in opposition to the demurrer, correctly note that J.C.’s claims are not bystander NIED claims.  They are direct claims for his own damages, because making a male student witness sexual abuse against female students can damage the male student as well.  Absent any on point authority, the demurrer to his claims is overruled. 

 

3.     Motion to Strike

a.     Allegations Concerning Statutory Duties

Defendants move to strike various allegations concerning statutory duties, contending the allegations do not actually give rise to statutory duties.  Plaintiffs, in opposition to the motion, do not argue that any specific statutory duty applies, but instead argue that there is no prejudice to Defendants as a result of the allegations remaining in the SAC.  The Court agrees.  The cases, detailed above, appear to govern the duties a school district and its principal owe to students.  Regardless of which particular statute does or does not govern those duties, it will be up to the plaintiffs to show, at trial, that Mak and/or the District failed to comply with its duties.  The motion to strike is denied.

 

b.     Attorneys’ Fees 

Plaintiffs seek to recover their attorneys’ fees expended in this action pursuant to Civil Code §1021.5.  Defendants contend the prayer for attorneys’ fees is inappropriate and should be stricken.  Plaintiffs contend it is appropriate and should remain. 

 

The Court considered this issue in connection with Defendants’ motion to strike, directed at the FAC in 22STCV16136.  The Court held:

Plaintiffs seek to recover attorneys’ fees pursuant to CCP §1021.5.  Notably, Plaintiffs do not currently have a properly pled claim against Defendants, and therefore the motion to strike is moot.  The Court will, however, consider the substance of the arguments raised in an attempt to assist the parties in resolving any issues raised by way of the SAC.

 

Pursuant to Community Youth Athletic Center v. City of National City (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1385, 1447, attorneys’ fees are appropriate under §1021.5 if (1) plaintiffs' action “has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest,” (2) “a significant benefit, whether pecuniary or nonpecuniary has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons” and (3) “the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make the award appropriate.” 

 

Defendants argue §1021.5 does not apply because this case does not seek enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest or the conveyance of a significant benefit upon the general public or a large class of persons.  Plaintiffs, in opposition, contend Jenkins continues to teach at 15th St Elementary, and therefore this action, if successful, will confer a benefit on a large class of persons (themselves and future students of Jenkins).  Defendants, in reply, contend attorneys’ fees are not appropriate because the complaint is not brought on behalf of the general public or as a class action. 

 

Defendants fail to cite authority for the position that §1021.5 fees are only available in PAGA or class action suits, and the Court knows of no such authority.  By way of example, in Conservatorship of Whitley (2010) 50 Cal.4th 1206, the Supreme Court held that §1021.5 attorneys’ fees were appropriate whether the plaintiff successfully created a procedural precedent that conferred a benefit on persons with developmental disabilities.  The Supreme Court held that the fact that the litigant had a personal interest in the outcome of the litigation did not foreclose the ability to obtain fees.  Notably, the Court did specify that a litigant who has a financial interest in the litigation may be disqualified from obtaining such fees when expected or realized financial gains offset litigation costs.  In this case, the Court cannot determine, at the pleading stage, whether Plaintiffs’ expected financial gains outweigh their litigation costs; in light of the allegation that Jenkins continues to work for LAUSD, the Court finds it is possible, if Plaintiffs’ claims are proven, that they will meet the test under §1021.5.  At the pleading stage, to the extent the motion is not moot, the motion would be denied. 

 

Defendants do not raise additional arguments in their motion directed at the SAC, and the motion is denied.

 

4.     Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff M.M.’s claims is sustained without leave to amend.  Defendants’ demurrer is otherwise overruled.  Defendants’ motion to strike is denied.

 

Defendants are ordered to file an answer to the FAC, with M.M.’s claims deemed dismissed, within ten days.
