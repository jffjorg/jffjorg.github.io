DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

ALERT

Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

 

Department M strongly encourages the use of  LA CourtConnect* for ALL hearings, without need for prior approval, unless live testimony by a witness is required.

 

The contact information for LA CourtConnect* is:

 

 

 https://lacourt.portalscloud.com/VCourt/

 

 

*Parties with a fee waiver on file may be eligible to appear at no/reduced cost


Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                                       Friday, January 3, 2025

Department M                                                                                                         Calendar No. 9

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

            Think 5 Entertainment, Inc., et al. v. Game Cloud Network, Inc., et al.

            24TRCV00196

1.      Nixon Peabody LLP’s, Counsel for Defendants Game Cloud Network, Inc., GameCloud Studios, Inc., and Aaron Baker, Motion to be Relieved as Counsel     

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Nixon Peabody LLP’s, Counsel for Defendants Game Cloud Network, Inc., GameCloud Studios, Inc., and Aaron Baker, Motion to be Relieved as Counsel is granted.      

 

            Background

 

            Plaintiffs filed the Complaint on January 19, 2024. Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint was filed on June 4, 2024. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiffs’ attempt to launch a virtual gaming and entertainment concept was thwarted by the conduct of Defendants. Defendants stole intellectual property and breached contracts. Plaintiffs allege numerous causes of action.

 

            Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

 

            Counsel states, in the declaration, valid reasons for withdrawal. Counsel states that there has been a breakdown in the relationship and irreconcilable differences. The Court finds that the attorney has filed the required declaration.  The Court also finds that the attorney has shown sufficient reasons why the motion to be relieved as counsel should be granted and why the attorney brought a motion under Code of Civil Procedure § 284(2) instead of filing a consent under section 284(1).

 

            Therefore, the motion to be relieved as counsel is granted.

 

            The attorney is relieved as counsel of record for the clients effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the signed order upon the client.

 

            The Court orders that the demurrers/motions to strike and CMC currently scheduled for January 16, 2025 are continued to February 27, 2025 to be heard on the same date as the demurrer and motion to strike which is scheduled for that date. Judicial economy and efficiency support the hearing of all the demurrers/motions to strike on the same date.

 

              Nixon Peabody LLP is ordered to give notice of the Court’s ruling.

2.      Portofino Hotel Partners, LP’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Request for Sanctions

3.      Portofino Hotel Partners, LP’s Motion to Deem Admitted Requests for Admissions, Set One, and Request for Sanctions  

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Portofino Hotel Partners, LP’s Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Requests for Sanctions are granted.

 

Portofino Hotel Partners, LP’s Motion to Deem Admitted Requests for Admissions, Set One, and Request for Sanctions is granted.   

 

Background

           

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on October 7, 2024. This is a residential unlawful detainer action.  

 

            Motions to Compel/Deem Admitted

 

CCP § 2030.290 states: “If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply…The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (CCP § 2030.290(b).) 

 

CCP § 2031.300 states: “If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, the following rules shall apply: The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”  (CCP § 2031.300(b)).

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280 states:

“If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply:

(a) The party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010). The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2033.210, 2033.220, and 2033.230.

(2) The party's failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

(b) The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

(c) The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220. It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”

 

First, the Court notes that in the notice of ruling of the ex parte application filed on December 11, 2024, Plaintiff refers to a motion to compel responses special interrogatories. However, no such motion was ever filed with the Court. Instead, Plaintiff filed a duplicate copy of the motion to compel responses to form interrogatories. Thus, no ruling can be made to a non-filed motion to compel responses to special interrogatories.

 

            Plaintiff served upon Defendant Form Interrogatories, Set One, Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Requests for Admissions, Set One, on October 21, 2024. (Decls., Scott Spinrad). Plaintiff contends that no responses were served to the discovery requests. (Id.)

 

            Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, and Requests for Sanctions are granted. Defendant is ordered to serve responses, without objections, within 10 days of this date.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Admitted Requests for Admissions, Set One, and Request for Sanctions is granted. Plaintiff’s requests for admissions, set one, are deemed admitted.

 

            The Court notes that recently Defendant filed a motion to vacate the Court’s ruling on the ex parte application to advance the hearing date of the instant motions. However, Defendant filed no written oppositions to the instant motions. Defendant’s own motion to vacate is supported solely by her self-serving contention that she did not receive notice of the ex parte application. As of this date, Defendant has not shown that she has responded to the discovery items at issue with these motions.

 

            Sanctions

 

Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions are granted.

 

Defendants is sanctioned the total amount of $1,280.00 which is the amount requested by moving party. The amount requested is reasonable. 

 

Sanctions are payable within 30 days of this date.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Donald Baxter, et al. v. Prime Healthcare Services, Inc., et al.

            24TRCV03805

1.      Donald Baxter’s Motion for Trial Preference

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Donald Baxter’s Motion for Trial Preference is denied without prejudice.  

 

Background

           

            Plaintiffs filed the Complaint on November 12, 2024. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiff Donald Baxter suffered injuries while in the care of Defendants.  Plaintiffs set forth the following causes of action: 1. Medical Malpractice; 2. Loss of Consortium; 3. Medical Battery; 4. NIED; 5. Vicarious Liability; 6. Negligent Hiring, Supervision, Retention; 7. Violation of Patient Bill of Rights; 8. Custodial Neglect.    

 

            Motion for Preferential Trial Date

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 36 states, in relevant part:

“(d) In its discretion, the court may also grant a motion for preference that is accompanied by clear and convincing medical documentation that concludes that one of the parties suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of survival of that party beyond six months, and that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting the preference.

(e) Notwithstanding any other provision of law, the court may in its discretion grant a motion for preference that is supported by a showing that satisfies the court that the interests of justice will be served by granting this preference.

            (f) Upon the granting of such a motion for preference, the court shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date and there shall be no continuance beyond 120 days from the granting of the motion for preference except for physical disability of a party or a party's attorney, or upon a showing of good cause stated in the record. Any continuance shall be for no more than 15 days and no more than one continuance for physical disability may be granted to any party.”

 

“[T]he decision to grant or deny a preferential trial setting rests at all times in the sound discretion of the trial court in light of the totality of the circumstances.” Salas v. Sears, Roebuck & Co. (1986) 42 Cal.3d 342, 344.

 

Plaintiffs move for a trial preference pursuant to CCP § 36(d) and (e), based on Plaintiff Donald Baxter’s health condition. Plaintiffs submitted no competent evidence with clear and convincing medical documentation which concludes that Donald Baxter suffers from an illness or condition raising substantial medical doubt of his survival beyond six months. Plaintiffs submitted no competent evidence that the interests of justice will be served by granting a trial preference. The motion is supported only by the declaration of counsel who attached the following: excerpts from the Patient Health Summary of Donald Baxter from UCLA Medical Center and Orthopedic Hospital; California Department of Aging’s webpage discussing hospice care; California Department of Aging webpage related to Hospice Care as it appeared on November 22, 2024. (Decl., Kenneth J. Melrose, ¶¶ 4-6, Exs. A-C.) Counsel provides no competent evidence to establish personal knowledge to adequately provide the requisite foundation for these documents.

 

The motion is specifically not brought under Section 36(a) which can be supported by the conclusory declaration of counsel. “An affidavit submitted in support of a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36 may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party. The affidavit is not admissible for any purpose other than a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36.” Code Civ. Proc., § 36.5. “The standard under [Code of Civil Procedure Section 36] subdivision (a), unlike under subdivision (d), which is more specific and more rigorous, includes no requirement of a doctor’s declaration.” Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534. Unlike other areas of the law, a declaration setting forth the health of the party in support of a motion for preference under CCP § 36(a) may be made by a conclusory and hearsay declaration from counsel.  Here, the motion is brought under Sections 36(d) and (e) which requires the submission of competent first-hand evidence rather than conclusory statements from the attorney and documents to which the attorney has not provided the proper foundation for its authenticity.

 

Therefore, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preferential Trial Setting is denied without prejudice.

 

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of this ruling.
