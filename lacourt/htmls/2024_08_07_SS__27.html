DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV10557    Hearing Date: August 7, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTIONS FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT; MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 8/7/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 19STCV10557 OLEKSANDR MELNYK vs BEVERLY HILLS BMW, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant Beverly Hills BMW

Responding Party: Plaintiff¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:       MOTION FOR SUMMARY IS GRANTED;

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS IS GRANTED IN PART AND DENIED IN PART

       

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Background

On March 27, 2019, Plaintiff filed his complaint alleging that he experienced issues with the steering wheel locking up on his vehicle and had Defendant BHB service the vehicle on March 20, 2017, to address this issue. On March 27, 2017, Defendant allegedly repaired the malfunction and returned the vehicle to Plaintiff. On March 28, 2017, Plaintiff alleges that while driving on San Gabriel Canyon Road in Los Angeles County, the steering wheel of his car locked up, preventing him from turning the steering wheel. As a result, his vehicle went down a cliff and rolled over.  He brought action against Beverly Hills BMW and Kiki Cholewki, an employee of Beverly Hills BMW, based on this accident, alleging causes of action for negligence, negligent repair and negligent misrepresentation.

Defendants Beverly Hills BMW and Kiki Chowleki have brought motions for summary judgment contending that no facts support Plaintiff’s action. 

Legal Standard

MSJ

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)    

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.) Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.)   

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.” (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.) It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence. (Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken. (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)    

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)    

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.” (Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence. While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues. [Citation.] Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.  If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.” (Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.) Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true. [Citation.] Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

Negligence

The elements for negligence are: (1) a legal duty owed to the plaintiff to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to the plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318.)  

Causation is an essential element of establishing Defendant’s liability in this case. (See Tribeca Companies, LLC v. First American Title Insurance Company (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1102-03.) Causation includes both actual cause and proximate cause. (See id. at 1103.) A defendant’s negligence is the actual cause of the plaintiff’s injury if it is a substantial factor in bringing about the harm. (See Mitchell v. Gonzales (1991) 54 Cal.3d 1041, 1052-53.)  

An essential element of negligence and premises liability claims are that “a defendant’s alleged misconduct was the cause in fact of the plaintiff’s damage.”  (Tribeca Companies, LLC v. First American Title Ins. Co. (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1088, 1102-03; Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998 (stating that the elements for premises liability are the same as those for negligence).)  “In California, the causation element of negligence is satisfied when the plaintiff establishes (1) that the defendant’s breach of duty (his negligent act or omission) was a substantial factor in bringing about the plaintiff’s harm and (2) that there is no rule of law relieving the defendant of liability.”  (Leslie G. v. Perry & Associates (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 472, 481.)  “‘If the conduct which is claimed to have caused the injury had nothing at all to do with the injuries, it could not be said that the conduct was a factor, let alone a substantial factor, in the production of the injuries.’”  (Mitchell v. Gonzales (1991) 54 Cal.3d 1041, 1052 (quoting Doupnik v. General Motors Corp. (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 849, 861).) 

Undisputed Facts

On March 28, 2017, Plaintiff Oleksandr Melnyk (“Plaintiff”) was driving the 2016 BMW M4 STD 2D Coupe motor vehicle bearing VIN WBS3R9C52GK338005 (“Subject Vehicle”) southbound on SR-39 (San Gabriel Canyon Road in Los Angeles County), when the Subject Vehicle left the roadway, traveled over a dirt berm and down an embankment on the right side of the roadway, landing on its left side at the bottom of the embankment (“Subject Accident”). Prior to the Subject Accident, the Subject Vehicle traveled along a straight roadway at first, then turned right along a right-hand curve in the roadway, then turned left along a left-hand curve in the roadway, and then returned towards straight while the roadway continued to curve left, resulting in the Subject Vehicle driving off the right side of the roadway.

Preliminary Matter

Discussion

        Initial Burden

At the heart of these summary judgment motions is whether Defendant Beverly Hills BMW was negligent in fixing Plaintiff's car locking issue, which allegedly led to the car's steering wheel locking up, or whether the incident was due to Plaintiff not steering correctly. Both the video and the Event Data Recorder (EDR) show that prior to the car leaving the roadway and traveling over a dirt embarkment, it first turned right and left. The video then shows the car traveling straight as it went over the dirt embarkment, with the EDR data correspondingly showing that the steering wheel returned to center before the event. The crucial issues is whether the driver steered incorrectly, allowing the steering wheel to return to center or the steering wheel locked up as Plaintiff has alleged in his complaint.

On May 1, 2017, Defendant’s expert Ryan Cram, along with Plaintiff’s expert, examined the vehicle and determined that the steering system was functional, the steering wheel was connected to the steering system, and when the wheel was turned left and right, the car's wheels moved accordingly without binding. There were no diagnostic trouble codes related to the steering system, and they found no defects or malfunctions in the steering system. (Cram 12-15.)

Defendant’s expert Michael Stapleford, who examined the event data recorder (EDR), noted frontal, side, and rollover events or crashes and reviewed 5 seconds of data preceding each of these three events. The data showed varying steering inputs. Stapleford opined that had there been a steering wheel lock as alleged by Plaintiff, the data from the EDR would show fixed angles (Stapleford ¶ 10). However, this was not the case as the angle data values kept changing preceding the frontal event. Furthermore, the expert examined the EDR data 5 seconds preceding the side event, a second collision/event that occurred 1.374 seconds after Plaintiff hit the embankment. This data showed steering input after the car hit the embankment and it showed that the steering wheel was turned to various angles even after the car hit the embankment and did not lock up at a fixed angle. Had the steering wheel “locked” during this 5-second period, it would have stayed at a fixed angle, with several consecutive boxes showing identical steering wheel angle data in the “Steering Input (deg)” column, but that was not the case here. (Stapleford ¶ 11.)

Overall, the expert opined that the subject accident was not caused by the steering wheel locking up. Instead, the evidence and documents indicate that the accident was caused by driver error, specifically the driver allowing the steering wheel to return to center while attempting to negotiate a left-hand curve in the roadway. (Stapleford ¶ 14.) Defendant has met its burden to show that it was not defendant’s negligence in fixing the steering system that caused the car to lock up that led to the accident.  

Plaintiff’s Argument

Plaintiff argues that Defendants have not met their burden because the video is inconsistent with BHB's theory; however, the video shows that the car turned left and right and then traveled straight and hit the embankment. This path is consistent with the EDR data showing that the wheel returned to center after turning left. The key question remains whether this was due to the driver's error in allowing the car to return to center or if it was due to the steering wheel locking up. Defendant’s expert examined the data and concluded that the data is not consistent with the steering wheel locking up, as the steering input would have stayed at a fixed angle had there been a lockup. Furthermore, both Plaintiff's and Defendant's experts examined the vehicle. Defendant’s expert, Ryan Cram, found the steering system to be functional, but Plaintiff did not present the opinion of his expert witness who examined the car or any expert witness statement for that matter to the contrary.

Plaintiff specifically argues that the steering wheel returned to nearly center, which suggests that the steering wheel likely locked in the near-center position, causing the BMW to travel straight over the embankment and off the road. Plaintiff contends that this prevented steering to the left as alleged in the complaint and stated in Plaintiff’s verified interrogatory responses. However, answers to interrogatories are admissible only against the party providing the answer, per CCP § 2030.410. They may be offered as admissions by the responding party (Ev.C. § 1220) or as prior inconsistent statement to impeach the responding party's testimony at trial (Ev.C. § 1235) but are inadmissible hearsay as to other parties and not admissible as evidence by the responding party to raise a triable issue of fact. Furthermore, complaints are also considered hearsay and are not admissible as evidence.

Plaintiff further argues that the vehicle returned nearly to center, then continued turning past center toward the right as it traveled over the dirt berm, down the embankment, and landed on its left side. Plaintiff contends that this movement "plainly and clearly shows to the naked, lay, and untrained eye that there was no steering to the right past the center," directly contradicting Mr. Stapleford’s opinions and the EDR data. The video, according to Plaintiff, clearly shows the BMW proceeding in a straight trajectory off the embankment, with no apparent steering in any direction, and particularly not to the left, suggesting that the steering wheel was likely locked in a near-center position. The court examined the video and it shows that once the vehicle hits the embankment, it generates a lot of dust, obscuring whether the car is traveling right or left from the point of view of the motorist who took the video from behind. Additionally, once the car hits the embankment, it becomes uncertain whether the front wheels are making contact with the road and whether turning the steering wheel would result in the car actually turning in the intended direction.

The situation is not as straightforward as Plaintiff has suggested. Questions such as to whether the car would actually turn right after driving off the embankment, the interpretation of the EDR data and other technical analysis should be evaluated by an expert witness with the requisite training and education to offer a qualified opinion on these complex matters. This type of analysis should not be left to Plaintiff's counsel or the court, neither of which possess the specialized qualifications required. Regrettably, Plaintiff has not provided any expert witness declaration. The only evidence submitted by Plaintiff includes a copy of the motion to bifurcate and Plaintiff’s discovery responses. Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to raise a triable issue of fact as to the issue of causation, and summary judgment is granted as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for negligence and second cause of action for negligent repair.

Since Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact regarding the steering wheel locking up, there is insufficient evidence to establish that Defendant failed to properly repair Plaintiff’s vehicle and made a false representation concerning a past or existing material fact. Therefore, summary judgment as to the third cause of action for false misrepresentation is granted.

Continuance

Plaintiff seeks a continuance to take the declarants' depositions and cites CCP § 473c(h), which states that if affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication suggest that essential facts to justify opposition may exist but cannot currently be presented for stated reasons, the court shall either deny the motion, order a continuance to allow for obtaining affidavits or for further discovery, or make any other just order. However, Plaintiff submitted no affidavit to support his request for a continuance, failed to specify what facts he seeks to obtain from the declarants’ depositions, and, most significantly, did not explain why these facts could not have been obtained over the past two years since the motions were filed in August 2022. Thus, Plaintiff’s request is denied.

 

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

On March 27, 2019, Plaintiff filed the present complaint. On March 27, 2024, the Court issued an Order granting Beverly Hills BMW’s motions to compel and ordered Plaintiff to provide responses to Defendant’s Requests for Production, Set Six, supplemental responses to RFP, Set Two, and supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Two, within 30 days of the date of this ruling. The Court also ordered Plaintiff to pay sanctions in the amount of $4,500.00. Defendant alleges that Plaintiff’s counsel stated that Plaintiff would return in April 2024 for his mental and physical examination. However, as of the date of the filing of the motions, no discovery responses had been served, no sanctions were paid, and no mental or physical examination had been conducted. Defendant now moves the Court for terminating, issue, evidentiary and/or monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.

Plaintiff argues that "Plaintiff will have complied by providing verified responses to Beverly Hills BMW’s Request for Admissions (Set 2), Beverly Hills BMW’s Form Interrogatories (Set 4), Kiki Cholewka's Request for Admissions (Set 1), and Kiki Cholewka’s Form Interrogatories (Set 1) now due on July 18, 2024 based on the graciously granted extension by counsel, Libman Decl, ¶.2 – exh. 1." (Oppo at p. 6.) But, the discovery order in question relates to RFP, Set Six, supplemental responses to RFP, Set Two, and supplemental responses to SROG, Set Two, and not to RFAs or Form Interrogatories as Plaintiff has alleged. So far, Plaintiff has not shown any indication of complying with the Court's March 27, 2024 discovery order.

Second, Plaintiff argues that the court did not explicitly state a time period for paying sanctions, suggesting an indefinite period is available. However, this interpretation is incorrect; in the absence of a specified timeline, sanctions must be paid within a reasonable period, typically 30 days from the issuance of the order, consistent with the court's requirements for discovery responses. Indefinitely delaying payment is not considered reasonable under any legal standard.

Furthermore, Plaintiff’s counsel indicates that Plaintiff does not intend to comply with the order to pay until the case is settled or a verdict is rendered in Plaintiff's favor. This stance indicates a deliberate non-compliance with the court’s order for monetary sanctions.

Third, Plaintiff's attorney now represents that he has retained the services of an immigration attorney in Ukraine to obtain a visa for travel back to the United States; however, no additional information was provided. There is no declaration from Plaintiff himself or from his retained immigration attorney detailing how long the visa process would take, the likelihood of success, or when Plaintiff can be reasonably expected to return to California. Moreover, the declaration does not address why Plaintiff was unable to access the internet in Ukraine to respond to discovery requests or otherwise fulfill his discovery obligations. Additionally, Plaintiff represented to Defendant that he would return to California in April 2024 to attend a physical and mental examination, but this did not materialize. Without more information, the court does not find Plaintiff’s counsel’s bare-bones declaration to be a sufficient indication that Plaintiff will comply with his discovery obligations.

It is true that there is a policy to determine matters on their merits, as Plaintiff has argued. However, the trial court has broad discretion in selecting the appropriate sanction, and its determination must be upheld absent an abuse of discretion. (Dept. of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 199.)  The court may impose a terminating sanction after considering the totality of the circumstances. This includes assessing the willfulness of the actions, the detriment to the propounding party, and the number of attempts to obtain discovery. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.) Courts are justified in imposing a terminating sanction where the "violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules." (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.)

The court has considered the totality of the evidence: Plaintiff initiated the lawsuit but subsequently left for Ukraine for a significant period. Since then, Plaintiff has failed to fulfill his various discovery obligations, specifically the court’s orders to respond to RFP Set 6, RFP Set 2, and SROG Set 2 (as well as to pay discovery sanctions – though such a failure does not support terminating sanctions). Plaintiff did not make any representations that he would provide the required discovery responses at issue. Furthermore, no concrete information has been provided by Plaintiff regarding when he will return to California and whether he will fulfill his discovery obligations. Based on the totality of the evidence and the sequence of events leading up to the present motion, the Court finds that it could impose terminating sanctions.  However, the Court is persuaded by the fact that Plaintiff did apparently recently comply with a different discovery request to refrain from issuing terminating sanctions.  (The Court recognizes that in light of its ruling above on Defendants’ motions for summary judgment, this determination re sanctions may not be much consolation to Plaitntiff.)  Based on that compliance, albeit that is not the discovery upon which Defendant brought this motion, the Court finds a lesser sanction than terminating sanctions to be appropriate.  In light of the discovery requested and ignored in relation to this motion, the Court grants evidentiary sanctions as follows: Plaintiff is precluded from introducing any photographs or videos of Plaintiff, the subject vehicle and the accident scene. 

Further, Plaintiff is sanctioned $3,305, the cost to Defendant in bringing its motion.  Defendant is ordered to pay this sanction within 30 days.

While the Court is not awarding terminating sanctions, it notes that the idea that Plaintiff can institute a legal proceeding and then take whatever action he wants without consideration of how his action impacts that lawsuit is misguided. He instituted this action in 2019 and essentially abandoned it. The Court recognizes the tragic circumstances currently taking place in Ukraine as a result of Russian aggression, but Plaintiff has not in any way established why he has been completely unable to participate in this action given the ability of technology to allow people throughout the world to connect, including people in Ukraine.  In fact, the Court finds it telling that throughout this litigation, which has included motions to compel, this motion for terminating sanctions, and a motion for summary judgment, Plaintiff has not submitted one declaration himself; the declarations that have been submitted in support of his positions are from his attorneys.

Background

On May 23, 2019, at approximately 8:20 p.m., Plaintiff Lisa Wiltrout (“Plaintiff”) and her husband Plaintiff Michael Valenzuela were walking on a pedestrian breezeway (the “Breezeway”) from the Road to Seoul Restaurant to a parking garage when the brick pavers composing the ground surface suddenly sank or gave way.  Plaintiff fell and fractured her left ankle.  

Plaintiffs brought this lawsuit against Defendant City of Alhambra, among others, alleging that the City was liable to (1) Plaintiff Wiltrout for the injuries she suffered based on a dangerous condition and (2) Plaintiff Valenzuela based on a loss of consortium.  Defendant City of Alhambra now moves the Court for summary judgment on the basis that the alleged defect is trivial and that the City did not have notice of the alleged dangerous condition and that Plaintiff Valenzuela’s action is wholly dependent on Plaintiff Wiltrout’s, so if her action is subject to summary judgment, his is as well.

Legal Standards

        Summary Judgment 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment or adjudication, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.)¿ 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.)¿ 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.)¿ 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].)

Dangerous Condition

Under Government Code § 835, a public entity is liable for injury caused by a dangerous condition of its property if the plaintiff establishes that (1) the property was in a dangerous condition at the time of the injury, (2) the injury was proximately caused by the dangerous condition, (3) the dangerous condition created a reasonably foreseeable risk of the kind of injury which was incurred, and (4) either:

“(a) a negligent or wrongful act or omission of an employee of the public entity within the scope of his employment created the dangerous condition; or

(b) the public entity had actual or constructive notice of the dangerous condition under Section 835.2 a sufficient time prior to the injury to have taken measures to protect against the dangerous condition.”

Notice

Government Code 835.2 states: 

(a) A public entity had actual notice of a dangerous condition within the meaning of subdivision (b) of Section 835 if it had actual knowledge of the existence of the condition and knew or should have known of its dangerous character.

(b) A public entity had constructive notice of a dangerous condition within the meaning of subdivision (b) of Section 835 only if the plaintiff establishes that the condition had existed for such a period of time and was of such an obvious nature that the public entity, in the exercise of due care, should have discovered the condition and its dangerous character.

(b) (1) In assessing the exercise of due care, the court looks at whether the existence of the condition and its dangerous character would have been discovered by an inspection system that was reasonably adequate (considering the practicability and cost of inspection weighed against the likelihood and magnitude of the potential danger to which failure to inspect would give rise) to inform the public entity whether the property was safe for the use or uses for which the public entity used or intended others to use the public property and for uses that the public entity actually knew others were making of the public property or adjacent property.

(b) (2) Whether the public entity maintained and operated such an inspection system with due care and did not discover the condition.

Evidentiary Ruling

The Court sustains Defendant’s objections No. 10 to Declaration of Brad Avrit on the basis that it misstates the testimony/evidence and No. 17 to Declaration of Brad Avrit on the basis that it lacks foundation and is speculative.

The Parties did not make any objections to evidence that were material to the Court’s ruling. (CCP § 437c(q).)¿  

Discussion

To meet its initial burden of showing that it had no notice of the alleged dangerous condition, Defendant City submitted a declaration from Chris Kustra, the claim director overseeing the City’s liability claims. Kustra searched the database for any claims of damage presented to the City at the location in question and found no prior claims for damages. (Kustra Decl. ¶ 2-6.)

There was, however, a prior request for services to be performed on the Breezeway.  Specifically on February 19, 2019, Kip Su, the City's Utilities Technician, noticed sunken bricks in the Breezeway while walking on it and filed a request for service.  That request pertains to a different portion of the Breezeway than the location where Plaintiff fell. The February 2019 service location was near the entrance of the Breezeway; the location of Plaintiff’s fall was near a manhole, a considerable distance from the entrance. (Su Decl. ¶ 6-8, Ex. J to Declaration of Ricky Vergara.) Su testified that had he observed a similar sunken condition at the location at issue, he would have reported it, but he did not. (Su Decl. ¶ 9.) “To establish ‘actual notice,’ it is not enough to show that the state employees had a general knowledge [of the dangerous condition]. There must be some evidence that the employees had knowledge of the particular dangerous condition in question.” (State v. Superior Court for San Mateo County (1968) 263 Cal.App.2d 396, 399.) 

Furthermore, Rick Vergara, a public works inspector for the City, declares that in responding to the February 19, 2019 request for service, he performed an inspection of the entire Breezeway and does not recall seeing any condition that needed repair other than the site identified in the February 2019 request for service. If he had observed any other condition in need of repair, he would have noted it in the request for service and recommended repair. (Vergara Decl. ¶ 7-8.)  In deposition, he likewise testified that he had inspected the entire alley in February in response to Su’s service request and noted only the sections of the Breezeway that were damaged. (Vergara Depo. 22:19-24.) When asked to clarify what he meant by inspecting the entire alley, Vergara explained that he inspected the whole Breezeway from the parking lot to Garfield Avenue, which allowed him to sufficiently view the area. He stated that the visual inspection occurred during working hours and took approximately five minutes. (Vergara Depo. 23:1-25:19.)

Defendant has met its initial burden that it had no actual notice or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition.

Furthermore, to prevail on constructive notice, Plaintiff must demonstrate that the condition at issue was obvious (Heskel v. City of San Diego (2014) 227 CA4th 313, 319-320.) Photos of the area taken by Plaintiff just weeks after the incident on June 5, 2019, did not reveal any visible sunken pavers, displacement, cracks, or any other distinguishing features from the surrounding tiles. Even with knowledge of the approximate location where Plaintiff allegedly fell, the Court is unable to precisely identify where she fell due to the non-obvious nature of the alleged dangerous condition. (Def. Ex. A.) It is not surprising that Vergara did not spot any signs of a dangerous condition at the location at issue when he allegedly performed the inspection in February 2019.

Triable Issue of Fact

Although as noted above the Court is striking pages 21 to 33 of the opposition, which contain all of Plaintiff's arguments regarding the issue of notice, even if the Court were to consider the arguments set forth in the opposition, it still does not find that Plaintiff has raised a triable issue of fact relating to notice.

Before the Court assesses whether the City has a reasonable inspection program in place for purposes of constructive notice, Plaintiff must make a threshold showing that the alleged dangerous condition was obvious. (Heskel v. City of San Diego (2014) 227 CA4th 313, 319-320.) Defendant has introduced photographs demonstrating that the alleged dangerous condition was not obvious.  (Def. Ex. A).  These photographs do not show any distinguishing features at the location where the alleged dangerous condition purportedly exists compared to the surrounding area or tiles.  Although there are photographs showing missing tiles, sunken tiles, and repairs being made to the location at issue (Exhibit I to Hernandez Decl.), Luis Hernandez, a senior concrete worker for the City, has declared that these photos were taken in October 2019, five months after the incident. (Hernandez Decl. ¶ 5-8.) And, of course, they were taken at the time repairs were being made.  This timeline is confirmed by the declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel’s legal assistant, Tracy Hughley. Hughley investigated the Breezeway on two occasions and took photographs. The first set of photos, taken in June 2019 (Ex. A to Hughley Decl.), show no signs of sunken tiles at the location at issue even after the incident occurred.  The second set, taken in September 2019 (Ex. B to Hughley Decl.), show missing tiles and repairs being conducted at the same location. Besides these photographs from September 2019, Plaintiff has not produced any affirmative evidence that the location at issue was visibly sunken or that the dangerous condition was obvious, which is necessary to prevail on the basis of constructive notice.

Plaintiff argues that Vergara did not inspect the entirety of the Breezeway, thereby missing the alleged dangerous condition that was supposedly obvious. However, Plaintiff’s argument selectively cites Vergara’s deposition and omits the part where he stated he visually inspected the whole Breezeway and did not observe any sunken tiles other than the areas identified by Su, which, as discussed above, do not include the location where Plaintiff fell. Plaintiff further contends that there is no documentation of Vergara's inspection of the Breezeway in February 2019; however, Vergara clearly states in his deposition that he conducted the inspection but only documented the areas that needed service on the request. (Vergara Depo. 25:1-19). The absence of written records does not demonstrate that the inspection did not take place, as Vergara has declared to the contrary. In fact, a good amount of Plaintiff's argument centers on the theme that Defendant's evidentiary support could have been stronger. For example, no documents were produced showing Vergara's inspection. However, Defendant is not required to provide all-encompassing documents as long as Defendant has met its burden, which it has. Plaintiff cannot create a triable issue by attacking the credibility of the moving party’s evidence. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c.)

Plaintiff also argues that between February 19, 2019, and May 23, 2019, no one from Defendant City, other than Vergara, went out to check whether any other pavers in the Breezeway were sinking. The Court finds this argument unpersuasive because Vergara, the city’s inspector, already conducted a visual inspection and found no sunkenness at the location in question. It is unclear why additional employees would need to perform further inspections, or what such additional inspections would entail.

Plaintiff argues that Vergara did not individually check each paver, did not use a leveling device, and did not employ a device to press and depress each paver. (Vergara Depo. 25:24-26) Plaintiff and her expert contend that the City or Vergara should have inspected each tile and tested them one by one to determine if a particular tile was dangerous. Plaintiff's expert states that had Vergara sufficiently inspected the stability of each brick paver during his inspection on February 19, 2019, he would have noticed the blank/empty space between the pavers and the gravel/sand underneath. (Avrit Decl. 8:3-6.) The expert attests to a potential condition beneath the tiles, but this simply supports Defendant's argument regarding the non-obvious nature of the dangerous condition. Given that there are likely thousands of tiles in the Breezeway, the Court finds it unreasonable to impose the burden on the City to inspect each tile individually when there is no visible indication of a dangerous condition.

Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue of fact that Vergara should have discovered the alleged dangerous condition during his inspection of the Breezeway just three months before the incident. Photographs provided by Plaintiff, Plaintiff counsel’s legal assistant, and the Defendant all show that the location at issue exhibited no signs of sunken pavers and lacked any distinguishing features that could deem it potentially dangerous compared to the surrounding tiles. Plaintiff has failed to introduce any affirmative evidence to the contrary and has not shown how Defendant had either actual or constructive notice of the alleged dangerous condition. Thus, the City’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

Moving Party: Plaintiff 

Responding Party: Defendant KECK MEDICAL CENTER OF USC

Ruling: MOTION FOR RELIEF¿IS DENIED

 

Background 

 

On January 25, 2021, Plaintiff filed suit against Defendant Keck Medical Center of USC. Defendant served discovery requests, including requests for admission to Plaintiff, and filed motions to compel responses on March 21, 2024. On February 8, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel, Marielys Acosta, filed a motion to be relieved as counsel. On March 21, 2024, the court granted Acosta’s relief as counsel and also deemed Defendant’s RFAs admitted. Plaintiff, now appearing pro per, moves the court for relief from the admission.

 

Legal standard

 

A party may withdraw or amend an admission made in response to a request for admission only on leave of court granted after notice to all parties. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.300, subd. (a).) The court may permit the withdrawal only if it determines the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.300, subd. (b).) The “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect” under CCP § 2033.300 have similar meanings as those words used in CCP § 473(b). (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1419.)

 

“Because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 2033.300 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief. Accordingly, the court's discretion to deny a motion under the statute is limited to circumstances where it is clear that the mistake, inadvertence, or¿neglect was inexcusable, or where it is clear that the withdrawal or amendment would not substantially prejudice the party who obtained the admission in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.” (New Albertsons, Inc., supra, 168 Cal.App.4th at 1420–21.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for relief under the incorrect CCP section. The applicable section is 2033.230, not CCP 473, though both sections provide relief for mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, which the court will consider. It is unclear from Plaintiff’s motion what the specific mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect is. Plaintiff alleges various failures by her attorney that purportedly harmed her case, such as not filing timely discovery responses or collaborating with Plaintiff on the discovery responses. Additionally, Plaintiff accuses her attorney of lying to the court in the motion to be relieved as counsel. The court is still unsure what the exact mistake or inadvertence was that led to the RFAs being deemed admitted against Plaintiff. Plaintiff seems to be arguing that this was due to her attorney's alleged incompetence or deliberate refusal to cooperate. However, these do not constitute a mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect as required for relief under 2033.230.[1] Thus, the motion is denied.
