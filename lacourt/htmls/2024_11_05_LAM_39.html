DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCP03332    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 5, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCP03332

MOTION: 

Motion for Entry of Satisfaction of Judgment

MOVING PARTY:

Respondent Kandela, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:

Assignee Scottsdale Indemnity Company

 

MOTIONS

Petitioners Porch.com, Inc. and Matthew Ehrlichman (“Petitioners”) participated in an arbitration of claims with Respondent Kandela, LLC (“Respondent”).  In its order of October 12, 2022, the Court confirmed the arbitration award and entered judgment pursuant to its terms.  Now, Respondent moves for an order for entry of satisfaction of judgment.  Assignee Scottsdale Indemnity Company (“Judgment Creditor”) opposes the petition.

ANALYSIS

“A money judgment may be satisfied by payment of the full amount required to satisfy the judgment or by acceptance by the judgment creditor of a lesser sum in full satisfaction of the judgment.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 724.010, subd. (a).)  If the judgment creditor fails to file an acknowledgement of satisfaction of the judgment upon payment in full, the judgment debtor may file a written demand that the judgment creditor file such acknowledgment with the Court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 724.050, subd. (a).)  The demand obligates the judgment creditor to file the acknowledgement within 15 days of receipt.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 724.050, subd. (c).)  If the judgment creditor fails to comply with the demand, the judgment debtor may file a noticed motion for entry of satisfaction of judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 724.050, subd. (d).)  If the Court determines the judgment is satisfied and the judgment creditor has not complied with the demand, the Court may order the judgment creditor to file the acknowledgment of satisfaction of judgment or direct the Court’s clerk to enter satisfaction of the judgment.  (Ibid.)

On October 12, 2022, the Court ordered judgment entered for Petitioners in the amount of $1,401,814, plus interest from July 25, 2022.  (See October 12, 2022 Judgment Following Arbitration.)  In its order of November 16, 2023, the Court also ordered Respondent to pay Petitioners’ attorney fees in the amount of $93,875.50.  (See November 16, 2023 Minute Order.)  Petitioners assigned the judgment and right to attorney fees to the Judgment Creditor.  (See April 29, 2024 Acknowledgement of Assignment of Judgment.)  On September 12, 2024, Respondent served a demand on Judgment Creditor that Judgment Creditor file an acknowledgment of satisfaction of judgment with the Court.  (See Declaration of Daniel M. Hayes, Exhibit 9.)  Judgment Creditor has not complied with the demand.  However, Respondent offers no evidence it satisfied the judgment.  Accordingly, the Court cannot determine the judgment is satisfied.  Instead, Respondent argues that a contractual offset applies.

In the assignment of their claims to Judgment Creditor, Petitioners acknowledged the Judgment Creditor paid their defense costs in the underlying action. (See April 29, 2024 Acknowledgement of Satisfaction of Judgment, Assignment of Claims, ¶ E.)  Respondent argues this bars Petitioners claim pursuant to the terms of the underlying agreement between the parties, the March 22, 2019 Asset Purchase Agreement (APA).  Pursuant to section 8.04, subdivision (f) of the APA, Petitioners and Respondent agreed should the parties face certain claims, Petitioners would indemnify Respondent and Respondent would indemnify Petitioners.  The parties also agreed, in such circumstances, the amounts by which the parties would indemnify each other “will be reduced by any amounts actually received under insurance policies . . . .”  (Hayes Decl., Exhibit 1, Attachment 4(b).)  However, a duty to indemnify only arises when the circumstances specified occur.  (Rossmoor Sanitation, Inc. v. Pylon, Inc. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 622, 628.) 

Respondent fails to explain how section 8.04, subdivision (f) is applicable to the judgment that has been entered against it.  As Respondent fails to show that provision is relevant to this dispute, Respondent has likewise failed to show section 8.09, which makes subdivision 8.04 the exclusive remedy for disputes that fall within its scope, applies.  Accordingly, the motion is denied.

In reply, among other things, Respondent argues the Judgment Creditor has taken a contrary position in Scottsdale Indemnity Company v. Kandela, LLC, et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2023, No. 23STCV01162) (case No. 23STCV01162) and, therefore, is estopped from arguing the APA does not apply.[1]  Respondent cannot raise this issue for the first time in its reply brief, as Judgment Creditor has not had the opportunity to respond.  “The general rule of motion practice, which applies here, is that new evidence is not permitted with reply papers.” (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 1522, 5137.)  Therefore, Respondent’s second request for the Court to take judicial notice, filed in support of its reply brief on October 29, 2024, is denied.  In any event, Respondent’s argument is unsupported. 

The doctrine of judicial estoppel applies when: “(1) the same party has taken two positions; (2) the positions were taken in judicial or quasi-judicial administrative proceedings; (3) the party was successful in asserting the first position (i.e., the tribunal adopted the position or accepted it as true); (4) the two positions are totally inconsistent; and (5) the first position was not taken as a result of ignorance, fraud, or mistake.” (Jackson v. County of Los Angeles (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 171, 183.)  Respondent fails to show the Court in case No. 23STCV01162 accepted Judgment Creditor’s position.

Respondent is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


[1]On October 10, 2024, Respondent filed a document entitled “Request for Judicial Notice” in which it referenced documents attached to a Declaration of Daniel M. Hayes, which was filed separately and concurrently.  Respondent requests that this Court take judicial notice of certain documents filed in this case, certain documents filed in case No. 23STCV01162, and a September 12, 2024 letter.  Petitioners have not objected to the request.    Although the Court will take judicial notice of those matters, the Court notes the Court does not take judicial notice of any truth of statements purportedly made in them.  (See e.g., Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 389, 400 [judicial notice not taken of truth of averments made in documents filed in medical malpractice case, including findings made by trial court]; Barri v. Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 428, 437-438 [court took judicial notice of existence but not truth of factual finding by federal court]; Richtek USA v. uPI Semiconductor (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 651, 659 [court took judicial notice of complaint appended to complaint in a related federal court case but could not use contents of complaints to resolve factual dispute]; Plumley v. Mockett (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1050-1051 [judicial notice of a court’s findings in underlying action was proper but not truth of those findings].)


Case Number: 22STCV22643    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

November 5, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

22STCV22643

MOTION: 

Motion to Compel Deposition

MOVING PARTY:

Defendant Harrison Kordestani

OPPOSING PARTY:

Plaintiff JJ Zhang

 

MOTIONS

            Defendant Harrison Kordestani (“Defendant”) moves to compel Plaintiff JJ Zhang (“Plaintiff”) to appear at his deposition.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a), if a party to the action fails to testify at deposition after service of a deposition notice and the party has not served a valid objection to that notice, the party who noticed the deposition may move for an order to compel the deponent to attend and testify at the deposition.  Plaintiff is a party to this action.  As such, he must appear for the deposition.  The motion is granted. 

The Court notes Plaintiff objected to the deposition notice and states Defendant was dilatory in scheduling the deposition more than two years after the action was file and a few months before the trial is to take place.  Those are not valid reasons for Plaintiff not to have made himself available for deposition.  If Plaintiff sought to avoid appearing or producing documents on specified topics, Plaintiff was required to seek a protective order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420.)  Plaintiff did not do so. The scope of the requested information is not an “error or irregularity” in the deposition notice, to which Plaintiff could object. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410.)

Defendant seeks sanctions in this motion.  However, Plaintiff argues Defendant unilaterally scheduled the deposition with knowledge neither Plaintiff or his cancel was available on the date it was scheduled; he timely objected to the deposition; Defendant cancelled the deposition; Plaintiff did not “fail to appear,” as Defendant claims, and, therefore, Defendant incurred no costs; and Defendant did not meet and confer to resolve the issues.  In reply, Defendant has countered the positions Plaintiff has taken with respect to this matter, including indicating Plaintiff has made no effort to be available for the noticed deposition by offering dates; rather, Plaintiff has been steadfast in stating his deposition can only be taken after the scheduled trial in early December.  

Under these circumstances, the Court finds Plaintiff’s failure to comply with Defendant’s deposition notice and to be reasonably available for deposition to be an abuse of the discovery process and awards sanctions, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 2023.030, subdivision (a).  Therefore, the Court imposes sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel of record, Dominique Westmoreland, in the amount of $1,260, which represents two hours of attorney time to prepare the motion and to attend the hearing at $600 per hour, plus the filing fee. 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to appear for deposition is granted.  Plaintiff is to appear for deposition within 30 days of notice of this order, unless Plaintiff stipulates otherwise. The scope of the deposition is as set forth in Exhibit 2 attached to the Declaration of John M. Boyko.

Plaintiff and his counsel of record, Dominique Westmoreland, jointly and severally, are to pay $1,260 in sanctions to Defendant, by and through counsel, within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

 


Case Number: 22STCV33046    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

32

HEARING DATE

November 5, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV33046

MOTION

Motion for Terminating Sanctions

MOVING PARTY

Defendants Huge USA, Inc. and Jin Ser Park

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Nam Suk Park

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Nam Suk Park (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Huge USA, Inc. (“Huge USA”) and Jin Ser Park (“Park,” collectively, “Defendants”) for wage and hour violations.  Defendants move for terminating sanctions, or, in the alternative, issue sanctions against Plaintiff.  Plaintiff opposes the motion. 

ANALYSIS

Defendants argue the Court should impose sanctions due to Plaintiff’s misconduct.  In support of this argument, Defendants cite Stephen Slesinger, Inc. v. Walt Disney Co. (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 736 (Slesinger).)  In that case, the Court held a trial court has inherent power to dismiss an action with prejudice “[w]hen a plaintiff's deliberate and egregious misconduct in the course of the litigation renders any sanction short of dismissal inadequate to protect the fairness of the trial.”  (Id., at p. 762.)  The plaintiff in Slesinger illegally obtained confidential information from the defendant by breaking into dumpster locations and taking copies of documents.  (Id., at pp. 767-768.)  The Court found the plaintiff’s acts, “whether characterized as civil wrongdoing, criminal wrongdoing, or both,” were “deliberate and egregious—more than adequate to invoke the court's exercise of its inherent power to dismiss.”  (Id., at p. 768.)

Defendants advance the testimony of Kyu Chul Shin (“Shin”), a former employee of Huge USA.  At deposition, Shin testified he accessed Huge USA’s computer system after he no longer worked for Huge USA, and he downloaded time records for another employee, Luis Rueda, at Plaintiff’s request.  (Declaration of Daniel E. Park, Exhibit B.)  Defendants also submit a declaration from Shin, which is consistent with his deposition testimony.  (Declaration of Kyu Chul Shin.)  Defendants have, therefore, shown Plaintiff directed Shin to access Huge USA’s computer system and violate the Comprehensive Computer Data Access and Fraud Act, thereby committing the civil wrong of invasion of privacy.  (Pen. Code, § 502, see Casillas v. Berkshire Hathaway Homestate Ins. Co. (2022) 79 Cal.App.5th 755, 768.) 

The Court finds this deliberate illegal access of Huge USA’s personal records is sanctionable.  However, the Court concludes dismissal is not required to vindicate the Court’s authority to regulate this proceeding.  (See Rail Services of America v. State Compensation Insurance Fund (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 323, 331[“Dismissal is a proper sanction to punish the failure to comply with a rule or an order only if the court’s authority cannot be vindicated through the imposition of a less severe alternative].)  At this time, the Court would impose Defendants’ requested sanction to instruct the jury at trial Plaintiff caused Shin to unlawfully access Huge USA’s business records.  Given this special instruction, the jury may infer Plaintiff’s testimony on other matters is untrue.  (See e.g., CACI No. 107 [to the extent the jury believes a witness has not told the truth about something important, it may choose not to believe anything the witness has said].)   Moreover, if through further discovery Defendants were to unearth additional evidence Plaintiff engaged in untoward conduct, the Court would entertain imposing harsher sanctions at trial. 

Defendants contend the Court should impose sanctions based on spoliation.  “Spoliation is the destruction or significant alteration of evidence, or the failure to preserve property for another's use as evidence, in pending or future litigation.”  (Hernandez v. Garcetti (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 675, 680.)  Under California law, a party cannot destroy evidence “in response to a discovery request after litigation has commenced . . . ,” or “in anticipation of a discovery request.”  (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, 12.)  

In this regard, Defendants argue Plaintiff failed to produce messages between Plaintiff and Shin.  Defendants do not advance any evidence Plaintiff intentionally deleted any messages to evade discovery.  Messages are commonly deleted for benign reasons, such as to free up memory on  cell phones.  Further, as Defendants concede, Plaintiff stated in response to discovery Plaintiff changed cell phones and lost messages.  (Park Decl., Exhibit C.)  Without more, Defendants have not demonstrated spoliation.  While Defendants also advance Shin’s declaration, his statements about Plaintiff’s deletion of their message history (Declaration of Kyu Chul Shin, ¿ 5) are vague and lack adequate foundation, despite Shin’s assertion Plaintiff informed him of the deletion.  Additionally, Shin admits the messages on his cell phone were lost when he changed cell phones himself (id., ¿ 6); therefore, lending some support to Plaintiff’s similar contention in that vein.  For those reasons, the Court declines to rely on Shin’s testimony in support of Defendants’ position on this point. 

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
