RULING: 

 

Defendant’s Motion For Terminating Sanctions is DENIED.

 

Defendant’s Motion For Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

 

Background 

 

On March 11, 2024, the Court heard Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions. The Court continued the hearing to March 22, 2024, and ordered Plaintiff to file a declaration concerning counsel's health condition and unpaid sanctions. Plaintiff filed two declarations demonstrating that the Court ordered discovery responses were provided and the outstanding sanctions were paid. Plaintiff’s counsel also declare that he is healthy enough to continue with litigating the case. Defendant contends that not all sanctions were paid.

 

Legal Standard 

 

Terminating sanctions are appropriate when a party persists in disobeying the court's orders. (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 795-796.)  A terminating sanction is a "drastic measure which should be employed with caution." (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at 793.) "A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction." (Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, 279-280.) While the court has discretion to impose terminating sanctions, these sanctions "should be appropriate to the dereliction and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery." (Deyo, 84 Cal.App.3d at 793.) "[A] court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations." (Ibid.) Discovery sanctions are not to be imposed for punishment, but instead are used to encourage fair disclosure of discovery to prevent unfairness resulting for the lack of information. (See Midwife v. Bernal (1988) 203 Cal.App.3d 57, 64 [superseded on other grounds as stated in Kohan v. Cohan (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 967, 971].)  

 

Analysis 

 

In the original motion, Defendant outlined a list of discovery violations, including the Court's orders for sanctions and demands for further responses. Specifically:

 

·        The Court's September 30, 2022, order imposed a sanction of $810.

·        The Court's September 5, 2023, order required a payment of $960.

·        The Court's December 18, 2023, order mandated further responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production of Documents, along with a sanction of $3,000 that had not yet been paid.

 

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff served the further responses as per the Court's December 18, 2023, order and mailed a check to Defendant for $3,960. Plaintiff’s counsel also declared that his test for cancer came out negative and he is now ready and able to work on the case. Plaintiff has substantially complied with court’s order for sanctions and production of further discovery responses, and thus less severe sanctions would produce compliance with the Court’s order. Thus, Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED.

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiff has yet to remit $3,579 in court-ordered monetary sanctions, despite Plaintiff's payment of $3,960. However, Defendant failed to provide the Court with a detailed accounting of how this figure was calculated. [TENTATIVE] Ruling re Defendant Dewyine Ross’s Application of Good Faith Settlement 

¿¿ 

Hon. Lee S. Arian¿ 

Dept. 27¿ 

March 22, 2023¿ 

1:30 p.m.¿¿¿ 

¿ 

RULING: 

 

Defendant Dewyine Ross’s Application of Good Faith Settlement is DENIED.

 

Background

 

On May 19, 2021, Plaintiff filed the present complaint, alleging Defendant Dewyine Ross crashed into a Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority bus. Plaintiff Elvis Presley was a passenger on the LACMTA bus. On November 6, 2023, Plaintiff settled with Defendant Dewyine Ross for $15,000. Defendant LA County Metropolitan Transportation Authority and Richard Walker contest the application, arguing the amount is grossly disproportionate to the tortfeasor's proportionate share of liability. However, neither party provided a rough approximation of Plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability.

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP section 877.6(a)(1) provides that “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors . . . .”¿ (Code. Civ. Proc., § 877.6(a)(1).)¿ “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”¿ (Id., § 877.6(c).)¿ Although a determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.¿ (Id., § 877(a).)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Factors to consider in determining if a settlement was made in good faith include “a rough approximation of plaintiffs’ total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial.”¿ (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.)¿ “Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of nonsettling defendants.”¿ (Id.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

The evaluation of whether a settlement was made in good faith is required to “be made on the basis of information available at the time of settlement.”¿ (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499.)¿ “[A] court not only looks at the alleged tortfeasor’s potential liability to the plaintiff, but it must also consider the culpability of the tortfeasor vis-à-vis other parties alleged to be responsible for the same injury.”¿ (TSI Seismic Tenant Space, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 159, 166.)¿ “Potential liability for indemnity to a nonsettling defendant is an important consideration for the trial court in determining whether to approve a settlement by an alleged tortfeasor.”¿ (Id.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Where a non-settling defendant contests a good faith settlement application, the moving party must make a sufficient showing of all the Tech-Bilt factors. (Weil & Brown at § 12:872 citing City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (Boyter) (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261.) Once there has been a sufficient showing, “the party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6(d).)  The party asserting the lack of good faith can establish that the proposed settlement was not a settlement made in good faith by showing the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark” in relation to the Tech-Bilt factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute.  (Tech-Bilt, Inc., supra, 38 Cal.3d at 499-500.) 

Analysis

In the motion or reply, the settling parties failed to provide a rough approximation of Plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability, a crucial Tech-Bilt factor. Defendant MTA disputes the settlement on the grounds that it is grossly disproportionate to the tortfeasor's liability share.  Without an approximation of Plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability, the Court cannot determine if the settlement is indeed grossly disproportionate. Therefore, the motion is DENIED without prejudice.

[TENTATIVE] Ruling re Plaintiff’s Motion for Order to Substitute in Decedent's Successor in Interest to Continue Action

¿¿ 

Hon. Lee S. Arian¿ 

Dept. 27¿ 

March 22, 2023¿ 

1:30 p.m.¿¿¿ 

¿ 

RULING: 

 

Defendant Plaintiff’s Motion for Order to Substitute in Decedent's Successor in Interest to Continue Action is GRANTED.

 

Background 

 

On 8/1/22, Plaintiff filed the present auto accident case. Plaintiff died on October 9, 2023, but Plaintiff’s claims survive her death. Frank Antonio Gastelum, the decedent's son and successor in interest, now seeks the Court's approval to be substituted as the successor in interest for this case.

 

Legal Standard 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 377.31 provides that the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, the decedent’s successor in interest may continue a decedent’s pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.30; see Adams v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 71, 78-79.) A successor in interest is the beneficiary of the decedent’s estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of the cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.11.) Section 377.33 provides that the court in which an action is continued may make any order concerning parties that is appropriate to ensure proper administration of justice, including the appointment of the decedent’s successor in interest as a special administrator or guardian ad litem.¿¿ 

 

Under Section 377.32(a), the person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent's successor in interest under this article, shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating all of the following:  

(1) The decedent's name.  
(2) The date and place of the decedent's death.  
(3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent's estate.”  
(4) If the decedent's estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent's cause of action to the successor in interest.  
(5) Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof:  
     (A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”  
     (B) “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent's successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”  
(6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.”  
(7) “The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.”  

(b) Where more than one person executes the affidavit or declaration under this section, the statements required by subdivision (a) shall be modified as appropriate to reflect that fact. 

(c) A certified copy of the decedent's death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Id., § 377.32(c).)¿¿ 

Analysis 

 

All requirements under CCP § 377.32 have been met. The affidavit of Frank Antonio Gastelum, attached to the motion, contains all information required under CCP § 377.32(a). Additionally, the decedent's death certificate was submitted, and a stipulation among all successors in interest appoints Frank Antonio Gastelum as their representative.

Thus, Plaintiff's present motion is GRANTED.

[TENTATIVE] Ruling re Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Conduct Financial Discovery on Defendants Clayton Damian Fischer and Southwark Metal Manufacturing

¿¿ 

Hon. Lee S. Arian¿ 

Dept. 27¿ 

March 22, 2023¿ 

1:30 p.m.¿¿¿ 

¿ 

RULING: 

 

Plaintiff’s Motions for Leave to Conduct Financial Discovery on Defendants Clayton Damian Fischer and Southwark Metal Manufacturing Are DENIED.

 

 

Background

 

On August 29, 2022, Plaintiff filed an auto collision complaint alleging that Defendant Clayton Damian Fischer was driving under the influence when he crashed into Plaintiff. At the time of the collision, Fischer was working within the course and scope of his employment with Co-Defendant Southwark Metal Manufacturing. Southwark was allegedly aware of Fischer’s drunk driving yet permitted him to drive the company car while under the influence. Plaintiff now seeks the Court's permission to conduct financial discovery on both Defendant Fischer and Defendant Southwark.[1]

 

Legal Standard

 

Civil Code section 3295(c) prohibits pretrial discovery by a plaintiff with respect to the financial condition of defendants absent a court order.  “Upon motion by the plaintiff supported by appropriate affidavits and after a hearing, if the court deems a hearing to be necessary, the court may at any time enter an order permitting the discovery otherwise prohibited by this subdivision if the court finds, on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented, that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294.  Such order shall not be considered to be a determination on the merits of the claim or any defense thereto and shall not be given in evidence or referred to at the trial.”  (Civ. Code, § 3295, subd. (c).)   

 

“A trial court considering a motion to permit discovery of a defendant’s financial condition must weigh the evidence submitted in support of and in opposition to the motion and determine whether the plaintiff has established a ‘substantial probability’ (Civ. Code, § 3295, subd. (c)) of prevailing on a claim for punitive damages.  [Citation.]  In this context, a ‘substantial probability’ of prevailing on a claim for punitive damages means that it is ‘very likely’ that the plaintiff will prevail on such a claim or there is ‘ “a strong likelihood ” ’ that the plaintiff will prevail on such a claim.  [Citation.]”  (Kerner v. Superior Court (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 84, 120 (Kerner).)   

 

Punitive damages may be imposed where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).)¿ “Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Civil Code section 3294 (c)(1).) Under the statute, malice does not require actual intent to harm. Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully or intentionally fails to avoid such consequences. (Pfeifer v.John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.) ‘Despicable’ is a powerful term that refers to circumstances that are ‘base,’ ‘vile,’ or ‘contemptible.’ (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.)¿¿ 

In Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89, the court held that driving while intoxicated does not always give rise to a claim for punitive damages: “[W]e do not agree that the risk created generally by one who becomes intoxicated and decides nevertheless to drive a vehicle to the public streets is the same as the risk created by an intoxicated driver’s decision to zigzag in and out of traffic at 65 miles per hour in a crowded beach recreation area at 1:30 in the afternoon on a Sunday in June.  The risk of injury to others from ordinary driving while intoxicated is certainly foreseeable, but it is not necessarily probable.” Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 89

 

The Dawes court went on to note, “Justice Franson aptly noted the distinction in his article on punitive damages in vehicle accident cases: ‘Allegations of intoxication, excessive speed, driving with defective equipment or the running of a stop signal, without more, do not state a cause of action for punitive damages.  [Par.]  On the other hand, if the facts show that the defendant intentionally drove his vehicle at a high speed into an intersection crowded with pedestrians, or if he drove at a high speed through a crowded residential area where children were playing in the street, a legitimate inference of actual malice perhaps could arise.  This would be particularly true if the defendant had not been drinking, or, if drinking, he was not under the influence to the point where he was incapable of being aware of the situation confronting him.  Under these circumstances, it reasonably might be said that the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.”  (Id. at 90.)   

 

Evidentiary Ruling

Motion to Conduct Financial Discovery Against Defendant Fischer: Defendant's objection No. 4 is sustained on the grounds of relevance and prejudice. Other objections are overruled.

 

Motion to Conduct Financial Discovery Against Defendant Southwark: Defendant's objections 1-5 are sustained due to a lack of foundation. Other objections are overruled.

 

Analysis

 

Punitive Damages Re Fischer

 

The Court reviewed the evidence provided by Plaintiff relating to Defendant Clayton Fischer’s alleged malice and notes that it all relates to the contention that Fischer was driving under the influence of alcohol: police report showing that Fischer was driving under the influence, Fischer pleading to a DUI, and Fischer’s deposition that he drank 4 cocktails sometime before the incident, etc.. However, allegations of intoxication… without more, do not state a cause of action for punitive damages. ' (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 82, 90.)

Plaintiff failed to show other circumstances, such as Fischer intentionally or willfully driving at high speed through a crowded residential area where children were playing in the street, to establish malice. “This would be particularly true if the defendant had not been drinking, or, if drinking, he was not under the influence to the point where he was incapable of being aware of the situation confronting him.”  (Id. at 90.)  The fact that Fischer was drinking actually undermines Plaintiff's argument for malice, as it is not evident if Fischer was aware of his surroundings to the degree necessary for a finding of willfulness.

Again, the evidence provided solely pertains to Fischer's driving under the influence. Plaintiff did not present evidence to show that Fischer engaged in conduct greater than that such that one could deem it despicable, as that term is understood under the law.

 

Punitve Damages Re Southwark

Plaintiff contends he is substantially likely to be awarded punitive damages against Southwark based in the first instance on the conduct of Fischer and then, relatedly Southwark’s related conduct.  As noted above, the Court is not convinced the evidence is sufficient as to Fischer for that purpose.  Then, the only evidence presented for punitive damages against Southwark beyond counsel's declaration and allegations in the complaint regarding Southwark's malice, is Defendant Fischer’s deposition where he states that as long as he was contributing a million dollars a month to Southwark, they never enforced any safety protocols regarding driving safety with him. The Court certainly perceives that to be bad conduct, it true.  But, the evidence does not demonstrate with substantial certainty that Southwark was aware of Fischer's drunk driving.  

It is the moving party's burden to demonstrate a substantial likelihood that Plaintiff can provide clear and convincing evidence of Defendants' malice, and Plaintiff has failed to meet this burden. Thus, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to conduct financial discovery against Defendant Fischer and against Defendant Southwark is DENIED.
