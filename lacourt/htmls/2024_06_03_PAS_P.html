DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21GDCV00328    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES


I.        INTRODUCTION  

This is an action for release of a mechanic’s lien.

The complaint was tried by way of default on January 10, 2024. The cross-complaint was struck on October 30, 2023. Plaintiff Gilbert Acosta now moves for an order fixing Plaintiffs’ fee award.

This motion was filed March 8, 2024. It is unopposed.

The motion is granted and fees are awarded in the amount requested.

         

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code Civ. Proc. §1033.5(a)(1) provides for attorney fees as costs when provided for by contract, statute, or law. Whoever prevails on a petition to release property from a mechanic’s lien is entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees. (Civ. Code § 8488(c).)

           Civ. Code §1717 (a) provides in part that “reasonable attorney’s fees shall be fixed by the court and shall be an element of the costs of suit.”

          PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084 contains the Supreme Court’s explanation of how fees are to be determined. It states that Civ. Code §1717, “reflects the legislative purpose ‘to establish uniform treatment of fee recoveries in actions on contracts containing attorney fee provisions.’ (Citation.) Consistent with that purpose, the trial court has broad authority to determine the amount of a reasonable fee.” Id. at 1095. PLCM teaches that fees are to be determined by calculating the “‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (Ibid.)        

 

III.     ANALYSIS

A. Prevailing Party

Here, Acosta argues that Plaintiffs (Acostas) are entitled to attorney’s fees. He urges “Acosta is the prevailing party in this matter as he obtained a judgment in his favor for the petition to remove Alpine Plastering’s mechanic’s lien. Moreover, Acosta is also the prevailing party as to the First Amended Cross-Complaint as the Court ordered it stricken on October 30, 2023.” (Motion p. 5: 18-21.) Defendant’s default was entered by the Court on January 10, 2024. (1/10/24 Minute Order.) The cross-complaint was stricken in its entirety on October 30, 2023. (10/30/23 Minute Order.) As the complaint concerned the release of a mechanic’s lien and default judgment was entered against Defendant and in favor of the Acostas, Plaintiffs are entitled to their attorney fees.

As Plaintiffs are the prevailing party in this action, they are the prevailing party in terms of CCP 1717. As such, they are entitled to attorney fees.

 

B. Reasonableness of Fees

The analysis of reasonableness of attorney fee awards considers “the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required and skills employed in handling the litigation, the attention given, the success of the attorney’s efforts, his learning, his age, and his experience in the particular type of work demanded…” (Berry v. Chaplin (1946) 74 Cal.App.2d 669, 678-679, citations omitted.) There is no requirement that billing records be provided to a trial court in connection with a motion for fees pursuant to contract. The declaration of counsel as to the hours spent on this litigation and the hourly rates charged for such time is all that is required. “[T]here is no legal requirement that an attorney supply billing statements to support a claim for attorney fees.” (Mardirossian & Associates, Inc. v. Ersoff (2007) 135 Cal.App.4th 257, 269.) “[T]here is no legal requirement that such statements be offered in evidence. An attorney’s testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records.” (Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, 293.)

Here, the declaration of Plaintiffs’ counsel, Ton Nu, provides a sufficient basis for the fee request. (Nu Declaration.) Nu provides backgrounds on the attorneys who billed on this case, Jillian Reyes, and himself. (Nu Decl. ¶¶ 6-7.) Attached to the motion are the billing records. (Exh. 3).

          The Court has examined the evidence presented by Plaintiffs and makes the following findings: the rates requested are reasonable and 615.6 hours is a reasonable amount of time spent, given the length of this case (5 years). As such, the Court GRANTS the motion for attorney fees in the amount of $30,037.00. The Court also grants the request for $6,628.64 in costs. (See Nu Decl. ¶ 12.)

           The Court awards $30,037.00 in attorney fees and $6,628.64 in costs, for a total of $36,665.64.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Plaintiff Gilbert Acosta’s motion for attorney fees is granted.

Plaintiffs’ counsel to give notice of this order.

Attorney fees and costs of $36,665.64 are awarded.

On the Court's own motion, the June 3, 2024 hearing date is vacated and Plaintiff's motions to compel are continued to June 18, 2024, which is the date of the case management conference. Plaintiff has filed three motions to compel initial discovery, one motion as to Defendant Julia Zhu, one as to Defendant UCAR LA Inc. and one as to Defendant Fantastic Garden LLC.  However, each motion improperly seeks to compel different types of discovery in a singular motion. This circumvents paying proper filing fees and is not allowed. (CCP § 1003, GC § 70617.)  (See also Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, [8:1140.1] at 8F-60 (The Rutter Group 2011) ["Motions to compel compliance with separate discovery requests ordinarily should be filed separately."]) Plaintiff is advised to avoid this practice in the future.

Plaintiff must pay the filing fees for 6 additional motions and show proof of such payment at the time of hearing on these motions.  However, addressing the merits, had these motions been properly brought (the Court also notes that as of May 30, 2024 at 11:30 AM, they are unopposed), the Court likely would have granted the motions in their entirety and imposed sanctions.

Therefore, the Court orders the parties to meet and confer and resolve these outstanding discovery matters prior to the case management conference.  The Court reminds the parties that civil discovery is intended to be a self-executing system with minimal judicial intervention.

The clerk is to give notice.
