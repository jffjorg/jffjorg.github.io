I.            INTRODUCTION

On June 14, 2019, plaintiffs Elva Zaldivar, Stephanie Zaldivar, and Marina Zaldivar (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), individually and as successor in interest to Jose Zaldivar (“Zaldivar”), filed this wrongful death action against defendants Steve Lee and Tracy Lee for negligence (under Labor Code section 3706) and premises liability.  

On December 6, 2021, Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).  Plaintiffs substituted Sean Lee (“Defendant”) for Doe 3 on December 7, 2021.

This action arises from the death of Zaldivar while performing improvements at a frozen desserts factory owned by non-party Sweety Novelty, Inc. (“Sweety”) on March 4, 2018.  The factory sits on property that Steve Lee and Tracy Lee own and lease to Sweety.

On May 24, 2022, the Honorable Colin Leis granted Steve Lee and Tracy Lee’s motion for summary adjudication as to the first cause of action for negligence under Labor Code section 3706 and denied their motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication as to the second and third causes of action.  Their renewed motion for summary judgment/adjudication is scheduled for hearing on December 5, 2023.

On July 28, 2023, Plaintiffs filed this motion, styled as a motion to compel further discovery responses and document production with respect to their Request for Production of Documents (Set One) propounded on Defendant.  In the notice of motion, Plaintiffs state they move for an order compelling Defendant to provide further responses to their Requests for Production Nos. 8, 11, 12, and 13, as well as the production of documents.  Plaintiffs specify that their request for documents includes “[Defendant’s] cellular phone(s) and any electronically stored information (ESI) including electronic data backup storage (such as an iCloud account) of the aforementioned cell phone for forensic examination and extraction/download by third-party forensic examiner ArcherHall Digitial Forensics.”  

Defendant filed an opposition brief on September 12, 2023.

Plaintiffs filed a reply brief on September 18, 2023.

II.          DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs allege that Defendant, along with Steve Lee and Tracy Lee, operate and own Sweety, and Zaldivar died while working on property improvements for an upcoming Safe Quality Food (“SQF”) inspection. (Memo., p. 1.)  Zaldivar was delegated to work on a ceiling project that required him to be hoisted in a defective cage/basket while it was on a forklift and that he fell and suffered fatal injuries to his head.  Plaintiffs further allege that Defendant, Sweety’s General Manager, retained an unlicensed contractor, John Woods (“Woods”), and authorized Woods to hire workers.  Defendant denies that he was involved with the project that led to Zaldivar’s death. 

The discovery requests at issue in this motion seek text messages between Defendant and several non-party individuals, including Woods, Stacey Lee, Patty Lee, and Gloria Knowles, from January 1, 2017, to December 31, 2018:

-      John Woods: According to Defendant’s deposition testimony, Woods was an independent contractor who worked for Sweety and would perform general maintenance services on its utilities and equipment; Woods also testified that Defendant was his supervisor from 2015/2016 to the present.  Defendant further testified that he discussed changes and improvements that would need to be made to the Sweety facility located on the property in order to obtain certain certifications, including repair to the ceiling.  Woods, in turn, hired Zaldivar.

-      Stacey Lee: In March 2018, Stacey Lee was head of the legal department of Jessie Lord’s Bakery.

-      Patty Lee: In March 2018, Patty Lee was the sales manager of Sweety and had been employed by Sweety since 2005.  She testified at her deposition that she did not have any discussions with Defendant regarding Woods’ work at Sweety.

-      Gloria Knowles: Gloria Knowles identified Defendant as her boss and the person in charge of managing Sweety. She stated that she had discussions with Defendant about handling the workers’ compensation claim for Zaldivar and had contacted OSHA.  Defendant instructed her to assist in the investigation of Zaldivar’s death.

Defendant initially served objection-only responses on January 27, 2023, and provided supplemental responses on February 10, 2023; Defendant stated he was unable to comply because the requested documents “do not exist.” On May 30, 2023, Defendant served additional supplemental responses and produced screenshots of text messages between him and Woods from March 18, 2017, to December 26, 2018.  Defendant asserted that within the relevant time period, he did not exchange any text messages with Stacey Lee.  Defendant also produced some screenshots of text messages between him and both Patty Lee and Gloria Knowles.  Unsatisfied with this production, on June 13 and June 28, 2023, Plaintiffs suggested that a third-party vendor extract text messages from a specific date range pursuant to specific search terms and recipients, and provided a proposal identifying the investigator and the protocol that would be followed. Defendant did not agree to a forensic extraction. 

          Thus, Plaintiffs move for an order compelling Defendant to produce his cell phones so that they can determine if Defendant is withholding text messages.  The requests for production sought “DOCUMENTS” which is defined as “(1) any and all ‘writings’ as defined in Evidence Code § 250; (2) all written, printed, typed, recorded, or graphic matter or [sic] any type or description, including, but not limited to, computer disks, CD-ROMS, CD-Rs, CD-RWs, writings, notes, drawings, graphs, charts, medical records of any nature, photographs, phonographic records or tapes, videotapes, computer records, cards, and tapes, and other data compilations from which information can be obtained, translated if necessary through detection devices into reasonable usable form, or tangible things, from which information can be obtained, and all non-identical copies, versions, amendments, modifications, drafts therefore; (3) any and all ESI [electronically stored information]of ANY DOCUMENT sought within the request.”  (Motion, Ex. A, pp.1-2.)

          Plaintiffs take issue with Defendant’s production and contend that it is deficient because there are significant gaps of time between the messages and only a limited number of text messages was produced.  Plaintiffs highlight the fact that Woods and Lee communicated via text message about Sweety at least every three to six days from January to February, but the last message sent before Zaldivar’s incident (which occurred on March 4, 2018) was sent on February 26, 2018.  (Motion, p. 9.)  Text messages between Defendant and Woods did not resume until March 15, 2018, 10 days after Zaldivar was injured; Defendant and Woods would communicate via text message at least every 4 days throughout March and April 2018.  

Similarly, Plaintiffs point out that Gloria Knowles and Defendant communicated by text message between April 13 to April 25, 2018, but not at any point between April 25, 2018, to December 31, 2018, which is a period of 8 months.  Plaintiffs ascribe malicious motives and state: “It is difficult to understand why Gloria Knowles and Sean Lee would have restricted their use of text messages to such a narrow period of time when Knowles testified that Sean Lee was her boss, that she informed him of the subject OSHA investigation, that he instructed her to assist with the aforementioned investigation, that she handled the workers’ compensation claim related to Decedent, and that she was involved in training Sweety employees at the Sweety Facility that Sean Lee managed and operated.” 

In addition, Plaintiffs note that Patty Lee and Defendant did not exchange any text messages between November 23, 2017, and March 12, 2018, and the produced messages only reference a single check to Woods over a period of 2 years.  Plaintiffs state that the discovery of additional payments by Patty Lee to Woods would uncover additional, undisclosed work by Woods at the Sweety facility, which would be relevant to how much knowledge Defendant had about Woods’ practices and standards and whether they were negligent prior to Zaldivar’s incident.

In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ motion for a forensic examination is premature because no inspection demand has been served for his cell phone.  The Court agrees.  Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.030 requires a party to specify the designate the documents, tangible things, or electronically stored information to be inspected, copied, tested, or sampled; a party is also required to specify the inspection, copying, testing, sampling, or other activity being demanded and the manner in which that activity will be performed.  Extracting data from a cell phone and “electronic data backup storage” does not appear in Plaintiffs’ requests for production.  Although the Code of Civil Procedure allows a party to compel, prevent, or limit the production, inspection, copying, testing, or sampling or electronically stored information to the same extent as physical documents or tangible things, Plaintiffs’ requests for production identify “all DOCUMENTS that refer or relate to any and all text messages” between Defendant and specific individuals.  Plaintiffs’ definition of “DOCUMENT” is impressively broad, but even a generous interpretation of their definition does not include a forensic examination of a cell phone. 

The Court notes that defense counsel, Jacquelene A. Robinson (Marcott), did not describe any method to ensure that their client had produced all responsive documents, nor does Defendant submit a declaration describing his efforts to produce all responsive documents.  Instead, Ms. Marcott’s declaration essentially speculates that the production is complete by stating “on information and belief” that the production reflects all of the responsive communications.  (Robinson Decl., ¶ 2.)  Therefore, even though the Court denies the motion for an order allowing a forensic examination at this time, the  Court notes that its ruling on this particular motion does not preclude Plaintiffs from serving a formal inspection demand for Defendant’s cell phone (or cell phones) in the future. 

Furthermore, even though the Court will not permit Plaintiffs to undertake a forensic examination of Defendant’s cell phone (or cell phones) at this time, the Court acknowledges Plaintiffs’ frustration with the possibility that there may exist text messages which have not yet been produced.  This frustration is caused, in part, by Defendant’s incomplete supplemental responses.  Defendant’s responses are deficient because he does not specify whether his compliance is “either in whole or in part” as required by Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.220.  If the statement of compliance was intended to only show compliance “in part”, Defendant fails to justify any of the objections on which he stands. 

For example, the Court queries whether call logs for the time period of January 1, 2017 to December 31, 2018, have been produced because these logs would reflect any history of text messages between Defendant and other individuals.  If they have not been produced, they should be produced because they are responsive to the requests for production at issue; any privilege issues can be addressed with the appropriate redactions and a privilege log.  Defendant did not object to Request for Production Nos. 8, 11, 12, or 13 on the grounds that they invaded his privacy or the privacy of third parties and those objections have been waived.   

Ultimately, Plaintiffs are entitled to a sworn statement from Defendant stating that he has produced all existing documents which are responsive to each request for production or to specify the basis for withholding any documents or information within documents.  Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ motion is GRANTED with respect to their request for further responses to Request for Production Nos. 8, 12, and 13. 

III.        CONCLUSION

Plaintiffs’ motion is GRANTED in part and Defendant is ordered to provide further verified, Code-compliant supplemental responses to Request for Production Nos. 8, 12, and 13 within 10 days of the date of this order.
