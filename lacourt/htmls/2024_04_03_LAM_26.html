DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 22STCV06775    Hearing Date: April 3, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

 

KEOINA FORD,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

GENERAL MOTORS LLC, ET AL.

 

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:  22STCV06775 

 Hearing Date: April 3, 2024 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

(1) PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND

 

(2) DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION 

 

 

Background

            On November 4, 2020, Plaintiff purchased a 2019 Chevrolet Impala, VIN # 1G11Z5SA2KU123817.  Plaintiff alleges the vehicle was delivered to Plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities and developed other serious defects and nonconformities.  Plaintiff delivered the vehicle to Defendant for repair on the nonconformities.  Plaintiff alleges  Defendant wrongfully denied warranty coverage for certain nonconformities and was unable to conform the vehicle to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of attempts.  Despite being unable to conform the vehicle to the express warranties after a reasonable amount of time as set forth in the Song Beverly Act (“SBA”), Defendant allegedly failed to either promptly replace the new motor vehicle or promptly make restitution under the SBA. 

            On February 24, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant General Motors, LLC (“GM”) as the manufacturer of the vehicle alleging (1) SBA—Breach of Express Warranty; (2) SBA—Breach of Implied Warranty; and (3) SBA-Civil Code §1793.2.

            On February 14, 2023, GM filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative Summary Adjudication.  On September 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Opposition to GM’s Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication.  On September 20, 2023, GM filed a Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition.  On September 20, 2023, Plaintiff also filed a Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint. 

            On September 27, 2023, GM’s Motion For Summary Judgment came on for hearing, as well as Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application to Advance the Hearing Date on Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Amend.  The Court granted the Ex Parte in part, setting Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File FAC for December 7, 2023.  The Court continued GM’s Motion for Summary Judgment to April 3, 2024. 

            On January 2, 2024, the Court continued the Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File FAC to April 3, 2024 to be heard with GM’s Motion for Summary Judgment.  Parties were instructed to brief the applicability of the California Supreme Court’s opinion reviewing Rodriguez v. FCA US, LLC (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 209 (“Rodriguez”).

GM’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT, OR IN THE ALTERNATIVE, SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff’s RJN of the California Supreme Court’s grant of review of Rodriguez is GRANTED.

Objections to Evidence

Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections Nos. 1-12 are OVERRULED. 

Legal Standard

            Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Code of Civil Procedure §437c(c).) From commencement to conclusion, the party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Company (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855.)

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §437c(f)(1), a party may properly seek summary adjudication of one or more causes of action, one or more affirmative defenses, the issue of punitive damages or the issue of duty.  (CCP §437c(f)(1).) “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim of damages or an issue of duty.”  (Id.)

            Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (CCP §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Id.) 

            “A party is entitled to summary judgment only if it meets its initial burden of showing there are no triable issues of fact and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. This is true even if the opposing party fails to file any opposition. The court's assessment of whether the moving party has carried its burden—and therefore caused a shift—occurs before the court's evaluation of the opposing party's papers. Therefore, the burden on the motion does not initially shift as a result of what is, or is not, contained in the opposing papers.”  (Mosley v. Pacific Specialty Insurance Company (2020) 49 Cal.App.5th 417, 434–435 (landlord’s failure to address issue of whether they were aware of their tenant’s marijuana growing operation was not grounds to grant summary judgment where moving party failed to satisfy its initial burden as to the issue); Thatcher v. Lucky Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1086-1087 (court cannot grant summary judgment based merely on lack of opposition; court must first determine if the moving party has satisfied its burden).)

            In addition, the evidence and affidavits of the moving party are construed strictly, while those of the opponent are liberally read.  (Government Employees Ins. Co. v. Sup. Ct. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 95, 100. “All doubts as to the propriety of granting the motion (whether there is any issue of material fact [Code of Civil Procedure] § 437c) are to be resolved in favor of the party opposing the motion (i.e., a denial of summary judgment).” (Hamburg v. Wal-Mart Stores, Inc. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 497, 502.)

Discussion

I.  Parties’ Positions

            GM’s Motion for Summary Judgment

            GM argues Plaintiff’s second cause of action for breach of implied warranty fails for three reasons.  GM argues (1) under California law, Plaintiff cannot maintain an action for breach of implied warranty because the vehicle was purchased used; and (2) Plaintiff cannot demonstrate any damages from the alleged breach, i.e. the difference between the value of the goods at the time and place of acceptance and the value of the goods had they been warranted.  GM argue Plaintiff has failed to identify any damages in discovery.

            GM argues Plaintiff’s first and third causes of action for breach of express warranty fails, because she did not buy a “new motor vehicle” and she did not receive any new or additional warranty coverage from GM.  GM relies on Rodriguez, which it argues is directly on point. 

            GM argues that, if any of the causes of action were to withstand summary judgment, it asks that the Court grant adjudication on specific items of damage.  GM argues it has no obligation to reimburse Plaintiff for the optional, non-GM products she bought with the vehicle as a matter of law.

            Plaintiff’s Opposition

            Plaintiff argues GM failed to submit admissible evidence that she purchased the vehicle as a “used” vehicle.  Plaintiff argues none of the documentary exhibits are properly authenticated, because GM’s counsel lacks personal knowledge to authenticate documents like the Retail Installment Contract. 

            Plaintiff argues Rodriguez is not binding authority.  Plaintiff argues the Court should follow Jensen v. North America, Inc. (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 112 (“Jensen”) instead.  Plaintiff argues Jensen expressly found that vehicles sold with a balance remaining on the manufacturer's new motor vehicle warranty are included in the SBA definition of “new motor vehicle.” 

            Plaintiff argues GM’s new car warranty is also specifically extended to all subsequent owners.  Plaintiff argues a trier of fact could determine that the vehicle was sold with a “new” car warranty.  Plaintiff argues the jury must determine whether the vehicle qualifies for relief under the SBA.

            GM’s Reply

            GM argues Plaintiff did not dispute any of its material facts with evidence.  GM argues Plaintiff merely responded to the separate statement with evidentiary objections.  GM argues it has satisfied its burden on summary judgment with admissible evidence.

            GM argues that based on the undisputed evidence, Plaintiff purchased her vehicle used.  GM argues Plaintiff therefore cannot sue for breach of implied warranty based on Nunez or breach of express warranty under the SBA based on Rodriguez. 

II.  Based on the undisputed facts, Defendant is entitled to summary judgment on Plaintiff’s Complaint

            GM establishes that the vehicle was sold used to Plaintiff.  Plaintiff produced a copy of the Retail Installment Sales Contract in response to discovery.  (Yaragachian Dec., ¶5, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff therefore admitted that the RISC attached as Exhibit A is the RISC she signed to purchase the vehicle and cannot object to it based on lack of authentication.  Based on Exhibit A, the vehicle sold was clearly “used,” as the RISC identifies it as such, and the odometer had 34,179 miles on it at the time of sale.  (Id.)

            In response, Plaintiff does not deny that the vehicle was “used” when sold.  Plaintiff’s only objection is that no one with personal knowledge authenticated the RISC.  However, Plaintiff verified in response to discovery that Exhibit A was the RISC.  Ford does not deny that the RISC attached as Exhibit A is the RISC pursuant to which she purchased the vehicle.  The VIN number and contract date also match the allegations regarding purchase in Plaintiff’s complaint.  (Complaint, ¶7.)  As such, Plaintiff fails to raise any issue of fact as to whether the vehicle was purchase as a “used” vehicle.

            Second cause of action for breach of implied warranty—GRANT

            Because Plaintiff purchased her vehicle used, GM as the manufacturer cannot be held liable for breach of implied warranty under the SBA.  (Nunez v. FCA US LLC (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 385, 398.)  “In this case, plaintiff's implied warranty claim fails as a matter of law, because in the sale of used consumer goods, liability for breach of implied warranty lies with distributors and retailers, not the manufacturer, where there is no evidence the manufacturer played any role in the sale of the used car to plaintiff.”  (Id.)

            “It is evident from these provisions that only distributors or sellers of used goods—not manufacturers of new goods—have implied warranty obligations in the sale of used goods. (See § 1795.5.) As one court has put it, the Song-Beverly Act provides similar remedies (to those available when a manufacturer sells new consumer goods) ‘in the context of the sale of used goods, except that the manufacturer is generally off the hook.’”  (Id. at 399.)  Plaintiff offers no evidence that GM’s role in the relevant events was as anything other than the manufacturer.  (Id.)  As such, Plaintiff’s second cause of action for breach of implied warranty under the SBA fails as a matter of law.  A manufacturer whose sole role in a vehicle transaction is as a manufacturer does not have any implied warranty obligations in the sale of used goods.  (Civ. C. §1795.5; Nunez, supra, 61 Cal.App.5th at 399.)

            First and Third Causes of Action for Breach of Contract and Civil Code §1793.2—GRANT

            There is currently a split in authority on the issue of whether the SBA’s express warranty provisions apply to sale of “used” vehicles, as acknowledged by the California Supreme Court in order granting review of Rodriguez v. FCA US, LLC.  (Plaintiff’s RJN, Ex. A.)  “Pending review, the opinion of the Court of Appeal, which is currently published at 77 Cal.App.5th 209, may be cited, not only for its persuasive value, but also for the limited purpose of establishing the existence of a conflict in authority that would in turn allow trial courts to exercise discretion under Auto Equity Sales, Inc. v. Superior Court (1962) 57  Cal.2d 450, 456, to choose between sides of any such conflict…The requests for an order directing depublication of the opinion are denied.”  (Id.) 

            Applying these principles, the Court finds the reasoning of Rodriguez persuasive and finds Jensen distinguishable.  Because Plaintiff purchased the vehicle used, the SBA’s express warranty provisions do not apply and Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is granted as to the first and third causes of action for breach of express warranty and Civil Code §1792.

            The precise same situation here was presented in Rodriguez.  The plaintiffs purchased a used vehicle with a balance remaining on an express warranty from the manufacturer when it was sold to them.  (Rodriguez, supra, 77 Cal.App.5th at 219.)  The plaintiffs maintained the vehicle qualified as a “new motor vehicle” under the “a dealer-owned vehicle and a ‘demonstrator’ or other motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer’s new care warranty” prong of Civil Code §1793.22(e)(2).  (Id.) 

            The Court of Appeals agreed with the manufacturer’s position that “other motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer’s new car warranty” refers to “vehicles that…have not been previously sold and are sold with new or full warranties.”  (Id. at 219.)  “To begin with, the phrase appears in a definition of new motor vehicles. That fact alone strongly suggests the Legislature did not intend the phrase to refer to used (i.e., previously sold) vehicles.”  (Id. at 220.) 

            Rodriguez also found that the “a dealer-owned vehicle and a ‘demonstrator’ or other motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer’s new care warranty” prong of Civil Code §1793.22(e)(2) was intended to apply to a “narrow category of basically new vehicles—they have never been previously sold to a consumer and they come with full express warranties” and the “most natural interpretation of the phrase ‘other motor vehicle sold with a manufacturer’s new car warranty’ is that it, too, refers to vehicles that have never been previously sold to a consumer and come with full express warranties.”  (Rodriguez, supra, 77 Cal.App.5th at 220.)     

            Rodriguez also distinguished Jensen.  “Jensen involved a lease by a manufacturer-affiliated dealer who issued a full new car warranty along with the lease. The issue was whether the leased car qualified as a ‘new motor vehicle’ under the Act.”  (Id. at 223.)  The crucial distinction was that the car in Jensen was leased with a “full manufacturer’s warranty issued by the manufacturer’s representative.”  (Id. at 224.)  Jensen was never presented with the question raised in Rodriguez and here—“whether a used care with an unexpired warranty sold by a third party reseller qualifies as a ‘new motor vehicle.’”  (Id.)  Rodriguez noted that this was an issue of first impression and answered in the negative.  This Court agrees. 

            Plaintiff’s vehicle does not qualify as a “new motor vehicle” as defined under Civil code §1793.22 and based on the undisputed facts.  Pursuant to the reasoning of Rodriguez, GM’s motion for summary judgment of the first and third causes of action for breach of express warranty and Civil Code §1793.2 is granted. 

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE A FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

Legal Standard

            “A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike.” (CCP §472.)

            “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.”  (CCP §473(a)(1).)

            The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified:  “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend; and, where the refusal also results in a party being deprived of the right to assert a meritorious cause of action or a meritorious defense, it is not only error but an abuse of discretion.”  (Morgan v. Sup.Ct. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530 (emphasis added).)  Courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint "at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial," absent prejudice to the adverse party. (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.)

            Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence or added costs of preparation, increased burden of discovery, etc.  (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488.)  Delay alone is insufficient grounds for denial of leave of amend.  If the delay in seeking the amendment has not misled or prejudiced the other side, the liberal policy of allowing amendments prevails. Indeed, it is an abuse of discretion to deny leave in such a case even if sought as late as the time of trial.  (Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565.)

Analysis

I.  Parties’ Positions

            Plaintiff’s Position

            Plaintiff asks that the Court grant leave to file a First Amended Complaint adding a cause of action under the federal Magnuson Moss Warranty Act.  Plaintiff argues the amended complaint would be based on the exact same facts as alleged in the original complaint and will not prejudice Defendant for that reason.

            Defendant’s Position

            Defendant argues leave to amend should be denied because Plaintiff’s cannot prevail on the three causes of action alleged in the complaint regardless of whether an action under the Magnuson Moss Warranty Act can be stated.  Defendant argues that leave to amend including these three causes of action under the SBA would be futile.  Defendant argues leave to amend should also be denied because Plaintiff waited two years to seek leave and Plaintiff provides no excuse for doing so.  Defendant argues the motion for leave to amend is merely an attempt to avoid its motion for summary judgment. 

            Plaintiff’s Reply

            Plaintiff argues leave to amend should be granted liberally.  Plaintiff argues there has been no ruling on Defendant’s MSJ, and it should not be assumed that Plaintiff cannot prevail on the three SBA claims alleged in the complaint.  Plaintiff also argues a Magnuson Moss claim can be pursued independently of SBA claims.  Plaintiff argues no prejudice will result if leave to amend is granted.  Plaintiff argues leave to amend must be granted. 

II.  Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File FAC is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part

            Any claims of prejudice asserted by Defendant have been resolved by the Court’s ruling on the motion for summary judgment.  Summary judgment is granted on Plaintiff’s three claims under the SBA.  Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file an FAC that includes the three SBA claims is denied.

            However, Defendant fails to establish prejudice if leave is granted as to the proposed Magnuson Moss cause of action.  Defendant argues Plaintiff inexcusably delayed in seeking leave.  Delay alone is insufficient grounds to deny leave to amend, and Defendant does not establish any prejudice if leave is granted as to the Magnuson Moss claim, e.g. delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or increased discovery burden.[1]  If required, a trial continuance can also be requested. 

            Moreover, nearly the identical procedural situation was presented in Dagher v. Ford Motor Co. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 905.  The plaintiff in Dagher filed a motion for leave to amend in addition to an opposition to a pending defense motion for summary judgment.  The trial court heard both plaintiff’s motion for leave and the defendant’s motion for summary judgment together, and granted the motion for summary judgment and denied the motion for leave.  The Court of Appeals affirmed the order granting summary judgment but reversed the order denying leave to amend as an abuse of discretion.  (Dagher, supra, 238 Cal.App.4th at 929.) 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint is GRANTED as to the proposed Magnuson Moss claim and DENIED as to the claims under the SBA.  Plaintiff is ordered to file a FAC that reflects the following:  (1) Paragraphs 9-49 of the Proposed FAC should be omitted; (2) the “Fourth Cause of Action” should be retitled “First Cause of Action”; and (3) the caption of the FAC should only reflect a single cause of action for violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act.  Any reference to or claim under the SBA should be omitted from the FAC. 

Conclusion and ORDER

            Defendant GM’s Motion for Summary Judgment of the Complaint is GRANTED.

            Plaintiff Ford’s Motion for Leave to File FAC is GRANTED as to the claim under Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act (Fourth Cause of Action in FAC) and DENIED as to the claims under the SBA (First through Third Causes of Action in FAC).

  [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants GEOFFREY H. PALMER, GEOFFREY H. PALMER d/b/a GH PALMER ASSOCIATES, and PALMER TEMPLE STREET PROPERTIES, LLC’s motion to bifurcate

 

Procedural Background

Plaintiff Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP initiated this action against Geoffrey H. Palmer, Geoffrey H. Palmer dba GH Palmer Associates, Palmer Temple Street Properties, LLC, and Palmer Temple Street Properties (collectively “Palmer”) seeking damages Plaintiff sustained when an arson at Palmer’s large-construction site led to fire, heat, smoke, ashes, debris; damage to, inhabitability of, and closure of Palmer’s law offices; and interference with Plaintiff’s business relations.  On February 23, 2023, the instant action was transferred to the instant department.  (Minute Order 2/23/23.)

On October 9, 2023, the Court denied without prejudice the motion to bifurcate filed by Geoffrey H. Palmer, Geoffrey H. Palmer d/b/a GH Palmer Associates, and Palmer Temple Street properties, LLC (hereinafter collectively “Palmer”) on September 14, 2023. 

On March 7, 2024, Palmer filed the instant motion to bifurcate trial.  On March 20, 2024, Cross-Defendant State of California (the “State”) filed an opposition.  On March 26, 2024, Palmer filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard

“The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States.”  (CCP § 1048(b).)  Whether there shall be a severance and separate trials of issues in a single action is a matter within the trial court’s discretion.  (McArthur v. Shaffer (1943) 59 Cal.App.2d 724, 727.)

Where a separate trial is ordered on the issue of liability, the procedure is commonly referred to as “bifurcation.”  The objective of bifurcation is to avoid wasting time and money on the trial of damages issues if the liability issue is resolved against plaintiff.  (Horton v. Jones (1972) 26 Cal.App.3d 952, 954.)  Granting or denying of a motion for bifurcation lies within the trial court’s sound discretion and is subject to reversal on appeal only for clear abuse.  (Grappo v. Coventry Financial Corp. (1991) 235 Cal.App.3d 496, 503-504.) 

Discussion

            Palmer Defendants seek to bifurcate trial to have (1) Phase One be a bench trial on the issue of duty (“Phase 1”), and (2) Phase Two to be jury trial on the remaining issues (“Phase 2”).  Palmer asserts that proceeding with a jury trial without first determining the issue of duty by the court could result in undue prejudice by allowing the jury to be exposed to irrelevant or inapplicable evidence.  Palmer also states that bifurcating the trial would serve the interests of judicial economy and efficiency because it could significantly narrow the issues for the jury trial and reduce the length.  Palmer also asserts that they complied with the requirements for filing their motion to bifurcate and argues that the motion is timely and proper.  Finally, Palmer points out that the State has waived any right to oppose the current motion for bifurcation because it did not oppose their previous motion to bifurcate and did not appear at the hearing for that motion.

            In opposition, the State argues that bifurcation is unnecessary because the Court has already ruled on the issue of Palmer’s duty in its motion for summary judgment ruling.  The State also states that Palmer has not demonstrated that they would suffer prejudice without a bifurcated trial or that bifurcation would result in a shorter jury trial.  Rather, the State points out that bifurcation could lead to duplication of witness testimony since the issues pertaining to Palmer’s equitable indemnity cause of action against Caltrans are closely intertwined with its negligence cause of action.  Further, the State argues that Palmer’s motion to bifurcate is untimely and unfairly prejudicial to Caltrans, highlighting procedural deadlines and the additional burdens placed on the State to the timing of the motion.  Finally, the State refutes Palmer’s claim that it waived any opposition to the motion by not opposing a previous motion to bifurcate, emphasizing that the previous motion was denied and that circumstances have changed since then.

            In reply, Palmer clarifies that their prior motion to bifurcate was not denied on its merits but deferred by the Court to the trial judge, indicating that the issue of duty has not been decided yet, contrary to the State’s representation.  Palmer also argues that their motion to bifurcate is timely and proper, initially filed well in advance of the trial and re-filed as instructed by the Court during a pre-trial hearing.  As to the issue of duty, Palmer maintains that this is a legal issue for the Court to decide, not a jury.  They argue that this is especially true in cases involving a property owner’s duty to protect against third-party criminal acts, which requires an extraordinarily high degree of foreseeability.  Palmer also asserts that they will be prejudiced if the trial is not bifurcated because the jury would be exposed to irrelevant or prejudicial evidence if duty is not determined by the Court first.  They also contend that bifurcation will promote judicial efficiency by narrowing the issues for the jury trial, potentially reducing its length.  

The Motion is Untimely, But the Court Exercises its Discretion to Consider the Merits

            This motion was untimely filed despite Palmer’s contention of timeliness.  Trial is set for April 15, 2024, but Palmer did not file the motion until March 7, 2024, with the hearing set for April 3, 2024.  An order for bifurcation must be made no later than 30 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 598.)  Although Palmer contends that their initial motion to bifurcate filed on September 14, 2023 was continued, the Court notes that it was denied without prejudice.  Notwithstanding this procedural defect, the Court will consider the merits of Palmer’s motion given its inherent authority to separate trials at any time.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1048.)  

The State Did Not Waive Opposition to the Instant Motion

            Given that the October 9, 2023 ruling on Palmer’s motion to bifurcate was to deny without prejudice rather than continue, the Court finds that the State did not waive its right to oppose a subsequently filed motion to bifurcate by failing to oppose the first motion filed on September 14, 2023.

The Issue of Duty has Not Been Determined

            The State contends that the issue of duty has already been determined because the Court previously denied Palmer’s motion for summary adjudication motion of the issue of duty.  The Court – presided by the Honorable David Sotelo – ruled on Palmer’s motion for summary adjudication of duty, where the Court found that: “[Palmer was] aware that parking signs across the street were set on fire. The Court finds that this is a ‘prior similar incident’ to the arson that occurred at the Da Vinci Project.”  (Lynch Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. C.)  This is the same issue of duty that Palmer seeks to have determined in Phase One of the proposed bifurcated proceeding.  (See e.g., Motion at pp.6:8-12, [“[T]he State is required to make a sufficient showing that Palmer owed a duty to it to take different or additional measures to guard against a third party’s arson. The State is required to set forth facts establishing that Palmer had knowledge of prior, similar acts of arson occurring at the Da Vinci Project. As explained above, notice of prior acts is required to trigger any duty by Palmer to undertake such enhanced security measures. Indeed, prior to the incident, there were no arson attempts committed at or directed toward the Da Vinci Project.”].) 

            While this is the same issue that has been previously addressed in the denial of Palmer’s motion for summary adjudication, “[d]enial of a motion for summary judgment does not establish any fact or resolve any issue; it merely determines that the issues will be decided later, at the time of trial.”  (Emerald Bay Community Assn. v. Golden Eagle Ins. Corp. (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1086.)  Therefore, the issue of duty must still be resolved at trial. 

Palmer Fails to Show That Bifurcation Would Promote the Convenience of Witnesses, Ends of Justice, or the Economy and Efficiency of Handling the Litigation

The Court finds that Palmer has failed to make a sufficient showing of the prejudice they would suffer without a bifurcated trial.  Palmer asserts that the jury would improperly hear testimony and consider evidence such as melted parking signs, which they claim is not related to the trespass from the freeway onto the project and intentional arson on December 8, 2014.  Palmer fails to sufficiently explain that such evidence would result in irreversible prejudice to Palmer.  The Court recognizes that bifurcation may result in a shorter jury trial, but this argument also is not sufficiently supported with evidence.  It is unclear for example which witnesses or how many would not be required for Phase 2.  Thus, there is a strong possibility that bifurcation would result in the duplication of testimony and would not promote the convenience, ends of the justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation.  

The State points out that regardless of whether the issue of Palmer’s duty is bifurcated, Palmer will have to present evidence, including witness testimony, that there either were, or were not, similar acts or circumstances that either did or did not make the Caltrans ROW fence and the Da Vinci Apartment construction site inherently dangerous.  Thus, the interests of witness economy and judicial efficiency weigh in favor of denying Palmer’s motion. 

CONCLUSIONS AND ORDER

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Geoffrey H. Palmer, Geoffrey H. Palmer d/b/a GH Palmer Associates, and Palmer Temple Street properties, LLC’s motion to bifurcate trial is DENIED.
