DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV01149    Hearing Date: June 28, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

June 11, 2024 – Continued to June 28, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23SMCV01149

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff David Kermani, as Trustee as the David Kermani Trust

OPPOSING PARTY

none  

 

MOVING PAPERS:

 

  1. Notice of Motion and Motion for Summary Judgment/Summary Adjudication; Memorandum of Points and Authorities
  2. Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts
  3. Appendix of Exhibits

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 15, 2023, Plaintiff David Kermani, as Trustee of the David Kermani Trust (“Plaintiff”) brought suit alleging two causes of action for (1) breach of written lease and (2) breach of written guaranty of lease against Defendants Gold Coast Crave, LLC and Cory Rooney aka Mark C. Rooney (“Defendants”).  Plaintiff now moves for summary judgment, or in the alternative summary adjudication, on the following issues:

 

(1) Issue No. 1: Plaintiff is entitled to summary adjudication in its favor on Plaintiff's First Cause of Action for Breach of Written Lease against Gold Coast; and

 

(2) Issue No. 2: Plaintiff is entitled to summary adjudication in its favor on Plaintiff's Second Cause of Action for Breach of Written Guaranty of Lease against Guarantor.

 

            Plaintiff’s motion is unopposed.

 

LEGAL STANDARDS – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT/ADJUDICATION

 

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.” ¿(Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850 (hereafter Aguilar).) ¿“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.” ¿(Ibid.; Smith v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 1463, 1474 [summary judgment standards held by Aguilar apply to summary adjudication motions].) 

 

Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.  Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court's evaluation of credibility.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th. at p. 840 [cleaned up]; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

 

A party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty if that party contends that there is no merit to the cause of action, defense, or claim for damages, or if the party contends that there is no duty owed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).)  “A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of a cause of action, an affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.”  (Ibid.)  A cause of action has no merit if: (1) one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded, or (2) a defendant establishes an affirmative defense to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (n); Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 583.)  Once the defendant has shown that a cause of action has no merit, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o)(2); Union Bank v. Superior Court, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 583.)  Additionally, in line with Aguilar, “[o]n a motion for summary adjudication, the trial court has no discretion to exercise.  If a triable issue of material fact exists as to the challenged causes of action, the motion must be denied. If there is no triable issue of fact, the motion must be granted.”  (Fisherman's Wharf Bay Cruise Corp. v. Superior Court (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 309, 320.) 

 

DISCUSSION

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.”  (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) 

 

Similarly, “A lender is entitled to judgment on a breach of guaranty claim based upon undisputed evidence that (1) there is a valid guaranty, (2) the borrower has defaulted, and (3) the guarantor failed to perform under the guaranty.”  (Gray1 CPB, LLC v. Kolokotronis (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 480, 486.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has provided the Declaration of David Kermani, which indicates as follows:

 

3. On or about August 6, 2020, Plaintiff, as landlord, and defendant Gold Coast Crave, LLC, a New York limited liability company ("Tenant" or "Gold Coast"), as tenant, entered into that certain written Commercial Lease Agreement (the "Original Lease") and that certain First Amendment to the Lease and Landlord's Consent (the "First Amendment" and together with the Original Lease, the "Lease" or "Commercial Lease") concerning the Premises. The term of the Lease was for five (5) years, commencing on September 1, 2020 and ending on August 31, 2025. The Lease provided for the payment of monthly base rent, pursuant to paragraph 3.A.(4) and Exhibit A to the Lease ("Base Rent") of:

 

(i) $6,500.00 per month from September 1, 2020 to August 31, 2021;

 

(ii) $9,200.00 per month from September 1, 2021 to August 31, 2022;

 

(iii) $9,476.00 per month from September 1, 2022 to August 31, 2023;

 

(iv) $9,760.28 per month from September 1, 2023 to August 31, 2024; and

 

(v) $10,053.08 per month from September 1, 2024 to August 31, 2025.

 

In addition to Base Rent, Tenant agreed to pay, as additional rent, Tenant's share of property taxes and property insurance, pursuant to Paragraph 14 of the Lease. Tenant also paid a security deposit under the Lease in the sum of $9,200.00 (the "Security Deposit"). Attached to the concurrently filed Appendix of Evidence ("Appendix") as Exhibit "2" is a true and correct copy of the referenced Commercial Lease, which is incorporated by reference herein.  

 

4. Concurrently with the execution of the Lease, defendant Cory Rooney, an individual, aka Mark C. Rooney ("Guarantor") executed a Guaranty of Lease (the "Guaranty"), which provides that Guarantor "jointly, severally, unconditionally and irrevocably guarantee[s] the prompt payment by Tenant of all rents and all other sums payable by Tenant s under said Lease and the faithful and prompt performance by Tenant of each and every one of the terms, conditions and covenants of said Lease to be kept and performed by Tenant." Attached to the Appendix as Exhibit "3" is a true and correct copy of the Guaranty, which is incorporated by reference herein. Tenant and Guarantors are collectively, "Defendants".

 

5. I was directly involved in negotiations concerning the Lease and the Guaranty, including the signing of same. I was also directly involved in personally collecting rent checks from Tenant. I also have personal knowledge of leasing commissions, advertising costs, property taxes, and prope1iy insurance expenses relating to the Premises. I also have personal knowledge of all rent payments by Tenant and all defaults by Tenant in the payment of rent under the Lease. I also have personal knowledge of the New Lease, defined in paragraph 7 of this declaration.

 

6. Pursuant to the terms of the Lease, Tenant entered into possession of the Premises on or about September 1, 2020, but Defendants have not performed under the Lease and Guaranty, respectively, by failing to pay Plaintiff certain Base Rent under the Lease.

 

7. Furthermore, in violation of, without limitation, paragraph 25 of the Lease, on or about January 11, 2023 (the "Abandonment Date"), Tenant abandoned the Premises and surrendered the keys to Plaintiff. Thereafter, Plaintiff diligently pursued mitigating its damages and secured a new lease (the "New Lease") concerning the Premises with a new lessee. The term of the New Lease is from July 15, 2023 through July 15, 2025. The new lessee began paying base rent only on July 15, 2023 at the rate of $5,000.00 per month through July 14, 2024, and the new lessee is obligated under the New Lease to pay base rent only at the rate of $5,500.00 per month for the period from July 15, 2024 through July 15, 2025.

 

8. Gold Coast under the Lease and Guarantor under the Guaranty remain jointly and severally liable for rental damages which were not mitigated by the New Lease, and which are available under, without limitation, paragraph 25 of the Lease and California Civil Code Section 1951.2 on account of the termination of the Lease due to Gold Coast's failure to perform under the Lease by its non-payment of rent and its abandonment of the Premises.

In view of Tenant's failure to perform under the Lease, and pursuant to paragraphs 3.A.(4), 25 and rent schedule in Exhibit "A" to the First Amendment, there remains the gross amount of rental damages owing to Plaintiff, as a result of Gold Coast's failure to perform under the Lease, in an amount of $340,372.32 for the period from December 1, 2021 through August 31, 2025 (the "Period"), comprised of:

(xi) Application of the Security Deposit under the Lease in the sum of $9,200.00 for the month of December 2021.

(Kermani Decl. ¶¶ 3-10.) 

 

Attached as Exhibit 2 is a fully executed copy of the lease agreement, which matches the description in Kermani’s declaration, except that the lease agreement indicates Defendant was to pay a “Security Deposit” in the amount of $9,200 plus an “Additional Security Deposit” in the amount of $9,200 due on January 1, 2021, whereas the Kermani declaration addresses only a single security deposit in the amount of $9,200.  Attached as Exhibit 3 is a copy of the Guaranty, signed by “Mark Cory Rooney” as described in the Kermani Declaration.

 

While Plaintiff’s evidence demonstrates that Plaintiff has incurred damages, before the Court can enter a specific monetary judgment in favor of Plaintiff, Plaintiff must provide a supplemental declaration addressing the “Additional Security Deposit” of $9,200 (i.e. whether Defendant actually paid it and whether an additional $9,200 should be deducted from the amount Plaintiff claims.)  Further, if the additional $9,200 should be deducted from the judgment, the proposed judgment should so reflect.

 

Notwithstanding the inconsistency regarding the “Additional Security Deposit” in the amount of $9,200, Plaintiff has demonstrated the elements of both claims are satisfied.  Namely, Plaintiff has demonstrated (1) the existence of the lease agreement (Kermani Decl. ¶ 3 and Ex. 2); (2) Plaintiff’s performance (Kermani Decl. ¶ 6); (3) Defendant’s breach (Ibid.); and (4) Plaintiff’s damages (Kermani Decl. ¶¶ 7, 9-10.)  Further, Plaintiff has demonstrated (1) the existence of a valid guaranty (Kermani Decl. ¶ 4 and Ex. 3); (2) Defendant’s default (Kermani Decl. ¶¶ 6, 8); and (3) Defendant’s failure to perform under the guaranty (Ibid.)

 

Moreover, because default has been entered against Defendant Gold Coast, the proper procedural vehicle to obtain a judgment against it is to request a default judgment.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 585.)  Summary judgment is not a “default judgment.”  (English v. IKON Business Solutions, Inc. (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 137-138.)  Importantly, Code of Civil Procedure, section 473, subdivision (b), which outlines the procedure for a party to request relief from a default judgment, does not apply to summary judgments.  (Ibid.)  Thus, granting Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment as to Defendants Gold Coast would deprive it of its rights and remedies as a defaulted defendant.  Thus, the Court will deny the motion regarding Defendant Gold Coast.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

With respect to Defendant Gold Coast Crave, LLC, Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment or in the alternative motion for summary adjudication is denied as procedurally defective. 

 

Concerning Defendant Cory Rooney, the Court continues the hearing on the motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative motion for summary adjudication, to August 1, 2024 at 8:30 A.M. in Department 207.  Plaintiff shall file and serve on or before July 19, 2024 supplemental papers addressing the issue noted above.

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.
