DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV00093    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

TIMOTHY COLOMEY

      

       v.

 

CITY OF LOS ANGELES

                                           

 Case No.:  21STCV00093

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  July 1, 2024

 

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s psychological evaluation is GRANTED.

 

On 1/4/2021, Plaintiff Timothy Colomey (Plaintiff) filed suit against the City of Los Angeles (Defendant), alleging whistleblower retaliation.

 

            Now, Defendant moves to compel the independent medical examination of Plaintiff.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant seeks to compel the independent medical examination of Plaintiff by Dr. David K. Wellisch, on the grounds that Plaintiff has put his mental condition in controversy.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues that this motion is an improper motion for reconsideration and cannot be considered because discovery is closed. Plaintiff also argues the motion should be denied on the merits.

 

            As to the first contention, the Court denied the ex parte because there was not sufficient time before the impending trial to conduct the examination. When discussing the ex parte at the Final Status Conference on May 2, 2024, the Court stated that it likely “ruled because of the trial date” and the limited time before trial made it “real easy to deny the ex parte.” The Court continued, “now that we have a little bit of breathing room, I don’t mind hearing [the] motion . . . I can actually hear it more on the merits, not on a hard-line rule, you’re way too late.” Further, the Court stated that it would advance the hearing to ensure sufficient time for the examination if the Court grants the motion to compel.

 

            As such, the Court disagrees that this motion has already been considered on the merits, or that Defendant is barred from bringing this motion.

 

The trial court has wide discretion and ability to control the proceedings before it, including the right to allow discovery at different times of a case. The authority is memorialized in Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050.

 

            Here, Plaintiff underwent an investigation of mental and emotional distress, psychological and psychiatric diagnoses, and mental health treatment on 1/8/2024. Plaintiff did not provide any notice of this examination until April 2024. Despite Plaintiff’s position is that fact discovery ended on 8/28/2023, Plaintiff scheduled his mental health expert examination four months after 8/28/2023, and the City only discovered the existence of the examination another four months thereafter. The need for the City’s mental health expert to examine Plaintiff became concrete in April 2024, and thus the timing of the City’s filing was not the result of undue delay.

 

            Plaintiff has not identified any meaningful prejudice that would result if the examination were to go forward. By contrast, Defendant would suffer prejudice if denied the opportunity to psychologically exam Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges substantial mental and emotional damage and suffering, both past and ongoing. (Cianci Decl. ¶¶3, 5, Exhs. 1, 3.) He states that he has received medical treatment and medication for these afflictions. (Cianci Decl. ¶¶7-8, Exhs. 5-6.) Further, Plaintiff believes his afflictions merit examination and designated a mental health expert who will base a large part of his expert testimony about Plaintiff’s emotional and mental issues on his January 2024 examination of Plaintiff. (Cianci Decl. ¶¶14-15.)

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s psychological evaluation is granted.

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  July    , 2024

Case Number: 23STCV15836    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

JILL BELASCO

 

         vs.

 

FOUNTAIN OF WELLBEING, INC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV15836

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  July 1, 2024

 

Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED IN PART, SUSTAINED IN PART. Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED as to the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action. Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED, WITH 15 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND, as to the eighth cause of action.

 

On 7/6/2023, Plaintiff Jill Belasco (Plaintiff) filed suit against Fountain of Wellbeing, Inc., Romie Chaudhari, and Unite4, LLC. On 3/28/2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (FAC) alleging: (1) failure to pay wages; (2) waiting time penalties; (3) failure to reimburse business expenses; (4) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200; (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress (IIED); (6) defamation; (7) declaratory relief; and (8) sexual harassment.

 

            On 4/30/2024, Defendants Fountain of Wellbeing, Inc. and Romie Chaudhari (collectively, Defendants) demurred to the fifth, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to state a claim as to the fifth, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action. Given that the fifth cause of action relies on allegations set forth in the sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action, the Court addresses that cause of action last. 

 

            As for the sixth cause of action for defamation, Defendants argue that this claim fails under a theory of vicarious liability or alter-ego. However, an employer can be held vicariously liable for defamatory statements made by an employee. (Agarwal v. Johnson (1979) 25 Cal.3d 932, 950.) Tellingly, Defendant does not cite any legal authority to show otherwise. (“This failure to cite pertinent legal authority is enough reason to reject the argument”); Akins v. State of California (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1, 50, 71 (contention waived by failure to cite legal authority). Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Chaudhari, acting as CEO of the company she was employed by, made defamatory statements that implicated her professional reputation. As such, Plaintiff has alleged facts which could show that Defendant Chaudhari engaged in defamatory conduct while acting within the scope of his employment, and thus has alleged facts which could show a basis of Defendant Fountain of Wellbeing’s vicarious liability.

 

            Defendants did not advance any substantive argument that Plaintiff’s substantive allegations with respect to defamation were insufficient.

 

            As for the seventh cause of action for declaratory relief, Defendant argues that “Belasco does not adequately allege the rights and duties at issue and does not state which legal theory under which she is seeking declaratory relief as required by California law.” (Demurrer, 10: 13-15.) However, Plaintiff clearly alleges that:

 

An actual controversy has arisen and now exists between Plaintiff and Defendants. Plaintiff contends that Defendant Chaudhari defaulted under the terms of the Stock Transfer Agreement by not paying the $100 consideration when due. Subsequently, the parties entered into a new agreement regarding the transfer of Plaintiff’s shares in FWB in exchange for giving Plaintiff value in the acquiring company upon closing of the sale of FWB to Buyer and Defendant Chaudhari's assumption of all liability the Cannabis Taxes.

 

…

 

Plaintiff is requesting a declaration by the court that Defendant Chaudhari defaulted on the Stock Transfer Agreement and the subsequent agreement, and therefore, Plaintiff remains the beneficial owner of her shares in FWB.

 

            (FAC ¶¶ 107, 109.)

 

            As such, Plaintiff seeks a determination that Defendant Chaudhari defaulted on various agreements (the material terms of which Plaintiff has alleged), and that Plaintiff remains the beneficial owner of her shares in Defendant FWB. These allegations are sufficient at the pleading stage to state a claim.

 

            As for the eighth cause of action, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s allegations do not amount to sexual harassment.

 

A claim for sexual harassment under FEHA must allege five elements: (1) plaintiff belongs to a protected group; (2) plaintiff was subject to unwelcome sexual harassment; (3) the harassment complained of was based on sex; (4) the harassment complained of was pervasive enough to alter the conditions of employment and create an abusive working environment; and (5) respondeat superior. (Kelley v. The Conco Companies (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 191)

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that, “[o]n numerous occasions during her employment, Plaintiff was forced to endure a hostile work environment, listening to Defendant Chaudhari bragging about his sex life in her presence, using graphic language to describe and allude to his sexual exploits.” (FAC ¶ 3.) The Court agrees that, as alleged, these allegations are insufficiently general and conclusory. A single allegation that Defendant Chaudhari “bragged” on an unspecified amount of occasions about his sex life is insufficient to show severe and pervasive conduct sufficient to alter the conditions of employment. However, the Court concludes leave to amend is appropriate to provide Plaintiff the opportunity to add more specific allegations.

 

Finally, as to the fifth cause of action, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not alleged facts which could show extreme and outrageous conduct. However, as set forth above, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged claims for defamation and violations of the Labor Code (which are not challenged here). Whether or not Defendants’ conduct, in fact, rises to the level of extreme and outrageous is a factual determination not properly made at this stage of proceedings.

           

            Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ demurrer is overruled in part, sustained in part. Defendants’ demurrer is overruled as to the fifth, sixth, and seventh causes of action. Defendants’ demurrer is sustained, with 15 days leave to amend, as to the eighth cause of action.

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: BC696133    Hearing Date: July 1, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

GOVIND VAGHASHIA

                          

         vs.

 

PRASHANT VAGHASHIA, et al.

 

 

 Case No.:  BC696133

Related Case Nos.: BC696798 and BS172969

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  July 2, 2024

 

 

            The Govind Parties’ motion for a stay of proceedings is DENIED. Govind’s Claim of Exemption is DENIED.

 

On 7/3/2018, Plaintiffs Govind Vaghashia (Govind) as an individual and on behalf of Graphic Research, Inc. dba Gtek/Bycan Systems filed a second amended complaint (SAC) against Prashant Vaghashia (Prashant), Graphic Research, Inc. dba Gtek/Bycan Systems (GRI), alleging: (1) involuntary dissolution; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; (3) accounting; and (4) violation of Corporations Code sections 1601 and 1602.

 

            Now, the Govind Parties seek a stay of proceedings. Prashant and Mita seek a determination of Govind’s claim of exemption. For ease, the Court has consolidated its analysis into a single ruling. 

 

Discussion

 

I.                   Stay of Proceedings

 

The Govind Parties seek a stay of proceedings based on their posting of over $19.82 million in bonds on May 21, 2024. The Govind Parties further argue that the Court should exercise its discretion to stay the litigation.

 

After review, the Court denies the requested relief.

 

In order to trigger the stay of orders for the payment of money, the Govind Parties are required to post 150% of the amount of the order. Cal. Civ. Proc. § 917.1. As set forth in Prashant’s and Mita’s opposition, the bonds for the orders dated December 1, 2023, and February 22, 2024, are insufficient. (Opp., 7:1-7:16.)

 

The Court has repeatedly denied the Govind Parties request for a discretionary stay, and the Court denies this request here.

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Govind Parties’ request for a stay is denied.

 

II.               Claim of Exemption

 

Govind Vaghashia submitted a Claim of Exemption over $1,191.29 in his savings account at Wells Fargo. Accordingly, Prashant and Mita seek a determination on the claim.

 

After review, the Court denies Govind’s claim of exemption.

 

In his claim, Govind claims that the property consists of life insurance policies, yet also declares that he is claiming only $1,191.29 from a Wells Fargo savings account. He also admits that the savings account is “property and money belonging to the account name, Govind Vaghashia.” Then, he claims that the $1,129.29 is “for the purpose of his three children.” None of Govind Vaghashia’s children are minors. Kavita Vaghashia is a licensed real estate broker, and is 30 years old. Sundeep Vaghashia is a real estate investor and manager of his father’s business, testified at trial in this case, and is 40 years old. Ronak Vaghashia is 37 years old.

 

Section 704.225 provides that money in a judgment debtor’s deposit account that is not otherwise exempt under this chapter is exempt “to the extent necessary for the support of the judgment debtor and the spouse and dependents of the judgment debtor.” (CCP. § 704.225.) Section 704.225 is a hardship exemption, permitting a judgment debtor to retain funds to the extent they are “needed to support a family.” Where Govind’s children are all mature adults and Govind has vast hotel and real estate holdings, the Court is not persuaded by his claim of hardship, or that $1,191.29 is “necessary to support a family.”

 

Based on the foregoing, Govind’s claim of exemption is denied.

 

It is so ordered.
