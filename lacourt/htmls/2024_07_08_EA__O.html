DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Therefore, attorneys are advised to check this website to determine if any changes or updates have been made to the tentative ruling.

Counsel may submit on the tentative rulings by calling the clerk in Dept. O at 909-802-1126 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. Submission on the tentative does not bind the court to adopt the tentative ruling at the hearing should the opposing party appear and convince the court of further modification during oral argument.

The Tentative Ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such filing will be considered by the Court in the absence of permission first obtained following ex-parte application therefore.



Case Number: 21STCV17437    Hearing Date: July 8, 2024    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

WHEELS INTERNATIONAL INC. DBA LENSO WHEELS’S MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND CROSSCOMPLAINT FOR LACK OF PERSONAL JURISDICTION is CONTINUED pending jurisdictional discovery and supplemental briefing re: ‘relatedness’ factor infra).  

 

Background

 

This is a negligence case. Plaintiffs MELISSA MOORS (wife) and KAYLEAH MOORS (daughter) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) bring forth the instant action for the death of Julian Moore (“decedent”) who was struck when the wheel of Defendant ALBERT BENJAMIN ESQUEDA’s vehicle detached and struck decedent’s motorcycle. The accident happened in Pomona, California.[1]

 

On May 10, 2021, Plaintiffs filed suit.

 

On June 8, 2022, EMS filed their Answer to Plaintiffs' Complaint.

 

On September 28, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint (FAC) against Defendants ALBERT BENJAMIN ESQUEDA (“Esqueda”); ANGELINA ULLOA (“Ulloa”); ADRIANA SIORDIA JIMENEZ; MARIO SIORDIA JIMENEZ; IN AND OUT TIRE, LLC a limited liability; EMS DISTRIBUTING LLC d/b/a ELEMENT WHEELS COM (sued herein as DOE 1); EMS DISTRIBUTING, LLC, d/b/a ) ELEMENT WHEELS, (sued herein as DOE 2); (“EMS”);[2] and AFFORDABLE WHEEL REPAIR (sued) herein as DOE 3) for:


1.     Negligence

2.     Negligence Per Se

3.     Strict Liability

4.     Product Liability

5.     Survivor Action.

 

On February 10, 2023, default was entered against Axe Wheels 2/10/23 as to Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

On February 24, 2023, Defendants/Cross-Defendants ADRIANA SIORDIA JIMENEZ and MARIO SIORDIA JIMENEZ (collectively as the “Jimenez Defendants”) filed a motion for determination of good faith settlement, which the court granted.[3]

 

On August 4, 2023, EMS filed a motion for leave to file a cross-complaint against Axe Wheels and other defendants seeking equitable indemnity, negligence, contribution, apportionment of fault and declaratory relief regarding the duty to defend and duty to indemnify, which the court granted on 8/20/23.[4]

 

On August 30, 2023, EMS filed its cross-complaint (CC) against Axe Wheels, Esqueda, Ulloa, In and Out Tires, Flores, and Alberto Sr. dba Affordable Wheel Repair for:

 

1.     Total Indemnity

2.     Equitable Indemnity

3.     Negligence

4.     Contribution

5.     Apportionment

6.     Declaratory Relief

 

On September 19, 2023, Esqueda and Ulloa filed his answer to EMS’ cross-complaint.

 

On September 19, 2023, Flores, Alberto Sr. dba Affordable Wheel Repair filed their answer to the CC of “Mission Oaks National Bank.”

Discussion

 

Lenso Wheels moves argues that California lacks personal jurisdiction over it because Plaintiffs’ claims in this lawsuit are unrelated to Lenso Wheels’ purported California contacts. (Motion p. 15:9-11.)

 

For reasons to be discussed below, standing alone, EMS’ position that Lenso selling wheels into California and an injury by a wheel sent[5] to California is insufficient to meet its burden to prove the second prong of the specific jurisdictional analysis below.[6] That said, the court allows for jurisdictional discovery to provide evidence as to the strength of the relationship among Lenso, California, and the litigation, the “essential foundation” of specific jurisdiction. (Ford Motor, infra, 141 S. Ct. at p. 1028.)

 

Personal jurisdiction, which may either be specific or general,[7] refers to a court’s authority to adjudicate the rights and liabilities of a defendant. The doctrine arises from the US Constitution’s Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment which limits the power of a state court to assert personal jurisdiction (i.e., power) over a nonresident defendant. (Asahi Metal Indus. Co. v. Superior Court (1987) 480 U.S. 102, 113.) Under California’s long-arm statute, California state courts may exercise personal jurisdiction “on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of this state or of the United States.” (Civ. Proc. Section 410.10.) Thus, California’s exercise of personal jurisdiction must not offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” (International Shoe Co. v. Washington, 326 U.S. 310, 3316.) As explained by the Court, where a company’s activities in the forum state are more limited, general jurisdiction may be lacking but jurisdiction may nonetheless be proper because the litigation is derived from obligations that “arise out of or are connected with the [company‘s] activities within the state.” (Id. at pp. 19-320.)

 

At issue here is specific jurisdiction.[8]

 

1.     Case Law Concerning Specific Jurisdiction

 

The specific jurisdictional analysis is “intensely fact-specific.” (Rivelli v. Hemm (2021) 67 Cal.App.5th 380, 393, emphasis added.) Both the United States Supreme Court and our high court have cautioned that the “ ‘minimum contacts’ test ... is not susceptible of mechanical application; rather, the facts of each case must be weighed to determine whether the requisite ‘affiliating circumstances’ are present.” (Kulko v. California Superior Court (1978) 436 U.S. 84, 92, 98 S.Ct. 1690, 56 L.Ed.2d 132 (Kulko.) “California courts routinely apply a three-part test to determine whether a court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant: (1) the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits,[9] (2) the controversy is related to or arises out of the defendant’s purposeful contacts with the forum, and (3) the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with fair play and substantial justice. (Pavlovich v. Superior Court (2002) 29 Cal.4th 262; see also Keeton v. Hustler Magazine, Inc. (1984) 465 U.S. 770, 774 (Keeton).) A plaintiff has the initial burden of demonstrating facts to support the first two factors, which establish the requisite minimum contacts with the forum state. The burden then shifts to the defendant to show that the exercise of jurisdiction would be unreasonable under the third factor. (Snowney v. Harrah’s Entertainment, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1054, 1062; see also see also Burger King Corp. v. Rudzewicz (1985) 471 U.S. 462, 477 (Burger King) [“where a defendant who purposefully has directed his activities at forum residents seeks to defeat jurisdiction, he must present a compelling case that the presence of some other considerations would render jurisdiction unreasonable.”].)

 

Accordingly, based on the foregoing authority, the issue presented is whether EMS’ suit—the distributor of the wheel—arises out of or relates to Lenso Wheels’ contacts with California—the seller of the subject wheel.

 

1.     Purposeful Availment

 

“[P]urposeful availment exists whenever the defendant purposefully and voluntarily directs its activities toward the forum state in an effort to obtain a benefit from that state.” (Snowney, supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 1067.) When a manufacturer makes a direct effort to serve the market for its product in the forum state, the requisite level of foreseeability is met. ((World–Wide Volkswagen Corp. v. Woodson, supra, 444 U.S. 286, 297 [100 S.Ct. 559, 567, 62 L.Ed.2d 490, 501].); see also (Asahi Metal Industry Co. v. Superior Court (1987) 480 U.S. 102, 112, 107 S.Ct. 1026, 1032, 94 L.Ed.2d 92 (plur. opn. by O'Connor, J. [For the purpose of deciding whether a defendant has minimum contacts or purposefully has availed itself of forum benefits, the relevant contacts are said to be with the forum, because it is the defendant's choice to take advantage of opportunities that exist in the forum that subjects it to jurisdiction.].) The purposeful availment requirement ensures that a defendant will not be haled into a jurisdiction solely as a result of random, fortuitous, or attenuated contacts. (Burger King, supra, 471 U.S. at p. 475.) In sum,  the purposeful availment inquiry focuses on the defendant's intentionality. (Pavlovich, supra, 29 Cal.4th at p. 269, 127 Cal.Rptr.2d 329, 58 P.3d 2.) 

 

EMS argues that Lenso Wheels directly, and indirectly distributes its products to numerous states in the United States, including California via several websites including eBay, Fitment Industries, Mr. Wheel Deal, and IPG parts. (Opp. p. 11.)[10] In fact, Lenso Wheels states that it has “from time to time, conducted business in California, including selling wheels to California residents over the years.” (Opp. p. 11, quoting Motion p. 14:21-22.) Indeed, California courts have consistently concluded that a foreign corporation purposefully avails itself of the benefits of the California forum when it knowingly sells and ships its products to California businesses for use in California. (See Jayone Foods, Inc. v. Aekyung Industrial Co. Ltd. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 543, 556–557, 242 Cal.Rptr.3d 705 (Jayone Foods) [South Korean company that “engaged in a number of direct sales transactions with multiple California distributors of its consumer products” purposefully availed itself of the benefits of doing business in California]; Luberski, Inc. v. Oleficio F.LLI Amato S.R.L. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 409, 419, 89 Cal.Rptr.3d 774 [Italian company that sold olive oil to California businesses and delivered it to California “had the expectation that the goods ... would be utilized in California” and thus “purposefully availed itself of the [California] forum”].

 

Lenso concedes this factor by arguing that the “focus here is not on purposeful availment” and then shifting to the second element. (Reply p. 3:25-26.)

 

Therefore, in the present matter, there is no question that Lenso has purposely availed itself of the privilege of conducting activities in California (e.g., by regularly advertising their wheels to California residents), invoking the benefits and protection of its laws, and Lenso does not contend otherwise. 

 

2.     Relatedness

 

The Supreme Court most recently addressed this factor of specific jurisdiction in (Bristol-Myers Squibb Co. v. Superior Court of California, San Francisco County (2017) — U.S. —, 137 S.Ct. 1773, 1779, 198 L.Ed.2d 395 (Bristol-Myers)) and Ford Motor Co. v. Montana Eighth Jud. Dist. Ct. (2021) — U.S. —, 141 S.Ct. 1017, 1024, 209 L.Ed.2d 225 (Ford).)

 

In Bristol-Myers, over 600 plaintiffs brought a consolidated action against the defendant pharmaceutical company (company that was not a California corporation nor developed the drug in California) in California alleging injuries caused by the defendant's drug. While some of the plaintiffs were California residents, most (i) had no connection to California (were not residents), (ii) they did not allege that they obtained/were prescribed the drug in California, (iii) they were not injured in California (i.e., did not ingest the drug in California), nor (iv) were the plaintiffs treated in California. In reversing the California Supreme Court’s decision, which found specific jurisdiction based upon a "sliding scale approach,” the Court stating that a defendant's general connections with the forum are not enough.” (Id. at p. 1781.) “What is needed—and what is missing here—is a CONNECTION between the forum and the specific claims at issue …The plaintiffs were not residents of California, did not claim to have suffered harm in California …[and] all the conduct giving rise to the nonresidents’ claims occurred elsewhere.” (Id. at p. 1779, emphasis added and capitalization added.) Thus, the “bare fact that [the company] contracted with a California distributor is not enough to establish personal jurisdiction in the State.” (Id. at p. 1783.)

 

Then, in Ford, the Court again “alter[ed] or refine[d]” specific jurisdiction jurisprudence. (Ford, 141 S.Ct. at p. 1032 (Alito J., concurring).[11] In a case that arose from car accidents involving Ford vehicles in Montana and Minnesota, the Court rejected Ford's position that “only a strict causal relationship” would be sufficient to establish the requisite connection between the lawsuits and Ford's forum-directed activities. (Id. at p. 1026.) The Court found the “essential foundation” of specific jurisdiction to be present because “Ford had advertised, sold, and serviced those two car models in both States for many years.” (Id. at p. 1028.) After all, the very point of Ford’s activities in the forum states was to put more Fords the forum states’ roads. As more directly put by Justice Alito in his concurring opinion, “Their residents, while riding in vehicles purchased within their borders, were killed or injured in accidents on their roads. Can anyone seriously argue that requiring Ford to litigate these cases in Minnesota and Montana would be fundamentally unfair?” (Id. at p. 1032, italics original.) In distinguishing from Bristol-Myers (which the defendant Ford relied upon), the court observed the following:

 

Yes, Ford sold the specific products in other States, as Bristol-Myers Squibb had. But here, the plaintiffs are residents of the forum States. They used the allegedly defective products in the forum States. And they suffered injuries when those products malfunctioned in the forum States. In sum, each of the plaintiffs brought suit in the most natural State—based on an “AFFILIATION between the forum and the underlying controversy, principally, [an] activity or an occurrence that t[ook] place” there. (Id. at at p. 1031, emphasis added and capitalization added.)

 

California has adopted a “substantial connection” test and held that the relatedness requirement is satisfied if “there is a substantial nexus or connection between the defendant's forum activities and the plaintiff's claim.” (Vons Companies, Inc. v. Seabest Foods, Inc., 14 Cal.4th 434, 456 (1996).) “[T]he more wide ranging the defendant‘s forum contacts, the more readily is shown a connection between the forum contacts and the claim. (Id. at p. 455.) Thus, “[a] claim need not arise directly from the defendant‘s forum contacts in order to be sufficiently related to the contact to warrant the exercise of specific jurisdiction.” (Id. at p. 452.)

 

Here, the parties do not appear to dispute the following facts, (which are gathered from both the opposition and reply:

 

-        On February 11, 2019, Esqueda ordered four Lenso Wheels a.k.a. "Axe Wheels" brand named wheels from EMS via its website

-        On February 11, 2019, EMS put in an order to Lenso Wheels creating a purchase order that indicated "drops shipping to customer," which is an instruction for Lenso Wheels to ship the goods directly to the customer, Albert Esqueda, in California

-        EMS also provided a shipping label to Lenso Wheels for it to send the wheels directly to Albert Esqueda at his address in Compton, California.

-        After receiving the purchase order, invoice, and Fed Ex mailing labels from EMS, Lenso Wheels placed shipping labels – that were provided by EMS – onto boxes to then be picked up by a third party (FedEx) and transported to EMS’s customer.

 

Lenso argues that the foregoing is unilateral conduct which is insufficient for specific jurisdiction. (Reply p. 4:22-23.) Not necessarily.

 

First, whether EMS is unilaterally drawing Lenso into a connection with California is a misplaced argument because the argument is relevant to general jurisdiction, not ‘relatedness’ for specific jurisdiction. (See e.g., Helicopteros Nacionales de Columbia, S.A., v. Hall, supra, at p. 408, emphasis added [“[T]he one trip to Houston by petitioner's chief executive officer for the purpose of negotiating the transportation services contract cannot be regarded as a contact of a ‘continuous and systematic’ nature, and thus cannot support an assertion of general jurisdiction.”]; see also International Shoe, supra, at p. 2851, emphasis added [general jurisdiction may only be asserted “over foreign (sister-state or foreign country) corporations to hear any and all claims against them when their affiliations with the State are so

‘continuous and systematic’ as to render them essentially at home in the forum State.”]; see also Goodyear Dunlop Tires Operations, S.A. v. Brown (2011) 131 S.Ct. 2846, 2851, emphasis added [General jurisdiction is appropriate over foreign corporations “when their affiliations with the State are so ‘continuous and systematic’ as to render them essentially at home in the forum State.”].)

 

Even if the “unilateral activity” of EMS is relevant in discussing this factor of the specific jurisdiction analysis, Lenso, essentially, proposes that sellers be insulated from a forum state’s jurisdiction. The court requires further briefing on this issue and should answering it require jurisdictional discovery it is granted.

 

For example, Lenso cites to Carretti v. Italplast (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 1236. In Carretti, a plaintiff filed suit against the manufacturer and distributor of a pasta making machine after he was injured. (Id. at pp. 1239-1240.) Italpast, an Italian corporation doing business in Italy, filed a motion to quash service of summons for lack of personal jurisdiction. (Id. at p. 1240.) Italpast has never SOLD goods directly to California users. The distributor was the only buyer from California who has purchased products from Italpast. In finding that there was no specific jurisdiction upon the Italian company, the court reasoned that the manufacturer “has not cultivated the California sales market by repeatedly sending merchandise to various California distributors. There is no evidence that at the time of sale Italpast knew its machines were being delivered to California, except on one occasion, and even then, it did not know if California was the ultimate destination, or just the port of entry.” (Id. at p. 1253.) Thus, Carretti seemingly stands for the proposition that should a distributor not SELL its products to CONSUMERS in the forum state, even if the product happens to arrive in the forum state, then there is no specific jurisdiction.

 

Similarly, Ford Motor, supra, the company was “urging,” “fostering,” and/or “encouraging” sales in a CONSUMER market. (Ford Motor, supra, 141 S. Ct. at pp. 1023, 1028, 1029, 1031.)

 

The recent case of LG Chem, Ltd. v. Superior Court of San Diego (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 348 (LG) is particularly instructive as it discusses the difference between a company serving a market versus a company serving any market, the latter which is insufficient to satisfy the second prong of the jurisdictional analysis. (Id. at p. 367.) In LG, the plaintiff was injured from LG’s battery purchased at a vape shop, which in no sense arose from LG’s sale of the batteries for electric vehicles. In describing this distinction, the court focused on the fact that the company’s sales of its industrial component batteries were made to companies in the electric vehicle industry. (Ibid.) Unlike Ford, “the market served by LG Chem's California sales was not a CONSUMER market.” (Ibid, emphasis and capitalization added.) In fact, LG “took steps to prevent” consumers from purchasing the batteries by placing warning labels on its batteries and “requiring its business customers to sign declarations of commitment promising not to resell its batteries to individual consumers.” (Ibid.) Under those facts, the court rejected the plaintiff’s contention jurisdiction is proper “in any jurisdiction that is part of the product's distribution chain.” (Id. at p. 368.) “That goes too far,” the court observed. (Ibid.)

 

Here, piecing together the rules from the foregoing cases, the rule appears as follows: the relatedness factor is satisfied when a company SYSTEMATICALLY (Ford Motor, supra, 141 S. Ct. at p. 1028) serves a CONSUMER market for a product in the forum State. Here, the court does not have enough facts about Lenso’s marketing, advertising, and other business-related activities.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the hearing is CONTINUED pending jurisdictional discovery and supplemental briefing.[12]
