     CASE NO.: 22VECV00601

 

ORDER DENYING KINGSTON PROPERTIES LLC’S MOTION TO COMPEL THE PLAINTIFF, FRANK DANDRIDGE, TO APPEAR AND TESTIFY AT DEPOSITION

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case arises of habitability issues Plaintiff Frank Dandridge (“Plaintiff”) allegedly experienced while leasing an apartment located at 5401 Sepulveda Blvd #14, Sherman Oaks, CA 91411. In connection with those issues, Plaintiff filed a Complaint against Defendants W-R-T Development Co. (“W-R-T”), Frank Warren, as Trustee of the Frank Warren and Joanne Warren Revocable Trust, Joanne Warren, as Trustee of the Frank Warren and Joanne Warren Revocable Trust (“Joanne”), Warren Properties, Inc. (“Warren”), Kingston Properties LLC (“Kingston”), and Hollywood Don LP (“Hollywood”).

Kingston now seeks to compel Plaintiff’s deposition.  

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (3) Breach of Warranty of Habitability; (4) Negligence; (5) Nuisance; (6) Violation of California Civil Code Section 1942.4; (7) Fraud; (8) Violation of Unfair Business Practices; (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (10) Battery (Bedbugs); (11) Trespass to Chattel; and (12) Elder Abuse.

On July 22, 2022, Hollywood filed its Answer to the Complaint.

On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed his First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging (1) Breach of Contract/Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment/Warranty of Habitability; (2) Negligence; (3) Nuisance; (4) Violation of California Civil Code Section 1942.4; (5) Violation of Unfair Business Practices; (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (7) Trespass to Chattel.

On July 25, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed his seventh cause of action in his FAC.

On November 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to his FAC, changing “W-R-T DEVELOPMENT CO., a California limited partnership” to “W-R-T DEVELOPMENT CO., a California general partnership.”

On November 14, 2023, the Court granted in part W-R-T, Warren, Kingston, and Hollywood’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed his Second Amended Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract/Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment/Warranty of Habitability; (2) Tortious Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability; (3) Negligence; (4) Nuisance; (5) Violation of California Civil Code Section 1942.4; (6) Violation of Unfair Business Practices; and (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

On January 8, 2024, Warren, Kingston, W-R-T, Joanne, and Hollywood filed their Answer.

On October 7, 2024, the Court denied Kingston’s Motions to Compel the Responses of Plaintiff to its Special Interrogatories and its Requests for Production. (“Motion”).

On November 20, 2024, Kingston filed the instant Motion to Compel the Plaintiff, Frank Dandridge, to Appear and Testify at Deposition (the “Motion”) and Declaration of David Larsen in Support of the Motion dated November 20, 2024 (“Larsen Decl.”).

On December 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed a “Partial Opposition” to the Motion.

On December 4, 2024, Kingston filed its Reply.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a)[1] provides that, “[i]f, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action… without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it…, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.410(a), provides that, “[a]ny party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with [the notice requirements in Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2025.210 through 2025.290] waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.”

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(b)(2) requires the motion to compel deposition be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition, by a declaration stating the moving party has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.290, which provides for a limit of seven hours of total testimony of a witness, does not apply to the deposition of a person designated as the person most qualified to be deposed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.290(b)(5).)

IV.          MEET AND CONFER

A motion set forth under Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450 must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration or, when the deponent does not attend the deposition, a “declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).)

Kingston’s counsel warned Plaintiff’s counsel he intended to file a Motion to Compel after receiving the objection to Kingston’s First Amended Notice of Deposition. (See Larsen Decl., ¶ 9, Ex. G.) However, Kingston’s counsel did not communicate with Plaintiff’s counsel at all after taking Plaintiff’s Non-Appearance at the previously noticed September 25, 2024, deposition. (See generally Larsen Decl. and Plaintiff’s Opposition, 3:25-26.) The Court notes that it denied Kingston’s last pair of discovery motions for failing to meet and confer. Kingston’s counsel has once again failed to properly meet and confer after Plaintiff’s non-appearance and before Kingston filed the Motion as required by section 2025.450(b)(2). Kingston’s counsel’s approach does not demonstrate a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of the issues presented in the Motion. As such, Kingston’s Motion is DENIED.

In addition, since Plaintiff’s counsel has not requested sanctions or provided any evidentiary support on which the Court might rely to sanction Defendants’ counsel for failing to meet and confer, the Court will not sanction Defendants’ counsel for violating section 2025.450(b)(2). However, the Court reminds counsel for both parties that failing to meet and confer prior to filing a discovery motion when a particular discovery motion so requires them, they could be subject to sanctions for misuse of the discovery process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010.)

Based on its review of the sequence of events leading up to this Motion, the Court is particularly concerned that counsel for both sides have failed to take the Court’s admonitions at the hearing on Kingston’s prior motions to compel and in its subsequent order to heart. Specifically, in its Order dated October 7, 2024, a full 44 days prior to Kingston’s filing of this motion, the Court stated it “takes seriously their obligations to work collaboratively and cooperatively for the sake of their clients, not to mention judicial efficiency.” Simply put, this is the last time the Court will warn counsel about their lack of civility.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Kingston’s Motion to Compel the Plaintiff, Frank Dandridge, to Appear and Testify at Deposition.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT HARBOR FREIGHT TOOLS USA, INC.’S MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

 

 

I.        BACKGROUND 

This is a Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”) action filed by Representative Plaintiff Sergio Gandarilla (“Plaintiff”) against Defendant Harbor Freight Tools USA, Inc. (“Defendant”). Plaintiff was employed by Defendant from July 21, 2020, until his termination on June 4, 2024. Plaintiff filed one cause of action against Defendant for violation of Labor Code § 2698 et seq.

Defendant now moves to compel Plaintiff’s claim to arbitration.  

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Labor Code § 2698[1], et seq.

On October 10, 2024, Defendant filed its Answer.

On November 8, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration (“Motion”).

On December 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition.

On December 10, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

“The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement.” (Avila v. Southern Cal. Specialty Care, Inc. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 835, 844.)

“California courts traditionally have maintained a strong preference for arbitration as a speedy and inexpensive method of dispute resolution… To this end, arbitration agreements should be liberally construed, … with doubts concerning the scope of arbitrable issues being resolved in favor of arbitration.” (Market Ins. Corp. v. Integrity Ins. Co. (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1095, 1098 (cleaned up).) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.... It seems clear that the burden must fall upon the party opposing arbitration to demonstrate that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.” (Bono v. David (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 1055, 1062 (cleaned up).)

California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”), including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (See Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-972.) The Court is empowered by Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so.¿¿¿

The party opposing the petition to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense. (See Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.) Accordingly, under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (See Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)

Thus, in deciding a motion to compel arbitration, trial courts must first decide whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then determine the second gateway issue of whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. (See Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Evidentiary Objections and Request for Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice of other Superior Court Minute Orders, but only as to the existence of those Minute Orders. The Court cannot take judicial notice of hearsay statements contained in court records. (See Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 768; Williams v. Wraxall (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 120, 130, fn. 7.)

          The Court also OVERRULES Defendant’s Objection to Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as it takes judicial notice of the existence of the Minute Orders only.

B.             Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is Denied

1.     There is a Valid Arbitration Agreement Between the Parties

The arbitration proponent must first recite verbatim, or provide a copy of, the alleged agreement. (See Sprunk v. Prisma LLC (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 785, 793.) A movant can meet this initial burden “‘by attaching a copy of the arbitration agreement purportedly bearing the opposing party’s signature.’” (Id. (quoting Rules of Court, rule 3.1330).) “[U]nless there is a dispute over authenticity, it is sufficient for a party moving to compel arbitration to recite the terms of the governing provision.” (Id. (citing Condee v. Longwood Mgmt. Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219).) “A voluntary acceptance of the benefit of a transaction is equivalent to a consent to all the obligations arising from it, so far as the facts are known, or ought to be known, to the person accepting.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1589.)

          Defendant has submitted evidence of a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. Defendant has attached a copy of its 2023 Dispute Resolution Policy (“2023 DRP”) signed by Plaintiff. (See Declaration of Matthew Finney filed November 8, 2024 (“Finney Decl.”), ¶¶ 13-14, Ex. 4.) By signing the 2023 DRP, Plaintiff agreed to:

“…to submit to final and binding arbitration any and all claims and disputes, whether they exist now or arise in the future, that are related in any way to my employment or the termination of my employment with Harbor Freight Tools, except as otherwise permitted by the DRP. I understand that final and binding arbitration will be the sole and exclusive remedy for any such claim or dispute against Harbor Freight Tools and any parent, subsidiary, affiliated and/or related entities, and its and their current and former directors, officers, supervisors, Associates, representatives, agents, and attorneys, and that by agreeing to use arbitration to resolve my disputes, both Harbor Freight Tools and I agree to forgo any right we each may have had to a jury trial on issues covered by the DRP, and forgo any right to bring claims on a class, collective, coordinated, consolidated, or representative basis.

 

In cases under the California Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”), Harbor Freight Tools and I agree to arbitrate only the individual PAGA claims based on Labor Code violations personally sustained by me. I also agree that the arbitration will be conducted before a mutually agreed upon arbitrator (or court-appointed arbitrator if the parties cannot agree), and that the designated arbitration provider’s rules will apply to the arbitration proceedings unless they conflict with the DRP. If the arbitration provider’s rules conflict with the DRP in any way, I agree that the DRP shall govern.”

(Finney Decl., ¶ 13, Ex. 4.)

Accordingly, Defendant has proved the existence of a valid and enforceable arbitration agreement.

2.     Plaintiff’s Claims are not Covered by the Arbitration Agreement

Defendant asserts “in response to [Defendant’s] request that he submit his claims to arbitration, [Plaintiff] argued that, because he purports to bring his claims only in a ‘representative’ capacity, and not individually, the Complaint does not allege any arbitrable claims under [PAGA].” (Motion, pg. 9.) Defendant argues Plaintiff’s representative argument is “pure sophistry” because Plaintiff cannot maintain a PAGA action unless he has suffered the alleged labor code violations. (See id. at 9, 12-13.) “In fact, if [Plaintiff] had not suffered from the alleged Labor Code violations asserted in the Complaint, he would not have standing to bring this action under PAGA.” (Id. at 13.) Defendant argues the recent Balderas v. Fresh Start Harvesting, Inc. (2024) 101 Cal.App.5th 533 (“Balderas”) case, allegedly relied on by Plaintiff when the parties discussed the arbitration agreement, does not stand for the proposition that a plaintiff can maintain a representative action without asserting any individual claims. (See id. at 12.) Rather, Defendant argues, “the Court of Appeal merely held that Balderas was not required to bring a separate individual PAGA claim seeking relief only for herself in order to maintain standing.” (Id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the parties expressly agreed to arbitrate only Plaintiff’s individual PAGA claims. (See Opposition, pg. 3.) Here, Plaintiff argues, Plaintiff did not bring any individual PAGA claims and Balderas controls. (See id. at 4.) “The central question in Balderas was whether an employee must file an individual claim to maintain a representative PAGA action. The issue was significantly influenced by the U.S. Supreme Court’s decision in Viking River Cruises, Inc. v. Moriana, 142 S.Ct. 1906 (2022) [“Viking River”], which suggested that PAGA plaintiffs need an individual claim to pursue representative-only PAGA claims.” (Id.) The Court in Balderas, Plaintiff argues, declined to follow Viking River, instead finding the plaintiff there could pursue representative claims without seeking individual relief. (See id. at 5.) “Based on the Balderas ruling, Plaintiff can proceed litigating his PAGA representative claim on behalf of Aggrieved Employees and the State alone in this Action, without prosecuting individual, non-representative claims. This is exactly what Plaintiff did here.” (Id. at 7.) Plaintiff argues he is an aggrieved employee for standing purposes and thus can bring the instant PAGA action in his representative capacity only. (See id. at 10.)  

In reply, Defendant argues the recently issued Rodriguez v. Lawrence Equipment, Inc. (2024) 106 Cal.App.5th 645 (“Rodriguez”) case “precludes the very idea that plaintiffs can assert PAGA representative claims without asserting an individual claim, holding that PAGA standing and the existence of individual claims based on Labor Code violations allegedly suffered are ‘identical issues.’” (Reply, pg. 5, quoting Rodriguez, 106 Cal.App.5th at 659.)

The Court finds Plaintiff claims are not subject to arbitration because he asserts his PAGA action in his representative capacity only. To possess standing to pursue PAGA claims a plaintiff must be an “aggrieved employee”, or one who has sustained a Labor Code violation committed by their employer. (See Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104, 1121.) PAGA standing only requires a plaintiff alleges “that he or she is (1) someone who was employed by the alleged violator and (2) someone against whom one or more of the alleged violations was committed.” (Adolph, 14 Cal.5th at 1120 (quotations and citations omitted); see also Balderas, 101 Cal.App.5th at 538 (“The Adolph court concluded that the Viking River requirement of having to file an individual PAGA cause of action to achieve standing to file a representative PAGA suit was incorrect.”).)

In applying Adolph’s holding, the Court of Appeal in Balderas found the plaintiff in that case had standing to bring a representative PAGA suit, although she did not file an individual PAGA claim, because she was an aggrieved employee. (See Balderas, 101 Cal.App.5th at 539.) “The statutory goal [of PAGA] is furthered by extending broad standing to aggrieved employees who do not depend on the viability or strength of a plaintiff's individual PAGA claim. The inability for an employee to pursue an individual PAGA claim does not prevent that employee from filing a representative PAGA action. (See Balderas, 101 Cal.App.5th at 537.)

Defendant’s argument can be simplified as follows: because Plaintiff must be an aggrieved employee to bring a PAGA action, it is impossible for Plaintiff to bring a PAGA action in his representative capacity without also bringing the action in his individual capacity. But that argument not only contradicts the holdings of Adolph and Balderas but also reads into those rulings a requirement that doesn’t exist. Plaintiff can bring this PAGA action in his representative capacity without also bringing the action in his individual capacity, as he has done here and as was explicitly contemplated in Balderas. Defendant’s citation of the recent Rodriguez case does not in any way alter Balderas’s holding and is not persuasive.[2]  

In short, Defendant cannot compel Plaintiff’s non-existent individual PAGA claim to arbitration. Such an action is beyond the scope of the arbitration agreement and contrary to applicable law.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion.

As a result of the Court’s foregoing analysis and conclusion, it declines to address the parties’ arguments about the DRP’s procedural or substantive unconscionability.

V.       CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration.
