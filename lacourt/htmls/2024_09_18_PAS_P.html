DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV00268    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER ON PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR AN ORDER CHARGING JUDGMENT DEBTOR’S MEMBERSHIP INTEREST IN LLC


INTRODUCTION

          This is a commercial loan dispute. Plaintiff Cadlerock Joint Venture, L.P. sued defendants Edith Enterprise Ltd., Helen Young, Does I-X, and Roe Corporations XI-XX on May 9, 2022, for breach of contract, unjust enrichment, and promissory estoppel. Plaintiff sought to recover an outstanding balance of approximately $25,000.00 on a line of credit issued to Defendants by CitiBank, who later assigned the contract to Plaintiff.

The Court entered judgment for Cadlerock and against Edith and Young in the amount of approximately $50,000.00. A cross-claim against Citi remains unresolved.

On July 11, 2024, Plaintiff moved for an order charging Defendant Young’s partnership interest in a company called “Monterey Edith LLC” (California Entity No. 200424610025) until the approximately $50,000.00 outstanding on the judgment is paid.

No party filed an opposition, and Plaintiff filed no reply.

“If a money judgment is rendered against a partner or member but not against the partnership or limited liability company, the judgment debtor’s interest in the partnership or limited liability company may be applied toward the satisfaction of the judgment by an order charging the judgment debtor’s interest pursuant to Section 15907.03, 16504, or 17705.03 of the Corporations Code.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 708.310.) For LLCs, “[Corporations Code] section [17705.03] provides the exclusive remedy by which a person seeking to enforce a judgment against a member or transferee may, in the capacity of judgment creditor, satisfy the judgment from the judgment debtor’s transferable interest.”

The Court’s record reflects that Defendant Young is a judgment debtor. Plaintiffs’ counsel conducted a debtor’s exam of Young on April 29, 2024, during which she confirmed “she was a member/owner and manager of [Monterey Edith LLC].” (Sayyar Decl., ¶ 7; id., Exh. 2 (Exam Transcript), 66:15-25, 67:1-13, 68:11-25, 69:1-10, 70:1-25, and 75:5-112.)

Money judgment has been rendered against Young, one of Monterey’s partners or members; per section 17705.03, her interest may be applied toward satisfaction of the judgment. 

The unopposed motion is granted.  Plaintiff is to give notice.

Dated: September 18, 2024

       JARED D. MOSES
JUDGE OF THE SUPERIOR COURT

[TENTATIVE]
ORDER ON CROSS-DEFENDANT CITIBANK’S MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

The motion is granted in
part, without leave to amend.

I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a commercial loan dispute. Plaintiff Cadlerock
Joint Venture, L.P. sued defendants Edith Enterprise Ltd. (“Edith”), Helen
Young (“Young”), Does I-X, and Roe Corporations XI-XX on May 9, 2022, for
breach of contract, unjust enrichment, and promissory estoppel. Plaintiff
sought to recover an outstanding balance of approximately $25,000.00 on a line
of credit issued to Defendants by CitiBank, who later assigned the contract to
Plaintiff.

          On April 3, 2023, Edith and Young (“Cross-Complainants”) cross-complained
against cross-defendant CitiBank, N.A. ( “Citi”). Cross-Complainants filed
their operative third amended cross-complaint (“TAXC”) on January 10, 2024. The
TAXC asserts causes of action for (1) breach of written and
implied-in-fact contract; (2) conversion; (3) negligence; (4) fraudulent
transfers; (5) unfair business practices; (6) breach of the implied covenant of
good faith and fair dealing; (7) financial elder abuse; (8) violations of the
Electronic Funds Transfers Act; and (9) a common count.

          On May 16, 2024, Citi moved for judgment on the TAXC. On
September 6, 2024, Cross-Complainants filed their opposition, and on September
11, 2024, Citi replied.

 

          II.      LEGAL STANDARD

A defendant may move for judgment on the pleadings
when the “complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of
action against the defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 438(b)(1) and (c)(1)(B)(ii).)
“A motion for judgment on the pleadings performs the same function as a general
demurrer, and hence attacks only defects disclosed on the face of the pleadings
or by matters that can be judicially noticed. Presentation of extrinsic
evidence is … not proper on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.” (Cloud
v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999, citations
omitted.)

          The
standard for ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings is essentially
the same as that applicable to a general demurrer: that under the state of the
pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears
that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Schabarum v.
California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216.) The court “must
accept as true all material facts properly pleaded, but does not
consider conclusions of law or fact, opinions, speculation,
or allegations contrary to law or facts that are judicially noticed.” (Stevenson
Real Estate Services, Inc. v. CB Richard Ellis Real Estate Services, Inc.
(2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 1215, 1219-1220, citing Shea Homes Limited
Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254.)

 

III.     EVIDENTIARY MATTERS

Citi
requests judicial notice of the Court’s August 7, 2023 order granting Citi’s
motion for judgment on the first amended cross-complaint. The request is
granted. (See Evid. Code, § 452(a), (c), (d).)

 

          IV.     MEETING AND
CONFERENCE

          Code of Civil Procedure section 439 requires a party moving
for judgment on the pleadings to meet and confer with its opponent prior to
filing. Citi’s counsel submitted a declaration that satisfies section 439. (See
Kenney Decl., ¶¶ 3-8.)

 

V.      ANALYSIS

A.
First and Sixth Causes of Action for Breach of Contract and Covenant

          1. Breach of Contract (First
Cause of Action)

A
plaintiff asserting breach of contract must plead (1) the existence of a
contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse, (3) breach of the
contract, and (4) causation and harm. (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 222
Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)

Citi
contends Cross-Complainants have not satisfied the first or third elements of their
breach of contract action, because their TAXC “vaguely refers to a written
contract and Citibank’s alleged breach of such contract without alleging any
facts relating to the specific terms which Citibank allegedly breached.” (Mot.,
3:19-21.) Citi cites this Court’s prior order, wherein the Hon. Judge Margaret
Oldendorf held “[t]he pleading does not contain facts supporting the existence
of an agreement ... including which terms Citibank is alleged to have
breached.” (08-07-2023 Order, 3:1-3.)

Cross-Complainants
have cured this deficiency. “A plaintiff may plead the legal effect of the
contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective
Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 199.) New
allegations in the TAXC specify the agreement Edith refers to – not only the
credit line agreement itself, but also the condition that it maintain a
checking account with Citi in order to access the line.

Specifically,
Cross-Complainants’ TAXC newly alleges: “Edith and Citibank entered into a binding
and enforceable contract ..., pursuant to which Edith was required to maintain
a business checking account with Citibank for direct withdrawal of loan
payments. Also, the business checking account must be used as borrower’s
primary operating account. Pursuant to that contract, Edith was required to
agree to these requirements before the Credit Line could be formally
activated.” (TAXC, ¶ 58.)

Cross-Complainants
allege Citi provided Edith with a line of credit; they also allege Edith’s
agreement to open an account at Citi was part of the consideration for that
credit line. (See TAXC, ¶ 58 [“Pursuant to the contract, Edith was required to
agree ...”].) The contract and its essential terms have been pled, as has
Citi’s breach.

          The motion is denied as to the first cause of action for
breach of contract.

 

2.
Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing
(Sixth Cause of Action)

The
motion is denied on the same basis as to Cross-Complainants’ sixth cause of
action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

“ ‘The [implied]
covenant of good faith and fair dealing [is] implied by law in every contract.’
(Citation.) The covenant is read into contracts and functions ‘as a supplement to
the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging
in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants)
frustrates the other party's rights to the benefits of the contract.’ (Racine
& Laramie, Ltd. v. Department of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11
Cal.App.4th 1026, 1031–1032.) The covenant also requires each party to do
everything the contract presupposes the party will do to accomplish the
agreement's purposes. (Harm v. Frasher (1960) 181 Cal.App.2d 405,
417.) A breach of the implied covenant of good faith is a breach of the
contract (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc.
(1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1393.), and ‘breach of a specific provision of the
contract is not ... necessary’ to a claim for breach of the implied covenant of
good faith and fair dealing (Carma Developers (Cal.), Inc. v. Marathon
Development California, Inc. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 342, 373 & fn. 12.).” (Thrifty
Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230,
1244.)

Cross-Complainants allege Citi interfered with their
performance by draining Edith’s checking account of funds while also requiring it
use only that account to pay off its line of credit. Accepted as true, this
action interfered with Edith’s performance, even though it was not expressly
contemplated in the parties’ agreement. Each of the other elements of the claim
– contract, performance, breach – is discussed above regarding the first cause
of action.

The motion is denied as to the sixth cause of action
for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

         

B.
Second Cause of Action for Conversion

Conversion
has three elements: (1) a plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of
property, (2) a defendant’s wrongful act or disposition of the property
that interferes with plaintiff's possession, and (3) damages. (McKell v.
Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1491.)

Citi
contends Cross-Complainants cannot state a cause of action for conversion
because, as a matter of law, no claim for conversion lies against a bank for
its mismanagement of deposited funds. Cross-Complainants argue Citi’s legal
authority for this rule is obsolete. Cross-Complainants cite Fong v. East
West Bank (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 224 (Fong);j the Fong court
held that “there is no special rule preventing a depositor from pursuing a
conversion action against the bank that holds his or her money”, and “courts
have found an action for conversion proper when the property at issue was some
form of financial interest.” (Id., at p. 232-233.)

          Citi replies that Fong is inapposite because it
“concerned a bank’s conversion of a negotiable instrument [a certificate of
deposit], ... [and a] bank can convert a negotiable instrument.” (Reply,
5:10-16.)

          The Court agrees with Citi. Although the Fong court
framed its discussion broadly, it only held, as relevant, that “[f]inancial
institutions can be liable to their depositors for transferring money out of
their accounts on forged instruments” (Fong, supra, at p.
235, italics added) – which is not alleged here. All other case law, including
the law cited in Fong, suggests an intangible asset may be converted,
but only if it is represented in a tangible instrument. (See Welco
Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 211-212 [wrongful
charges to credit card may be conversion because “there can be a conversion of
intangible rights represented by special instruments”].) Properly construed,
Cross-Complainants’ quote from Fong confirms that just because a
plaintiff deposits funds with a bank doesn’t mean he can’t sue them for
converting other assets apart from those deposited funds.

          The motion is granted without leave to amend as to the
second cause of action for conversion.

 

          C. Third Cause of Action for Negligence

          The Court granted judgment on the pleadings on Cross-Complainants’
third cause of action in its August 2023 order because “Edith alleges only
economic harm and thus the claim is barred by the economic loss rule.” (08-07-2023
Order, 5:22-23.)

          Cross-Complainants have not amended their TAXC to allege
non-economic harm. In the portion of their pleading related to the third cause
of action, Cross-Complainants discuss Citi’s duties at length. (See TAXC, ¶¶
88-97.) But as to harm, they still allege only that “Edith have [sic]
suffered harm as a result of Citibank’s conduct.” (TAXC, ¶ 101.)

          All the pleadings in this case make clear that this is
primarily a financial dispute. Cross-Complainants have been given three
opportunities to identify non-economic injury, and they have not. The motion is
granted without leave to amend.

 

          D. Fourth, Seventh, and Eighth Causes of Action for
Fraudulent Transfer, Financial Elder Abuse, and Violations of the Electronic
Funds Transfer Act.

Cross-Complainants
did not oppose Citi’s motion as to the fourth, seventh, and eighth causes of
action. The motion is granted as to each, without leave to amend. As to the
fourth cause of action, Cross-Complainants allege in their pleading that Citi
did not transfer Edith’s funds outside of the bank, but continues to maintain
them “in a ‘special account’ ”. (See TAXC, ¶ 106.) Without a transfer, there
can be no fraudulent transfer.

As
to the seventh cause of action, Cross-Complainants have not pled facts
showing Young was an elder at relevant times. And they have only pled that
Edith’s property was taken, not Young’s.

As
to the eighth cause of action: the Court is persuaded that the Electronic Funds
Transfer Act applies to consumer accounts; the account in question was Edith’s
business account. More importantly, Cross-Complainants have not opposed this
portion of Citi’s motion, so the Court cannot ascertain what allegations in the
TAXC could establish liability under the Act.

The
motion is granted without leave to amend as to the fourth, seventh, and eighth
causes of action.

 

E.
Fifth and Ninth Causes of Action for Unfair Business Practices and Common Count

The
Court denied judgment on the pleadings as to the fifth cause of action in its
August 2023 Order. (See 08-07-2023 Order, 7:12 [“a claim under the UCL is
adequately alleged”].) Judgment remains denied.

As
to the ninth cause of action, the Court indicated in its prior order that a
common count would be pled when a contract or agreement was established. (Id.,
7:13-20.) As discussed above, an agreement has been pled. The motion fails as
to the ninth cause of action.

 

          VI.     CONCLUSION
AND ORDER

The motion is denied as to the first, fifth, sixth,
and ninth causes of action.

The motion is granted without leave to amend as to the
second, third, fourth, seventh, and eighth causes of action.

[Tentative] Order Granting Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel

I.                Background

This action alleges civil rights and breach of contract claims stemming from the lease of a vehicle from Defendant California Auto Resources Service, Inc. (“CARS”).  Plaintiff Boxiang Liu (“Liu”) alleges that he arranged for the lease of a vehicle, to be used during a planned three-month stay in California with his pregnant wife.  Liu alleges that he made payments to the WeChat account of Defendant Boan “Andy” Yan (“Yan”), an employee of CARS, to reserve the vehicle.  Upon arriving and taking delivery, Liu discovered defects and mechanical problems with the vehicle and notified CARS.  Liu negotiated with Yan via WeChat for return of the vehicle and refund of the payment, yet he never received the refund.  Moreover, during discussions, Yan used rude and vulgar language toward Liu, reflecting animus based on his gender identity and gender expression, and threatened to beat up Plaintiff Chengguang Wu, Liu’s agent.

Based on these allegations, the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges the following causes of action: (1) violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act, (2) violation of the Ralph Civil Rights Act, (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (4) breach of contract.  On June 15, 2023, Plaintiffs amended the Complaint to add Defendant Fengjiao Gao as Doe 1.

On August 16, 2024, Jeffrey C.P. Wang, Michael G. York, Kathleen E. Alparce, Jessica A. Crabbe, and WHGC, P.L.C. filed the instant Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel (“Motion”).  No opposition has been filed.

II.             Legal Standard

¿Code of Civil Procedure, section 284 states that “the attorney in an action…may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination, as follows: (1) upon the consent of both client and attorney…; (2) upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 284; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.)  The withdrawal request may be denied if it would cause an injustice or undue delay in proceeding; but the court's discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably.  (See Mandell v. Superior (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4; Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.)

In making a motion to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with procedures set forth in Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.  The motion must be made using mandatory forms:

1.     Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel directed to the client – (MC-051);

2.     Declaration “stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship” the reasons that the motion was brought (MC-052);

3.     Proposed Order (MC-053).

(Ibid.)  The forms must be timely filed and served on all parties who have appeared in the case.  (Ibid.)  If these documents are served on the client by mail, there must be a declaration stating either that the address where client was served is “the current residence or business address of the client” or “the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d)(1).)

III.           Discussion

Counsel Jeffrey C.P. Wang, Michael G. York, Kathleen E. Alparce, Jessica A. Crabbe, and WHGC, P.L.C. have properly filed a Notice of Motion and Motion (MC-051).  They have also filed a Declaration in Support of the Motion (MC-052) explaining that:

a) Client has breached a material term of an agreement with, or obligation to attorney relating to the representation, and attorney has given client a reasonable warning after the breach that attorney will withdraw unless client fulfills the agreement or performs the obligation. (Rules and Prof. Conduct, Rule 1.16(b)(5).)

b) By other conduct, the failure to cooperate, it has been rendered unreasonably difficult for attorney to carry out employment effectively.  (Rules of Prof. Conduct, Rule 1.16(b)(4).)

(MC-052 ¶ 2.)  Counsel have confirmed Defendant’s address within the past 30 days by email.  (Id. at ¶ 3b(1)(a).)  They have also filed a Proposed Order, Form MC-053.

All documents were served on Defendant and Plaintiffs by electronic service and mail.  (MC-051, p. 3; MC-052, p. 3; MC-053, p. 3.)

Given that Counsel have complied with all of the requirements for the Motion and there is no showing that withdrawal would cause injustice or undue delay in the proceedings, the Court grants the Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel.

The Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel as to Defendant California Auto Resources Services, Inc., filed by Jeffrey C.P. Wang, Michael G. York, Kathleen E. Alparce, Jessica A. Crabbe, and WHGC, P.L.C. is GRANTED.“After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)  The Order on this Motion will not be effective “until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on Plaintiff has been filed with the court.” (Ibid.)

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES


I.                INTRODUCTION

On October 11, 2022, Plaintiff Kevin Howe (“Howe”) filed an action against Defendant Raul Ramirez (“Ramirez”) for (1) breach of contract, (2) unjust enrichment, and (3) common count for money had and received.  Plaintiff alleges that the parties entered into two written agreements, “First Ramirez Note” and “Second Ramirez Note,” whereby Defendant borrowed money from Plaintiff with the promise to repay the amounts borrowed along with 10% interest and additional funds.  Defendant breached the agreements by failing to pay Plaintiff the total balance due in a timely manner.

On January 12, 2023, pursuant to Plaintiff’s request, the Court dismissed the entire action without prejudice.

On April 30, 2024, the Court vacated the dismissal entered on January 12, 2023, and entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $75,000 pursuant to a Settlement Agreement entered into by the parties.

On May 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Memorandum of Costs and on May 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed proof of serving the Notice of Entry of Judgment on Defendant.

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Attorney’s Fees (“Motion”).  No opposition has been filed.

The motion is granted.

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

Civil Code section 1717 states in pertinent part: “[i]n any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce¿that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the¿party¿prevailing¿on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to¿other¿costs.”  (Civ. Code, § 1717, subd. (a)).

A prevailing party in a lawsuit includes “the party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant.”  (Code of Civ. Proc., §1032, subd. (a)(4).)

A prevailing party in entitled to recover costs, including attorney’s fees, as a matter of right.  (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1032, subd. (a)(4), 1032, subd. (b), 1033.5.)  Furthermore, attorney’s fees are allowable as costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).)

The calculation of attorney’s fees in California begins with the “lodestar” method – multiplying the number of hours reasonably expended by the reasonable hourly rate.  A computation of time spent on a case and the reasonable value of that time is fundamental to a determination of an appropriate attorneys’ fee award.  The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)  Such an approach anchors the trial court’s analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney’s services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary.  (Id. at p. 48, fn. 23.)  After the trial court has performed the lodestar calculations, it shall consider whether the total award so calculated under all of the circumstances of the case is more than a reasonable amount and, if so, shall reduce the section 1717 award so that it is a reasonable figure.  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095-1096.)

As explained in Graciano v. Robinson Ford Sales, Inc.:

“[T]he lodestar is the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community; it may be adjusted by the court based on factors including, as relevant herein, (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award. [Citation.]  The purpose of such adjustment is to fix a fee at the fair market value for the particular action. In effect, the court determines, retrospectively, whether the litigation involved a contingent risk or required extraordinary legal skill justifying augmentation of the unadorned lodestar in order to approximate the fair market rate for such services. . . . This approach anchors the trial court's analysis to an objective determination of the value of the attorney's services, ensuring that the amount awarded is not arbitrary.” [Internal citations and internal quotation marks omitted.]

((2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 140, 154.)  “It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion.  [Citations.]  The value of legal services performed in a case is a matter in which the trial court has its own expertise. . . . The trial court makes its determination after consideration of a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.  [Citations.]”  (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623-624.)

No specific findings reflecting the court’s calculations are required.  The record need only show that the attorney fees were awarded according to the “lodestar” or “touchstone” approach.  The court’s focus in evaluating the facts should be to provide a fee award reasonably designed to completely compensate attorneys for the services provided.  The starting point for this determination is the attorney’s time records.  (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of Calif. State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 395-397 [verified time records entitled to credence absent clear indication they are erroneous].)  However, California case law permits fee awards in the absence of detailed time sheets.  (Sommers v. Erb (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1644, 1651; Dunk v. Ford Motor Co. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 1794, 1810; Nightingale v. Hyundai Motor America (1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 99, 103.)  An experienced trial judge is in a position to assess the value of the professional services rendered in his or her court.  (Ibid.; Serrano, 20 Cal.3d 25 at 49.)

III.          ANALYSIS

            Plaintiff moves for a court order determining that he is the prevailing party in the instant action and the Settlement Agreement authorizes an award of attorney’s fees. Moreover, Plaintiff seeks attorney’s fees in the amount of $5,989.50 for 12.1 hours of attorney time in enforcing the Settlement Agreement.

            According to Plaintiff, the parties settled the instant dispute in December 2022.  (Green Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 1.)  The Settlement Agreement contained the following provision: “In the event of any Party’s breach of this Agreement, any other Party will be entitled to reasonably [sic] attorneys' fees and costs incurred in connection with the enforcement of this Agreement.”  (Ibid.)  Defendant breached the Agreement by failing to make payments toward the settlement amount, thus, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Enforce Settlement.  (Howe Decl. ¶ 3.)  On April 30, 2024, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant pursuant to the Settlement Agreement.

            Plaintiff argues that he is entitled to an award of attorney’s fees because the Settlement Agreement authorized reasonable attorney’s fees and costs to be awarded to the non-breaching party and he prevailed in enforcing the Settlement Agreement following Defendant’s breach.  Plaintiff also argues that the attorney’s fees requested are reasonable because they were actually charged by Plaintiff’s counsel to enforce the Settlement Agreement and the time spent was necessary, as demonstrated in the billing spreadsheet filed with the Declaration of Abraham L. Niman.  (Niman Decl. ¶¶ 2-3, Ex. 2.)

            The Court finds that judgment was entered in favor of Plaintiff and thus, Plaintiff is the prevailing party in the instant action.  Moreover, the Settlement Agreement did authorize an award of attorney’s fees and costs.  Having reviewed the billing spreadsheet presented by Plaintiff’s counsel, the Court finds the billing rate and time spent in enforcing the Settlement Agreement to be reasonable.

            Thus, the Court grants Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees in the amount of $5,989.50.

IV.          CONCLUSION AND ORDER

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff Kevin Howe’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is GRANTED in the amount of $5,989.50.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S DEPOSITION


I.                INTRODUCTION

            On April 7, 2023, Plaintiff Cynthia Ricks (“Ricks”) filed an action against Defendant L.A. Downtown Medical Center, Inc.[1] (“Medical Center”) and Does 1 to 10 for medical negligence.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant breached its duty as a healthcare provider and as a result, while she was a patient at Defendant’s facility, she slipped and fell on a foreign substance in the cafeteria incurring serious injuries.  Defendant filed an Answer on July 21, 2023. 

On August 15, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel the Deposition of Plaintiff Cynthia Ricks.

The motion is granted.

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (a) provides:

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”

            The motion shall “(1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice” and “(2) be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).)

 

 

III.           ANALYSIS

Defendant moves for an order compelling the deposition of Plaintiff Cynthia Ricks on the ground that she did not appear at several noticed depositions or serve any objections prior to the depositions.

On July 21, 2023, Defendant electronically served the first Notice of Testimony and Records Deposition of Plaintiff Cynthia Ricks set for October 25, 2023.  (Kim Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Defense counsel requested alternative dates to depose Plaintiff in case Plaintiff or her counsel were unavailable.  (Id. at ¶ 4, Ex. B.)  On October 20, 2023, Defendant electronically served the second Notice of Testimony and Records Deposition set for November 28, 2023.  (Id. at ¶ 5, Ex. C.)  On November 27, 2023, Defendant reached out to Plaintiff’s counsel to confirm Plaintiff’s deposition set for November 28, 2023.  (Id. at ¶ 6, Ex. D.)  She was informed that Plaintiff would not be appearing at the deposition, thus, the parties agreed on a new date for the deposition in January 2024.  (Ibid.)  On November 27, 2023, Defendant electronically served the third Notice of Testimony and Records Deposition set for January 23, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 7, Ex. E.) 

On January 11, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel informed defense counsel that Plaintiff was unable to talk and only her daughter had information regarding the circumstances.  (Id. at ¶ 8, Ex. F.)  Defense counsel requested the deposition of Plaintiff’s daughter.  (Ibid.)  On March 4, 2024, Defendant electronically served the fourth Notice of Testimony and Records Deposition set for March 25, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 9, Ex. G.)  On March 25, 2024, Plaintiff’s daughter was deposed and testified that she was not aware of any physical or mental condition preventing Plaintiff from speaking at a deposition; she was unavailable because she was in Las Vegas, Nevada.  (Id. at ¶ 10, Ex. H.)  On April 22, 2024, Defendant electronically served the fifth Notice of Testimony and Records Deposition set for June 6, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 11, Ex. I.) 

On May 30, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel informed defense counsel that Plaintiff was in the hospital for an uncertain period.  (Id. at ¶ 12, Ex. J.)  On July 2, 2024, Defendant electronically served the sixth Notice of Testimony and Records Deposition set for August 1, 2024.  (Id. ¶ 13, Ex. K.)  On July 31, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel requested a link to allow for the deposition to be taken remotely and Defendant provided the link.  (Id. at ¶ 14, Ex. L.)  Plaintiff did not serve an objection to the deposition set for August 1, 2024.  (Id. at ¶ 15.)  On August 1, 2024, Plaintiff failed to appear at the deposition.  (Id. at ¶ 16, Ex. M.)  The court reporter prepared and certified a certificate of non-appearance.  (Ibid.)  Following Plaintiff’s failure to appear at the August 1, 2024, deposition, defense counsel left multiple phone messages and sent emails to Plaintiff’s counsel, but she did not receive a response.  (Id. at ¶¶ 18-20, Ex. N.)  As of the date of the Motion, Defendant has not received a response from Plaintiff or Plaintiff’s counsel.  (Id. at ¶ 21.)

Defendant argues that good cause exists for an order compelling Plaintiff’s attendance at a deposition given that she has failed to appear at six properly noticed depositions.  After Plaintiff failed to appear at the deposition set for August 1, 2024, defense counsel attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel but did not receive any responses.  In opposition, Plaintiff’s counsel states that he has “had difficulties in getting [his] client's cooperation for her deposition. She is an elderly woman with with [sic] Medical Issues.”  (Yeager Decl. ¶ 2.)

The Court finds that Defendant has presented sufficient evidence of his efforts to schedule Plaintiff’s deposition and Plaintiff’s continuous failure to attend the noticed depositions without serving valid objections.  Defendant has also demonstrated that he has attempted to resolve the issue with Plaintiff’s counsel prior to filing the instant Motion.  Plaintiff’s opposition does not dispute Defendant’s claims.  However, the Court notes that Defendant has not complied with Code Civil Procedure section 2025.450, subdivision (b)(2), which requires the moving party to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”  As demonstrated by previous deposition notices, Defendant intends to demand production of various documents.

Based on the evidence presented, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s attendance at a properly noticed deposition within 30 days of notice of the Court’s order.  The Court does not make a ruling regarding any demands for production of documents.

IV.           CONCLUSION AND ORDER

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant L.A. Downtown Medical Center, Inc.’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Deposition is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear at a properly noticed deposition within 30 days of notice of the Court’s order.

Defendant L.A. Downtown Medical Center, Inc. is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 23AHCV01728    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE AND VACATE AND ENTER ANOTHER AND DIFFERENT JUDGMENT


I.                INTRODUCTION

            On July 27, 2023, Plaintiff Deeson Pan (“Plaintiff” or “Pan”), in propria persona, filed an action against Defendant Xuesong Guan (“Defendant” or “Guan”) and Does 1 through 10 for (1) breach of contract, (2) constructive fraud, (3) negligent misrepresentation of facts, (4) unfair business practice in violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200, et seq., and (5) unfair competition law, Business and Professions Code section 17204.  The action arises out of an alleged dispute related to the property located at 1420 S. Del Mar Ave, Unit #6, San Gabriel, California 91776.  Plaintiff alleges that he is the rightful owner of the property, and that Defendant has rented the property and been collecting and retaining rent from tenants residing at the property without Plaintiff’s approval.

On August 24, 2023, Defendant, in propria persona, filed an Answer to the Complaint.  After retaining counsel, on June 4, 2024, Defendant filed an Amended Answer with the Court’s approval.

On July 15, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Have Requests for Admission Deemed Admitted as to Plaintiff, filed on April 29, 2024.  The Court found that Defendant had served Plaintiff with Requests for Admission on January 23, 2024, but Plaintiff failed to serve any responses.  Thus, the Court granted the Motion and Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,500.

On August 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Sanctions.

On the same day, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Set Aside and Vacate and Enter Another and Different Judgment in response to the Court’s July 15, 2024, order.  Defendant filed an Opposition on August 29, 2024, and Plaintiff filed a Reply on September 12, 2024.

The Motion is denied.

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

According to Code of Civil Procedure section 663,

A judgment or decree, when based upon a decision by the court, or the special verdict of a jury, may, upon motion of the party aggrieved, be set aside and vacated by the same court, and another and different judgment entered, for either of the following causes, materially affecting the substantial rights of the party and entitling the party to a different judgment:

1.     Incorrect or erroneous legal basis for the decision, not consistent with or not supported by the facts; and in such case when the judgment is set aside, the statement of decision shall be amended and corrected.

2.     A judgment or decree not consistent with or not supported by the special verdict.

III.           ANALYSIS

Plaintiff moves for an order to set aside and vacate the judgment entered on July 15, 2024, to amend and correct the statement of decision filed on July 24, 2024, and to enter another and different judgment, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 663.  Plaintiff argues that the court’s decision is legally incorrect and/or erroneous and not supported by the facts, and these errors have materially affected Plaintiff’s substantial rights.

The Court notes that Plaintiff has not filed a sworn declaration attesting to the facts presented in the Amended Memorandum of Points and Authorities or authenticating the exhibits filed along with the Motion.

In the Memorandum, Plaintiff argues that on February 20, 2024, he submitted responses to the Requests for Admission (“RFAs”) served by Defendant, two days before the deadline.  Plaintiff has attached a copy of the responses with a proof of service indicating that the responses were served by mail and email on Defendant at 4907 Willmonte Ave, Temple City, California 91780, on February 20, 2024.  Despite receiving the responses prior to the deadline, Defendant filed a Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted.  Plaintiff states that as a self-represented litigant navigating the complexities of the legal process, he was not aware of the need to verify the responses.  Defendant’s attorney had a professional obligation to request verified responses or file a motion to compel allowing Plaintiff an opportunity to correct the mistake.  Defendant’s “decision to file a Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions Admitted was premature and unnecessarily punitive, disregarding the principles of fairness and due process.”  (Amended Mem. p. 2.)

Plaintiff argues that the Court’s judgment should be set aside because the Court incorrectly found that Plaintiff had failed to respond to the RFAs in a timely manner.  The Court failed to account for Plaintiff’s status as a self-represented litigant and inadvertent omission of the verification.  The Court prematurely granted the Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted without considering a less severe alternative, allowing Plaintiff an opportunity to correct the verification error.  Thus, the Court’s decision was legally erroneous and inconsistent with principles of fairness.  Finally, the Court erroneously imposed sanctions as Plaintiff’s omission was unintentional and could have been cured if provided the opportunity.

            In opposition, Defendant presents the following evidence.  Defendant’s former counsel served Plaintiff with RFAs on January 23, 2024.  (Andion Decl. ¶ 3.)  Former counsel was substituted out on February 7, 2024, and new counsel was substituted in on February 27, 2024.  (Ibid.)  After being retained, new counsel, James Andion, learned that Plaintiff had not responded to the RFAs and filed the Motion to Have Requests for Admission Deemed Admitted.  (Id. ¶¶ 3-4.)  Plaintiff did not respond to the motion and at the hearing on July 15, 2024, he did not raise the claim that he had served the responses, thus, the Court granted the Motion.  (Id. at ¶ 4.)  On August 27, 2024, Defendant discovered that she had received an email from Plaintiff on February 20, 2024, with PDF files containing discovery responses.  (Id. ¶ 8.)  The responses were not verified and only contained objections, and the proof of service indicated that the responses had also been mailed to an address that was not Defendant’s address of record.  (Id. at ¶¶ 8-9.)  Defendant denies that she received these responses by mail.  (Ibid.)

            Defendant also requests attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $1,500 for expenses incurred in responding to the instant Motion and appearing at the hearing.  (Andion Decl. ¶ 11.)

            Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s failure to serve compliant responses was due to his own neglect.  Moreover, Plaintiff did not file an opposition or raise any arguments at the hearing on July 15, 2024.  If Plaintiff had indeed intended to comply with his discovery obligations, he would have responded to the motion and served another copy of his discovery responses.  Finally, Defendant argues that setting aside the Court’s July 15, 2024, order would unfairly prejudice Defendant by reopening issues that would lead to increased litigation costs, prolonged resolution of the case, and impairment of the discovery proceedings.

            In his Reply, Plaintiff argues that objections to discovery do not need to be verified.  Moreover, Defendant was aware of Plaintiff’s responses prior to retaining new counsel and instead of filing a motion to provide verified responses, Defendant deceived the Court by stating that she did not receive any responses.  Plaintiff also argues that pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473, subdivision (a), and 576, the Court may make amendments in the furtherance of justice.  Defendant’s admission that she received the responses reflects malice and shows that she intentionally sought to sanction Plaintiff.

The Court makes the following findings.

First, Plaintiff’s motion is not supported by any evidence as he has not filed a sworn declaration attesting to the facts presented in the Amended Memorandum of Points and Authorities or authenticating the exhibits filed along with the Motion.

Second, Plaintiff improperly presents new legal arguments in the Reply, claiming that the Court should vacate the judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 473, subdivision (a), and 576.  “Points raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument.”  (American Drug Stores, Inc. v. Stroh (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1446, 1453.)

Third, given that the responses to the RFAs only contained objections, they did not need to be verified pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.240, subdivision (a).

Fourth, if the Court were to consider the exhibits filed by Plaintiff, it would be clear that Plaintiff did not properly serve the responses to the RFAs.  Plaintiff sent the responses directly to Defendant by email and by mail to 4907 Willmonte Ave, Temple City, CA 91780.  However, between February 7, 2024, and February 27, 2024, Defendant was self-represented, and the Substitution of Attorney form indicates that Defendant’s address was 1420 S. Del Mar, San Gabriel, CA 91776.  Furthermore, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1010.6, a self-represented party may only be served by electronic transmission after expressly consenting to receive service by email.  Plaintiff has not presented any evidence that Defendant had consented to service by email.

Fifth, the Court notes that a party moving to deem RFAs admitted is under no obligation to meet and confer regarding missing or defective responses and Plaintiff has not cited any authority to show otherwise.  (See Code of Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (b).)

            Thus, Plaintiff’s argument that Defendant proceeded to file the motion to deem requests for admission admitted, despite receiving responses, is without merit.  Plaintiff had an opportunity to file an opposition to the motion demonstrating that he acted with substantial justification in failing to properly serve the discovery responses, but despite being aware of the Motion and attending the hearing, Plaintiff did not raise any opposing arguments.  Finally, as to Plaintiff’s argument that the Court failed to account for Plaintiff’s self-represented status, the Court reminds Plaintiff of the following well-established principle of law:

"Under the law one may be his own attorney if he wants to be. A layman with resources who insists upon exercising the privilege of representing himself must expect and receive the same treatment as if represented by an attorney -- no different, no better, no worse."

Monastero v. Los Angeles Transit Co. (1955) 131 Cal. App. 2d 156.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has not presented sufficient evidence to show that there was an “incorrect or erroneous legal basis” for the Court’s ruling or that the ruling was “not consistent with or not supported by the facts.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 663.)  Plaintiff’s Motion is denied.

Furthermore, the Court does not find that the instant Motion was filed in bad faith or with the intention to cause delay and denies Defendant’s request for additional sanctions against Plaintiff.

IV.           CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff Deeson Pan’s Motion to Set Aside and Vacate and Enter Another and Different Judgment is DENIED.
