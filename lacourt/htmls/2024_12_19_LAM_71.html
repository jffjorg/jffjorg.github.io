DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.   

Case Number: 21STCV32398    Hearing Date: December 19, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DCK SALES, CORPORATION, 

 

         vs.

 

GRAYMILLS CORPORATION.

 Case No.:  21STCV32398

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  December 19, 2024

 

Plaintiff DCK Sales, Corporation’s motion for attorneys’ fees and costs is granted in the reduced total amount of $225,378.25, comprised of $225,378.25 in attorneys’ fees and $0.00 in total expenses.

 

Plaintiff DCK Sales, Corporation (“DCK”) (“Plaintiff”) moves for an order awarding it attorneys’ fees in the total amount of $901,513, comprised of $890,013 in attorneys’ fees, including $11,500 incurred on the instant motion, and costs in the amount of $35,308 against Defendant Graymills Corporation (“Graymills”) (“Defendant”).  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2; Notice of Ex Parte Application, pgs. 2-3; C.C.P. §§1032, 1033.5; 820 Ill. Comp. Stat. Ann. 120/1 et seq.)  Plaintiff moves on the basis that this Court already ruled in its August 7, 2023, Order (“ISRA Order”) that the Illinois Sales Representative Act (“ISRA”) applies in this action, and Defendant has admitted to non-payment of commissions owed to Plaintiff during the course of the action and accepted liability under the ISRA in the $120,000 998 Offer and judgment entered on July 17, 2024 (“Judgment”).  (Notice Motion, pg. 2.)  Plaintiff moves on the basis that both the 998 Offer and the Judgment expressly preserve its right to seek its fees and costs in this matter.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.)

 

CRC Violations

CRC Rule 3.1354(b) provides in part, “[a]ll written objections to evidence must be served and filed separately from the other papers in support of or in opposition to the motion. Objections to specific evidence must be referenced by the objection number in the right column of a separate statement in opposition or reply to a motion, but the objections must not be restated or reargued in the separate statement. Each written objection must be numbered consecutively . . ..”  (CRC Rule 3.1354(b), emphasis added.)

Plaintiff fails to consecutively number its 73-pages long evidentiary objections in violation of CRC Rule 3.1354(b).  The Court noted in its July 21, 2023, ruling on ISRA Order this exact same CRC violation by Plaintiff.  (See 8/7/23 Ruling on Submitted Matter, pgs. 1-2 of 7.)  Plaintiff has the audacity to cite to CRC Rule 3.1113(d) in its reply regarding Defendant’s overlong opposition papers.  (Reply, pg. 2 n.1.)  The Court in its discretion does not consider Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to (a) the Declaration of Gerald G. Knapton (“Knapton”), (b) the Declaration of Andrew Bassak (“Bassak”), (c) the Declaration of Kristen Shields (“Shields”), and certain exhibits thereto. 

CRC Rule 3.113(d) provides, in part, “[e]xcept in a summary judgment or summary adjudication motion, no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 15 pages. . . . The page limit does not include the caption page, the notice of motion and motion, exhibits, declarations, attachments, the table of contents, the table of authorities, or the proof of service.”  (CRC Rule 3.113(d), emphasis added.)

Defendant’s 17-pages long opposition is in violation of CRC Rule 3.113(d) because it exceeds the 15-page limit, and Defendant did not apply to the court ex parte for permission to file a longer memorandum, as allowed in CRC Rule 3.113(e).  The Court in its discretion does not consider the pages in excess of the 15-page limit in Defendant’s opposition.

 

           Request for Judicial Notice

           Defendant’s 12/6/24 request for judicial notice of the ISRA (820 ILCS 120/01-120/03) (D-RJN, Exh. 1) is granted.

 

Background

On September 1, 2021, Plaintiff filed its operative Complaint against Defendant alleging eleven causes of action.  On April 3, 2024, Plaintiff accepted Defendant’s C.C.P. §998 offer of judgment for $120,000 (“§998 Offer”).  This Court entered Judgment on July 17, 2024 (“Judgment”).

On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion.  Defendant filed its opposition on December 6, 2024.  Plaintiff filed its reply on December 12, 2024.

On December 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application regarding the instant motion.

 

Discussion

This Court ruled in the ISRA Ruling that Illinois law and the ISRA apply to the instant action.  (See 8/7/23 Ruling on Submitted Matter.)

This Court stated in the Judgment Pursuant to C.C.P. §998: “Plaintiff DCK’s entitlement to and the amount of any recoverable statutory costs pursuant to Code of Procedure section 1032, including reasonable attorneys’ fees, if any, shall be determined by the court after entry of this judgment in accordance with applicable law and according to proof.”  (7/17/24 Judgment, pg. 2.)

C.C.P. §1032(a)(4) provides:

“Prevailing party” includes the party with a net monetary recovery, a defendant in whose favor a dismissal is entered, a defendant where neither plaintiff nor defendant obtains any relief, and a defendant as against those plaintiffs who do not recover any relief against that defendant. If any party recovers other than monetary relief and in situations other than as specified, the “prevailing party” shall be as determined by the court, and under those circumstances, the court, in its discretion, may allow costs or not and, if allowed, may apportion costs between the parties on the same or adverse sides pursuant to rules adopted under Section 1034.

 

(C.C.P. §1032(a)(4).)

The ISRA provides that a principal that fails to pay commissions due “shall pay the sales representative’s reasonable attorney’s fees and court costs.”  (820 Ill. Comp. Stat. Ann. 120/3; see also C.C.P. §1033.5(10)(B).)  Under Illinois law, “a prevailing party, for purposes of awarding attorney fees, is one that is successful on a significant issue and achieves some benefit in bringing suit. A party that receives judgment in his favor is usually considered the prevailing party.”  (J.B. Esker & Sons, Inc. v. Cle-Pa’s Partnership (2001) 325 Ill.App.3d 276, 280-281.)

Here, the Judgment entered by this Court states:

Plaintiff DCK shall have and recover from Defendant Graymills the sum of One Hundred Twenty Thousand Dollars and No Cents ($120,000.00) comprised of: (a) $3,350.00 in commissions due from Defendant Graymills to Plaintiff DCK upon Graymills’ termination of DCK as an independent sales representative for Graymills in 2020, which amount Graymills acknowledges was not timely paid to DCK as required by 820 ILCS 120/2, and (b) $116,650.00, in compromise of all of Plaintiff DCK’s remaining disputed claims for unpaid commissions and interest over the ten year period from September 1, 2011 to September 1, 2021. This judgment amount is in full satisfaction of all of Plaintiff DCK’s claims against Graymills, including DCK’s claims for damages any kind (including DCK’s claim for exemplary damages per 820 ILCS 120/3), restitution, and pre-judgment interest.

 

(7/17/24 Judgment, pgs. 1-2, emphasis added.)

           Plaintiff recovered $3,350.00 in commissions under its ISRA cause of action, for which it now moves for attorneys’ fees, and $116,650.00 in compromise of Plaintiff’s remaining claims.  Accordingly, Plaintiff is the prevailing party in this action and is entitled to an award of attorneys’ fees and costs.

 

Reasonable Fees

To calculate a lodestar amount, the Court must first determine the reasonableness of the hourly rates sought by Plaintiff’s counsel. The Supreme Court of California has concluded that a reasonable hourly lodestar rate is the prevailing rate for private attorneys “conducting non-contingent litigation of the same type.”  (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133; see also Merchandise National Bank of Chicago v. Scanlon (1980) 86 Ill.App.3d 719, 731 [adopting lodestar computation].)

Plaintiff’s Counsel provides the following hourly rates for attorneys who worked on this case: (1) Evan Pitchford ($450.00/hour to $525/hour); (2) Shaahin Jafari-Moafi ($325.00/hour); (3) Derrick Chu ($375.00/hour); (4) Eric S. Engel ($550.00/hour); (5) Alec Pressley ($325.00/hour); (6) Raef Cogan ($350.00/hour); (7) Mark D. Kremer ($550.00/hour); (8) John Conkle ($550.00/hour).  (See Decl. of Pitchford ¶¶5-6, 10, Exhs. A-B.)[1]  Plaintiff fails to sufficiently demonstrate his counsel’s hourly rates are reasonable in their community of practice in their specialized area of law.  (Decl. of Pitchford ¶¶5-6, 10, Exhs. A-B.)  Plaintiff does not provide the training and experience of counsel other than Evan Pitchford and Eric Engel.  (See Decl. of Pitchford ¶¶5-6; Decl. of Engel ¶¶2-3.)

 

Billed Hours

The party seeking fees and costs bears the burden to show “the fees incurred were allowable, were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation, and were reasonable in amount.”  (Nightingale v. Hyundai Motor America (1994) 31 Cal.App.4th 99, 104.) To satisfy this burden, evidence and descriptions of billable tasks must be presented in sufficient detail, enabling the court to evaluate whether the case was overstaffed, the time attorneys spent on specific claims, and the reasonableness of the hours expended.  (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 486-487.)

Plaintiff’s counsel provides that the total number of hours billed to litigate this matter was $1,932.10 hours.  (Decl. of Pitchford, Exh. B at pg. 49 of 49.)  The amount of billed hours is wildly excessive for a matter that did not involve a single demurrer, motion for summary judgment, or any trial preparation.  Nearly 2,000 hours expended on primarily discovery disputes and meeting and conferring regarding discovery is not reasonable in amount.  A total of nine noticed motions, excluding ex parte applications and the instant motion, were filed in the instant action in the span of this case’s 951 days of existence.

Further, the instant motion fails to provide that its counsel’s hourly rates are reasonable in their community of practice in their specialized area of law and obfuscates the hours incurred on the ISRA cause of action.

Plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees mirrors that of the plaintiffs in Guillory v. Hill.  In Guillory v. Hill, the plaintiffs prevailed against the Orange County Sheriff’s Department on their civil rights claims and were awarded less than $5,400.  (Guillory v. Hill (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 802, 806.)  Despite the amount of this award, Plaintiffs filed a motion for attorneys’ fees seeking nearly $3.8 million which the trial court denied.  (Id.)  On appeal, the Court of Appeal assessed whether the fee was reasonable based on the degree of success, the amount of fees requested and the credibility of the supporting evidence.  (Id. at pg. 811.) The Guillory plaintiff argued that because it did not seek “any specific amount” in the complaint, the $5,400 award demonstrated their success.  (Id. at pg. 814.)  The Court of Appeal disagreed given that the Guillory plaintiffs sought nearly $1 million dollars at the outset.  (Id.)  As to the amount of fees and supporting evidence, the Guillory Court agreed that substantial evidence supported the trial court’s conclusion that the billing records showed “overinclusive, unculled, redundant, redacted, and padded billing entries, thereby ‘destroying the credibility of the submission and . . . justifying a severe reduction.’” (Id. at pgs. 814-815.) Thus, the Guillory Court affirmed the lower court’s rejection of plaintiffs’ requests for attorney’s fees.  (Id. at pg. 816.)

Here, as in Guillory, Plaintiff requests nearly $1 million in attorneys’ fees and costs despite only recovering $3,350 under the ISRA.  Most of Plaintiff’s billing entries are unrelated to the ISRA.  For example, 79 entries were for research unrelated to the ISRA (DCK Appendix of Declarations, Exh. A at pgs. 24-25), 112 entries were for managing and organizing documents (id. at pgs. 26-27), 101 entries were for analysis and strategic planning unrelated to the ISRA (id. at pgs. 32-33), 264 entries were for preparing or responding to discovery (id. at pgs. 45-49), and 225 entries were just for meeting and conferring regarding discovery issues (id. at pgs. 57-60).  None of these narrative entries make any reference whatsoever to the ISRA nor were they labelled as ISRA-related work by the code description.  Plaintiff failed to allocate the entries specifically related to the ISRA to which it alleges it is entitled.  Per the ISRA’s express language, Plaintiff can only recover fees related to the ISRA itself—namely any failure to pay commissions.  Therefore, Plaintiff failed to meet its burden to demonstrate these fees were reasonable or related to the single ISRA claim it succeeded on.

Nonetheless, Defendant’s resistance to providing Plaintiff with Code-compliant discovery responses were also a major factor in the unnecessarily excessive litigation in this matter.  “A defendant cannot litigate tenaciously and then be heard to complain about the time necessarily spent by the plaintiff in response.”  (Peak-Las Positas Partners v. Bollag (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 101, 114; Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 638.)

Balancing these factors, the Court reduces elects to grant Plaintiff’s request for fees but reduces them by 75%.  Accordingly, Plaintiff’s requested fees are granted in the reduced amount of $225,378.25.

 

Costs

CRC Rule 3.1700 states, “A prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of the notice of entry of judgment or dismissal by the clerk . . ..” (CRC 3.1700.)  It is well settled law in California that “the omission of an item from a cost memo may be regarded as a waiver of the prevailing party’s claim to that item.”  (Gorman v. Tassajara Development Corp. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 44, 70; see also Hotchkiss v. Smith (1895) 108 Cal. 285, 286-287[“it was incumbent upon plaintiff to include and claim [for fees and expenses] in her cost bill, or they were waived”].)

On August 1, 2024, Plaintiff submitted a memorandum of costs for the total amount of $19,107.63.  (8/1/24 Memorandum of Costs.) 

           Plaintiff now requests an additional $35,308 in costs not explicitly requested in Plaintiff’s memorandum of costs.  (Compare 8/1/24 Memorandum of Costs with Notice of Motion, pg. 2 n.1.)  Plaintiff provides no explanation for why such costs were omitted from its memorandum of costs and presents no substantive argument in its moving papers pertaining exclusively to these $35,308 in costs.

           Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for costs separate from those requested in its memorandum of costs filed on August 1, 2024, is denied.

 

Conclusion

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion for attorneys’ fees is granted in the reduced total amount of $225,378.25, comprised of $225,378.25 in attorneys’ fees and $0.00 in costs.

Defendant Hollywood Foreign Press Association’s motion to compel responses from pro per Plaintiff Husam Asi to its Request for Production of Documents (Set One) is granted.  Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified responses pursuant to C.C.P. §2031.210 and to produce the documents, electronically stored information, and/or other things requested without objections within 20 days of this ruling.

 

Defendant Hollywood Foreign Press Association (“HFPA”) (“Defendant”) moves to compel responses from pro per Plaintiff Husam Asi (“Asi”) (“Plaintiff”) to its Request for Production (Set One) (“RFP”).  (Notice MTC, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§2031.010, et seq.)  Further, the HFPA requests an order compelling Plaintiff to produce all communications he had with third party David Quinto (“Quinto”) about this case, which Plaintiff is improperly withholding on the basis that Quinto is an attorney (though not his attorney).  (Notice MTC, pg. 2.)

 

Background

On April 30, 2024, Defendant propounded RFP on Plaintiff.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶2, Exh. 1.)  To date, Plaintiff has not provided written responses or objections to the Requests; however, he did produce documents on May 16 and June 15, 2024, together with a spreadsheet titled “lawyers_email_logs 2022-2024.”  (Decl. of Ellison ¶3, Exh. 2.) 

Defendant filed the instant motion on September 16, 2024.  Plaintiff filed his opposition on December 6, 2024.  Defendant filed its reply on December 12, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

“The party claiming the privilege has the burden of establishing the preliminary facts necessary to support its exercise, i.e., a communication made in the course of an attorney-client relationship.”  (Costco Wholesale Corp. v. Superior Court (2009) 219 Cal.4th 725, 733.)  This is done by providing a privilege log, which contains sufficient information for judicial evaluation of a claim of privilege.  (Best Products, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 1181, 1188.)  “Once that party establishes facts necessary to support a prima facie claim of privilege, the communication is presumed to have been made in confidence and the opponent of the claim of privilege has the burden of proof to establish the communication was not confidential or that the privilege does not for other reasons apply.”  (Costco Wholesale Corp., 219 Cal.4th at pg. 733.)

 

Discussion

Having reviewed Defendant’s motion to compel responses from Plaintiff the Court rules as follows: 

Defendant’s motion to compel RFP is granted pursuant to C.C.P. §2031.300.  Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified responses pursuant to C.C.P. §2031.210 and to produce the documents, electronically stored information, and/or other things requested without objections within 20 days of this ruling.

Plaintiff cannot withhold his communications with Quinto on the grounds of the attorney-client privilege because Plaintiff concedes that they never had an attorney-client relationship and Plaintiff never intended to hire Quintos.  (Decl. of Ellison ¶5, Exh. 5; see People v. Navarro (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 146, 157 [information obtained by lawyer in conversation with person who is seeking legal assistance and intends on hiring the lawyer is privileged even if actual employment does not result].)  Plaintiff bears the burden of establishing his privilege claim.  (See Titmas v. Superior Court (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 738, 745.)  Plaintiff has not met this burden.  Plaintiff’s communications with Quinto, reflected on Plaintiff’s privilege log, are not privileged because there was no attorney-client relationship between Quinto and Plaintiff, and no such relationship was contemplated.

Accordingly, Defendant’s motion to compel responses is granted.

         

Conclusion

Defendant’s motion to compel further responses from Plaintiff to RFP is granted.  Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified responses pursuant to C.C.P. §2031.210 and to produce the documents, electronically stored information, and/or other things requested without objections within 20 days of this ruling.

A.   Demurrer

Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party must meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading and obviate the need for filing the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a).)

The demurring party shall file and serve with the demurrer a declaration stating either of the following: (A) The means by which the demurring party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer, and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the objections raised in the demurrer. (B) That the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Defendants’ counsel declares that on October 8, 2024, she met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel by videoconference to discuss the instant demurrer, and the parties did not reach an agreement to resolve the issues presented in the instant demurrer.  (See Decl. of Goldsmith ¶2.)  Defendants’ counsel’s declaration is sufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a).  Therefore, the Court will consider Defendants’ demurrer.

Plaintiff alleges IP Stack is a visitor identification service.  (FAC ¶59.)  Plaintiff alleges the Website sends a request to ipstack.com to harvest the visitor’s IP address.  (SAC ¶21.)

Plaintiff alleges that pursuant to Section 9.5 of the Partnership Agreement, the Partners agreed to perform all other acts and to execute and deliver all. other documents as may be necessary or appropriate to carry out the intent and purpose of this Partnership Agreement.  (SAC ¶21.) 

Plaintiff alleges that pursuant to Section 9.8 of the Partnership Agreement, the Partners agreed that if any litigation is commenced between the Partners or their representatives concerning any provision of the Partnership Agreement or the rights and duties of the Partners, the prevailing party shall be entitled to its/his reasonable attorney’s fees in addition to any such relief granted.  (SAC ¶22.) 

Plaintiff alleges that on or about September 19, 2005, it was agreed between the Partners that Plaintiff, No Soo Kim, individually, would be substituted in place of Yongsusan USA, Inc. as a Partner under the Partnership Agreement, which was endorsed in writing.  (SAC ¶23, Exh. 1.)

 

Summary of Demurrer

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s 4th and 6th causes of action on the basis that Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action for conversion and unjust enrichment, respectively.  (Demurrer, pgs. 4-8.)

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Failure to State a Cause of Action

Conversion (4th COA)

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.”  (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.)

“It is not necessary that there be a manual taking of the property; it is only necessary to show an assumption of control or ownership over the property, or that the alleged converter has applied the property to his own use.”  (Shopoff & Cavallo LLP v. Hyon (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 1489, 1507.)

“[C]onversion is a strict liability tort. It does not require bad faith, knowledge, or even negligence; it requires only that the defendant have intentionally done the act depriving the plaintiff of his or her rightful possession.”  (Voris v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1141, 1158.)

“‘Money cannot be the subject of a cause of action for conversion unless there is a specific, identifiable sum involved, such as where an agent accepts a sum of money to be paid to another and fails to make the payment.’ A ‘generalized claim for money [is] not actionable as conversion.’”  (PCO, Inc. v. Christensen, Miller, Fink, Jacobs, Glaser, Weil & Shapiro, LLP (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 384, 395, internal citations omitted.)

Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that commencing in 2020 and continuing to the present, Defendants KGR and Kenneth unilaterally, and without Plaintiff’s prior consent or knowledge, took Plaintiffs 50% interest in the Partnership and have refused to pay Plaintiff his 50% of the Restaurant’s revenue.  (SAC ¶42.)

Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that in or around May of 2020 and February of 2021, Defendants KGR and Kenneth received the sum of $120,000.00 and $166,368.000, respectively, from the government’s Paycheck Protection Program to assist business owners keep their workforce employed during the Covid pandemic (collectively, “PPP Funds”).  (SAC ¶43.)

Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that in or around May of 2020 and May of 2021, Defendants KGR and Kenneth received the sum of $1,424,527.00 from the Small Business Administration’s Restaurant Revitalization Fund for emergency assistance (“RRR Funds”).  (SAC ¶44.)

Plaintiff alleges the Restaurant’s revenue, PPP Funds, and RRR Funds were Partnership assets which Plaintiff had a 50% interest in.  (SAC ¶45.)  Plaintiff alleges that without informing Plaintiff that they had received the PPP Funds and RRR Funds, along with revenue for the Restaurant from 2020, Defendants converted said funds for their own personal use.  (SAC ¶45.)

Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that Defendants intentionally exercised dominion and control over the PPP Funds, RRR Funds, and the 50% interest in the Restaurant’s revenue.  (SAC ¶46.)  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ continued control over the PPP Funds, RRR Funds, and the 50% interest in the Partnership, including the Restaurant’s revenue, is inconsistent with Plaintiffs rights as a 50% Partner in the Partnership.  (SAC ¶46.)

Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ conversion of the PPP Funds, RRR Funds, and the 50% interest in the Partnership, including the Restaurant’s revenue, caused damages to Plaintiff in a sum subject to proof at trial.  (SAC ¶47.)

Plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for conversion. Corporation Code §16501 provides, “A partner is not a coowner of partnership property and has no interest in partnership property that can be transferred, either voluntarily or involuntarily.”  (Corp. Code §16501; see id. §16101(a)(15) [“‘Property’ means all property, real, personal, or mixed, tangible or intangible, or any interest therein.”]; see id. §16502 [“The only transferable interest of a partner in the partnership is the partner’s share of the profits and losses of the partnership and the partner’s right to receive distributions.”].)  Plaintiff alleges, “[p]ursuant to Section 2.2 of the Partnership Agreement, the Partners agreed that Plaintiff shall have the right to pay off any remaining balance of the Additional $500,000.00 Capital in a lump sum at any time. Should Plaintiff elect to pay off any remaining balance of the Additional $500,000.00 Capital in a lump sum, Plaintiff shall immediately be entitled to receive its 50% share of profits from the Partnership.”  (SAC ¶13.) 

Plaintiff does not allege that he elected to pay off any remaining balance of the Additional $500,000.00 Capital in a lump sum, entitling him to receive its 50% share of profits from the Partnership.  Further, Plaintiff is not entitled to the PPP Funds, RRR Funds, and the 50% interest in the Partnership, including the Restaurant’s revenue because those assets belong to the Partnership, not Plaintiff.  Plaintiff’s only transferable interest in the Partnership is his share of the profits and losses of the Partnership and his right to receive distributions.  (Corp. Code §16502.)

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 4th cause of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Unjust Enrichment (6th COA)

The elements of an unjust enrichment claim are the “receipt of a benefit and [the] unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.”  (Peterson v. Cellco Partnership (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1583, 1593, citing Lectrodryer v. SeoulBank (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 723, 726.)

“As a matter of law, an unjust enrichment claim does not lie where the parties have an enforceable express contract.”  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1370; see also California Medical Association, Inc. v. Aetna U.S. Healthcare of California, Inc. (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 151, 172 [“a quasi-contract action for unjust enrichment does not lie where, as here, express binding agreements exist and define the parties’ right.”].)

Plaintiff alleges Defendants were unjustly enriched by their use of monies and assets belonging to the Partnership including the Real Property and Restaurant to the damage and detriment to Plaintiff.  (SAC ¶57.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants have unjustly retained the benefit of the monies and assets belonging to the Partnership, including the Real Property and the Restaurant and the 50% interest in the partnership without providing adequate compensation to Plaintiff.  (SAC ¶58.)

Plaintiff alleges Defendants have unjustly retained a benefit at the expense of Plaintiff and should be required to disgorge all amounts by which they have been unjustly enriched.  (SAC ¶59.)

Plaintiff fails to allege a cause of action for unjust enrichment because Plaintiff has plead the existence an enforceable agreement between KGR and Plaintiff that covers the subject of the Real Property and partnership assets. (See Hedging Concepts, Inc. v. First Alliance Mortgage Co. (1996) 41 Cal.App.4th 1410, 1420 [“When parties have an actual contract covering a subject, a court cannot—not even under the guise of equity jurisprudence—substitute the court’s own concepts of fairness regarding that subject in place of the parties’ own contract.”].)

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 6th cause of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s SAC is sustained with 20 days leave to amend as to the 4th and 6th causes of action.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

B.    Motion to Strike

In light of this Court’s ruling on Defendants’ demurrer, Defendants’ motion to strike is denied as moot.

 

Conclusion

Defendants’ motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s SAC is denied as moot.

Moving Party to give notice.
