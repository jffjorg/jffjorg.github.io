[TENTATIVE] ORDER STRIKING AFFIRMATIVE DEFENSES; DENYING MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AMENDED ANSWER

 

Dept. 48

8:30 a.m.

July 5, 2024

 

On June 6, 2023, Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District filed an answer to Plaintiff Ciro R. Pineda’s first amended complaint.

On May 22, 2024, Defendant filed a stipulation and proposed order to file an amended answer including new forty-second and forty-third affirmative defenses.

On May 24, 2024, the Court declined to sign the order and set an Order to Show Cause why each of the affirmative defenses should not be stricken under Code of Civil Procedure section 436 (b) as not being affirmative defenses and/or being improperly pleaded in accordance with law.  The Court also stated that Defendant may seek to amend its answer by motion or at the time of Defendant’s response to the OSC.

On June 5, 2024, Defendant filed a motion for leave to file an amended answer.  Defendant did not file anything else regarding the OSC.

The answer to a complaint must contain the “general or specific denial of the material allegations of the complaint controverted by the defendant” and a “statement of any new matter constituting a defense.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.30, subds. (b)(1)-(2).)  “The phrase ‘new matter’ refers to something relied on by a defendant which is not put in issue by the plaintiff.”  (Walsh v. West Valley Mission Community College District (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1532, 1546.)  Where the answer sets forth facts showing some essential allegation of the complaint is not true, such facts are not new matter but are denials.  (Ibid.)  The same pleading of “ultimate facts” rather than evidentiary matter or legal conclusions is required as in pleading a complaint.  The answer must aver facts as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and which are alleged in the complaint.  (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashimi (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384.)  Defenses must be pleaded in the nature of “yes, the allegations [of the complaint] are true, but . . .”  (Id. at p. 383.)

Defendant’s first and eleventh affirmative defenses are not truly affirmative defenses.  An allegation that Plaintiff’s claims fail to state a cause of action is a legal assertion that does not depend on factual allegations.

The second through ninth, fourteenth, fifteenth, seventeenth, twenty-fourth, twenty-fifth, twenty-ninth, thirtieth, thirty-sixth through thirty-eighth, and forty-first affirmative defenses do not contain any ultimate facts.

The tenth affirmative defense alleges that Plaintiff has not sustained any damages.  This is not an affirmative defense; rather, this is an allegation that Plaintiff cannot establish every element of his claims.

The thirty-first affirmative defense alleges that “any judgment entered herein may be paid by periodic payments rather than in a lump sum.”  Similarly, the thirty-second affirmative defense alleges, “Defendants are entitled to a reduction of any judgment against Defendants for collateral source payments paid or obligated to be paid for services or benefits provided prior to the commencement of trial.”  The fortieth affirmative defense alleges that Defendant is not liable for punitive damages.  The forty-second affirmative defense states, “Defendant reserves the right to assert additional affirmative defenses and to supplement, alter, or change its answer and defenses upon revelation of more definitive facts and upon undertaking of discovery and investigation in this matter.”  These are not affirmative defenses.

The twenty-third affirmative defense alleges that Plaintiff failed to file a certificate of merit pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1, subdivisions (f) and (g).  This is false.  Plaintiff did file certificates of merit, and upon review of the certificates of merit, the Court granted Plaintiff permission to serve Defendant in a February 9, 2023 order.

The thirty-ninth affirmative defense alleges that Defendant is not liable for an injury resulting from the act or omission of one of the Defendant’s employees where the employee is immune from liability, but there are no ultimate facts about any employee’s immunity.

The Court orders the above affirmative defenses STRICKEN from Defendant’s answer that was filed on June 6, 2023.

The twelfth, sixteenth, eighteenth through twenty-second, twenty-sixth through twenty-eighth, and thirty-third through thirty-fifth affirmative defenses allege that Defendant, a public entity, is not liable or has immunity under certain Government Code sections.  These affirmative defenses are appropriately pleaded.

The thirteenth affirmative defense alleging the statute of limitations does not require alleging facts, but it does require pleading the specific section and subdivision.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 458; Martin v. Van Bergen (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 84, 91.)  This affirmative defense is appropriately pleaded.

Defendant’s motion for leave to file an amended answer seeks to add new forty-second and forty-third affirmative defenses under Education Code sections 35330 and 44808.  These are appropriate affirmative defenses.  However, the motion is DENIED with respect to filing the proposed amended answer in its current form with all other affirmative defenses.

Defendant is granted leave to file an amended answer that remedies the defects identified in this order.  Defendant is ordered to file its amended answer within 30 days.

On November 8, 2023, Petitioner Stris & Maher LLP sfiled a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award.  On January 4, 2024, Respondent Daniel S. March filed an opposition.

On May 23, 2024, Petitioner filed a Motion to Grant the Petition to Confirm Attorney-Client Fee Arbitration Award.  The Court did not grant Petitioner leave to amend the Petition, and Respondent March had already responded to the Petition.  The Court orders the May 23, 2024 Motion STRICKEN for not being filed in accordance with law or a court order.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)

On May 29, 2024, Petitioner filed a Notice of Entry of Dismissal and Proof of Service.  No dismissal was entered—or even requested—and the Notice does not attach a copy of any dismissal.  The Court orders the May 29, 2024 Notice STRICKEN.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)

On June 3, 2024, Petitioner dismissed Respondent Tony Diab.

On June 21, 2024, Respondent March filed an opposition to the May 23, 2024 motion.

On June 28, 2024, Petitioner filed a reply.

A party may petition the court to confirm, correct, or vacate an arbitration award, and a response to a petition may request that the court dismiss the petition or confirm, correct, or vacate the award.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1285, 1285.2.)  The petition or response must set forth (1) the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate, (2) the names of the arbitrators, and (3) the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, or attach a copy.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1285.4, 1285.6.)  The court must either confirm the award as made, correct the award and confirm it as corrected, vacate the award, or dismiss the proceeding.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1286.)

The Petition does not include a copy of the agreement to arbitrate.  Arbitration of attorney-client fee disputes is voluntary for a client and is only mandatory for an attorney when commenced by the client.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 6200, subd. (c).)  This arbitration was commenced by Petitioner, the attorney/firm in the underlying dispute, so arbitration was not statutorily mandated.  (See Petition Ex. 6(c) [Arbitration Award] at p. 1.)

“[T]he party seeking to enforce an award must prove by a preponderance of the evidence that a valid arbitration contract exists.  The court may not confirm an award without first finding the parties agreed in writing to arbitrate their dispute, unless a judicial determination of the issue has already been made (e.g., by a court considering a petition to compel arbitration).  The burden upon the award’s proponent to prove the existence of a valid agreement, and the court’s duty to determine the issue, are reflected in the statutory requirement that the proponent recite or attach the contract.”  (Toal v. Tardif (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1208, 1220.)

Accordingly, the Hearing on Motion to Confirm Arbitration Award is CONTINUED to August 29, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

Petitioner is ORDERED to file a copy of the agreement to arbitrate no later than five court days before the continued hearing.
