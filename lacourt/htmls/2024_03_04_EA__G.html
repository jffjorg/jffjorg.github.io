Case Number: 22PSCV00799    Hearing Date: March 4, 2024    Dept: G

Defendant General Motors, LLC’s Motion to Tax Counsel’s Memorandum of Costs

Respondent: Plaintiff Manuel Meza, Jr.

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant General Motors, LLC’s Motion to Tax Counsel’s Memorandum of Costs is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART.

BACKGROUND

This is a lemon law action. In September 2018, Plaintiff Manuel Meza, Jr. entered into a warranty contract with Defendant General Motors, LLC (General Motors) by purchasing a 2018 Chevrolet Camaro. Subsequently during the warranty period, Meza alleges the vehicle developed defects with its transmission system.

On July 29, 2022, Meza filed a complaint against General Motors and Does 1-50, alleging (1)¿violation of Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (d); (2) violation of Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (b); (3) violation of Civil Code section 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3); (4) breach of express warranty; and (5) breach of implied warranty of merchantability.

On September 29, 2023, General Motors sent Meza a settlement offer that Meza accepted on October 31. On January 5, 2024, Meza submitted a memorandum of costs. On January 18, General Motors filed the present motion.

A hearing on the present motion is set for March 4, 2024, with a final status conference on May 1 and a jury trial on May 21.

ANALYSIS

General Motors moves to tax Meza’s memorandum of costs with regards to requests for $167.29 in jury fees, $404 in service of process costs, $12.34 in notice of change of address costs, and $384.91 in discovery motion costs. For the following reasons, the court GRANTS General Motors’s motion as to Meza’s request for service of process costs and discovery motion costs and DENIES General Motors’s motion on all other grounds.

Legal Standard

Pursuant to Rule 3.1700, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court, a party may contest costs through a motion to strike or tax costs. “If the items appearing in a cost bill appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that they were not reasonable or necessary. On the other hand, if the items are properly objected to, they are put in issue and the burden of proof is on the party claiming them as costs.” (Ladas v. California State Auto. Assn. (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 774 (Ladas).) In addition to items allowable as costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a), the court has discretion to award other costs if not prohibited by Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (b) and “reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation rather than merely convenient or beneficial to its preparation.” (Ladas, supra, 19 Cal.App.4th at p. 774, quoting Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(2).)

Jury Fees

In Meza’s memorandum of costs, Meza claims $167.29 in fees for a notice of case management conference and posting of jury fees. General Motors argues this cost should be stricken because the present case has not gone to trial. But General Motors fails to provide any authority that limits the recovery of jury fees as costs to cases where a jury trial was held. Because jury fees are recoverable as costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a)(1), the court DENIES General Motors’s motion to tax this cost.

Service of Process Costs

Meza’s memorandum of costs claims $404 in costs for the service of Felix Chevrolet. General Motors contends this cost should be stricken because this cost references the service of deposition notices on Felix Chevrolet, a non-party to this action. (Quezada Decl., ¶ 5.) General Motors also notes that Meza never actually conducted a deposition of Felix Chevrolet’s employees. (Quezada Decl., ¶ 5.) In response, Meza fails to provide any argument or explanation as to why the deposition of Felix Chevrolet was reasonably necessary for the case against General Motors. Thus, the court GRANTS General Motors’s motion to tax this cost.

Notice of Change in Address Costs

Meza’s memorandum of costs claims $12.34 in costs for notice of change of address filed with the court. General Motors maintains this cost should be stricken because it is a business overhead cost and unrelated to the litigation of Meza’s claims. But General Motors fails to provide any authority for this argument and the court notes filing fees are recoverable as costs. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (a)(1).) Thus, the court DENIES General Motors’s motion to tax this cost.

Discovery Motion Costs

Last, Meza’s memorandum of costs claims $384.91 in costs for Meza’s motion to compel further discovery and the informal discovery conference. General Motors argues this cost should be stricken because the costs were optional and Meza’s motion practice was one-sided and unnecessary. General Motors also argues Meza has failed to provide sufficient information to support such costs. On the second point, the court agrees as Meza’s memorandum of costs appears to inappropriately lump the filing fees for all of Meza’s discovery motions into one entry. And while Meza provided invoices for these costs in a declaration, Meza has failed to organize them coherently and include specific itemizations of the costs for each filing. Thus, the court GRANTS General Motors’s motion to tax this cost.

CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, General Motors’s motion to tax costs is GRANTED as to Meza’s request for service of process fees and discovery motion costs and DENIED on all other grounds.

Case Number: 22PSCV03008    Hearing Date: March 4, 2024    Dept: G

Defendant Al Bayaan Islamic Center, Inc.’s Demurrer to Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano’s Unverified Complaint

 

Respondent: Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano

 

Defendant Al Bayaan Islamic Center, Inc.’s Motion to Strike Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano’s Complaint

 

Respondent: Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Al Bayaan Islamic Center, Inc.’s Demurrer to Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano’s Unverified Complaint and Motion to Strike Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano’s Complaint are CONTINUED to a date to be determined at the hearing in Department G (Pomona).

 

Defendant’s counsel is also ordered to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the Demurrer and Motion to Strike and to file a supplemental declaration describing such meet and confer efforts at least nine (9) court days before the next scheduled hearing on the Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a premises liability action. In January 2021, Plaintiff Elias Delgado Serano was climbing a ladder at a property in La Puente when Delgado Serano slipped and fell. Delgado Serano alleges the property was owned, maintained, managed, and operated by Defendants Mian Construction and Al Bayaan Islamic Center, Inc. (Al Bayaan).

 

On December 20, 2022, Delgado Serano filed a complaint against Mian Construction, Al Bayaan, and Does 1-10, alleging (1) premises liability and (2) general negligence.

 

On January 16, 2024, Al Bayaan filed the present demurrer and motion to strike. On the same day, Al Bayaan’s counsel attempted to meet and confer by sending an email to Delgado Serano’s counsel. (Josephson Decl., ¶ 5-6.)

 

A hearing on the present demurrer and motion to strike is set for March 4, 2024, along with a case management conference.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Al Bayaan demurs to Delgado Serano’s entire Complaint. For the following reasons, the court finds parties did not adequately meet and confer.

 

Legal Standard

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), prior to filing a demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” This section further provides that “the demurring party shall identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and identify with legal support the basis of the deficiencies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(1).)

 

While Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a)(4) makes clear failing to meet and confer is not grounds to overrule a demurrer, courts “are not required to ignore defects in the meet and confer process” and if the court determines “no meet and confer has taken place, or concludes further conferences between counsel would likely be productive, it retains discretion to order counsel to meaningfully discuss the pleadings with an eye toward reducing the number of issues or eliminating the need for a demurrer, and to continue the hearing date to facilitate that effort.” (Dumas v. Los Angeles County Bd. of Supervisors (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 348, 355 & fn. 3.)

 

Discussion

 

In this case, Al Bayaan’s counsel filed a declaration stating counsel attempted to meet and confer by sending an email to Delgado Serano’s counsel on January 16, 2024. (Josephson Decl., ¶ 5.) Because Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 places the burden to meet and confer telephonically or in-person on Al Bayaan’s counsel, the court finds this attempt insufficient as Al Bayaan’s counsel did not attempt to reach out to Delgado Serano’s counsel over the telephone or in-person and a letter is not a code-compliant form of meeting and conferring. The court also notes the attempt to meet and confer was untimely as Al Bayaan’s counsel was supposed to meet and confer at least five days before the demurrer was due.[1] (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2) Thus, the court finds a continuance of the hearing on Al Bayaan’s demurrer and motion to strike is appropriate.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Al Bayaan’s demurrer and motion to strike are CONTINUED to a date to be determined at the hearing in Department G (Pomona).

 

Al Bayaan’s counsel is also ordered to meet and confer with Delgado Serano’s counsel regarding the demurrer and motion to strike and to file a supplemental declaration describing such meet and confer efforts at least nine (9) court days before the next scheduled hearing on the demurrer and motion to strike.


[1] Al Bayaan was served with substitute service on May 15, 2023, and copies of the summons and Complaint were mailed on May 16. As a result, Al Bayaan’s responsive pleading was due forty days from May 16 on June 25. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 415.20, subd. (b) [service is completed by tenth day from mailing date]; 412.20, subd. (a)(3) [time to respond to summons is thirty days].)
