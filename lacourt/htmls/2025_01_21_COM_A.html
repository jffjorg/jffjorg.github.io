DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO DISCOVERY REQUESTS, TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED AND FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: January 21, 2025

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 03/12/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

MOVING PARTY:              Plaintiff Steen Sprouffske

RESPONDING PARTY:     None

1.     Background

This is a partition action. Plaintiff Steen Sprouffske (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he and Defendant George Moa (“Defendant”) own the property located at 11579 Virginia Ave., Lynwood, CA 90262 (the “Property”) as tenants in common. Plaintiff alleges that the parties have been unable to come to an agreement as to how to divide the property.

2.     Discussion

a.      Motion to compel further discovery responses.

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production, Set One; Form Interrogatories, Set One; and Special Interrogatories, Set One. Defendant did not file an opposition to the instant motion.  Plaintiff served these discovery requests on April 24, 2024.  More than two months after his responses were due, Defendant served written responses and belated objections to each set of discovery on August 12, 2024.

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)  “On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that … [a] statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] … [a] representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] … [a]n objection in the response is without merit or too general.”¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (a).) Such a motion shall be accompanied by a declaration stating that the demanding party made reasonable attempts to meet and confer with the nonmoving party in good faith. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); See also, Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300.)

A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (Code of Civ. Proc. §2016.040.) The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution of each issue. This rule is designed to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order…’ “Argument is not the same as informal negotiation” (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1293-1294, citing Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431 1435, 1437.) Attempting informal resolution means more than the mere attempt by the discovery proponent “to persuade the objector of the error of his ways.” (Townsend, supra at p. 1435). A reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with [opposing] counsel.... (Id.) Rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.” (Id. at p. 1439.)

Based on Plaintiff’s declarations, Plaintiff has made adequate attempts to meet and confer. Plaintiff attempted to communicate with Defendant concerning deficiencies in Defendant’s discovery responses but was unable to obtain a timely response from Defendant. Plaintiff filed the instant motion 42 days after Defendant served discovery responses, only three days before the deadline, during which time Plaintiff sent Defendant a meet and confer letter outlining deficiencies and requesting further communication and negotiation, and attempted to follow up multiple times. Defendant informed Plaintiff more than once that Defendant would call Plaintiff regarding Defendant’s discovery responses, but Defendant did not call Plaintiff.

After consideration of Plaintiff’s arguments and separate statements, the court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses as to requests for production 29 through 57. Defendant has waived all objections by failing to timely serve the objections in response to Plaintiff’s discovery requests. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 407.) The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses as to form interrogatories 2.3; 2.4; 2.5; 2.6; 2.8; 2.12; 2,13; 14.1; 50.4; 50.5; and 50.6. The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses as to special interrogatories 2 and 4 through 39. The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses as to special interrogatories 1 and 3, as Defendant adequately responded to each of these interrogatories.

//

b.      Motion to have Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions deemed admitted.

Plaintiff also separately moves for an order deeming admitted Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One, also served on Defendant on April 24, 2024.  Defendant failed to serve any response to those requests, although this motion has been pending since June 2024.  If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, all objections to the requests are waived.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2033.280.)  The requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Ibid.)  The court shall make the order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.  Defendant failed to do so.  (See, January 15, 2025 Declaration of Callum Armijo.)

3.     Sanctions

Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $6,220 for costs and fees incurred, or anticipated to be incurred, in connection with the motion to compel further discovery responses, and $4,060 in connection with the motion to deem Requests for Admissions admitted.  Plaintiff seeks an award of monetary sanctions against Defendant George Moa and his “counsel.”  The court finds that monetary sanctions are warranted pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, 2031.300 and 2033.280, The court finds counsel’s hourly rate of $400.00 is reasonable.     

With respect to the motion to compel furthers, the court declines to award 6 of the hours of attorney time requested for reviewing opposition, preparing a reply and preparing for the hearing as no opposition was filed, and no reply was therefore necessary or actually filed.  The court also declines to award $180 in fiing fees.  The three motions were filed as one, as reflected in the reservation receipt attached to the notice of motion. That receipt also indicates that no fee was charged, but in any event the standard filing fee is $60.00. The court finds that $3,800 is a reasonable monetary sanction for fees and costs incurred in connection with Plaintiff’s unified motion to compel further responses.

The court further finds that monetary sanctions are mandatory pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subdiv. (c), in connection with Plaintiff’s motion to deem Requests for Admission admitted.  However, the $4,060 Plaintiff seeks is unreasonable, as the motion is very redundant of the motion to compel further responses, no separate statement was required, no opposition or reply was filed, and all matters will be heard at the same hearing.  Plaintiff’s request for reimbursement for time spent drafting the discovery requests is improper. The court awards $1260.00, comprised of the three hours spent drafting the motion at $400.00/hour, and the $60 filing fee. 

The court denies Plaintiff’s generic request for an award of monetary sanctions against Defendant’s “counsel,” as the notice of motion failed to identify “every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought,” as is required by Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.040.

4.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS in part Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production, Set One; Form Interrogatories, Set One; and Special Interrogatories, Set One.  Defendant is ordered to serve verified, code compliant responses without objection not later than two weeks following Plaintiff’s service of notice of this order.  The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to deem Requests for Admissions admitted, and each of those requests is hereby deemed admitted.

Defendant George Moa is ordered to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $5,060.00 to Plaintiff not later than February 24, 2025.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

MOVING PARTY:              Defendant FCA US, LLC

1.     Background

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Jose Hernandez Ruiz (“Plaintiff”) alleges that, on July 23, 2024, Plaintiff entered into a warranty agreement with defendant FCA US, LLC (“Defendant”) regarding a 2018 Jeep Grand Cherokee, vehicle identification number 1C4RJEBG7JC141837 (the “Vehicle”), which was manufactured and/or distributed by Defendant. Plaintiff alleges that the Vehicle contained or developed defects that substantially affected its use, value, and/or safety.

Plaintiff has alleged causes of action for violation of subdivision (d) of Civil Code § 1793.2; violation of subdivision (b) of Civil Code § 1793.2; violation of subdivision (a)(3) of Civil Code § 1793.2; breach of the implied warranty of merchantability; fraudulent inducement – concealment; and negligent repair.

2.     Discussion

Defendant demurs to Plaintiff’s third cause of action for violation of Civil Code § 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3) on the grounds that the complaint fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment on the grounds that the complaint fails to meet the pleading standard for fraud and fails to allege sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿(SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿(Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)¿

           a. Violation of Civil Code § 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3)

Civil Code § 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3) states that manufacturers of consumer goods must "[m]ake available to authorized service and repair facilities sufficient service literature and replacement parts to effect repairs during the express warranty period.”

Defendant contends that the complaint only states conclusions of law and fails to allege what parts or literature were not available to repair facilities. Defendant also contends that the complaint fails to state damages with specificity. In opposition, Plaintiff contends that the specific parts or literature that were in Defendant’s possession and not made available to repair facilities is information entirely in Defendant’s possession and therefore a matter to be worked out in discovery.

The court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged facts in the complaint to constitute a cause of action for violation of Civil Code § 1793.2, subdivision (a)(3). At the demurrer stage, all factual allegations in the complaint are deemed true. Plaintiff need not allege specifically what parts or literature were not available to repair facilities at the demurrer stage, as that is a matter for discovery. Furthermore, Plaintiff has adequately alleged damages. Plaintiff states in the complaint that his damages are comprised of defects and nonconformities in the Vehicle that have substantially impaired its use, safety, and/or value. (Compl. ¶¶ 15-16.) Plaintiff also states in the cause of action that all previous paragraphs are incorporated by reference. (Compl. ¶ 50.) Plaintiff has therefore alleged sufficient facts to state a cause of action. The demurrer is overruled as to the third cause of action.

           b. Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment

The necessary elements of a fraud claim consist of (1) a misrepresentation; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the falsity; (3) the intent to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance on the defendant’s statements or lack thereof; and (5) resulting damages. (Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1185-1186.) In order to demonstrate fraudulent concealment, a plaintiff must show that (1) the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant concealed a material fact from the plaintiff; or (4) the defendant makes some partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. (Los Angeles Memorial Coliseum Com. v. Insomniac, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 803, 831.) Fraud in the inducement is a subset of fraud that occurs when a promisor’s consent to an agreement is induced by fraud. (Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 839 (Dhital).) Fraud must be alleged with specificity, such that the complaint alleges the factual underpinnings of the claim. (Rattagan v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2024) 17 Cal.5th 1, 43 (Rattagan).) In a fraudulent concealment case, the complaint must include specific allegations “establishing all the required elements, including (1) the content of the omitted facts, (2) defendant's awareness of the materiality of those facts, (3) the inaccessibility of the facts to plaintiff, (4) the general point at which the omitted facts should or could have been revealed, and (5) justifiable and actual reliance, either through action or forbearance, based on the defendant's omission.” (Id., at pp. 43-44.)

Defendant contends that the complaint fails to establish that Defendant had a duty of disclosure to Plaintiff because there is no transactional relationship between Defendant and Plaintiff. Defendant also contends that the complaint fails to allege corporate ratification of fraud or identify what material information was fraudulently concealed, and that Plaintiff’s fraud claim fails the two-part test set out in Rattagan. In opposition, Plaintiff contends that a vehicle manufacturer owes a duty to its customers to disclose defects, that corporate ratification of fraud is not necessary to plead, that the complaint adequately alleged that Defendant fraudulently concealed the engine defect in the Vehicle, and that the two-part test is inapplicable because, under Rattagan, the economic loss rule does not apply to fraudulent concealment cases.

The court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged facts in the complaint to constitute a cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment. Plaintiff is correct that the two-part test that Defendant seeks to apply is, under Rattagan, not applicable to this case because the economic loss rule does not apply to fraudulent concealment cases. (Rattagan, supra, 17 Cal.5th at p. 38.) Plaintiff alleged that “FCA committed fraud by allowing the Subject Vehicle to be sold to Plaintiff without disclosing that the Subject Vehicle equipped with the 3.6L engine was defective.” (Compl. ¶ 59.) This is sufficient to allege that Defendant possessed exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to Plaintiff and that Defendant concealed those material facts from Plaintiff, which establishes that Defendant had a duty to disclose. Defendant argues that the complaint does not allege that Defendant sold the Vehicle to Plaintiff, and that absent an alleged agency relationship, vehicle manufacturers are not liable for the wrongs of their dealerships; however, a direct transactional relationship is not required to establish a duty to disclose. (OCM Principal Opportunities Fund, L.P. v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 835, 859.) Additionally, Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff must allege the names of individuals who made the allegedly fraudulent representations is nonsensical in the context of a fraudulent concealment claim. Defendant’s citation to Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153 is inapposite because Tarmann concerned fraudulent misrepresentation, not fraudulent concealment.

Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant had a duty to disclose the engine defect in the Vehicle and failed to do so, the point at which Defendant should have disclosed the defect, that Plaintiff would not have purchased the Vehicle if not for the concealment, and that Plaintiff suffered damages as a result of the concealment. Plaintiff has therefore alleged facts sufficient to establish a cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment. The demurrer is overruled as to the fifth cause of action.

3.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer. 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.
