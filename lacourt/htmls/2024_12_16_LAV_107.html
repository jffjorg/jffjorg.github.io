DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV05376    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc.,

 

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

Lester Finnell,

 

Defendant.

   CASE NUMBER             DEPARTMENT

       23VECV05376            107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS

 

Deny

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

Plaintiff Creditors Adjustment Bureau, Inc’s (“Plaintiff”) assignor entered into an agreement with Defendants Lester Finnell aka Lester Crawford Finnell III aka Lester C Finnell dba Seven Safe Services adba Seven Safe Security aka Seven Safe Security Consultant aka Seven Safe and Does 1 through 10 (“Defendants”). (Complaint ¶ 11.) Plaintiff’s assignor agreed to provide workers compensation insurance policies to Defendants and Defendants agreed to pay a premium in accordance with the terms of each policy. (Complaint ¶ 11.) The agreement required Defendants to allow Plaintiff’s assignor to access Defendants’ business records to conduct an audit. (Complaint ¶ 14.) Defendants breached the agreement by failing to provide Plaintiff’s assignor access to Defendants’ records. (Complaint ¶¶ 14-15.)  

 

On December 4, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of contract; (2) open book account; (3) account stated; and (4) reasonable value. (Complaint pp. 3-6.) 

 

On June 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for summary adjudication on the breach of contract cause of action. On September 30, 2024, this court granted Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication. (09/30/24 pp. 1, 13.) 

 

On October 10, 2024, Defendant Lester Finnell (“Defendant”) filed a motion for reconsideration of this court’s order granting Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication. On December 3, 2024, this court denied that motion. (12/03/24 p. 10.)

 

On December 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for sanctions (the “Motion”). On November 21, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. On December 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply. On December 10, 2024, Defendant filed a sur-reply.

 

I.                TIMELINESS

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. 

 

The Motion, opposition, and reply were timely filed under section 1005.

 

Defendant also filed a sur-reply to Plaintiff’s reply. Section 1005, subdivision (b) does not provide for the filing of a sur-reply. Defendant’s counsel also did not request leave to file a sur-reply in this matter. The court is not persuaded that Plaintiff’s reply raised issues justifying a sur-reply. Accordingly, this court does not consider Defendant’s sur-reply filed December 10, 2024.

 

II.             SAFE HARBOR PERIOD

 

“A party seeking sanctions under section 128.7 follows a two-step procedure. [Citation] First, the moving party serves notice of the motion for sanctions on the offending party. [Citation] Service of the motion starts a safe harbor period during which the motion cannot be filed with the court. [Citation] If the challenged paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation or denial is not withdrawn or corrected within a prescribed number of days, the second step is to file or present the motion for sanctions to the court.” (Primo Hospitality Group, Inc. v. Haney (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 165, 173-174.)

 

 “[A] party is not entitled to sanctions pursuant to section 128.7 unless the target of the motion has had a full 21 days to withdraw the allegedly offending paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation, or denial.” (Broadcast Music, Inc. v. Structured Asset Sales, LLC (2022) 75 Cal.App.5th 596, 599.)  “[A] moving party cannot file a motion for sanctions until the 22nd day after the motion was served.” (Id.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel submits a declaration stating they emailed and mailed Defendant’s counsel a copy of the Motion on October 22, 2024, informing counsel that this Motion would be filed if Defendant’s counsel did not withdraw the motion for reconsideration by November 12, 2024. (Brown Declaration filed 11/18/24 ¶ 5.) The declaration states that a copy of the letter is attached to the declaration as Exhibit 3. (Brown Declaration filed 11/18/24 ¶ 5.) There is no Exhibit 3 attached to the declaration. However, this court has no reason to doubt that Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel the letter. Accordingly, the safe harbor period began on October 22, 2024, and Plaintiff was not permitted to file the Motion until November 13, 2024.

 

Plaintiff filed the Motion on November 18, 2024. Accordingly, Defendant’s counsel had the full safe-harbor period to withdraw the motion for reconsideration.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 contains the requirements for a motion for reconsideration. “A violation of [Code of Civil Procedure section 1008] may be punished as a contempt and with sanctions as allowed by Section 128.7.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008(d).)

 

“Every pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper shall be signed by at least one attorney of record in the attorney's individual name, or, if the party is not represented by an attorney, shall be signed by the party.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(a).) “By presenting to the court, whether by signing, filing, submitting, or later advocating, a pleading, petition, written notice of motion, or other similar paper, an attorney or unrepresented party is certifying that to the best of the person's knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances . . .

 

(1)   It is not being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation.

 

(2)   The claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law.

 

(3)   The allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery.

 

(4)   The denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief.

 

 (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(b).)

 

“If, after notice and a reasonable opportunity to respond, the court determines that subdivision (b) has been violated, the court may, subject to the conditions stated below, impose an appropriate sanction upon the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation. In determining what sanctions, if any, should be ordered, the court shall consider whether a party seeking sanctions has exercised due diligence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7(c.)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is frivolous under section 1008 because “Defendant . . . clearly failed to satisfy subparts (3) and (4) . . . requiring ‘new or different facts, circumstances or law’ and a declaration with specific information.” (Motion p. 3.) Specifically, Plaintiff argues Defendant’s motion for reconsideration was based on facts known at the time of the original motion for summary adjudication and did not include the required declaration. (Motion pp. 4-6.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff is seeking duplicative sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure sections 1008, subdivision (b) and 128.7. (Opposition p. 2.)  Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (d) states that “[a] violation of this section may be punished as a contempt and with sanctions as allowed by Section 128.7.” By citing both sections 1008 and 128.7, Plaintiff is merely citing the applicable law. Moreover, section 128.7, subdivision (c)(1) states that “[a] motion for sanctions under this section shall be made separately from other motions or requests[.]” Thus, Plaintiff is not seeking duplicative sanctions.

Defendant further argues Plaintiff’s counsel’s statement that “this court did not mention to Defense counsel about the filing of a reconsideration motion . . . IS AN OUTRAGEOUS LIE[.]” (Opposition p. 3) Plaintiff’s counsel stated in her declaration that “[a]lthough I distinctly remember [Defendant’s counsel] arguing extensively about his non-receipt of the certified mailing and his assertion that there were three total tenants in the suite in question, I have no recollection of the Court remotely suggesting that Defendant filed a Motion for Reconsideration so he could further argue this same exact point.” (Brown Declaration filed 11/18/24 ¶ 3.) Although Plaintiff’s and Defendant’s counsel may have different recollections about what transpired at the summary adjudication hearing, there is no evidence before this court that Plaintiff’s counsel lied to this court.

Defendant also argues the motion for reconsideration was not frivolous. (Opposition pp. 3-4.)

 

As to the declaration, this court found in its ruling on the motion for reconsideration that the declaration sufficiently identified the “new” evidence the motion for reconsideration was based upon. (12/03/24 Minute Order p. 4.)

 

This court finds that many of Defendant’s arguments raised in the motion for reconsideration were frivolous. In the motion for reconsideration, Defendant argued there were disputes of material fact relating to who caused the audit not to be scheduled, whether section 11760.1 requires “receipt” of the certified mail, whether there was a compelling business reason leading to the non-occurrence of the audit, whether the estimate premium was reasonable, and whether defendant refused three requests for audit under section 11760.1. (See 12/03/24 Minute Order pp. 5-8.) As stated in this court’s ruling on the motion for reconsideration, this court considered, and rejected, these arguments in its ruling on Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication. (12/03/24 Minute Order pp. 5-8.) Thus, these arguments, did not constitute “new or different facts, circumstances, or law” that could justify a motion for reconsideration under section 1008.

 

The final argument Defendant raised in the motion for reconsideration related to the three separate businesses operating out of Defendant’s counsel’s business address where Plaintiff sent the certified mail. (12/03/24 Minute Order p. 7.)

 

Applying only section 1008, this court stated in its ruling on Defendant’s motion for reconsideration that the three businesses operating out of Defendant’s counsel’s business address was not “new” evidence.  (12/03/24 Minute Order p. 7.)

 

In the motion for reconsideration, Defendant also cited section 473, subdivision (b), and stated that “[t]he several facts concerning thereof can and must be raised now in this reconsideration motion since they were either not in existence at the time of the original MSJ hearing or was presented but late in writing and was objected to by the Plaintiff —and as such can now be presented for reconsideration via CCP 473(b) and Mink, supra.” (Motion for Reconsideration p. 5.) At the hearing on the motion for reconsideration, Defendant argued Avila v. Chua (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 860, supported applying section 473, subdivision (b), to the motion for reconsideration.

 

In Avila v. Chua, supra, 57 Cal.App.4th at p. 868, the trial court denied a motion for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 473 that was “entered after the trial court struck appellant’s late-filed opposition to respondents’ summary judgment motions and granted summary judgment.” The Court of Appeal held that the trial court erred. (Id.) The Court stated that section 473 applied because the case was “directly analogous to a default judgment” and “[t]here was no litigation on the merits.” (Id. at p. 866-868.) The Court further noted that “Appellant [had] submitted declarations which directly contradicted respondents’ most crucial proposed undisputed facts, but those declarations were not considered by the court. Appellant’s response was stricken, and the matter proceeded to summary judgment and judgment as if by default.” (Id. at p. 868.) Avila v. Chua, was disapproved of by The Urban Wildlands Group, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (2017) 10 Cal.App.5th 993, 1000, where the Court of Appeal stated that “[a]s expressly worded, section 473(b) applies only to relief sought in response to defaults, default judgments or dismissals” and “[s]ummary judgments are neither defaults, nor default judgments, nor dismissals.”

 

Avila is distinguishable from the facts at issue here because this court fully considered the merits and both parties’ arguments relating to the motion for summary adjudication. (See 09/30/24 Minute Order.) However, because Avila has been disapproved of, and not expressly overruled, this court declines to find Defendant’s argument applying Avila and section 473 to be frivolous or not warranted by existing law.

 

Finally, Plaintiff argues sanctions should be imposed because “both defendant and defense counsel . . . offered false testimony to this court when they denied receipt of some of the mailings sent by both SCIF and Plaintiff . . .” (Motion pp. 7-8.) Plaintiff further argues “defense counsel submitted yet additional false testimony regarding the number of tenants utilizing the Suite where the certified mail was delivered.” (Motion p. 8.) Plaintiff submits evidence that there is only one other business currently operating out of Defendant’s counsel’s business address but submits no other evidence that the testimony discussed above is false. (Opposition pp. 5-6.)  Plaintiff’s arguments are speculative, and this court declines to award sanctions on the grounds Defendant and Defendant’s counsel offered false testimony to this court.

 

Accordingly, this court declines to impose sanctions under sections 1008 and 128.7.

 

V.              CONTEMPT SANCTIONS

In the reply, Plaintiff references emails between Plaintiff’s counsel and Defendant’s counsel and argues Defendant’s counsel’s “vulgar and unprofessional behavior also warrants contempt sanctions.” (Reply p. 9.)

 

Plaintiff did not argue in the Motion that Defendant’s counsel’s unprofessional behavior warranted contempt sanctions. (Motion p. 2.) Courts “ordinarily [will not] consider issues raised for the first time in a reply brief.” (United Grant Corp. v. Malibu Hillbillies, LLC (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 142, 157.) “An issue is new if it does more than elaborate on issues raised in the opening brief or rebut arguments made by the respondent in respondent’s brief.” (Id.)

 

Further, Plaintiff brought this Motion under section 128.7. “Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 . . . authorizes trial courts to impose sanctions to check abuses in the filing of pleadings, petitions, written notices of motions or similar papers.” (Musaelian v. Adams (2009) 45 Cal.4th 512, 514.)

 

Defendant’s counsel’s unprofessional emails were not submitted to this court in Defendant’s motion for reconsideration. Plaintiff cites no authority for the proposition that Defendant’s counsel’s unprofessional emails are sanctionable under section 128.7. Plaintiff also identifies no other authority that authorizes this court to sanction Defendant’s counsel for these emails.

 

Accordingly, this court declines to hold Defendant in contempt under section 128.7 solely for counsel’s unprofessional emails.

 

VI.           CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion is denied.

COURT’S[TENTATIVE] DECISION RE:     MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION

 

(1) Grant motion to compel; (2) Grant sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,185.00.

 

I.                BACKGROUND

 

On or about May 27, 2023, Plaintiff Tal Porat (“Plaintiff”) purchased a 2023 GMC Canyon (the “Vehicle”). (Complaint ¶ 6.) The Vehicle was defective. (Complaint ¶ 12.) On May 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant General Motors LLC (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 100 alleging causes of action for: (1) breach of implied warranty; (2) breach of express warranty; and (3) violation of the Song Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. (Complaint p. 1.)

 

On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s person(s) most qualified (the “Motion”). On October 16, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. On October 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

On October 29, 2024, the parties participated in an informal discovery conference. (10/29/24 Minute Order.)

 

II.             MEET AND CONFER

 

A motion to compel a deposition must “be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(b)(2).)¿

 

Plaintiff states that it “first requested dates of availability [to] accommodate [Defendant] and its counsel’s schedule” and, after serving Defendant with the Notice of Deposition, “Plaintiff once again offered Defendant an opportunity to offer dates of availability to reschedule the deposition.” (Motion p. 4.) Finally, Plaintiff states that “Plaintiff’s meet and confer letter confirmed that Plaintiff agrees to limit the deposition to the categories Defendant requested it be limited to.” (Motion p. 4.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to meet and confer before filing the motion. Defendant states that it “consistently offers its [person most qualified] for deposition on multiple Categories to Plaintiff’s counsel in other cases appropriate to the facts and issues of the particular case and has previously produced hundreds of pages of documents” (Opposition p. 3.) Defendant further argues that “[s]imply demanding different deposition dates is not meeting and conferring in good faith, as the mere date of the deposition is not the real dispute here.” (Opposition p. 3.)

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues “Defendant has never actually produced its [person most qualified] in those other cases” and “Plaintiff has continuously asked for dates that are convenient for [Defendant] and defense counsel.” (Reply p. 4.) Plaintiff also states that “Defendant never provided dates of availability and is now attempting to mask the issue by arguing that Plaintiff did not meet and confer by specifically responding to Defendant’s objections.” (Reply p. 4.)

 

On July 11, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel a letter requesting dates of availability for scheduling the deposition. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 2.) Defendant did not respond. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 2.) Plaintiff then served its Notice of Deposition. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 3.) On August 28, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiff with objections to Plaintiff’s Notice of Deposition. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 4.) On August 29, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel requesting Defendant’s dates of availability and agreeing to limit the scope of the deposition. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 5.) Defendant’s counsel states it has no record of receiving Plaintiff’s August 29, 2024, letter. (Valencia Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

Even if Defendant did not receive the letter, this court has no reason to believe Plaintiff’s counsel did not send it to Defendant. Accordingly, this court finds that Plaintiff made sufficient meet and confer efforts before filing the Motion.

 

III.           LEGAL STANDARD

 

“Any party may obtain discovery within the scope delimited by Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 2017.010), and subject to the restrictions set forth in Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 2019.010), by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action. The person deposed may be a natural person, an organization such as a public or private corporation, a partnership, an association, or a governmental agency.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)

 

“If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.230.)

 

“The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.280(a).)¿¿ 

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(a).)¿¿

 

“Any party served with a deposition notice that does not comply with Article 2 (commencing with Section 2025.210) waives any error or irregularity unless that party promptly serves a written objection specifying that error or irregularity at least three calendar days prior to the date for which the deposition is scheduled, on the party seeking to take the deposition and any other attorney or party on whom the deposition notice was served.”(Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410(a).)

 

IV.           DISCUSSION

 

The parties disagree about what the dispute is in this matter.

 

Defendant argues that “the mere date of the deposition is not the real dispute here.” (Opposition p. 3.) Instead, Defendant argues that the dispute here is whether Defendant should be compelled to provide irrelevant testimony and information and trade secret material. (Opposition pp. 3-6.)  

 

Plaintiff argues that “Plaintiff has agreed to limit the scope of the deposition to those categories requested by Defendant” and “Defendant’s refusal to provide any dates for the deposition on the agreed upon categories is the only dispute here and the basis for Plaintiff’s motion to compel.” (Reply p. 4.)

 

Plaintiff requests this court “order [Defendant] to designate and produce its Person(s) Most Qualified for deposition within fifteen (15) days.” (Motion p. 7.) Defendant states it has agreed to produce its person most qualified for a deposition “at a mutually agreeable time and place.” (Opposition p. 2.) Accordingly, it appears that the only remaining dispute here is when Defendant must produce its person most qualified for a deposition.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.010, “[a]ny party may obtain discovery within the scope delimited by Chapter 2 (commencing with Section 2017.010), and subject to the restrictions set forth in Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 2019.010), by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including . . . an organization such as a public or private corporation, a partnership, [or] an association[.]” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.010.)  Thus, Plaintiff is entitled to depose Defendant’s person most qualified. However, despite Plaintiff repeatedly requesting Defendant’s dates of availability, (Ex. 1; Ex. 4) Defendant has not suggested date(s) for the deposition to occur. Accordingly, this court orders Defendant to produce its person most qualified at a deposition within thirty (30) days’ notice of this court’s order.

 

To the extent there remains a dispute about the scope of the deposition, Defendant may move for a protective order and Plaintiff may file a motion to compel answers or produce documents. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.480(a); 2025.420.)

 

V.              SANCTIONS

 

If a motion to compel a deposition is granted, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450(g)(1).)

 

Plaintiff seeks $1,860.00 in monetary sanctions. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 7; Motion p. 7.) This includes the $60.00 motion filing fee, two and a half hours in attorneys’ fees for preparing the Motion and supporting documents, half an hour for reviewing and replying to the opposition, and one hour for attending the hearing. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 7.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel tried to schedule the deposition at a mutually agreeable time. (Sadgan Declaration ¶¶ 2-3.) Defendant’s counsel did not respond to that letter. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 2.) Plaintiff then served Defendant with a Notice of Deposition. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 3.) Defendant served Plaintiff with its objections on August 28, 2024. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 4.) On August 29, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel a meet and confer letter. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 5.) In the August 29, 2024, letter, Plaintiff’s counsel agreed to limit the deposition’s scope and asked Defendant to provide its dates of availability to schedule the deposition. (Sadgan Declaration ¶ 5.) Defendant’s counsel submits a declaration stating that, “[a]fter having conducted a diligent search, [Defendant] has no record of receiving Plaintiff’s [August 29, 2024] letter.” (Valencia Declaration ¶ 4.)

 

This court acknowledges Defendant has no record of receiving Plaintiff’s August 29, 2024, letter. However, Defendant appears to have received Plaintiff’s July 11, 2024, letter. (Valencia Declaration ¶ 2.) And it was Defendant’s refusal to provide Plaintiff with dates of availability that led Plaintiff to file this Motion. Accordingly, this court finds that Defendant did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances make imposing sanctions unjust.

This court further finds that two and a half hours of attorneys’ fees for preparing a boilerplate lemon law motion is excessive.

Accordingly, this court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant in the reduced amount of $1,185.00, reflecting the $60.00 motion filing fee, one hour for preparing the Motion, half an hour for reviewing the opposition and preparing a reply, and one hour for attending the hearing, at $450.00 per hour.

VI.           CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants Plaintiff’s Motion and orders Defendant to produce its person most qualified for a deposition within thirty (30) days’ notice of this court’s order. This court also grants Plaintiff’s request for sanctions against Defendant in the reduced amount of $1,185.00.
