DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV45351    Hearing Date: September 10, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 10, 2024               TRIAL DATE: November 12, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Barry Ben-Ron et al. v. Maria De Lourdes Ruiz.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV45351

           

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Barry Ben-Ron and Brian Sean Costello

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Maria De Lourdes Ruiz

 

CASE HISTORY:

  • 12/13/21:         Complaint filed.
  • 06/15/22:         Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a breach of contract case. Plaintiffs purchased a property from Defendant which Plaintiffs allege was subject to a PACE loan. Plaintiffs contend that Defendant agreed to pay off the loan through escrow, such that the property would be transferred to Plaintiffs clear of any lien and failed to do so, despite receiving money in an amount equal to the balance on the loan, from escrow.

 

            Plaintiffs move for summary adjudication of whether each defendant owed a duty to Plaintiffs to pay off a PACE lien through escrow for sale of the subject property to Plaintiffs, and for summary adjudication of Defendant’s First Affirmative Defense.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiffs’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is CONTINUED to Friday, October 11, 2024, at 9:00 AM.

 

            The parties are directed to serve and file supplemental briefing as to the proper interpretation of Paragraphs 13 and 17 of the Agreement, with particular regard to the meaning of the terms “encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters,” “monetary liens of record,” and “real property taxes and assessments” and the application of those terms to the Notice of Assessment recorded against the Property. Briefing shall be conducted according to the following schedule:

 

Plaintiff’s Supplemental Brief due Wednesday, September 18, 2024.

 

            Defendant’s Supplemental Opposition due Monday, September 30, 2024.

 

            Plaintiff’s Supplemental Reply due Friday, October 4, 2024.

 

            All briefs shall be limited to ten pages each, not including captions, tables of contents and authorities, or proofs of service.

 

            Moving parties to give notice.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

            Plaintiffs move for summary adjudication of the issue of whether Defendants owed a duty to either Plaintiff to pay off a PACE lien through escrow for sale of the subject property to Plaintiffs, and for summary adjudication of Defendant’s First Affirmative Defense.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition by a plaintiff is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a defendant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

Analysis

 

            Plaintiffs request a determination that Defendants owed a duty to both Plaintiffs to pay off the PACE lien on the subject property through escrow for the sale of that property. Plaintiffs also request summary adjudication of Defendant’s First Affirmative Defense that the Seller Property Questionnaire contains the printed language “This disclosure is not intended to be part of the contract between Buyer and Seller.” As the issues of duty and the affirmative defense are intertwined, the Court will address them together.

 

            The undisputed facts of this case are that, in October 2020, Plaintiffs and Defendant executed a written contract for the purchase of real property located at 212 East 24th Street, Los Angeles, CA. (Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Fact & Defendant’s Separate Statement of Disputed Fact Nos. 1, 8.) The contract was prepared on a form entitled California Residential Purchase Agreement and Joint Escrow Instructions with a Seller Multiple Counter Offer. (Id.) The Agreement was signed on October 10, 2020 by Defendant, as Seller, and Plaintiff Ben-Ron as Buyer. (Id.) Plaintiff Ben-Ron assigned an interest in the agreement to Plaintiff Costello. (Id. No. 11.) Paragraph 13.B of the Agreement provides:

 

Title is taken in its present condition subject to all encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters, whether of record or not, as of the date of Acceptance except for (1) monetary liens of record (which Seller is obligated to pay off) unless Buyer is assuming those obligations or taking the Property subject to those obligations; and (ii) those matters which Seller has agreed to remove in writing.

 

(SSUMF No. 2, 9; SSDF No. 2, 9.) Defendant and Plaintiffs also signed a Seller Property Questionnaire indicating that the property was subject to a PACE lien. (SSUMF Nos. 3-4, 10, 12; SSDF Nos. 3-4, 10, 12.) Specifically, Paragraph 14.E of the Questionnaire asks if the seller is aware of “Any PACE lien (such as HERO or SCEIP) or other lien on your Property securing a loan to pay for an alteration, modification, replacement, improvement, remodel or material repair of the Property?” (SSUMF No. 4, 12; SSDF No. 4, 12.) The box labeled “Yes” next to Paragraph 14.E is checked, and in the “Explanation” section there is a handwritten statement “Has a Loan for improvements that will be paid through escrow.” (Id.) At the time of the sale, there was a recorded PACE lien on the subject property. (SUMF Nos. 5, 13, SSDF Nos. 5, 13.)

 

            Plaintiffs categorically state that they did not agree to assume the obligations on the PACE lien, nor did they agree to take the property subject to the PACE lien. (SSUMF Nos. 6, 13.) Further, Defendant admitted in her deposition that she understood that the bank mortgage and PACE lien were to be paid through escrow. (SSUMF Nos. 7, 14.) In opposition, Defendant argues that the PACE Lien is properly considered an “assessment” by the express terms of the Notice of Assessment. (SSDF Nos. 6, 14; see RJN Exh. E.) Defendant contends that under Paragraph 17 of the Agreement, certain listed items “shall be paid current and prorated between Buyer and Seller as of close of escrow,” including real property taxes and assessments. (SSDF Nos. 6, 14.) In reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant neglects that paragraph 17 expressly states that it applies only “[u]nless otherwise agreed in writing. (See Plaintiffs’ Exh. C. ¶ 17.) Plaintiffs contend that the parties executed such an agreement in the form of the Seller Property Questionnaire via the handwritten explanation that the loan would be paid through escrow.

 

            The effect of the Seller Property Questionnaire is also the subject of the First Affirmative Defense, which contends that the Questionnaire is not a promise to pay off the PACE loan through escrow, as alleged in the Complaint, because the Questionnaire states in Paragraph 1 that “[t]his disclosure is not intended to be part of the contract between Buyer and Seller.” (See Answer to Complaint.) Plaintiffs argue that the handwritten explanation stating that the Pace Lien would be paid through escrow overrides the form language in Paragraph 1 under general rules of contract interpretation. (See Civ. Code § 1651 [handwritten contract language controls over printed; original language controls over form language].) In opposition, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs beg the question by assuming that the Questionnaire is a contract in the first place. According to Defendant, the Questionnaire, by its express language, is not a contract and therefore is not subject to contractual rules of interpretation. In reply, Plaintiffs dismiss Defendant’s argument as “sophistry” and stand on their position.

 

            It appears that, in focusing on whether the Questionnaire constitutes an agreement in writing, the parties have neglected the threshold issue of whether the Agreement’s reference to “assessments” properly includes the PACE lien. This question is subject to “well-established” rules for contract interpretation in the context of a summary adjudication motion:

 

“The interpretation of a contract is a judicial function. [Citation.] In engaging in this function, the trial court ‘give[s] effect to the mutual intention of the parties as it existed’ at the time the contract was executed. [Citation.] Ordinarily, the objective intent of the contracting parties is a legal question determined solely by reference to the contract's terms. [Citation.]” (Wolf v. Walt Disney Pictures & Television (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 1107, 1125-1126, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585 (Wolf).)

 

“The court generally may not consider extrinsic evidence of any prior agreement or contemporaneous oral agreement to vary or contradict the clear and unambiguous terms of a written, integrated contract. [Citations.] Extrinsic evidence is admissible, however, to interpret an agreement when a material term is ambiguous. [Citations.]” (Wolf, supra, 162 Cal.App.4th at p. 1126, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585; see also Pacific Gas & Electric Co. v. G.W. Thomas Drayage & Rigging (1968) 69 Cal.2d 33, 39-40, 69 Cal.Rptr. 561, 442 P.2d 641 [if extrinsic evidence reveals that apparently clear language in the contract is, in fact, “susceptible to more than one reasonable interpretation,” then extrinsic evidence may be used to determine the contracting parties’ objective intent].)

 

“The interpretation of a contract involves ‘a two-step process: First the court provisionally receives (without actually admitting) all credible evidence concerning the parties’ intentions to determine “ambiguity,” i.e., whether the language is “reasonably susceptible” to the interpretation urged by a party.  If in light of the extrinsic evidence the court decides the language is “reasonably susceptible” to the interpretation urged, the extrinsic evidence is then admitted to aid in the second step – interpreting the contract. [Citation.]’ [Citation.]” (Wolf v. Superior Court (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1343, 1351, 8 Cal.Rptr.3d 649 (Wolf II); [citations].)

 

“When there is no material conflict in the extrinsic evidence, the trial court interprets the contract as a matter of law. [Citations.] This is true even when conflicting inferences may be drawn from the undisputed extrinsic evidence [citations] or that extrinsic evidence renders the contract terms susceptible to more than one reasonable interpretation. [Citations.] If, however, there is a conflict in the extrinsic evidence, the factual conflict is to be resolved by the jury. [Citations.]” (Wolf, supra, 162 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1126-1127, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585; see id. at p. 1134, 76 Cal.Rptr.3d 585 [“that extrinsic evidence may reveal an ambiguity subjecting a contract to more than one reasonable interpretation does not mean resolution of that ambiguity is necessarily a jury question. Absent a conflict in the evidence, the interpretation of the contract remains a matter of law”].)

 

(Brown v. Goldstein (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 418, 432-33.)

 

When a dispute arises over the meaning of contract language, the first question to be decided is whether the language is ‘reasonably susceptible’ to the interpretation urged by the party.  If it is not, the case is over.  If the court decides the language is reasonably susceptible to the interpretation urged, the court moves to the second question: what did the parties intend the language to mean? ... Thus, where contract language is clear and explicit and does not lead to absurd results, we ascertain intent from the written terms and go no further. If the contract is capable of more than one reasonable interpretation, it is ambiguous, and we apply the standard rules of interpretation in order to give effect to the mutual intention of the parties.  In sum, courts must give a reasonable and commonsense interpretation of a contract consistent with the parties' apparent intent. 

 

(Department of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Lawrence Livermore National Security, LLC (2015) 239 Cal. App. 4th 1060, 1066 [internal quotation marks, ellipses, and citations omitted].)

 

            The Court desires briefing from the parties as to the proper interpretation of Paragraphs 13 and 17 of the Agreement, with particular regard to the meaning of the terms “encumbrances, easements, covenants, conditions, restrictions, rights and other matters,” “monetary liens of record,” and “real property taxes and assessments” and the application of those terms to the Notice of Assessment recorded against the Property.

HEARING DATE:     September 10, 2024               JUDGMENT: July 25, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         Renee Lotenero, et al. v. BMW of North America, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV12388           

 

MOTION FOR PREJUDGMENT INTEREST

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiffs Renee Lotenero and Steve Siegrist

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant BMW of North America, LLC

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/12/22: Complaint filed.

·         07/03/24: Jury verdict rendered.

·         07/25/24: Judgment on special verdict.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a lemon law action filed on April 12, 2022. Plaintiffs entered into a Retail Installment Sales Contract for a 2017 BMW i3 which developed electrical system defects. Plaintiffs sued the manufacturer, BMW of North America, LLC, for breach of warranty under the Song Beverly Act. A jury verdict was rendered on July 3, 2024 in favor of Plaintiffs and against Defendant.

 

Plaintiffs move for an award of prejudgment interest.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Prejudgment Interest is GRANTED in the amount of $4,830.14.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiffs seek an award of prejudgment interest as the prevailing parties after a jury trial.

 

//

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiffs, in their reply papers, request that the Court take judicial notice of (1) Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration in this action; and (2) the Declaration of Scott D. Sharp in support of that motion. Neither document is material to the Court’s ruling. Plaintiffs’ request is therefore DENIED.  (Gbur v. Cohen (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 296, 301 [“[J]udicial notice . . . is always confined to those matters which are relevant to the issue at hand.”].) 

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiffs seek an award of prejudgment interest pursuant to Civil Code section 3287(a), or, alternatively, in a reduced amount pursuant to section 3287(b).

 

            Civil Code section 3287 provides, in relevant part:

 

(a) Every person who is entitled to recover damages certain, or capable of being made certain by calculation, and the right to recover which is vested in him upon a particular day, is entitled also to recover interest thereon from that day, except during such time as the debtor is prevented by law, or by the act of the creditor from paying the debt. This section is applicable to recovery of damages and interest from any such debtor, including the state or any county, city, city and county, municipal corporation, public district, public agency, or any political subdivision of the state. 

 

(b) Every person who is entitled under any judgment to receive damages based upon a cause of action in contract where the claim was unliquidated, may also recover interest thereon from a date prior to the entry of judgment as the court may, in its discretion, fix, but in no event earlier than the date the action was filed.

 

(Civ. Code § 3287(a)-(b), bold emphasis added.) 

 

The case law interpreting Civil Code section 3287 provides some guidance to its application . . . . As discussed above, "[t]he policy underlying authorization of an award of prejudgment interest is to compensate the injured party--to make that party whole for the accrual of wealth which could have been produced during the period of loss. [Citations.]" (Citation omitted.)  

 

The court in Cassinos also said: "The test for recovery of prejudgment interest under [Civil Code] section 3287, subdivision (a) is whether defendant actually know[s] the amount owed or from reasonably available information could the defendant have computed that amount. [Citation.]" (Citations omitted.) "The statute [Civil Code section 3287] does not authorize prejudgment interest where the amount of damage, as opposed to the determination¿of liability, 'depends upon a judicial determination based upon conflicting evidence and is not ascertainable from truthful data supplied by the claimant to his debtor.' [Citations.]" ( Citation omitted.) Thus, where the amount of damages cannot be resolved except by verdict or judgment, prejudgment interest is not appropriate. (Citation omitted.)  

 

(Wisper Corp. v. Cal. Commerce Bank (1996) 49 Cal.App.4th 948, 960, bold emphasis and underlining added.) 

 

Entitlement to Interest Under Section 3287(a)

 

            Plaintiffs specifically seek an award of prejudgment interest in the amount of $7,681.11 under subdivision (a), or, alternatively, in the amount of $6,315.58 under subdivision (b), based purely on the purchase price of the subject vehicle of $28,319.60, including a sale price of $24,191.00, finance charges of $1,822.09, and $2,306.51 in taxes and fees, all of which were set forth in the sales contract. (See Declaration of Jeffrey Mukai ISO Mot. Exh. A.)  Plaintiffs do not seek prejudgment interest on other damages, as Plaintiffs concede that those amounts were neither known nor could they be calculated in advance. Plaintiffs argue the amount of damages was capable of being made certain because the underlying purchase contract for the vehicle set the price of the vehicle at $28,319.60, and thus, Defendant was capable of calculating interest on that price if Plaintiffs were successful in pursuing restitution under the Song-Beverly Act. Plaintiffs calculate the total amount of prejudgment interest at $7,681.11. This calculation is derived from an assessed interest rate on the purchase price of $28,319.60 of 10% per annum, pursuant to Civil Code section 3829, divided by 365 days per year. Thus, at a corresponding daily interest rate of $7.7587 per day, multiplied by 990 days between the date of breach on October 18, 2021 and the date of the verdict on July 3, 2024, the total amount owed, according to Plaintiffs, is $7,681.11. Alternatively, if the award is calculated based on the date that the Complaint was filed on April 12, 2022, the total amount of interest applying the same calculation would be $6,315.58.

 

Defendant argues that Plaintiffs are not entitled to prejudgment interest because Plaintiffs’ damages were not certain or capable of being made certain until a judicial determination was made based on conflicting evidence. In support of this contention, Defendant cites Duale v. Mercedes Benz USA, LLC, a 2007 Song-Beverly case in which the Court of Appeal concluded that the determination of damage to the plaintiff from the alleged defects in the vehicle at issue were in dispute and could not be resolved except by verdict. (Duale v. Mercedes Benz USA, LLC (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 718, 729; see also Warren v. Kia Motors America, Inc. (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 24, 43-45.) Defendant contends that, because there were similar disputed issues of nonconformity, damages (including offsets for mileage on the vehicle), and the application of civil penalties, Duale and its progeny are on all fours with this case.

 

            Plaintiffs, anticipating this line of attack, argue that Defendant overextends the reach of Duale to claim that whenever the amount of damages is in dispute for any reason, a plaintiff is not entitled to prejudgment interest. As Plaintiffs state, the Court of Appeal concluded in Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. that Duale’s holding that prejudgment interest was unavailable turned entirely on the facts of that case, and was not a general rule that prejudgment interest is not available under the Song-Beverly Act. (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1010.) Defendant, in response, argues that Doppes concerned only whether a trial court has jurisdiction to award prejudgment interest in a Song-Beverly action, and therefore did not limit the effect of Duale. The Court does not share Defendant’s view because, if that were true, Defendant’s reading of Duale would effectively render Doppes a dead letter whenever liability or damages are in dispute—which is to say, whenever a Song-Beverly action is brought. Defendant’s reliance on Duale is not entirely misplaced, however. In that case, the Duale court concluded that determination of the award required the jury to make numerous factual determinations involving the existence of any defects and whether they impaired the use, value, or safety of the vehicle at issue. (Duale, supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 729.) Moreover, the Court of Appeal in Warren found that, even if damages under the sale contract of the vehicle could have been calculated, incidental or consequential damages required factual determinations at trial. (Warren, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at 44-45.) Similar disputes existed in this case. Nevertheless, well-established precedent holds that “[t]he existence of a bona fide dispute between the parties as to the amount owing under an express contract does not render that sum unliquidated.” (See, e.g., Rabinowitch v. California Western Gas Co. (1967) 257 Cal.App.2d 150, 161.) Further still, the Court of Appeal has repeatedly upheld awards of prejudgment interest in Song-Beverly actions even where liability was disputed. (See, e.g., Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at 1009-1010; Lukather v. General Motors, LLC (2010 181 Cal.App.4th 1041, 1053.)

 

Considering the rule that “certainty” regarding prejudgment interest is broadly construed, (see Chesapeake Industries Inc. v. Togoya Enterprises, Inc. (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 901, 907), the Court concludes, based on the fixed value of the contract, that the potential damages were certain insofar as they could be computed under the contract based on the state of the vehicle. The Court therefore finds that Defendant is entitled to prejudgment interest under section 3287(a). The Court thus declines to address the issue of whether interest should be awarded under section 3287(b).

 

Effect of Offset

 

            Defendant argues that, even if Plaintiffs are entitled to interest, Plaintiff’s calculation neglects to include the jury’s calculated mileage offset. Plaintiffs do not address this argument in opposition. As the mileage offset is part of the proper measure of actual damages, the Court is persuaded by the logic of Defendant’s argument. The Court will therefore apply the mileage offset for value of use of $2,951.46, for an offset purchase price of $25,440.14.

 

Interest Rate

 

            Defendant also argues that Plaintiffs are improperly seeking interest at a rate of 10%, rather than 7%. Under Civil Code section 3289, interest on a breach of a contract that does not specify an interest rate is fixed by statute at 10%. (Civ. Code § 3289.) Article XV, section 1 of the California Constitution also provides:

 

The rate of interest upon a judgment rendered in any court of this State shall be set by the Legislature at not more than 10 percent per annum. Such rate may be variable and based upon interest rates charged by federal agencies or economic indicators, or both.

 

In the absence of the setting of such rate by the Legislature, the rate of interest on any judgment rendered in any court of the State shall be 7 percent per annum.

 

(Cal. Const. art. XV § 1.) Defendant contends that section 3289 is inapplicable because this is a Song-Beverly action, not an action in contract. Plaintiffs, in reply, argue that a rate of 10% is available for actions that “arise out of a contract” under the statute, or, alternatively, for claims involving goods used primarily for personal purposes under article XV section 1. The Court disagrees with Plaintiffs’ interpretation of the law. Article XV section 1 permits deviation from the default interest rate only when set by the Legislature or when set forth in a written contract. (Cal. Const. Art. XV § 1.) Plaintiffs point to no term in any contract with Defendant that sets forth a 10% interest rate. Further, the Court is not persuaded that Bishop v. Hyundai stands for the proposition that a Song-Beverly claim is a claim based upon a contract, as Plaintiffs argue in the context of interest under Civil Code section 3287(b), which the Court otherwise does not address. (Bishop v. Hyundai (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 750.) In that case, the Court of Appeal, in determining the availability of emotional distress damages, examined the language of the Song-Beverly Act, concluding that the statutory remedies under the Act were expressly limited to remedies available under the Uniform Commercial Code. (Bishop, supra, 44 Cal.App.4th at 757-58.) The Court of Appeal did not hold, however, that a Song-Beverly claim is, in fact, a claim arising from contract. Plaintiffs’ argument thus exceeds the limits of the Bishop ruling. The Court therefore finds that the correct interest rate is not 10%, as claimed by Plaintiffs, but rather 7%, pursuant to Article XV section 1 of the California Constitution.

 

            Thus, applying the formula described above, the Court calculates that the correct amount of prejudgment interest is $4,830.14 derived from an annual interest of $1,780.81, divided by 365 days, for a daily interest of $4.88 accruing for a total of 990 days.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Prejudgment Interest is GRANTED in the amount of $4,830.14.

CASE:                         Michael Tootle v. Wedgewood Homes Realty, LLC, et al.

MOTION FOR SANCTIONS PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 128.7

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Wedgewood Homes Realty, LLC, Wedgewood, LLC, and Neighborhood Stabilization, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff Michael Tootle

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         06/11/24: Complaint filed.

·         06/11/24: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for employment discrimination and wrongful termination. Plaintiff alleges that he was harassed because of his race.

 

Defendants move for sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendants’ Motion for Sanctions is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendants move for sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Defendants request that the Court take judicial notice of (1) the Complaint in the action Tootle v. Wedgewood Homes Realty, LLC, et al., Nevada Eighth Judicial District Court Case No. A-23-873467C (the “Nevada Action”); (2) Defendants’ Motion to Compel Arbitration in the Nevada Action; (3) Plaintiff’s Opposition to the Motion to Compel Arbitration; (4) Defendants’ Reply in Support of the Motion to Compel Arbitration; (5) the Notice of Entry of Order Granting the Motion to Compel Arbitration; (6) Plaintiff’s Complaint in this action; and (7) Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint in this action.

 

            Defendants’ requests are GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

Compliance with the Safe Harbor Provision of Code of Civil Procedure § 128.7(c)(1)

 

Defendants seek sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7. The safe-harbor provision of section 128.7 provides, in relevant part:

 

A motion for sanctions under this section shall be made separately from other motions or requests and shall describe the specific conduct alleged to violate subdivision (b). Notice of motion shall be served as provided in Section 1010, but shall not be filed with or presented to the court unless, within 21 days after service of the motion, or any other period as the court may prescribe, the challenged paper, claim, defense, contention, allegation, or denial is not withdrawn or appropriately corrected.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 128.7(c)(1).)

 

Defendants complied with this provision by serving Plaintiff with the motion on June 27, 2024. (Declaration of Dean A. Rocco ISO Mot. ¶ 2.) Plaintiff therefore had until July 22, 2024 to withdraw the Complaint. This time was further extended to July 24, 2024, as July 22 and 23 were considered court holidays due to a ransomware attack which disabled the Court’s electronic services. Plaintiff did not withdraw the Complaint, and Defendants filed this motion on July 31, 2024. Thus, Defendants met the requirements of the safe harbor provision.

 

Analysis

 

            Defendants seek sanctions on the ground that this action is being presented for an improper purpose and is not warranted by existing law or a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law.

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, if the Court determines that subsection (b) has been violated, it may “impose an appropriate sanction upon the attorneys, law firms, or parties that have violated subdivision (b) or are responsible for the violation.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 128.7(c).) Violations of subsection (b) include presenting allegations “primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation” or are not “warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 128.7(b)(1)-(2).) Sanctions under this section “shall be limited to what is sufficient to deter repetition of this conduct or comparable conduct by others similarly situated.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 128.7(d).) The sanction may include, “if imposed on motion and warranted for effective deterrence, an order directing payment to the movant of some or all of the reasonable attorney’s fees and other expenses incurred as a direct result of the violation.” (Id.)

 

To obtain sanctions on this ground, the moving party must show the “party’s conduct in asserting the claim was objectively unreasonable,” meaning that “any reasonable attorney would agree that [it] is totally and completely without merit.” (Bucar v. Ahmad (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 175, 189.) Indeed, even “the fact that a plaintiff fails to provide a sufficient showing to overcome a demurrer or to survive summary judgment is not, in itself, enough to warrant the imposition of sanctions.” (Peake v. Underwood (2014) 227 Cal.App.4th 428, 448.)

 

            Defendants contend that the conduct of Plaintiff’s counsel in filing this action was objectively unreasonable in light of the history of the Nevada Action. Plaintiff filed a class action complaint in the Eighth Judicial District Court of Nevada on July 5, 2023, alleging that he was discriminated against and ultimately terminated on the basis of his race while employed as a house flipper for Defendants. (RJN Exh. 1.) Plaintiff asserted claims for racial discrimination, hostile work environment harassment, retaliation, wrongful termination, and intentional and negligent infliction of emotional distress. (Id.) Defendants moved to compel arbitration on the basis that Plaintiff had agreed to arbitrate claims arising out his employment and agreed to delegate threshold questions of arbitrability to the arbitrator. (RJN Exh. 2.) The Nevada District Court granted the motion on October 3, 2023, finding that the parties had entered into an arbitration agreement governed by the Federal Arbitration Act, that the threshold questions of enforceability were delegated to the arbitrator, and Plaintiff had failed to demonstrate unconscionability. (RJN Exh. 5.) The Nevada District Court therefore ordered the matter to arbitration and dismissed the Nevada Action without prejudice. (Id.)

 

            Defendants argue that the Complaint in this action filed June 11, 2024, which alleges substantially identical facts and similar legal claims, was filed for an improper purpose and not warranted by law because the matter had already been ordered to arbitration and dismissed by the Nevada District Court. Defendants assert, without elaboration, that Plaintiff’s Complaint in this action is barred by res judicata, collateral estoppel, and comity. Defendants’ assertions do not establish these contentions. Moreover, the Court is not prepared to find that it is objectively unreasonable to refile an action in Los Angeles, California when, as the undisputed facts establish, the Arbitration Agreement requires the arbitration to take place in Los Angeles and be governed by the California Arbitration Act to the extent the act does not conflict with the Federal Arbitration Act. (Defendants’ RJN Exh. 2 § 2(e); (k).) Applying that language, the parties would be obliged to bring an action in Los Angeles, at minimum, to confirm, correct, or vacate an arbitration award pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1285. The Court also rejects the notion that this filing constitutes an attempt to evade the Nevada District Court’s order, as section 1281.4 of the Code of Civil Procedure expressly permits a party to move to stay an action when arbitration of an issue has been ordered by “a court of competent jurisdiction, whether in this State or not,” and that motion, when made, “shall” be granted. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.4.)

 

            For these reasons, the Court is not persuaded that the filing of the Complaint in this action standing alone is objectively unreasonable.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Sanctions is DENIED.
