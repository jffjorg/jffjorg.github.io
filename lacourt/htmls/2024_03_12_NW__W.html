DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19VECV01611    Hearing Date: March 12, 2024    Dept: W

almas & son, llc v. nai capital, inc.

 

DEMURRER with motion to strike the first amended complaint

 

Date of Hearing:        March 12, 2024                     Trial Date:       March 4, 2023

Department:              W                                            Case No.:        19VECV01611

 

Moving Party:             Defendant Patrick Ortiz

Responding Party:     No opposition.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Almas & Son, LLC alleges it entered into an agreement with Defendants Nai Capital, Inc. and Patrick Ortiz where Defendants were to find Plaintiff a suitable tenant for their commercial building. Plaintiff alleges Defendants represented to Plaintiff they found a tenant who would run a legal smoke shop and ran a background check on the tenant; however, these representations were false. Plaintiff alleges Defendants did not run a background check and the tenant operated a cannabis shop in violation of the agreement and as prohibited by the

Department of Cannabis Regulation.  Defendant previously demurred to the First Amended complaint and that demurrer was sustained with leave to amend on November 16, 2023.  Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint on December 18, 2023, for the first time appending the lease agreement.  Defendants now demur to the Second Amended Complaint on the ground that none of the causes of action can survive given contrary provisions in the parties’ lease agreement.   Plaintiff did not file an opposition to the demurrer.  

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff’s failure to file an opposition to the demurrer established its “acquiescence ... the demurrer [wa]s meritorious.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 8.54(c) [“A failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion.”].)

 

But even if plaintiff had opposed the demurrer, the court agrees with the moving party that the language of the lease agreement is fatal to its claims against defendants.  Each of Plaintiff’s alleged causes of action is based on allegations that are directly contradicted by the Lease signed by Plaintiff, which has now been attached to the complaint.   There can be no breach of contract, fraud, breach of fiduciary duty, fraud or unfair business practice or negligence given that Plaintiff’s own representations in the Lease establish not only that no such “representations” were made by the Broker, but, further, that Plaintiff conceded its own investigations were the source of its decision to Lease to the tenant, that Broker has no liability for the fitness, suitability and/or performance of the tenant under the Lease, the use clause in the Lease expressly forbade the tenant’s wrongful conduct and Plaintiff agreed that the Lease was the final agreement of the parties, superseding all others. As a matter of law, Plaintiff cannot allege any representations (false or otherwise by Broker) and cannot establish reliance or causation for any claims when Plaintiff expressly stated that it was relying on Plaintiff’s own investigations and advisors and not upon Broker whom Plaintiff agreed was not liable/responsible for the tenant.  The earlier letter attached as Exhibit 1, which is not legible and is not signed as far as the court can determine by the parties to be bound, is superseded as a matter of law by the later lease.  

 

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained without leave to amend. 

 


Case Number: 22VECV01112    Hearing Date: March 12, 2024    Dept: W

BLACKCOMB GROUP, INC., V. JPMORGAN CHASE BANK, NATIONAL ASSOCIATION

 

DEFENDANT JP MORGAN CHASE BANK n.a.’S MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFF BLACKCOMB GROUP, INC.’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Date of Hearing:        March 12, 2024                                 Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01112

 

Moving Party:            Defendant JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A.

Responding Party:     Plaintiffs Blackcomb Group, Inc.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 4, 2022, Plaintiff Blackcomb Group, Inc. filed a complaint against Defendant JP Morgan Chase Bank National Association for breach of contract, accounting, and conversion. Plaintiff opened an account with Chase in November 2020, ending in 3103. Plaintiff alleges it made an initial deposit of $3,784.29 and a second deposit of $23,000.00 for a total amount of $26,784.29. Plaintiff further alleges Chase closed the account ending in 3103 without prior notice to Plaintiff (when the account balance was $26,784.29) and sent Plaintiff a check reimbursing them for the $3,784.29 but failed to reimburse Plaintiff for the $23,000.00 deposit.

 

Defendant moved for judgment on the pleadings. On December 26, 2023, this court granted the motion, in part, with leave to amend and on January 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A.’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND

 

discussion

 

Defendant JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. moves to strike Paragraph 20 and Plaintiff’s prayer for relief with respect to its cause of action for breach of contract, paragraph 2 on the grounds that Plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees is based on an indemnification provision, not a provision for attorneys’ fees, which is not subject to Civil Code Section 1717, and is therefore an irrelevant, false, and improper matter inserted in any pleading and should therefore be stricken.

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (CCP §436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (CCP §436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (CCP §436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (CCP §437.)

 

Chase Bank argues the Deposit Account Agreement (“DAA”) does not contain an attorneys’ fees provision subject to Civil Code Section 1717. Specifically, the pertinent portion of the DAA states:

 

If we receive any legal process relating to you or your account, you authorize us to comply with it. “Legal process” means any document that appears to have the force of law that requires us to hold or pay out funds from your account, including a garnishment, attachment, execution, levy or similar order.

…

If any action, including administrative proceedings, garnishment, tax levies, restraining orders or another action is brought against you or your account, you will be liable to us for any loss, cost or expense (including attorneys’ fees) resulting from our compliance with any legal process. …

 

Chase Bank argues this provision is an indemnity provision, not an attorneys’ fees provision, and it is therefore not subject to Civil Code Section 1717. Accordingly, there is no basis for Plaintiff’s claim to attorneys’ fees and Plaintiff’s allegations regarding attorneys’ fees should be stricken. Chase Bank contends although Section IX, subsection K includes attorneys’ fees in the enumerated items recoverable, “[a] provision including attorney fees as an item of loss in an indemnity clause is not a provision for attorney fees in an action to enforce the contract.” (Myers Bldg. Ind., Ltd. v. Interface Technology, Inc. (1993) 13 Cal.App.4th 949, 971.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the prayer for attorney fees should not be stricken because the plain language of the clause does not indicate it is part of an indemnity agreement. Plaintiff contends in the event that Defendant here is the prevailing party, the clause could arguably be used by Defendant for a fees award and since Section 1717(a) makes that reciprocal, Plaintiff is also entitled to its fees if he is the prevailing party.

 

The court finds the request for attorney fees improper. “Under Civil Code section 1717, a reciprocal right to attorney fees in all parties to a contract arises where the contract accords a right to such fees to one party but not the other.  However, the inclusion of attorney fees as an item of loss in a third party claim indemnity provision does not constitute a provision for the award of attorney fees in an action on contract as is required to trigger operation of Civil Code section 1717.” (Campbell v. Scripps Bank (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 1328, 1336-1337 (internal citations omitted).) One exception is when the indemnity provision calls for enforcing the indemnity agreement. (See Baldwin Builders v. Coast Plastering Corp. (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 1339, 1345 holding the express language of the attorney fee clauses authorizes the recovery of attorney fees where one of the parties to the agreement brings an action to enforce the indemnity.) Here, the attorney's fees clause at issue is included in an indemnity provision but does not allow for attorney fees in enforcing the agreement. As such, attorney fees are not available.

 

Accordingly, Defendant JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A.’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.


Case Number: 22VECV01159    Hearing Date: March 12, 2024    Dept: W

FAUSTINO PACHECO MORALES V. RAVIT OHANA, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS RAVIT OHANA AND SHARON OHANA’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT AND DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:        March 12, 2024                                             Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                                    Case No.:        22VECV01159

 

Moving Party:            Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Faustino Pacheco Morales and Defendants Ravit and Sharon Ohana entered into an agreement whereby Plaintiff was to transport dirt from one of Defendants’ properties to another. Plaintiff submitted invoices for payments; however, Defendants have failed to pay.

 

On August 15, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants asserting causes of action for breach of contract, common counts, and quantum meruit. 

 

Request for entry of default was entered February 14, 2023. The court then denied Plaintiff’s request for entry of default judgment. Plaintiff then sought request for entry of default again on June 12, 2023.

 

On April 19, 2023, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint. Request for entry of default was again entered June 12, 2023 for Ravit Ohana and June 15, 2023 for Sharon Ohana. The court entered default judgment on November 9, 2023.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Defendants’ Sharon Ohan and Ravit Ohana’s Motions to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendants Ravit Ohana and Sharon Ohana move the court for an order to vacate and set aside the Default and Default Judgment entered against Defendants pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure section 473(d) and California Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section  473(d) provides that the “court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” Where a service of summons has not resulted in actual notice to a party in time to defend the action, the party may move the court to set aside default and default judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 473.5, subd. (a).)  The motion must be made no later than two years after entry of default or 180 days after service of a written notice that default or default judgment has been entered.  (Ibid.)  The party must act with diligence upon learning of the judgment.  (Trackman v. Kenney (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 175, 180; Code Civ. Proc., §473.5, subd. (a).) 

 

Defendants argue they were never served with the summons and complaint and the proofs of service are facially invalid. Defendants point to the proofs of service where service purportedly occurred on September 29, 2022; January 9, 2023; and April 23, 2023. On September 29, 2022, Defendants were purportedly served by substituted service on Maria Gonzalez; however, Defendants contend they never authorized Ms. Gonzalez to accept service of the complaint on their behalf. As to the January 9, 2023 proofs of service, Defendants argue they are facially invalid because 3b. states substituted service occurred but then checks off 5a. for personal service. Regarding the April 23, 2023 proofs of service, Defendants, under penalty of perjury, attest the proof of service is completely false and states they were never personally served with any documents related to this case. (Ravit Decl. ¶7; Sharon Decl. ¶9.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendants were properly served on October 13, 2022 and April 23, 2023 by a registered process server and Defendants have not been diligent in in seeking to set aside the default and default judgment. Plaintiff contends Defendants had knowledge of the lawsuit as early as February 14, 2023 when Plaintiff first sought to enter default against Defendants. Moreover, Plaintiff contends Defendants do not have a meritorious defense.

 

The court finds service improper and grants Defendants’ motions to set aside default and default judgment. Plaintiff presents the declaration of process server Ron Wilkinson. Wilkinson attests as to the January 9, 2023 and April 23, 2023 service of summons and complaint, Wilkinson knocked on the door and Ms. Sharon Ohana opened the door. (Wilkinson Decl. ¶4, 6.) Wilkinson attests he said “Sharon?” and to which a female purportedly responded yes. (Wilkinson Decl. ¶4.) Wilkinson further attests he asked Sharon for her husband, Ravit. (Wilkinson Decl. ¶6.) However, Sharon Ohana is a male and Ravit Ohana is a female. (Sharon Decl. ¶3, Exh. A; Ravit Decl. ¶3, Exh. A.) As such, the court finds Defendants evidence against service sufficiently rebuts that presumption. (See Lebel v. Mai (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 1154, 1163.) The court further notes Defendants have established a meritorious defense and it is the policy of the law to favor, whenever possible, a hearing on the merits.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Sharon Ohan and Ravit Ohana’s Motions to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment is GRANTED.
