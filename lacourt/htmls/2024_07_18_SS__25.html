TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Plaintiff Maxwell + Bessie, Inc.’s Motion for Evidence Sanctions is DENIED.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On February 23, 2023, Plaintiff Maxwell + Bessie, Inc. (“MB”) filed an action against Defendant Michael Onagbesan aka Abayomi Oladapo Onagbesan, dba Ona International Inc., Onas Inc., and Onas Media Network (“Onagbesan”) for breach of written and oral agreements and breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, arising out of three alleged agreements between the parties.  Plaintiff filed an Amended Complaint on June 27, 2023.

 

On August 3, 2023, Defendant, in propria persona, filed a General Denial.

 

On March 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the following discovery motions: (1) Motion to Compel Defendant’s Initial Responses to Specially Prepared Interrogatories (Set 1) and (2) Motion to Compel Defendant’s Initial Responses to Request for Production (Set 1).  On April 18, 2024, the Court granted both of the discovery motions and ordered Defendant to serve responses within 20 days of notice of the Court’s order.

 

On June 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Evidence Sanctions against Defendant (“Motion”).  No opposition has been filed. 

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Where a party willfully disobeys a discovery order, courts have discretion to impose terminating, issue, evidence, or monetary sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010(d), (g), 2023.030; R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.040 requires that “[a] request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.”  Furthermore, the notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.040.)

 

Monetary sanctions may be imposed “ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct…unless [the Court] finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a).)

 

Issue sanctions may be imposed “ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(b).)

 

Evidence sanctions may be imposed “by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(c).)

 

In more extreme cases, the Court may also impose terminating sanctions by “striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings,” “staying further proceedings,” “dismissing the action, or any part of the action,” or “rending a judgment by default” against the party misusing the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(d).)  The court should look to the totality of the circumstances in determining whether terminating sanctions are appropriate.  (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77¿Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)  Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.  (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196¿Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.)  “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.”  (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.)  “Although in extreme cases a court has the authority to order a terminating sanction as a first measure [citations], a terminating sanction should generally not be imposed until the court has attempted less severe alternatives and found them to be unsuccessful and/or the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective.”  (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible and Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604-605.)

 

In City of Los Angeles v. PricewaterhouseCoopers, LLC, the Court ruled that Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2023.010 and 2023.030 list the available discovery sanctions but do not independently authorize a court to impose specific sanctions.  (PriceewaterhouseCoopers (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 466, 503-504.)  Thus, a court’s authority to impose the sanctions specified in §§ 2023.010 and 2023.030 must arise from a statute governing a particular method of discovery.  (Ibid.)

 

III.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for a court order imposing evidentiary sanctions against Defendant for failure to comply with the Court’s April 18, 2024, Order.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel, James Lee Reynolds, states that on the same day as the Court’s Order, Plaintiff served, via regular US mail and email, Defendant with the Notices of Court rulings ordering him to comply with the discovery requests within twenty days of the Order.  (Reynolds Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 2.)  Factoring in service time, Defendant’s Code-compliant responses had to be served no later than May 20, 2024.  (Ibid. at ¶ 3.)  Around April 22, 2024, Defendant served “a non-descriptive pile of documents, with bate-stamped numbers 1 through 145.”  (Ibid. at ¶ 4, Ex. 3.)  On April 21, 2024, Defendant sent an email to Plaintiff indicating that some of the documents he had mailed had been returned to him; this was due to a missing unit number in the address.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. 4.)  By May 20, 2024, Plaintiff had not received the Court ordered verified, Code-compliant initial responses and production .  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6-7, Ex. 5.). Counsel states that it is not clear from the “pile of non-descriptive legal documents” if these documents are a response to the discovery as the caption lists October 31, 2023, and does not identify which document is a responsive document to a requested category.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)

 

Plaintiff requests that Defendant be prohibited from placing the following into evidence:

 

1)      1.1: All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s First Cause of Action (Breach of Written Agreement).

2)      1.2: All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s Second Cause of Action (Breach of Oral Agreement).

3)      1.3: All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff s Third Cause of Action (Breach of Oral Agreement).

4)      1.4: All facts, witnesses, documents, custodian of documents, physical evidence, and custodian of physical evidence supporting Defendant’s non-liability as to Plaintiff’s Fourth Cause of Action (Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith).

5)      1.5: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) Plaintiff’s claims in this action (starting January 1, 2020, to the present).

6)      1.6: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) to any communication between Defendant and Plaintiff regarding Plaintiff’s claims in this action (starting January 1, 2020, to the present).

7)      1.7: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) to any notes between Defendant and Plaintiff regarding Plaintiff’s claims in this action (starting January 1,2020, to the present).

8)      1.8: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) to any payment of monies by Defendant to Plaintiff regarding Plaintiff’s claims in this action (starting January 1, 2020, to the present).

9)      1.9: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) Defendant’s alleged non-liability to Plaintiff regarding Plaintiff’s claims in this action (starting January 1,2020, to the present).

10)  1.10: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) documents required to be identified and disclosed in response to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set 1.

11)  1.11: Any document, communication, audio media, visual media, physical evidence or electronic media that mentions, refers to, or relates to (which as to each is discoverable by a reasonable search and inquiry) physical evidence required to be identified and disclosed in response to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set 1.

 

Plaintiff argues that evidence sanctions are proper and appropriate, and a showing of willfulness is not required.  (Mot. p. 7.)  Defendant was served with the Special Interrogatories and Request for Production around August 4, 2023, and was subsequently ordered to provide verified, Code-compliant initial responses.  (Ibid. at pp. 8-9.)  However, as of the date of the instant Motion, Defendant has refused to comply with the discovery request and the Court’s Order.  (Ibid. at pp. 8-9.)  Thus, the Court should prohibit Defendant from introducing any evidence at trial relating to the categories listed in the Motion.  (Ibid. at p. 9.)

 

            Defendant has not opposed the Motion.

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.290 governs the imposition of sanctions in regard to failure to respond to interrogatories.  Section 2030.290(c) states that:

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey an order compelling answers, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

(Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(c), emphasis added.)

 

Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.300 governs the imposition of sanctions in regard to failure to respond to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.  Section 2031.300(c) states:

 

… the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey the order compelling a response, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

(Code of Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c), emphasis added.)

 

Based on §§ 2030.290(c) and 2031.300(c), the Court has authority to impose any of the sanctions specified in § 2030.030.

 

When determining whether evidentiary sanctions are appropriate, merely failing to obey a Court order is sufficient and a showing of willful disobedience is not required.  (Aghaian v. Minassian, 64 Cal.App.5th 603, 618-620.)  The Court has discretion to determine whether sanctions are appropriate.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2030.210 through 2030.310 set forth the requirements for providing Code-compliant responses to interrogatories.  Similarly, §§ 2031.210 through 2031.310 set forth the requirements for providing Code-compliant responses to a request for production.  Plaintiff argues that the Defendant has disobeyed the Court’s Order compelling compliance with Plaintiff’s discovery requests because he has served “a non-descriptive pile of documents” and, as a result, seeks evidentiary sanctions regarding eleven matters that prohibit Defendant from presenting any evidence regarding non-liability or relating to Plaintiff’s claims.

 

In Rutledge v. Hewlett-Packard Co., the Court reaffirmed the trial court’s broad discretion when imposing discovery sanctions.  (Rutledge, 238 Cal.App.4th 1164, 1193.)  The Court explained that this discretion must be exercised for the purpose of compelling disclosure of discoverable information not solely to punish the offending party.  (Ibid.)  Moreover, “the sanction chosen should not provide a windfall to the other party, by putting the prevailing party in a better position than if he or she had obtained the discovery sought and it had been favorable.  [Citations.]”  (Ibid.)  In Rutledge, the Court found that the trial court’s refusal to order evidentiary sanctions, despite seven prior orders to comply with discovery, was not an abuse of discretion because if sanctions had been granted, they “would provide a windfall to appellants relieving them of their burden of proving their theory of liability.”  (Ibid. at 1194.)

 

First, the Court notes that Plaintiff has not submitted any of the documents produced by Defendant and characterized as a “non-descriptive pile of documents” other than Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration claiming that the responses are not Code-compliant.  Second, the evidentiary sanctions sought are broad and prohibit Defendant from producing any evidence regarding liability or relating to Plaintiff’s claims, thus, eliminating Plaintiff’s burden to prove their case.  These sanctions will not serve the purpose of compelling the disclosure of discoverable information, but instead, will provide a windfall to the Plaintiff. Accordingly, the Court does not find that the evidentiary sanctions sought are appropriate at this stage in the proceedings.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Evidence Sanctions is denied.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Plaintiff Maxwell + Bessie, Inc.’s Motion for Evidence Sanctions is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE. 

 

The Parties are ordered to meet and confer via telephone regarding outstanding discovery responses, including a discussion as to which documents produced to date i.e. “non-descriptive pile of documents” pertain to items 1.1 through 1.11, within the next 20 days. 

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On July 21, 2023, Plaintiff Allen R. King (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendant Menachem Grossbaum (“Defendant”) for breach of contract and common counts.  Plaintiff alleges that he was hired to perform legal services for Defendant, but Defendant failed to pay Plaintiff’s legal fees and costs.

 

On August 23, 2023, Defendant filed a Notice of Stay of Proceedings due to arbitration of attorney fees and costs under Business and Professions Code § 6201.  The Court notes that Defendant did not file a motion or petition to compel arbitration and did not attach a copy of request for arbitration to the Notice, as required.

On June 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Terminate Stay of Proceedings.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

A party may seek a court order compelling the parties to arbitrate a dispute covered by the agreement.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.)  Generally, the court must grant the request unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting¿rulings on common issues.

 

When seeking to compel arbitration, the initial burden lies with the moving party to demonstrate the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by prepondernace of evidence.  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841-42; Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021), 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 164-65.)  It is sufficient for the moving party to produce a copy of the arbitration agreement or set forth the agreement’s provisions.  (Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  The burden then shifts to the opposing party to prove by a preponderance of evidence any defense to enforcement of the contract or the arbitration clause.  (Ruiz, 232 Cal.App.4th at 842; Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  Subsequently, the moving party must establish with the preponderance of admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties.  (Ibid.)  The trial court then weighs all the evidence submitted and uses its discretion to make a final determination.  (Ibid.)  “California law, ‘like [federal law], reflects a strong policy favoring arbitration agreements and requires close judicial scrutiny of waiver claims.’”  (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 31.)  If the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until arbitration is completed.  (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1281.4.)

 

III.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves for a court order terminating the stay of proceedings in the instant case.  He states that Defendant requested arbitration with the Attorney-Client Mediation and Arbitration Services (ACMAS) of the Los Angeles County Bar and subsequently filed a Notice of Stay of Proceedings with the Court.  (King Decl. ¶ 3.)  On November 6, 2023, Plaintiff received an email from the ACMAS case coordinator notifying him that Defendant did not wish to move forward with the arbitration.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  Since then, Plaintiff has not received any additional communication regarding the arbitration.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s Motion to be moot.  Given that Defendant did not follow the proper procedure to stay the proceedings by seeking a court order compelling arbitration, the proceedings were not stayed and trial is currently set for January 17, 2025.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Terminate Stay of Proceedings is denied as moot.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the reasons discussed above,

 

Plaintiff Allen R. King’s Motion to Terminate Stay of Proceedings is DENIED as MOOT.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On March 14, 2024, Petitioner Cordova Estates (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition to Declare Mobile Home Abandoned (“Petition”) against Respondents Laverle Minnie Durham (“Durham”) and Michael Len Coutu (“Coutu”).  Pursuant to Civil Code § 798.61, Petitioner seeks a declaration that the mobilehome located at 27361 Sierra Highway Space 246, Canyon Country, CA 91351, described as 1965 American, Decal Number AAK2033, Serial No. S53323/S53323B (“Mobile Home”) is abandoned.  (Pet. ¶ 5.)  Petitioner also seeks storage charges in the amount of $916.80 per month owed from August 1, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)

 

On June 27, 2024, default was entered against Defendant Coutu and on July 16, 2024, default was entered against Defendant Durham.

 

No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.    Determination of “Abandoned Mobilehome”—Civ. Code § 798.61(a)(1)

 

            Pursuant to Civil Code § 798.61(a)(1), an “‘abandoned mobilehome’ means a mobilehome about which all of the following are true:

 

(A) It is located in a mobilehome park on a site for which no rent has been paid to the management for the preceding 60 days.

 

(B) It is unoccupied.

 

(C) A reasonable person would believe it to be abandoned.

 

(D) It is not permanently affixed to the land.”

 

            Petitioner submits the declaration of Nancy Must, Property Supervisor for Cordova Estates.  (Must Decl. ¶ 3.)  According to Must and the California Department of Housing and Community Development, the agency responsible for licensing and titling mobilehomes, Respondents Durham and Couto are the registered owners of the mobilehome located at 27361 Sierra Highway Space 246, Canyon Country, CA 91351, described as 1965 American[1], Decal Number AAK2033, Serial No. S53323/S53323.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 5-6, Ex. 2.)  There are no legal owners of the mobilehome.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5.)

 

            Must explains that, to the best of her knowledge and belief, the Mobilehome is unoccupied and was unoccupied prior to September 5, 2023, the date of posting the 30-Day Notice.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7-8, Ex. 1.)  The Mobilehome is not permanently affixed to the space and is located in the Park on a site for which no rent or storage fees have been paid since July 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 6, 8.)

 

            The Court finds that the Mobilehome falls within the Civil Code § 798.61’s definition of “abandoned.”

 

B.     Notice of Belief of Abandonment—Civ. Code § 798.61(b)

 

Civil Code § 798.61(b) requires:

 

“After determining a mobilehome in a mobilehome park to be an abandoned mobilehome, the management shall post a notice of belief of abandonment on the mobilehome for not less than 30 days, and shall deposit copies of the notice in the United States mail, postage prepaid, addressed to the homeowner at the last known address and to any known registered owner, if different from the homeowner, and to any known holder of a security interest in the abandoned mobilehome.  This notice shall be mailed by registered or certified mail with a return receipt requested.”

 

On September 5, 2023, a 30-day Notice of Belief of Abandonment was posted on the Mobilehome.  (Must Decl. ¶ 8, Ex. 1; 6-27-24 Proofs of Service - Trevillyan Decl. ¶¶ 2-3.)  On the same day, the Notice was mailed via certified mail, return receipt requested, to Respondents at 27361 Sierra Highway Space 246, Canyon Country, CA 91351.  (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds that Petitioner has satisfied the requirements of § 798.61(b).

 

C.     Petition for Judicial Declaration—Civ. Code § 798.61(c)

 

Civil Code § 798.61(c)(1) states:

 

“Thirty or more days following posting pursuant to subdivision (b), the management may file a petition in the superior court in the county in which the mobilehome park is located, for a judicial declaration of abandonment of the mobilehome. A proceeding under this subdivision is a limited civil case. Copies of the petition shall be served upon the homeowner, any known registered owner, and any known person having a lien or security interest of record in the mobilehome by posting a copy on the mobilehome and mailing copies to those persons at their last known addresses by registered or certified mail with a return receipt requested in the United States mail, postage prepaid.”

 

On March 14, 2024, more than 30 days after posting and serving the Notice of Belief of Abandonment, Petitioner filed the instant Petition.  Petitioner has submitted proof that the Petition was served on Respondents Durham and Coutu on May 18, 2024, by posting a copy of the Petition at 27361 Sierra Highway Space 246, Canyon Country, CA 91351, and mailing the Petition to the same address via certified mail, return receipt requested.  (6-17-24 Proofs of Service.)  It appears that the envelope addressed to Coutu was returned to sender indicating that he is deceased, and the envelope addressed to Durham has been returned to sender as there is no mail receptable.  (Ibid. at p. 3.)

 

            The Court finds the service requirements of § 798.61(c) have been satisfied.

 

D.    Charges, Attorney’s Fees, and Costs

 

Civil Code § 798.61(d)(2) states:

 

“If, at the hearing, the petitioner shows by a preponderance of the evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome has been satisfied and no party establishes an interest therein at the hearing and tenders all past due rent and other charges, the court shall enter a judgment of abandonment, determine the amount of charges to which the petitioner is entitled, and award attorney's fees and costs to the petitioner.”

 

Petitioner seeks monthly storage charges of $916.80 per month for eleven (11) months, from August 1, 2023, through June 2024, for a total of $10,084.80.  (Must Decl. ¶¶ 9-10.)  The monthly charge is calculated based on the amount of rent Respondents were paying for the space at the time the Mobilehome was abandoned.  (Ibid., Ex. 3.)  Petitioner argues that the amount requested is reasonable because a similarly sized mobilehome would incur storage charges of $86.50 per day in the City of Los Angles, totaling $2,595.00 per month.  (Ibid. at ¶ 10.)

 

Petitioner also seeks attorney’s fees and costs but does not request a specific sum.  (Pet. p. 3.)  In its proposed order, Petitioner lists costs of $225.  This represents the cost of filing. 

 

The Court finds the storage charges requested to be reasonable and grants Petitioner’s request for storage charges in the amount of $10,084.80, plus $225 for costs.

 

The Court notes that the proposed order lists an erroneous description of the mobilehome as “1992 Goldenwest.”  Petitioner is ordered to file a corrected proposed order.

 

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Petitioner Cordova Estates’s unopposed Petition to Declare Mobilehome Abandoned is GRANTED.  Petitioner is awarded $10,084.80 in storage charges plus $225 for costs, for a total of $10,309.80.
