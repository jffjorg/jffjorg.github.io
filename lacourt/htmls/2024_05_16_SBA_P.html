Case Number: 23TRCV01505    Hearing Date: May 16, 2024    Dept: P

(1) MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES AND IMPOSING MONETARY SANCTIONS

(2) MOTION FOR ISSUE SANCTIONS AND MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT FOR THE FAILURE TO COMPLY WITH THE COURT’S ORDER

The court considered the moving papers. No opposition or reply has been filed.

RULINGS

The MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL RESPONSES AND IMPOSING MONETARY SANCTIONS is GRANTED in its entirety. Defendant 11651-11652 York Avenue, LLC is ordered to serve its initial responses, without objections, to plaintiff Wilfredo Cardenas’ Special Interrogatories (Set Two) within 30 days of this ruling. The defendant is further ordered to pay the plaintiff sanctions of $1,084.80.

The MOTION FOR ISSUE SANCTIONS AND MONETARY SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT FOR THE FAILURE TO COMPLY WITH THE COURT’S ORDER is GRANTED IN PART and DENIED IN PART as follows. The request to impose issue sanctions on defendant 11651-11652 York Avenue, LLC is DENIED without prejudice. The

request to impose monetary sanctions against the defendant is GRANTED. Defendant 11651-11652 York Avenue, LLC is ordered to pay plaintiff Wilfredo Cardenas additional sanctions of $2,350.40. The defendant is further ordered to comply with this court’s order dated February 28, 2024, within 30 days of this ruling. If the defendant fails to timely comply with the order, the plaintiff may file a renewed motion to impose issue sanctions on the defendant.

BACKGROUND

On 5/11/2023, plaintiff Wilfredo Cardenas (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants 11651-11652 York Avenue, LLC (“Defendant”), and Does 1 to 20, inclusive, asserting causes of action for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of implied warranty of habitability, (3) breach of implied warranty of quiet enjoyment, (4) negligence, (5) nuisance, and (6) retaliatory acts.

The Complaint alleges the following. At all relevant times, Plaintiff was a resident and tenant of 11651 York Ave #4, Hawthorne, CA 90250 (the “Property”). Compl., ¶ 1. From the day Plaintiff moved into the Property and throughout his tenancy, there have been substandard conditions affecting the habitability of the Property. Compl., ¶ 13. Those habitability problems include maggot infestation, electric issues, and severe water damage on the ceilings. Compl., ¶¶ 14, 15. The defendants were aware of those conditions but not only failed to cure them, but refused to do so throughout the lease. Compl., ¶ 28.

On 10/16/2023, Plaintiff amended the complaint to substitute J.K. Residential Services, Inc. for the defendant sued fictitiously as Doe 1.

On 1/22/2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to his Form Interrogatories (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), Requests for Production (Set One), and Requests for Admission (Set One) that were served on Defendant on August 7, 2023. No opposition to the motion was filed.

On 2/28/2024, the court granted the motion’s request to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Nos. 4.1 and 12.1, Special Interrogatories Nos. 4, 5, 6, 9, 15, and 42, Requests for Production, Nos. 7, 8, 9, and 21, and Requests for Admission Nos. 3, 4, and 9. The court also ordered Defendant to pay sanctions of $1,898.40 to Plaintiff within 30 days.

On 3/22/2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel Defendant’s initial responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set Two) and to impose monetary sanctions against Defendant and its attorney of record, Ronald Whitaker, jointly and severally.

On 3/26/2024, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application to shorten the time for the hearings on the instant motions.

On 3/26/2024, Defendant filed its opposition to the ex parte application, admitting that its discovery responses were overdue, but arguing (among other things) that it has been diligently attempting to compel additional information that the court had ordered Defendant to produce.

On 3/27/2024, the court continued the hearing on the ex parte application to 4/2/2024.

On 4/2/2024, the court denied the ex parte application.

As of 5/15/2024, Defendant has not filed any oppositions to the instant motions.

LEGAL STANDARD

A. Legal Standard for Motions to Compel Initial Responses to Interrogatories

Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the responding party shall serve responses to the discovery unless, on motion of the propounding party, the court has shortened the time for response, or, on motion of the responding party, the court has extended the time for response. Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).

Failure to serve timely responses results in the responding party waiving their right to object to the discovery unless the court, on motion, relieves the party from the waiver. Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).

In addition, if a party fails to serve timely responses, the party making the discovery request may move for an order compelling responses. Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).

Monetary sanctions shall be imposed against any party who unsuccessfully opposes motions to compel responses to interrogatories unless the court finds that “the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).

B. Legal Standard for Sanctions Based on Misuse of the Discovery Process

“Misuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. ¶ …. ¶ …. ¶ (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery.” Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (a)(1).

“To the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method or any other provision of this title, the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose the following sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process:

(a) The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. …. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

(b) The court may impose an issue sanction ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.

(c) The court may impose an evidence sanction ….

(d) The court may impose a terminating sanction ….”

Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.

DISCUSSION

A. Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories

Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that on February 6, 2024, he served Defendant with Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories (Set Two) by electronic service, but no responses have been served. Declaration of Joshua Greer, filed March 22, 2024 (“Greer Decl.”), ¶¶ 4, 5; Exhibit A – a copy of the interrogatories.

Defendant has not filed any opposition to the motion.

Accordingly, in light of Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration, the request to compel Defendant’s responses to the interrogatories is granted.

Plaintiff moves for an order imposing sanctions of $1,084.80 against Defendant and its counsel of record, Ronald Whitaker, jointly and severally. The requested amount consists of 2.4 hours Plaintiff’s counsel spent on the moving papers at a billing rate of $452 per hour. Greer Decl., ¶ 6.

The Court finds the requested sanctions reasonable and grants them against Defendant only, not defense counsel.

B. Motion for Issue and Monetary Sanctions

Plaintiff also moves for an order imposing issue and monetary sanctions against Defendant for disobeying the Court’s order on February 28, 2024, requiring the Defendant to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Nos. 4.1 and 12.1, Special Interrogatories Nos. 4, 5, 6, 9, 15, and 42, Requests for Production, Nos. 7, 8, 9, and 21, and Requests for Admission Nos. 3, 4, and 9.

As issue sanctions, Plaintiff seeks an order:

(1) Preventing Defendant from introducing any evidence, either directly or tangentially through the testimony of any expert witness or expert witness

disclosures or otherwise, pertaining to maintenance performed by Defendant in Plaintiff’s rental unit related to mold and mold remediation;

(2) Preventing Defendant from arguing that they did not have any notice of Plaintiff suffering from health conditions;

(3) Preventing Defendant from introducing any evidence, directly or tangentially, of any maintenance or corrective efforts pertaining to water leaks from January 2016 until present; and

(4) That RFA 9 be deemed admitted.

Motion, p. 2:11-20.

Plaintiff’s counsel attests to the following facts in support of the motion. Defendant has not provided the discovery responses despite the parties conferring regarding the discovery, the parties attending an Informal Discovery Conference on November 21, 2023, the court’s order on February 28, 2024, and Plaintiff’s offer to give Defendant until March 20, 2024, to serve the responses. Declaration of Joshua Greer, Esq. In Support Of Plaintiff’s Motion for Issue and Monetary Sanctions, filed March 22, 2024 (“Greer Decl.”), ¶¶ 2-11. Instead, on the deadline that Plaintiff’s counsel had proposed, March 20, 2024, defense counsel emailed stating that Defendant planned to serve the discovery responses by that date, but Defendant was still gathering documents. Greer Decl., ¶ 12. Defense counsel requested until March 27, 2024, but Plaintiff filed this motion on March 22, 2024, before that date. Greer Decl., ¶ 12.

Neither party has filed any papers updating the court regarding whether the defendant eventually served the required discovery responses.

“The trial court has broad discretion in selecting discovery sanctions, subject to reversal only for abuse. [Citations.] The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery and, in choosing a sanction, should ‘“attempt [ ] to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.”’ [Citation.]” Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992 (“Doppes”).

“The trial court cannot impose sanctions for misuse of the discovery process as a punishment.” Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.

Further, “discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination. ‘Discovery sanctions “should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.”’ [Citations.]”. Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.

“If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.” Doppes, supra, 174 Cal.App.4th at p. 992.

The court finds that Defendant has misused the discovery process by failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery.

However, the court finds that issue sanctions are not warranted at this time, given that Defendant indicated it was willing to comply with the court’s order dated February 28, 2024, but only needed more time.

Instead, the court finds additional monetary sanctions proper.

Plaintiff seeks sanctions of $8,407.20. The amount consists of 4.2 hours counsel spent on the moving papers, 1 hour counsel anticipated spending on the reply, and 1 hour counsel anticipates spending at the hearing, a total of 6.2 hours at counsel’s billing rate of $452 per hour (equals $2,802.40) “multiplied by three for further sanctions for failure to produce responses and contempt of court equat[ing] to a total of $8,407.20 ….” Greer Decl., ¶ 16.

As stated above, the court cannot impose sanctions for misuse of the discovery process as a punishment. In addition, no reply was filed.

Accordingly, the court will only impose sanctions of $2,350.40 (i.e., 5.2 hours of counsel’s time at a billing rate of $452 per hour) against Defendant.

Payment of sanctions is to be made within 30 days of the receipt of notice of this ruling.

Case Number: 23TRCV02149    Hearing Date: May 16, 2024    Dept: P

Moving Parties: Defendant City of Los Angeles

Responding Party: Plaitniff Kristine Lopez Parks

Motion for Order for Undertaking Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1030

The court considered the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

RULING

The motion for undertaking pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1030 is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

On July 3, 2023, plaintiff Kristine Lopez Parks (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against defendants Los Angeles International Airport (“LAX”), City of Los Angeles (“City”), County of Los Angeles (“County”), State of California, and California Department of Transportation, arising from an incident where Plaintiff fell while riding an escalator when it malfunctioned. The Complaint alleges the sole cause of action premises liability – dangerous condition of public property.

On November 6, 2023, City filed its answer to the Complaint.

On December 1, 2023, the People of the Statement of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation (erroneously sued as “State of California” and “California Department of Transportation”) filed its answer to the Complaint.

On February 8, 2024, Plaintiff voluntarily dismissed the People of the Statement of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation from this action.

On April 9, 2024, City filed the instant motion for order for undertaking pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1030. On May 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed her opposition. Thereafter, on May 7, 2024, City filed its reply.

LEGAL STANDARD

“When the plaintiff in an action or special proceeding resides out of the state, or is a foreign corporation, the defendant may at any time apply to the court by noticed motion for an order requiring the plaintiff to file an undertaking.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (a).) The plaintiff is not required to file an undertaking unless “there is a reasonable possibility that the moving defendant will obtain judgment in the action or special proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (b).) The defendant is not required to show that there is no possibility that the plaintiff can prevail at trial, but rather must demonstrate only that it is reasonably possible that the defendant will prevail. (Baltayan v. Estate of Getemyan (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1427, 1432-1433.) The defendant must also submit an affidavit stating the nature and amount of costs and attorney’s fees the defendant has incurred, and expects to incur throughout the remainder of the proceedings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (b).)

If the court grants the motion and the plaintiff fails to file the undertaking within the time allowed, the court must dismiss the plaintiff’s action or special proceeding as to the defendant in whose favor the order requiring the undertaking was made. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1030, subd. (d).)

DISCUSSION

City moves for the order requiring Plaintiff to post an undertaking in the amount of $72,500 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1030.

Here, it is undisputed that Plaintiff is a resident of New Mexico. (Rosenthal Decl. ¶ 5, Exh. B, Plaintiff’s Response to Form Interrogatories, No. 2.5.

Plaintiff’s complaint states that Plaintiff was injured because “a defective, cracked, broken and/or deteriorated escalator was allowed to exist and be left in disrepair” and that as “plaintiff was ascending on the escalator, suddenly and without warning, the escalator malfunctioned [which] caused Plaintiff to lose his (sic) balance and fall and sustain injuries and damages.” (Compl. p. 7.)

In its motion, Defendant provides evidence that the escalator was regularly maintained and inspected and that there were no reports of “malfunctions, mechanical issues, or erratic behavior” of the escalator in question prior to the incident. (Andrade Decl. ¶ 3.) Defendant also provides evidence of Plaintiff’s contemporary statement to the police, wherein she stated that she “arrived on a Southwest flight, grabbed her two bags and proceeded to the up escalator from baggage…she walked onto the escalator while pulling her luggage behind her; at about the halfway point, her luggage seemed to get stuck [and] pulled her causing her to loose (sic) her balance and (illegible).” (Sanatana Decl., Exh. A, p.2.) Defendant’s attorney expects to incur various recoverable expenses, including: 1) Appearance Fees ($500); 2) Transcription Services: ($5,000); 3) Expert Fees ($50,000); 4) Independent Medical Exam ($5,000); 5) Subpoena Service Fees ($3,500); 6) Court Reporter Fees ($4,000); 7) Witness Fees ($2500); and, 8) Jury Fees ($1,000). Therefore, Defendant is seeking an undertaking by plaintiff in the amount of $72,500.

In her opposition, Plaintiff asserts that she was handicapped at the time of the incident and was directed to the escalator by an airport employee when an elevator was available. The court finds that this assertion is contrary to the assertions in the complaint and in the various discovery responses provided to the Defendant. Therefore, the court disregards Plaintiff’s declaration as to her being handicapped and seeking wheelchair assistance.

Given the evidence presented by the parties, the court finds that Defendant has shown “it is reasonably possible it will prevail at trial.” The court also finds that the requested undertaking is reasonable considering the technical expertise required at trial to provide evidence as to escalator maintenance as well as medical expertise required at trial to refute Plaintiff’s assertion of damages. Therefore, Defendant’s demand for an undertaking pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1030 is well taken.

Next the court addresses Plaintiff’s assertion that she is indigent and unable to post a bond for the undertaking. In support of this assertion, Plaintiff states: “I am in a dire financial

situation at this time and not only am I unemployed and my sole income source is from the government (Social Security Benefits), but my bank account balance is in negative. Attached hereto as Exhibit “A” is a true and correct copy of my receipt evidencing a $-157.92 balance in the account.” (Parks Decl. ¶ 12.) Exhibit “A” is a photograph of a printed receipt with the words “Total,” “Cash,” and “Change Due” on separate lines each followed by: “0.00.” Below those entries is the typed text: “Cancel Account – Withdraw failed: Transaction Canceled.” Below that typed text is a handwritten entry: “(-157.92)”. Under that handwritten entry is the typed text: “We offer more than check cashing” with a variety of services described. Notably, a checking or savings account is not one of the services listed.

The court notes that no fee waiver was requested by plaintiff. The court finds the affidavit submitted by Plaintiff to be insufficient to determine that Plaintiff is indigent and unable to obtain a bond. Therefore, the court declines to exercise its discretion under Section 995.240 to waive an otherwise valid request for undertaking.

Accordingly, the motion for order for undertaking pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1030 is granted.

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
