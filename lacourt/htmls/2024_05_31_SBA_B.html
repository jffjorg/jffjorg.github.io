DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV01147    Hearing Date: May 31, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

PAUL WORKMAN,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV01147

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          May 31, 2024 

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Paul Workman

Responding Party:                  Defendant The People of the State of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation

Motion for Relief from Gov. Code §945.4 and Permission to File a Late Government Claim

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

On April 19, 2023, plaintiff Paul Workman filed a complaint against City of Los Angeles, T.C. Real Estate Ltd., and Coral Realty, Inc. for dangerous condition of public property and negligence.  Plaintiff alleges that on November 4, 2022, plaintiff was riding his bicycle through the parking lot intending to ride onto the city sidewalk to continue his journey.  However, as plaintiff rode from the parking lot toward the nearest crosswalk by the city sidewalk, the front wheel of his bicycle reached a drop of approximately 11 inches between surface of the parking lot and the surface of the city sidewalk.  Said drop was a concealed trap not marked in any meaningful manner and constituted a dangerous condition since the drop in elevation between the parking lot and the sidewalk could not be seen by plaintiff.  As a result, plaintiff flipped off his bicycle onto the sidewalk and sustained serious injuries.

On June 9, 2023, T.C. Real Estate Ltd. and Coral Realty, Inc. filed a cross-complaint against City of Los Angeles.

On July 3, 2023, City of Los Angeles filed a cross-complaint against T.C. Real Estate and Coral Realty.

On September 12, 2023, plaintiff filed an amendment designating ADR/Preferred Business Properties as Doe 1.

On December 13, 2023, the Court granted plaintiff’s motion for relief from Gov. Code §945.4 as against State of California.

On January 16, 2024, plaintiff filed amendments designating California Department of Transportation as Doe 2 and State of California as Doe 3.

On March 30, 2024, defendants and plaintiff entered into a stipulation and the Court ordered that the order dated December 13, 2023 be vacated, that the hearing on the motion be rescheduled, and that the motion be re-served on State of California and California Department of Transportation.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Before a suit for damages may be filed against a public entity, a claimant must present a timely written claim to the public entity and the claim must have been acted upon by the board or deemed rejected.  Gov. Code §945.4; Munoz v. State of Cal. (1995) 33 Cal. App. 4th 1767, 1777.  A claim relating to a cause of action for injury to person or personal property must be presented to the public entity not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.  Gov. Code §911.2.  Munoz, supra, 33 Cal. App. 4th at 1777.

If a claimant fails to present a claim within the statutory period, he may apply “within a reasonable time not to exceed one year after the accrual of the cause of action” to the public entity for leave to present a late claim.  Gov. Code §911.4.  If the public entity denies leave to present a late claim, a claimant may petition the Court under Government Code §946.6 for relief from the requirements of Government Code §945.4.  Munoz, supra, 33 Cal. App. 4th at 1777.

Additionally, a petition pursuant to Government Code §946.6 must be “filed within six months after the application to the board is denied or deemed to be denied pursuant to Section 911.6.”  Gov. Code §946.6(b); see also City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (1993) 14 Cal. App. 4th 621, 627.  This requirement is mandatory.  Lineaweaver v. S. Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1983) 139 Cal. App. 3d 738, 741.

If the §946.6 petition is timely filed, the court may relieve a claimant from the requirements of §945.4 if it makes certain findings, including:  (1) the claimant made a timely application to the public entity for leave to present a late claim; (2) the application was denied; and (3) the failure to timely present a claim was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.  Gov. Code §946.6(c).  In determining whether relief is warranted under Government Code §946.6, the court considers the petition, any affidavits submitted in support of or in opposition to the petition, and any other evidence presented at the hearing.  Gov. Code §946.6(e); Munoz, supra, 33 Cal. App. 4th at 1778.

“The showing required for relief under [§] 946.6 because of mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect is the same as required under [CCP] [§] 473 for relieving a party from a default judgment.”  Ebersol v. Cowan (1983) 35 Cal.3d 427, 435.

DISCUSSION

            Under Gov. Code §946.6, plaintiff Paul Workman requests relief from the government claims statute under Gov. Code §945.4 as against State of California.

            As alleged in the complaint, the incident occurred on November 4, 2022.  The herein motion for relief was filed on October 27, 2023 (and refiled on March 25, 2024).

            Plaintiff explains that on December 2, 2022, plaintiff filed a timely government claim against defendant City of Los Angeles, believing that the sidewalk and its curb where the incident occurred, were within the possession and control of the City of Los Angeles.  The City of Los Angeles denied the claim on January 25, 2023, but did not indicate that the denial was based on lack of possession or control over the site of the incident.  During discovery, defendants T.C. Real Estate and Coral Realty responded on August 21, 2023 to plaintiff’s special interrogatories that “based on information and belief the City of Los Angeles or its contractors constructed the vertical drop.”  On September 26, 2023, defendant City of Los Angeles responded to plaintiff’s form interrogatories that “[t]he construction of the vertical drop was performed by State of California [Caltrans] or by persons acting on behalf of the State of California” and that “[t]he subject vertical drop is the property of State of California.”  Plaintiff argues that from the date of his injuries through September 26, 2023, there was no reasonable way for plaintiff to know that the 11” vertical drop in the curb was constructed by the State of California and its department Caltrans.

            Plaintiff asserts that on October 27, 2023, plaintiff filed a government claim against the State of California and Caltrans, along with a Request for Leave to Present a Late Claim.  Plaintiff contends that due to the fact that the one-year limitation to file motions for relief from the six months deadline to file a government claim will expire prior to the 45-days for the denial or acceptance of the claim and/or request, he seeks relief.  He further contends that his failure to timely present the claim was through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect in that he and his counsel believed that the City of Los Angeles was in control and possession of the public sidewalk, which is located in the city of Los Angeles, where the incident occurred.

            In opposition, defendant argues that plaintiff has not met his burden of showing that his failure to file a timely claim was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect because he has not shown reasonable diligence, including what if any investigation took place to identify what public entities owned, controlled, maintained, or managed the location, other than “seemingly assuming that the Incident Location was located within the City of Los Angeles.”   Defendant contends that plaintiff and his counsel had “easily available access to multiple sources that would have identified that the stretch of Lincoln Boulevard where the incident occurred is” CA-1, a state highway, including from multiple state highway markers and Google Maps.

            In reply, plaintiff argues that although plaintiff’s counsel may have made an erroneous assumption that the City of Los Angeles is the sole owner of the location because the location of the incident is a sidewalk at an intersection that is owned and controlled by the City of Los Angeles, it was reasonable to conclude that a sidewalk at a city street is within the responsibility  and ownership of the city.  Further, plaintiff contends, the crosswalk button signage at the location indicates that it is controlled by the city and two sewer covers on the two sides of the accident scene are embossed with the city’s name.  Also, plaintiff asserts, the work repair orders referred the work repair requests to the Los Angeles Department of Transportation.  Plaintiff also argues that counsel was diligent by sending a safety engineer expert who inspected the scene in March 2023.  Plaintiff further contends that in counsel’s experience, a denial of a government claim based on “wrong entity” would have been indicated but it was not in this case.  Plaintiff also points out that the City of Los Angeles Fire Department responded to the incident and the LA Police Department investigates collision accidents at the location.  See Etan Lorant decl.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that the §946.6 petition was timely filed.  See stipulation and order dated March 20, 2024. The Court also finds that the claimant made a timely application to the public entity (State of California and Caltrans) for leave to present a late claim.  Although the Court is unaware whether the State denied the application, the Court also finds that the failure to timely present a claim was the result of counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

            The motion is thus GRANTED.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.
