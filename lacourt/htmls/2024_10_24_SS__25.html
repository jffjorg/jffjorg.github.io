ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

In 2022, Petitioner Alison Kendall (“Petitioner”), a licensed California architect, signed a contract with Respondents Chikako Bryner Van Deusen and Roger Van Deusen (“Respondents”). (Amended Petn., p. 2; Amended Petn. Exh. A.) The parties eventually entered into a dispute over aspects of Petitioner’s service, and Petitioner submitted the dispute to the American Arbitration Association (“AAA”). (Amended Petn., p. 2; Amended Petn., Exhs. A, B; 9/30/24 Smith Decl. in Support of Demurrer and Motion to Strike, Exh. 2.)

 

On August 7, 2023, an arbitration by the AAA occurred. The arbitrator, Marion T. Hack, issued a final ruling on August 16, 2023, naming Petitioner as the prevailing party and awarding her $27,182.  (Amended Petn., Exh. B.)

 

On September 6, 2023, self-represented Petitioner filed a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (“Original Petition”) in which she sought to confirm the Final Arbitration Award of $27,182. At the January 18, 2024 Case Management Conference, Petitioner informed the Court that Respondents had made partial payment resulting in the amount sought falling below the $25,000 jurisdictional limit. Accordingly, the Court ordered the matter reclassified as a civil limited jurisdiction case and subsequently reassigned it to Department 25.

 

On July 24, 2024, Petitioner, represented by counsel, filed a First Amended Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award (“Amended Petition”), in which she sought the amount of $18,299.20 in recognition of Respondents’ partial payment. Specifically, while the Amended Petition seeks to confirm the award, in Section 10, Petitioner requests that the Court correct the award, noting that while the award is accurate, Petitioner wishes to acknowledge a credit for partial payment which reduces the amount owed plus interest at the legal rate from the date of the award. The Court notes the Proof of Service attached to the Amended Petition indicates the filing was electronically served on Respondent’s Counsel Ames Smith at an email address listed on the June 20, 2024 Notice and Declaration of Archibald Smith Automatically Extending Respondent’s Time to File a Responsive Pleading following the Original Petition.

 

On September 3, 2024, Respondent Roger Van Deusen (hereinafter “Respondent”) filed the instant Demurrer to the Amended Petition and a Motion to Strike. The Court notes the Proof of Service attached to the Demurrer and Motion to Strike indicates the filings were electronically served on Petitioner’s Counsel Collins + Collins LLP at email addresses listed on the Amended Petition. On September 25, 2024, Respondent filed a Notice of Non-Opposition by Petitioner.

 

On October 1, 2024, Respondent filed a separate Motion to Strike in conjunction with the Demurrer to the Amended Petition identical to the Motion to Strike filed with the Demurrer on September 3, 2024.

 

On October 2, 2024, Petitioner filed untimely Opposition briefs to the Respondent’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

 

On October 3, 2024, Petitioner filed an Ex Parte Application to Continue Hearing of Respondent’s Demurrer and Motion to Strike from October 7, 2024 to October 15, 2024 or to a date thereafter convenient for the Court. Petitioner explained the request for the continuance was on the grounds that Petitioner failed to file a timely Opposition due to the mistake, inadvertence, and excusable neglect of counsel, and the continuance would allow Respondent time to file a Reply. (10/3/24 Ex Parte Application, p. 1.) Respondent opposed the application.

 

On October 4, 2024, the Court granted Petitioner’s Ex Parte Application and continued the Demurrer and Motion to Strike hearing to October 24, 2024. (10/4/24 Minute Order.) In light of the new hearing date, Petitioner’s Opposition briefs filed on October 2, 2024 are considered on the merits. (See Code of Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

 

On October 17, 2024, Respondent filed Reply briefs to Petitioner’s Oppositions to the Demurrer and Motion to Strike.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

“Regardless of the particular relief granted, any arbitrator's award is enforceable only when confirmed as a judgment of the superior court.” (O'Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants¿(2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 278.) “Once a petition to confirm an award is filed, the superior court must select one of only four courses of action: it may confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the petition.” (EHM Productions, Inc. v. Starline Tours of Hollywood, Inc.¿(2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 1058, 1063.) “It is well settled that the scope of judicial review of arbitration awards is extremely narrow.” (California Faculty Assn. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 935, 943.) 

 

“A response to a petition under this chapter may request the court to dismiss the petition or to confirm, correct or vacate the award.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1285.2.) 

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

The Court notes Respondent does not cite authority supporting his assertion that a demurrer is the proper procedural mechanism for attacking a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award. As noted above, the Court’s authority once a Petition is filed is to confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the Petition. However, Petitions to Confirm Arbitration Awards have been subject to demurrers, and here the Court considers the demurrer as equivalent to a request to dismiss the Petition and vacate the arbitration award. (See Precision Automotive v. Northern Ins. Co. of New York (1967) 252 Cal.App.2d 1036.)

 

 

III.              Discussion

 

A.     Service of the Arbitration Award & Timeliness of Petition (CCP §§ 1283.6, 1288, 1288.4) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 provides that: “The neutral arbitrator shall serve a signed copy of the award on each party to the arbitration personally or by registered or certified mail or as provided in the agreement.”¿(Emphasis added.)

 

“A petition to confirm an award shall be served and filed not later than four years after the date of service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner. A petition to vacate an award or to correct an award shall be served and filed not later than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 1288.)

 

“No petition [to confirm arbitration award] may be served and filed … until at least 10 days after service of the signed copy of the award upon the petitioner.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1288.4.)

 

Here, the Final Arbitration Award, dated August 16, 2023 and attached to Petitioner’s Petition as Exhibit 1 and Amended Petition as Exhibit B, does not include a proof of service or other document demonstrating the neutral arbitrator served a copy of the Final Arbitration Award on the parties. (Amended Petn., Exh. B.) In addition, the Arbitration Agreement does not appear to specify terms for service of the Award different from those set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6. However, the Court notes Petitioner’s Original Petition filed on September 6, 2023 includes a copy of an email dated August 28, 2023 from Petitioner to Respondent which purports to attach the August 16, 2023 Arbitration Award Letter. (Original Petn., Exh. 2.) While the precise date of service of the Final Arbitration Award on the parties by the arbitrator is uncertain, it appears the parties received a copy of the Final Arbitration Award sometime between August 16, 2023 and August 28, 2023. Respondent does not raise issues regarding receipt of the Final Arbitration Award.

 

Given then that the parties received the Final Arbitration Award sometime between August 16, 2023 and August 28, 2023, Petitioner needed to serve and file a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award within four years from August 28, 2023 or serve and file a Petition to Vacate or Correct Award within 100 days from August 28, 2023 to be timely. (Code of Civ. Pro. § 1288.)

 

Petitioner’s Original Petition was a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award, which Petitioner filed on September 6, 2023 and attempted to serve on Respondent on June 11, 2024. (Original Petn.; 6/20/24 Decl. Automatically Extending Respondent’s Time to File Responsive Pleading, fn. 1, Exh. A, ¶ 2.) However, while the filing and service was timely under Code of Civil Procedure section 1283.6 given that it occurred within four years after the service of the Final Arbitration Award on Petitioner, the Court notes service of the Original Petition on Respondent may have been improper as there is no proof of service, and it appears Petitioner served Respondent with the Original Petition herself. (6/20/24 Decl. Automatically Extending Respondent’s Time to File Responsive Pleading, fn. 1, Exh. A, ¶ 2.) While Respondent does not deny having received the Original Petition, he does not concede that the June 11, 2024 service of the Original Petition on him was proper. (6/20/24 Decl. Automatically Extending Respondent’s Time to File Responsive Pleading, Exh. A.)


            Petitioner then filed and served the Amended Petition on July 24, 2024, which, if the Amended Petition is a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award, would satisfy all statutory requirements as it would be timely – the filing occurred less than a year from the service of the signed copy of the Final Arbitration Award – and was served electronically upon Respondent in accordance with an electronic service agreement between the parties. (7/29/24 Decl. Extending Respondent’s Time to File Responsive Pleading to Amended Petn., Exh. 2, p. 2; Code of Civ. Pro. § 1288.) However, if Petitioner intended the Amended Petition to be a Petition to Correct Arbitration Award, then it would be untimely given it was filed and served well after 100 days after the date of service of the signed copy of the Final Arbitration Award on Petitioner. (Code of Civ. Pro. § 1288.)

 

The Court notes there is a discrepancy in the type of relief requested by the Petitioner in the Amended Petition. On the first page of the Amended Petition, Petitioner marked the Amended Petition as a “Petition to Confirm”; however, on the third page of the Amended Petition, Petitioner marked in ¶10b that she is requesting the Court “correct the award and enter judgment according to the corrected award.” (Amended Petn., pp. 1, 3, italics added.) While Petitioner mentions in the Opposition brief that the Amended Petition does not seek to alter or “ ‘correct’ the arbitration award,” (Opposition, p. 2), the Petition itself is not clear on this matter.

 

Accordingly, the Court is not able to fully address the timeliness of the Amended Petition until Petitioner clarifies whether her Amended Petition is a Petition to Confirm or to Correct Arbitration Award.

 

B.     The Arbitrator’s Authority

 

Respondent argues the Amended Petition should be dismissed because the arbitrator had no authority or subject matter jurisdiction to arbitrate the dispute, as the arbitration provision in the parties’ contract was “expressly limited to only disputes where there has been an unsuccessful mediation.” (Demurrer, p. 1.) As such, Respondent argues the arbitration ruling cannot be enforced. To support his claim, Respondent points, in part, to the portion of the contract that states:  “Any claim, dispute or other matter in question arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be subject to mediation as a condition precedent to binding dispute resolution.” (Amended Petn., Exh. A, p. 16.)

 

Respondent also argues objections to subject matter jurisdiction, such as here, may never be waived, and thus his contention is timely.  (Demurrer, pp. 5-6, citing United Firefighters of Los Angeles v. City of Los Angeles (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1576, 1581.)

 

In Opposition, Petitioner argues the arbitrator has authority to determine their own jurisdiction by virtue of the AAA rules. (Demurrer Opposition, p. 3, citing Rodriguez v. American Technologies, Inc. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1123; Dream Theater, Inc. v. Dream Theater (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 547, 549, 557.) Nonetheless, Petitioner states Respondent waived any challenge to arbitrability by failing to ask the arbitrator to “rule on arbitrability or compel mediation before proceeding to the arbitration on the merits.”  (Demurrer Opposition, p. 5, italics added.) As such, Petitioner states Respondent “cannot wait until after an award is made to claim that an issue is beyond the arbitrator’s authority.” (Demurrer Opposition, p. 5, citing J.C. Gury Co. v. Nippon Carbide Indus. (USA) Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 1300, 1302.) Finally, Petitioner requests leave to amend any defects to the extent the Court finds grounds to sustain Respondent’s demurrer. (Demurrer Opposition, p. 6.)

 

In Reply, Respondent argues Petitioner fails to address numerous arguments raised in the demurrer and thus forfeits them. (Demurrer Reply, p. 1.) Respondent reiterates arguments made in the demurrer and acknowledges that the “parties agree that the scope of the arbitrator’s authority turns on their contractual intent.”  (Demurrer Reply, p. 4.) Respondent also argues the contract states “the intent of the parties to allow for binding arbitration subject to the AAA rules only when mediation had first been attempted without success is inescapable.”  (Demurrer Reply, p. 5.)

 

As relevant here, the parties’ contract states:

 

“§ 8.2 Mediation

 

§ 8.2.1 Any claim, dispute or other matter in question arising out of or related to this Agreement shall be subject to mediation as a condition precedent to binding dispute resolution. If such matter relates to or is the subject of a lien arising out of the Architect's services, the Architect may proceed in accordance with applicable law to comply with the lien notice or filing deadlines prior to resolution of the matter by mediation or by binding dispute resolution.

 

§ 8.2.2 Mediation, unless the parties mutually agree otherwise, shall be administered by the American Arbitration Association in accordance with its Construction Industry Mediation Procedures in effect on the date of this Agreement. The parties shall share the mediator's fee and any filing fees equally. The mediation shall be held in the place where the Project is located, unless another location is mutually agreed upon. Agreements reached in mediation shall be enforceable as settlement agreements in any court having jurisdiction thereof.

 

§ 8.2.3 If the parties do not resolve a dispute through mediation pursuant to this Section 8.2, the method of binding dispute resolution shall be the following: Arbitration pursuant to Section 8.3 of this Agreement.”

 

            (Amended Petn., Exh. A, p. 16.)

 

While Respondent’s arguments are somewhat persuasive, it is not immediately apparent as a matter of law that the arbitrator did not have authority to arbitrate the claim at issue, and the question appears to ultimately be one of contract interpretation. Indeed, AAA highlighted in a letter to the parties that since the parties could not come to an agreement about mediation, the matter would proceed to arbitration. (9/30/24 Smith Decl. in Support of Demurrer and Motion to Strike, Exh. 2.) Given the contract’s ambiguity on the proper protocol in the event the parties disagree about the condition precedent of mediation (which arguably could be said to make the mediation unsuccessful, a circumstance which would then necessitate arbitration pursuant to the contract), the AAA’s read of the specific contract’s terms offers a plausible interpretation given the circumstances and the language of sections 8.2.2 and 8.2.3.

 

As such, the Amended Petition is not invalid on its face. A general demurrer does not like to only part of a cause of action. If there are sufficient allegations to entitle plaintiff to relief, other allegations cannot be challenged by general demurrer. Daniels v. Select Portfolio Servicing, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal. App. 4th 1150, 1167.  “Objections that a complaint is ambiguous or uncertain, or that essential facts appear only inferentially, or as conclusions of law, or by way of recitals, must be raised by special demurrer, and cannot be reached on general demurrer.” Johnson v. Mead (1987) 191 Cal. App. 3d 156, 160. Special demurrers are not permitted in limited civil cases. The grounds for special demurrer must be raised, if at all, as affirmative defenses in the answer. Code of Civ. Procedure § 92( c).

 

Furthermore, moving party argues that the arbitrator lacked subject matter jurisdiction, this argument is misplaced in a demurrer, because the moving party is not making a claim that the court is lacking subject matter jurisdiction. Code of Civil Procedure §430.10(a) permits general demurrer on grounds that the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.” (Code of Civ. Procedure §430.10). The challenge is to the court’s power to grant relief.  The demurrer lies only where it appears from the face of the complaint that the court is not competent to act. Buss v. J.O. Martin Co. (1966) 241 Cal.App. 2d 123, 133.  Thus, a demurrer is not the proper procedural mechanism for attacking a Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award.

 

As already mentioned, the Court’s authority once a Petition is filed is to confirm the award, correct and confirm it, vacate it, or dismiss the Petition.

 

Accordingly, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer.

  

IV.              Motion to Strike

 

Respondent moves the Court to strike 10b and 10d of the Amended Petition without leave to amend because (1) Petitioner untimely filed the Amended Petition more than 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on Petitioner and improperly requested a new form of relief, and (2) Petitioner’s requested modification to add additional interest goes beyond “mere correction.” (Notice of Motion, p. ii; Code of Civ. Proc. § 92(d).)

 

In Opposition, Petitioner contends Respondent’s motion should be denied because the Amended Petition does not seek to alter or “ ‘correct’ the arbitration award”; rather, Petitioner states the Amended Petition seeks to account for Respondent’s partial payment and adjust for interest accrued on the arbitration award since it was awarded.  (Strike Opposition, pp. 2-3.) Petitioner contends such interest is allowed pursuant to California Civil Code section 3287. (Opposition, p. 3, citing County of Solano v. Lionsgate Corp. (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 741, 753; Tenzera, Inc. v. Osterman (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 16, 21-22.)

 

In Reply, Respondent argues Petitioner fails to address numerous arguments raised in the motion to strike and thus waives them. (Strike Reply, p. 1.) Respondent further argues Petitioner’s Amended Petition is untimely, the arbitration award’s calculation of interest is erroneous, the Court may not modify the award to provide additional interest, and Petitioner’s Amended Petition is to correct, not confirm, the arbitration award. (Strike Reply, pp. 2-3.)  Finally, Respondent contends the arbitrator’s interest award is final and thus Petitioner is not entitled to any additional interest past July 12, 2023. (Strike Reply, p. 5.)

 

As a preliminary matter and as discussed above, the Court is unable to fully address the question of timeliness until Petitioner clarifies whether her Amended Petition is a Petition to Confirm or to Correct Arbitration Award.

 

As to Petitioner’s requested modification, the Court notes the discrepancy in the type of relief requested by the Petitioner in the Amended Petition, as mentioned above. On the first page of the Amended Petition, Petitioner marked the Amended Petition as a “Petition to Confirm”; however, on the third page of the Amended Petition, Petitioner marked in ¶10b that she is requesting the Court “correct the award and enter judgment according to the corrected award.” (Amended Petn., pp. 1, 3.) Petitioner then explains that: “The award itself is accurate. However, Petitioner acknowledges a credit for a partial payment made by Respondent after the date of the award, which reduces the amount owed to $18,299.20, plus interest at the legal rate from the date of the award on August 16, 2023.”  (Amended Petn., p. 3.) 

 

The court has discretion to grant leave to amend. (Code of Civil Procedure § 472a(d); Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal. App. 4th 761, 768-769).

 

Based on the discrepancy in the type of relief requested in the Amended Petition, the court GRANTS the Motion to Strike with leave to file Second Amended Petition to Confirm Contractual Arbitration Award within the next twenty (20) days.

 

V.                Conclusion & Order

 

The Court OVERRULES the Demurrer.

 

The Court GRANTS the Motion to Strike.

 

Petitioner is to file Second Amended Petition to Confirm Contractual Arbitration Award within the next twenty (20) days.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

This is an automobile accident case. On January 27, 2023, plaintiff State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (Plaintiff) filed the complaint, alleging that defendant Ildefonso Torres Chavez (Defendant) negligently collided their vehicle with the property of a person insured by Plaintiff.

 

On May 20, 2024, the parties stipulated to a judgment in favor of Plaintiff in the amount of $14,000, of which $10,000 was to be paid by Defendant’s insurance carrier and $4,000 was to be paid by Defendant.

 

On May 30, 2024, the court dismissed the case without prejudice based on the stipulation for entry of judgment.

 

On August 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed this motion to set aside/vacate dismissal and to enter judgment. No opposition has been filed.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6, subdivision (a), if parties stipulate for settlement of the case in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, the court may, upon motion of a party, enter judgment pursuant to the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

           

III.              Discussion

 

Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a stipulation that Defendant will pay Plaintiff a total of $14,000, plus costs of suit and prejudgment interest. The stipulation included a clause that, in the event of Defendant’s default, Plaintiff may cause judgment to be entered pursuant to the stipulation for the full amount of the agreed-upon judgment, less any money paid prior to the date of breach. (Stip. for Entry of Judg., ¶ 6.)

 

In the order for dismissal filed on May 30, 2024, the court retained jurisdiction of the case pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6.

 

Plaintiff, in the motion and in the declaration of Susan M. Benson, has stated that Defendant has defaulted on payments required under the stipulation, the remaining principal owed totaling $3,000. (Benson Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff sent a default payment letter to Defendant on June 21, 2024. (Benson Decl., ¶ 6.) Nonetheless, Defendant has failed to pay any money since the Notice of Default was sent. (Benson Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

Plaintiff requests an entry of judgment against Defendant in the sum of $3,000, plus costs to file suit and serve the lawsuit of $472.30 and filing costs of $75, for a total judgment of $3,547.30. The court finds these costs to be reasonable.

 

Based thereon, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to set aside dismissal and to enter judgment.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal and to Enter Judgment is GRANTED.

 

The Dismissal entered on 05/30/2024 is set aside and vacated.

 

Judgment is entered against the Defendant in the sum of $3,000, plus costs to file suit and serve the lawsuit of $472.30, and filing costs of $75, for a TOTAL JUDGMENT of $3,547.30

Case Number: 23STLC01596    Hearing Date: October 24, 2024    Dept: 25

Hearing Date:                         Thursday October 24, 2024

Case Name:                             SHERWOOD MOBILE HOME PARK vs LAWRENCE F. FITZGERALD JR.

Case No.:                                23STLC01596

Motion:                                   Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement

Moving Party:                         Cross-Defendant The Hertz Corporation and specially appearing defendant Andrew Litt

Responding Party:                   None

Notice:                                    OK

 

Tentative Ruling:                    The Court GRANTS the Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            This action arises out of an automobile accident that occurred on June 18, 2021. On March 8, 2023, Plaintiffs ROSA E. LOPEZ MARMOLEJO, RUTH E. VALENZUELA GIL and ISABELA A. VALENZUELA (“Plaintiffs”) filed a lawsuit against HERTZ, ANDREW LITT and FILBERTO MARTINEZ (collectively, “Defendants”). FILBERTO MARTINEZ filed a Cross-Complaint against HERTZ and ANDREW LITT on May 9, 2024 (Bridwell Decl. ¶ 3.)

 

            On August 14, 2024, Cross- Defendants Andrew Litt and The Hertz Corporation (“Settling Defendants”) filed the instant motion.

             

            On August 28, 2024, the parties filed a notice of unconditional settlement.

           

            On September 11, 2024, Cross-Complainant Filberto Martinez dismissed Cross-Defendants Andrew Litt and The Hertz Corporation.

 

            On September 12, 2024, Plaintiffs dismissed Defendant Filberto Martinez. No oppositions have been filed. 

 

            On September 17, 2024, on the Court’s own Motion, the Hearing on Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement was continued to October 24, 2024 at 10:00 a.m. in Department 25 at Spring Street Courthouse. Notice of Ruling was served by moving party on September 17, 2024.

 

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Settling Defendants move the Court for an order determining that Settling Defendants have entered into a good faith settlement with Plaintiffs ROSA E. LOPEZ MARMOLEJO, RUTH E. VALENZUELA GIL and ISABELA A. VALENZUELA (“Plaintiffs”) and Cross-Complainant FILBERTO MARTINEZ.

 

            In addition, Settling Defendants ask the Court to enter an order barring recovery for equitable indemnity, contribution and/or partial or comparative indemnity against Settling Defendants by any tortfeasor who may not as yet have filed and/or served a cross-complaint for the same against Settling Defendants.

 

                                                                OPPOSITION

 

            None.

 

REPLY

 

            None.

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Motion for Court Determination of Good Faith Settlement

A.                                        Legal Standard

 

            Section 877 states in relevant part: Where a release, dismissal with or without prejudice, or a covenant not to sue or not to enforce judgment is given in good faith before verdict or judgment to one or more of a number of tortfeasors claimed to be liable for the same tort, . . . it shall have the following effect: (a) It shall discharge the party to whom it is given from all liability for any contribution to any other parties. Section 877.6 provides in pertinent part, as follows: (a) Any party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors . . . shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff or other claimant and one or more alleged tortfeasors . . . . (c) A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor . . . from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor . . . for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault. (d) The party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof on that issue . . . (Emphasis added.)

 

            In Tech-Bilt, the Court set forth various factors to be considered by the trial court in determining whether a settlement has been entered into in good faith. (See, Tech-Bilt, supra at 499-500.) These TechBilt factors include the following: (1) An approximation of Plaintiff’ total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability therefor; (2) The amount of consideration given to the Plaintiff by the settling parties in settlement; (3) The allocation of settlement proceeds among the various Plaintiff, (4) The financial condition of the Settling Cross-Defendants and their ability to respond to a judgment against them; and (5) The existence of collusive, fraudulent, or other tortious conduct by the settling parties in connection with the negotiation for and construction of the settlement documents which is injurious to the interests of the non-settling parties.

 

 

1.                  Merits

           

            Here, the Allocation of the Settlement is as follows: (1) Rosa Marmolejo for $4,400; (2) Ruth Valenzuela for $4,400; (3) Isabella Valenzuela for $500; (4) Filberto Martinez for $5,700; and (5) David Sosa (passenger in Martinez vehicle) for $15,000. Hertz’s involvement in the subject accident is limited to Hertz’s ownership of a rental vehicle and Federal law bars claims of vicarious liability for harm arising during the period of a rental contract or lease pursuant to the Graves Amendment, codified in 49 U.S.C. Section 30106. Further, there is no evidence of any liability on the part of the Settling Defendants for the subject incident as the Rental company. (Bridewell Decl. ¶ 9.) Thus, the settlement properly reflects that a settlor should pay less in settlement than they would if they were found liable after trial.  Moreover, there is no collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct has occurred between the settling parties because the negotiations between Plaintiffs’ counsel, Cross-Complainants’ counsel and the Settling Defendants’ counsel were done at “arms length and without intent to conspire, scam or deceive any other party in this matter.” (Bridwell Decl. ¶ 10.) Additionally, “[t]he parties have executed Releases as to all claims against the Settling Defendants. The settlement agreement between the parties of all claims related to the subject incident against the Settling Defendants was not memorialized into a written settlement agreement but for the attached Releases of all claims against the Settling Defendants in exchange of the payments is set forth in the Release.” (Bridwell Decl. ¶ 5; Ex. A.) Notably, no oppositions were filed.

 

III.       Conclusion

 

             The Court GRANTS Cross-Defendant The Hertz Corporation and Specially Appearing Defendant Andrew Litt’s Motion for Determination of Good Faith Settlement with Plaintiffs Rosa E. Lopez Marmolejo, Ruth E. Valenzuela Gil and Isabela A. Valenzuela, and Cross-Complainant Filberto Martinez.
