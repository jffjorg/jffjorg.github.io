DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


            All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDept71@LACourt.org.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.

            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    

            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 
            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  

Case Number: 22STCV34320    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MARKELLA THANOU, et al., 

 

         vs.

 

CEDARS-SINAI MEDICAL CENTER.

 Case No.:  22STCV34320

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  May 17, 2024

 

Defendant Cedars-Sinai Medical Center’s demurrer to Plaintiffs Markella Thanou’s and Amalia Thanou’s complaint is sustained with 10 days leave to amend.

Defendant Cedars-Sinai Medical Center’s motion to strike is denied as moot.

Plaintiff Markella Thanou’s motion for trial preference is continued to June 10, 2024, at 8 a.m.  Plaintiff’s supplemental brief is due by May 24, 2024, and Defendant’s opposition is due by May 31, 2024.

 

Defendant Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (“CSMC”) (“Defendant”) demurs to Plaintiffs Markella Thanou (“Markella”) and Amalia Thanou’s (“Amalia”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) complaint (“Complaint”).  (Notice of Demurrer, pg. 1.)  Defendant also moves unopposed to strike portions of the Complaint.  (Notice of MTS, pgs. 1-2.)

Plaintiff Markella moves for an order granting her trial preference.  (Notice Trial Preference, pg. 1; C.C.P. §§ 36(a), (c), (e), (f); CRC, Rule 3.1335.)

 

Background

This action arises out of Defendant’s diagnosis, treatment, and care of Demetrios Fotinatos (“Fotinatos”) (“Decedent”).  (Complaint, at pg. 4.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendant failed to take reasonable care in performing Decedent’s admission to the hospital, for which he should have never been admitted, and subsequently treating him with Xanax, on or about July 24, 2021, when he was over the age limit for being given Xanax and Defendant was told that he had an adverse reaction to Xanax and he was not supposed to take it.  (Complaint, at pg. 4.)  Plaintiffs allege that prior to Decedent’s admission, he would walk three miles a day, and he became a zombie after being in Defendant’s care and never recovered to be how he was before being admitted.  (Complaint, at pg. 4.)  Plaintiffs allege as a result of Defendant’s negligence, Decedent suffered injuries to his person and was transferred to rehab where he was misfed while lying down, which ultimately resulted in his death on October 24, 2021.  (Complaint, at pg. 4.)  Plaintiffs allege as a result of Defendant’s negligence, he was subjected to continuous and unimaginable pain and suffering and being unnecessarily subjected to take prescribed strong medication because of the failure to diagnose him properly.  (Complaint, at pg. 4.)  Plaintiffs allege Decedent’s health deteriorated after that and ultimately resulted in his death.  (Complaint, at pg. 4.)  Plaintiffs allege Decedent sustained various losses, including but not limited to pain, suffering, emotional distress.

On October 27, 2022, the court denied Amalia’s request for fee waiver and ordered that she pay filing fees within ten days.  Amalia did not pay the fees.  On November 16, 2022, the clerk voided the Complaint, pursuant to Government Code §68634(g), as to Amalia.

A.   Defendant’s Demurrer

Summary of Demurrer

Defendant demurs to Plaintiffs’ causes of action for professional negligence and general negligence on the basis the causes of action fail to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action and are uncertain.  (Demurrer, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f).)

 

           Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a), emphasis added.)  A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Defendant’s counsel declares that on March 22, 2024, he attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel by letter.  (Decl. of Moulin ¶9.)  Defendant’s counsel declares that on April 3, 2024, he met and conferred on the telephone with Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss the complaint, demurrer, motion to strike, and the possibility of an amended pleading.  (See Decl. of Moulin ¶9.)  Defendant’s counsel declares that on to date no amended pleading has been served or filed, necessitating the instant demurrer and motion to strike.  (See Decl. of Moulin ¶10.)  Defendant’s counsel’s declaration is sufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a).  Accordingly, the Court will consider the instant demurrer.

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

          

           Failure to State a Claim

           Professional Negligence

“The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.”  (Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.)

“On causation, the plaintiff must establish ‘it is more probable than not the negligent act was a cause-in-fact of the plaintiff’s injury.’ ‘A possible cause only becomes ‘probable’ when, in the absence of other reasonable causal explanations, it becomes more likely than not that the injury was a result of its action.’ ‘[C]ausation in actions arising from medical negligence must be proven within a reasonable medical probability based on competent expert testimony, i.e., something more than a “50-50 possibility.”’ ‘[T]he evidence must be sufficient to allow the jury to infer that in the absence of the defendant’s negligence, there was a reasonable medical probability the plaintiff would have obtained a better result.’” (Belfiore-Braman v. Rotenberg (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 234, 247, internal citations omitted.)

“The Medical Injury Compensation Reform Act (MICRA) contains numerous provisions effecting substantial changes in negligence actions against health care providers, including a limitation on noneconomic damages, elimination of the collateral source rule as well as preclusion of subrogation in most instances, and authorization for periodic payments of future damages in excess of $50,000. While in each instance the statutory scheme has altered a significant aspect of claims for medical malpractice, such as the measure of the defendant’s liability for damages or the admissibility of evidence, the fundamental substance of such actions on the issues of duty, standard of care, breach, and causation remains unaffected.”  (Flowers v. Torrance Memorial Hospital Medical Center (1994) 8 Cal.4th 992, 999.)

Plaintiffs fail to allege a duty of care owed by Defendant to Plaintiffs; Plaintiffs merely allege a duty of care owed by Defendant to Decedent.  An essential element of a cause of action for medical negligence is a physician-patient relationship giving rise to a duty of care.  (Mero v. Sadoff (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1466, 1471.)  A third party does not ordinarily have standing to bring a claim asserting a violation of someone else’s rights. (Powers v. Ohio (1991) 499 U.S. 400, 410; People ex rel. Becerra v. Superior Court (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 486, 500-501.)  Plaintiffs failed to assert survivor claims, which would give them standing to assert a cause of action for medical negligence.  (C.C.P. §377.30; Grant v. McAuliffe (1953) 41 Cal.2d 859; Brenner v. Universal Health Services of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2017) 12 Cal.App.5th 589.) 

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s cause of action is sustained with 10 days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiffs’ Complaint is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

B.   Defendant’s Motion to Strike

In light of the Court’s ruling on the demurrer, Defendant’s motion to strike is denied as moot.

 

C.   Plaintiff Markella’s Motion for Trial Preference

Legal Standard

Parties who are 70 years of age or older (or who reach that age during pendency of the action) may be entitled to preference if they establish to the court’s satisfaction that: (1) they have a “substantial interest in the action as a whole”; and (2) their health is “such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party’s interest in the litigation.”  (C.C.P. §36(a), (c)(2).)

The preference motion must be supported by a “declaration of the moving party” (or presumably, the moving party’s attorney) “that all essential parties have been served with process or have appeared.”  (C.C.P. §36(c)(1).)

Trial priority is not mandatory and absolute merely because one of the parties is age 70. The court has discretion to determine the extent of that party’s interest and find as a matter of fact the risk posed of that party’s death or incapacity if trial is delayed.  (C.C.P. § 36(a).)

 

Discussion

           Plaintiff Markella’s motion and supporting declaration fails to demonstrate Markella is entitled to a motion for trial preference.  Markella’s attorney declares Markella is currently 93 years old.  (Decl. of Opia-Enwemuche ¶5.)  However, admissible evidence is still required as to the party’s age (e.g., declarations by party or admissible records showing he or she is over 70).  Markella’s attorney’s declaration is not sufficient for this purpose, and the Declaration of Opia-Enwemuche fails to attach the noted Exhibit 1 of her identification card.  (See Decl. of Opia-Enwemuche ¶5.)  The Court therefore is inclined to continue the instant motion to allow Markella’s attorney to file an amended declaration with the necessary evidentiary support to demonstrate Markella’s advanced age.

Markella’s attorney declares she is currently in ill health as she suffers numerous illnesses such as congestive heart failure, chronic kidney disease, Hypertension, has a pacemaker, has Hyperlipidemia, and suffers from Atrial fibrillation, for which she is currently on various medications.  (Decl. of Opia-Enwemuche ¶6.)  Markella’s attorney declares on information and belief that Markella has a grave heart ailment and now has Cirrhosis of the liver, due to her heart failure.  (Decl. of Opia-Enwemuche ¶8.)  Markella’s attorney’s evidence supporting these facts would sufficiently supplement admissible evidence of Markella’s advanced age.

Accordingly, Markella’s motion is continued to allow for supplemental briefing on this matter.

 

Conclusion

Markella’s motion is continued to June 10, 2024, at 8 a.m.  Plaintiff’s supplemental brief is due by May 24, 2024, and Defendant’s opposition is due by May 31, 2024.

Moving party to give notice.
