[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO COMPEL INSPECTION OF SUBJECT PRODUCT OR, IN THE ALTERNATIVE, MOTION TO COMPEL DEMAND FOR PRODUCTION

 

Dept. 3

8:30 a.m.

September 20, 2023

 

 

 

 

          On June 23, 2023, defendants/cross-complainants Ace Hardware Corporation and The Grommet (collectively, “Defendants”) filed this motion for an order compelling plaintiff Martina Silas (“Plaintiff”) to produce the active noise-cancelling ear plugs (the “Subject Product”) that are the subject of this litigation.  On April 22, 2022, Defendants demanded to inspect the Subject Product as part of its Request for Production of Documents (the “First Demand”).  (Diaz Decl., Ex. A.) Plaintiff served a response on May 27, 2022, objecting to the First Demand as vague and not in compliance with the statute governing the inspection of tangible things.  (Diaz Decl., Ex. B.) Plaintiff also stated:

To the extent Plaintiff makes the product available, there are chain of custody issues that must be worked out; agreements regarding how the original Product would be shipped, to whom it would be shipped, whose hands it would pass through, where it will be during what times, what precautions will be taken with it, and when and if it will be returned [plaintiff demands it be returned a reasonable time prior to trial;. Plaintiff still needs the Product for her own testing purposes, and it

testing must be handled appropriately.

          On December 27, 2023, Defendants served a demand to inspect the Subject Product on January 30, 2023 (the “December Demand”).  (Diaz Decl., Ex. C.)  Plaintiff refused to allow the inspection on the grounds that Defendants failed to file a motion to compel further response to the First Demand within the 45-day period to do so.  (See Diaz Decl., Ex. D, Meet and Confer Correspondence.) 

          Subsequently, on March 1, 2023, Defendants noticed Plaintiff’s deposition and included a demand to produce the Subject Product (“Deposition Demand”).  (Diaz Decl., Ex. E.)  Plaintiff served objections to the deposition notice on March 30, 2023.  (Diaz Decl., Ex. F.) 

          The parties attended an IDC on June 6, 2023, and this motion was filed on June 23, 2023. 

A.   Motion to Compel Compliance

In meet and confer correspondence, Defendants argued that Plaintiff’s previous objections to their First Demand were valid and that had they moved to compel production, the motion would have been denied, therefore, the December Demand for inspection is not a renewed demand, but a wholly distinct one which addresses Plaintiff’s previous objections and not subject to the 45-day deadline.  (Id.) In the alternative, Defendants argue that there is no deadline because Plaintiff previously indicated that she would comply with the First Demand inspection if several conditions were met.

Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.030 requires the demanding party to specify a reasonable time and place for the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, and “[s]pecify any inspection, copying, testing, sampling, or related activity that is being demanded, as well as the manner in which that activity will be performed, and whether that activity will permanently alter or destroy the item involved.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.220 provides that a statement of compliance shall state “that the production, inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, and related activity demanded, will be allowed either in whole or in part, and that all documents or things in the demanded category that are in the possession, custody, or control of that party and to which no objection is being made will be included in the production.”  Section 2031.240 requires a responding party to object by stating “with particularity any . . . , tangible item . . . falling within any category of item in the demand to which an objection is being made” and “[s]et forth clearly the extent of, and the specific ground, for the objection.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.240.) 

Plaintiff objected to Defendants’ first demand for the Subject Product because the demand did “not contain any of the required information regarding what type of “testing” will be done, by whom, where, when, and for how long defendants [sic] are asking to have the original Product out of Plaintiff’s possession and control.”  (Diaz Decl, Ex. B, p. 3.)  Plaintiff suggests she is willing to produce the Subject Product available if Defendants addressed certain issues regarding the chain of custody and the form of testing, but her response is not a model statement of compliance as defined by statute.  (Id.)  Therefore, to the extent that Defendants wish for the Court to issue an order “compliant with the parameters and conditions set forth in [Plaintiff’s] response” to the First Demand, the Court is unable to do so because the statement of compliance is incomplete. 

Defendants claim that they addressed Plaintiff’s concerns about chain of custody and testing parameters when they served their December Demand and Deposition Demand, but the Court disagrees.  The First Demand and the December Demand are vague and do not specify what kind of “non-destructive” testing is to occur.  The Deposition Demand is more specific, but still deficient in detail because it does not specify the kind of inspection or testing that would be performed and how long it would take.  These types of issues would typically be handled when the parties met and conferred, but Plaintiff has improperly argued that Defendants are jurisdictionally barred from seeking a “further response” Therefore, Defendants’ motion for an order compelling Plaintiff to produce the Subject Product in accordance with either the December Demand or the Deposition Demand is also denied.

However, Plaintiff’s assertion that Defendants are jurisdictionally barred from any further attempts to inspect the Subject Product is not well-taken.  Defendant’s prior demands were too vague and if they had filed a motion to compel a further response to either of these demands on the grounds that Plaintiff’s objections were meritless, the motion would have been denied.  This does not preclude Defendants from drafting a demand for production which is more specific in scope.  Plaintiff relies on Professional Career Colleges, Magna Institute, Inc. v. Superior Court (1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 490, 494, which is inapposite because it addressed a different discovery device; specifically, a second special interrogatory that both parties assumed “was for all intents and purposes the same as the first.”  Here, a Code-compliant demand would, by definition, not be “for all intents and purposes the same as the [earlier demands].”

Accordingly, although the Court denies Defendants’ motion, it does so without adopting Plaintiff’s interpretation of any jurisdictional deadline by which Defendants may compel the production of the Subject Product for inspection and testing.  Additionally, if Defendants serve another inspection demand which specifically identifies the time, place, and manner of inspection or testing, the Court finds good cause to grant Defendants’ leave to specify an earlier date of production, as long as it is at least 5 days after service of the demand.   (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030, subd. (c)(2).) 

Defendants’ motion is DENIED.

Both parties’ request for sanctions are DENIED.

I.            INTRODUCTION 

On April 4, 2022, plaintiff Pauline Yip (“Yip”) filed this action (the “Lead Case”) against Eastern Horizon LLC (“Eastern Horizon”) for breach of contract relating to a single-family residence located at 37 Twiggs in Irvine, California (the “Subject Property”). Yip alleges that Eastern Horizon has refused to sell the Subject Property to her at an agreed-upon price.  On August 9, 2022, Eastern Horizon filed a cross-complaint relating to the Subject Property against Yip and Home Surface, Inc. dba Spectrum Realty (“Spectrum”) for breach of a lease agreement and listing agreement, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, breach of fiduciary duty, and declaratory relief. 

          On August 9, 2023, this action was deemed related to the action captioned Pauline Yip v. Eastern Horizon, LLC, et al. (Case No. 23STCV18152) (the “Related Case”).  This Related Case is brought against Eastern Horizon, Chong Ding aka Virginia Ding (“Ding”), and Joy Wang as the trustee of the 2004 Anchen Gift Trust Dated December 23, 2004.  Yip asserts claims for fraud, negligent misrepresentation, quiet title, cancellation of instrument, declaratory relief, and equitable indemnity relating to the same Subject Property underlying the Lead Case.

          On August 23, 2023, Yip filed this motion to consolidate the Lead Case and the Related Case for all purposes, including trial.  

          Eastern Horizon filed an opposition brief on September 7, 2023.

          Yip filed a reply brief on September 18, 2023.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

          Cases may be consolidated in the court’s discretion when they involve “a common question of law or fact.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1048, subd. (a); Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978-979.) When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; the Court may order all the actions consolidated and make such orders concerning the proceedings as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1048, subd. (a).)  Consolidation serves two purposes: (1) to promote efficiency by avoiding unnecessary duplication of proceedings, and (2) to avoid inconsistent resolution of the same legal or factual issue.  (See Mueller v. J.C. Penney Co. (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 713, 722; Garden Grove Community Church v. Pittsburgh-Des Moines Steel Co. (1983) 140 Cal.App.3d 251, 262.)

III.        DISCUSSION

Yip argues that both the Lead Case and Related Case arise from a dispute over the title to the Subject Property and will involve common questions of law and fact surrounding the same transaction. Yip contends that the same witnesses and evidence will be introduced in both cases and that consolidation will avoid the duplication of resources, such as the use of a court interpreter, and avoid inconsistent testimony and rulings.

Eastern Horizon opposes consolidation as “premature” because the defendants in the Related Case have not been served and have not yet appeared.  Eastern Horizon cites to Chamberlain v Wakefield (1949) 95 Cal.App.2d 280, 290-291, in which the appellate court held that a trial court did not abuse its discretion by denying a motion to consolidate because the defendants in the second action had not yet been served.  This argument is unpersuasive because on September 11, 2023, Yip filed a proof of service of summons in the Related Case showing that Ding was served by substituted service on September 8, 2023.  Yip argues in her reply brief that the Lead Case and Related Case involve defendants that are controlled by Ding and represented by the same counsel, Safarian Choi & Bolstad, LLP.  (Reply, p. 3.) 

The cases may not be at issue yet, but Yip has shown (and Eastern Horizon does not dispute) that both the Lead Case and the Related Case involve similar issues and parties.  Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Yip’s motion to consolidate.    

IV.         CONCLUSION

The motion to consolidate is GRANTED.  Case No. 22AHCV00192 is the lead case and all papers are to be filed in this action.  All dates in the Related Case are vacated.
