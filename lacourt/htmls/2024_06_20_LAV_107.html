DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01970    Hearing Date: June 20, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Mary Howard,

 

Plaintiff.

 

   v.

 

El Camino Shopping Center, et al.,

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    22VECV01970                      107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Reconsideration

 

 

 

                        

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

Plaintiff Mary Howard moves for this court to reconsider its May 15, 2024 order granting Defendant El Camino Shopping Center, LLC’s requests for monetary sanctions in its motions to compel further. Defendant El Camino Shopping Center, LLC requests additional monetary sanctions in opposition to Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration. Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED. Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

This is a premises liability negligence case. On December 2, 2020, plaintiff Mary Howard (“Plaintiff”) “fell in a recessed painted and camouflaged area of the asphalt” in the parking lot of a shopping center maintained by defendant El Camino Shopping Center, LLC (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”). (Complaint p. 4.) 

 

On November 10, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a form complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) negligence, (2) willful failure to warn, and (3) dangerous condition of public property. (Complaint p. 4.) 

 

On May 15, 2024, this court heard three motions to compel further (the “Original Motions”), each of which requested sanctions, brought by Defendant against Plaintiff. This court granted one of the motions to compel further, denied the other two as moot, and granted sanctions for each of the three motions. (05/15/24 Minute Order p. 7.)

 

On May 24, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion for reconsideration (the “Motion”), which moves to reconsider this court’s May 15 order granting Defendant’s requests for monetary sanctions in the Original Motions. On June 6, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition (the “Opposition”) to the Motion, which requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel. On June 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply (the “Reply”) to the Opposition.

 

II. PARTIES’ POSITIONS

 

  1. The Motion

 

Plaintiff states that she opposed the Original Motions even though they were never served upon her “in order to preserve Plaintiff’s fundamental rights of due process and procedural regularity despite not knowing the precise contents of the [Original Motions].” (Motion p. 3.) Plaintiff argues that this court’s “inability to take notice that Plaintiff could not properly respond to the unserved motions rendered an unjust outcome and improper sanctions” that does not comply with the intent of California Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.300, which allows motions to compel further responses to interrogatories. (Id. at p. 4.) Plaintiff cites California Rules of Court 3.1345 and argues that this court “had no jurisdiction to grant sanctions because the motions were not served.” (Id. at pp. 4–5.) Plaintiff contends that Defendant offered no proof “that they did in fact serve the [Original Motions] in some way, as Plaintiff proved the contrary with its fax log.” (Id. at p. 5.) Plaintiff states that she raised that service of the Original Motions was deficient an an informal discovery conference and in opposition to the Original Motions, and she argues that she “never acquiesced to the court’s error” and did not “waive[] her right to proper service by participating in court proceedings.” (Id. at pp. 5–6.) Plaintiff repeats that Defendant “did not dispute the fact that they did not properly serve Plaintiff” in their reply to Plaintiff’s opposition to the Original Motions and contends that “[s]ilence in this context can reasonably be interpreted as an acknowledgment or concession of the validity of Plaintiff's argument regarding deficient service.” (Id. at pp. 6–7.) Finally, Plaintiff states that the Motion is timely. (Id. at p. 7.)

 

The Motion is accompanied by a declaration (the “Kruger Declaration”) signed under penalty of perjury by Plaintiff’s counsel.

 

  1. The Opposition

 

Defendant states that “[t]he grounds for seeking reconsideration are limited to those set forth in the Code of Civil Procedure section 1008 and are jurisdictional.” (Opposition p. 2–4.) It argues that “[a] motion for reconsideration cannot be used to ask the Court to change its mind” and that Plaintiff fails to “present new or different facts, circumstances, or law and a satisfactory explanation for [Plaintiff’s] failure to present the new or different facts, circumstances, or law earlier in the case.” (Ibid.) “But, more importantly for purposes of this motion, the Court has already considered this evidence [brought by Plaintiff that the Original Motions were not served] and addressed this issue… in Plaintiff’s oppositions and during oral argument.” (Ibid.) Defendant also states that the sanctions “remain justified” because “Plaintiff’s amended discovery responses were unverified.” (Ibid., emphasis original.) Defendant denies that the Original Motions were not served and states that such “allegation…has already been argued in front of this Court.” (Id. at p. 4.) Defendant also challenges Plaintiff’s contention that she did not know the contents of the Original Motions. (Ibid.) Defendant argues that “Plaintiff presents the same false evidence and ask the Court to come to a different conclusion.” (Ibid.) Defendant further argues that “even if Plaintiff offered new facts, she could not explain, and cannot provide a valid reason, for not obtaining and submitting the evidence previously.” (Id. at p. 5.) Defendant also argues that the court’s ruling on the Original Motions was “correct when issued and remains correct now” because Defendant was forced to bring the Original Motions and Plaintiff’s responses were not verified. (Id. at pp. 5–6.) Defendant states that this court “only granted one of the three sanction requests against Plaintiff” and that “[i]f any order is to be reconsidered,” it should be the sanctions which were denied. (Id. at p. 6.) (It should be noted that this contention is not true: this court granted sanctions in either the full amount requested or in a reduced amount for all three of the Original Motions.) Finally, Defendant argues that this court “should sanction Plaintiff[] under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5,” which is “justified against a party who brings a motion to reconsider without showing any new or different facts or law or providing any explanations for why those new facts or law were not presented earlier.” (Id. at p. 7.) Defendant contends that the Motion is “bereft of merit” and that “Plaintiffs’ sole purpose for filing this frivolous motion is to cause unnecessary delay.” (Ibid.)

 

  1. The Reply

 

Plaintiff contends that “[the Motion] outlines evidence which states that the Court did not have not have jurisdiction to grant sanctions because the Court based its ruling on whether Plaintiff was prejudiced by the unserved motions, and that is not the legal standard,” which is “evidence of new or different circumstances exists” because “[a]t the hearing the Court instead considered whether Plaintiff was prejudiced by the unserved motions.” (Reply pp. 1–2.) Plaintiff argues that the Original Motions were not filed in good faith because “Defendant only allotted Plaintiff three (3) business days to supplement her responses which was unreasonable.” (Id. at p. 2.) Plaintiff also argues that Defendant’s meet and confer efforts before filing the Original Motions were inadequate. (Id. at pp. 2–3.) Plaintiff argues that the sanctions Defendant requests in the Opposition are unwarranted because she brought the Motion in good faith and not to cause unnecessary delay. (Id. at p. 3.) “Contrary to [Defendant’s] assertion, Plaintiff has not repeated facts previously presented to the court. Instead, Plaintiff has introduced critical new material that was not previously addressed and is pivotal to the resolution of this matter.” (Ibid.) Finally, Plaintiff argues that “Defendant's attempt to introduce new information regarding the verifications of Plaintiff’s supplemental responses in its opposition is improper and should not be entertained by the Court” but rather “should be subject to a separate motion.” (Id. at p. 4.)

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

  1. The Motion for Reconsideration

 

“When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1008, subd. (a).)

 

“Section 1008, subdivision (a) requires that a motion for reconsideration be based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law. A party seeking reconsideration also must provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce the evidence at an earlier time.” (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212.) “A trial court may not grant a party's motion for reconsideration that does not comply with section 1008.” (Cox v. Bonni (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 287, 312.)

 

The Kruger Declaration meets the requirements of an affidavit and states “what application was made before, when and to what judge, [and] what order… w[as] made.” (Kruger Decl. ¶¶ 2, 4–5.) The Kruger Declaration states that the Motion is made “on the basis that the Court did not have jurisdiction to grant sanctions because the Motions to Compel were never served upon my office and therefore prejudice is not relevant to the determination.” (Id. at ¶ 7.) The Motion argues that because the Original Motions were never served upon Plaintiff, this court’s award of sanctions in its ruling on the Original Motions does not comply with the intent of the discovery statutes, this court did not have jurisdiction to grant sanctions in its ruling on the Original Motions, and Plaintiff’s argument in opposition to the Original Motions on the substance of the Original Motions did not constitute a waiver of her right to be served because she raised the issue of service. (Motion pp. 4–6.) Thus, the “new or different facts, circumstances, or law” on which basis the Motion seeks reconsideration of this court’s May 15 grant of sanctions in its order on the Original Motions is the standard for service of discovery motions, which Plaintiff argues this court misapplied by considering prejudice to Plaintiff.

 

This is not “new or different facts, circumstances, or law” for purposes of a motion for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a). As the Motion admits, Plaintiff raised this argument in her opposition to the Original Motions and during oral argument at the May 15 hearing on the Original Motions. (Motion p. 3.) Thus, the exact argument on which the Motion is premised has already been considered, and rejected, by this court before the court made its May 15 ruling on the Original Motions. Additionally, Plaintiff makes no showing or argument that she could not have or why she did not raise any new or different facts, circumstances, or law at an earlier time, before the May 15 hearing on the Original Motions.

 

Moreover, the Motion is premised on the factual allegation that the Original Motions were never served on Plaintiff and that this court’s May 15 order on the Original Motions considered only prejudice to Plaintiff caused by a lack of service of the Original Motions. However, as this court’s May 15 order on the Original Motions observes, there was evidence in the record that the Original Motions were properly served on Plaintiff in the form of the proofs of service filed by Defendant. Thus, Defendant did provide evidence that the Original Motions were properly served on Plaintiff, which this court considered before making its order on the Original Motions.

 

Thus, Plaintiff shows neither that the Motion is based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law, nor a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce the evidence at an earlier time, both of which are required for a court to grant a motion for reconsideration brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a).

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion.

 

  1. Sanctions

 

Defendant seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,575.00 under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, subdivision (a) provides, in relevant part, “[a] trial court may order a party, the party's attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay. Although a “motion for reconsideration improperly reiterate[a] previous points and attempted to present matters which he could have presented previously,” in order to award sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5, the trial court must find that the moving party “engaged in bad faith or that the reconsideration motion was frivolous or that it was intended solely to cause unnecessary delay” and must “provide a detailed, written explanation of the conduct or circumstances to justify its order of sanctions.” (Lucas v. Santa Maria Public Airport Dist. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1017, 1028.)

 

We do not find that Plaintiff engaged in bad faith or that the Motion was frivolous or intended solely to cause unnecessary delay, and sanctions under Code of Civil Procedure section 128.5 for Plaintiff’s bringing of the Motion are therefore unwarranted. Defendant makes no showing that and we see no evidence that Plaintiff acted in bad faith or that Plaintiff brought the Motion solely to cause unnecessary delay. Like in Lucas v. Santa Maria Public Airport Dist., supra, 39 Cal.App.4th 1017 at 1028, this is true even though the Motion improperly reiterates previous arguments made before the May 15 order on the Original Motions and makes no showing that its arguments could not have been raised previously.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: Relieved as Counsel

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s attorney Casey E. Kilday (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel.

 

Counsel’s motion is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Patricia Laborde (“Plaintiff”) slipped and fell while shopping at a grocery store controlled by defendants Ralphs Grocery Company and The Kroger Company on October 4, 2022.

 

Plaintiff filed a complaint on January 9, 2023 alleging causes of action for negligence and premises liability.

 

Counsel filed the instant motion to be relieved as counsel (the “Motion”) on May 28, 2024. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. LEGAL STANDARD

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 284, subd. (2)). California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

 

1.      Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051));

2.      A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under California Code of Civil Procedure § 284, subdivision (1)  (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052));

3.      Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and

4.      The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)).

 

The motion should be denied if withdrawal would prejudice the client. (Rules Prof. Conduct, Rule 3-700(A)(2); Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Proc. Before Trial, 9:385.2 (2008); 1 Witkin Cal. Pro., Attorneys §72 (2008).) The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice. (Mandell v. Superior Court (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case. (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.) A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship (or personality clash) is grounds for withdrawal. (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal. Rptr. 2d 554, 559.)

 

III. DISCUSSION

 

(A) Notice—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(a)

 

A notice of motion and motion to be relieved as counsel under California Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) must be directed to the client and must be made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-051).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

(B) Declaration—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(c)

 

The motion to be relieved as counsel must be accompanied by a declaration on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-052). The declaration must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (2) is brought instead of filing a consent under Code of Civil Procedure section 284, subdivision (1).

 

The Motion complies with this requirement by explaining on form MC-052 that “[t]here has been a breakdown of the attorney-client relationship precluding counsel's ability to effectively represent the client in this matter.”

 

(C) Service—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(d); Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, Rule 4.35

 

The notice of motion and motion, the declaration, and the proposed order must be served on the client and on all other parties who have appeared in the case. The notice may be by personal service or mail. If the notice is served on the client by mail under Code of Civil Procedure section 1013, it must be accompanied by a declaration stating facts showing that either:

 

1.      The service address is the current residence or business address of the client; or

2.      The service address is the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.

 

As used in this rule, “current” means that the address was confirmed within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved. Merely demonstrating that the notice was sent to the client's last known address and was not returned is not, by itself, sufficient to demonstrate that the address is current. If the service is by mail, Code of Civil Procedure section 1011, subdivision (b) applies.

 

When an attorney files a motion to withdraw as attorney of record for a fiduciary, service must be made by citation. The citation must be served in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure sections 415.10 or 415.30. If the fiduciary resides outside of California, service may also be made in the manner provided in Code of Civil Procedure section 415.40. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, Local Rules, rule 4.35.)

 

The Motion complies with this requirement. Each filing of the Motion includes a proof of service indicating that it was served on Plaintiff by first-class and certified mail. Counsel’s declaration on MC-052 states that the filings of the Motion were served on Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s last-known address, that the address at which service was effected was confirmed to be Plaintiff’s current address within the past 30 days, and that Counsel has been unable to confirm that the address is current or to locate a more current address for Plaintiff after mailing the Motion filings return receipt requested and calling Plaintiff’s phone number.

 

(D) Order—Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362(e)

 

The proposed order relieving counsel must be prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel—Civil (form MC-053) and must be lodged with the court with the moving papers. The order must specify all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known. If no hearing date is presently scheduled, the court may set one and specify the date in the order. After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.

 

The Motion complies with this requirement.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion.
