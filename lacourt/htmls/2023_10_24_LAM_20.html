DEPARTMENT 20 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


TENTATIVE RULINGS  


SUBMISSION INSTRUCTIONS

      STANLEY MOSK COURTHOUSE, DEPARTMENT 20 - JUDGE KEVIN C. BRAZILE

Counsel may submit on the tentative ruling without appearing at the hearing by emailing Dept. 20 as soon as possible after reviewing a posted tentative. Though the Court makes every effort to post tentatives at least a day ahead of the hearing, this cannot be guaranteed due to the volume of motions. The email address is smcdept20@lacourt.org. In the subject line include:

1) The name and number of the case;
2) The word "SUBMITTING" or “NOT SUBMITTING” in all caps; and
3) The date of hearing. 

In the body of the email include your name, contact information, and the party you represent (i.e. Defendant/moving party; Plaintiff/opposing party). Include all other parties on the email by "cc". Do not include any comments, questions or other information on your email.

PLEASE DO NOT call the court to submit on the tentative or to confirm receipt of your email.  If you follow the instructions above, you will receive an automatic reply to your email confirming receipt of your email. If all parties submit, the tentative ruling will become the final ruling after the hearing date, the court will sign applicable orders/judgments, if any, and the final ruling will be posted online with the minute order.   The moving party shall give notice of the final ruling. 

If you submit on the tentative, you must immediately notify the other side by email that you will not appear at the hearing. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion.

Tentative rulings are not invitations nor opportunities, to file further documents relative to the hearing in question.  No such document will be considered by the Court.

RULES ON USING EMAIL THE COURT


 


No ex-parte communications via email. Always copy all parties in all emails to Court.


Do not use email to file documents in Court. All documents must be filed in the Clerk’s office.  Emails are not part of the official Court record.  Do not use the email for any other purpose other than submitting/not submitting on tentative rulings or as ordered by the Court.  Do not "cc" the Court on emails among the attorneys, parties or others.  Do not use the email to ask questions regarding a case. For frequently asked questions go to the Court Information for Department 20 at www.lacourt.org.
  The Court will not respond to inappropriate emails.


 


WARNING: Inappropriate use or misuse of the Court’s email or violation of these or other rules may result in sanctions, including blocking receipt of emails by that sender, after the first misuse/violation.

BACKGROUND 

 

On April 20, 2020, Plaintiff Danise Marler (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant.”) alleging two causes of action for failure to accommodate disability and failure to engage in interactive dialogue. These claims arise out of Plaintiff’s employment with Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”) as a special education teacher from 2014 through 2018.

On September 28, 2020, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint.

On January 13, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint against Defendants LAUSD, Michelle Brownridge Keller, and Does 1 through 50, stating causes of action under FEHA for harassment, retaliation, failure to accommodate, failure to engage in the interactive process, and failure to prevent discrimination.

On February 5, 2021, Defendants LAUSD and Keller filed a Demurrer to the SAC, which was SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

On June 21, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Third Amended Complaint.

On July 23, 2021, Defendants LAUSD and Keller filed an Answer.

On August 23, 2023, Defendants LAUSD and Keller filed a Motion for Sanctions.

On October 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed an untimely Opposition.

On October 17, 2023, Defendants filed a Reply.

 

DISCUSSION 

Applicable Law 

 

            Under California Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030, the court may impose sanctions on anyone “engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process.” These include monetary sanctions, issue sanctions, evidentiary sanctions, and terminating sanctions. (CCP § 2023.030 (a-d).)

            Additionally, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.410(b) a party may object to a deposition notice. However, under subsection (b), “if an objection is made three calendar days before the deposition date, the objecting party shall make personal service of that objection pursuant to Section 1011 on the party who gave notice of the deposition. Any deposition taken after the service of a written objection shall not be used against the objecting party under Section 2025.620 if the party did not attend the deposition and if the court determines that the objection was a valid one.

 Procedural Issue

            Plaintiff’s opposition was filed late. According to CCP § 1005(b), all opposition papers must be filed with the court and served on the party at least nine court days before the hearing. Here, the hearing is set for October 24, 2023, and thus, nine court days prior is October 11. Here, Plaintiff’s filed their opposition on October 13th.

            However, under Rule 3.1300(d), “No paper may be rejected for filing on the ground that it was untimely submitted for filing. If the court, in its discretion, refuses to consider a late filed paper, the minutes or order must so indicate.” Under the Court’s discretion, it will consider the late filed paper.

 

Application to Facts 

 

Defendants move for evidentiary sanctions, striking the deposition testimony that was taken outside the presence of Defendants’ counsel.

Plaintiff served deposition notices for Jenny Parada, with a scheduled deposition set for July 10, 2023. Defendants objected on grounds of unavailability. However, despite these objections, Plaintiff proceeded with Ms. Parada’s deposition, without Defendants’ counsel present. (Motion 5: 25 – 6: 2.) The same circumstances occurred as to Mr. Kaloostian: a noticed deposition was objected to, yet the deposition still took place without defense counsel. (Id. at 6: 3-9.)

Defendants argue that Plaintiff misused the discovery process pursuant to CC § 2030.010(a), by “persisting, over objection and without substantial justification, in an attempt to obtain information or materials that are outside the scope of permissible discovery.” Additionally, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs failed to meet and confer and continued with the deposition, in violation of CCP § 2030.010(b) and (c). Therefore, under CCP § 2023.030(c), a court may impose evidentiary sanctions “by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.” Defendants seek to have the deposition testimony that was obtained outside the presence of counsel excluded from being used. (Motion 7: 14-20.)

Plaintiff, in response, argues that Plaintiff complied with valid discovery rules. Moreover, under CCP § 2025.410(b), a deposition “usability” can be determined by a court if a raised objection was valid. Defendants had the opportunity to file a motion to quash or move for protective order if “they believed the deposition was improper or burdensome.” Plaintiff further argues that Defendants’ motion is procedurally defective as it did not comply with Rule 3.1345(a)(7), as it failed to include a separate statement. (Opp. 1-4.) Additionally, Department 20 requires an Informal Discovery Conference for discovery disputes, and parties are to participate in an IDC prior to filing any discovery motions, aside from a motion to compel initial responses. Lastly, Plaintiff argues that the Defendant’s order “does not specify the provision of information that the defendants already possess, nor does it grant them the right to request such sanctions.” (Opp. 9: 1-2.)

The Court finds that Defendants have failed to comply with Rule 3.1345. When a party is seeking issue or evidentiary sanctions, that party must file a separate statement. “Except as provided in (b), any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the response to such a request must be accompanied by a separate statement.” (Rule 3.1345(a).) While Defendants request in their Reply that a separate statement is not necessary, and the Court determined that one should have been filed, Defendants request “this Court grant an exemption to the filing of a Separate Statement pursuant to C.R.C. Rule 3.345(b). However, subsection (b) sates that a separate statement is not required when a response have not been provided to a discovery request or “when a court has allowed the moving party to submit--in place of a separate statement--a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” The Court finds that neither of these circumstances have been met; subdivision (a) does not apply, and the Court has not allowed the moving party to submit an outline of the discovery request in lieu of a separate statement. The Court finds Mills v. U.S. Bank instructive. There, plaintiffs filed a separate statement, but it was not code compliant as they grouped responses together. The Court of Appeal found that because plaintiffs failed to comply with former rule 335[1], the trial court was well within its discretion to deny the motion to compel discovery on that basis.” (Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893.) Here, unlike in Mills where plaintiffs filed a separate statement but failed to comply with the requirements, Defendants failed to file an initial separate statement. Thus, the Court is within its sound discretion to deny the Motion for Sanctions on the basis that Defendants failed to comply with Rule 3.1345.

 

             

CONCLUSION 

 

The Motion for Sanctions is DENIED, without prejudice.

Case No.:                                23STCV06398

Motion:                                  Special Motion to Strike under CCP § 425.16 (Anti-SLAPP)

Responding Party:                  Cross-Complainant Michelle Chen Frost

Ruling:                                    The Special Motion to Strike under CCP § 425.16 is GRANTED.

If counsel do not submit on the tentative, they are strongly encouraged to appear by LACourtConnect rather than in person due to the COVID-19 pandemic. 

 

 _____________________________________________________________________________

 

BACKGROUND 

 

On March 22, 2023, Plaintiff Manuel Gomez, Jr., (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants MFrost, Inc., dba Madeline Garden; Michelle Chen Frost dba Madeline Garden; Ray Frost, dba Madeline Garden, and Does 1-20 (“Defendants”) arising out of wage and hour violations, personal injury and illegal eviction. The complaint alleged 14 causes of action: unpaid overtime, unpaid minimum wages, non-compliant wage statements, wages not timely paid during and upon separation of employment, illegal tip collection, unpaid meal period premiums, unpaid rest period premiums, violation of California Business & Professions Code §§ 17200, et seq., failure to provide employment records in violation of Labor Code §§ 1198.5 & 432.7, wrongful eviction, conversion, uninhabitable conditions, negligence, and premises liability.

 

On May 30, 2023, Cross-Complainant Michelle Frost filed a Cross-Complaint.

On September 19, 2023, Cross-Defendant Manuel Gomez, Jr., filed the operative Special Motion to Strike under CCP § 425.16.

On October 11, 2023, Cross-Complainant filed an Amended Cross-Complaint, which was REJECTED.

On October 17, 2023, Cross-Defendant filed a Reply.

 

DISCUSSION 

Applicable Law 

“The anti-SLAPP procedures are designed to shield a defendant’s constitutionally protected conduct from the undue burden of frivolous litigation.”  (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 393.)  “The anti-SLAPP statute does not insulate defendants from any liability for claims arising from the protected rights of petition or speech.  It only provides a procedure for weeding out, at an early stage, meritless claims arising from protected activity.”  (Id. at p. 384.)  

“Resolution of an anti-SLAPP motion involves two steps.  First, the defendant must establish that the challenged claim arises from activity protected by section 425.16.  If the defendant makes the required showing, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate the merit of the claim by establishing a probability of success.”  (Baral v. Schnitt, supra, 1 Cal.5th at p. 384 [citation omitted].)  The California Supreme Court has “described this second step as a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure.’  The court does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims.  Its inquiry is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment.  It accepts the plaintiff’s evidence as true, and evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law.  ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’”  (Id. at pp. 384-385 [citations omitted].) 

 

Application to Facts 

Cross-Defendant argues that the Cross-Complaint is subject to the anti-SLAPP statute. As stated above, there are two prongs to determine if an anti-SLAPP motion should prevail.

 

  1. First Prong: Arising from Protected Activity

Cross-Defendant argues that the first prong is satisfied. The only cause of action in the Cross-Complaint is for defamation. The allegations raised in the Cross-Complaint are related to Plaintiff’s initial lawsuit for labor violations. Cross-Complainant contends that the Michelle Frost “tried to help Plaintiff in rehabilitating his life back into society, and instead of thanking her, Plaintiff filed a lawsuit to enforce his rights to be paid appropriately under the California Labor Code and other various related claims.” (Motion 10: 4-8.)

Cross-Complainant Frost did not file an opposition, but rather filed an Amended Cross-Complaint. However, this was REJECTED, as leave of court is required. The Amended Cross-Complaint does not include the defamation cause of action, but raises five new causes of action for conversion, private nuisance, trespass, assault, and intentional infliction of emotional distress.

The first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis requires the defendant to make “a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one arising from protected activity.”  (Equilon Enterprises v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67 (Equilon); § 425.16, subd. (b)(1).)  “[T]he statutory phrase ‘cause of action . . .  arising from’ means simply that the defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.  [Citation.]  In the anti-SLAPP context, the critical point is whether the plaintiff’s cause of action itself was based on an act in furtherance of the defendant’s right of petition or free speech.”  (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 78 (City of Cotati).) As such, a trial court must examine the elements of a claim and determine what actions supply those elements and consequently form the basis for the defendant’s alleged liability.  (Park, supra, 2 Cal.5th at p. 1063.) Allegations of protected activity that are “ ‘merely incidental’ or ‘collateral’ ” or that “merely provide context, without supporting a claim for recovery, cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute.”  (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 394 (Baral).)  “In deciding whether the ‘arising from’ requirement is met, a court considers ‘the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.’ ”  (City of Cotati, at p. 79, quoting § 425.16, subd. (b).)

California Code of Civil Procedure § 425.16(e) states, “act in furtherance of a person's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue” includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law…”

The Court finds that the Cross-Complaint filed by Cross-Complainant Frost falls within the four categories of the anti-SLAPP statute. As the Court of Appeal stated in Cordoba Corp., “activity protected by section 425.16 includes “any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding.” (§ 425.16, subd. (e)(1).) This right of petition encompasses the act of filing a lawsuit.” (Cordoba Corp. v. City of Industry (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 145, 151.)

The Cross-Complaint specifically states that the allegations in the original complaint are baseless “and intended to damage my reputation as well as the reputations of It’s All Family.org and Madeline Garden.” (Cross-Complaint 3: 1-3.) The Cross-Complaint further states that the “plaintiff’s action are an attempt to harm my reputation,” and that the evidence “presented in this cross-complaint counters the plaintiff’s allegations and highlights the true nature of our relationship…” (Cross-Complaint 3: 20-22.)

Thus, Cross-Defendant has met its initial burden, and the first prong of the Anti-SLAPP statute has been satisfied. Thus, the burden shifts to Cross-Complainant.

2.     Second Prong: “Probability of prevailing on the claims”

When Defendants carry their initial burden to make a prima facie showing that the claims are subject to CCP § 425.16, the burden shifts to Plaintiff to establish a probability that it will prevail at trial on that claim by making a prima facie showing on that claim.  (Roberts v. Los Angeles County Bar Assoc. (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 604, 613.)  “Put another way, the plaintiffs ‘must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.’ [Citations.]”  (Feldman v. 1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1477-78).  “Thus, plaintiffs' burden as to the second prong of the anti-SLAPP test is akin to that of a party opposing a motion for summary judgment.”  (Id.)  While the Court does not weigh the evidence, “the trial court must consider facts so as to make a determination whether [Cross-Complainants] can establish a prima facie probability of prevailing on [their] claims.”  (Blanchard v. DIRECTV, Inc. (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 903, 921.) 

Cross-Complainant has failed to file an opposition to present any evidence that they had a probability of prevailing on the claims. Moreover, Cross-Complainant filed an Amended Cross-Complaint, which struck the defamation cause of action, and raised five new causes of action.[1] However, as Defendant argues in his reply, and the Court of Appeal in Contreras stated, ‘‘A plaintiff ... may not seek to subvert or avoid a ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion by amending the challenged complaint ... in response to the motion.’” (Contreras v. Dowling (2016) 5 Cal.App.5th 394, 411.) Therefore, Plaintiff has failed to their burden of establishing that there was a probability of prevailing on the merits. Plaintiff not only failed to provide any evidence, but struck the defamation cause of action; therefore, the Court finds this act dispositive that the cause of action was improper.

Thus, Cross-Complainant has failed to establish its burden as to the second prong.

 

             Therefore, the first prong has been met by Cross-Defendants, establishing that the basis for the suit is a protected activity, i.e., the cross-complaint was based on the initial filing of a lawsuit, and the second prong has not been met, as Cross-Complaint failed to provide any evidence that the matter would prevail on the merits.

 

Fee Request:

Cross-Defendant also requests to recover fees and costs, to be determined in a later noticed motion. The Court finds that the fee request is proper and will be determined in a later filed fee motion. As the Court in Sandlin stated, “because a defendant who has been sued in violation of his or her free speech rights is entitled to an award of attorney fees, the trial court must, upon defendant's motion for a fee award, rule on the merits of the SLAPP motion even if the matter has been dismissed prior to the hearing on that motion.” (Sandlin v. McLaughlin (2020) 50 Cal.App.5th 805, 821.)

CONCLUSION 

 

The Special Motion to Strike under CCP § 425.16 is GRANTED.

Ruling:                                    The Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

         On March 20, 2017, Plaintiffs Praxedes E. Running and the Praxedes E. Running Trust filed the operative Third Amended Complaint against Covina Irrigating Company (“CICO”), City of Azusa (“City”), Azusa Valley Water Company (“AVWC”), San Gabriel River Water Committee (“SGRWC”), George Morrow, Fran Delach, Chet Anderson, William McIntyre, Andrew McIntyre, and Canyon Water Company arising

         On September 13, 2023, CICO filed the instant motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict. On September 25, 2023, Plaintiffs opposed. On October 17, 2023, CICO replied.

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

         The party against whom a verdict has been rendered may move the court for judgment notwithstanding the verdict, and the court shall grant the motion “whenever a motion for directed verdict for the aggrieved party should have been granted had a previous motion been made.” (Code Civ. Proc. 629(a).) The purpose of such a motion is to challenge whether the opposing party's evidence was sufficient to prove the claims or defenses asserted and embodied by the jury's verdict. (Hauter v. Zogarts (1975) 14 Cal.3d 104, 110.)

In ruling on a motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict, the court does not weigh the evidence or determine the credibility of witnesses. (Hauter, supra, 14 Cal.3d at p. 110.) In other words, the party in whose favor the verdict was rendered is “entitled to the benefit of every favorable inference which may reasonably be drawn from the evidence and to have all conflicts in the evidence resolved in his favor.” (Castro v. State of California (1981) 114 Cal.App.3d 503, 507.) “A motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict may be granted only if it appears from the evidence, viewed in the light most favorable to the party securing the verdict, that there is no substantial evidence in support.” (Sweatman v. Department of Veterans Affairs (2001) 25 Cal.4th

62, 68.)

Application to Facts

         CICO’s argument that Plaintiffs’ entire cause of action for negligence against CICO should have been barred by the applicable three-year statute of limitations is without merit. The Court already granted a directed verdict limiting the jury’s deliberations for the three-year statute of limitations for Plaintiffs’ negligence claims to a period within the statute of limitations. The Court ruled that negligence claims and any other related claims or damages that accrued, occurred, or were caused before June 13, 2013, were barred. Further, the Court discussed that triable issue of fact exists as to when the statute of limitations began to run on Plaintiff’s causes of action when ruling on Defendants’ motion for summary judgment. The Court found as follows:

With regard to the argument concerning Steve Running, Plaintiff disputes that any alleged knowledge on the part of Steve Running regarding the status of water in the canal can be imputed to Plaintiff. Although it is undisputed that on September 27, 2010, Steve Running signed a letter petition to CICO acknowledging that water was no longer flowing through the canal, Steve Running testified at deposition that he did not read what he signed. (Joint Revised Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts in Support of Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication of Third Amended Complaint, and Plaintiff’s Responses (“MF”) 9, 10.) This raises a triable issue of fact as to whether Steve Running did, in fact, have knowledge that water was no longer flowing through the canal. There is also no evidence that Steve Running otherwise had knowledge that water was no longer flowing through the canal in 2010. Moreover, Plaintiff submits evidence that Plaintiff’s daughter, Patricia Beecham, who held a General Power of Attorney to act for Plaintiff, did not know that the canal was dry until she received a nuisance notice in October 2015. (Beecham Decl., ¶ 14; MF 11.) Lastly, the Court finds that a triable issue of fact exists as to whether the visibility of the canal from the house would have placed Plaintiff (through her children) on notice that the canal was out of service, as Plaintiff’s daughters testified to various reasons why the canal would have been dry other than the canal being taken out of service. (Beecham Decl., ¶ 2; Bowers Decl., ¶ 2.) Accordingly, the Court finds that a triable issue of fact exists as to when the statute of limitations began to run on Plaintiff’s causes of action, and summary judgment is denied on that basis. 

 

(Nov. 30, 2018, Amended Order MSJ.)

         With respect to the damages for Plaintiffs’ negligence claims against CICO, sufficient evidence was presented for the jury to find in favor of Plaintiffs and award damages in the amount of $81,000.00. The Court finds that the jury properly relied on testimony and exhibits that supported that Plaintiffs cleaned and cleared the canal, a job that CICO routinely previously did. (Opp. p. 6-12.) Plaintiffs submitted evidence of receipts for costs and expenses which they incurred in doing the cleanup work. CICO points out that Plaintiffs fail to identify any trial transcript evidencing any testimony actually given at the trial. However, CICO fails to explain why that particular evidence is necessary to support the verdict. Indeed, the Court sees no basis upon which to disturb the jury’s findings. It appears CICO merely wishes the Court to view the evidence in the light most favorable to it, which is not the standard for the instant Motion.

For these reasons, the motion for judgment notwithstanding the verdict is DENIED.

Request for Judicial Notice

         The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice in full.

Request to Strike

The Court declines to rule on Plaintiffs’ request to strike as it is procedurally improper. Government Code section 70617 (a) states: “Except as provided in subdivisions (d) and (e), the uniform fee for filing a motion, application, or any other paper requiring a hearing subsequent to the first paper, is forty dollars ($40).  Papers for which this fee shall be charged include the following: (1) A motion listed in paragraphs (1) to (12), inclusive, of subdivision (a) of Section 1005 of the Code of Civil Procedure.”  Government Code section 70617 (f) states: “Regardless of whether each motion or matter is heard at a single hearing or at separate hearings, the filing fees required by subdivisions (a), (c), (d), and (e) apply separately to each motion or other paper filed. The Judicial Council may publish rules to give uniform guidance to courts in applying fees under this section.” Plaintiff has essentially filed two motions but only one filing fee.

CONCLUSION

         The Motion for Judgment Notwithstanding the Verdict is DENIED.
