DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV01015    Hearing Date: September 25, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANTS’ MOTION TO EXPUNGE MECHANIC’S LIEN

I.         INTRODUCTION

 Plaintiff Bruce Wayne Miller dba Glowtex Builders (Plaintiff) alleges that Defendants Stefan Kazachki and Georgi Stefanov Kazachki (Defendants) entered into a written construction contract with Plaintiff for the real property located at 325 Camino Verde, South Pasadena, California 91030 (the Subject Property).  Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Georgi Stefanov Kazachki is the owner of the Subject Property and that Defendants failed to pay Plaintiff all sums due upon completion of the work and has brought causes of action for breach of contract, enforcement of mechanic’s lien, interest payments, and unjust enrichment.

 

On March 27, 2024, Defendants filed a motion to expunge the mechanic’s lien and for award of attorney’s fees.

 

            Defendants’ motion to expunge mechanic’s lien is DENIED. The court also determines that Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees under Civil Code section 8488(c) in an amount to be determined at a later hearing

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A claim of mechanic’s lien shall be in a verified written statement and shall include, among other things, “[a] proof of service affidavit completed and signed by the person serving a copy of the claim of mechanics lien” that shows “the date, place, and manner of service, and facts showing that the service was made in accordance with this section.” (Civ. Code, § 8416 subd. (a)(7).) Service shall be made “by registered mail, certified mail, or first-class mail, evidenced by a certificate of mailing, postage prepaid, addressed to the owner or reputed owner at the owner's or reputed owner's residence or place of business address or at the address shown by the building permit on file with the authority issuing a building permit for the work.” (Civ. Code, § 8416 subd. (c)(1).) “Service of the copy of the claim of mechanics lien by registered mail, certified mail, or first-class mail, evidenced by a certificate of mailing, postage prepaid, is complete at the time of the deposit of that first-class, certified, or registered mail.” (Civ. Code, § 8416, subd. (d).) Delivery is not required to enact proper service of process so long as “the procedures implementing the constitutional requirements of due process were followed giving assurance that service really had been made.” (West v. West (1979) 92 Cal.App.3d 120, 124 (West).) When proof of service is sufficient, it is “of no legal import that a party actually may not have received notice.” (Oats v. Oats (1983) 143 Cal.App.3d 416, 420.)  The petitioner has the burden of producing evidence as to the issue of compliance with the service and date for hearing requirements of a mechanic’s lien. (Civ. Code, § 8488, subd. (a).)

 

Civil Code section 8488(c) states that “[t]he prevailing party is entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees.”  

 

III.      ANALYSIS

            Defendants move for an order expunging the mechanic’s lien on the Subject Property. Defendants also seek attorney’s fees and costs in the amount of $3,600.00.

 

Plaintiff served Defendant Georgi Stefanov Kazachki with a verified written statement of the mechanic’s lien on August 9, 2022, by first class certified mail. The proof of service includes the date, place, and manner of service, as well as facts indicating compliance with Civil Code section 8416. (Amend. Compl., Exh. B.) Plaintiffs have alleged that Defendant Georgi Stefanov Kazachki is the owner of the Subject Property. The mechanic’s lien lists Defendant Georgi Stefanov Kazachki as the sole owner or reputed owner of the Subject Property, although the amended complaint is unclear as to whether Stefan Kazachki is also an owner of the Subject Property. (Amend. Compl. Exh. B.; Amend. Compl. pp. 2, 5 ¶¶ 4, 20.)

 

Defendants allege that service upon them was improper because it was not received and was instead returned to sender by the US Postal Service. (Mot. Exp. Mech. Lien, p. 7 ¶ 2.) However, receipt of service is not required for service by mail to be proper. Service is instead complete as of the time of deposit. (Civ. Code, § 8416, subd. (d); West, supra, 92 Cal.App.3d at p. 124.) Plaintiff has provided evidence that service was properly mailed to the Subject Property to serve Defendant Georgi Stefanov Kazachki. (Amend. Compl., Exh. B.)

 

Defendants further allege that the address to which service was mailed was incorrect and that service was therefore improper. (Reply to Opp. To Mot. Exp. Mech. Lien, p. 4 ¶¶ 1-2.) However, Defendants have failed to provide any declarations or other evidence showing that this address was incorrect or that service was otherwise improper. Without proper evidence establishing that the Subject Property is not the residence or place of business address of Defendant Georgi Stefanov Kazachki, Defendants have failed to carry their initial burden of proof as to the issue of compliance with service requirements.  Therefore, the motion fails.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Petitioner’s motion to expunge mechanic’s lien is DENIED.

The court also determines that Plaintiff is entitled to attorney’s fees under Civil Code § 8488(c) in an amount to be determined at a later hearing.

 JUDGE OF THE SUPERIOR COURT

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART AND DENYING IN PART DEFENDANTS' MOTION TO STRIKE

I.        INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Bruce Wayne Miller dba Glowtex Builders (Plaintiff) alleges that Defendants Stefan Kazachki, Georgi Stefanov Kazachki, and Green Earth Building, LLC (collectively, Defendants)[1] entered into a written construction contract with Plaintiff for the real property located at 325 Camino Verde, South Pasadena, California 91030 (the Subject Property).  Plaintiff alleges that Georgi is the owner of the Subject Property and that Defendants failed to pay Plaintiff all sums due upon completion of the work. Plaintiff has brought causes of action for breach of contract, enforcement of mechanic’s lien, common cause, and unjust enrichment.

          On December 4, 2023, Defendants filed this motion to strike portions of the first amended complaint. No opposition was filed.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code of Civil Procedure section 436 provides: “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:

(a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.

(b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” Grounds for the motion must appear on the face of the pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., §437.) An irrelevant matter is any of “(1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense. (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense. (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 requires that, before filing a motion to strike, the moving party shall meet and confer¿in person or by telephone¿with the party who filed the pleading that is subject of the motion for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(2).)  The party must also file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a)(3).) 

 

III.     ANALYSIS

          Defendants ask the court to strike portions of the first amended complaint on the grounds that they contain irrelevant, false, or improper matter or are not drawn in conformity with the laws of California.

 

                    1. Meet and Confer Requirement

Defendants submitted a declaration of their attorney, Katharine Miller, stating that Defendants commenced the meet and confer process but did not receive a response from Plaintiff. (Miller Decl., ¶ 2.) The Court finds that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made, satisfying the meet and confer requirement.

 

                    2. Motion to Strike

          Defendants seek to strike allegations regarding paragraphs 9, 10, 13, and 18 of the amended complaint, as well as paragraphs 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, and 9 of the prayer for relief and the fourth cause of action in its entirety.

                              Paragraphs 9, 10, 13, and 18

Defendants allege that allegations within paragraphs 9, 10, 13, and 18 of the amended complaint are irrelevant or contrary to Exhibit A of the amended complaint, which is the construction contract between Plaintiff and Defendants. (Motion to Strike, p. 2)

Paragraph 13 concerns the formation of the contract between Plaintiff and Defendants, stating that Stefan entered into the agreement on behalf of himself and Georgi. (Amend. Compl., p. 4 ¶ 13.) Defendants allege that Paragraph 13 is irrelevant and should be stricken because the contract itself is incorporated into the amended complaint and the prior paragraph already described the existence of the contract. Paragraph 13 of the amended complaint does not merely restate the existence of the contract, but describes that Stefan entered into the contract for himself and as an agent for Georgi, an allegation not described in the contract or in prior paragraphs. It is relevant and therefore is not stricken.

Paragraphs 9 and 10 of the amended complaint are not contrary to the exhibits. Paragraphs 9 and 10 allege amendments or extra work orders added to the construction contract. (Amend Compl., p. 3 ¶¶ 9-10.) Under the contract, both amendments and extra work orders must be signed and in writing. Plaintiff has not submitted evidence of signed writings altering the contract. (Amend Compl., Exhb. A.) However, this is insufficient to establish that Plaintiff’s allegations are false, as the creation of extra work orders is not an unusual circumstance. Accordingly, Paragraphs 9 and 10 of the amended complaint are not contrary to the exhibits and are not stricken.

Paragraph 18 of the amended complaint is contrary to the exhibits. Paragraph 18 alleges that the contract allows for attorney’s fees and expert fees incurred to pursue the claim. (Amend Compl., p. 5 ¶ 18.) The contract contains no provisions concerning attorney’s fees or expert fees in circumstances of litigation. (Amend Compl., Exhb. A.) Plaintiff has not alleged that the parties amended the contract to include attorney’s fees or expert fees. Accordingly, Paragraph 18 of the amended complaint is contrary to the exhibits and is stricken.

          The Prayer for Relief

Defendants seek to strike portions of the introductory statement and paragraphs 1 and 4 of the prayer for relief as being contrary to the contract. These portions concern Plaintiff’s prayer for interest. The contract contains no provisions concerning interest. (Amend Compl., Exhb. A.) However, prejudgment interest is provided for by law. Accordingly, these portions of the prayer for relief are not stricken.

Defendants seek to strike portions of the paragraphs 2 and 3 of the prayer for relief as being contrary to the contract. These portions concern Plaintiff’s prayer for attorney’s fees. The contract contains no provisions concerning attorney’s fees. (Amend Compl., Exhb. A.) Accordingly, these portions of the prayer for relief are stricken.

 

Defendants also seek to strike paragraph 8 of the prayer for relief because it requests damages under a stop notice, which the amended complaint otherwise lacks allegations about. Because the amended complaint lacks allegations about a stop notice, paragraph 8 of the prayer for relief is irrelevant and is stricken.

          The Fourth Cause of Action

Defendants seek to strike the fourth cause of action, for unjust enrichment, in its entirety. Defendant previously demurred to this cause of action and the Court sustained the demurrer. Therefore, the fourth cause of action is stricken.

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

         

The motion to strike is therefore GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

Defendants are ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 22AHCV01198    Hearing Date: September 25, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTION TO TAX COSTS

I.        INTRODUCTION

           Plaintiff Franses Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”) brought this action against Defendant City of Pasadena (“Defendant”) on December 1, 2022, alleging injury while riding an e-scooter on a public street. On March 18, 2024, Defendant served an Offer to Compromise, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 998, in the amount of zero dollars, on Plaintiff. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 3.) Plaintiff did not accept the offer and Defendant moved for a trial continuance. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 3–4.) Defendant then filed a motion for summary judgment, which the Court granted on July 11, 2024. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 4.)

          Defendant filed its Memorandum of Costs seeking recovery of $54,476.03. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 4.) Plaintiff now seeks to strike and/or tax costs in Defendant’s Memorandum.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Code of Civil Procedure section 1032, subdivision (b) provides that a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs. Section 1033.5 itemizes those costs that are recoverable.

          California Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(b)(1) requires that a motion to tax costs musts be filed within 15 days of service of the cost bill. Rule 3.1700(b)(2) provides, “Unless objection is made to the entire cost memorandum, the motion to strike or tax costs must refer to each item objected to by the same number and appear in the same order as the corresponding cost item claimed on the memorandum of costs and must state why the item is objectionable.” ¿Technically, a motion to strike challenges the entire costs bill whereas a motion to tax challenges particular¿items or amounts.¿ 

          “If the items in a cost memorandum appear proper, the verified memorandum is prima facie evidence the expenses were necessarily incurred by the ‘prevailing party.’ [Citation.] To controvert this evidence, the burden is on the objecting party to present evidence showing the contrary. [Ibid.]” (Whatley-Miller v. Cooper (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1103, 1115.) “[A] party’s ‘mere statements in the points and authorities accompanying its notice of motion to strike cost bill and the declaration of its counsel are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing [that the costs were necessarily incurred].’” (Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1266.)

III.     ANALYSIS

          Plaintiff moves the court to tax Defendant’s memorandum of costs on the grounds that: (1) the court did not order recovery of expert costs or attorney fees, (2) Defendant’s Offer to Compromise was made in bad faith, was unreasonable, and does not reflect the substantial damage to Plaintiff. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 4–8.) Plaintiff argues that the court should tax the costs for expert fees, attorney fees, and costs labeled “Other” in their entirety. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 9–10.) Plaintiff also argues that Defendant cannot recover the full amount of their requested deposition costs and service of process costs. (Plaintiff’s Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, pp. 8–9.)

           In its opposition, Defendant argues that the court has the discretion to award expert fees, and reasonableness of an Offer to Compromise is just one factor the court can consider. (Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, pp. 4–5.) Defendant also argues that its Offer to Compromise was reasonable under the circumstances, and Plaintiff overlooks that waiving costs is a considerable sum. (Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, pp. 5–6.)

          In reply, Plaintiff argues that the Offer to Compromise was unreasonable because Defendant served it on Plaintiff a little over two weeks before serving its motion for summary judgment. (Reply to Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, p. 3.) Plaintiff argues that she had no knowledge that a motion for summary judgment would be forthcoming, and Defendant did not make efforts to schedule a mediation to avoid incurring expert expenses. (Reply to Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, p. 3.)

 

          Plaintiff’s core contention is that Defendant’s Offer to Compromise was unreasonable, rendering attorney fees and expert witness fees unrecoverable. An Offer to Compromise must be made in good faith to be valid, which requires that the offer be “realistically reasonable under the circumstances of the case.” (Jones v. Dumrichob, supra, 63 Cal.App.4th 1258 at p. 1262 [internal citations omitted.].) Reasonableness is measured by a determination that the offer represents a reasonable prediction of both parties’ standing after trial. (See Whatley-Miller v. Cooper (2013) 212 Ca.App.4th 1103, 1112 [“whether the offer represents a reasonable prediction of the amount of money, if any, defendant would have to pay plaintiff following a trial, discounted by an appropriate factor for receipt of money by plaintiff before trial, all premised upon information that was known or reasonably should have been known to the defendant.” (internal citation omitted).].) “If the offer is found reasonable by the first test, it must then satisfy a second test: whether [plaintiff's] information was known or reasonably should have been known to [defendant]. This second test is necessary because the section 998 mechanism works only where the offeree has reason to know the offer is a reasonable one.” (Id. at p. 1113.)

          The timing of the Offer to Compromise does not weigh against its reasonableness. Defendant’s later filing of a motion for summary judgment does not affect Plaintiff’s ability to examine and evaluate the Offer to Compromise at the time it was made. Whether the offer is reasonable “depends upon the information available to the parties as of the date the offer was served.” (Westamerica Bank v. MBG Industries, Inc. (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 109, 130.) Defendant served Plaintiff its Offer to Compromise on March 18, 2024. (Reply to Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, p. 2.) The City then served its motion for summary judgment on April 4, 2024, with a hearing date of July 19, 2024. (Reply to Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, p. 3.) Plaintiff was on notice of Defendant’s possible defenses by its Answer, filed on January 5, 2023—over a year before Defendant filed the motion for summary judgment. This is a reasonable amount of time for Plaintiff to conduct discovery, assess the merits of the case, and intelligently consider an Offer to Compromise. 

          Defendant’s likelihood of prevailing at the time the Offer to Compromise was made also weighs toward reasonableness of the Offer to Compromise. The Court granted Defendant’s motion for summary judgment. As the Jones v. Dumrichob court noted, “we are not obliged to ignore the reality that respondent prevailed at trial. In fact, the trial result itself constitutes prima facie evidence that the offer was reasonable.” (Jones v. Dumrichob, supra, 63 Cal.App.4th at p. 1264. The fact that Defendant prevailed on its motion for summary judgment indicates that there was a high likelihood it would prevail, including at the time the Offer was made.

          Finally, Plaintiff argues that the Offer to Compromise amount weighs against reasonableness given the circumstances of the case and the high cost of litigation. As the Jones v. Dumrichob court noted, “Appellants overlook the fact that in offering to have judgment entered against him, respondent was also waiving his considerable cost bill against which appellants' likelihood of success in the case must have been weighed.” (Jones v. Dumrichob, supra, 63 Cal.App.4th at p.1263. Though the Offer to Compromise was for zero dollars, the costs of litigation at the end of a trial would be a substantial sum.

Considering the foregoing, the court assesses Plaintiff’s remaining arguments to tax costs below.

 

          Item 4 – Deposition Costs                                $ 17,467.92

          Deposition costs are recoverable. (Code Civ. Proc., §1033.5, subd. (a)(3).) Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs tabulates deposition costs as $19,757.92.

Plaintiff argues that Defendant is not entitled to costs for taking the depositions of Plaintiff’s expert witnesses, because those costs would be expert fees, voided due to their bad faith Offer to Compromise. Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a)(3), explicitly identifies “taking . . . necessary depositions” as recoverable. (Code Civ. Proc., §1033.5, subd. (a)(3).) Plaintiffs provide no evidence that Defendant’s listed fees for taking deposition of witnesses—even expert ones—were not incurred or wrongly identified.

However, Plaintiff presents evidence that Defendant’s deposition cancellation costs, totaling $1,890, were caused by Defendant’s Counsel’s own fault. (Declaration of Andrea Posey, ¶ 4.) Defendant concedes that one of those costs is not recoverable due to fault, and additionally identified a tabulation error. (Declaration of Erin Dunkerly, ¶¶ 4–5.) However, the remaining two cancellations of $550 were in response to the court’s tentative order to grant summary judgment. (Declaration of Erin Dunkerly, ¶ 4.) Defendant is entitled to those costs. Defendant’s adjusted cost for depositions is $17,467.92.

 

          Item 5 – Service of Process                              $ 2,658.11   

          Costs actually incurred in effecting service are recoverable. (Code Civ. Proc., §1033.5, subd. (a)(4).) Plaintiff does not contest these fees in her Reply.

 

Item 8 – Witness Fees                                      $ 30,250.00

           Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (b)(1), provides for recovery of expert witness fees as costs. As discussed above, expert witness fees are recoverable in this case. Plaintiff argues that expert witness Dr. Bijan Zardouz’s 30 hours for record review are unreasonable when compared to time spent by Plaintiff’s expert witnesses and Defendant’s other expert witnesses reviewing records. (Reply to Opposition to Motion to Strike or Tax Costs, p. 4.) However, Plaintiff does not provide evidence supporting this claim. As such, Plaintiff has not met her burden to show this cost was not necessarily incurred.

 

          Item 10 – Attorney fees                                    $ 0.00

          Code of Civil Procedure section1033.5, subdivision (a)(10) provides for the recovery of attorney’s fees, when authorized by contact, statute, or law. As discussed above, attorney fees are recoverable in this case. However, Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs lists this amount as “pending.” (Memorandum of Costs, p. 1; Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, p. 2.) Furthermore, Defendant does not include this line item in their reply. Thus, the court awards $0 in attorney fees.

           

          Item 16 – Other                                               $ 0.00

           Defendant’s Memorandum of Costs identified costs under line 16 titled “Other,” in the amount of $4,468.11. (Memorandum of Costs, p.1.) Plaintiff sought to tax these costs entirely because there was no description or supporting documentation of those costs. (Motion to Strike and/or Tax Costs, pp. 9–10.) Defendant identified a clerical error, and instead requested costs for service of process, as discussed above, and withdrew the remaining costs in this line item. (Declaration of Erin Dunkerly, ¶ 6.) The Court accordingly adjusts this cost to $0.00.

 

IV.     ORDER

          The instant motion is DENIED. Defendant is awarded their adjusted Memorandum of Costs, totaling $50,376.03.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PRO HAC VICE APPLICATION

On October 27, 2024, Circle K Stores Inc. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Honex Energy, Inc., (“Honex”), Gurfateh Oil Inc. (“Gurfateh”), Kulwinder Saini, (“Saini”) and Hong Lee (“Lee”), (collectively “Defendants”). The Complaint asserts causes of action for (1) specific performance, (2) breach of contract, and (3) breach of guaranty.

The Plaintiff alleges that Honex and Gurfateh breached their contractual obligations by purchasing fuel from unauthorized vendors and removing branding before the contract term ended. Further, the Plaintiff asserts that Defendants Saini and Lee breached their personal guarantees by failing to ensure payment and performance under the contract. 

Plaintiff retained out-of-state attorney Anthony W. Austin, who has applied for admission to appear pro hac vice in this matter. His application is currently before the Court.

.

I.                Legal Standard

 

California Rules of Court, rule 9.40 contains the requirements for a pro hac vice application.

            Subdivision (a) mandates that to be eligible for pro hac vice admission, the applicant must be an attorney of good standing and eligible to practice in any bar of the United States court or the highest court of any state, who has been retained to appear in a particular cause pending in a court of this state. Such person may, in the court’s discretion, be permitted to appear pro hac vice upon written application. The applicant must not be a resident of California, regularly employed in California, or regularly engaged in substantial business in California.

 

Pursuant to subdivision (c), the applicant must submit a verified application for pro hac vice admission with notice of hearing provided to all parties.

Pursuant to subdivision (d) the application must state:

- the applicant’s residence and office address;

- the courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission;

- that the applicant is a licensee in good standing in those courts;

- that the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court;

- the title of each court and cause of action in which the applicant has filed an application to appear pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted;

- the name, address, and telephone number of the active licensee of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.

Pursuant to subdivision (e) the applicant must pay a reasonable fee to the State Bar along with a copy of the application that is served on the State Bar.
