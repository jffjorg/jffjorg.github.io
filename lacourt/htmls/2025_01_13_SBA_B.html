DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV00758    Hearing Date: January 13, 2025    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

ADAN F APARICIO, et al.,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.: 22TRCV00758

 

 

 

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR COMPANY, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          January 13, 2025

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiffs Adan F Aparicio and Darline Aparicio  

Responding Party:                  Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Qualified

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED. Defendant is to produce a person most qualified within 10 days of the date of notice of this order to testify on the matters for examination identified in plaintiffs’ Third Amended Notice of Deposition of the Person Most Qualified for Defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc.

BACKGROUND

            On February 16, 2025 the parties shall “celebrate” the 2 year anniversary of when Plaintiff commenced noticing the deposition of Defendant that is the subject of this motion.

            IT SHOULD NEVER TAKE 2 YEARS TO SCHEDULE A DEPOSITION.

            On August 30, 2022, plaintiffs Adan F Aparicio and Darline Aparicio filed a complaint against American Honda Motor Company, Inc. and Does 1-50 for violations of statutory obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act based on the purchase of a 2020 Honda Pilot. Plaintiffs allege that during the warranty period, the subject vehicle contained or developed defects including, but not limited to, a defective audio system. Compl., ¶ 12. Plaintiffs presented the subject vehicle to defendant and its representatives for repair; however, defendant and its representatives were unable and/or failed to service or repair the subject vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts. Compl., ¶¶ 14-15.  

            Plaintiffs now move to compel the deposition of defendant’s person most qualified along with the production of documents at deposition. Defendant opposes the motion, to which plaintiffs replied.

            Jury trial is set to commence on February 24, 2025. Thus, the discovery cutoff date is January 25, 2025. CCP § 2024.020(a).

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

            The court OVERRULES plaintiffs’ evidentiary objection number 1 to the declaration of Zaeema Ejaz submitted in opposition to the motion.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            In California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541. “Under the discovery statutes, information is discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186. “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” Ibid. “When discovery requests are grossly overbroad on their face, and hence do not appear reasonably related to a legitimate discovery need, a reasonable inference can be drawn of an intent to harass and improperly burden.” Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431. 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by taking in California the oral deposition of any person, including any party to the action.” CCP § 2025.010. CCP §§ 2025.210 through 2025.280 provide the requirements for (among other things) what must be included in a deposition notice, when and where depositions may be taken, and how and when the notice must be served. A deposition must be “scheduled for at least 10 days after service of the deposition notice.” CCP § 2025.270(a).

“The service of a deposition notice under Section 2025.240 is effective to require any deponent who is a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party to attend and to testify, as well as produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing for inspection and copying.” CCP § 2025.280 (a).

CCP § 2025.410 (a) requires any party to serve a written objection at least three days before the deposition if the party contends that a deposition notice does not comply with the provisions of sections 2025.210 through 2025.280.

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of that party, or a person designated by an organization . . .  without having served a valid objection . . . fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” CCP § 2025.450 (a).

Any such motion to compel must show good cause for the production of documents and, when a deponent has failed to appear, the motion must be accompanied “by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” CCP § 2025.450(b). “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224. To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact. Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs seek an order compelling defendant to designate and produce for deposition a person(s) most qualified (PMQ) on all categories “identified in [p]laintiffs’ Notice of Deposition of the Person Most Qualified for [d]efendant AHM and Demand to Produce Documents at Deposition; and (2) directing [d]efendant to comply within ten (10) calendar days.” Not. of Mot. at p. 1:6-10.

Evidence in Support of the Motion

Counsel for plaintiffs, Corinna Jiang (“Jiang”), provides a declaration in support of the motion. Ms. Jiang states the following: on February 3, 2022, plaintiffs purchased the subject vehicle. Jiang Decl., ¶ 3. Plaintiffs visited the dealership on at least ten different occasions for the subject Infotainment System Defect, all occurring during the warranty period. Jiang Decl., ¶ 4. Ms. Jiang sets forth the issues with the infotainment system in plaintiffs’ vehicle. Jiang Decl., ¶ 4.

Plaintiffs served four deposition notices for the PMQ of defendant, to which defendant objected. Jiang Decl., ¶¶ 5-12; Exhs. 1-7. The history recited in the Motion reveals a failure on the part of Defendant Honda to work constructively to schedule deposition and a failure on the part of Plaintiff’s counsel to work cooperatively to attempt to address the categories of witness(s) that would be produced.  It also shows an inexplicable failure of Plaintiff to pursue the deposition for over a year.

 

Commencing February 16, 2023 Plaintiff’s counsel began the effort to obtain the deposition of Defendant Honda’s PMQ.  The deposition was noticed for March 16, 2023.  Objection was made by Honda on March 9, 2023, noting that no witness would be produced.

An “Amended Notice of Deposition” was served over a year after the first Notice, on July 8, 2024.  It set the deposition for August 6, 2024.  Objection was served.

A “Second Amended Notice” was served on August 14, 2024 selecting the date of August 29, 2024 for the deposition.  August 22, 2024 saw yet another Objection served, and no deposition was to take place. 

Finally, the Third Amended Notice was served on November 5, 2024 scheduling the deposition for November 19, 2024.

Numerous communications were undertaken seeking dates for the deposition.  Nothing was advanced in the effort to address scheduling the deposition or of addressing the objections to the categories of topics to be addressed at deposition.

 

The Court is told by Honda that “[o]n December 26, 2024, [10 days after this motion was filed] Honda offered July 11, 2025 as a date for the deposition of Honda’s PMK.”

IT SHOULD NOT TAKE 6 MONTHS TO SCHEDULE A DEPOSITION.

 

Defendant Honda complains that Plaintiff failed to “meet and confer” regarding the objections to the categories of topics requested in the various Notices.  Plaintiff’s Reply asserts that “AHM’s obligation here is to produce its PMQ for deposition, at which point it may assert its objections during course of deposition, after which Plaintiffs may choose which categories to further pursue. AHM is not permitted to withhold its PMQ from deposition entirely.”  “If AHM would actually appear for deposition in the first place, at that point it would be known which categories and testimony Plaintiffs need to further pursue. The issue here is simply that AHM refused to provide available dates or appear for deposition in the first place.”

While that may be one means or addressing category objections, it is shortsighted to claim that this was the only (or even the best) way to deal with the objections.  Since Honda apparently believed that it need not produce a witness for some of the categories, it is not efficient to simply proceed to the deposition and then discuss the categories of witnesses (who did not attend) that might then be produced on yet another date.  It is wiser to discuss the categories and attempt to resolve the breadth of the categories so that the deposition takes place with both sides knowing which categories shall be produced.  Plaintiff should have engaged Honda to address the categories so that a witness or witnesses could be brought forth.  Perhaps agreement could have been reached.

 

The operative deposition notice is the Third Amended Notice of Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Qualified and Demand to Produce Documents at Deposition (the “Third Amended Deposition Notice”), which was served on defendant on November 5, 2024, and noticed the deposition for November 19, 2024. Jiang Decl., ¶ 11; Exh. 6. Counsel states that defendant has not responded to any of her meet and confer efforts, and that “[p]laintiffs have been seeking this deposition since February of 2023.” Jiang Decl., ¶ 17.

In her supplemental declaration, Ms. Jiang states that she attempted to meet and confer with defendant. Jiang Supp. Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. 9. Ms. Jiang states that “[t]o date, [d]efendant has not provided any alternative dates for its PMQ deposition to occur prior to the current trial date.” Jiang Supp. Decl., ¶ 5.

Evidence in Opposition to the Motion

            In opposition to the motion, defendant presents evidence that it offered the date of deposition of July 11, 2025 for defendant’s PMQ. Ejaz Decl., ¶ 3; Exhs. A, F. Defendant also presents evidence that plaintiffs failed to meet and confer prior to filing the instant motion. Ejaz Decl., ¶¶ 8-9; Exh. F.

The operative Third Amended Deposition Notice is attached as Exhibit 6 to the declaration of Ms. Jiang in support of the motion.

The court has reviewed such notice. The Third Amended Deposition Notice sets forth the following nineteen (19) matters on which the deponent is to be examined:

but not limited to, all lemon law documents and all records, invoices, and other documentation relating to the lease of the subject vehicle. Jiang Decl., Exh. 6 at pp. 8-12.

Analysis

Initially, the court rejects defendant’s argument that plaintiffs have failed to establish good cause for the matters on which PMQ testimony is sought. Opp’n at p. 4:14-5:6. Defendant cites to CCP § 2031.310(b)(1) in support of its argument. Such code section, however, concerns motions to compel further responses. See CCP § 2031.310. Here, plaintiffs are seeking to compel deposition testimony and the production of documents at deposition. CCP § 2031.310 therefore has no applicability to the instant motion.

The Matters for Examination

Defendant argues that matters for examination nos. 18, 30, 32-34 seek grossly overbroad testimony and that matters comprising such topics consists of information “protected from discovery by the attorney-client privilege or the work product doctrine, or would consist of discovery on discovery.” Opp’n at p. 5:20-25. The court, however, notes that plaintiffs only seek deposition testimony on 19 topics.

Defendant also contends that a majority of the matters of examination including nos. 1, 4, 5, 10, 12, 13, 17, 22, 26, and 31 “seek testimony regarding [p]laintiff’s alleged request for a vehicle repurchase” and that “[p]laintiff did not make a repurchase request to Honda.” Opp’n at p. 5:12-15.) Only matter for examination no. 7 concerns a repurchase request. Thus, the court fails to see the applicability of defendant’s argument as plaintiffs have indicated that defendant failed to repurchase the subject vehicle. Mot. at p. 2:6-7.

The court rejects defendant’s argument that the discovery that plaintiffs seek is neither relevant nor proportional to the amount in controversy nor important to resolving the issues in this case. Opp’n at p. 6:8-8:22.

Here, the court finds that the matters for examination articulated in the Third Amended Deposition Notice are relevant to plaintiffs proving their claims in this action. The matters for examination relate to: the purported defect in the infotainment system, the subject vehicle, the dispute resolution program of defendant, defendant’s search for documents that may be relevant to this action, or obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. Jiang Decl., Exh. 6 at pp. 6-7. The court does not find the requested matters for testimony to be overbroad. The court notes that the objecting party has the burden of justifying any objections to discovery. Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255. In law and motion practice, factual evidence is presented to the court by way of declarations. Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court, supra, 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224. The declaration of Zaeema Ejaz in opposition to the motion is devoid of any factual basis for the objections to the Third Amended Deposition Notice at issue. Defendant has therefore failed to justify any of its objections to the discovery at issue. The court does not find any of the matters for examination to be vague, overly broad, harassing, unduly burdensome, or ambiguous. Defendant has also not substantiated its objections on the grounds of the attorney-client privilege, work product doctrine, or that the matters seek confidential/proprietary information.

The Court would also note that objections regarding issues of claimed privilege rarely justify a total refusal to produce a witness at deposition.  Generally the assertion of a claim of privilege requires an analysis of the particular inquiry.

The Court therefore GRANTS plaintiffs’ request that defendant produce a PMQ to provide testimony at deposition on the categories for examination set forth in the Third Amended Deposition Notice.

The Request for Production of Documents

The Motion appears to Defendant and the Court to request that the Deposition AND DOCUMENTS described in the Notice be ordered produced.  “The categories seeks testimony and documents directly relevant…”  However, Plaintiff clarified in the Reply:

“AHM’s Opposition also makes arguments related to document requests (which are a non-issue in this particular motion).”

“Plaintiffs are perplexed as to why AHM would argue against producing documents; when the sole issue in the present issue is that AHM has refused to provide dates for deposition. Plaintiffs do not seek any production of documents through the instant Motion.”

Therefore the Court need not address the document portion of the Deposition Notice.

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED. Defendant is to produce a person most qualified within 10 days of the date of notice of this order to testify on the matters for examination identified in plaintiffs’ Third Amended Notice of Deposition of the Person Most Qualified for Defendant American Honda Motor Company, Inc.

BLANCA ROSA QUEZADA GARCIA; ANTONIO PEREZ GARCIA, Plaintiff, vs. ABEL ARTEAGA DE LEON; ARTLUX BUILDERS CORP.; et al.

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO INTERVENE

Infinity Select Insurance Company on behalf of Artlux Builders Corp. seeks to intervene in this lawsuit.  Code of Civil Procedure § 387.  It is the insurer of Defendant Artlux Builders Corp.  Artlux Builders Corp. has been suspended as a corporation, and hence lacks the ability to defend itself in this action.  Revenue and Taxation Code section 23301 et seq. and Reed v. Norman (1957) 48 Cal.2d 338.  As its insurer, Infinity Select may become liable for any judgment rendered against Artlux Builders Corp.  Therefore, Infinity Select has the requisite interest in this action such that it may properly intervene in this case.  Western Heritage Ins. Co. v. Superior Ct. (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1196, 1205.

“As already noted (see fn. 9, ante.), section 11580(b)(2) “provides insurance policies issued in California shall either contain the following *1205 provision or shall be construed as if this provision is in the policy: ‘A provision that whenever judgment is secured against the insured or the executor or administrator of a deceased insured in an action based upon bodily injury, death, or property damage, then an action may be brought against the insurer on the policy and subject to its terms and limitations, by such judgment creditor to recover on the judgment.’ Thus, a judgment creditor who has prevailed in a lawsuit against an insured party may bring a direct action against the insurer subject to the terms and limitations of the policy. [Citation.]” (Kaufman & Broad, supra, 136 Cal.App.4th at p. 223, 39 Cal.Rptr.3d 33.)

This exposure to such direct liability has been repeatedly held sufficient to create a basis for insurer intervention in a third party action against the insured.12 “Intervention may ... be allowed in the insurance context, where third party claimants are involved, when the insurer is allowed to take over in litigation if its insured is not defending an action, to avoid harm to the insurer.”13 (Royal Indemnity Co. v. United Enterprises, Inc. (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 194, 206, 75 Cal.Rptr.3d 481.)”  Western Heritage Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1196, 1204–1205 [132 Cal.Rptr.3d 209, 216]

No Opposition has been filed.

A proposed Answer-in-Intervention has been attached to the Motion.

The Court finds that Infinity Select Ins. Co. is entitled to intervene in this action.  The Motion is GRANTED.

ORDER:

1.  The Motion to seeking leave to intervene is GRANTED.

2.  Infinity Select Insurance Company is ordered to file its proposed Answer-in-Intervention within 10 days of receipt of the Order signed by the Court.
