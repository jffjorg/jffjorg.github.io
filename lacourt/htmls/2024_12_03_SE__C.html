DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22NWCV00772    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: C

MARTINEZ v. AMERICAN HONDA MOTOR CO., INC.

CASE NO.:  22NWCV00772

HEARING:  December 3, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

 

#9

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication to the sixth and seventh affirmative defenses is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

This is a lemon law action. On August 29, 2022, Plaintiff Andrea Martinez (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”) regarding her 2019 Honda Pilot, (“Subject Vehicle”) purchased on March 19, 2019. The Complaint alleges Defendant sold the Subject Vehicle knowing there was a transmission defect. The Complaint alleges three causes of action: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of express warranty, (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – breach of implied warranty, and (3) Fraudulent Inducement – Concealment.

 

Plaintiff now moves for summary adjudication as to Defendant’s sixth and seventh affirmative defenses (“Motion”). Defendant’s sixth affirmative defense states that Defendant has a third-party dispute resolution process, and that Plaintiff made no effort to use such process. Defendant’s seventh affirmative defense states that Defendant possesses a “qualified” third party dispute resolution process, barring Plaintiff from recovering “any civil penalty.”

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court overrules Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to Declaration of Alexander Alarcon ¶ 3, Exhibits A, B, and C.

 

Legal Standard

 

Civil Code of Procedure section 437c, subdivision (f)(1) states that “[a] party may move for summary adjudication as to. . .one or more affirmative defenses. . if the party contends. . .that there is no merit to an affirmative defense as to any cause of action. . .A motion for summary adjudication shall be granted only if it completely disposes of [an affirmative defense].”

“The plaintiff bears the initial burden to show there is no triable issue of material fact as to the defense and that he or she is entitled to judgment on the defense as a matter of law. In so doing, the plaintiff must negate an essential element of the defense, or establish the defendant does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain evidence needed to support the defense.” (See's Candy Shops, Inc. v. Superior Court (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 889, 900.) If the Plaintiff fails to make the requisite showing, it is unnecessary to examine the Defendant’s evidence and the motion must be denied. (Ibid.) Yet if the Plaintiff makes the requisite showing, the burden shifts to Defendant to establish the existence of a dispute of material fact. (Ibid.)

 

Discussion

 

Sixth Affirmative Defense – Third-Party Dispute Resolution Process

 

Defendant’s sixth affirmative answer states that Defendant has a third-party dispute resolution process under Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (c), and that Plaintiff has made no effort to use such process. Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (c) requires buyers to partake in a qualified third-party dispute resolution process before asserting the “reasonable number of attempts” presumption under section 1793.22, subdivision (b).

 

In this Motion, Plaintiff argues Defendant does not maintain a qualified third-party dispute resolution process because Defendant’s program, the National Center for Dispute Settlement (“NCDS”) program, is not certified by the California Department of Consumer Affairs (“DCA”) as required by Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (d)(9). (UMF Nos. 3-10.) Defendant concedes that the NCDS program is uncertified by the DCA. Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivisions (d)(1)-(9) set forth requirements for “qualified third-party dispute resolution process[es].” Subdivision (d)(9) requires any such process to obtain “certification by the Department of Consumer Affairs pursuant to Chapter 9 (commencing with Section 472) of Division 1 of the Business and Professions Code.”

 

The Court finds Plaintiff has met her burden in establishing that Defendant does not have a “qualified” dispute resolution process under Civil Code section 1793.22.

 

In an attempt to meet its burden, Defendant argues there is a dispute of material fact as to whether the NCDS program is in “substantial compliance” with Civil Code section 1793.22. Defendant cites language from Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (e)(2), which states the following: “If the manufacturer maintains a qualified third-party dispute resolution process which substantially complies with Section 1793.22, the manufacturer shall not be liable for any civil penalty pursuant to this subdivision.” (Italics added.) Defendant argues that the NCDS program effectuates the legislative intent of the Song-Beverly Act—to provide a fair and timely method to address warranty issues outside of court and to limit civil liability for manufacturers that provide such program. (DF No. 4.) Defendant argues that despite its lack of certification by DCA, the NCDS program meets critical requirements under section 1793.22 such as “binding arbitration decisions, specific timelines for compliance, and cost-free consumer access.” (Mot. p. 5, lines 27-28; DF Nos. 4, 7, 8, 9.)

 

The Court finds Defendant has not met its burden in establishing a genuine issue of material fact. The cases cited by Defendants, Dyna-Med, Inc. v. Fair Employment & Housing Com. (1987) 43 Cal.3d 1379, 1386 and Spicer v. City of Camarillo (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1423, do not pertain to the Song-Beverly Act at all. Although Oregel v. American Isuzu Motors, Inc. (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1094 is a Song-Beverly Act case, it does not speak to the requirements of Civil Code section 1793.22.

 

The language of Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (d) is clear that “(d) [a] qualified third-party dispute resolution process shall be one that does all of the following: (9) Obtains and maintains certification by the Department of Consumer Affairs. . .” (Italics added.) Business and Professions Code section 472, subdivision (c) defines a “qualified third-party dispute resolution process” as a process “which operates in compliance with subdivision (d) of Section 1793.22 of the Civil Code and this chapter and which has been certified by the [DCA] pursuant to this chapter.” Such language indicates certification by the DCA is a key requirement to be a “qualified” dispute resolution process.

 

Statutory language suggests that DCA’s certification process was created to ensure compliance with Civil Code section 1793.22. Business and Professions Code section 472.2, subdivision (b) states that the DCA “. . . shall determine whether the third-party dispute resolution process is in substantial compliance with subdivision (d) of Section 1793.22 of the Civil Code and this chapter. If the department determines that the process is in substantial compliance, the department shall certify the process.” Such language suggests that the legislature intended the DCA, not courts, to determine whether a dispute resolution process substantially complies with Section 1793.22. Defendant has thus failed to meet its burden in proving a genuine dispute of material fact as to the whether is maintains a qualified dispute resolution process.

 

The motion for summary adjudication as to the sixth affirmative defense is GRANTED. 

 

Seventh Affirmative Defense

 

Defendant’s seventh affirmative defense states that Defendant possesses a “qualified third party dispute resolution process,” barring Plaintiff from recovering “any civil penalty.”

 

Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (e)(2) states the following: “If the manufacturer maintains a qualified third-party dispute resolution process which substantially complies with Section 1793.22, the manufacturer shall not be liable for any civil penalty pursuant to this subdivision.”

 

Plaintiff argues that Defendant does not maintain a qualified third-party dispute resolution process.  Plaintiff also argues that even if Defendant maintained a qualified dispute resolution process, such process would not shield Defendant from any civil penalties for willful conduct under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (c).

 

Civil Code section 1974, subdivision (c) states the following: “If the buyer establishes that the failure to comply was willful, the judgment may include. . .a civil penalty.” Plaintiff argues that maintaining a qualified dispute resolution process would only shield Defendant from civil penalties for non-willful conduct under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (e)(2). Plaintiff cites Jernigan v. Ford (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 488, 492 to support the contention that a dispute resolution process precludes plaintiffs from recovering civil penalties for non-willful conduct under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (e)(2), but it does not preclude plaintiff from recovering civil penalties for willful conduct under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (c).

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has met her burden. Plaintiff has made a prima facie showing that Defendant does not maintain a “qualified third-party dispute resolution process” that would shield it from any civil penalty under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (e)(2). The seventh affirmative defense fails on that ground alone.

 

Further, Plaintiff has made a prima facie showing that even if Defendant maintained a qualified third-party dispute resolution process, Plaintiff would not be precluded from collecting civil penalties for willful conduct under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (c). Jernigan v. Ford (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 488, 492 interprets the language of Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (e)(2):

 

“Under that language, where there is a dispute resolution process “the manufacturer shall not be liable for any civil penalty under this subdivision.” (Italics added.) Consistent with usage throughout the Act, “subdivision” can refer only to subdivision (e) of section 1794. By use of the phrase “under this subdivision,” the Legislature limited the exemption. It applies only to the civil penalties for nonwillful violations recoverable under section 1794, subdivision (e), and does not apply to penalties which may be available under another subdivision, i.e., 1794, subdivision (c).”

 

(Italics added.)

 

Defendant argues that the NCDS program “substantially complies” with the requirements of Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (d), and thus it is a qualified third-party dispute resolution process. The Court has rejected this argument above.

 

Defendant further asserts there is a genuine issue of material fact as to whether “[Defendants]’s NCDS program substantially complies with the Act, whether [Defendant] acted in good faith, and whether its conduct rises to the level of willfulness.” (Opp., p. 8, lines 11-13.) Defendant essentially asserts that civil penalties for willful conduct are not warranted where it acted in good faith to comply with the requirements of Civil Code section 1793.22, subdivision (d).

 

Defendant confuses the issues here. The issue is not whether Defendant acted willfully in failing to certify its dispute resolution program. The issue of willfulness goes to whether Defendant intentionally violated the substantive provisions of the Song-Beverly Act. The Court notes that Plaintiff’s claim for a willful violation pursuant to Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (c) is based on Defendant’s willful failure to promptly repurchase or replace Plaintiff’s vehicle. (Reply, p. 1, fn.1.) Plaintiff, through this Motion, seeks to prove that Defendant does not have a qualified third-party dispute resolution process, and thus, it has no protection for civil liability of non-willful conduct. Plaintiff argues that even if Defendant has such a process, Plaintiff would not be precluded from seeking civil penalties under Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (c) for willful conduct. The Court agrees. Whether Defendant willfully or non-willfully complied with the requirements for qualified third-party dispute resolution processes is irrelevant.

 

The Motion for Summary Adjudication as to the seventh affirmative defense is GRANTED. 


Case Number: 23NWCV00436    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: C

GONZALEZ v. DIAMOND ENVIRONMENTAL SERVICES, L.P., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV00436

HEARING: December 3, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

#10

TENTATIVE ORDER

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One Nos. 15.1 and 17.1 are GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. Part of the motion is continued to January 14, 2025 at 10:30.

Plaintiff is awarded $1,875.00 in sanctions.

Plaintiff to give notice.

Background

This is a premises liability action. On February 9, 2023, Plaintiff Hermelinda Gonzalez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Diamond Environmental Services, L.P. (“Diamond”) and Does 1 to 50. On June 27, 2023, Plaintiff amended the Complaint to add Nuconcepts, Interstate Batteries, Inc., and Clarios International, Inc.as Doe Defendants. The Complaint alleges a battery device contained in a portable restroom exploded, causing Plaintiff’s injuries. The Complaint asserts three causes of action: 1) General Negligence, 2) Premises Liability, and 3) Products Liability.

On June 28, 2023, Diamond filed a Cross-Complaint against Nuconcepts, Interstate Batteries, Inc., and Clarios International, Inc.

On September 8, 2023, Andy Gump, Inc., dba Nuconcepts (erroneously sued as Nuconcepts) filed a Cross-Complaint against Diamond, Interstate Batteries, Inc. and Clarios, LLC (erroneously sued as Clarios International, Inc.).

On September 14, 2023, Interstate Batteries, Inc. filed a Cross-Complaint against Diamond, Andy Gump, Inc., and Clarios, LLC.

On December 21, 2023, Clarios, LLC filed a Cross-Complaint against Diamond, Interstate Batteries, Inc., and Andy Gump, Inc.

On August 23, 2023, Plaintiff served Form Interrogatories, Set One to Andy Gump, Inc. (“Gump”). (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. 1.) Plaintiff granted Gump’s requests for an extension to respond by November 22, 2023. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶ 4.)

On November 22, 2023, Gump served verified responses to the Form Interrogatories, Set One. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. 2.)

Following a meet and confer letter from Plaintiff, on January 26, 2024, Gump again served further verified responses, providing objections. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶¶ 5-6; Exhs. 3, 4.) After multiple meet and confer attempts as to Form Interrogatories, Set One Nos. 15.1 and 17.1, and multiple deadline extensions, Gump refused to provide the requested further responses, suggesting Plaintiff make a supplemental discovery demand as discovery develops. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶¶ 7-9; Exh. 6, 7.) Gump has not provided further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 15.1 and 17.1.

Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses from Gump to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 15.1 and 17.1. Plaintiff also request monetary sanctions.

Legal Standard

On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that (1) an answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete, (2) an exercise of the option to produce documents under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate, or (3) an objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).) California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345 requires the moving party to file a separate statement containing factual and legal reasons to compel further responses. The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections thereto. (Coy v. Sup.Ct. (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.) ¿¿Where general boilerplate objections are made, courts have authority to compel further responses (but not to find a waiver of privilege objections).¿(Best Products, Inc. v. Sup. Ct. (2004) 119 Cal.App. 4th 1181, 1189.)¿¿

The court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)¿¿California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348 states that a “court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed.”

Meet and Confer

A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300, subd. (b).)

Plaintiff’s counsel has sent two meet and confer letters detailing deficiencies with Gump’s responses and further responses. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶¶ 5-6; Exhs. 3, 5.) On March 15, 2024, after Plaintiff once again offered to extend the time for Gump to provide further responses, Gump suggested that Plaintiff “make a supplemental demand as discovery moves down the road.” (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶¶ 7-9; Exhs. 6, 7.) Considering the parties could resolve this dispute despite attempts to meet and confer, the Court finds the meet and confer requirement has been met.

Discussion

Form Interrogatory No. 15.1

Plaintiff moves to compel further as to Form Interrogatory, Set One, No. 15.1, which reads as follows:

“Identify each denial of a material allegation and each special or affirmative defense in your pleadings and for each:

a) state all facts upon which you base the denial or affirmative defense

b) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and

c) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your denial or special affirmative defense, and state the name, ADDRESS and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT.”

(Zurita-Cruz Decl. ¶ 3; Exh. 1.)

Plaintiff particularly seeks to elicit further responses regarding the facts, witnesses, and documents supporting Gump’s Fourth Affirmative Defense for Contribution/Comparative Fault, Eighth Affirmative Defense for Intervening/Superseding Cause, Fourteenth Affirmative Defense for Complete Performance, Fifteenth Affirmative Defense for Unintended/Abnormal Use, Sixteenth Affirmative Defense for Misuse/Modification, Twenty-Third Affirmative Defense for Spoliation, Twenty-Fourth Affirmative Defense for Reasonable Care, Twenty-Ninth Affirmative Defense for Act of God, Thirtieth Affirmative Defense for No Liability for Doe Defendants, and Thirty-Second Affirmative Defense for Joinder.

Although Defendant has not filed an opposition, the Court will consider Gump’s general objections and responses as to each affirmative defense. Gump argues that Plaintiff has not been deposed and has not undergone a medical

examination, so the interrogatories are premature. Gump argues the interrogatories seek information that would violate attorney-client privilege and work-product privilege. Gump further argues that discovery is ongoing, and it reserves the right to provide further information as discovery continues.

The Court finds that Gump’s general objections and further responses are inadequate. Gump’s objections based on attorney-client privilege and work product privilege are unclear as how they apply to any responsive documents. Gump’s objections that such responses are premature lack merit. Gump’s responses state that each defense is based on Gump’s “information and belief” but fails to state facts upon which its belief is grounded in. If Gump does not have the sufficient personal knowledge to respond, it shall state so, but also shall make a “a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.220.) Gump’s responses that “Discovery is continuing” are insufficient.

Plaintiff also seeks further responses for the phone number and address of Thomas Field, the person identified with personal knowledge of such facts supporting the affirmative defenses. Gump’s further responses state the following: “Thomas Field; who may be reached through Tharpe & Howell, LLP; 15250 Ventura Blvd, Sherman Oaks, CA 91403; (818) 205-9955.” Based on Gump’s general objections to the Form Interrogatories, Set One and the meet and confer emails, it seems Gump is objecting to providing Field’s address due to privacy reasons. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶¶ 5-7; Exhs. 2, 4, 6.)

Plaintiff must avail herself of this method to contact Thomas Field. This portion of the motion is continued to January 14, 2025 at 10:30 for an update and a continued hearing on the motion to compel. If plaintiff is dissatisfied with her ability to reach Thomas Field, then the responding party may be required to provide its last known contact information. Phone numbers and addresses of witnesses are relevant and commonly discoverable. (See People v. Dixon (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 414, 443 (“The disclosure of the names and addresses of potential witnesses is a routine and essential part of pretrial discovery.”); see also Puerto v. Superior Court (2008) 158 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1253 (finding witnesses’ phone number and addresses, “while personal, [are] not particularly sensitive, as [they are] merely contact information” and are “generally discoverable”); see also Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010 (“Discovery may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter.”).) Gump has filed no opposition providing reasons for why Field’s right to privacy would be severely intruded upon. Further, Plaintiff has agreed not to contact Thomas Field without first contacting Gump’s counsel. (Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶¶ 5-6; Exhs. 3, 5.) The Court finds Field’s contact information is discoverable, but defers ruling on this portion of the motion.

The Court grants the rest of the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatory, Set One, No. 15.1.

Form Interrogatory No. 17.1

Plaintiff moves to compel further as to Form Interrogatory, Set One, No. 17.1, which reads as follows:

“Is your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories an unqualified admission? If not, for each response that is not an unqualified admission:¿¿

a. state the number of the request;¿

b. state all facts upon which you base your response;¿

c. state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of¿those facts; and¿

e. identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.”

(Zurita-Cruz Decl., ¶ 3; Exh. 1.)

Plaintiff argues that Gump has failed to adequately respond to subdivision (c) in its responses, which requires Thomas Field’s address and phone number. As discussed previously, the Court finds that Field’s contact information is discoverable, but defers ruling on this issue.

The Court continues the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatory, Set One, No. 17.1 to January 14, 2025 at 10:30.

Sanctions

Plaintiff’s counsel requests $2,250.00 in sanctions. Plaintiff’s counsel seeks attorney’s fees for 9 hours of work (2 hours to draft two meet and confer letters, 3.5 hours to prepare this Motion, .5 hours to review the opposition, 1 hour to prepare the reply, and 2 hours for court appearance) at counsel’s rate of $250.00 per hour. The Court reduces the hours by 2.5 hours because no opposition or reply was filed, and the court appearance will take no longer than 1 hour. The Court thus awards sanctions in the amount of $1,875.00 (7.5 hours x $250.00).
