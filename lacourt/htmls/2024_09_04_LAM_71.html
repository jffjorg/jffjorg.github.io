DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.

Cross-Complainant and Defendants’ 8/19/24 request for judicial notice of (1) Declaration of Saul Reiss in Support of Defendants’ Opposition to Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication; and (2) Declaration of Ara Baljian in Support of Opposition by Defendants to Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Adjudication is denied because this Court does not need to take judicial notice of filings on the instant docket.

 

Background

Defendants retained Perkins to offer an expert opinion on: (1) the total potential yield, total potential value, and the total potential ratio of dry cannabis flower, dry small cannabis flowers, and dry trim of cannabis crops grown outdoors and in hoop houses at 3333 Avena St., Lompoc, California (“Premises”); and (2) the amount of cannabis that the joint venture actually transferred and or sold. (Decl. of Young ¶4, Exh. 1 at pg. 2.)

Cross-Defendants filed the instant motion on June 14, 2024.  Defendants and Cross-Complainant filed their opposition on August 19, 2024.  Cross-Defendants filed their reply on August 27, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

A motion in limine may be granted where a witness’ opinion is based on assumptions unsupported by any evidence.  (Evid. Code §803 [“The court may, and upon objection shall, exclude testimony in the form of an opinion that is based in whole or in significant part on matter that is not a proper basis for such an opinion.”].)  

“An expert’s opinion that something could be true if certain assumed facts are true, without any foundation for concluding those assumed facts exist in the case before the jury, does not provide assistance to the jury because the jury is charged with determining what occurred in the case before it, not hypothetical possibilities.”  (Jennings v. Palomar Pomerado Health Systems, Inc. (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 1108, 1117-1121 [upholding trial court’s exclusion of expert testimony under Evidence Code §801 where the expert’s conclusion was not supported by a reasoned explanation].)  

An expert can rely on representations made by others.  (People v. Veamatahau (2020) 9 Cal.5th 16, 33.)  Indeed, an expert’s reliance on representations by others “does not mean the trustworthiness of that information cannot be explored through examination.”  (Id.)

Under Evidence Code §802, “a court may inquire into, not only the type of material on which an expert relies, but also whether that material actually supports the expert’s reasoning” and conclude that the expert’s opinion should be excluded.  (Sargon Enterprises, Inc. v. University of Southern California (2012) 55 Cal.4th 747, 770-771; San Francisco Print Media Co. v. The Hearst Corp. (2020) 44 Cal.App.5th 952, 963 [stating trial court is authorized to exclude expert opinions it determines lack a foundational basis].)

 

Discussion

Cross-Defendants’ motion does not challenge Perkins’ qualifications, specialized knowledge, skill, experience, training, or education as a cannabis expert. It is thus undisputed that Perkins is qualified to testify as a cannabis expert, pursuant to Evidence Code §§801(b) and 802.

Evidence Code §803 deals with exclusion of expert testimony that is based in whole or in part on improper matter. Section 803 states:

The court may, and upon objection shall, exclude testimony in the form of an opinion that is based in whole or in part on matter that is not a proper basis for such an opinion. In such case, the witness may, if there remains a proper basis for his opinion, then state his opinion after excluding from consideration the matter determined to be improper.

 

(Evid. Code §803.)

Cross-Defendants contend that Perkins has relied on improper matter consisting of unsupported assumed facts lacking foundation, and that his opinions are based on mere speculation and conjecture.  However, Perkins relied on Plaintiff’s own evidence produced in support of their claims in this matter.  (Decl. of Reiss ¶10, Exh. D at pgs. 127:3-129:8.)

Based on Perkins’ deposition testimony, he relied on Plaintiff’s information, specifically the information on page 3 of their brochure, in rendering his opinions in this matter.  It is also clear from Bates Numbers (RBM002179-RBM-002185) that Plaintiff produced that brochure.  Plaintiff thus represents the information as being truthful and competent evidence.  “The proper role of an expert is to help the jury understand the significance of case-specific facts proven by competent evidence, not to place before the jury otherwise unsubstantiated assertions of fact.”  (People v. Valencia (2021) 11 Cal.5th 818, 837.)

Here, Perkins was given matter containing Plaintiff’s written representations on the cannabis production at the Property during the period at issue before his deposition.  Because the information pertains to what the cannabis expert is testifying to, it is of the type of matter that a cannabis expert may reasonably rely upon in rendering his opinions on the cannabis at the Property, including production, during that period at issue.  (Evid. Code §§801(b), 802.)

Further, Cross-Defendants’ motion does not state that Plaintiff’s marketing brochure contains improper matter.  Given the existence of foundational reliability, “the strength of an expert’s assumptions affects the weight rather than the admissibility of the opinion.”  (Howard Entertainment, Inc. v. Kudrow (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1102, 1121.)  For those reasons, Evidence Code §803 does not apply.

Cross-Defendants also have not shown that Perkins’ opinion on actual production, which he is expected to opine on and is based on Defendants’ cannabis sales spreadsheet Defendants gave him, would be more prejudicial than probative.  (Decl. of Reiss ¶10, Exh. D at pgs. 122:21-123:9, 130:19-131:1.)

Accordingly, Cross-Defendants’ motion is denied.

 

Conclusion

Cross-Defendants’ motion to disqualify Defendants’ and Cross-Complainants’ expert witness, Perkins, is denied.

HARRY GINOZA,

INVESTMENT PARTNERS, INC.

Plaintiff Harry Ginoza’s unopposed motion for an order to substitute Plaintiff’s niece, Linda Ginoza-Grovier, in place and stead of Plaintiff Harry Ginoza is continued to September 25, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.  Plaintiff is to file Exhibit A to the Declaration of Ginoza-Grovier by September 6, 2024.

 

Plaintiff Harry Ginoza (“Ginoza”) (“Plaintiff”) moves unopposed for an order granting his niece, Linda Ginoza-Grovier (“Ginoza-Grovier”), who is the trustee of the Harry Ginoza Trust, be substituted in as Plaintiff in place and stead of Ginoza.  (Notice Motion, pgs. 1-2; C.C.P. §377.32.)  Plaintiff moves on the grounds that Ginoza died during the pendency of this action and Ginoza-Grovier is Ginoza’s successor-in-interest as trustee of the Harry Ginoza Trust.  (Notice Motion, pg. 2.)

 

Procedural History

Plaintiff filed his operative Complaint on April 13, 2023, against Defendant Investor Partners, Inc. (“Investor Partners”) (“Defendant”) for five causes of action: (1) cancellation of written instruments; (2) declaratory relief; (3) quiet title; (4) fraud; and (5) breach of fiduciary duty.

Plaintiff filed the instant motion on May 16, 2024.  As of the date of this hearing no opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard

C.C.P. §377.32 provides the following information is required for a statement from a successor in interest:

(a)  The person who seeks to commence an action or proceeding or to continue a pending action or proceeding as the decedent’s successor in interest under this article, shall execute and file an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating all of the following:

 

(1)   The decedent’s name.

(2)   The date and place of the decedent’s death.

(3)   “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate.”

(4)   If the decedent’s estate was administered, a copy of the final order showing the distribution of the decedent’s cause of action to the successor in interest.

(5)   Either of the following, as appropriate, with facts in support thereof:

(A)   “The affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding.”

(B)   “The affiant or declarant is authorized to act on behalf of the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) with respect to the decedent's interest in the action or proceeding.”

(6)   “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding.”

(7)   “The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct.”

(b)  Where more than one person executes the affidavit or declaration under this section, the statements required by subdivision (a) shall be modified as appropriate to reflect that fact.

 

(c)  A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate shall be attached to the affidavit or declaration.

 

(C.C.P. §377.32.)

 

Discussion

Ginoza-Grovier executed and filed an affidavit or a declaration under penalty of perjury under the laws of this state stating all of the following: (1) the decedent’s name (Decl. of Ginoza-Grovier ¶2); (2)   the date and place of the decedent’s death (id.); (3) “No proceeding is now pending in California for administration of the decedent’s estate” (id. at ¶3); (5A) “The affiant or declarant is the decedent’s successor in interest (as defined in Section 377.11 of the California Code of Civil Procedure) and succeeds to the decedent’s interest in the action or proceeding” (id. at ¶¶4-5); (6) “No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent in the pending action or proceeding” (id. at ¶6); and (7) “The affiant or declarant affirms or declares under penalty of perjury under the laws of the State of California that the foregoing is true and correct” (id. at pg. 3).

However, Plaintiff fails to attach a certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate to her declaration in violation of C.C.P. §377.32(c).  (See Decl. of Ginoza-Grovier ¶7)

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

BEL ESPRIT BUILDERS, INC, 

 

         vs.

 

BEN WILKINS, et al.

 Case No.:  24STCV00029

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 5, 2024

 

Defendants Ben Wilkins’, Karen Wilkins’, and the Wilkins Family Living Trust’s demurrer to the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action of Plaintiff Bel Esprit Builders, Inc.’s first amended complaint is overruled.

The Court, sua sponte, strikes Plaintiff’s 4th cause of action because Plaintiff was not granted leave of Court to make such amendments to its pleading.

 

Defendants Ben Wilkins (“Ben”), Karen Wilkins (“Karen”), and the Wilkins Family Living Trust (“Trust”) (collectively, “Defendants”) demur to the 1st, 2nd, 3rd, and 4th causes of action in Plaintiff Bel Esprit Builders, Inc.’s (“Bel Esprit”) (“Plaintiff”) first amended complaint (“FAC”) on the basis the causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to allege causes of action against Defendants and are stated ambiguously and unintelligibly.  (Notice of Demurrer, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§430.10(e), (f), (g).)

 

Background

Plaintiff filed its initial Complaint against Defendants on January 2, 2024, alleging three causes of action: (1) breach of contract; (2) common counts; and (3) foreclose a Mechanic’s Lien.

On May 29, 2024, this Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action in the initial Complaint with 20 days leave to amend.  (5/29/24 Minute Order.)

Plaintiff filed the operative FAC on June 4, 2024, alleging four causes of action: (1) breach of contract; (2) common counts; (3) foreclose a Mechanic’s Lien; and (4) theft.

Plaintiff’s Complaint arises out of its entry into a written contract (“Contract”) with Defendant whereby it agreed to provide services for Ben’s and Karen’s residence located at 8181 Gould Avenue, Los Angeles, California (“Project”).  (See FAC ¶8, Exh. A.)  Plaintiff alleges it is, and at all times relevant herein was, a licensed California general contractor that specializes in construction consultation.  (FAC ¶8.)  Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that the Contract is signed by Karen.  (FAC ¶8.) 

Plaintiff alleges Ben and/or Karen directed Plaintiff to send “the Proposal” to Eric Chun, which it did via email on August 8, 2022.  (FAC ¶9, Exh. B.)  Plaintiff alleges Ben and Karen were copied on the email.  (FAC ¶9.)  Plaintiff alleges on or about August 15, 2022, Plaintiff received notice from Ben that Eric Chun was his representative for the work done on the Project.  (FAC ¶9, Exh. C.)  Plaintiff alleges at some point Eric Chun terminated his relationship as Ben and Karen’s representative. (FAC ¶9, Exh. D.)  

Plaintiff alleges it performed all obligations imposed upon it by the terms of the Contract except for those excused by Defendants’ breach or prevented by Defendants’ acts.  (FAC ¶10.) 

Plaintiff alleges the agreed price and reasonable value of the labor and materials Plaintiff furnished to Defendant was and is the sum of $54,063.98.  (FAC ¶11.)  Plaintiff alleges Ben and Karen paid Plaintiff the sum of $25,000.00, leaving a balance due of $29,063.98, and nothing was paid or credited thereon.  (FAC ¶11.)

Plaintiff alleges Defendant became indebted to it within the last year for labor, services, and materials rendered at the special instance and request of Defendant and for which Defendant promised to pay Plaintiff the reasonable value.  (FAC ¶14.)  Plaintiff alleges the labor, services, and materials furnished to Defendant were worth the sum of $29,063.98, and nothing was paid or credited thereon.  (FAC ¶14.)  Plaintiff alleges although demand was made therefore, Defendant failed, neglected, and refused to pay the sum of $29,063.98.  (FAC ¶16.) 

Plaintiff alleges it duly gave Defendants a written preliminary notice as prescribed by and in accordance with the requirements of Civil Code §3097, and on November 13, 2023, it recorded its Mechanic’s Lien with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office.  (FAC ¶¶18-19, Exh. E.)  Plaintiff alleges no notice of completion of the Project and/or cessation of labor thereon was ever filed in the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office, and at the time of the filing of Plaintiff’s Mechanic’s Lien, ninety days had not elapsed or expired since the completion of the Project or any cessation of labor thereon.  (FAC ¶20.)   

Plaintiff alleges its work was necessary and required for the convenient use and occupation of the Project.  (FAC ¶21.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants have or claim to have some estate, lien, right, title, or interest in or upon the Project or some part thereof, and all such claims or liens are subject, subsequent, and subordinate to Plaintiff’s Mechanic’s Lien.  (FAC ¶22.)  Plaintiff alleges it is owed and seeks a Judgment for its lien in the amount of $29,063.98 against the subject property.  (FAC ¶23.) 

Plaintiff alleges Defendants obtained building materials they knew belonged to or were owned by Plaintiff.  (FAC ¶25.)  Plaintiff alleges when Defendants obtained the building materials, they intended to and did permanently deprive Plaintiff of the materials while keeping the materials for themselves.  (FAC ¶25.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants retained possession of the materials despite Plaintiff’s demand that the materials be returned.  (FAC ¶26.)

On July 10, 2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer.  On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition.  Defendants filed their reply on August 28, 2024.

 

          Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a), emphasis added.)  A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

Defendants’ counsel declares she attempted to contact Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone on July 3, 2024, to meet and confer, but was not able to connect with anyone in his office or leave a voicemail.  (Decl. of Stepanian ¶5.)  Defendants’ counsel declares that she attempted on July 5, 2024, to contact Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone but was unable connect with anyone in his office or leave a voicemail.  (Decl. of Stepanian ¶6.)  Defendants’ counsel’s declaration is sufficient under C.C.P. §430.41.  Accordingly, the Court will consider the instant demurrer.

 

1.     Demurrer

Summary of Demurrer

Defendants demur to the 1st cause of action on the basis Defendants are not a party to the written contract, as demonstrated by the attached Exhibit A to Plaintiff’s FAC, and therefore the alleged contract is illegal, ambiguous, deficient, and unenforceable.  (Demurrer, pg. 1.)  Defendants demur to the 2nd and 3rd causes of action in Plaintiff’s Complaint on the basis the causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action against Defendants.  (Demurrer, pg. 1.)  Defendants demur to the 4th cause of action on the basis Plaintiff fails to establish criminal intent.  (Demurrer, pg. 1.) Defendants demur to all causes of action on the basis they are uncertain.  (Demurrer, pg. 1.)

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

If the exhibit is ambiguous and can be construed in the manner stated in the complaint, the court must accept the construction offered by plaintiff.  (SC Manufactured Homes, Inc. v. Liebert (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 68, 83.)

         

Breach of Contract (1st COA)

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) resulting damage to the plaintiff.”  (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.”  (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)  In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also “plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.”  (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Insurance Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.)

Plaintiff alleges it entered into a written contract with Defendants who breached their obligation. (FAC ¶8.)  Plaintiff attached and incorporated by reference a true and correct copy of the alleged contract as Exhibit A.  (FAC ¶8, Exh. A.)  A review of the Contract shows that Defendant entered into a contract with an individual named Eric Chun, who is not named herein as a party.  Exhibit A is a letter addressed to “Eric Chun,” with the subject line “PROPOSAL,” and directly below the subject line states, “Bel Esprit proposes to provide all labor and material to accomplish the following.”  (FAC ¶8, Exh. A.)  Defendants’ names are not printed anywhere on the document.  Plaintiff alleges on information and belief that the Contract is signed by Karen.  (FAC ¶8.)

Plaintiff alleges Ben and/or Karen directed Plaintiff to send the Proposal to Eric Chun, which Plaintiff did via email on August 8, 2022.  (FAC ¶9, Exh. B.)  Plaintiff alleges on or about August 15, 2022, Plaintiff received notice from Ben that Eric was his representative for the Project.  (FAC ¶9, Exh. C.)

Plaintiff sufficiently alleges Karen signed the Contract (FAC ¶8) and that the Contract was sent to Ben and Karen’s authorized agent, Eric Chun, at Ben and Karen’s request (FAC ¶¶8-9, Exhs. B-C).  Plaintiff alleges the terms of the contract.  (FAC ¶8, Exh. A.) 

Defendants’ argument that this Court should not enforce the Contract on the basis it is illegal is unavailing.  Generally, a contract made in violation of a regulatory statute is void.  (Vitek, Inc. v. Aluarado Ice Palace, Inc. (1973) 34 Cal.App.3d 586, 591.)  The rule is not without exception, however, and before the rule can be applied in any case of a statute prohibiting or enjoining things to be done, with a prohibition or a penalty, or a penalty only for doing a thing which it forbids, the statute must be examined as a whole, to find out whether the makers meant a contract in contravention of it should be void.  (Id. at pgs. 591-592.)

In Hinerfeld-Ward, Inc. v. Lipian, the court determined that an oral home improvement contract may be enforceable where the owners were sophisticated and actively involved with the construction project, the work was completed to the owner’s satisfaction, and it would be an injustice not to enforce the contract.  (Hinerfeld-Ward, Inc. v. Lipian (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 86, 94-95.)  The Hinerfeld-Ward Court relied on the California Supreme Court’s opinion, Asdourian v. Araj (1985) 38 Cal.3d 276, which held that Business & Professions Code §7159 does not contain any express prohibition against the enforcement of a contract; therefore, an oral agreement may be enforceable under certain circumstances, including where the work has been completed, performed under authority granted by the owners, and was ratified by the owners.  (Hinerfeld-Ward, Inc., 188 Cal.App.4th at pg. 92.)

Here, Plaintiff sufficiently alleges it fully performed all obligations imposed upon it by the terms of the Contract (FAC ¶10), that the work has been completed and performed under authority granted by the owners, and that Defendants would be unjustly enriched by not enforcing the Contract (see FAC ¶¶9, 11).  Despite the violations of Business and Professions Code §§7150-7168 et seq. presented by the form of the Contract, the deficiencies present do not necessitate this Court to render the Contract void on its face.

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 1st cause of action is overruled.

 

Common Count (2nd COA)

“In the common law action of general assumpsit, it is customary to plead an indebtedness using ‘common counts.’ In California, it has long been settled that the allegation of claims using common counts is good against special or general demurrers. The only essential allegations of a common count are ‘(1) the statement of indebtedness in a certain sum, (2) the consideration, i.e., goods sold, work done, etc., and (3) nonpayment.’” (Farmers Insurance Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460, internal citations omitted.)

Plaintiff sufficiently alleges “Defendant” owes Plaintiff a sum certain, that labor, services, and materials were rendered, and nonpayment.  (FAC ¶¶14-16.)  Defendants’ repeat their arguments with regard to the unenforceability of the Contract, which are also unavailing as to the instant cause of action.

Accordingly, Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s 2nd cause of action is overruled.

 

Foreclosure of Mechanic’s Lien (3rd COA)

“[T]he elements of a cause of action to foreclose on a mechanic’s lien [are]: [(1) the lienholder’s] labor, services, and materials were used in a work of improvement[; (2)] the reasonable value of its contribution[; and (3)] . . . the date the work was complete.”  (Vought Construction Inc. v. Stock (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 622, 637, review denied (Jan. 25, 2023).)  The lien claimant has the burden of establishing the validity of the lien.  (Basic Modular Facilities, Inc. v. Ehsanipour (1999) 70 Cal.App.4th 1480, 1485.)

Plaintiff alleges it duly gave Defendants a written preliminary notice as prescribed in Civil Code §3097.  (FAC ¶18.)  Plaintiff alleges on November 13, 2023, it recorded its Mechanic’s Lien with the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office under and by virtue of the Civil Code.  (FAC ¶19, Exh. E.)  Plaintiff alleges no notice of completion of the Project and/or any cessation of labor thereon was ever filed in the Los Angeles Recorder’s Office, and at the time of the filing of Plaintiff’s Mechanic’s Lien, ninety days had not elapsed or expired since the completion of the Project or any cessation of labor thereon.  (FAC ¶20.)

Plaintiff alleges its work was necessary and required for the convenient use and occupation of the Project.  (FAC ¶21.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendants have or claim to have some estate, lien, title, or interest in or upon the Project or some part thereof, and all such claims or liens are subject, subsequent, and subordinate to Plaintiff’s Mechanic’s Lien.  (FAC ¶22.)  Plaintiff alleges it is owed and seeks a Judgment for its lien in the amount of $29,063.98 against the Subject Property.  (FAC ¶23.)

Plaintiff sufficiently alleges its labor, services, and materials were used in a work of improvement (FAC ¶14), the reasonable value of its contribution (FAC ¶16), and that no notice of completion of the Project or any cessation of the labor thereon was ever filed with the County Recorder’s Office (FAC ¶20).  (Vought Construction Inc., 84 Cal.App.5th at pg. 637.)  Plaintiff fails to attach Exhibit E to the FAC.  However, there is no requirement that a copy of the Mechanic’s Lien must be attached to the pleading in order to sufficiently plead a foreclosure of Mechanic’s Lien cause of action.  (See id.) 

Defendants’ argument that Plaintiff’s Mechanic’s Lien was untimely filed goes beyond the four corners of the FAC; the Court must take the allegations in the FAC as true.  Plaintiff’s cause of action alleges that on November 13, 2023, it recorded the Mechanic’s Lien (FAC ¶19) and at the time of the filing of Plaintiff’s Mechanic’s Lien ninety days had not elapsed or expired since the completion of the Project or any cessation of labor thereon (FAC ¶20).  Defendant’s argument that Eric Chun’s statement in Exhibit D of the FAC indicating that Ben texted him to “STOP WORK” on September 8, 2022, at 8:00 p.m. is ambiguous in terms of the period of time that work was directed to stop and must be construed favorably for Plaintiff; the instruction stated in Exhibit D does not lead to the assumption that all work ceased from that date forward.  (SC Manufactured Homes, Inc., 162 Cal.App.4th 68, 83 [“If the allegations in the complaint conflict with the exhibits, we rely on and accept as true the contents of the exhibits. However, in doing so, if the exhibits are ambiguous and can be construed in the manner suggested by plaintiff, then we must accept the construction offered by plaintiff.”].)

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s 3rd cause of action is overruled.

 

Theft (4th COA)

California Penal Code §496 (c) provides: “Any person who has been injured by a violation of subdivision (a) or (b) may bring an action for three times the amount of actual damages, if any, sustained by the plaintiff, costs of suit, and reasonable attorney’s fees.”  (Pen. Code §496(c).)

Penal Code §496(a) provides:

Every person who buys or receives any property that has been stolen or that has been obtained in any manner constituting theft or extortion, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, or who conceals, sells, withholds, or aids in concealing, selling, or withholding any property from the owner, knowing the property to be so stolen or obtained, shall be punished by imprisonment in a county jail for not more than one year, or imprisonment pursuant to subdivision (h) of Section 1170.

 

(Pen. Code §496.)

          The Court declines to address the demurrer to Plaintiff’s 4th cause of action and will address the cause of action, sua sponte, in a motion to strike.

 

Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is overruled as to the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action.

Moving Party to give notice.

 

2.     Motion to Strike

Legal Standard

C.C.P. §436 provides that the Court may . . . at any time within its discretion and upon terms it deems proper, “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  (C.C.P. §436(b).) 

 

Theft (4th COA)

It is the general rule that when a court “sustains a demurrer with leave to amend, the scope of the grant of leave is ordinarily a limited one. It gives the pleader an opportunity to cure the defects in the particular causes of action to which the demurrer was sustained, but that is all.”  (Community Water Coalition v. Santa Cruz County Local Agency Formation Commission (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1317, 1329.)  “The Plaintiff may not amend the complaint to add a new cause of action without having obtained permission to do so, unless the new cause of action is within the scope of the order granting leave to amend.”  (Id., quoting Harris v. Wachovia Mortgage, FSB (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1018, 1023.)  “[L]eave to amend must be construed as permission to the pleader to amend the cause of action which he pleaded in the pleading to which the demurrer has been sustained.” (People ex rel. Department of Public Works v. Clauson (1957) 248 Cal.App.2d 770, 785.)

The prior demurrer and motion to strike did not address a cause of action pertaining to theft.  Here, in the FAC, the 4th cause of action is newly stated against Defendants without leave of this Court.  Plaintiff was not permitted to amend its pleading in this manner based on this Court’s May 29, 2024, ruling, or by Plaintiff moving for leave of this Court to amend.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s 4th cause of action is stricken from Plaintiff’s FAC.

 

Conclusion

The Court overrules Defendants Ben Wilkins’, Karen Wilkins’, and the Wilkins Family Living Trust’s demurrer to the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd causes of action of Plaintiff Bel Esprit Builders, Inc.’s first amended complaint.

The Court, sua sponte, strikes Plaintiff’s allegations against Defendants in the 4th cause of action.
