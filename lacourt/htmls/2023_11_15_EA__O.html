DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

The Court may change tentative rulings at any time. Therefore, attorneys are advised to check this website to determine if any changes or updates have been made to the tentative ruling.

Counsel may submit on the tentative rulings by calling the clerk in Dept. O at 909-802-1126 before 8:30 the morning of the hearing. Submission on the tentative does not bind the court to adopt the tentative ruling at the hearing should the opposing party appear and convince the court of further modification during oral argument.

The Tentative Ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question. No such filing will be considered by the Court in the absence of permission first obtained following ex-parte application therefore.



Case Number: 20STCV24771    Hearing Date: November 15, 2023    Dept: O

Tentative Ruling

 

(1)   DEFENDANT COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES’ DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFFS’ SIXTH AMENDED COMPLAINT is OVERRULED in part (i.e., 2nd COA because Plaintiffs have pled a mandatory duty (policy to notify a relative)) and SUSTAINED in part (i.e., as to 8th COA). Leave to amend is TBD as to the 8th COA—the court request supplemental briefing.

 

(2)   DEFENDANT COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES’ MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF PLAINTIFFS’ SIXTH AMENDED COMPLAINT is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part and TBD in part.

 

The court continues the hearing Monday, January 8, 2023 at 10 AM. Plaintiffs are to provide their supplemental brief by Thursday, November 30, 2023 and the County is to provide its supplemental brief by Friday, December 15, 2023.

 

Background

 

This case arises from the death of NOAH CUATRO (“Noah”). Plaintiffs EVANGELINA HERNANDEZ (“Eva”), individually and as Successor in Interest to the Estate of NOAH CUATRO; A.C. (“AC”), a minor, by and through her Guardian ad Litem, Evangelina Hernandez; E.C. (“EC”), a minor, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Evangelina Hernandez; R.C. (“RC”), a minor, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Evangelina Hernandez[1] allege the following against Defendants COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES (“County”) and HATHAWAY-SYCAMORES CHILD AND FAMILY SERVICES (“Hathaway-Sycamores” or “Hathaway”)[2]: On July 6, 2019, Noah was murdered by his parents, both of whom are awaiting trial. Noah’s death occurred after multiple reports of abuse and torture had been made to the Los Angeles County Department of Children and Family Services ("DCFS"). The allegations against Hathaway are predicated upon vicarious liability (retaining an unlicensed trainee (Erika Schreefel (“Schreefel”)) who did not communicate accurate information to the dependency court (DC) that created the misimpression that Noah and his parents were progressing towards reunification when in fact the family problems remained the same or worsened during this time frame) and independent negligence for its failure to supervise an unlicensed trainee. As for the County’s liability, it is predicated upon DCFS’s alleged breaches of various duties.  

 

On July 1, 2020, Plaintiffs filed the action.

 

On March 12, 2021, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint.

 

On June 24, 2021, Plaintiffs filed their second amended complaint.

 

On September 20, 2021, Plaintiffs filed their third amended complaint.

 

On January 14, 2022, the court issued its final ruling which sustained without leave to amend Hathaway’s demurrer.

 

On October 27, 2022, the court granted the County’s motion for judgment on the pleadings (MJOP) with leave to amend.[3]

 

On November 14, 2022, Plaintiffs filed their fourth amended complaint.

 

On March 14, 2023, the appellate court issued its opinion regarding Hathaway’s demurrer, reversing the trial court’s ruling.

 

On April 27, 2023, the court granted Plaintiffs’ motion for leave to file an amended complaint.

 

On May 10, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their fifth amended complaint (5AC) alleging eight causes of action (COA) for:

Legal Standard

A demurrer, in pertinent part, may be asserted when the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code of Civ. Proc., §430.10 subds. (e).) Accordingly, a demurrer tests the sufficiency of a pleading, and the grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) Lastly, CCP section 430.41 requires the parties to meet and confer before the filing of a demurrer.

Demurrer

 

The County again demurs to the 2nd COA for Negligence (Failure to Notify) and 8th COA for Negligence on the grounds that both fail to state sufficient facts to state COA for negligence. To the extent a minimal discussion of certain rules is provided, the court directs the parties to the court’s 7/20/23 ruling (regarding County’s demurrer to the 5AC).

 

I.                2nd COA for Negligence/Failure to Warn (Eva v. County)  

 

As discussed in the court’s 7/20/23 ruling, there are two ways in which a public entity may be liable: (i) vicarious liability and (ii) direct liability. (Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.4th 1112, 1127-1128.) “The Tort Claims Act draws a clear distinction between the liability of a public entity based on its own conduct, and the liability arising from the conduct of a public employee. Although the Act provides that a public employee generally is liable for an injury caused by his or her act or omission ‘to the same extent as a private person’ (Gov.Code, § 820, subd. (a)) and that, when the act or omission of the public employee occurs in the scope of employment the public entity will be vicariously liable for the injury (Gov.Code, § 815.2), the Act contains no provision similarly providing that a public entity generally is liable for its own conduct or omission to the same extent as a private person or entity. Rather, the Act provides that a public entity is not liable for an injury ‘[e]xcept as otherwise provided by statute....’” (Ibid.)

 

A.     Direct vs. Vicarious Liability

 

Unlike the basis for Hathaway’s liability which is explicitly predicated upon vicarious liability for Schreefel’s negligence (¶63), vicarious liability is not expressly alleged in the 6AC. Rather, the 6AC appears to be largely predicated upon direct liability.[4] Take for example, the following allegations:

 

-        “In reliance on Ms. Schreefel’s negligent opinion, the dependency court, over the objection of DCFS, decided Noah should be returned to his parents. Ms. Schreefel’s negligent opinion and HATHAWAY-SYCAMORES’ negligent failure to communicate fully, clearly, and accurately with the dependency court were substantial factors in the Court’s November 1, 2018, decision to return Noah.” (¶53, emphasis added)

-        "On November 6, 2018, Ms. Schreefel spoke with Noah’s social worker, who said that DCFS wanted Noah to be placed with [Eva].” (¶57, emphasis added)

-        “In reliance on Ms. Schreefel’s negligently wrong conclusion, the dependency court, over the objection of DCFS, ordered Noah to be returned to his parents on an extended visitation until a further hearing on November 9, 2018.” (¶67, emphasis added)

-        “On or about May 15, 2019, a DCFS caseworker, Susan Johnson, filed a 26-page petition to remove Noah from his parents’ custody . . . However, although this petition was granted, it was willfully ignored by DCFS.” (¶79, emphasis added)

-        “[T]he May 15, 2019 Order mandated Noah’s removal from his parents’ custody also mandated a medical and/or sexual abuse examination … DCFS willfully ignored this order and the examination never took place.” (¶80, emphasis added.)[5]

 

In sum, the allegations are that DCFS’s social workers knew of the ongoing abuse to Noah, made objections to the dependency court (DC), and made petitions to remove Noah from his parents’ custody (May 15, 2019 petition ¶117 and Susan Johnson’s 26-page petition ¶79), but that, ultimately, DCFS “ignore[d] the dependency court’s removal order and order for a forensic sexual abuse exam.” (¶116, emphasis added.)

 

With that, the court will first focus on whether Plaintiffs have alleged that the County is directly liability for the alleged failure to notify Eva of certain proceedings/actions. (See also Opp. p. 9:25-27 [“The County is liable for its failure to notify Eva of its own first party decision not to execute the warrant even after the dependency court determined Noah was in danger. Eva’s claim is not for failure to warn as much as failure to notify.”], italics added.)

 

B.     Direct Liability via the County’s Alleged Violation of Its Own Policy

 

Direct liability “requires identification of a ‘specific statute declaring [the entity] to be liable, or at least creating some specific duty of care[6] [by the agency in favor of the injured party], and not on the general tort provisions of Civil Code section 1714.’” (de Villers v. County of San Diego (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 238, 252, quoting Eastburn v. Regional Protection Authority (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1175, 1183; see also Davidson v. City of Westminster (1982) 32 Cal.3d 197, 203 [“[A] duty may arise … if ‘(a) a special relation exists between the actor and the third person which imposes a duty upon the actor to control the third person's conduct, or (b) a special relation exists between the actor and the other which gives the other a right to protection.’”].) Additionally, “[a] public entity may be directly liable for failure to discharge a mandatory duty.” (County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 627, 637.) “Where a public entity is under a mandatory duty imposed by an enactment that is designed to protect against the risk of a particular kind of injury, the public entity is liable for an injury of that kind proximately caused by its failure to discharge the duty unless the public entity establishes that it exercised reasonable diligence to discharge the duty.” (Id, at pp. 637-638, quoting Gov. Code, § 815.6.)

 

Here, the 6AC alleges that DCFS policy required social workers to notify Eva that DCFS was petitioning the DC to remove Noah from his parents because Noah was in danger, that the petition was granted, and that DCFS decided not to execute the warrant and to leave Noah in danger. (See e.g., 6AC ¶117 [list of alleged breaches].) According to Plaintiffs’ case citation, a policy created by a public entity is the equivalent of a regulation and is required to be followed. (Opp p. 9, citing County of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 627, 639.) Indeed, a mandatory duty exists if it requires a public agency to take particular action.

 

“Government Code [section] 815.6 contains a three-pronged test for determining whether liability may be imposed on a public entity: (1) an enactment must impose a mandatory, not discretionary, duty ...; (2) the enactment must intend to protect against the kind of risk of injury suffered by the party asserting section 815.6 as a basis for liability ...; and (3) breach of the mandatory duty must be a proximate cause of the injury suffered.’ [Citation.] Whether an enactment is intended to impose a mandatory duty is a question of law for the court.” (Id. at pp. 638-639, quoting Becerra v. County of Santa Cruz (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 1450, 1457.)[7]

 

Prong No. 1

 

As to the first prong—wherein the application of [Government Code] section 815.6 requires that the enactment at issue be obligatory, rather than merely discretionary or permissive, in its directions to the public entity—DCFS policy required the Children’s Social Worker (CSW) applying for the removal order to notify parents or relatives, like [Eva], who previously had custody of the child who is the subject of the petition.

 

Specifically, the allegation, found in ¶106, is as follows:

 

[A] former DCFS CSW testified that in May 2019 DCFS policy required the CSW applying for the removal order to notify parents or relatives, like [Eva], who previously had custody of the child who is the subject of the petition. Ms. Hernandez Aviles testified, ‘That’s the norm usually. When there is a warrant being served, we notify parents and if they had a previous placement that they were thriving in.’ [citation omitted]. Ms. Hernandez Aviles further testified that [Eva] should have been notified of the application for removal of Noah.”].)

 

Therefore, as Plaintiffs have alleged that there was a mandatory policy (“DCFS policy required the CSW applying for the removal order to notify parents or relatives”), then this prong is satisfied.

 

Prong No. 2

 

As to the second prong—wherein a plaintiff must show the injury is “one of the consequences which the [enacting body] sought to prevent through imposing the alleged mandatory duty”—that is somewhat alleged. The purpose behind the notification requirement is to seemingly place the child in a non-abusive household and to have the new caregiver involved in the process.

 

Therefore, for purposes of a demurrer, the court deems this prong satisfied.

 

Prong No. 3

 

As to third prong—causation—Plaintiffs allege that the failure to notify Eva left Noah with his dangerous and abusive parents, ultimately causing his death. (¶¶118, 119; see also ¶115 [“Had [Eva] informed the dependency court and hired an attorney to protect her rights and those of Noah’s. DCFS robbed [Eva] the opportunity to protect her rights and those of Noah’s, leading to his death. DCFS is blameworthy because it showed a callous disregard for [Eva] special relationship including her visitation rights and duties as the caregiver.”].)

 

Therefore, the third and final prong is satisfied.

 

Accordingly, Eva has sufficiently alleged direct liability against the County based upon the County’s purported to abide by its mandatory duty to notify a relative (here, Eva).

 

i.                 County’s Arguments that DCFS Policies are Not Regulations for Purposes of Mandatory Duty Law

 

First, to the extent that the County explains Plaintiffs’ case citations to Peterson v City of Long Beach (1979) 24 Cal.3d 238 and Lehto v. City of Oxnard (1985) 171 Cal.App.3d 285 are inapposite because the passage of Evidence Code section 669.1—which holds that a public entity policy is not considered a regulation unless it has been formally adopted as a statute, an ordinance, or a regulation (Demurrer p. 12, see also Reply p. 5)—County of Los Angeles, supra, 102 Cal.App.4th 627 does not turn on Evidence Code section 669.1. Therefore, even assuming section 669.1 shows an intent to vitiate Peterson’s holding (which held that a public entity’s policy is the equivalent of a regulation for mandatory duty purposes), Plaintiffs have provided otherwise valid, binding, and instructive authority. The County, however, does not address the County of Los Angeles case.

 

Second, to the extent that the County argues Thompson v. County of Los Angeles (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 376, 380–381 controls because Eva does not allege any violation of a nondiscretionary duty or a regulation (Reply p. 5:10-11), the May 2019 testimony of the CSW provides that the notification requirement was mandatory.

 

In Thompson, the plaintiff argued that the county violated its duty to make necessary collateral contacts by not speaking to the child’s doctor. The provision came from the state's Child Welfare Services Manual of Policies and Procedure. (Id. at p. 380.) The Thompson court determined that the “collateral contacts provision gives social workers discretion to determine which contacts are necessary. As a matter of law, it does not create a mandatory duty.” (Id. at p. 382.)  The court reasoned the provision was discretionary rather than mandatory because “the universe of people with ‘knowledge’ of a child's ‘condition’ is vast. It could include a child's parents, siblings, grandparents, aunts, uncles, cousins, school bus drivers, teachers, principals, school nurses, classmates, friends, friends’ parents, neighbors, police officers, doctors, and dentists, to name a few.” (Id. at p. 381).

 

Here, however, parents or relatives who previously had custody of the child would be far limited in scope (i.e., only Noah’s parents or Eva). 

 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiffs have sufficiently alleged a mandatory duty.

 

Therefore, as the 2nd COA does state a mandatory duty, which is sufficient to allege facts of negligence, the demurrer as to the 2nd COA is OVERRULED. The court need not address whether a special relationship existed between the County’s employees and Eva (but see Motion to Strike below).

 

1.     8th COA for Negligence (Minor Plaintiffs v. County)

 

Previously, in response to the County’s arguments that (1) Minor Plaintiffs are barred from asserting any claims because they failed to assert claims within six months of claims rejection(s) and (2) the COA is time barred because it does not relate back to the original complaint, the court observed that Minor Plaintiffs’ “Government Tort Claims indicated that they were aware of sufficient facts to plead a cause of negligence against the County based on their own abuse as early as December 2019.” (See July 20, 2023 Ruling.) As for the relation back doctrine, the court determined that “the factual allegations in the previous complaints related to the death of Noah” such that there was no relation to the now asserted Minor Plaintiffs’ relief for “harm directly inflicted upon them by their parents.” (Ibid.)[8] “In effect, even if the same parties are involved (parents as the perpetrators and the County/DCFS) and the same period is involved, the same facts (i.e., facts of abuse to Minor Plaintiffs) are not involved such that it sounds as its own separate action.” (Ibid.)

 

In sum, the court ruled that the 8th COA for negligence asserted by the Minor Plaintiffs is both time-barred because they (1) filed claims based upon their own claims more than 6 months after rejection of their Government Tort Claims and/or (2) the statute of limitations (SOL) expired because the negligence COA was asserted more than two years after the alleged negligence.  

 

Now, though the court granted leave to amend, the 8th COA does not assert new facts. Rather, as noted by the County, Minor Plaintiffs have instead asserted legal arguments in their 6AC as to why the 8th COA is timely.

 

Under section 911.2 of the Government Claims Act, before filing a lawsuit against a public entity, a plaintiff must present the claim to the public entity within six months of the date the claim accrues. (Gov. Code, § 911.2, subd. (a); see also Shirk v. Vista (2007) 42 Cal.4th 201, 208-209.) Generally, the claim for negligence accrues during the last act of abuse. (Id. at p. 210.) This is notably so as Minor Plaintiffs’ negligence COA is based upon the “physical and mental pain and suffering.” (6AC p. 38.)

 

Here, the last possible date that the Minor Plaintiffs were allegedly abused would have been the date that they were removed from their parents’ care. Plaintiffs, however, have not provided this date. Plaintiffs merely allege that Eva briefly got custody of the children sometime in 2022. (¶175(d)(3)). But when were the children removed from their home? Was it sometime after Noah died? Or was it sometime when the parents were indicted? This date of the last act of abuse is imperative as the lawsuit must have been filed six months from that date.

 

To the extent that Plaintiffs argue that equitable tolling applies, equitable tolling does not save the sixth months claims presentation requirement. (See Willis v. City of Carlsbad (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 1104, 1121.) This is because equitable tolling is a judicially created doctrine applicable to the SOL, but the SOL is not the equivalent to the claims presentation requirement. In fact, the latter is an element of the plaintiff’s cause of action. (See Shirk, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 209.) With that, while a SOL defect must clearly appear on the face of the complaint, here, the claims presentation requirement, as element of the COA, must be sufficiently pled. Plaintiffs, however, have not sufficiently pled the element.

 

Tolling Until Minor Plaintiffs Turn 18

 

Plaintiffs allege and argue that the lawsuit is tolled until the minor Plaintiffs turn 18. However, as noted in demurrer and not discussed in opposition, subdivision (b) of section 352 provides that section 352 “shall not apply to an action against a public entity or public employee upon a cause of action for which a claim is required to be presented in accordance with” the Government Claims Act.” The statute’s tolling provision therefore does not apply in this suit against the County and its employees. (See County of Los Angeles v. Super. Ct. (2005) 127 Cal. App. 4th 1263, 1270.) (Demurrer p. 18.)

 

Therefore, tolling does not apply.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the demurrer is OVERRULED in part (i.e., as to 2nd COA) and SUSTAINED in part (i.e., as to 8th COA). Though based on the facts offered by the 6AC the negligence COA appears timed barred as Plaintiffs did not comply the claims presentation requirement, the court asks for supplemental briefing (rather than leave to amend), to present sufficient facts as to the issue.

 

Motion to Strike[9]

 

A.    Re: “General Allegations” in Paragraphs 89-95 Relating Solely to minor Plaintiffs’ 8th COA Must be Stricken as Irrelevant

 

The County avers that without the Minor Plaintiffs’ negligence COA, allegations about the Minor Plaintiffs’ alleged claims of abuse are unrelated to the lawsuit (i.e., immaterial), and thus may be stricken.[10]

 

This is TBD pending supplemental briefing, but likely granted should the court sustain the 8th COA without leave to amend.

 

B.    Re: Eva Hernandez’ Allegations in Support of Her 2nd COA Are Irrelevant and Improper

 

The County seeks to strike certain allegations in the 2nd COA because no mandatory duty existed.

 

However, based on the court’s determination, a mandatory duty has sufficiently been alleged. Therefore, this request is DENIED as to Paragraphs 103-09.

 

As for paragraph 110, which alleges a special relationship, the court did not reach the issue on demurrer. That said, as a matter of law, a special relationship would not exist between County and Eva.

 

As explained by the County, the County itself cannot be held to be in a special relationship with a member of the public that requires the County to warn or protect that person. (Demurrer pp. 11-12, citing Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal. 4th 1112, 1130–1131. A public entity does not “become liable to each individual for all foreseeable harm.” (Id. at 1131.) Rather, a county can be held liable through respondent superior for an employee’s breach of duty. (Ibid.)

 

Here, however, the allegations regarding special relationship do not appear to be based upon the relationship between a County employee and Eva but rather the County and Eva. (See Complaint ¶110.) To the extent that Plaintiffs in opposition aver Zelig is inapposite (Opp. p. 9:23-27), the court is admittedly not entirely certain as to Plaintiffs’ explanation. If anything, the explanation evidences lack of a special relationship as Plaintiffs maintain the relationship is between the County and Eva, not an employee and Eva. Though the court previously granted leave to amend the facts for clarity, the court once again provides it.

 

Therefore, the requested is GRANTED as to allegations regarding a special relationship, with leave to amend.

 

C.    Re: Allegations Relating to the Conduct of Noah’s Parents After His Death (Paragraphs 120-125)[11] Must be Stricken in their Entirety as They Are Irrelevant to the Present Action.

 

The County avers these statements are wholly unrelated and improper, serving no purpose but to add inflammatory, misconstrued, irrelevant allegations.

 

In Opposition, Plaintiffs explain that these allegations “lay out ongoing abuses committed against minor plaintiffs that County social workers failed to discover through the years of alleged investigations conducted by County employees.” (Opp. p. 7.)

 

Here, though the court understands Plaintiffs’ position in that the facts demonstrate the emotional impact on Eva (“facts demonstrate the consequences of County’s breaches to all plaintiffs” (Opp. p. 9)), the consequences are immaterial for purposes of the negligence COA. As noted by the County in reply, the only facts relevant to Noah’s death are whether the County’s purported breach(es) caused Noah’s death. As the facts regarding Noah’s July 5, 2019 death do not pertain to a duty, a breach, causation, and damages, the facts are irrelevant. An irrelevant allegation is one that is “not essential to the statement of claim or defense” or pertinent to a claim or defense. (MTS, p. 6, citing Code of Civ. Proc., section 431.10(b), (c).)  Pursuant to CCP section 435(a), the court may strike irrelevant language in a pleading.

 

Therefore, as the paragraphs are irrelevant, the court GRANTS the MTS as to Paragraphs 120-125, with leave to amend.

 

D.    Re: Paragraph 175(d) and its Numerous Subparts Contain Improper Legal Arguments That Must be Stricken If the Eighth Cause of Action Survives County’s Concurrently Filed Demurrer

 

TBD pending supplemental briefing, but likely granted without leave to amend should the court sustain the 8th COA.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the MTS is granted in part and denied in part and TBD in part.

Tentative Ruling

 

(1)   UBER TECHNOLOGIES, INC. AND JIANG XU’S NOTICE OF MOTION AND MOTION TO SEAL UNREDACTED MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT AND SUPPORTING DOCUMENTS is GRANTED.

 

(2)   UBER TECHNOLOGIES, INC.’S AND JIANG XU’S JOINT MOTION FOR DETERMINATION OF GOOD FAITH SETTLEMENT is GRANTED.

                                                                                                          

Background

 

This case arises from a motor vehicle accident.

 

On January 5, 2023, Plaintiff FRANCISCO ROMERO filed suit against Defendants JIANG XU, an individual; UBER TECHNOLOGIES INC., a Corporation; ANTONIO PEREZ, an individual for negligence. (Plaintiff was the passenger in an Uber vehicle operated by Defendant Xu when Defendant Xu’s and Perez’s vehicles collided.)

 

On February 22, 2023, Uber filed its answer.

 

On March 14, 2023, Defendant Perez filed his answer and a cross-complaint against Xu, Romero, and Uber for indemnity (total and implied), declaratory relief, and apportionment of fault. That same day, Xu filed his answer.

 

On September 21, 2023, Uber and Xu (collectively, “Uber Defendants”) filed the instant motions.

 

On November 7, 2023, Uber Defendants filed a reply to both, indicating that no oppositions (due 11/1) were received by either Plaintiff Romero or Defendant Perez.[1]

 

 

Legal Standard for Motion to Seal

 

A party that requests that a record or portion of a record be filed under seal must file a motion or an application for an order sealing it, which must be accompanied by a supporting memorandum and a declaration containing facts sufficient to justify the sealing. (Cal. Rules of Ct., Rule 2.551(b)(1).) A copy of the motion or application to seal must be served on all parties that have appeared in the case. Unless the judge orders otherwise, any party that already has access to the records to be placed under seal must be served with a complete, unredacted copy of all papers as well as a redacted version. (Id., subd. (b)(2).) Other parties must be served with only the public redacted version. The moving party must lodge the record with the court when the motion or application is made, unless good cause is shown for not lodging it or it has been lodged previously. The lodged record is conditionally under seal pending the judge's determination of the motion or application. (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.551(b)(4).) 

 

Here, the Settlement Agreement entered by the Settling Parties contains a confidentiality clause restricting the disclosure of information, including, but not limited to, the terms of the agreement, settlement amount, and parties. (See Motion p. 6, citing Hinshaw, Winkler, Draa, Marsh & Still v. Superior Court (1986) 51 Cal.App.4th 233, 241 [confidential settlement agreements are afforded the same privacy protection as a bank account or tax information].) The purpose is to protect the vested interest of the settling parties in keeping their financial information private. (See Motion p. 7, citing Carmel-bv-the-Sea v. Young (1970) 2 Cal.3d 259, 268 [Financial privacy concerns can be overriding interests that overcome a right of public access.].) More specifically as to Uber, it regularly keeps the terms and amounts they agree to pay in settlement confidential in order to protect themselves in future settlement discussions with other parties during pre-litigation and litigation processes. Otherwise, public disclosure of the terms of the Settlement Agreement could prejudice the Uber Defendants’ ability to negotiate specific provisions in other future settlement agreements.

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the court grants Uber Defendants’ unopposed motion. 

 

Legal Standard for Good Faith Settlement (GFS)

 

Under section 877.6 of the California Code of Civil Procedure,[2] “[a] determination by the court that [a] settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor . . . from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor . . . for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (c).) “The party asserting the lack of good faith has the burden of proof on that issue.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (d).) 

 

Section 877.6 requires “that the courts review [settlement] agreements made under its aegis to insure that the settlements appropriately balance the . . . statute’s dual objectives” (i.e., providing an “equitable sharing of costs among the parties at fault” and encouraging parties to resolve their disputes by way of settlement). (Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 494 (Tech-Bilt).)

 

In Tech-Bilt, the California Supreme Court set forth the factors to consider when determining whether a settlement was made in good faith. The Tech-Bilt factors are:

 

(1) A rough approximation of plaintiff’s total recovery and the settlor’s proportionate liability;

(2) The amount paid in settlement;

(3) The allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs;

(4) A recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial;

(5) The financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants; and

(6) The existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of the non-settling defendants.

 

(Tech-Bilt, supra, 38 Cal.3d at pp. 498-501.)

 

Discussion

 

Mindful that the financial terms of the agreement are confidential, the court will provide a vague analysis.

 

In cases involving multiple tortfeasors, the common-law principle that each tortfeasor is personally liable for any indivisible injury of which is or her negligence is a proximate cause has commonly been expressed terms of “joint and several liability,” which is central to California negligence law. Under joint and several liability, each tortfeasor whose negligence is a proximate cause of an indivisible injury remains individually liable for all compensable damages attributable to that injury. (Aidan Ming-Ho Leung v. Verdugo Hills Hosp. (2012) 55 Cal.4th 291.) Whether individuals are "joint tortfeasors” depends upon whether they caused one indivisible injury or the same wrong; the "same wrong" may emanate from two successive independent torts and does not require unity of purpose, action, or intent by the two or more tortfeasors. (In re JTS Corp. (2010) 617 F.3d 1102, 1116-1117. Put simply, a plaintiff need only claim that the tortfeasors caused the same harm. (Id. at p. 1117, citing Lafayette v. County of Los Angeles (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 547, 555-556.)

 

As for economic damages (e.g., medical expenses, loss of earnings, loss of use of property, etc.), liability is held by each defendant, provided that the plaintiff recovers only once for the full amount. (See Restatement (Second) of Torts § 875 (1977).) On the other hand, for noneconomic damages, Section 1431.2 provides that the liability of each defendant “shall be several only and shall not be joint. Each defendant shall be liable only for the amount of non-economic damages allocated to that defendant in direct proportion to that defendant’s percentage of fault, and a separate judgment shall be rendered against the defendant for that amount.” (Motion p. 5, quoting Cal. Code of Civ. Proc., §1431.2.)

 

Here, though only portion of the Uber Defendant’s settlement proceeds will be attributed to non-economic damages, the Defendants’ settlement with Plaintiff more than accounts for their several obligation to Plaintiff’s non-economic damages since the settlement covers more than two times Plaintiff’s past economic damages. (Motion pp. 5-6.) Thus, though the purpose of GFS motions is to encourage settlements—which may entail a settling defendant to pay less than his proportionate share of the anticipated damages (Abbott Ford, Inc. v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 858, 874-875)—here, Uber Defendants’ payment of more than its share of economic damages suggests that the settlement was made in good faith.

 

To the extent that the settlement is so far “out of the ballpark,” Defendant Perez has not filed an opposition. (City of Grand Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261 [“[C]onclude that only when the good faith nature of a settlement is disputed, it is incumbent upon the trial court to consider and weigh the Tech-Bilt factors. That is to say, when no one objects, the barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient.”].)

 

With that, as a GFS motion does not require perfect or even nearly perfect apportionment of liability but rather that the settlement not be grossly disproportionate to the settlor’s fair share and moving Defendants have made that showing, the court grants the motion. (Motion p. 4, citing Abbott Ford, Inc., supra, 43 Cal.3d at pp. 874-875). 

 

 

 

 

Conclusion

 

Based on the foregoing, the GFS between Plaintiff and Uber Defendants is granted.
