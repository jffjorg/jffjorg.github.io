DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 21STCV44256    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

TIANMU INVESTMENT HONG KONG LIMITED, a Hong Kong Limited Liability Company,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

MAR CANTON, an individual; COURTNEY SOLOMON, an individual; CINELOU HOLDINGS, LLC, a California Limited Liability Company; ATMOSPHERE ENTERTAINMENT MM, INC., a California corporation; and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          21STCV44256

 Hearing Date:   September 20, 2024

 Trial Date:        October 22, 2024

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant Mark Canton’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or Alternatively, Summary Adjudication [RES ID # 5348]

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Tianmu Investment Hong Kong Limited (Tianmu) sues Defendants Mark Canton (Canton), Courtney Solomon (Solomon), Cinelou Holdings LLC (Cinelou), Atmosphere Entertainment MM, Inc., and Does 1 through 50 (collectively, Defendants) pursuant to a December 3, 2021 Complaint, asserting causes of action for: (1) Breach of Agreement (Robotech Agreement); (2) Breach of Agreement (DI Agreement); (3) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (4) Negligent Misrepresentation; (5) Fraud; (6) Violations of California Corporations Code §§ 25400, 25401, et seq.; and (7) Violation of California Corporations Code § 25504, et seq.

This action involves a contractual agreement (Robotech Agreement) in which Tianmu agreed to invest $6,700,000 in a film Defendants were producing titled “Robotech.” (Compl., ¶¶ 15-17.) The Robotech Agreement provided that Plaintiff would be paid a $500,000 producer fee, a $6,200,000 recoupment fee, plus one-third of the gross proceeds of Robotech payable to Defendant Canton subject to a 1% cap. (Compl. ¶18.) Despite multiple representations by Defendants that Robotech was moving forward and set to be released in 2018, Robotech and other film projects were never produced. (Compl. ¶¶ 19-33.)

On July 4, 2017, Tianmu and Defendant Cinelou entered into the Debt Investment Agreement (DI Agreement) in which Tianmu loaned Defendants $5,000,000 in connection with three of Defendants other film projects. (Compl., ¶43.) The DI Agreement stated that Tianmu would be repaid the loan amount, plus 17.5% interest, within twelve months after the Defendants received the loan. (Compl. ¶44.) The Defendants did not produce any motion picture projects under the DI Agreement and failed to repay the loan amount. (Compl. ¶¶ 45-46.)

B. Relevant Procedural History

On August 25, 2023, Default was entered against Defendant Atmosphere Entertainment MM, Inc.

On April 26, 2024, Defendants Solomon and Cinelou moved for summary judgment, or in the alternative, for summary adjudication.

On July 15, 2024, the Court granted in part and denied in part the motion. The Court granted Summary Adjudication to both Solomon and Cinelou as to the Breach of the Robotech Agreement. The Court granted Summary Adjudication to Solomon as to the Breach of the DI Agreement and Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing. The Court denied Summary Adjudication to Cinelou as to the Breach of the DI Agreement and Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing. B. Legal Standard

A motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact for trial or that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. (Code of Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

A party may also seek summary adjudication of select causes of action, affirmative defenses, claims for damages, or issues of duty, which may be made by a standalone motion or as an alternative to a motion for summary judgment and proceeds in all procedural respects like a motion for summary judgment, but which must completely dispose of the challenged cause of action, affirmative defense, claim for damages, or issue of duty. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subds. (f)(1)-(2), (t).)

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that no triable issue of material fact exists. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) This burden on summary judgment or adjudication “is more properly one of persuasion rather than proof, since he must persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find, and not to prove any such fact to the satisfaction of the court itself as though it were sitting as the trier of fact.” (Id. at p. 850, fn. 11.)

If the moving party meets this burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to make a rebuttal prima facie showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. (Id. at p. 849.) “[I]n ruling on motions for summary judgment courts are to ‘“liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.”’ [Citations].” (Cheal v. El Camino Hospital (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 736, 760.)

C. Analysis

1. First Cause of Action, Breach of Robotech Agreement

Establishing a claim for breach of contract requires a showing of “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.) “Further, the complaint must indicate on its face whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct.” (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458–459.)

It is undisputed that Canton is not a party to the Robotech Agreement. (Canton Decl. ¶ 2; Fowler Decl. Ex. A; UMF No. 4.) Rather, Tianmu argues that Canton is an alter ego of Cinelou. (Opp. 18.) To support this argument, Tianmu states that Canton was “located at the same address as the other Defendants at the time of the Robotech Agreement.” (Opp. p. 18.) This is insufficient to show a finding of alter ego. (Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538.)

Thus, Tianmu has not met its burden of proof to establish that a genuine issue of material fact exists as to the breach of contract claims.

However, in an excess of caution, the Court will also address the substance of the breach of contract, Robotech Agreement, claim.

On summary judgment, Canton argues that this claim fails because (1) no payment to Tianmu is due under the Robotech Agreement, (2) any alleged promises contrary to the written terms are inadmissible parole evidence, and (3) Tianmu cannot establish liability against Canton for the contract claim under an alter ago theory. To support these arguments, Canton presents the Declarations of John D. Fowler and Mark Canton, the Robotech Agreement, and Tianmu’s Responses to Cinelou’s Request for Admission (RFA) and Special Interrogatories. The Canton Declaration states at paragraph 2 that “[T]o date, production of Robotech has not commenced. No fees or proceeds from Robotech have been paid to myself or to Atmosphere.” (Canton Decl. ¶ 2:10-11; UMF Nos. 2-3.) The Fowler Declaration attached a copy of the Robotech Agreement as Exhibit A, which states at paragraph 5.2, “Purchaser hereby represents and warrants that neither [Cinelou] nor any representative or member of [Cinelou] has made any representation or warranty with respect to the profitability or success of the Picture or Subsequent Production (as applicable), including without limitation […] (B) no assurance that the Picture or a Subsequent Production (as applicable) will be produced or completed.” (Fowler Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A; UMF No. 5.)

In addition, paragraph 9 of the Robotech Agreement states, “This Agreement and the other documents delivered pursuant to the terms hereof set forth all of the promises, covenants, agreements, conditions and understandings among the parties hereto with respect to the subject matter hereof and supersede all prior and contemporaneous agreements and understandings, inducements or conditions, expressed or implied, oral or written, with respect thereto. [Tianmu] acknowledges that no representation or promise not expressly contained in this Agreement bas been made by [Cinelou] or any of its agents, employees or representatives.” (Fowler Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A; UMF No. 6.) Furthermore, the Fowler Declaration attaches a copy of Tianmu’s RFA. (Fowler Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. D) In response to RFA No. 2: “Admit that Robotech has not been produced,” Tianmu states “Admitted.” (Fowler Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. D, p. 3:8-11.) In response to RFA No. 3: “Admit that no income has been generated from Robotech,” Tianmu states “Plaintiff is without sufficient knowledge to deny or admit.” (Fowler Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. D, p. 3:13-16.)

In response, Tianmu argues that Canton breached the Tianmu Agreement by not producing the film, not paying Tianmu the producer fee, not paying Tianmu the recoupment fee, and not paying any share of the defined gross proceeds of the film payable. (Opp. p. 10.) Tianmu argues that the Defendants made frequent promises that the film would be produced, told Tianmu that progress regarding the production was underway, and repeatedly told Tianmu that the film was moving forward. (Opp. p. 10.)

To support these arguments, Tianmu presents the Declaration of Jun Fang, the director of Tianmu Investment Hong Kong Limited. In paragraph 16, the Fang Declaration states: “Canton represented to Tianmu that ‘we are making progress. The latest script is in and Sony feels good and excited. We are waiting for the Director to finish editing his next movie in 4 weeks and we will all meet and hopefully move forward at last. Going to be great.’” (Fang Dec. ¶ 16.) The Fang Declaration states in paragraph 9 that: “Defendant Canton repeatedly told Plaintiff that essential progress regarding the production were underway. Plaintiff relied on these statements. In email communications after the signing of the agreement, Canton repeatedly told Plaintiff that the Picture was moving forward, but in fact did not actually fulfill the agreement and did not explain the state of the development of the project.” (Fang Dec. ¶ 9.)

However, Tianmu presents no evidence showing that Canton had a duty to complete Robotech, where the Robotech Agreement contains express language stating that Cinelou makes no assurances that there ever will be any economic interest payable to Tianmu and that Cinelou makes no assurances that the film will be produced or completed. Further, Tianmu presents no evidence to refute Canton’s contention that the Robotech Agreement was completely integrated such that any other agreements, understandings, inducements, or conditions are barred under the parol evidence rule.

Thus, based on the evidence presented, there is no triable issue of material fact as to the breach of contract, Robotech Agreement, claim.

Accordingly, summary adjudication is GRANTED as to the first cause of action

2. Second Cause of Action, Breach of DI Agreement

Similarly, it is undisputed that Canton is not a party to the DI Agreement. (Canton Decl. ¶ 3; Fowler Decl. Ex. B; UMF No. 11.). As discussed above, Tianmu has not presented evidence to support a finding of alter ego.

Accordingly, summary adjudication is GRANTED as to the second cause of action.  

3. Third Cause of Action, Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

Because Canton is not a party to either contract, the third contractual cause of action fails as well.

Accordingly, summary adjudication is GRANTED as to the third cause of action. 

4. Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action, Negligent Misrepresentation and Fraud

“The elements of negligent misrepresentation are ‘(1) the misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable ground for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another's reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation, and (5) resulting damage.’” (National Union Fire Ins. Co. of Pittsburgh, PA v. Cambridge Integrated Services Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 35, 50.)

“The elements of intentional misrepresentation ‘are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) knowledge of falsity, (3) intent to induce reliance, (4) actual and justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.’” (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1245.)

On summary judgment, Canton argues that these claims fail because (1) Tianmu did not pay Cinelou the $5 million investment under the DI Agreement, so there can be no actual reliance or damages, (2) Tianmu does not specify any false statements or promises that were made to him prior to execution of the Robotech Agreement, and (3) Tianmu cannot prove that any reliance was reasonable. (Mot. p. 11.) To support these arguments, Canton argues that Tianmu is a “sophisticated institutional investor who invests in similar projects” and that “[t]he Robotech Agreement expressly disclaims any guarantees of Robotech’s profitability, production or release date.” (Mot. p. 11; UMF No. 5) Based on the evidence presented and legal arguments raised, Canton has met his initial burden of proof because a jury could find that there are no triable issue of fact regarding whether Canton made a negligent or intentional misrepresentation to Tianmu that Tianmu justifiably relied on.

The burden thus shifts to Tianmu to make a rebuttal prima facie showing that a jury could find triable issues of material fact. Tianmu argues that in November 2015, prior to signing the Robotech Agreement, Canton represented to Tianmu that Tianmu “was guaranteed to recoup its investment plus a guaranteed profit” if it invested in Robotech. (Fang Decl. ¶ 4.) Tianmu also presents evidence that Dennis Pelino, an agent of Canton and Cinelou, sent an email representing that the film was projected to “generate an expected 500 million USD global box office.” (Fang Decl. ¶ 5, Ex. B.) The evidence does not show any alleged misrepresentations by Canton regarding the DI Agreement (See Fang Decl. ¶¶ 19-27, Exs. L-Q). However, based on the evidence, and construing the evidence liberally in favor of the opposing party, there is a triable issue of fact whether Canton misrepresented the likelihood of Robotech’s success and whether Tianmu reasonably relied on the alleged misrepresentation.

Accordingly, summary adjudication is DENIED as to the fourth and fifth cause of action. 

5. Sixth and Seventh Causes of Actions for Violations of Corporations Code Sections §§ 25400 and 25401, et. seq., and 25504 et. seq.

Pursuant to Corporations Code Section 25400, subdivision (d), it is unlawful for “a broker-dealer or other person selling or offering for sale or purchasing or offering to purchase the security, to make, for the purpose of inducing the purchase or sale of such security by others, any statement which was, at the time and in the light of the circumstances under which it was made, false or misleading with respect to any material fact, or which omitted to state any material fact necessary in order to make the statements made, in the light of the circumstances under which they were made, not misleading, and which he knew or had reasonable ground to believe was so false or misleading.” (Corp. Code, § 25400(d).)

            Pursuant to Corporations Code Section 25401, “[i]t is unlawful for any person to offer or sell a security in this state, or to buy or offer to buy a security in this state, by means of any written or oral communication that includes an untrue statement of a material fact or omits to state a material fact necessary to make the statements made, in the light of the circumstances under which the statements were made, not misleading.” (Corp. Code, § 25401.)

            Canton does not make any arguments in his motion or reply discussing the sixth and seventh causes of action for violations of the Corporations Code.  Thus, Canton has not met his initial burden to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact.

Accordingly, summary adjudication is DENIED as to the sixth and seventh cause of action.

III. Demurrer

A.    Meet and Confer

B.    Procedural Deficiencies

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1110 requires that “[a] notice of motion must state in the opening paragraph the nature of the order being sought and the grounds for issuance of the order.”

The Melrose Defendant’s Notice of Demurrer requests an order sustaining the Demurrer to the entire FAC, the third cause of action for statutory remedies per Penal Code § 496 and the sixth cause of action for conversion. The Melrose Defendant’s request for an order sustaining the cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty only appears in their memorandum of points and authorities. This violates Rules of Court rule 3.1110. The party against whom an order may be issued is entitled to adequate notice

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1112 requires that “the papers filed in support of a motion must consist of at least the following: (1) A notice of hearing on the motion; (2) The motion itself; and (3) A memorandum in support of the motion…”

Howard’s combined opposition and request for leave to amend to add an additional cause of action should have been submitted separately. The combined opposition and leave to amend is not sufficient to provide Defendants with adequate notice. This not only violates the requirement in California Rules of Court, Rules 3.1112, but basic due process.

C.    Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) This device can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a [general] demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.)

A demurrer for uncertainty tests whether the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10, subd. (f).) 

“A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616, disapproved on other grounds in Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guar. Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 46 [holding claims for unfair business practices need not be pled specifically, impliedly disapproving Khoury].) As a result, a special demurrer for uncertainty is not intended to reach failure to incorporate sufficient facts in the pleading but is directed only at uncertainty existing in the allegations already made. (People v. Taliaferro (1957) 149 Cal.App.2d 822, 825, disapproved on other grounds in Jefferson v. J.E. French Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 717, 719-720 [statute of limitations question].) 

D.    Analysis

1.     Demurrer to the Entire FAC, Failure to Join Indispensable Party

The Melrose Defendants demur to the entire FAC on the grounds that Howard failed to join indispensable parties, and that the entire FAC is vague and uncertain.

Code of Civil Procedure § 389 subdivision (a) defines the person who should be joined if possible, known as a “necessary party.” A party is necessary if (1) in his absence complete relief cannot be accorded among those already parties or (2) he claims an interest relating to the subject of the action and is so situated that the disposition of the action in his absence may (i) as a practical matter impair or impede his ability to protect that interest or (ii) leave any of the person already parties subject to a substantial risk of incurring double, multiple, or otherwise inconsistent obligations by reason of his claimed interest.”

A party found “necessary” pursuant to subdivision (a) may then be found “indispensable” pursuant to subdivision (b).  (County of Imperial v. Superior Court, (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 13, 26.)  A determination that a party is necessary is the predicate for a determination of whether the party is indispensable. (Deltakeeper v. Oakdale Irrigation Dist., (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 1092, 1100.)

Code of Civil Procedure § 389 subdivision (b) provides: “If a person as described in paragraph (1) or (2) of subdivision (a) cannot be made a party, the court shall determine whether in equity and good conscience the action should proceed among the parties before it, or should be dismissed without prejudice, the absent person being thus regarded as indispensable.  The factors to be considered by the court include: (1) to what extent a judgment rendered in the person’s absence might be prejudicial to him or those already parties; (2) the extent to which, by protective provisions in the judgment, by the shaping of relief, or other measures, the prejudice can be lessened or avoided; (3) whether a judgment rendered in the person’s absence will be adequate; (4) whether the plaintiff or cross-complainant will have an adequate remedy if the action is dismissed for nonjoinder.”

A necessary party is regarded as indispensable if the court determines, in equity and good conscience, that the action must be dismissed in the party’s absence in light of, inter alia, whether a judgment rendered in the party’s absence will be adequate.  TG Oceanside, L.P. v. City of Oceanside, (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 1355, 1365-66.  The controlling test for whether a necessary party is indispensable is whether “the plaintiff seeks some type of affirmative relief which, if granted, would injure or affect the interest of a third person not joined.  Save Our Bay v. San Diego United Port District, (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 686, 692.  In other words, a third party is indispensable if his or her rights must necessarily be affected by the judgment. Each of the four factors in subdivision (b) must be considered, but “no factor is determinative or necessarily more persuasive than another.”  (County of Imperial, supra, 152 Cal.App. 4th at 35.)

A failure to join an indispensable party is not a jurisdictional defect in the fundamental sense of jurisdiction.  The court has the power to render a decision as to the parties before it in the absence of an indispensable party.  It is for reasons of equity and convenience only that a court will not proceed with a case where it determines that an indispensable party is absent and cannot be joined. (Save Our Bay, supra, 42 Cal.App.4th at 693.)

The Melrose Defendants argue that Brian Witzer and the Law Offices of Brian Witzer (the Witzer Parties) are indispensable parties to the action and that neither Howard, nor the Melrose Defendants, can obtain complete relief without the addition of the Witzer Parties. (Memo. p. 5.) Otherwise, the Melrose Defendants state that they are potentially subject to incurring multiple or inconsistent obligations. (Memo p. 5.) The FAC states that in early 2020, Melrose became Witzer’s partner and joint-venturer. (FAC ¶ 22) Howard states that whenever he “would interact with Witzer, he would also interact with Melrose, and vice-versa” and that “Melrose and Witzer would refer to each other as partners…” (FAC ¶¶ 24(b)-(c).) In July 2020, Witzer, “aided and abetted by Melrose,” [… ] “intentionally misdirected and misappropriated at least ‘six figures’ of Howard’s funds…” (FAC ¶ 39.) The FAC alleges that Melrose, Witzer, and the other Defendants “lie[d] to Howard and engage[d] in malfeasance as to the Underlying Lawsuit; and continued to aid and abet and conspire with [the Witzer Parties] to defraud, breach fiduciary duty and otherwise damage Plaintiff.” (FAC ¶ 45.)

Here, throughout the FAC the actions of the Witzer Parties and the Melrose Defendants are inextricably intertwined. In the ‘Damages’ section of the FAC, virtually every allegation charges “Witzer and Melrose.” For example, paragraph 81(d): “During the course of the claim, Witzer and Melrose …. instructed Plaintiff not to work…”, paragraph 81 (e): “During the course of the claim, Witzer and Melrose … insisted that Plaintiff obtain exceedingly expensive litigation funding”, paragraph 81(f): “During the course of the claim, Witzer and Melrose … failed to alert Plaintiff to the fact that he had a significant malpractice claim…”, paragraph 81(h): “During the course of the claim, Witzer, Melrose…intentionally discouraged Howard from using available healthcare insurance…” and so forth.

In addition, the FAC mixes the actions of the Witzer parties and the Melrose Defendants in describing the scope of the causes of action. For example, the scope of the first cause of action for fraud “addresses acts and omissions occurring while Howard was being ‘represented’ under his contract with [the Witzer Parties], and then under his contract with [the Melrose Defendants.] (FAC ¶ 85.) The FAC alleges various acts of malfeasance causing Howard’s damages that span from 2017 to 2024, however Witzer did not engage Melrose until 2020. (FAC ¶ 80, 22).

The Witzer Parties are necessary parties because the FAC alleges that there is a lack of independence between the Witzer Parties and the Melrose Defendants. While the Witzer Parties are not directly relevant to determining whether the FAC alleges liability against the Melrose Defendants for their own acts of malfeasance, it would be inequitable to hold the Melrose Defendants responsible for the actions of the Witzer Parties, especially for conduct that occurred during the three years before Melrose partnered with Witzer. Thus, the FAC fails to join a necessary party.

Accordingly, for that reason alone, the Demurrer to the FAC must be SUSTAINED. It is noted that Plaintiff states his intention to add Witzer to the complaint, at least to the extent there are any insurance proceeds. (Opp. at p. 10.)

2.     Demurrer to the Entire FAC, Vague and Uncertain

For many of the same reasons, the FAC is vague and uncertain. The FAC fails to provide the Melrose Defendants with adequate clarity regarding the specific allegations made against them. In opposition, Howard points to numerous portions in the FAC referencing allegations made against the Melrose Defendants. However, the uncertainty stems from the lack of clarity in how the allegations against them distinguish their liability from that of the Witzer Parties, and the other Defendants. This issue is particularly pronounced given that many of the accusations of malfeasance and resulting damages pertain to the period before Witzer and Melrose became partners. Thus, the accusations against the Melrose Defendants are not sufficiently certain as to give the Melrose Defendants adequate notice of the allegations against them, specifically.

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the FAC are SUSTAINED on the grounds of uncertainty as well.  

            Since the Court has sustained the Demurrer to the entire FAC due to failure to join a necessary party and on uncertainty grounds, the Court need not analyze Defendant’s arguments for demurrer to specific causes of action. Upon amendment, plaintiff should keep in mind the requirement that, unless relating to fraud or another heightened standard of pleading, the complaint must contain not conclusions of law, but rather ultimate (though not evidentiary) facts supporting each claim as to each defendant. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.)

IV. Motion to Strike

A.    Meet and Confer

Defendants have met the motion to strike meet and confer requirement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 435.5 subdivision (a)(3)(B). (Mot. to Strike, ¶ 2.)

B.    Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (a) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (b) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subds. (a), (b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782 [“Matter in a pleading which is not essential to the claim is surplusage; probative facts are surplusage and may be stricken out or disregarded”].)

For the purposes of a motion to strike pursuant to Sections 435 to 437 of the Code of Civil Procedure, the term “pleading” generally means a demurrer, answer, complaint, or cross-complaint, (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (a)), and an immaterial allegation or irrelevant matter in a pleading entails (1) an allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense, (2) an allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense, or (3) a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subds. (b)(1)-(3), (c)).

C.    Analysis

The Melrose Defendants move to strike the following from the FAC: Page 67, lines 18-19, Prayer No. 2: “As to all Defendants except for Lee, for treble damages and attorney fees per Penal Code section 496.”

Defendants argue that the FAC does not plead sufficient facts to establish that Melrose knowingly gave substantial assistance to someone who performed wrongful conduct, and thus the third cause of action for statutory remedies under Penal Code section 496 cannot be stated; thus, the prayer for relief cannot support this relief.

Because the demurrer has been sustained, the motion to strike is MOOT.

V. Conclusion

Defendants Kenneth Melrose dba Law Office of Ken J. Melrose and Law Office of Kenneth J. Melrose, PC’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend.

 

Defendants Kenneth Melrose dba Law Office of Ken J. Melrose and Law Office of Kenneth J. Melrose, PC’s Motion to Strike is DENIED as moot.
