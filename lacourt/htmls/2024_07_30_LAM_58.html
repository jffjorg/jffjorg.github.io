DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV39168    Hearing Date: July 30, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             July 30, 2024

Case Name:                 Neman et al. v. Bahari et al.

Case No.:                    22STCV39168

Motion:                       Special Motion to Strike (Anti-SLAPP)

Moving Party:             Cross-Defendants Morad Ben Neman and Lancaster Eagle, LLC

Responding Party:      Cross-Complainant Caster Modelo, LLC

 

Tentative Ruling:      The special motion to strike is denied.

 

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs Morad Ben Neman and Lancaster Eagle, LLC sued defendants Payam Bahari, Freydoon “Fred” Bahari Moghadam, Haim Bahari, Tal Bahari, 114 Grand Cypress, LLC, Cavalini, Inc., and Caster Modelo, LLC, on December 16, 2022, and filed their operative second amended complaint (“SAC”) on February 9, 2024. The SAC asserts causes of action for breach of a joint venture agreement, breach of fiduciary duty, aiding and abetting the same, and fraudulent conveyances. Plaintiffs allege they entered a joint real estate investment venture with Defendants, then Defendants exploited the venture’s investment leads and directly invested for their own gain in properties discovered by Plaintiffs for the venture.

 

Defendant Caster Modelo, LLC (“Modelo”) cross-complained against Plaintiffs and against Vivoli Saccuzo, LLP on April 19, 2024, and filed its operative first amended cross-complaint (“FAXC”) on May 3, 2024. The FAXC asserts causes of action for an accounting, breach of contract, conversion, declaratory relief, and receipt of stolen property. Modelo alleges that it and Plaintiff/Cross-defendant Lancaster Eagle entered a written tenancy-in-common agreement (“TIC”) related to a property located in Lancaster (“Property”). The parties expressly disclaimed any joint venture arrangement in the TIC. When the parties sought to sell the Property, Lancaster Eagle first threatened to scuttle the deal unless it received more than 50% compensation from the sale, then it stole collectively-owned funds from the TIC.

 

On June 5, 2024, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants specially moved to strike Modelo’s second cause of action for breach of contract, third cause of action for conversion, and fifth cause of action for receipt of stolen property, under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, California’s “anti-SLAPP” law. On July 17, 2024, Modelo filed its opposition. On July 23, 2024, Plaintiffs replied.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 permits a defendant to specially move to strike a cause of action that arises from certain conduct privileged under the law. The Legislature recognizes these sorts of actions, termed strategic lawsuits against public participation (“SLAPPs”), as a tactic in which a litigant may impose litigation costs on a party for exercising fundamental First Amendment rights. Thus, under section 425.16, a party may move to strike a cause of action that arises from any act of that person in furtherance of the person's right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue, and may recover her fees and costs for doing so.   

 

Section 425.16 posits a two-step process for determining whether an action is a strategic lawsuit against public participation. Courts first decide whether moving parties have made a prima facie showing that the attacked claims arise from a protected activity, including defendants’ right of petition or free speech.  (See e.g. Healy v. Tuscany Hills Landscape & Recreation Corp., (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 1, 5; Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 278; Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16(e).) Moving parties have the initial burden to demonstrate that a cause of action is subject to a special motion to strike.  (Martinez v. Metabolife Inter. Ins. (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 186; Fox Searchlight Pictures Inc. v. Paladino (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 294, 304.) If the defendant makes a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one arising from protected activity, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to establish a likelihood of prevailing on the complaint. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16.) If that party fails to do so, then the Court may strike the claim.

 

Discussion

 

            Prong One: Protected Activity

 

Under the first step of the analysis, the court must decide whether the moving parties (Plaintiff/Cross-defendants Neman and Lancaster Eagle) have met the threshold burden of showing that the opposing party’s cause of action arises from the moving party’s constitutional rights of free speech or petition. This burden may be met by showing the act underlying the Plaintiff/Cross-defendants’ cause of action falls within one of the four categories of conduct set forth in Code of Civil Procedure Section 425.16, subdivision (e). The categories include, as relevant here, “(1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, [and]¿(2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law....”

 

Plaintiffs/moving parties argue subdivisions (e)(1) and/or (e)(2) describe Modelo’s second, third, and fifth causes of action, because all three causes of action assertedly arise from litigation between Plaintiffs and Modelo.

 

In opposition to the anti-SLAPP motion, Modelo argues that its claims do not arise from the litigation initiated by Plaintiffs, but from their misappropriation of TIC funds and larger-scale breaches of the parties’ agreement.

 

The Court need not accept either party’s characterization of the FAXC at the first prong of the anti-SLAPP inquiry. (See Wilson v. Cable News Network, Inc. (2019) 7 Cal.5th 871, 887 [courts may go beyond the pleadings and consider evidence at prong one].) An anti-SLAPP movant might show, with evidence, that a complaint arises from protected activity, even if a plaintiff has artfully framed its allegations to fall outside the auspices of section 425.16. (Ibid.) “[I]t is the defendant’s activity allegedly giving rise to liability that is the focus of the anti-SLAPP scheme, not the manner in which the plaintiff pleads causes of action.” (Sheley v. Harrop (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 1147, 1164 (Sheley).)

 

The moving parties cite Wilson, supra, to argue that the Court must evaluate the facts underlying Modelo’s claims to determine if section 425.16 applies. But Plaintiff/Cross-defendants have not given the Court facts to consider. Neman’s declaration concedes it was improper to write a check from the parties’ joint account to his own lawyer. (Neman is silent on whether the funds were returned to the account.) Otherwise, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants’ declarations fail to address the conduct underlying the claims asserted in the Cross-complaint.

 

As the parties seeking anti-SLAPP protection from the Cross-complaint, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants bear the burden to show their alleged conduct was protected under section 425.16. They merely assert in conclusory fashion that “the very face of Caster Modelo’s Cross-Complaint makes clear the [challenged] causes of action arise from protected conduct ....” (MPA, 14:9-10.)

 

As Plaintiff/Cross-defendants characterize it, Modelo has sued them solely because they have sued Modelo. Plaintiffs assert the second cause of action arises from “Lancaster Eagle’s assertion ... of a joint venture relationship in this case,” which is an assertion Plaintiffs have made in the course of this judicial proceeding and therefore protected under section 425.16(e)(1).

 

But in the second cause of action of the FAXC, Modelo accuses Lancaster of breaching nine sections of the TIC. (FAXC, ¶ 44(a)-(i).) Among the sections allegedly breached is section 2 of the TIC, “Disclaimer of Entity Status”, which among other things, according to Modelo, forbids Lancaster from acting as Modelo’s agent. (Id., subp. (a).) Plaintiffs argue that these claims of breach arose from protected activity. First, whether the parties created an agency relationship is a disputed fact in this litigation. Plaintiff/Cross-defendants cannot use the anti-SLAPP law against Modelo merely because the parties have opposing views of that ultimate fact.  Second, the moving parties offer no evidence for the assertion.

 

Moreover, Plaintiffs disregard the other eight breaches alleged by Modelo. The cause of action relies on allegations of, for instance, improper distributions of TIC funds (FAXC, ¶ 44(c) and (f)), failing to provide access to books and records (id. (g)), and taking various actions on behalf of the TIC without proper authorization. (id. (b), (d), (e), (h)). These are all simple breaches of Plaintiffs’ duties under the TIC which do not – have anything to do with protected activity or Plaintiffs’ litigation.

 

            Even if Modelo’s Cross-complaint was a response to Plaintiffs’ suit, this does not mean it is “arising from” the suit.  As our Supreme Court explained in City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, the mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purpose of the anti-SLAPP statute. And even if a cause of action was “triggered” by protected activity, that does not establish that the claim arose from it.  (Id. at pp. 76-78.)  “In the anti-SLAPP context, the critical consideration is whether the cause of action is based on the defendant’s protected free speech or petitioning activity.”  (Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 89.)  Plaintiffs’ motion urges this Court to disregard these precedents.

 

Similarly, the conversion and civil theft causes of action arise from Plaintiffs’ alleged misappropriation of funds. Although Modelo alleges some of that money went to pay Plaintiffs’ lawyer, it also alleges the funds were stolen, and it does not know how the funds were eventually used, if at all. (FAXC, ¶¶ 24-25.) Modelo’s claims arise from the funds’ alleged theft, not how the funds may have been spent.  The elements of conversion do not include how wrongfully obtained property is used. (See Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) Accordingly, such asides are merely incidental to the cause of action.

 

Plaintiff/Cross-defendants’ motion seeks to strike entire causes of action when most, if not all, of the specific claims within them are unprotected activities.[1]  The Court does not view any of the causes of action in Modelo’s Cross-complaint as “mixed” – in the anti-SLAPP context, “a cause of action that rests on allegations of multiple acts, some of which constitute protected activity and some of which do not.”  (Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1010.) Here, the moving parties have not established that any protected activity was the basis for any claim asserted in Modelo’s Cross-complaint. 

 

But even if any claim arose from protected activity, the motion seeks to strike entire causes of action. As our Supreme Court has made clear, in a mixed cause of action case, that approach is mistaken.  In Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, the Court explained: “Typically, a pleaded cause of action states a legal ground for recovery supported by specific allegations of conduct by the defendant on which the plaintiff relies to establish a right to relief.  If the supporting allegations include conduct furthering the defendant’s exercise of the constitutional rights of free speech or petition, the pleaded cause of action ‘aris[es] from’ protected activity, at least in part….”  (Id. at pp. 381-382.) The high court noted that some courts, “have held that the motion lies only to strike an entire count as pleaded in the complaint.”  In Baral, the Court concluded that such a practice leads to “anomalous results when the count is supported by allegations of unprotected activity as well as protected activity.”  (Id. at p. 383.)  As applicable here, the Supreme Court explained:  “At the first step, the moving defendant bears the burden of identifying all allegations of protected activity, and the claims for relief supported by them. When relief is sought based on allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the unprotected activity is disregarded at this stage.”  (Id. at p. 396.)  

 

Even where, as here, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants move to strike entire causes of action that include multiple claims, a “claim-by-claim approach” rather than “attempting to evaluate a cause of action as a whole” is the proper analysis.  Our Supreme Court’s so concluded in Bonni v. St. Joseph Health System (2021) 11 Cal.5th 995, 1010-1011. Requiring a much more refined approach than Plaintiff/Cross-defendants utilize here, the Bonni decision explained that “a claim is subject to an anti-SLAPP motion to strike if its elements arise from protected activity. [Citations.] Courts deciding an anti-SLAPP motion thus must consider the claim’s elements, the actions alleged to establish those elements, and whether those actions are protected.”  (Id. at p. 1015.)  Here, at most, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants have pointed to a line or two in the Cross-complaint that – they speculate – could indicate that Modelo is seeking redress because Plaintiffs used stolen funds to pursue this litigation. Thus, they argue, the entire breach of contract cause of action, and the related causes of action for conversion and receiving stolen property, must be stricken. This argument is overbroad misconceives the statute.

 

First, in their motion, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants failed to identify the specific allegations of protected activity. Instead they opted to strike entire causes of action. Second, as Baral explained, those activities do not form the basis of any claim for relief:  “Assertions that are ‘merely incidental’ or ‘collateral’ are not subject to section 425.16 [Citations.].  Allegations of protected activity that merely provide context, without supporting a claim for recovery, cannot be stricken under the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Baral, supra, 1 Cal.5th at p. 394.)  Even if Plaintiff/Cross-defendants had specifically pointed to allegations that their breach of contract resulted in funding this litigation, or that converting jointly held funds were paid to their own lawyers, such allegations are not part of the elements of the claims, are incidental, and not subject to a special motion to strike. 

 

Plaintiff/Cross-defendants rely on Sheley v. Harrop (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 1147, but that case does not aid them.  In Sheley, the majority shareholders of a corporation sued the minority shareholder for, inter alia, refusing to allow access to corporate records. (Id. at pp. 1154-1155.) The minority shareholder cross-complained, accusing the plaintiffs of “paying themselves excessive salaries, ... wrongfully converting corporate assets, ... filing and maintaining a frivolous lawsuit against [her] and ... failing to make pro-rata disbursements to [her] ... .” (Id. at p. 1155, italics and bracketing in original.)  The Court of Appeal modified the trial court order, striking only the allegations referring to maintaining a lawsuit.

 

Here, Modelo’s Cross-complaint does not allege that Plaintiff/Cross-defendants breached the TIC or converted joint funds by filing their lawsuit.  Plaintiff/Cross-defendants Neman and Lancaster fail to identify any specific claims in the amended Cross-complaint that are based on their protected activity. Rather, they seek to strike all claims in a cause of action, including those based on unprotected activity. 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff/Cross-defendants have failed to meet their burden at prong one.

 

None of Plaintiff/Cross-defendants’ other authorities requires a different result.

 

In PrediWave v. Simpson Thacher & Bartlett LLP (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 1204 and Optional Capital, Inc. v. Akin Gump Strauss, Hauer & Feld LLP (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 95, the parties’ postures were fundamentally different than they are in this case. In each of those cases, clients or litigants had directly sued attorneys for conduct occurring during, or in close proximity to, litigation. Similarly, in Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, the plaintiff unsuccessfully tried to characterize consumer protection lawsuits as unfair business practices.

 

In Kashian v. Harriman (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 892 (Kashian), the moving party prevailed because the plaintiffs’ cause of action “arose directly from [the defendant’s] acts or statements ... made in connection with environmental litigation”. (Kashian, supra, at p. 907.) The Kashian court’s discussion distinguishing another case, Kajima Engineering & Construction, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 921 (Kajima), is informative.

 

In Kajima, a construction contractor sued the City of Los Angeles; the City cross-complained, claiming fraud, unfair business practices, and related claims.  Contractor filed an anti-SLAPP motion, arguing, as Plaintiffs do here, that the City was suing the contractor for suing the City. (See Kajima, supra, at pp. 924-925.) The Kajima court opened its decision by stating: “We publish this opinion ... to emphasize that a cross-complaint or independent lawsuit filed in response to, or in retaliation for, threatened or actual litigation is not subject to the anti-SLAPP statute simply because it may be viewed as an oppressive litigation tactic.” (Id., at p. 924.) The Kajima court permitted the City’s cross-complaint to survive because the City’s claims arose from the contractors’ suspect “bidding and contracting practices,” not its litigation against the City. (Ibid.)

 

The cross-complaint in Sheley, as in this case, depended on allegations of corporate malfeasance that had nothing to do with protected conduct. Unlike the cross-complaint in Sheley, the FAXC here makes few express references to litigation, and none of those provide elements for the claim; they are merely contextual and incidental. Similarly, like the cross-complaint in Kajima, the allegations in Modelo’s FAXC relate to the underlying dispute that launched this litigation. But this does not mean that any claim in the FAXC is based on, or arose from, protected litigation activity.

 

Plaintiffs fail to carry their threshold burden to show the cross-complaint arises from protected activity under section 425.16.

 

            “Prong Two”: Likelihood of Prevailing

                       

The FAXC does not arise from activity protected by section 425.16. Because Plaintiffs failed to carry that threshold burden, Modelo does not have to show a likelihood of prevailing on its claims.

 

Conclusion

 

The motion to strike is denied.


[1]             Plaintiff/Cross-defendants’ notice of motion is made “on grounds the second, third, and fifth asserted causes of action within the Cross-Complaint arise out of Cross-Defendants’ constitutionally protected conduct….”


Case Number: 23STCV12479    Hearing Date: July 30, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:             July 30, 2024 

Case Name:                 Dominic Adua Nyaaba v. Quility & Asurea Insurance Services

Case No.:                    23STCV12479

Motion:                       Demurrer

Moving Party:             Defendant Quility & Asurea Insurance Services LLC

Responding Party:      N/A - Unopposed

 

Tentative Ruling:      Defendant Quility & Asurea Insurance Services LLC’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED in its entirety. Leave to amend is denied.

 

 

Background –

 

Procedural Background

            This case arises out of the termination of the alleged employer-employee relationship

between Plaintiff Dominic Adua Nyaaba (“Nyaaba” or “Plaintiff”) and Defendant Quility-

Asurea Agency Insurance Services, LLC (“Quility” or “Defendant”) which Plaintiff alleges

constitutedn a breach of contract. Plaintiff filed an initial Complaint on June 5, 2023. On May 8, 2024, the Court sustained Defendant’s demurrer to the initial Complaint in its entirety, granting leave to amend. On May 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC).

 

            Defendant Quility demurs to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint. No opposition was filed.

 

Factual Background

            Plaintiff alleges that he began an employer-employee relationship with Defendant via contract on May 25, 2021 to sell insurance services. (FAC, pgs. 2 & 5.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant promised to pay Plaintiff $100,000.00 annually. (FAC, pg. 4, lines 5-9.) However, Plaintiff was allegedly never paid proper compensation nor commissions, and alleges he was wrongfully terminated “due to his accent and originality.” (FAC, pg. 43, lines 3-4.) During his employment, Plaintiff alleges that he suffered assault, battery, and emotional distress. As a result, Plaintiff claims he lost wages and sues for a total amount of $165,700.00.  

 

Meet and Confer

            “Before filing a demurrer…the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a).) Filed concurrently with their moving papers, Defendant files the Declaration of Alina N. Gatto which states that the parties conferred telephonically on June 12, 2024, but were unable to come to an agreement. Nonetheless, the requirements of Code Civ. Proc. §430.41(a) have been satisfied. The Court now turns to the demurrer.   

 

Discussion –

 

Legal Standard

            “[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (See Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].) For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hosp. Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Analysis

 

a)     Breach of Contract Claim

            To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

            Plaintiff’s first listed cause of action is for “loss wages”. Upon demurrer, Defendant notes that lost wages are a form of damages not a cause of action. The Court agrees. Within the cause of action, Plaintiff attempts to plead a breach of contract claim, attaching portions of the alleged contract. The prior demurrer was sustained because Plaintiff did not demonstrate the existence of a contract nor a meeting of the minds. That deficiency remains.

 

            First, Defendant points out that the purported contract attached identifies the contracting party as Asurea Insurance Services, Inc., not Quility-Asurea Insurance Services, LLC, the defendant here. With no allegations tying the contract to Defendant, the cause of action fails for lack of a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant. Second, the attached contract is missing several pages, so much so that the terms of the contract are unclear. Finally, consideration is lacking. Plaintiff first alleges that Defendant promised to pay Plaintiff $100,000.00 annually. (FAC, pg. 4, lines 5-9.) But Plaintiff also alleges that he “would make $100,000.00 every 24 months.” (Id. at pg. 6, lines 25-27.) Additionally, Plaintiff states in the FAC that Plaintiff would be providing “his time, energy, intelligence, and skill, insurance license, E&O License, Anti-Money laundering license to work for the organization.” (Id. at pg. 7, lines 6-9.) However, the complaint is unclear as to what services Plaintiff was to provide Defendant in actuality. Moreover, because the attached contract is missing several pages, none of the terms of the bargained for exchange are available for review by the Court. Accordingly, the demurrer to the breach of contract claim is sustained.     

 

b)    Assault and Battery Claim

            Plaintiff also alleges both assault and battery. “The essential elements of a cause of action for assault are: (1) defendant acted with intent to cause harmful or offensive contact, or threatened to touch plaintiff in a harmful or offensive manner; (2) plaintiff reasonably believed [he or] she was about to be touched in a harmful or offensive manner or it reasonably appeared to plaintiff that defendant was about to carry out the threat; (3) plaintiff did not consent to defendant’s conduct; (4) plaintiff was harmed; and (5) defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing plaintiff’s harm.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669.)

 

            “The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant’s conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff’s position would have been offended by the touching.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 668-669.)

 

            As to the assault claim, the FAC states that “On September 20, 2020, Chadd Parker and Elmer subjected me to mockery in the office located at 600 Brand Boulevard, Glendale California. In the event of trying to solve the issue of selling mortgage insurance products to clients, Chadd held all the documents in my hands, and snatched them from my hands. Krystal Yelled [sic] at me and pulled out a gun. Elmer chased me out of the office…” Here, Plaintiff alleges assault was committed by three separate individuals. However, it is unclear what relationship these individuals have to Defendant, how Defendant is liable for their actions, nor why either of them was not made party to the suit.

 

            As for battery, the FAC fails to mention how Defendant, or any employee or agent thereof, made physical contact with Plaintiff’s person. Accordingly, the demurrer to the assault and battery claims is sustained.  

 

c)     Negligence Claims

            In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

            Here, the FAC states that Defendant was negligent “when the office manager shouted plaintiff out of a meeting by stating, ‘not now’.” The FAC goes on to state that Defendant “owed a duty to protect plaintiff because he was hired…” However, as previously noted, the employment relationship is based on a contract that Plaintiff has not demonstrated exists. Therefore, the FAC identifies no cognizable legal duty on the part of Defendant. Without a legal duty, any cause of action based in negligence cannot stand. Accordingly, the demurrer to Plaintiff’s negligence claims is sustained.  

 

d)    Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

            “The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” (Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.)

 

            The demurrer to the cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress fails because the FAC fails to identify any extreme and outrageous conduct by Defendant. The FAC states that “Chadd Parker instead of accepting my sales refused to accepted sale and assaulted.” As stated above, it is unclear who Chadd Parker is and what relationship he has to Defendant to confer liability. Without alleging facts as to the misconduct of Defendant and how Defendant caused severe or extreme emotional distress, the cause of action fails. The demurrer is sustained.

 

e)     Wrongful Termination and Discrimination Claims

            “The elements of a claim for wrongful discharge in violation of public policy are (1) an employer-employee relationship, (2) the employer terminated the plaintiff’s employment, (3) the termination was substantially motivated by a violation of public policy, and (4) the discharge caused the plaintiff harm. It is well established that a termination premised on an employee’s refusal to violate either a statute or an administrative regulation may support a claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy.” (Nosal-Tabor v. Sharp Chula Vista Medical Center (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 1224, 1234, citation omitted.)

 

            The FAC’s final claim is a wrongful termination claim where Plaintiff claims that part of the reason he was fired was for his “accent and originality.” However, the FAC is devoid of any facts demonstrating bias or animosity against any protected characteristic of Plaintiff. Without those facts a discrimination claim cannot survive. Similarly, the FAC fails to allege how the termination was motivated by a violation of public policy, without it any wrongful termination claim falls to demurrer. Therefore, the demurrer to both claims is sustained.   

 

Leave to Amend

            Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349 [court shall not “sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is any reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment”]. Plaintiff has the burden to show that the defects can be cured. This is Plaintiff’s second attempt to plead a cause of action. There is no reasonable possibility of successful amendment.  Leave to amend is denied.  

 

Conclusion –

            Defendant Quility & Asurea Insurance Services LLC’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint is sustained in its entirety without leave to amend.
