DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 22STCV38179    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:   May 8, 2024                                       

 

CASE NAME:           D.L. v. DOE 1, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                22STCV38179

 

DEMURRER WITH MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant DOE 1

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff D.L

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      An order sustaining the demurrer.

2.      An order striking various portions.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Demurrer is OVERRULED.

2.      Motion to Strike is DENIED.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

On December 7, 2022, Plaintiff D.L. (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants DOE 1, a New York Corporation, and DOE 2, a California nonprofit corporation (Defendants) for the following causes of action: (1) Negligent Hiring, Supervision, and Retention; (2) Negligent Supervision of a Minor, and (3) Negligence.

 

On August 7, 2023, Defendant DOE 1 filed a motion to strike portions of the Complaint.

 

On August 8, 2023, Defendant DOE 1 filed a demurrer to the Complaint.

 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) with the same three causes of action.

 

On November 28, 2023, Defendant DOE 1 filed a demurrer and motion to strike portions for the FAC. The Court sustained the demurrer, denied the motion to strike as moot, and granted Plaintiff leave to file an amended complaint.

On February 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint with the same three causes of action.

 

The SAC brings an action to recover damages on behalf of an adult victim of childhood sexual assault pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 340.1. SAC alleges that Lowell Banks (Banks), now deceased, met Plaintiff through Defendants’ Highland Park Congregation, where Banks held a position of authority. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to warn them about Banks' history of inappropriate behavior with minors, which led to plaintiff's sexual assault, and that DOE 1 had records of Banks' prior sexual misconduct but chose to keep them hidden to protect DOE 1’s reputation.

 

On May 30, 2023, Defendant filed a Demurrer with a Motion to Strike. On May 31, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Opposition to the Demurrer. On June 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Opposition to the Motion to Strike.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

The Court DENIES Defendant’s request for judicial notice of fact. The court grants Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as to Exhibits 1, 2, and 3 under Evidence Code § 452(a), (c), and (h) as the documents were a part of the statutory law of California. However, the court only takes judicial notice of the forgoing documents as to “the existence, content, and authenticity of public records and other specified documents”; it does not take judicial notice of the truth of the factual matters asserted in those documents. (Dominguez v. Bonta (2022) 87 Cal. App. 5th 389, 400.)

 

Demurrer

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. …. The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

Motion to Strike

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.) “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” (Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768.)

 

Meet and Confer:

 

Prior to filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to satisfy their meet and confer obligations pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. §430.41, and demonstrate that they so satisfied their meet and confer obligation by submitting a declaration pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. §430.41(a)(2) & (3). The Declaration of Defendant DOE 1’s counsel indicates that the parties met and conferred telephonically on February 16, 2024. (Declaration of Brian D. Walters ¶ 4.) The parties were unable to reach an agreement on DOE 1’s issues, necessitating the instant motion. (Id.)

 

ANALYSIS:

 

Defendant demurs on two grounds. First, the SAC has not sufficiently pled prior knowledge by DOE 1 of Banks misconduct that created a risk of childhood sexual assault. Second, Plaintiff has not sufficiently pled that knowledge by DOE 1 that the failure to take reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of childhood sexual assault led to Bank’s sexual assault of Plaintiff.

Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1. The most current iteration of the statute provides in relevant part:  

 

“(a) There is no time limit for the commencement of any of the following actions for recovery of damages suffered as a result of childhood sexual assault: 

(1) An action against any person for committing an act of childhood sexual assault. 

(2) An action for liability against any person or entity who owed a duty of care to the plaintiff, if a wrongful or negligent act by that person or entity was a legal cause of the childhood sexual assault that resulted in the injury to the plaintiff. 

(3) An action for liability against any person or entity if an intentional act by that person or entity was a legal cause of the childhood sexual assault that resulted in the injury to the plaintiff. 

…

(p) This section applies to any claim in which the childhood sexual assault occurred on and after January 1, 2024. Notwithstanding any other law, a claim for damages based on conduct described in paragraphs (1) through (3), inclusive, of subdivision (a), in which the childhood sexual assault occurred on or before December 31, 2023 may only be commenced pursuant to the applicable statute of limitations set forth in existing law as it read on December 31, 2023. 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 340.1.) 

 

 

1.      Knowledge of Banks’ Risk for Childhood Sexual Assault  

DOE 1 argues that the SAC has not pled sufficient non-conclusory facts to plead that DOE 1 has knowledge of any misconduct by Banks that created a risk of childhood sexual assault. DOE 1 contends that Plaintiff SAC has failed to improve from the FAC because the complaint still contains conclusory statements that do not give rise to the cause of action. Defendant cites to Santillan v. Roman Cath. Bishop of Fresno and Doe v. City of Los Angeles, where the courts held that an entity being sued for acts of molestation by its agent or employee must have had either actual or constructive knowledge or notice of the perpetrator’s unlawful sexual conduct. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531; Santillan v. Roman Cath. Bishop of Fresno, (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 708, 717–18.)

 

Plaintiff argues that she sufficiently alleged that DOE 1 had knowledge and notice of Banks’ prior inappropriate and sexual misconduct of minors through DOE 1’s database and recording policies and procedures prior to Bank’s abuse of Plaintiff and does not need show actual knowledge/notice of unlawful sexual misconduct.

 

Here, the Court finds that DOE 1’s argument is without merit. First, the SAC does plead that DOE 1 had actual knowledge because it alleges that DOE 1 had records of Banks' prior sexual misconduct but chose to keep them hidden to protect DOE 1’s reputation. (SAC ¶ 30.) The SAC further alleges that DOE 1 had policies in place that prohibited disclosure to the police when they were informed of or knew about allegations or reports of childhood sexual abuse. (Id. ¶ 25.) Moreover, it appears that DOE 1 misconstrues the Court’s February 9, 2024 Minute Order. The Court sustained the demurrer of the FAC because it only contained boilerplate language that lacked minimal information, which rendered the allegations conclusory. (See. 02/09/24 Minute Order.) The SAC by contrast, does provide more factual assertions on the how, when, where, or under what circumstances DOE 1 obtained knowledge because the SAC alleges that “DOE 1 received and compiled records of reports, complaints, and allegations of prior inappropriate conduct by Banks with minors before Plaintiff’s abuse began in approximately 1958.” (Id.) DOE 1’s reliance on Pridonoff to support its contention that the SAC only contains conclusory allegations is misplaced. As stated above, a demurrer can only rely on the complaint and any judicially noticed documents. During the pleading stage, Plaintiff need not fully allege the information upon which these statements are made. Taking the allegations as true, the SAC alleges that DOE 1 was aware of Bank’s prior conduct because it kept records that it failed to disclose to the congregation. Factual disputes surrounding the basis of the Plaintiff’s allegation are more properly reserved for the discovery phase of the litigation, but for the purposes of a demurrer, the SAC does sufficiently plead that DOE did have knowledge of Banks’s unlawful conduct.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged knowledge by Defendant.

 

2.      Reasonable safeguards  

DOE 1 similarly argues that the SAC has not sufficiently alleged Defendant’s failure to take reasonable steps or implement reasonable safeguards to avoid acts of childhood sexual assault by using non-conclusory facts.

 

Plaintiff argues that DOE 1 failed to take reasonable steps to implement or enforce reasonable safeguards to prevent childhood sexual assaults because DOE 1’s policies and procedures were antithetical and inhibited investigation, prevention, and dissemination that a reasonable employer would have implemented to prevent childhood assaults by continuing to employ Banks despite its knowledge of his pedophilic past and completely failed to supervise him.

           

The Court finds that DOE 1’s argument fails. The SAC alleges that DOE 1 did not implement reasonable safeguards because its policies prevented disclosure of Banks' past sexual misconduct with minors to protect their reputation. (SAC ¶ 31.) In fact, the SAC alleges that “DOE 1 expressly prohibited any dissemination of Banks’ known pedophilic tendencies, prior reports and complaints of inappropriate conduct with minors, or warning to congregants about Banks’ known prior abuse and inappropriate conduct including to Plaintiff’s parents prior to Banks’ abuse of Plaintiff.” (Id.) At the beginning of the factual allegations, the SAC asserts that “DOE 1 [established a] mandatory processes for the discipline of members accused of wrongdoing, including sexual abuse of minors… An Elder is subject to church discipline for any misdeeds that occurred in church or in the Elder’s personal life.” (Id. ¶ 22.) The SAC further alleges that “DOE 1 also mandated that if an employee such as Banks was suspected of child abuse and moved to another congregation, then the new congregation should not be informed of the prior abuse and/or investigation by the prior congregation.” (Id. ¶ 23.) Taken together, Plaintiff’s allegations contend that DOE 1’s policy was antithetical to providing reasonable safeguards because the policies essentially stymied investigation, prevention, and dissemination efforts that could have stopped further abuse.

 

Thus, Defendant DOE 1’s argument fails.

 

            Demurrer is OVERRULED.

1. Paragraph 25 as follows “…then not only would DOE 1 forbid its other Elders and

members from informing the police….”;

2. Paragraph 25 as follows: “…and warn individual parents of DOE 3’s dangerous

propensities…”;

minors…”;

5. Paragraph 35 in its entirety;

6. Paragraph 38 subsection (c) in its entirety;

7. Paragraph 39 subsection (c) in its entirety;

8. Paragraph 48 in its entirety;

9. Paragraph 50 as follows “…but failed and/or refused to take any affirmative action,

including but not limited to notifying law enforcement.”

 

Defendant’s basis for striking these portions are: (1) Plaintiff alleges conduct that occurred after the alleged abuse, which is barred by the statute of limitations under CCP § 436; (2) the allegations failed to report the alleged abuse to either members of the congregation or law enforcement agencies is not in conformity with the law; and (3) the allegations provide for disclosure of confidential information protected under clergy/penitent privilege. The Court disagrees, noting the arguments raised before. Moreover, the Court agrees with the Plaintiff that the portions cited are relevant to the claims asserted. Finally, the assertion of clergy-penitent privilege is irrelevant and disingenuous because privilege only applies to statements made for the purpose of receiving penitence and would have no applicability to the portions cited.

 

Therefore, the Motion to Strike is DENIED.

For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

            Demurrer is OVERRULED. Motion to Strike is DENIED. Defendant DOE 1 is to provide an Answer within 20 days.

CASE NAME:           S.T. v. Eric Weinberg, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                23STCV21986

 

DEMURRER TO FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Liquid Theory, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff S.T.

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action for Sexual Harassment because the Complaint is barred by the statute of limitations and for failure to state sufficient facts to revive Plaintiff’s claims;

2.      Demurrer to the Ninth Cause of Action for Negligence because the Complaint is barred by the statute of limitations and for failure to state sufficient facts to revive Plaintiff’s claims;

3.      Demurrer to the Eleventh Cause of Action for Aiding and Abetting because the Complaint is barred by the statute of limitations and for failure to state sufficient facts to revive Plaintiff’s claims and plead that Liquid Theory had specific intent.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Liquid Theory’s Demurrer to the Third, Ninth and Eleventh Causes of Action is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

On September 12, 2023, Plaintiff S.T. (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Eric Weinberg (“Weinberg”), Guitar and Pen Productions, Inc. (“G&P”), MTV Networks (“MTV”), and Liquid Theory, Inc. (“Liquid Theory”) alleging eleven causes of action for: (1) Sexual Assault, (2) Sexual Battery, (3) Sexual Harassment, (4) Gender Violence, (5) False Imprisonment, (6) Assault, (7) Battery, (8) Intentional Inflection of Emotional Distress, (9) Negligence, (10) Respondeat Superior, and (11) Aiding and Abetting.

According to the FAC, Weinberg sexually assaulted and harassed Plaintiff while she was a working actress on MTV’s show Death Valley and after she left the show. Liquid Theory maintained a professional working relationship with Weinberg directly and by and through Defendant G&P by entering into agreements for Weinberg to serve as creator, producer and/or writer for shows it produced, distributed and/or controlled the creation and filming of, including but not limited to Death Valley at or around 2011. (FAC ¶ 13.)

On February 5, 2024, the Court sustained MTV’s demurrer to the Complaint as to the Third cause of action for Sexual Harassment, Ninth cause of action for Negligence, and Eleventh cause of action for Aiding and Abetting, on the grounds that each claim is barred by the statute of limitations and for failure to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

On February 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), which alleges the same eleven causes of action as the original complaint.

On March 26, 2024, Liquid Theory filed the instant demurrer to the Third, Ninth and Eleventh causes of action for Sexual Harassment, Negligence, and Aiding and Abetting. Plaintiff filed an opposition on April 25, 2024, and MTV replied on May 1, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD:

The grounds for a demurrer must appear on the face of the pleading or from judicially noticeable matters. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a); Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Ibid.)

A demurrer assumes the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading. (Blank, supra, 39 Cal. 3d at p. 318.) No matter how unlikely or improbable, the plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.) But this does not include contentions; deductions; conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice. (Blank, supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)

Pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may demur to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment. (Hahn, supra, 31 Cal.4th at p. 745.)

 

Meet and Confer 

Prior to filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to satisfy their meet and confer obligations pursuant to Code of Civ. Proc. (CCP) §430.41 and demonstrate that they so satisfied their meet and confer obligation by submitting a declaration pursuant to CCP §430.41(a)(2) and (3). Here, according to the Declaration of Michon Spinelli, the parties met and conferred via email and telephonically on March 26, 2024. (Spinelli Decl. ¶ 3.) The parties were unable to reach an agreement. (Id. at ¶ 4.) Accordingly, the meet and confer requirement is met. 

ANALYSIS:

 

Statute of Limitations

Liquid Theory argues that Plaintiff’s claims are time-barred pursuant to CCP §§ 335.1. In the opposition, Plaintiff argues that her claims were revived by CCP §340.16(b) and (e).

CCP § 340.16 provides in relevant parts:

 (a) In any civil action for recovery of damages suffered as a result of sexual assault, . . . the time for commencement of the action shall be the later of the following:

(1) Within 10 years from the date of the last act, attempted act, or assault with the intent to commit an act, of sexual assault against the plaintiff.

(2) Within three years from the date the plaintiff discovers or reasonably should have discovered that an injury or illness resulted from an act, attempted act, or assault with the intent to commit an act, of sexual assault against the plaintiff.

(b) . . . (3) This section applies to any action described in subdivision (a) that is based upon conduct that occurred on or after January 1, 2009, and is commenced on or after January 1, 2019, that would have been barred solely because the applicable statute of limitations has or had expired. Such claims are hereby revived and may be commenced until December 31, 2026.

(e) (1) Notwithstanding any other law, any claim seeking to recover damages suffered as a result of a sexual assault . . . that would otherwise be barred before January 1, 2023, solely because the applicable statute of limitations has or had expired, is hereby revived, and a cause of action . . . may be commenced between January 1, 2023, and December 31, 2023.

(2) This subdivision revives claims brought by a plaintiff who alleges all of the following:

(A) The plaintiff was sexually assaulted.

(B) One or more entities are legally responsible for damages arising out of the sexual assault.

(C) The entity or entities, including, but not limited to, their officers, directors, representatives, employees, or agents, engaged in a cover up or attempted a cover up of a previous instance or allegations of sexual assault by an alleged perpetrator of such abuse. . . .

(4) For purposes of this subdivision:

(A) “Cover up” means a concerted effort to hide evidence relating to a sexual assault that incentivizes individuals to remain silent or prevents information relating to a sexual assault from becoming public or being disclosed to the plaintiff, including, but not limited to, the use of nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements. . . .

(C) “Legally responsible” means that the entity or entities are liable under any theory of liability established by statute or common law, including, but not limited to, negligence, intentional torts, and vicarious liability.

(5) This subdivision revives any related claims, including, but not limited to, wrongful termination and sexual harassment, arising out of the sexual assault that is the basis for a claim pursuant to this subdivision.

 

Subsection (e)

Defendant MTV’s demurrer to the original complaint was sustained on the ground that Plaintiff failed to sufficiently plead the cover-up requirement for CCP § 340.16(e). In the FAC, Plaintiff added allegations that MTV attempted to cover up two instances of sexual assault. (See FAC ¶ 7b.) Plaintiff also added allegations that Liquid Theory covered up past incidents. Paragraph 7c of the FAC states, in its entirety:

 

“Plaintiff is informed and believes and thereon alleges that prior to February 2011, Defendant LIQUID THEORY, INC. including by its officers, directors, representatives, employees, and/or agents, engaged in a cover up or attempted a cover up of a previous instance or allegations of sexual assault by an alleged perpetrator of such abuse. This cover up by LIQUID THEORY, INC. including by its officers, directors, representatives, employees, and/or agents included a concerted effort to hide evidence relating to a sexual assault that incentivizes individuals to remain silent or prevents information relating to a sexual assault from becoming public or being disclosed to the plaintiff, including, but not limited to, the use of nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements.”

 

In its demurrer, Liquid Theory argues that the cover-up allegations in the FAC are conclusory and do not include any specific facts. Indeed, Plaintiff’s allegations of cover-up by Liquid Theory merely repeats the statutory language codified in CCP § 340.16(e). To survive a demurrer, a complaint must “allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action”. (See, e.g., C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal. 4th 861, 872 [emphasis added].) Here, the only facts that Plaintiff alleges as to Liquid Theory are the following: Liquid Theory “maintained a professional working relationship with Weinberg” (FAC ¶ 13); Liquid Theory failed to investigate once it discovered Weinberg’s sexual assault of Plaintiff on the set of Death Valley (FAC ¶¶ 14, 30); Liquid Theory controlled and operated the Death Valley production (FAC ¶ 56); and Liquid Theory contracted with Weinberg knowing that he was a sexual predator (FAC ¶¶ 99, 113). Plaintiff does not provide any factual basis for claiming that Liquid Theory previously covered up sexual assault. Even at the demurrer stage, a plaintiff must allege facts to support their causes of action. If the only requirement was to list the statutory elements of each claim, no demurrer would ever be sustained.

Plaintiff has failed to plead sufficient facts to allege a cover-up within the meaning of CCP § 340.16(e). Subsection (e) cannot revive Plaintiff’s claims against Liquid Theory.

 

Subsection (b)

 

In her opposition, Plaintiff contends that CCP § 340.16(b) also revives Plaintiff’s claims against Liquid Theory. To support this argument, Plaintiff cites to dicta in the Court of Appeal’s decision in Jane Doe #21 (S.H.) v. CFR Enterprises, Inc. (2011) 93 Cal.App.5th 1199. Footnote 11 of the Jane Doe #21 decision states: “the causes of action permitted under subdivision (a) include “causes of action against persons or entities other than the alleged person who committed the” alleged sexual assault or battery. (§ 340.16, subd. (b)(2).)”

In reply, Liquid Theory contends that the revival provision of Subsection (b) is more limited than that of Subsection (e). Specifically, Subsection (b) is narrower in that it limits revival to claims for “sexual assault”, which is specifically defined as “any of the crimes described in Section 243.4, 261, 264.1, 286, 287, or 289, or former Sections 262 and 288a, of the Penal Code, assault with the intent to commit any of those crimes, or an attempt to commit any of those crimes.” (CCP § 340.16(b)(1).) Subsection (b) does not revive “related claims” such as Plaintiff’s claims against Liquid Theory. Liquid Theory cites to the August 25, 2022 Assembly Floor Analysis for AB 2777. It appears that the Legislature intended that Subsection (b) only “revives claims for sexual assault”, whereas Subsection (e) is meant to revive “any claim seeking to recover damages suffered as a result of a sexual assault.” (AB 2777 Assem. Floor Analysis, Aug. 25, 2022, ¶¶3-4.)

Liquid Theory also points out that unlike Subsection (b), Subsection (e) unambiguously sets specific revival requirements for non-perpetrator entity defendants. This distinction is persuasive. The Court agrees with Liquid Theory that, to adopt Subsection (b)’s standards as applying to non-perpetrator entities, when there are more detailed, specific, and later-adopted provisions under subsection (e) which do address non-perpetrator entities, would be to reject basic tenets of statutory construction. (See, e.g., Arbuckle-Coll. City Fire Prot. Dist. v. Cnty. of Colusa (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 1155, 1166 (2003) [it is “presumed that when the Legislature has enacted a specific statute to deal with a particular matter, it would intend the specific statute to control over more general provisions of law that might otherwise apply”].)

Therefore, there is no basis for revival of Plaintiff’s claims against Liquid Theory under CCP § 340.16(b).

 

No part of CCP § 340.16 provides for the revival of Plaintiff’s claims.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s claims against Liquid Theory are time-barred.

For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

Defendant Liquid Theory’s Demurrer to the Third, Ninth, and Eleventh Causes of Action are

SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

(4) For purposes of this subdivision:

(A) “Cover up” means a concerted effort to hide evidence relating to a sexual assault that incentivizes individuals to remain silent or prevents information relating to a sexual assault from becoming public or being disclosed to the plaintiff, including, but not limited to, the use of nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements. . . .

(C) “Legally responsible” means that the entity or entities are liable under any theory of liability established by statute or common law, including, but not limited to, negligence, intentional torts, and vicarious liability.

(5) This subdivision revives any related claims, including, but not limited to, wrongful termination and sexual harassment, arising out of the sexual assault that is the basis for a claim pursuant to this subdivision.

 

Subsection (e)

 

MTV’s demurrer to the original complaint was sustained on the ground that Plaintiff failed to sufficiently plead the cover-up requirement for CCP § 340.16(e). In the FAC, Plaintiff added allegations of two cover-up incidents. (See FAC ¶ 7b.) The first cover-up occurred during the 2003 season of MTV’s reality show, The Real World: San Diego. (Id.) During filming for the show, a woman was raped in the Real World house. (Id.) The woman made a police report. (Id.) According to a published search warrant application, an attorney for MTV restricted police access to certain areas of the house and withheld documents and footage unless a court order was obtained. (Id.) The second cover-up occurred in 2009 and involved Tonya Cooely, a contestant in The Real World/Road Rules Challenge. (Id.) Plaintiff alleges that Cooley was raped with a toothbrush by fellow cast members while intoxicated. (Id.) Plaintiff alleges that thereafter, MTV did not tell Cooley about the assault and removed the toothbrush. (Id.) MTV later settled Cooley’s lawsuit and entered into a confidentiality agreement. (Id.)

 

In the present demurrer, MTV argues that the cover-up allegations in the FAC are insufficient on three grounds. First, MTV argues that the cover-ups are unrelated to Plaintiff’s claims. (Dem., 6:16-17 [“different victim, different show, different team”].) The Code does not require that an alleged prior cover-up involve the same perpetrator or victim. (See CCP § 340.16(e)(3)(C) [“engaged in a cover up or attempted a cover up of a previous instance or allegations of sexual assault by an alleged perpetrator”]; emphasis added.) The court in Jane Doe #21 (S.H.) v. CFR Enterprises, Inc. (2011) 93 Cal.App.5th 1199 remarked that the statutory language is not ambiguous, and “nothing . . . suggests the Legislature intended section 340.16(e) to require a plaintiff to allege that defendants sought to cover up a prior assault committed by the same perpetrator who later allegedly assaulted the plaintiff. (Jane Doe #21 at 1212 [fn 16].) Here, the incidents that Plaintiff alleges MTV has covered up are sufficiently similar to Plaintiff’s situation. Both victims in the cover-ups were women who appeared in television projects broadcast by MTV, just like Plaintiff. [1]

 

Second, MTV argues that the 2003 and 2009 incidents were not covered up because MTV made public statements about them, and details of those events were and remain public. This argument is misleading. The Code defines cover-up as “a concerted effort to hide evidence relating to a sexual assault that incentivizes individuals to remain silent or prevents information relating to a sexual assault from becoming public or being disclosed to the plaintiff, including, but not limited to, the use of nondisclosure agreements or confidentiality agreements.” (CCP § 340.16(e)(4)(A).) Moreover, Subsection (e) clearly states that allegations of an attempted cover up also suffice. (CCP § 340.16(e)(3)(C) [“engaged in a cover up or attempted a cover up”].) MTV’s contention that it willingly revealed some aspects of the prior incidents does not negate Plaintiff’s cover-up allegations.

 

Third, MTV contends that the cover-up allegations in the FAC lack specificity because they do not plead “who from MTV engaged in this alleged concerted effort or when, why, or how MTV began to direct this alleged concerted effort towards Plaintiff.” (Dem., 17:16-18.) The Court disagrees. The FAC alleges that MTV “officers, directors, representatives, employees and/or agents” removed the toothbrush that was used in the 2009 assault of Ms. Cooley, publicly stated that Ms. Cooley was partially responsible for the assault, and entered into a confidentiality agreement when settling Ms. Cooley’s claims. (FAC ¶ 7b.) Subsection (e) expressly identifies confidentiality agreements as a possible means of cover-up. (CCP § 340.16(e)(4)(A).) As to the 2003 cover-up, the FAC alleges that the victim woke up to herself being filmed by “the production’s camera crew”, and an “attorney for MTV” restricted police access to certain areas of the house/filming location. (FAC, ¶ 7b.)

 

Cover up is sufficiently pled. Accordingly, CCP § 340.16(e) revives Plaintiff’s claims.

 

Whether the FAC states sufficient facts to constitute the causes of action

 

MTV argues in passing that, in addition to being barred by the statute of limitations, Plaintiff’s causes of action against MTV are also fail to state sufficient facts.

 

Sexual Harassment

 

There is no common law cause of action for action for sexual harassment. (Myers v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1426-1427 [“There is no common law cause of action for sexual harassment, but conduct constituting sexual harassment may be alleged in common law claims such as battery and intentional infliction of emotional distress”]; Medix Ambulance Service, Inc. v. Superior Court (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 109, 118-119 [“plaintiff cites no authority supporting a common law cause of action for sexual harassment”; “cause of action for sexual harassment is a creature of statute”].)

 

Therefore, MTV’s demurrer as to the third cause of action for Sexual Harassment is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

Negligence

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) Here, the FAC alleges that MTV owed Plaintiff a duty to exercise reasonable care in the hiring, supervision, and retention of its personnel, and MTV breached that duty by hiring Weinberg, knowing that he is a sexual predator. (FAC ¶¶ 96, 97, 99.) Plaintiff suffered damages as a result. (FAC ¶ 100.) Plaintiff has sufficiently pled negligence.

 

In its demurrer, MTV argues summarily that Plaintiff’s negligence claim is “governed exclusively by workers’ compensation.” (Notice of Dem.; Dem., 12:13-16 [“Separately, if Plaintiff contends that MTV employed her, then, the exclusive remedy for injuries suffered as a result of employer negligence is workers’ compensation”].) Ordinarily, an employee’s rights against his or her employer for on-the-job injuries lie solely under the workers’ compensation law—i.e., when the “conditions of compensation” are present (Labor Code § 3600), the employer is immune from civil damages liability because workers’ compensation is the injured employee’s “exclusive remedy.” (Labor Code §§ 3600, 3601, 3602(a).) The Court agrees with Plaintiff that MTV’s argument is fact-based and premature at the demurrer stage.

 

Accordingly, MTV’s demurrer as to the ninth cause of action for Negligence is OVERRULED.

 

Aiding and Abetting

 

The Court noted in its February 5, 2024 order granting MTV’s demurrer to the original complaint that Aiding and Abetting is not a cause of action, but rather a theory of liability. Plaintiff did not correct this in the FAC and continues to assert Aiding and Abetting as a cause of action.

 

MTV’s demurrer as to the eleventh cause of action for Aiding and Abetting is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

Defendant MTV’s Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND and to the Eleventh Cause of Action WIHTOUT leave to amend.

 

Defendant MTV’s Demurrer to the Ninth Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.
