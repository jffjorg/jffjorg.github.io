DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV02647    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Sanchez v. Mizban, et al.

CASE NO.:                21STCV02647

MOTION:                  Motion for Reconsideration; Vacate Order per CCP §473(b)

HEARING DATE:   July 12, 2024

 

Legal Standard

 

A non-prevailing party may make a motion to reconsider and enter a different order under the following conditions: (1) brought before the same judge that made the order sought to be reconsidered; (2) made within 10 days after service upon the party of the notice of entry of the order (extended under CCP § 1013 for type of service); (3) based on new or different facts, circumstances or law than those before the court at the time of the original ruling; (4) supported by a declaration stating the previous order, by which judge it was made, and the new or different facts, circumstances or law claimed to exist; and (5) the motion must be made and decided before entry of judgment. (CCP, § 1008.)  

 

The legislature intended to restrict motions for reconsideration to circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or circumstance not previously considered and some valid reason for not offering it earlier. (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.)¿¿Thus, “[t]o be entitled to reconsideration, a party should show that (1) evidence of new or different facts exist, and (2) the party has a satisfactory explanation for failing to produce such evidence at an earlier time.” (Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342.) The moving party must demonstrate that such newly discovered evidence could not, with reasonable diligence, have previously discovered and produced. (Wilcox v. Ford (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 1170.) The court may also reconsider its prior interim orders on its own motion to correct its own errors. (Le Francoise v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, 1107-09.)¿ 

 

“No application to reconsider any order or for the renewal of a previous motion may be considered by any judge or court unless made according to this section.” (CCP § 1008(e)); see Powell v. County of Orange (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1573, 1577 [“The name of a motion is not controlling, and, regardless of the name, a motion asking the trial court to decide the same matter previously ruled on is a motion for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008.”].) Therefore, the moving party must comply with the requirements of section 1008, regardless of the title of the noticed motion.

 

A motion for reconsideration must be made to the same judge. (CCP § 1008(a).) “A trial court’s discretion to reconsider another judge’s prior ruling is necessarily narrow and usually only appropriate when the prior judge is unavailable.” (Marriage of Oliverez (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1247.) “[O]ne trial court judge may not reconsider and overrule a ruling by another trial court judge, unless the first judge is unavailable.”  (Davcon, Inc. v. Roberts & Morgan (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1355, 1361.) 

 

Reconsideration cannot be granted based on claims the court misinterpreted the law in its initial ruling because this is not a “new” or “different” matter.¿ (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.)

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff Gabriel Sanchez moves for reconsideration of this Court's April 11, 2024, order granting Defendants Kasra and Nasser Mizban’s motion for summary adjudication as to Defendants’ Eleventh Affirmative Defense, limiting Plaintiff’s damages to economic damages pursuant to pursuant to Civil Code section 3333.4. In the alternative, Plaintiff moves to vacate the Court’s April 11, 2024, order under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b)’s discretionary relief provision.

 

New or Different Circumstances

 

Plaintiff argues new or different facts exist that were not previously considered by the Court and that they could affect its ruling. To wit, Plaintiff cites a declaration from Allstate which is “conclusive on the issue” of coverage. (Hakim Decl., Ex. 3.)

 

Plaintiff admits that the declaration is not truly a “new or different” fact within the meaning of Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, and fails to provide an excuse for failing to produce this evidence at the hearing. Plaintiff was aware that he needed to marshal evidence to oppose the motion and create a dispute of fact as to the existence of insurance coverage. For example, Plaintiff discusses how discovery was conducted in 2023 for Allstate to confirm coverage. (Hakim Decl., ¶¶4-7.) Plaintiff therefore had access to all such documentation well before the hearing. Plaintiff has therefore not satisfied the procedural requirements for reconsideration. For this reason alone, the Court cannot grant the motion.

 

Even if considered, the proffered declaration merely provides a legal conclusion regarding the interpretation of a contract. It is black letter law that the interpretation of an insurance contract is the role of the Court. (Foster-Gardner, Inc. v. National Union Fire Insurance Company (1998) 18 Cal.4th 857, 879.) This declaration does not attempt to show any ambiguity in the agreement which would create a dispute of fact. (Id.) Moreover, the declaration does not put into dispute any of the material facts, including the express terms of the insurance agreements at issue.

 

Accordingly, the motion is DENIED.

 

Discretionary Relief under CCP §473(b)

 

Such relief is unavailable. “No application to reconsider any order or for the renewal of a previous motion may be considered by any judge or court unless made according to this section.” (CCP § 1008(e)); see Powell v. County of Orange (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1573, 1577 [“The name of a motion is not controlling, and, regardless of the name, a motion asking the trial court to decide the same matter previously ruled on is a motion for reconsideration under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008.”].) Therefore, the moving party must comply with the requirements of section 1008. Moreover, in considering such requirements, the Court would not exercise its discretion under section 473(b) to vacate the order.


Case Number: 23SMCV04996    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Nikfarjam v. Garcia

CASE NO.:                23SMCV04996

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Initial Discovery Responses

HEARING DATE:   7/12/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

 If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.) 

 

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP section 2031.010 inspection demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300.) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion. Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required. 

 

ANALYSIS 

 

Defendant, Carol Garcia moves to compel Plaintiff Farshid Nikfarjam to respond, without objections, to Special Interrogatories, Set No. One, Form Interrogatories, Set No. One, and Demand for Production of Documents, Set No. One (collectively, the “Subject Discovery”), within fifteen (15) days.

 

This action concerns personal injuries and damage from an automobile accident that occurred on October 25, 2021.  Defendant propounded the Subject Discovery by mail on January 19, 2024. Plaintiff did not serve responses within the original due date of February 21, 2024. (Saakian Decl., Exs. A.) On April 16, 2024, Defendant informed Plaintiff of their intent to move to compel. (See Id., Exs. B.)

 

Defendant demonstrates that Plaintiff has failed to respond to duly issued discovery. Accordingly, the motions to compel are GRANTED.  Further responses are due within 10 days without objection.

 

SANCTIONS 

 

Sanctions are mandatory. The Court must sanction any party that unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response, “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (See, e.g., CCP, § 2030.290(c).) Plaintiff has failed to oppose, and therefore failed to justify their failure to respond.

 

Defendant requests sanctions of $1,760.00, $760.00, and $760.00 for the respective motions to compel. (See Saakian Decl., ¶ 5.) Using the lodestar method, and considering the duplicative nature of the three motions, the Court finds that a reasonable sanction for the three motions would be $2,280.00, inclusive of costs, which is $760.00 a motion.  Sanctions are awarded against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, Matt Kahoonim, jointly and severally.

 

Sanctions are payable within 30 days.


Case Number: 24SMCV00099    Hearing Date: July 12, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Fanfixapp LLC, et al., v. Passes Inc., et al.

CASE NO.:                24SMCV00099

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production

HEARING DATE:   7/12/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

            In the absence of contrary court order, a civil litigant’s right to discovery is broad. “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action . . . if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010; see Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301.)  If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling response to the demand. (CCP § 2031.300(b); see Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403-404.) However, when responses are served, the proper motion is a motion to compel further responses, which is governed by CCP §§ 2030.300 and 2031.310. A motion to compel further responses must set forth specific facts showing “good cause” justifying the discovery sought by the demand and must be accompanied by a declaration showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court. (CCP §§ 2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).)

 

            A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).) A motion for order compelling further responses “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (CCP § 2031.310(b)(1).) Absent a claim of privilege or attorney work product, the moving party meets its burden of showing good cause by a fact-specific showing of relevance. (See Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) If the moving party has shown good cause for the RPDs, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Id.) 

 

Analysis

           

Defendant Passes, Inc. moves to compel further responses from Plaintiffs FanFixApp and SuperOrdinaryCo USA, Inc. as to the Requests for Production of Documents (RPD) Nos. 2-3 and 26.

 

RPD no. 2: DOCUMENTS sufficient to show the process OR work efforts by which FANFIX learned OR created each of the ALLEGED TRADE SECRETS.

 

Plaintiff objects on the grounds of: a) attorney-client privilege, work-product doctrine, or other privilege or protection; b) trade secret or other confidential reasons, c) that the request as vague and ambiguous as to the “process,” “work efforts,” “learned,” and “created”; and d) the request imposes an undue burden.

 

Plaintiff provides no basis in the record that any of the sought documents would be protected by the attorney client privilege/work product doctrine. Plaintiff has not supplied a privilege log for the Court to evaluate the claims of privilege. Furthermore, the Court finds no ambiguity in the terms “process,” “work efforts,” “learned,” and “created,” which may be construed with their everyday meaning.  Objections for being “overbroad,” “burdensome,” or “oppressive” are only valid when the responding party is asserting an undue burden based on the “quantum of work required” to satisfy the production. (West Pico Furniture Co. v. Superior Court (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.)  Plaintiff does not substantiate their claims of undue burden in responding to this request. Plaintiffs do not provide any estimate as to the time and labor which would be required to respond to the request.  

 

Trade secrets may be disclosed in discovery where the trade secret information is directly relevant and there is no less intrusive means exist. The creation process/efforts of the trade secrets go directly to the heart of the case, i.e., trade secret misappropriation. A plaintiff in a trade secret case has the burden of proving secrecy under Civil Code Section 3426.1. Documents showing the process or work efforts by which Fanfix learned or created the alleged trade secrets would tend to prove or disprove the secret nature of the alleged trade secret. Plaintiff offers no reasonable alternative to disclosure. Thus, the trade secret privilege would not bar disclosure.

 

Plaintiff provides the following substantive response: The trade secrets at issue, as alleged in the operative Complaint and described in the identification provided pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure 2019.210 (the “Identification”), are in large part methodologies and compilations, such that the types of documents called for by the request either do not exist, or require a review of virtually every communication and/or transmittal sent or received by multiple employees, some of which long pre-date the events relevant to this lawsuit, as well as extensive communications with third-party creators, agents, managers, and vendors by which the information at issue was obtained, and the relationships jeopardized or interfered with were established. (Emphasis added.)

 

This is not a code complaint response. It does not state whether Plaintiff will produce documents, that it cannot locate such documents after reasonable inquiry, or that it cannot comply because such documents do not exist. Essentially, Plaintiff re-asserts its overruled burden objections. Moreover, the process by which Plaintiff created or learned the trade secrets is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence concerning the secret nature of the trade secrets. As stated, a plaintiff has the burden of proving secrecy under Civil Code Section 3426.1. Knowing how these trade secrets were created may reveal that some of the information was or was not secret. Accordingly, a further response is required.

 

RPD no. 3: All DOCUMENTS AND THINGS REFERRING OR RELATING TO Internet-based OR other publicly accessible sources from which FANFIX at ANY time has located ANY of the ALLEGED TRADE SECRETS.

 

Response: “Plaintiff does not publish its trade secrets, and to the extent the trade secrets alleged in the operative Complaint and described in the Identification derive at all from publicly available sources, it is well established that such information, when compiled, synthesized, analyzed, or modified, can constitute a trade secret, and otherwise warrants protection as confidential.”

 

Again, Plaintiff does not provide a code-complaint response. Plaintiff’s response suggests that it does not have responsive documents, but does so in an evasive fashion. If Plaintiff denies that such documents ever existed, it must provide a statement which substantively complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230:

 

“A representation of inability to comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.”

 

Accordingly, a further response is required.

 

RPD no. 26: For the period beginning January 1, 2023, REGARDING FANFIX’s products and services, DOCUMENTS AND THINGS RELATING TO FANFIX’s past, present OR future, AND actual OR projected (a) profits; (b) operational AND other costs; OR (c) ANY measure of market share, i ncluding such information by product line if prepared by FANFIX to reflect OR break out such product line information.

 

Plaintiff submitted an objection-only response:

 

Plaintiff objects to this request on the ground and to the extent that it seeks information protected from disclosure by the attorney-client privilege, work-product doctrine, or other privilege or protection. Plaintiff further objects to this request to the extent it seeks information constituting trade secret or other confidential, proprietary, or sensitive business information, the disclosure of which could result in irreparable harm or competitive injury to Plaintiff, or impinge upon the privacy rights of or confidentiality obligations to any third party. Plaintiff further objects to the terms and/or phrases “REGARDING,” “products and services,” “DOCUMENTS AND THINGS,” “RELATING TO,” “past, present OR future,” “actual OR projected,” “profits,” “operational AND other costs,” “measure of market share,” “product line,” and “break out” as vague, ambiguous, unintelligible, and imposing or purporting to impose upon Plaintiff undue burden or burden in excess of that imposed by the Code of Civil Procedure. Plaintiff further objects to this request as grossly overbroad, and duplicative of another in this set. Plaintiff further objects to this request as premature expert discovery governed by the provisions of Code of Civil Procedure sections 2034.010, et seq. Plaintiff further objects on the ground that the request seeks information that is either not relevant to the claims and defenses presently asserted in this action, or has probative value significantly outweighed by the burden of and/or potential harm resulting from its disclosure.

 

Plaintiff fails to substantiate any of their objections with the requisite evidentiary foundation for the same reasons discussed above. This request seeks documents relating to Plaintiffs’ (a) profits; (b) operational and other costs; or (c) any measure of market share. A trade secret plaintiff may recover damages for the “actual loss caused by the misappropriation” and for “unjust enrichment caused by misappropriation that is not taken into account in computing damages for actual loss.” (Civ. Code § 3426.3(a); Ajaxo v. E*Trade Group (2005) 135 C.A.4th 21, 57, 63, 37 C.R.3d 221 [unjustified enrichment theory gave trial court discretion to award royalties in the absence of provable damages]; see Sargon Enterprises, Inc. v. University of Southern Calif. (2012) 55 Cal.4th 747, 773-774 [“damages for the loss of prospective profits are recoverable where the evidence makes reasonably certain their occurrence and extent”].) Documents related to profits, costs, and market share from January 1, 2023, related to Fanfix’s products and services, would be relevant to show Plaintiff’s actual loss stemming from misappropriation and breach of any confidentiality clauses. As such, the request appears reasonably tailored to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence relating to Plaintiff’s lost profits resulting from the alleged trade secret misappropriation.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

Further responses are ordered within 30 days.
