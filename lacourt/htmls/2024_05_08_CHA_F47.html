DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 22CHCV01321    Hearing Date: May 8, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 5/8/24                                                                       TRIAL DATE: 8/12/24

Case #22CHCV01321

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

(Requests for Production of Documents, Set 1)

 

Motion filed on 12/15/23.

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Oliva Morena Posada

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order compelling Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set 1, Nos. 22-23, 25-29, 32, 45-46 and 48-49.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP in the amount of $2,125.00.

 

RULING: The motion is granted as set forth below.

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff Olivia Morena Posada aka Olivia M. Posada’s (Plaintiff) lease/purchase of a 2022 Honda CR-V (the Vehicle.  Plaintiff contends that the Vehicle is defective and Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (Honda) has been unable to service or repair the Vehicle to conform to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of opportunities.  Plaintiff further contends that despite the foregoing, Honda has failed to promptly replace/repurchase the Vehicle as required under the Song-Beverly Act.    

 

As a result, on 12/7/22, Plaintiff filed this action against Honda for: (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty and (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty.  Honda filed an answer to the complaint on 1/3/23. 

 

On 3/14/23, Plaintiff propounded Requests for Production of Documents, Set 1, on Honda.  (Tran Decl., Ex.A).  On 4/25/23, Honda served responses to the requests.  (Id., Ex.B).  Plaintiff contends that in response to Requests 22-23, 25-29, 32, 45-46 and 48-49 Honda failed to properly respond/object.  Despite meet and confer efforts and several extensions of time to file a motion to compel further responses (with the last extension ending on 12/15/23), Honda failed to provide further responses.  (Tran Decl., Ex.C-E).    

 

On 12/15/23, Plaintiff filed and served the instant motion seeking an order compelling Honda to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set 1, Nos. 22-23, 25-29, 32, 45-46 and 48-49.  Additionally, Plaintiff requests an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP in the amount of $2,125.00.  Honda has not opposed or otherwise responded to the motion. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the action or to the determination of any motion made in the action, if the matter itself is admissible or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  See CCP 2017.010.  Doubts as to relevance are generally resolved in favor of allowing discovery.  Colonial Life & Acc. Ins. Co. (1982) 31 C3d 785, 790.

 

CCP 2031.310(a) provides:

 

“On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

 

The subject discovery requests seek Honda’s training materials and policies and procedures for complying with the Song Beverly Act’s requirements (Requests 22-23, 25-29, 32), all documents evidencing complaints by owners of 2022 Honda CR-V vehicles regarding any of the complaints the subject vehicle was presented to Honda’s authorized repair facilities for repair during the warranty period and all documents evidencing warranty repairs to 2022 Honda CR-V vehicles regarding any of the components that Honda or Honda’s authorized repair facilities performed repairs on under warranty (Requests 45-46), and Honda’s application and any response to any application for certification of its third party dispute resolution process by the Department of Consumer Affairs (Requests 48-49).  Honda has objected to the requests on the grounds of ambiguity, overbreadth, undue burden, harassment, unintelligibility, relevance, privacy, trade secret, attorney-client privilege, and requires Honda to respond on behalf of another entity.    

 

The information/documents sought is/are relevant to Plaintiff’s claims under the Song-Beverly Act because they will show whether others experienced the same issues Plaintiff experienced with the Vehicle.  See Donlen (2013) 217 CA4th 138, 154.  Additionally, the documents  may show the willfulness of Honda’s refusal to repurchase or replace the Vehicle based on its policies and procedures which is also relevant to the issue of civil penalties.  See Johnson (2005) 35 C4th 1191, 1198-1199; Oregel (1995) 90 CA4th 1094; Kwan (1994) 23 CA4th 174, 186; Civil Code 1794(c).    

 

Similarly, documents regarding similar customer complaints that Plaintiff had with the Vehicle from owners of other 2022 Honda CR-V vehicles and the same warranty repairs made to the  Vehicle on other 2022 Honda CR-V vehicles (Requests 45-46) may show that the complaints Plaintiff had and the repairs made to the Vehicle were widespread, and that Honda should have used that information in evaluating whether the Vehicle qualified for repurchase or replacement under the Song-Beverly Act.

 

In addition to being relevant to Plaintiff’s claims for breach of the Song Beverly Act because they will assist in establishing the existence of a defect or nonconformity in the Vehicle and Honda’s failure/inability to repair that defect or nonconformity in the Vehicle, the documents may refute Honda’s affirmative defenses, such as lack of maintenance, misuse or abuse, or modifications to the Vehicle being the cause of the nonconformities to warranty that Plaintiff complained of.

 

In response to certain of the requests (Requests 22-23, 25-29, 32), Honda has also included an objection based on attorney-client privilege without providing the necessary information/ privilege log for Plaintiff or the Court to evaluate the privilege claim.  See CCP 2031.240(c).  As such, it cannot be determined whether Honda is properly withholding documents based on the attorney-client privilege in response to these requests.    

 

If documents regarding Honda’s third-party dispute resolution process in California that is certified by the Department of Consumer Affairs (Requests 48-49) do not exist, Plaintiff may be entitled to civil penalties without having to prove Honda’s willful violation of the Song-Beverly Act.  Civil Code 1794(e); Jernigan (1994) 24 CA4th 488, 491-493.

 

Honda has failed to establish the validity of its objections to the subject requests.  Contrary to Honda’s assertions the requests are not vague, ambiguous, overbroad or unduly burdensome. 

 

Plaintiff is also entitled to an award of sanctions against Honda and its counsel, Clark Hill LLP, in the amount of $1,850.00 (4 hours for associate to prepare motion at $250/hour + 1 hour for review of motion at $395/hour + hour to prepare for hearing and appear at $395/hour + $60 filing fee) for their failure to comply with their discovery obligations. CCP 2031.310(h); (Tran Decl. ¶¶13-14).   

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted.  Honda is ordered to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set 1, Nos. Nos. 22-23, 25-29, 32, 45-46 and 48-49 within 30 days.  Additionally, sanctions are imposed against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP, in the amount of $1,850.00, payable within 30 days.

Case #22CHCV01321

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES

(Special Interrogatories, Set 1)

 

Motion filed on 12/15/23.

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Oliva Morena Posada

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc.

NOTICE: ok

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order compelling Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set 1, Nos. 10, 46, 47 and 48.    Additionally, Plaintiff requests an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP in the amount of $2,430.00.

 

RULING: The motion is granted as set forth below.

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff Olivia Morena Posada aka Olivia M. Posada’s (Plaintiff) lease/purchase of a 2022 Honda CR-V (the Vehicle.  Plaintiff contends that the Vehicle is defective and Defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (Honda) has been unable to service or repair the Vehicle to conform to the applicable express warranties after a reasonable number of opportunities.  Plaintiff further contends that despite the foregoing, Honda has failed to promptly replace/repurchase the Vehicle as required under the Song-Beverly Act.    

 

As a result, on 12/7/22, Plaintiff filed this action against Honda for: (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty and (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty.  Honda filed an answer to the complaint on 1/3/23. 

 

On 3/14/23, Plaintiff propounded Special Interrogatories, Set 1, on Honda.  (Tran Decl., Ex.A).  On 4/25/23, Honda served responses to the interrogatories.  (Id., Ex.B).  Plaintiff contends that Honda’s responses to Special Interrogatories 10, 46, 47 and 48 are deficient.  Despite meet and confer efforts and several extensions of time to file a motion to compel further responses (with the last extension ending on 12/15/23), Honda failed to provide further responses.  (Tran Decl., Ex.C-E).    

 

On 12/15/23, Plaintiff filed and served the instant motion seeking an order compelling Honda to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set 1, Nos. 10, 46, 47 and 48.    Additionally, Plaintiff requests an order imposing sanctions against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP in the amount of $2,430.00.  Honda has not opposed or otherwise responded to the motion. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the action or to the determination of any motion made in the action, if the matter itself is admissible or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  See CCP 2017.010.  Doubts as to relevance are generally resolved in favor of allowing discovery.  Colonial Life & Acc. Ins. Co. (1982) 31 C3d 785, 790.

 

CCP 2030.300(a) provides:

 

“(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

 

To establish his claim under the Song-Beverly Act, Plaintiff must prove, among other things, that the Vehicle contained a defect, covered by Honda’s warranty, which substantially impaired its use, value, or safety and which Ford was unable to repair after a reasonable number of attempts.  See Civil Code 790 et seq.; CACI No. 3201.  The information sought in the subject interrogatories is relevant to Plaintiff’s claims because it would tend to show whether a widespread defect or nonconformity, of the same type that Plaintiff experienced, exists in these types of vehicles.  The information could also assist in refuting Honda’s affirmative defenses which claim that Plaintiff and/or others misused or abused the Vehicle or engaged in unauthorized or unreasonable use which caused the problems with the Vehicle.  If a substantial number of other owners of the same type of vehicle experienced the same defects and nonconformities, such information would tend to show that the defects claimed in Plaintiff’s Vehicle were not caused by misuse, abuse, or unreasonable use.

 

The Special Interrogatories at issue are relevant Plaintiff’s claims as they seek information regarding Honda’s contentions as to why the Vehicle does not qualify for repurchase (Special Interrogatory 10) and the five symptoms and components with the highest repair rates per thousand for 2022 Honda CR-V vehicles (Special Interrogatories 46-48).  Specifically, Special Interrogatory 10 seeks information regarding Honda’s contention that the Vehicle did not qualify for a repurchase.  Honda’s reliance on CCP 2030.230 regarding production of documents in response to the interrogatory is insufficient as it leaves Plaintiff to guess which specific documents contain the facts supporting Honda’s contention that the Vehicle does not qualify for a repurchase under the Song Beverly Act.

 

Special Interrogatories 46-48 seek information regarding the five symptoms and components with the highest repair rates per thousand for 2022 Honda CR-V vehicles.  Information about other similar vehicle complaints may show the existence of a widespread defect or non-conformity, or show when it arose, and may also show Honda’s knowledge of wide-spread problems with the vehicle and Honda’s failure to act despite this knowledge.  Information in response to these interrogatories may show that the symptoms exhibited by the Vehicle were among the most commonly occurring problems with this year, make, and model vehicle.  Information showing the top five components and symptoms at issue, if they are the same as the problems experienced by Plaintiff, could show that Honda had knowledge about a widespread defect or nonconformity and still failed to act.  Such information is relevant to proving whether a civil penalty is warranted because it would tend to show whether Honda made its determination not to repurchase Plaintiff’s vehicle despite having information indicating that these types of vehicles contained a defect or nonconformity.   See Lukather (2010) 181 CA4th 1041, 1051.

 

Honda has failed to meet its burden of justifying its objections in response to this motion to compel further responses.  See Fairmont Insurance Co. (2000) 22 C4th 245, 255; Coy (1962) 58 C2d 210, 220-221.

 

Plaintiff is also entitled to an award of sanctions against Honda and its counsel, Clark Hill LLP, in the amount of $1,600.00 (3 hours for associate to prepare motion at $250/hour + 1 hour for review of motion at $395/hour + hour to prepare for hearing and appear at $395/hour + $60 filing fee) CCP 203.300(d); (Tran Decl. ¶¶9-10).   

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted.  Honda is ordered to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set 1, Nos. 10 and 46-48 within 30 days.  Additionally, sanctions are imposed against Honda and its counsel of record, Clark Hill LLP, in the amount of $1,600.00, payable within 30 days.
