DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00355    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Chelsey Binz filed 22LBCV00355 against Defendants, LBMMC, et al. for damages arising out of complications that arose during her labor and delivery.  The complications ultimately caused damage to her child, Indiana Rayne Van Den Oever.   

 

22LBCV00355 has been consolidated with 23LBCV00839, in which the minor child brings her own claims arising out of the birth. 

 

2.     Petition to Approve Compromise of a Minor

The parties have entered into a global settlement of the action, pursuant to which LBMMC and Drs. Bhatia and Askari will pay the minor child a confidential amount, which is detailed in the petition, filed under seal, as well as the proposed order thereon.    

 

The Court has reviewed the settlement and finds it is fair and reasonable.  Counsel seeks to recover 33% of the total settlement in attorneys’ fees.  The Court typically restricts fee awards in cases involving minors to 25% of the gross settlement.  The Court is, however, aware that this case has been pending for over two years and has been heavily litigated, including numerous summary judgment motions, such that the Court finds the 33% allocation to be fair and reasonable. 

 

Pursuant to CRC 7.952, unless excused by the Court, both the petitioner and the minor child must appear and testify before the Court grants the petition.  The minor child is three years old and severely disabled; the Court therefore finds good cause to excuse her from appearing.  The Court will grant the petition if Petitioner appears and testifies to the satisfaction of the Court.   


Case Number: 23LBCV00385    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Shirin El Sharkawi filed this action against Defendants, Kelvin Anderson, Sr. and Rhone Love, as well as Nominal Defendant, World Famous VIP Records, LLC for breach of contract and related claims.  Plaintiff filed the original complaint on 2/27/23.  Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint on 5/17/23.  Defendants demurred to the FAC, and the parties were ordered to meet and confer in good faith.  The meet and confer efforts resulted in the filing of the Second Amended Complaint, which Plaintiff filed on 2/01/24.  On 6/11/24, the Court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the SAC with leave to amend on the ground that the FAC revealed the action is barred by the statute of limitations, the SAC omitted the fatal allegations without explanation, and Plaintiff was obligated to “plead around” those fatal allegations by way of a Third Amended Complaint.  Plaintiff filed the TAC on 6/21/24.  Defendant demurred to the TAC, but prior to the hearing, Plaintiff filed her 4AC.  The Court, on 12/03/24, noted that filing a 4AC without leave of court was improper, but permitted the pleading to stand and specially set a hearing on a demurrer to the 4AC for 1/09/25. 

 

  1. Demurrer to Fourth Amended Complaint

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares the parties met and conferred telephonically, but were unable to resolve the issues presented by way of the demurrer.  The Court will rule on the demurrer on its merits.

 

c.     Prior Ruling on Statute of Limitations

Defendant demurs to the 4AC on the ground that it is barred by the statute of limitations.  The Court has substantively considered this issue once previously, in connection with its ruling on the demurrer to the SAC.  The Court ruled as follows:

Defendants also argue the SAC is barred by the four-year statute of limitations.  The parties agree that the four-year statute of limitations applies, and also agree that the SOL begins to run when the agreement is breached. 

Defendants argue the SAC establishes the agreement was breached immediately, because Plaintiff alleges the agreement called for her to receive payments, and she admits, at ¶101, that she was NEVER paid anything per the parties’ agreement.  Alternatively, they argue it was breached no later than December of 2017, when Plaintiff alleges Anderson acted as sole owner (¶57 of SAC, ¶¶65, 68 of FAC). 

It is not clear, from the face of the SAC, whether the entity defendant was making any profit to which Plaintiff was entitled, when such profit was generated, when it was due to Plaintiff and not paid, etc.  The face of the SAC, therefore, does not show a bar based on the failure to make payments.

¶57 alleges Defendant breached the parties’ agreement by claiming exclusive ownership of VIP Records.  No date is alleged.  ¶¶65 and 68 of the FAC allege:

In December 2017, funds from the contract with the City of Long Beach and personal funds of Ms. El Sharkawi secured a lease-to-own building with VIP as a tenant. Both partners signed the Agreement. Ms. El Sharkawi was tasked with licensing the building with the City of Long Beach and opening its doors. Mr. Anderson Sr. was to move his store from the current location and contribute to the rental, with revenues to be used to maintain the building.

Mr. Anderson did not relocate his store and continued generating revenue from his existing location, and created challenges for Ms. El Sharkawi, who had to invest additional funds to keep the new building operational.

These allegations have been omitted from the SAC.  Pursuant to Lockton v. O’Rourke (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1051, 1061, a plaintiff has a duty to “plead around” admissions made in earlier iterations of her complaint; if she fails to do so, the earlier admissions are “read into” the amended complaint.  The above allegations appear to make clear that there was a material breach of the parties’ agreement more than four years prior to the filing of the complaint. 

Notably, Plaintiff did not address this argument in opposition to the demurrer.  The Court is inclined to sustain the demurrer on the ground that the entire complaint is barred by the four-year statute of limitations.  The Court notes that this is the first ruling on a pleading challenge, as Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint before Defendants responded to the original complaint, and filed her SAC after meet and confer efforts in connection with the pleading challenge to the original complaint.  The Court will hear oral argument, at the hearing, concerning whether to grant leave to amend or not.  The Court will grant leave to amend if and only if Plaintiff can plead around her earlier allegations in a manner that saves the complaint from the statute of limitations. 

 

d.     Analysis

As an initial note, Defendant consistently refers to the statute of limitations as being “tolled” and contends Plaintiff’s causes of action are “tolled.”  Defendant misuses this word.  Black's Law Dictionary defines "tolling" as "to abate" or "to stop the running of (the statutory period)" (Don Johnson Productions, Inc. v. Rysher Entertainment, LLC, 209 Cal.App.4th 919 (2012)). This definition is consistent with the general legal understanding that tolling refers to the suspension of a statute of limitations, meaning the clock stops running for a certain period and resumes once the tolling period ends (Don Johnson Productions, Inc. v. Rysher Entertainment, LLC, 209 Cal.App.4th 919 (2012)).

 

In any event, Defendant establishes that Plaintiff has omitted, from the TAC and 4AC, any allegations about Defendant’s failure to relocate the store and her resultant obligation to invest additional funds in the store.  Plaintiff argues, in opposition to the demurrer, that the 4AC is not barred by the statute of limitations because (a) a breach does not occur upon signing a contract, but upon breach, (b) Plaintiff has the right to treat an anticipatory breach as a breach and immediately seek damages, or to wait until the time for performance arises and treat nonperformance as breach, (c) when there are successive breaches, Plaintiff has the right to wait to elect to treat the breach as terminating the contract, (d) the continuing violation doctrine applies, and (e) the breach did not occur until April of 2019. 

 

Plaintiff’s first argument is that a breach does not occur upon signing a contract, but instead upon breach of the contract.  This is, of course, true.  Defendant’s point, however, is that the original complaint affirmatively alleged that Defendant breached the contract by failing to relocate the store, which was supposed to happen in December of 2017. 

 

Plaintiff’s second argument is that Plaintiff was not obligated to sue based on anticipatory breach and was permitted to wait until actual nonperformance occurred.  An "anticipatory breach" of contract, according to Black's Law Dictionary, occurs when the promisor repudiates the contract before the promisor's performance under the contract is due. This repudiation can be either express or implied (Hewlett-Packard Co. v. Oracle Corp., 65 Cal.App.5th 506 (2021)). An express repudiation is a clear, positive, unequivocal refusal to perform, while an implied repudiation results from conduct where the promisor puts it out of his power to perform, making substantial performance of his promise impossible (Mammoth Lakes Land Acquisition, LLC v. Town of Mammoth Lakes, 191 Cal.App.4th 435 (2010)).  It is unclear why Plaintiff raises the doctrine of anticipatory repudiation.  Defendant’s argument is that Plaintiff’s original complaint alleged actual nonperformance in December of 2017, and Plaintiff failed to plead around this allegation by way of her subsequent complaints.  There is no anticipatory breach at issue.

 

Plaintiff’s third arguments is that there were multiple breaches and Plaintiff was not obligated to sue upon the first breach.  Plaintiff relies on Romano v. Rockwell Internat., Inc. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 479, 489 to support her position.  Romano, however, considered the anticipatory breach doctrine, and is of no import for the reasons discussed above.  The Court has researched the multiple breaches doctrine independently, and finds that it applies in the context of installment contracts or periodic payments.  See, for example, White v. Moriarty (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1290, 1299, holding that, when a promissory note calls for periodic payments, the statute of limitations on each such payment starts running whenever that payment is missed.  Thus, if a defendant fails to make numerous payments, some more than four years prior to suit and some less than four years prior to suit, the plaintiff can recover on the missed payments within the past four years, but not those more than four years prior to filing suit.

 

This issue is a bit complicated.  In this case, Plaintiff alleges Defendant breached the contract in 2017 by failing to relocate the business.  Defendant then commenced breaching the contract, repeatedly, by failing to make payments.  Would the 2017 breach bar the entire action, or would Plaintiff be permitted to sue for missed payments that occurred within four years prior to filing the lawsuit?  Neither party meaningfully briefs this issue.  Because Defendant has the burden to show, on demurrer, that the 4AC is barred, the Court is inclined to overrule the demurrer and permit Plaintiff to sue on any breaches that occurred within four years prior to the filing of the original complaint. 

 

In an abundance of caution, the Court has considered Plaintiff’s fourth and fifth arguments.  Plaintiff’s fourth argument is that the continuing violations doctrine saves his case.  Plaintiff relies on Komorova v. National Credit Acceptance, Inc. (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 324, 343.  Komorova discusses the continuing violation doctrine, which applies in the context of workplace harassment and other similar tort suits.  Plaintiff cites nothing applying the doctrine in the breach of contract context, and the Court knows of no authority permitting it to be applied in that manner. 

 

Plaintiff’s final argument is that the breach occurred in 2019.  This argument, however, ignores the allegations of the original complaint, which form the basis of the demurrer. 

 

e.     Conclusion

The demurrer to the breach of contract cause of action is overruled.  Defendant failed to meet his burden to show that, even if a breach did occur in 2017, it would bar a claim for breaches that occurred within four years prior to the filing of the complaint.  This ruling is without prejudice to Defendant’s right to re-raise the issue at another stage of litigation, providing on point authority if he chooses to do so.

Plaintiff, Jane Doe A.R., a minor, by and through her GAL, Jasmina Roman, filed this action against Defendants, Denny’s, Inc., LB DENN, LLC, and Jimmie Kirkwood for damages arising out of an assault that occurred in a Denny’s bathroom.  The specifics of the assault will be discussed below.  The original complaint remains operative, and includes causes of action for:

Assault – Kirkwood

Battery – Kirkwood

IIED – Kirkwood

Negligence – all defendants

Premises Liability – Denny’s and DENN

 

Plaintiff dismissed Denny’s, Inc. from the complaint, leaving LB DENN, LLC (“DENN”) and Kirkwood as the remaining defendants. 

 

2.     Motion for Summary Judgment

a.     Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.) 

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

e.     General Law Governing a Restaurant’s Liability for Third Party Criminal Acts

A restaurant's liability for a criminal attack on its premises generally hinges on the foreseeability of the criminal conduct and the measures taken by the restaurant to prevent such incidents. The key principles derived from the search results are as follows:

1.         Foreseeability of Criminal Conduct: The restaurant's duty to protect patrons from criminal attacks depends significantly on whether the criminal conduct was foreseeable. This foreseeability can be established by prior similar incidents or other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults at the location (Tan v. Arnel Management Co., 170 Cal.App.4th 1087 (2009)). For example, in Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, the court held that heightened foreseeability was satisfied by showing prior similar criminal incidents or other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults in that location (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, 36 Cal.4th 224 (2005)).

2.         Duty to Take Preventative Measures: If heightened foreseeability is established, the restaurant may have a duty to take reasonable measures to protect patrons. This can include providing security guards or other preventative measures. However, if there is no evidence of heightened foreseeability, the restaurant may not be required to take such burdensome measures (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, 36 Cal.4th 224 (2005)). In Williams v. Fremont Corners, Inc., the court found that general awareness of potential criminal conduct was insufficient to establish a duty to provide security guards without evidence of prior similar incidents (Williams v. Fremont Corners, Inc., 37 Cal.App.5th 654 (2019)).

3.         Special Relationship and Reasonable Measures: Restaurants have a special relationship with their patrons, which imposes a duty to take reasonable measures to protect them against foreseeable criminal attacks. This duty includes taking appropriate action during ongoing criminal conduct to protect patrons (Morris v. De La Torre, 36 Cal.4th 260 (2005)). In Morris v. De La Torre, the court emphasized that the special-relationship-based duty extended to the parking lot and required reasonable measures to protect patrons (Morris v. De La Torre, 36 Cal.4th 260 (2005)).

4.         Minimally Burdensome Measures: Even in the absence of heightened foreseeability, a restaurant may still be required to take minimally burdensome measures to address imminent dangers. For instance, in Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, the court noted that the bar had a duty to respond to unfolding events by taking simple and minimally burdensome measures to address imminent danger (Ericson v. Federal Express Corp., 162 Cal.App.4th 1291 (2008)).

 

In summary, a restaurant's liability for a criminal attack on its premises depends on the foreseeability of the attack, the existence of a special relationship with patrons, and whether the restaurant took reasonable and appropriate measures to prevent or respond to the attack.

 

f.      Facts

Most of the facts relating to the incident are undisputed.  Plaintiff was seven at the time of the incident.  She, her mother, her two brothers, and her mother’s boyfriend went to the subject Denny’s shortly after midnight on the night of the incident.  Shortly before one a.m., Plaintiff needed to use the restroom.  Her mother’s boyfriend escorted her to the restroom, but then returned to the booth where the family was seated.  During the approximately nine minutes that Plaintiff was in the restroom, Kirkwood entered the restroom, entered her stall, and ordered her to perform oral sex on him; Plaintiff refused, and Kirkwood punched her in the face.  The incident concluded when another woman entered the stall, saw Kirkwood masturbating, and reported the ongoing incident to Denny’s staff, who broke down the door to the stall so she could get out. 

 

Prior incidents, as will be discussed below, play a role in determining whether or not Denny’s had an obligation to prevent the crime.  The basic facts relating to prior incidents are also not disputed.  There had been prior incidents outside of the restroom, including fights between customers and drunken disorderly incidents.  There had been prior incidents inside the restroom, including breaking the urinal, breaking windows, breaking soap dispensers, graffiti on the walls, and lying on the restroom floor.  Prior to the incident, Denny’s had security guards because homeless people regularly used the restroom; at some point Denny’s ceased using security guards and instead used a coin system, whereby patrons would obtain a special coin from employees in order to use the restroom; the coin was the same for men and women. 

 

Kirkwood was a paying customer on the date of the incident, and used a coin to enter the restroom.

 

g.     Analysis

Defendant’s primary argument is that there were no prior similar assaults at the property, and therefore Defendant owed Plaintiff no duty to prevent the subject attack.  As noted above, the issue of duty and breach turns on whether the incident was foreseeable, and a sliding scale is used based on the burden that would be placed on the defendant if the proposed security measures were deemed to be legally required.  By way of example, a low level of foreseeability will give rise to an obligation to fix a broken lock on a door, but only a very high level of foreseeability (prior similar acts) will give rise to an obligation to hire security guards.  This is so because fixing a broken door is minimally burdensome, while hiring security guards is extremely burdensome. 

 

Thus, in order to analyze this motion, the Court must first determine what security measures Plaintiff is proposing Defendant should have employed.  Plaintiff’s complaint is vague in this regard.  At ¶¶35-36 of her complaint, Plaintiff alleges Defendant had a duty to control Kirkwood and protect Plaintiff from harm.  At ¶¶46-47, Plaintiff alleges Defendant had a duty to control, monitor, and/or supervise Kirkwood.  In response to SROGs, Plaintiff contended Defendant was liable because it failed to make reasonable inspection of the premises to guard against possible dangers and failed to make the property reasonably safe for intended or foreseeable users and uses, including to warn of latent or concealed dangers; it is unclear what security measures Plaintiff is suggesting by way of this response. 

 

To the extent Plaintiff is alleging Defendant was obligated to prevent the attack by employing security guards, Defendant met its moving burden to show there were no prior similar incidents, and Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of material fact in this regard.  Numerous cases hold that, in order to require a landlord to employ security guards, there must have been prior highly similar incidents. 

 

A restaurant is generally required to provide security guards to prevent an assault only under circumstances where there is a high degree of foreseeability of such criminal acts. This high degree of foreseeability is typically established through evidence of prior similar incidents of violent crime on the premises or other serious indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of violent criminal assaults.  The Supreme Court of California has consistently held that a high degree of foreseeability is required to impose a duty on a business owner to hire security guards. This foreseeability is rarely proven without prior similar incidents of violent crime on the premises.  (Tan v. Arnel Management Co., 170 Cal.App.4th 1087 (2009)).

 

The existence of prior similar incidents of violent crime on the premises is crucial to establish the requisite foreseeability. For example, in the case of  Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, the court found that the bar proprietor had no duty to provide security guards because there was no evidence of heightened foreseeability in the form of prior similar incidents or other indications of a reasonably foreseeable risk of a violent criminal assault on the bar's premises (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, 36 Cal.4th 224 (2005)). Similarly, in Williams v. Fremont Corners, Inc., the court concluded that general knowledge of the possibility of violent criminal conduct was not enough to create a duty to provide security guards without evidence of prior similar incidents (Hanouchian v. Steele, 51 Cal.App.5th 99 (2020)).

 

The courts have recognized that the monetary and social costs of hiring security guards are significant, and the obligation to provide adequate patrols to deter criminal conduct is not well defined. Therefore, imposing such a duty requires a high degree of foreseeability (Delgado v. Trax Bar & Grill, 36 Cal.4th 224 (2005)), (Hanouchian v. Steele, 51 Cal.App.5th 99 (2020)).

 

In summary, a restaurant is required to provide security guards to prevent an assault only when there is a high degree of foreseeability of such criminal acts, typically established through evidence of prior similar incidents of violent crime on the premises. Without such evidence, the duty to hire security guards is generally not imposed.

 

Plaintiff failed to show there had been any prior similar incidents.  Plaintiff’s cited incidents involve either patrons fighting with one another outside the restrooms and/or patrons committing vandalism inside the restrooms.  None of the incidents involves pedophilia, or indeed any form of sexual assault.  They therefore fail to meet the test detailed above.

 

Plaintiff appears, in opposition to the motion, to be making the alternative argument that Defendant had an obligation to ensure the coins for the restrooms were different for men and women, such that Kirkwood could have never entered Plaintiff’s restroom.  Plaintiff notes that Defendant did so after the incident.  This is, of course, evidence of a subsequent remedial measure, which is not admissible to show fault.  Evidence Code §1151.

 

Using different coins would, of course, be minimally burdensome.  The Court’s concern, however, is that incidents of sexual assault on minors happen to both boys and girls, and holding that there is a legal duty to provide different coins seems to suggest that there is a duty to prevent a man from sexually assaulting a girl (or a woman from sexually assaulting a boy), but no duty to prevent a same-gender sex crime from occurring.  This is nonsensical and therefore liability cannot be based on the failure to provide different coins for each restroom. 

 

h.     Conclusion

Defendant met its moving burden to show there had been no prior similar incidents, such that there was no duty to provide security guards.  Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of material fact in this regard, and failed to show Defendant had a duty to provide any other security measures that would have prevented the subject attack.  The motion for summary judgment is therefore granted.

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Jose A. Aguayo and Eufrosina Hernandez filed this action against Defendant,American Honda Motor Co., Inc. for violations of the Song-Beverly Act, breach of implied warranty, and fraudulent inducement – concealment. 

 

  1. Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s attorney but they were unable to resolve the dispute.  Defense Counsel fails to state whether the meet and confer efforts were in person or by telephone, as required by the Code.  Defense Counsel must so clarify in the future in connection with this and other actions.

 

c.     Parties’ Positions

Defendant demurs to the entire complaint on the ground that it is barred by the statute of limitations.  Defendant demurs to the fraud-based cause of action, contending is barred by the statute of limitations, does not allege a transactional relationship giving rise to a duty to disclose, and is precluded by the economic loss doctrine. 

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the complaint is timely, all essential elements are pled, a transactional relationship is not required under California law to find a duty to disclose, and the economic loss doctrine does not act as a bar to their claims.    

 

d.     Statute of Limitations

Commercial Code provides for a four-year statute of limitations for Song-Beverly Actions.  CCP §338(d) provides for a three-year statute of limitations on a fraud claim. 

Defendant argues Plaintiffs allege they purchased the vehicle in 2018, but did not file suit until 2024; Defendant contends there are no specific facts pled in the complaint to show delayed discovery.  Plaintiffs argue their allegation, at ¶23 of their complaint, that they did not discover Defendant’s wrongful conduct until “shortly before filing this Complaint as the Subject Vehicle continued to exhibit symptoms of defects following Defendant’s unsuccessful attempts to repair them.” 

 

Plaintiffs’ complaint does not include any allegations concerning whether and when defects were first discovered, whether and when Plaintiffs took the vehicle in for servicing, what happened when the vehicle was presented to the dealership for servicing, etc.  Plaintiffs must so plead, because the complaint appears to be barred by the statute of limitations, which gives rise to an obligation to plead specific facts showing the complaint is not time-barred.  See Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 808.

 

The demurrer on this ground is sustained with leave to amend.

 

  1. Fraud

i.              Duty of Disclosure

Defendant’s second argument is that Plaintiff failed to plead fraud with specificity.  Ordinarily, fraud claims are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading which necessitate pleading facts showing “how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered." (Stansfield v. Starkey (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 59, 73.)  However, the specificity rule is intended to apply to affirmative misrepresentations and not to concealment. (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 89 Cal.Rptr.3d 659).  Notably, the idea that a plaintiff could allege who concealed something, where the thing was concealed, when it was concealed, etc., is nonsensical; the nature of the claim is a failure to speak, which cannot possibly include these details.

  1. Transactional Relationship

The elements of a fraud claim based on concealment are: “(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 310–311). 

 

There are “four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.” (Id. at 311). If a fiduciary relationship does not exist, but the latter three circumstances are present, Plaintiff must still show “the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant from which a duty to disclose can arise.” (Id. at 311).

 

            A duty to disclose may arise as a result of a transaction between the parties. However, the transaction "must necessarily arise from direct dealings between the plaintiff and the defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large." (Bigler-Engler at 312 [noting that the duty of a manufacturer to warn consumers of a product’s hazards and faults applies in the context of strict products liability actions but does not apply in a suit for intentional misrepresentation.]).

 

            The Court is aware of the Court of Appeals’ decision in Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, wherein the plaintiffs sued Nissan North America, Inc. (Nissan), alleging a transmission defect.  The plaintiffs alleged claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and a common law fraud claim alleging that Nissan fraudulently concealed the defects and induced them to purchase a car. (Dhital at 838).

 

            The California Supreme Court granted review of Dhital on February 1, 2023, however, the request for depublication was denied. (Dhital v. Nissan North America (Cal. 2023) 304 Cal.Rptr.3d 82, 83). On 12/18/24, the Supreme Court dismissed the petition for review.  Dhital is therefore binding.

 

            The Dhital court concluded that allegations were sufficient to support the existence of a buyer-seller relationship between the parties as “Plaintiffs alleged that they bought the car from a Nissan dealership, that Nissan backed the car with an express warranty, and that Nissan's authorized dealerships are [the manufacturer’s] agents for purposes of the sale of Nissan vehicles to consumers. In light of these allegations, we decline to hold plaintiffs’ claim is barred on the ground there was no relationship requiring Nissan to disclose known defects." (Dhital at 845).

 

ii.             Economic Loss Doctrine

Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s damage claim for fraudulent concealment is barred by the economic loss rule which provides that “[i]n general, there is no recovery in tort for negligently inflicted ‘purely economic losses,’ meaning financial harm unaccompanied by physical or property damage.” Dhital, supra 721-722. However, Dhital held that the tort of fraudulent inducement by concealment is excepted from the economic loss rules since "the duty that gives rise to tort liability is either completely independent of the contract or arises from conduct which is both intentional and intended to harm. (Dhital at 828). Accordingly, since Plaintiff’s claim for inducement by concealment is adequately alleged, it is not barred by the economic loss rule. 

 

  1. Conclusion

The demurrer to the entire complaint on the ground that it is barred by the statute of limitations is sustained with leave to amend.  The demurrer is otherwise overruled. 

 

  1. Motion to Strike

Defendant moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages from the complaint.  The prayer for punitive damages is made in connection with the fraud cause of action only.  A well-pled cause of action for fraud supports a claim for punitive damages.  The motion to strike is moot in light of the ruling on the demurrer, but the Court notes that, if Plaintiffs are able to plead around the statute of limitations, the prayer for punitive damages will survive.    

 

  1. Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend as detailed above.  The motion to strike is moot.  Plaintiffs must file an amended complaint within twenty days.  Defendant must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter.  If Defendant intends to challenge the amended pleading, Defense Counsel must file a meet and confer declaration establishing the parties met and conferred in person or by telephone, as required per Code. 

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: BC685348    Hearing Date: January 9, 2025    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Justin, Sarah, and Sawyer Morgenthaler filed this action against Defendants, Juan Antonio Haromartinez, Turbo Express, Inc., General Motors LLC, General Motors Company, General Motors Holding LLC, Enterprise Rent-a-Car Company of Los Angeles, LLC, and EAN Holdings, LLC for damages arising out of an automobile accident. 

 

  1. Motion to Seal

On 8/14/24, Lear Corporation filed this motion to seal, setting it for hearing on 1/09/25.  Lear Corporation seeks an order that the documents Plaintiffs filed in opposition to Lear’s summary judgment motion remain redacted and sealed in the court file.  Lear contends these documents were filed conditionally under seal in light of the parties’ confidentiality stipulation, and the documents include trade secret protected information, such that they should remain under seal.

 

a.             Law Governing Motions to Seal

(1) Scope of Rules. C.R.C., Rules 2.550 and 2.551 apply to trial court records sealed or proposed to be sealed by court order. They do not apply to discovery motions and records in discovery proceedings, but do apply to discovery materials used at trial or submitted for adjudication of matters other than discovery proceedings. (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(a).)

 

(2) Records Presumed To Be Open. “Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open.” (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(c).)

 

(3) Findings Required To Seal Records.  The court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it expressly finds facts that establish all of the following:

 

(a) “There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record.” (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(d)(1).)

 

            (b) “The overriding interest supports sealing the record.” (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(d)(2).)

 

(c) “A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed.” (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(d)(3).)

 

            (d) “The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored.” (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(d)(4).)

 

            (e) “No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(d)(5).)

 

(4) What Constitutes an “Overriding Interest?”  The Advisory Committee Comment to C.R.C., Rule 2.550 notes that the rule leaves the determination of what constitutes an “overriding interest” to case law and that, in appropriate circumstances, courts have found various statutory privileges, trade secrets, and privacy interests to be “overriding interests.”

 

(5) Court Approval Required. A record may not be filed under seal without a court order or based solely on the agreement or stipulation of the parties. (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(a).)

 

(6) Procedure for Confidential Documents.  A party who files or intends to file documents disclosed during discovery that are subject to a confidentiality agreement or a protective order but does not intend to request sealing must (a) lodge the unredacted records and other documents that disclose their contents conditionally under seal, (b) file redacted copies that do not disclose the contents, and (c) give written notice to the party who produced the records that the records and documents lodged under seal will be placed in the public court file unless that party files a motion or application to seal the records. (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(b)(3)(A).) Within 10 days, the party who receives the notice may file a motion or application to seal the records or to extend the time to file. If the party does so, the documents remain conditionally under seal until the court rules on the application or motion. If the party does not do so, the clerk “promptly” places the documents in the public file. (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(b)(3)(B).)

 

(7) Contents of Sealing Order.  The order sealing a record must be narrowly tailored, “specifically set forth the facts that support the findings” and “[d]irect the sealing of only those documents and pages, or, if reasonably practicable, portions of those documents and pages, that contain the material that needs to be placed under seal.” All other portions of the documents must be placed in the public file. (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(e).) The order must state whether the order itself, the register of actions, any other court records, or any other records in the case are to be sealed and whether any person other than the court is authorized to inspect the record. (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(e)(2), (e)(3).) Unless the sealing order provides otherwise, the parties may not disclose the contents of any sealed materials in subsequently filed records or papers. (C.R.C., Rule 2.550(e)(4).) 

 

There must be no less restrictive means to achieve the overriding interests.  These findings embody constitutional requirements for a request to seal court records, protecting the First Amendment right of public access to civil trials.  (See NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV), Inc. v. Superior Court (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1178, 1217-1218; Huffy Corp. v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 97, 104; People v. Jackson (2005) 128 Cal.App 4th 1009, 1026 – in determining whether to seal records, courts must weigh constitutional requirements for disclosure against such factors as privacy rights.)

 

(8) Grant of Motion or Application.  If the court grants the motion or application, the clerk replaces the cover sheet with a file-endorsed copy of the court's order and labels the record “sealed by order of the court on (date).” (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(e)(1).)

 

(9) Denial of Motion or Application.  If the motion or application is denied, the clerk returns the lodged record to the requesting party and does not place it in the case file unless that party notifies the clerk in writing within 10 days after denial that the record is to be filed. (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(b)(6).)

 

(10) Custody of Sealed Records.  Sealed records are securely filed and kept separate from the public file in the case. (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(f).) Where voluminous records in the possession of a public agency are to be sealed, the order may direct the agency to maintain the records in a secure fashion. (C.R.C., Rule 2.551(g).)

b.             Trade Secret Confidentiality

Pursuant to Farrar v. Direct Commerce, Inc. (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 1257, 1272, a sealing order is appropriate to protect trade secret and proprietary commercial information.  Am. Honda Motor Co. v. Superior Court (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 1367, 1378 considered an issue similar to the one posed here: whether it was appropriate to seal documents relating to internal Honda communications opining concerning potential design defects.  The court of appeals affirmed the filing of such documents under seal.  The documents at issue in Plaintiffs’ opposition to Defendants’ summary judgment motion are substantially similar documents, such that sealing them is appropriate under the standard above.  Notably, the parties have a confidentiality agreement concerning these documents, and the parties lodged all of the documents under seal. 

 

The motion to seal the subject documents is granted.  The lodged, sealed records will be handled by the Judicial Assistant in accordance with the procedure detailed above.
