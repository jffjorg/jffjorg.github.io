CENTINELA SKILLED NURSING & WELLNESS CENTRE, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          March 21, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Centinela Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre West, LLC and Brius Management Company

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Esperanza Asberry, et al.

(1)   Demurrer to FAC

(2)   Motion to Strike

 

The court considered the moving and opposition papers.

RULING

            The demurrer to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action in the FAC are OVERRULED.  The motion to strike is DENIED.  Defendants are ordered to file an answer within 20 days.  

BACKGROUND

            On September 30, 2021, plaintiffs Esperanza Asberry, Samuel L. Asberry, Jr., and Candis Ashberry aka Candis Asberry, ind. and as heirs and successors in interest to Samuel L. Asberry filed a complaint against Centinela Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre, Centinela Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre West, Centinela Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre West, LLC, Brius Management Company, Mohsen Mobasser, Marie Faye Sorianosos, Donte Asberry, and Tessie Asberry for (1) wrongful death, (2) violations of Elder Abuse Act, (3) violation of Health & Safety Code §1430(b), and (4) wrongful death.

            On February 22, 2023, the case was transferred from the PI Hub to Dept. M.

            On October 31, 2023, the court sustained with 30 days leave to amend defendants’ demurrer to the second and third causes of action in the complaint.  The court found that the allegations were insufficient that plaintiffs had standing as they had not complied with CCP 377.32.  As to the Second and Third Causes of Action, the court found that the allegations were insufficient as they were conclusory and plaintiffs had not pled with particularity.

            On November 30, 2023, plaintiffs filed a declaration pursuant to CCP §377.32.

            On November 30, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC for (1) wrongful death (Elder Abuse Act), (2) violations of Elder Abuse Act, (3) violations of Health & Safety Code §1430(b) (survival claim), and (4) wrongful death (negligence).

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Demurrer

            When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

            Strike

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense.

            (b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.

            (c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

            The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

            Defendants Centinela Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre West, LLC, Brius Management Company, Mohsen Mobasser, and Marie Faye Sorianosos demur to the First, Second, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain as they are barred by the statute of limitations and plaintiffs lack standing.

            The FAC alleges that individually and collectively, defendants owned, controlled, operated, designed, built, maintained, inspected, furnished, supervised, and/or controlled the real property and facility at 950 South Flower St., Inglewood.  FAC, ¶29.  In summer of 2018, decedent suffered from a stroke and was briefly hospitalized at Centinela Hospital Medical Center.  He was discharged and returned home.  Later that year, decedent suffered from a second stroke, became paralyzed on the right side of his body, and was again hospitalized at Centinela Hospital.  This time, he was discharged to a skilled nursing facility in or about Gardena.  Because of the long distance and travel time between plaintiff’s residence and the skilled nursing facility in Gardena, decedent was moved to Centinela Skilled Nursing & Wellness Centre in about late 2019.  Id., ¶30.  Decedent, who was about 81 years old, was placed into the custodian care and custody of defendants at the facility and was a resident there.  Decedent was admitted to the facility for rehabilitation, physical therapy, blood pressure and cholesterol regulation assistance, treatment and care for symptoms of dementia and strokes, and other limiting conditions.  Plaintiff hoped decedent would eventually rehabilitate enough to return home.  Id., ¶31.

            During decedent’s residence at the facility, defendants knew that decedent was in a compromised physical state.  Decedent could not really move, feed himself, or care for himself.  As a result, decedent required close supervision, close monitoring, assistance with daily functions, and medical attention to ensure decedent’s health, safety, and wellbeing.  Id., ¶32.  Particularly noteworthy, decedent arrived with critical needs arising from dementia and paralysis on the right side of his body.  Id., ¶33.  Defendants also knew, by virtue of decedent’s physical state, that decedent was dependent on defendants for all activities of daily living including, feeding and dietary planning, skin care, hygiene, infection control/prevention, toileting, medical care, and physical rehabilitation/exercise.  Id., ¶34.

            Defendants were required to provide decedent with care for some or all of decedent’s fundamental and basic needs at the facility.  Id., ¶35.  During decedent’s residency at the facility, defendants failed to protect decedent from health and safety hazards, including illnesses, infections, and COVID-19.  During decedent’s residency, defendants’ conduct directly and unambiguously put decedent in harm’s way, in a blatant disregard of the high risk of injury and death.  Id., ¶36.  Specifically, as result of decedent’s admission/residency at the facility and defendants’ withholding of and failure to provide care, decedent suffered from substantial weight loss, malnutrition, dehydration, a bed sore on his buttocks, infection, rapid loss of motor skills, and physical harm, and a COVID-19 diagnosis – all of which contributed to acute respiratory failure and caused decedent’s death on April 29, 2020.  Id., ¶37.

            Prior to March 2020, plaintiff Esperanza visited decedent at the facility almost every morning before work.  At the start of almost every visit, Esperanza would change decedent’s soiled adult diaper/incontinence pads, provide showers, feed, hydrate, and generally care for decedent.  She provided care the facility had failed to provide in her absence.  Id., ¶38.  After March 2020, defendants restricted residents’ family members’ access to the facility due to COVID-19.  Id., ¶39.  Throughout decedent’s residence at the facility, defendants repeatedly withheld and failed to provide decedent with bathing and hygienic monitoring, unsoiled adult incontinence pads/adult diapers, adequate posture adjustments, hydration, nutrition, adequate fall prevention, and necessary monitoring and assistance.  Id., ¶¶40-48.  During Esperanza’s visits, staff were frequently and regularly missing or nowhere to be seen.  Id., ¶ 49.  From the time of arrival at the facility until death, it was apparent that decedent declined in overall health and body.  During decedent’s stay, decedent did not receive the attention and care ordered by the doctors and required by the care plan.  This mistreatment occurred well before any COVID-19 diagnosis.  Id., ¶54.

                        Statute of limitations

            Plaintiffs allege, citing to Emergency Rule 9, that “any and all claims applicable to this action were tolled from April 6, 2020, until October 1, 2020.”

            As to the First Cause of Action for wrongful death (Elder Abuse Act) and Fourth Cause of Action for wrongful death (negligence), defendants argue that the one-year statute of limitations applies for claims based on professional negligence under CCP §340.5.  Defendants contend that plaintiffs allege that decedent died on April 29, 2020, and that the complaint was not filed until September 30, 2021.

            As to the Third Cause of Action for violation of Health & Safety Code §1430(b), defendants argue that the statute of limitation bars the claim as it is subject to a one-year statute of limitations as it is an action upon a statute for a penalty under CCP §340.

            In opposition, plaintiffs argue that the demurer violates the rule as to “successive demurrers.”  The court notes that there are two additional defendants who are demurring who did not demurrer to the complaint.  Plaintiffs also argue that the First, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action are not barred by the statute of limitations.

            The court rules as follows:  “[F]or a demurrer based on the statute of limitations to be sustained, the untimeliness of the lawsuit must clearly and affirmatively appear on the face of the complaint and matters judicially noticed.”  Coalition for Clean Air v. City of Visalia (2012) 209 Cal. App. 4th 408, 420.  The Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1 statute of limitations (applicable to causes for ‘assault, battery, or injury to, or for the death of, an individual caused by the wrongful act or neglect of another’) is facially applicable to elder abuse actions and provides a two-year limitation period. . . .”  Benun v. Superior Court (2004) 123 Cal. App. 4th 113, 125-126.  Under the Elder Abuse Act, “’neglect’ within the meaning of Welfare and Institutions Code section 15610.57 covers an area of misconduct distinct from ‘professional negligence.’”  Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 783; see also CACI 3103.  “Thus, the statutory definition of ‘neglect’ speaks not of the undertaking of medical services, but of the failure to provide medical care.” Covenant Care, supra, at 783.  The allegations support a claim of neglect under the Elder Abuse Act.

            As plaintiffs allege that decedent died on April 29, 2020 and the complaint was filed within two years on September 30, 2021, the First and Fourth Causes of Action are not barred on their face.  Further, even if the statute of limitations is one-year, the claims are not barred.  As explained by plaintiffs, CCP §364 requires a notice of intent to sue for actions against providers.  “If the notice is served within 90 days of the expiration of the applicable statute of limitations, the time of the commencement of the action shall be extended 90 days from the service of the notice.”  CCP §364(d).  Plaintiffs contend that they served their notice of intent to sue on April 28, 2021 and that the statute of limitations was extended until July 28, 2021, and that COVID Rule 9 extended the original statute of limitations of April 29, 2021 to October 1, 2021.

            As to the Third Cause of Action for violation of Health & Safey Code §1430(b), it is a civil action for “statutory damages” and not an action for a penalty.  See Jarman v. HCR ManorCare, Inc. (2020) 10 Cal. 5th 375, 379 (“The available remedies include . . . ‘up to five hundred dollars ($500)’ in statutory damages.”).  See also Mou v. SSC San Jose Operating Company LP (2019) 415 F.Supp.3d 918, 928 (“Because the Court finds that section 1430(b) provides for damages, the statute of limitations set forth in California Code of Civil Procedure 338(a) applies.”).  CCP §338(a) provides for a three-year statute of limitation for any “action upon a liability created by statute, rather than a penalty or forfeiture.”  Thus, the court finds that this claim is not barred by the statute of limitations on its face.

Standing

            Defendants argue that plaintiffs lack standing as to the Second Cause of Action for elder abuse and that plaintiffs, as successors-in-interest, are not entitled to any damages under the Elder Abuse statute.  Rather, the cause of action “belongs to Decedent” and plaintiffs are only enforcing the action.  Defendants make the same argument as to the Third Cause of Action.

            In opposition, plaintiffs argue that they have standing under the “interested person” standard under Welf. & Inst. Code §15657.3, which states that an “interested person” includes an “heir, devisee, child, spouse . . . and any other person having a property right in . . . the estate of a decedent which may be affected by the proceeding.”  See Probate Code §48.  As to defendants’ damages argument, plaintiffs contend, a demurrer is not an appropriate vehicle to raise such a claim.

            The court finds that plaintiffs have standing.  As previously noted, plaintiffs complied with CCP §377.32 and allege that they are decedent’s surviving spouse and children and have a property right in decedent’s estate.  As to the third Cause of Action, “[a] . . . successor in interest of a current or former resident or patient of a skilled nursing facility . . . may bring a civil action against the licensee of a facility who violates any rights of the resident or patient . . . .”  Health & Safety Code §1430(b)(1).  Further, plaintiffs properly allege that they are seeking damages as decedent’s successor in interest.  See FAC, ¶92.

            Thus, the demurrer to the First, Second, Third, and Fourth Causes of Action in the FAC are OVERRULED.

 

                        Motion to Strike

            Defendants request that the court strike paras. 77 and 78 and prayer under the First Cause of Action for attorney’s fees under Welf. & Inst. Code §15657 and Health & Safety Code §1430(b) and prayer under the first Cause of Action for exemplary and punitive damages.

            Defendants argue that a claim for wrongful death does not allow for attorney’s fees or punitive/exemplary damages.  Defendants assert that the wrongful death statute under CCP §377.61 allows for recovery of damages that are not recoverable under CCP §377.34.   CCP §377.34 allows for recovery of punitive or exemplary damages.

            In opposition, plaintiffs assert that defendants do not argue that the FAC does not sufficiently allege the predicate Elder Abuse Act violations, which is what the First Cause of Action is based upon.  Plaintiffs also contend that they have properly alleged alternative theories of recoveries and damages.

            The court finds that plaintiffs’ allegations and prayer for attorney’s fees and prayer for punitive damages under the first Cause of Action are not improper.

            The motion to strike is DENIED.

 

ORDER

The demurrer to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action in the FAC are OVERRULED.  The motion to strike is DENIED.  Defendants are ordered to file an answer within 20 days. 

            Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of the ruling.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Interrogatories

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.

Request for Production of Documents

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of “good cause” is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487.

Request for Admissions

Pursuant to CCP § 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).  “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions.  Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.”  Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶ 8:1370, citing CCP § 2033.280(b).  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  CCP § 2033.280(c).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Jamie McDaniel requests that the court compel plaintiff The Roosk Group Inc. to respond without objections to defendant’s initial form and special interrogatories and requests for production of documents (set two) and to deem admitted the requests for admission.  Defendant Friendly Llama, Inc. requests that the court compel plaintiff to respond without objections to defendant’s initial special interrogatories and requests for production of documents.

Defendants explain that they served the discovery requests on plaintiff on December 12, 2023.  Responses were due on January 16, 2024.  As defense counsel had not received responses, on January 22, 2024, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter to plaintiff’s counsel requesting responses without objections by January 26, 2024.  On January 23, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel sent an email to defense counsel stating, “I do not show the referenced requests in our system.  Please resend them so that we can review and address them.”  On the same date, defense counsel forwarded the December 12, 2023 email along with the discovery requests to plaintiff’s counsel.  The December 12 email was addressed to four persons at Callagy Law, plaintiff’s counsel’s law firm.  On February 2, 2024, defense counsel emailed plaintiff’s counsel and asked if plaintiff would be providing responses without objection.  On February 5, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel responded:  “We do intend to provide responses to the discovery requests that were inadvertently overlooked.  I do not intend to provide those responses without objections where those objections are warranted.  If need be, I’ll file appropriate motions to address any dispute over whether any objections have been waived.  We are reviewing the requests and working with our client on responses.”  As of the date of filing the motions on February 16, defense counsel had not received responses.

In opposition, plaintiff contends that any failure to respond by the original January 16 response date was caused by plaintiff’s lack of knowledge that the requests had been issued and served and that they had been “overlooked” during the back-and-forth exchanges in resolving other discovery issues and that “[i]t is nothing more than human error.”  Plaintiff also argues that sanctions are inappropriate because “[d]efendant has been advised that responses will be provided” and that there was no basis or need for defendant to file their motions.

In reply, defendants reiterate that plaintiff failed to respond by the due date.  Defendants assert that plaintiff’s argument that its failure to respond should be excused because it was unintentional is irrelevant as to the applicable discovery statutes.  Defendants also argue that they are “not obligated to wait for Roosk to produce information at some indeterminate future date.  Roosk waived its objections and then never committed to a deadline when the information would be produced.” 

The court finds that defendants properly served discovery requests and plaintiff failed to respond by the statutory due date.  Thus, plaintiff waived its objections.  There has been no indication that plaintiff has served any responses.  The motions are GRANTED.

 

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Defendants request $975 (at $325/hr.) for each motion against plaintiff.  The court finds that sanctions are not warranted against plaintiff as it was plaintiff’s counsel that admitted to inadvertently missing the email service of the discovery requests.  Defendants did not request sanctions against plaintiff’s counsel.  The request is thus denied.

BACKGROUND

LEGAL AUTHORITY

45-Day Rule:  This motion must be served within 45 days after service of the response in question (extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, or fax; see CCP §1013); otherwise, the demanding party waives the right to compel any further response to the CCP §2031.010 demand.  CCP §§2031.310(c), 2016.050; see Sperber v. Robinson (1994) 26 Cal. App. 4th 736, 745.  The 45-day time limit is mandatory and jurisdictional.  Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 1403, 1410.  The parties, however, can also agree in writing on a specific later date by which to file the motion to compel.  CCP §2031.310(c).

Meet-and-Confer Requirement:  The motion to compel further responses must be accompanied by a declaration showing “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the issues outside of court (so-called “meet and confer”).  CCP §§2016.040, 2031.310(b)(2).

Separate Statement:  Any motion involving the content of a discovery request or the responses to such a request shall be accompanied by a separate statement.  This includes a motion to compel further responses to demand for inspection of documents or tangible things.  CRC Rule 3.1020(a)(3). 

Interrogatories

CCP §2030.300 states:  “(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:   (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. . . . (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.  (b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (c) Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories. . . .”

CCP §2030.220 states:  “(a) Each answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.  (b) If an interrogatory cannot be answered completely, it shall be answered to the extent possible.  (c) If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff S.W. requests that the court compel defendant Ginder Marshall, M.D. to provide further responses to Special Interrogatory, No. 6.

            No. 6 requests:  “Identify all persons who made Complaints of Misconduct against Marshall from 2000 to present.  (As used herein, the term ‘Identify’ shall mean to provide the name and last known contact information including phone number, address, business address (if applicable).” 

            As background, plaintiff explains that on March 23, 2023, she served Special Interrogatories, Set One to defendant.  On June 26, defendant served responses.  On August 8, 2023, plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to defense counsel.  Defendant agreed to provide supplemental responses by August 25, 2023, and to extend plaintiff’s deadline to file motions to compel further to September 1, 2023.  Defendant did not serve supplemental responses.  On September 1, 2023, plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses, including as to Special Interrogatory, No. 6.  Defendant served supplemental responses on September 20, 2023.  At the hearing on October 4, 2023, the court deemed the motion moot as defendant had served supplemental responses.  The court awarded sanctions against defense counsel. 

            As further background, on October 13, 2023, plaintiff’s counsel sent a second meet and confer letter regarding the supplemental responses, including as to No. 6.  On October 19, 2023, counsel agreed to attend an IDC on the issue of defendant revealing the name and identifying information for the 2018 complaint of misconduct against defendants.  Counsel agreed to extend plaintiff’s motion to compel furthers deadline to two weeks after the parties attended an IDC.  On October 27, 2023, defendant served his second supplemental responses, including as to No. 6, but only identified plaintiff and the 2008 complainant against defendant.  In a response to Special Interrogatory No. 4, defendant had confirmed three complaints of misconduct against defendant in 2008, 2018, and 2023.  Defendant did not identify the person who made the 2018 complaint against defendant.  Counsel were unable to coordinate scheduling an IDC before the case was reassigned.  Purportedly, on January 18, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel obtained information that the court does not conduct IDCs.  The court notes that that information is incorrect and that it does conduct IDCs.  On January 23, 2024, defense counsel responded that defendant would not serve responses to No. 6 identifying the 2018 complainant without a court order. 

            In response to No. 6, defendant objected on the following grounds:  vague and ambiguous as to “all persons” and overly broad as to time; seeks information that is not relevant or likely to lead to the discovery of admissible information; may violate the attorney-client privilege, attorney work product doctrine, Evidence Code §1157; violates HIPAA, the physician-patient privilege, and the privacy rights of third parties. 

            Defendant’s supplemental response asserted the same objections.  Defendant’s second supplemental response asserted the same objections and provided two names, but not the name from a purported 2018 complaint against defendant.

            Plaintiff argues that such information is highly relevant as to notice, negligence, and lack of enforcement of reasonable safeguards to prevent abuse to defendants’ patients and that defendant has put the issue of prior complaints of misconduct against him at issue.  Plaintiff also states her willingness to enter into a stipulated protective order to protect the names of any third parties.

            In opposition, defendant argues that the motion is untimely and unreasonably so because although counsel agreed to an extension for plaintiff to file a motion to compel further until two weeks after an IDC, “the ability for the plaintiff to obtain an IDC date . . . has come and gone due to her own dilatory conduct.”  Defendant also argues that plaintiff’s request is inherently violative of the rights of third parties.  Defendant further asserts that the information requested is irrelevant, inadmissible, and prejudicial as to Dr. Marshall and less intrusive means exist to obtain the information, although he does not identify any other means.

 

            The court rules as follows:  The motion is timely as counsel agreed to an extension.

            Defendant objected on the grounds that identifying the former patient who had made a complaint against him would violate HIPAA and the non-party’s right to privacy.  The right of privacy is protected by Article I, section I of the California Constitution.  The right to privacy, however, is not absolute and even private information can be disclosed in some circumstances. The court must carefully balance the right of privacy against the need of discovery.  Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 855-856.  Upon objection, the party seeking discovery must show a “compelling need” for the non-party’s information. A compelling need is demonstrated where the information is “directly relevant” and “essential to the fair resolution” of the lawsuit.  Id. at 859; Alch v. Superior Court (2008) 165 Cal. App. 4th 1412, 1431-1432.  Even where the balance weighs in favor of disclosure of private information, the scope of the disclosure will be narrowly circumscribed and is permitted only to the extent necessary for a fair resolution of the lawsuit. Britt, supra, 20 Cal. 3d. at 859.

            Medical information is protected by the right of privacy.  See John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal. 4th 1177, 1198 (citation omitted).  Thus, the non-party has a right to privacy and defendant’s objection has merit.  In balancing plaintiff’s purported need for the information and the non-party’s right to privacy, the court finds that plaintiff has not shown a “compelling need.”  Plaintiff asserts relevance based on notice and negligence, but plaintiff is asserting intentional torts.  Further, any need does not outweigh the non-party’s right to privacy.

The motion is thus DENIED.

            Sanctions

            Plaintiff requests $900 in sanctions against defendant Ginder Marshall, M.D. and his counsel of record. The court finds that defendant acted with substantial justification.  Thus, sanctions are not warranted.

 

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
