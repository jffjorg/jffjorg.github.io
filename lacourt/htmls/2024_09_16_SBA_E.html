DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02553    Hearing Date: September 16, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

BEATRIZ WATT,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV02553

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

HEIDI EMKE aka HEIDI LINDE EMKEME and DOES 1 to 10, inclusive,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             September 16, 2024   

Moving Parties:          Defendant Heidi Emke          

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Beatriz Watt

Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Dismissal of Action

            The court considered the moving papers, the opposition, and the reply.

RULING

            The motion for terminating sanctions and dismissal of action is DENIED in part.

            Plaintiff’s counsel R. Christopher Harshman is to pay the previously ordered sanctions plus an additional $750 to defense counsel. All discovery responses without objection and sanctions are due by 4:00 pm on September 20, 2024. Any additional failure by Plaintiff’s counsel will result in contempt proceedings and if appropriate notification to the State Bar.

 

BACKGROUND

            On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff Beatriz Watt (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action against Defendant Heidi Emke aka Heidi Linde Emkeme (“Defendant”) and DOES 1 to 10, alleging causes of action for (1) Motor Vehicle and (2) General Negligence.

 

            On January 16, 2024, Defendant filed her Answer.

 

            On April 17, 2024, Defendant filed Motions to Compel Form Interrogatories (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), Request for Production (Set One), and Motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted. The matters were unopposed and came for hearing on May 29, 2024. This Court granted all four motions and imposed sanctions against Plaintiff. Plaintiff was ordered to provide objection-free responses and pay a total of $2,246.60 in sanctions within 20 days.

 

            On August 8, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions and dismissal of the action. On September 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an untimely opposition that was not reviewed by the Court. On September 9, 2024, when the case was called for hearing the Court continued the matter until September 16, 2024, so that the Court could consider the opposition.     

 

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Code of Civil Procedure Section 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method..., the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose... [monetary, evidence, and terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process....” Code of Civil Procedure Section 2023.010 provides that “[m]issues of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following:... (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.... (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery....”

 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390 (quoting Lang v. Hachman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246).) “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’” (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390 (citation omitted).) 

 

“Under this standard, trial courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders.” (Id. (citing Lang, supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1244- 1246); see, e.g., Collisson X Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1617-1622 (terminating sanctions imposed after defendants failed to comply with one court order to produce discovery); Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange (1991) 231 Cal App 3d 481, 491 (disapproved on other grounds in Garcia v. McCucchen (1997) 16 Cal.4th 469, 478, n. 4) (terminating sanctions imposed against plaintiff for failing to comply with a discovery order and for violating various discovery statutes).) 

 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant moves for an order terminating sanctions and to dismiss this lawsuit on the grounds that Plaintiff has unreasonably and unjustifiably refused to obey discovery statutes and the Court’s orders regarding discovery.

 

            Here, Defendant served discovery requests on January 15, 2024 on Plaintiff. (Labat Decl., ¶2.) Defendant contends Plaintiff failed to provide responses or request any discovery extensions. (Id. at ¶3.) Further, Defendant contends she made good faith attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel from February 22, 2024 through April 17, 2024, which were futile. Subsequently, Defendant filed three motions to compel responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), Request for Production (Set One), and a motion to deem Request for Admissions Admitted. All four motions were granted on May 29, 2024 and Plaintiff was ordered to provide objection-free responses and pay $2,246.60 in sanctions within 20 days. (Ex. A.) Defendant argues she has neither received the court-ordered discovery responses nor the $2,246.60 in sanctions.

 

            The Court finds that Plaintiff has engaged in conduct that is an abuse of the discovery process. First, Plaintiff failed to serve any discovery responses, which gave rise to the underlying motions to compel and deem request for admissions admitted. Next, Plaintiff failed to file a timely opposition to the underlying motions to compel and deem request for admissions admitted. Now, Plaintiff has failed to timely oppose the present motion or offered any acceptable explanation for the failure to obey the prior court orders regarding discovery. A such, Plaintiff has shown she is disinterested in prosecuting this case. However, punishing the client is not the appropriate response for Plaintiff’s counsel’s failures.

 

            This Court is ordering Plaintiff’s counsel R. Christopher Harshman to pay the previously ordered sanctions plus an additional $750 to defense counsel. All discovery responses without objection and sanctions are due by 4:00 pm on September 20, 2024. Any additional failure by Plaintiff’s counsel will result in contempt proceedings and if appropriate notification to the State Bar. 

            Therefore, Defendant Heidi Emke aka Heidi Linde Emkeme’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions and Dismissal of Action is DENIED in part.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 437c(p)(1), a plaintiff or cross-complainant has the initial burden on summary judgment or adjudication of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on that cause of action. Once that burden is met, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.

 

On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party’s supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion. (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21; Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)

 

Furthermore, on summary judgment, the issues are defined by the pleadings. (See Physicians Comm. For Responsible Medicine v. McDonald’s Corp. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 554, 568 [“the issues framed by the pleadings are the only issues a motion for summary judgment must address.”]; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., ITD., v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472; Bostrom v. County of San Bernardino (1995) 35 Cal. App. 4th 1654, 1663; 580 Folsom Assocs. v. Prometheus Dev. Co. (1990) 223 Cal. App. 3d 1, 14, 18 [burden to address theories “reasonably contemplated by the opponent’s pleading”]; Melican v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2007) 151 Cal. App. 4th 168, 182 [not requiring party to negate an unstated claim].)

 

Courts cannot decide summary judgment or adjudication motions based upon credibility. (AARTS Prods., Inc. v. Crocker Nat’l Bank (1986) 179 Cal.App.3d 1061, 1064 [“[T]he role of the court in summary judgment proceedings is not to weigh the evidence, but to determine whether there exists a triable issue of material fact.”]; Habitat Trust for Wildlife, Inc. v. City of Rancho Cucamonga (2009) 175 Cal. App. 4th 1306, 1342.) That said, the affidavits and declarations of the moving party are strictly construed and those of the opponent are liberally constructed. (Howe v. Pioneer Mfg Co. (1968) 262 Cal. App. 2d 330, 336-37.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Statement of Facts

 

First Cause of Action

 

In or around June 1992, Defendant opened an American Express Account (“Account 1”). (Plaintiff’s Undisputed Material Facts “UMF” #1.) The use of Account 1 was governed by a Cardmember Agreement. (UMF #2.) American Express sent a copy of the original Cardmember Agreement to Defendant along with the physical credit card and updated the terms and conditions periodically, sending the updated agreements to its cardmembers. (UMF #3.) By retaining and using Account 1, Defendant agreed to be bound by the current terms and conditions of the Cardmember Agreement. (UMF #4.) Defendant utilized Account 1 for obtaining credit and purchasing goods and services. (UMF #5.) American Express maintained an open book account for Account 1 through monthly billing statements, which itemized all transactions, including purchases, advances, fees, interest, and payments. (UMF #8.) The statements were generated every month, stored internally by American Express, and mailed to Defendant. (UMF #8.) Defendant had a period of sixty (60) days to dispute any billing errors in writing, but there are no unresolved disputes, and Account 1 is accurate as stated. (UMF #9, #10.) The current outstanding balance on Account 1 is $64,992.44, as per the Cardmember Agreement and the latest statement. (UMF #11.)

 

Second Cause of Action

 

In or around November 1993, Defendant opened another American Express Account (“Account 2”). (UMF #12.) The terms and conditions for Account 2 were also governed by a Cardmember Agreement. (UMF #13.) American Express sent the original Cardmember Agreement and the credit card to the Defendant and periodically provided updates to the agreement’s terms. (UMF #14.) By retaining and using Account 2, Defendant agreed to comply with the updated terms and conditions. (P’s UMF #15.) Defendant used Account 2 for credit transactions and payment for goods and services. (UMF #16.) American Express maintained Account 2 through monthly billing statements that detailed all transactions, fees, interest, and payments. (UMF #19.) These statements were generated monthly, stored internally by American Express, and mailed to the Defendant. (UMF #19.) Defendant was allowed sixty (60) days to contest any billing discrepancies, but no disputes remain unresolved, and Account 2 is accurate. (UMF #20, #21.) The outstanding balance on Account 2 is $22,109.59, as specified in the Cardmember Agreement and the most recent statement. (UMF #22.)

 

Analysis

 

            Account Stated

 

Plaintiff argues that an account stated exists between the parties. An “account stated” is an agreement that confirms the correctness of an account’s balance and the indebtedness of one party to another, based on previous transactions. (Trafton v. Youngblood (1968) 68 Cal.2d 17, 25.) Plaintiff contends that such an agreement was implied because Defendant did not dispute any of the monthly billing statements sent for both accounts. (UMFs #1-22.) The Cardmember Agreement, which was sent to Defendant along with the credit card, governs the terms of both accounts. (UMF #2, 3, 13, 14.) Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s continued use of the card is an implied acceptance of these terms. (UMF #3-4, 14-15.) As Defendant did not object to the statements within the 60-day period provided for disputes, Plaintiff argues that the accounts are correct and undisputed, thus establishing an account stated. (UMF #9, 20.)

 

 

Open Book Account

 

Plaintiff claims that an open book account exists between the parties. Under California law, a “book account” is a detailed statement kept in a reasonably permanent form, which constitutes the principal record of transactions between a debtor and creditor. (Code Civ. Proc., § 337a.) Plaintiff maintains that American Express’s business records, including monthly billing statements generated and stored on its internal network, meet the criteria of a book account. (UMF #8, 19.) These records were kept in an electronic format, which is considered sufficient to establish a book account. (Costerisan v. DeLong (1967) 251 Cal.App.2d 768, 770-771.) As there are no unresolved disputes regarding the accounts Plaintiff argues that Defendant is liable under the Complaint. (UMF #10, 21.)

 

Quantum Meruit

 

Plaintiff contends that it is entitled to recovery under the principle of quantum meruit, which allows for compensation whenever one party has received a benefit at the expense of another that should, in equity and good conscience, be returned. (Mains v. City Title Ins. Co. (1949) 34 Cal. 2d 580, 586.) Here, Defendant requested and was issued two American Express credit cards, used to make purchases and obtain credit. (UMF #1-5, 12-16.) Plaintiff states that Defendant has failed to pay the balance due on either account, which represents the reasonable value of the services provided by American Express. (UMF #8-11, 19-22.)

 

            Defendant fails to provide any opposition. The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied its burden in showing that there is no triable issue of fact as to the claims in the complaint. Plaintiff has presented sufficient evidence to establish that an account stated exists, as Defendant did not dispute the billing statements within the allowed time frame, and an open book account is properly maintained by Plaintiff in accordance with legal standards. Additionally, Plaintiff has demonstrated entitlement to recover under the principle of quantum meruit due to Defendant’s use of the credit card services and failure to pay the outstanding balances.

 

Therefore, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment.
