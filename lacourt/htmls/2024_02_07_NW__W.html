DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01134    Hearing Date: February 7, 2024    Dept: W

YOSEF COHEN, et al. v. KAMBIZ KAMANGAR, et al.

 

motions to compel discovery

 

Date of Hearing:        February 7, 2024                               Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01134

 

BACKGROUND

 

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiffs Yosef Cohen and Vahe Ter-Galstyan filed a complaint against Defendants World Tech Toys, Inc., World Trading 23, Inc., Kevork Kouyoumjian, Kambiz Kamangar, and Kamir Distribution, LLC asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Oral Joint Venture Agreement; (2) Unlawful, Fraudulent & Unfair Business Practices; (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty; and (4) Fraud. On January 12, 2023, Plaintiffs amended their complaint adding two additional causes of action for Breach of Partnership Agreement and Declaratory Relief.

 

Plaintiffs allege Defendant Kamangar made fraudulent representations to Plaintiff Cohen regarding a business opportunity arising from the Pandemic. Plaintiffs allege Defendant Kamangar represented to Plaintiff Cohen Defendant KOUYOUMJIAN had extensive contacts in China as a result of his companies, World Trading 23, Inc. and World Tech Toys, Inc., which were involved in importing goods from China for sale in the United States and actively encouraged Plaintiff Cohen and Plaintiff Ter-Galstanyan to order the gloves. Plaintiffs allege Defendants failed to perform in accordance with the term so of the Agreement as well as their fiduciary duty.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

1.      Defendant Kevork Kouyoumjian’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Vahe Ter-Galstanyan to Respond to Written Discovery and for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED.

2.      Defendant Kevork Kouyoumjian’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Yosef Cohen to Respond to Written Discovery and for Monetary Sanctions is GRANTED.

3.      Defendant Kambiz Kamangar’s Motion to Compel Discovery Responses of Plaintiffs (Special Interrogatories, Set One) and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED.

4.      Defendant Kambiz Kamangar’s Motion to Compel Discovery Responses of Plaintiffs (Requests for Production of Documents, Set One) and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED.

5.      Defendant Kambiz Kamangar’s Motion for Order that Requests for Admission (Set One) be Deemed Admitted is GRANTED.

6.      Defendant Kambiz Kamangar’s Motion to Compel Discovery Responses of Plaintiffs (Form Interrogatories, Set One) and Request for Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Both moving parties, Defendant Kambiz Kamangar and Defendant Kevork Kouyoumjian request this court take judicial notice of several court documents regarding the criminal actions against Plaintiffs in support of their motions to compel discovery. The court GRANTS the Moving Defendants’ request for judicial notice.

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    DEFENDANT KEVORK KOUYOUMJIAN’S MOTION TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF VAHE TER-GALSTANYAN TO RESPOND TO WRITTEN DISCOVERY AND FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

 

Defendant Kevork Kouyoumjian moves this court for an order to compel Plaintiff Vahe Ter-Galstanyan to provide responses, without objection, to Requests for Production of Documents, Special Interrogatories, and Form Interrogatories, to order Ter-Galstanyan to produce all requested documents within his possession, custody, and control, and for monetary sanctions against Ter-Galstanyan in the amount of $1,840.00. Kouyoumjian brings this motion on the grounds that Ter-Galstanyan failed to serve any response to Kouyoumjian’s Requests for Production of Documents, Special Interrogatories, or Form Interrogatories, which were served on September 1, 2023. Only one filing fee was paid; nonetheless, two additional filing fees are owed.  

 

Where a party fails to serve timely responses to discovery requests, the court may make an order compelling responses. (CCP §§ 2030.290, 2031.300, 2033.280; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (CCP §§2030.290(a), 2031.300(a), 2033.280(a); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.010, the propounding party may also move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2033.280(b).) Under California law, unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses.  (See Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636 (holding that unverified responses are tantamount to no responses).

 

In July of 2023, counsel for Defendant Kouyoumjian became aware that Plaintiffs Yosef Cohen and Vahe Ter-Galstanyan had pled guilty to felonies involving money laundering. (McKown Decl. ¶2.) As a result, Defense counsel e-mailed Plaintiffs’ counsel, Mr. Campbell, to determine whether Plaintiffs would dismiss this case. (McKown Decl. ¶2, Exh. A.) Mr. Campbell said he would reach back shortly and thereafter, Defense counsel informed Mr. Campbell Defendants were prepared to serve written discovery in the event a dismissal was not filed. (McKown Decl. ¶2, Exh. A.) Defense counsel followed up at the end of July. (McKown Decl. ¶3, Exh. B.) After waiting another month for Mr. Campbell to respond, Defense counsel served a complete set of written discovery on Plaintiffs on September 1, 2023. (McKown Decl. ¶4, Exh. C, D-F.) Plaintiffs’ responses to written discovery were due on or before October 1, 2023. (McKown Decl. ¶4.) However, Plaintiffs did not provide any responses or objections by October 1, 2023 nor did Plaintiffs request or otherwise obtain any extension of the deadline. (McKown Decl. ¶4.) On October 9, 2023, Mr. Campbell emailed me acknowledging Plaintiffs’ failure to respond timely to outstanding discovery and requesting a 30-day extension to respond. (Co McKown hen Decl. ¶5.) Defense counsel indicated his willingness to grant the 30-day extension upon the conditions (1) that Plaintiffs agree that all of their objections to the written discovery propounded by Kouyoumjian were waived; and (2) that if Plaintiffs failed to provide responses by the extended deadline (October 31, 2023), Kouyoumjian (and the other defendants) may proceed with filing discovery motions. (McKown Decl. ¶5, Exh. G.) On October 13, 2023, Mr. Campbell sent a written response confirming his acceptance of these conditions. (McKown Decl. ¶6, Exh. H.) Based on the 30-day extension provided by Kouyoumjian, Plaintiffs’ responses to written discovery, without objection, were due on or before October 31, 2023. (McKown Decl. ¶7.) Plaintiffs, however, did not provide any responses on or before October 31, 2023. (McKown Decl. ¶7.) On November 9, 2023, Defense counsel notified Mr. Campbell he would be moving forward with the motions to compel. (McKown Decl. ¶8, Exh. I.)

 

The court finds Plaintiff Ter-Galstanyan has failed to timely respond to Defendant Kouyoumjian’s properly served discovery request. Defendant’s unopposed motion to compel Plaintiff Ter-Galstyan’s responses to interrogatories and request for production of documents is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide complete responses, without objection, and all documents within 30 days.

 

Sanctions

 

If a propounding party moves for and obtains a court order compelling a response, the court shall impose monetary sanctions against the party failing to timely respond to interrogatories and demands for inspection unless that party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c), 2033.280(c); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th at 404.) 

 

Defendant requests $1,840.00 in fees and costs for approximately 2.5 hours preparing the motion, .5 hours preparing any reply brief or non-opposition, and .5 hours attending the hearing on the matter. (McKown Decl. ¶¶9-10.) Counsel’s hourly rate is $500.00. (McKown Decl. ¶11.)

 

The court GRANTS Defendant’s request for attorney fees of $1,840.00.

Case Number: 23VECV04903    Hearing Date: February 7, 2024    Dept: W

SANDRA FLORES vs ABRAHAM BEN DAVID, et al.

 

plaintiff’s motion to compel arbitration pursuant to arbitration agreement

 

Date of Hearing:        February 7, 2024                               Trial Date:       None

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV04903

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff Sandra Flores

Responding Party:     Defendants Mulholland Security Centers LLC, Abraham Ben David, and Avi ‘Coby’ Jacoby  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wrongful termination case. On November 6, 2023, Plaintiff Sandra Flores filed a complaint against Defendants Mulholland Security Centers LLC, Abraham Ben David, and Avi ‘Coby’ Jacoby for 1. Constructive Discharge in Violation of Public Policy (FEHA); 2. Constructive Discharge in Violation of Public Policy (Failure to Pay Owed Wages); 3. Discrimination and Disparate Treatment Based on National Origin; 4. Harassment; 5. Failure to Prevent Harassment; 6. Misclassification as Exempt Employee; 7. Failure to Pay Minimum Wage; 8. Failure to Pay Overtime Wages; 9. Meal Period Liability; 10. Rest Break Liability; 11. Failure to Pay Accurate Itemized Wage Statements; 12. Waiting Time Penalties; 13. Violation of Business & Professions Code Section 17200 et seq.; 14. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; 15. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; and 16. Liquidated Damages for Wage Theft and Failure to Pay Wages Plus Interest.

 

Plaintiff began working for Defendants in August 2021 as a sales manager. Plaintiff alleges during her employment she was subjected to discrimination based on her race, sex, ancestry, and national origin. Plaintiff further alleges Defendants regularly denied Plaintiff the ability to take compliant meal periods and rest breaks as well as failed to pay Plaintiff overtime. When Plaintiff reported the incidents to Defendants, Plaintiff alleges Plaintiff began to experience adverse employment and due to the hostility, was forced to resign.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings Pursuant to Arbitration Agreement is GRANTED.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTION

 

Defendants submit evidentiary objections to Corey A. Pingle, Esq.’s declaration submitted in support of the motion to compel arbitration. Defendants objections 1-4 are SUSTAINED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiff Flores moves this court for an order to stay proceedings pursuant to written agreement of the parties to arbitrate any controversy or claim.

 

“On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1281.2(a)-(b).) “Because the existence of the agreement is a statutory prerequisite to granting the petition, the petitioner bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413.)¿ 

¿ 

“California law, ‘like [federal law], reflects a strong policy favoring arbitration agreements and requires close judicial scrutiny of waiver claims.’”¿ (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 31.) If the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until arbitration is completed.¿ (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)¿ 

 

Plaintiff contends on August 20, 2021, Plaintiff and Defendants entered into an agreement to arbitrate any and all employment disputes. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1.) The Arbitration Agreement provides: “In the event of a dispute between the parties over the execution of the attached contract, the parties will waive their right to litigate these issues in court and instead will resolve their dispute through binding arbitration in Canoga Park, California.” (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1, ¶1.) Pursuant to the terms of the Arbitration Agreement, Plaintiff made a demand of arbitration on April 4, 2023. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 2.) However, Defendants failed to comply by failing to make the necessary payment to American Arbitration Association. (Pingle Decl. ¶7.)

 

Defendants oppose the motion. The court takes the time to note it seems peculiar that Defendants, who themselves drafted the Arbitration Agreement, now seek court intervention to deem the agreement unconscionable. Although the court will, in good faith, analyze Defendants’ unconscionability argument, Defendant's argument is an unusual one.

 

In opposition, Defendant first argues the Arbitration Agreement is not enforceable because it was never signed by the parties. The court disagrees. “[T]he writing memorializing an arbitration agreement need not be signed by both parties in order to be upheld as a binding arbitration agreement.”  (Serafin v. Balco Properties Lrd., LLC (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 165, 176.)  “ ‘[I]t is not the presence or absence of a signature [on an agreement] which is dispositive; it is the presence or absence of evidence of an agreement to arbitrate which matters.’ [Citation.]  Evidence confirming the existence of an agreement to arbitrate, despite an unsigned agreement, can be based, for example, on ‘conduct from which one could imply either ratification or implied acceptance of such a provision.’  [Citation.]”  (Ibid.)  In the present case, while Defendants did not execute the Arbitration Agreement, Defendants authored the Arbitration Agreement, and printed the Arbitration Agreement upon Defendants’ letterhead, and produced the agreement to Plaintiff as a term of employment. The court further notes the Arbitration Agreement does not require a signature. (See Gorlach v. Sports Club Co. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1497, 1509 holding “where an arbitration agreement requires a signature to be effective, courts have found that unsigned arbitration agreements are unenforceable.”

 

Defendants also argue that the arbitration agreement was not properly authenticated. The court agrees. Plaintiff is ordered to submit a declaration laying the proper foundation. The court continues to discuss the substantive unconscionability argument below.

 

Defendants argue the arbitration clause has strong elements of substantive unconscionability. Defendant contends the agreement does not fulfill the basic requirements of an enforceable, valid arbitration agreement. Regarding arbitration costs, it allows the arbitrator to shift fees or costs to the prevailing party rather than placing the responsibility of payment of costs of arbitration exclusively on the employer. Moreover, it fails to provide for a “neutral” arbitrator; rather, it states that “the arbitration shall be conducted by one arbitrator” and fails to make the full range of statutory remedies available; it does not seem to permit any discovery, let alone, “adequate discovery;” it does not provide for a “written arbitration that will reveal the essential findings and conclusions on which the award is based.”

 

The court disagrees. Unconscionability has both a ‘procedural’ and a ‘substantive’ element.” (A & M Produce Co. v. FMC Corp. (1982) 135 Cal.App.3d 473, 486.) An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both procedural and substantive unconscionability is shown. (Stirlen v. Supercuts, Inc. (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1533.) Defendant does not attempt to make a procedural argument. Even so, “[s]ubstantive unconscionability focuses on the actual terms of the agreement and evaluates whether they create ‘overly harsh’ or ‘‘one-sided’ results’ [Citations] that is, whether contractual provisions reallocate risks in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.  [Citation] Substantive unconscionability ‘may take various forms,’ but typically is found in the employment context when the arbitration agreement is ‘one-sided’ in favor of the employer without sufficient justification, for example, when ‘the employee’s claims against the employer, but not the employer’s claims against the employee, are subject to arbitration.’ [Citations].”  (Roman v. Superior Court (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1469-1470.)  In determining whether an arbitration agreement is unconscionable, the Court considers whether the agreement: (1) provides for a neutral arbitrator; (2) provides for reasonable discovery; (3) requires a written award; (4) provides for the same remedies that otherwise would be available in court; and (5) does not require employees to bear costs unique to arbitration.  (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 102-103.) 

 

First, the Arbitration Agreement explicitly provides if this dispute is related to employment, the employer will bear the cost of arbitration. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1, ¶6.) Moreover, although it is silent as to discovery, express discovery language is not necessary because, “when parties agree to arbitrate statutory claims, they also implicitly agree, absent express language to the contrary, to such procedures as are necessary to vindicate that claim.” (Ramos v. Superior Court (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1042, 1062, quoting Armendariz, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 106.) As for a neutral arbitrator, although the agreement does not explicitly state ‘neutral’, it says one must be selected (with no limitations) and if the parties cannot agree to one, the arbitrator will be selected by the AAA. This seems to provide for a neutral arbitrator. Lastly, the Arbitration Agreement does provide the “parties agree to enter arbitration through the American Arbitration Association and abide by the Commercial Arbitration Rules of the American Arbitration Association. (Pingle Decl., Exh. 1, ¶2.) The Commercial Arbitration Rules explicitly provides an award shall be in writing.   Further, in a reply declaration, Ms. Flores expressly states that she signed the arbitration agreement and agreed to be bound by it.  While ordinarily the court does not consider evidence submitted for the first time with the reply declaration, the court finds it appropriate to consider such evidence here.  If defendant has evidence to refute that it was signed and understood as Ms. Flores attests, the court may grant permission to defendant to file a further declaration.

 

The court notes Plaintiff also requests sanctions in the amount of $1,500.00 with their moving papers.   The court denies the request for sanctions as it was not set forth in the notice of motion, as required. 

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED.
