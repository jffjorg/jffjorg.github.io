Case Number: 21STLC02852    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

Eli’s Collision Repair of South Bay, Inc. v. Brown, et al.

21stlc02852

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On April 12, 2021, Plaintiff Eli’s Collision Repair of South Bay, Inc., also known as Eli’s Collision Repair III, a California Corporation (“Plaintiff” or “Eli’s”) filed an action against Gannon Brown (“Brown”) and USAA Casualty Insurance Company, a Texas Corporation (“USAA”), (collectively “Defendants”) for (1) civil damages for theft, (2) conversion, (3) claim and delivery, (4) restitution (unjust enrichment), (5) breach of contract, (6) fraud (intentional misrepresentation), and (7) negligence.

 

On December 8, 2021, the Court denied Defendant Brown’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons and ordered him to file and serve a responsive pleading within 20 days of the order.      (12-8-21 Minute Order.)

 

On December 28, 2021, Defendant Brown filed a Demurrer to the first and third causes of action in Plaintiff’s Complaint.  The Court overruled Defendant Brown’s Demurrer.  (2-14-22 Minute Order.)

 

On February 17, 2022, Defendant Brown filed an Answer to the Complaint and a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Eli’s.  On March 22, 2022, Brown filed a First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC”) against Eli’s for (1) violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, (2) breach of common law warranty, (3) promissory fraud, and (4) breach of contract.

 

On June 24, 2022, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Eli’s filed a Demurrer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint’s first, third, and fourth causes of action.  On September 7, 2022, the Court sustained the Demurrer to the FACC’s first cause of action for violation of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act with 25 days’ leave to amend and continued the hearing on the Demurrer to the third and fourth causes of action.  (9-7-22 Minute Order.)  On October 3, 2022, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Brown filed a Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SACC”).  On October 10, 2022, the Court overruled the Demurrer as moot following the filing of the SACC.  (10-10-22 Minute Order.)

On December 14, 2022, Plaintiff/Cross Defendant Eli’s filed another Demurrer with Motion to Strike portions of the SACC.  On February 27, 2023, the Court sustained Eli’s Demurrer to the SACC’s first and third causes of action without leave to amend.  (2-27-23 Minute Order.)  The Court also granted Eli’s Motion to Strike portions of the SACC.  (Ibid.)

 

On January 19, 2023, pursuant to Plaintiff’s oral request, the Court dismissed Defendant USAA without prejudice.  (1-19-23 Order of Dismissal.)

 

On August 21 and 24, 2023, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Eli’s filed the two instant Motions:

 

(1)   Motion to Compel Deposition of Gannon Brown and Production of Vehicle for Inspection and Order Imposing Monetary Sanctions of $3,633.80 (“MTC Deposition”);

(2)   Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle for Inspection and Impose Monetary Sanctions of $5,016.30 (“MTC Production”).

 

On September 1, 2023, the Court granted Eli’s Ex Parte Application for Order Continuing Trial (and All Related Dates), or in the Alternative, an Order Shortening Time for Hearing of Same.  (9-1-23 Minute Order.)  The Court ordered the trial continued to November 30, 2023, along with all related discovery and trial deadlines.  (Ibid.)

 

On September 5, 2023, Defendant/Cross-Complainant Brown filed a single Opposition to both Motions.  No reply has been filed.  On September 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Reply to the Opposition.  The Court did not receive the Reply prior to the September 18, 2023, hearing on the Motion.  On September 18, 2023, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion to further deliberate on the matter of sanctions.  (9-18-23 Minute Order.)

 

Eli’s filed an Answer to the SACC on September 5, 2023.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.    Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Eli’s moves to compel the attendance of Defendant/Cross-Complainant Brown at an oral deposition and the production of the subject 2017 Porsche 911 Carrera (“Porsche”) for physical and photographic inspection at the deposition.  (MTC Dep. p. 2.)  Eli’s also requests that the Court impose monetary sanctions in the amount of $3,633.80 against Brown and his counsel for their willful failure to appear at the deposition and produce the tangible item requested.  (Ibid.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450(a) provides:

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.”

 

The motion shall “(1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice” and “(2) be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450(b)(2).)

 

On July 20, 2023, Eli’s served Brown’s counsel with a Notice of Deposition and Demand for Inspection of Vehicle, setting the deposition and inspection for August 3, 2023, at 1:30 p.m.  (Thomas Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. 1.)  On July 21, 2023, Brown’s attorney served the following three objections to the Notice.  (Ibid. at, Ex. 2.)  Counsel objected on the basis that (1) the deposition was set after the discovery cut-off date, (2) the date of inspection was not at least 30 days after service of the Demand for Inspection, and (3) the notice failed to provide a reasonable place of inspection.  (Ibid.)  Eli’s argues that the “objections are specious and without any colorable legal justification” as the discovery cutoff date is August 29, 2023, and providing 30-day notice and location of inspection are not requirements for depositions.  (MTC Dep. p. 4.)  Eli’s adds that the attorneys for each party met and conferred telephonically in good faith regarding the motion and Defendant’s objections on August 16, 2023.  (Thomas Dec.)  However, the attorneys could not come to an agreement and Defendant Brown refuses to submit to a deposition or produce the Porsche for inspection.  (Ibid.)

 

Eli’s argues that it has a right to depose Defendant and request inspection of tangible things pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.010 and has properly served a notice of deposition.  (MTC Dep. p. 5.)  The inspection was to take place at Eli’s in Inglewood and the deposition would take place in the conference room.  (Ibid.)  The location was selected due to its convenience to both parties, special lighting booths for visual and photographic inspection, and because it was the same location where Defendant Brown approved the color match for painting of the Porsche.  (Ibid.)  Eli’s has good cause for demanding production and inspection of the Porsche because it will produce evidence of the matching paint color for the purpose of trial.  (Ibid. at pp. 5-6.)  Moreover, Defendant’s objections are “without any factual or legal justification.”  (Ibid. at p. 6.)

Despite earlier objections, Defendant Brown does not oppose the Motion.  (Oppos. p. 3.)  However, he expresses concerns that “Plaintiff would exploit the inspection of the vehicle as an opportunity to refuse to release the vehicle back to him, and instead claim that it had the right to retain possession pursuant to a mechanic’s lien.”  (Ibid. at p. 4.)  The concern arises from the demands made in the Complaint regarding possession of the vehicle.  (Ibid.)  Defense counsel has offered to produce the vehicle in a police or sheriff’s department parking lot, or other neutral outdoor location; however, Plaintiff’s counsel has refused.  (Ibid.)  Defense counsel has also requested a stipulation to a court order to have the vehicle released immediately after inspection or after 30 minutes of inspection, however Plaintiff’s counsel has again refused.  (Ibid. at pp. 4-5.)  Thus, Defendant agrees to the inspection either at a neutral outdoor location or with the condition that the vehicle be released after the conclusion of inspection.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)

 

In its Reply, Eli’s reiterates that Defendant’s objections are specious and without merit.  (Reply p. 4.)  It again states that the visual inspection location is critical because it is the same location where Defendant approved the paint color to be applied to the Porsche and therefore, “is a critical part of [Plaintiff’s] case.”  (Ibid.)  Defendant has not presented a justifiable reason and admissible evidence in refusing to submit to the deposition and producing the vehicle for inspection.  (Ibid. at pp. 4-5.)

 

On September 18, 2023, the Court found that Plaintiff has satisfied the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.450 and is entitled to compel Plaintiff’s attendance and testimony at deposition.  (9-18-23 Minute Order.)  Plaintiff has also presented good cause for compelling production of the vehicle for inspection.  (Ibid.)

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Deposition and Production is GRANTED.  Defendant is ordered to appear at a properly noticed deposition, within thirty (30) days of the Court’s Order.  Furthermore, the Court imposes the condition that the vehicle be immediately returned to Defendant after conclusion of inspection or upon expiration of two (2) hours, whichever occurs first.

 

B.     Requests for Production

 

A party must respond to requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).)  If a party to whom requests for production of documents is directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c).)  The party also waives the right to make any objections, including ones based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2024.020(a), 2031.300.)  No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

On August 9, 2022, Plaintiff served Defendant with a Demand for Inspection and Production of Vehicle, set for September 12, 2022, at Eli’s repair facility in Inglewood, CA.  (MTC Prod. - Thomas Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 1.)  Defendant did not respond or serve an objection to the Demand.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  On September 8, 2022, defense counsel asked for a three-week extension to respond to the written discovery and to reschedule the vehicle inspection date.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. 3.)  On September 12, 2022, the attorneys discussed rescheduling the vehicle inspection date and agreed to schedule to a mutually convenient date, September 30.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)  Plaintiff’s counsel served Defendant with an amended Demand for Inspection and Production of Vehicle, set for September 30, 2022.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7, Ex. 2.)  On September 27, 2022, defense counsel asked for an extension to respond to the written discovery, but agreed to keep the inspection date of September 30, 2022; Plaintiff’s counsel provided a one-week extension to respond to the written discovery.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  Plaintiff’s counsel traveled more than 3.5 hours for the inspection on September 30, 2022, however, Defendant did not appear with the vehicle.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9-10.)  On October 3, 2022, defense counsel explained that Defendant’s failure to appear was due to a miscommunication between Defendant and his attorney.  (Ibid. at ¶ 11.)  On December 9, 2022, the attorneys met and conferred over the telephone regarding the instant Motion.  (Ibid. at ¶ 12.)  On December 15, 2022, defense counsel offered to present the vehicle for inspection on January 10, 2023, thus, Plaintiff served a Second Amended Demand for Inspection of Vehicle.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 13-14, Exs. 3-4.)  Despite several extensions, Defendant served an objection to the Second Amended Demand for Inspection.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 15-16, Ex. 5.)  Between January 5 and 9, 2023, the attorneys exchanged several meet and confer emails regarding Defendant’s objection and insistence that conditions be imposed on the inspection.  (Ibid. at ¶ 17.)  On January 10, 2023, Defendant Brown failed to present the Porsche for inspection.  (Ibid. at ¶ 18.)  On August 16, 2023, the attorneys met and conferred telephonically but did not come to an agreement.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 19-20.)

 

Plaintiff states that it has a good faith basis for inspection of the Porsche as “a visual and photographic inspection of the Porsche at the same place and under the same conditions s when Mr. Brown approved the color match is of critical importance in this case.”  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 21-22.)

 

Defendant presents the same objections to the instant Motion as he does to the Motion to Compel Deposition and Production of Vehicle.

 

In its Reply, Plaintiff argues that Defendant Brown waived all objections to the production demand and did not present any legal justification for failure to produce the vehicle for inspection.  (Reply p. 4.)  Furthermore, Defendant’s arguments regarding his fear that Eli’s would retain the vehicle are without justification as there is no indication that Plaintiff would unlawfully seize the Porsche.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)  The Complaint states that Plaintiff was seeking a Court order to possess the vehicle, “not the lawless theft of the Porsche.”  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff has not agreed to any other location for the visual inspection, because inspection of the vehicle in the exact location where Defendant approved the paint color is critical to the lawsuit.  (Ibid. at pp. 5-6.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that it propounded a demand for production on Defendant and Defendant has improperly served objections, after objections had been waived, and has failed to produce the vehicle for inspection.  Although Plaintiff was not obligated to meet and confer with Defendant, Plaintiff’s counsel has extensively attempted to resolve the issue informally.

 

For this reason, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle for Inspection.  The vehicle must be produced for inspection at a time and date determined by Plaintiff.  To address Defendant’s concerns, the Court imposes the condition that the vehicle be immediately returned to Defendant after conclusion of inspection or upon expiration of two (2) hours, whichever occurs first.

 

C.    Request for Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §¿2023.010(d)).

 

Furthermore, §§ 2025.450 and 2031.300 authorize the Court to impose monetary sanctions if a party fails to respond to requests for production or attend a scheduled deposition, unless the Court finds that the party subject to the sanctions acted with substantial justification or that the circumstances make imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

            Plaintiff requests $3,633.80 in sanctions for the Motion to Compel Defendant Brown’s Deposition, as follows: attorney’s fees for five (5) hours for preparing the Motion, three (3) hours for reviewing the Opposition and preparing a reply, and one (1) hour for appearing at the hearing, at a discounted hourly rate of $395 per hour, plus $78.80 in motion filing fees and electronic filing charges.  (Thomas Decl. ¶¶ 16-19.)

 

Plaintiff requests $5,016.30 for the Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle, as follows: attorney’s fees for 2.5 hours in traveling to the inspection location, one (1) hour for waiting for Defendant Brown, five (5) hours for preparing the Motion, three (3) hours for reviewing the Opposition and preparing a reply, and one (1) hour for appearing at the hearing, at a discounted hourly rate of $395 per hour, plus $78.80 in motion filing fees and electronic filing charges.  (Thomas Decl. ¶¶ 23-27.)

 

Defendant opposes Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions.

 

First, Defendant argues that Plaintiff waited too long to notice the deposition.  (Oppos. p. 5.)  Trial in the instant case was originally set for October 11, 2022.  (Ibid.)  Discovery proceedings should have been completed on or before the 30th day and discovery motions should have been filed on or before the 15th day before trial, as required by Code of Civil Procedure §2024.020(a).  (Ibid.)   Thus, the motion cut-off date was on October 11, 2022.  (Ibid. at pp. 5-6.)  The fact that the Court vacated and postponed the trial date does not alter the discovery deadlines, as discovery may be reopened via a noticed motion.  (Ibid. at p. 6.)

 

Second, the deposition did not provide sufficient statutory notice, as notice was served on July 20, 2023, for an inspection set for August 3, 2023.  (Ibid. at p. 7.)  Thus, the Notice failed to comply with Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.030(c)(2), which requires notice to be served as least 30 days before the inspection date.  (Ibid.)  Although the Court issued an ex parte order regarding re-opening discovery on September 1, 2023, this order did not exist at the time the parties met and conferred.  (Ibid.)  Given that “the ground has shifted under the Defendant’s feet,” Defendant no longer opposes the Motions.  (Ibid.)  However, Defendant does request that the Court does not impose monetary sanctions.  (Ibid.)

 

In its Reply to Defendant’s opposition to sanctions imposed for the Motion to Compel Deposition, Plaintiff argues that on September 7, 2023, the Court vacated trial and set a Trial Setting Conference for January 19, 2023.  (MTC Dep. – Reply p. 3.)  Months later, a new trial date of September 28, 2023, was scheduled.  (Ibid.)  On August 30, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Ex Parte Application to continue the trial date along with related discovery and motion cut-off dates.  (Ibid.)  Thus, Defendant’s opposition to the sanctions is legally unsupported and irrelevant.  (Ibid. at pp. 3-4.)  Moreover, Plaintiff argues that only a 10-day notice is required when a request for production is made in a notice of deposition.  (Ibid. at p. 5.)

 

In its Reply to Defendant’s opposition to sanctions imposed for the Motion to Compel Production, Plaintiff argues that justice requires Defendant to reimburse Plaintiff for attorney’s fees incurred in “wasted travel and waiting time at the inspection site” as Defendant did not appear after confirming that he would produce the vehicle for inspection.  (MTC Prod. - Reply p. 7.)  Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s conduct “is part of a larger pattern of deliberate misuse of the discovery process – by Defense counsel on behalf of Mr. Brown to delay and obstruct discovery.”  (Ibid.)  Accordingly, the Court has a sufficient basis to grant sanctions for Defendant’s misuse of discovery.  (Ibid. at pp. 7-8.)  Moreover, Defendant has not presented any facts demonstrating that he acted with substantial justification or that the circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.  (Ibid. at pp. 8-9.)

 

Having reviewed the parties’ arguments, the Court makes the following findings.

Plaintiff has complied with the requirements of the instant Motions and Defendant does not oppose the Motions.  Moreover, the Court does not find that Defendant has presented substantial justification or circumstances that would make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

However, the Court does find that Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is excessive.  Accordingly, the Court grants sanctions as follows.

 

For the Motion to Compel Deposition, the Court grants sanctions in the amount of $1,263.80 for one (1) hour of preparing the Motion, one (1) hour of preparing the Reply, and one (1) hour for appearing at the hearing, at a rate of $395 per hour, plus $78.80 in motion filing fees and electronic filing charges.

 

For the Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle, the Court grants sanctions in the amount of $1,263.80 for one (1) hour of preparing the Motion, one (1) hour of preparing the Reply, and one (1) hour for appearing at the hearing, at a rate of $395 per hour, plus $78.80 in motion filing fees and electronic filing charges.  The Court also grants sanctions in the amount of $500 for Defendant’s failure to produce the vehicle for inspection at a previously confirmed time and date, for a total of $1,763.80 in sanctions.

 

III.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Eli’s Collision Repair of South Bay’s Motion to Compel Defendant’s Deposition and Production of Vehicle for Inspection.  Defendant is ordered to appear at a properly noticed deposition, within thirty (30) days of the Court’s Order.  Furthermore, the Court imposes the condition that the vehicle be immediately returned to Defendant after conclusion of inspection or upon expiration of two (2) hours, whichever occurs first.  The Court also grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $1263.80, to be paid by Defendant Brown within thirty (30) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Eli’s Collision Repair of South Bay’s Motion to Compel Production of Vehicle for Inspection.  The vehicle must be produced for inspection at a time and date determined by Plaintiff.  To address Defendant’s concerns, the Court imposes the condition that the vehicle be immediately returned to Defendant after conclusion of inspection or upon expiration of two (2) hours, whichever occurs first.  The Court also grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $1763.80, to be paid by Defendant Brown within thirty (30) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

Case Number: 21STLC04630    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

Nerfitte Wanzo v. Dameon Powell

21stlc04630

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On June 21, 2021, Plaintiff Nerfitte Wanzo (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Dameon Powell (“Defendant”) for motor vehicle and general negligence arising out of an alleged motor vehicle accident on June 1, 2019.

 

On October 27, 2021, pursuant to Plaintiff’s request, the Court entered default against Defendant.  (10-27-21 Request for Entry of Default.)  On February 15, 2023, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Set Aside Default and vacated the default.  (2-15-23 Minute Order.)  Defendant filed an Answer on the same day.

 

On November 17, 2021, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Related Case.

 

On May 8, 2023, the Court denied Counsel Philip Paul DeLuca’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel without prejudice.  (5-8-23 Minute Order.)

 

On May 10, 2023, Counsel DeLuca filed the instant Motion to be Relieved as Counsel as to Plaintiff Nerfitte Wanzo (“Motion”).  No opposition was filed.

 

On July 18, 2023, the Court continued the hearing on the Motion to allow Counsel an opportunity to file supplemental papers showing that Plaintiff was properly served with the moving papers.  (7-18-23 Minute Order.)  On July 20, 2023, Counsel filed supplemental papers.  On August 21, 2023, the Court noted that Counsel had not presented sufficient evidence that Plaintiff was properly served with the moving papers and continued the hearing one final time.  (8-21-23 Minute Order.)  On August 22, 2023, Counsel filed another supplemental declaration.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 284 states that “the attorney in an action…may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination, as follows: (1) upon the consent of both client and attorney…; (2) upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 284; CRC 3.1362.)  The withdrawal request may be denied if it would cause an injustice or undue delay in proceeding; but the court's discretion in this area is one to be exercised reasonably.  (See Mandell v. Superior (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4; Lempert¿v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.)

 

In making a motion to be relieved as counsel, the attorney must comply with procedures set forth in Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362.  The motion must be made using mandatory forms:

 

1.     Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel directed to the client – (MC-051);

2.     Declaration “stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship” the reasons that the motion was brought (MC-052);

3.     Proposed Order (MC-053).

 

(Ibid.)  The forms must be timely filed and served on all parties who have appeared in the case.  (Ibid.)  If these documents are served on the client by mail, there must be a declaration stating either that the address where client was served is “the current residence or business address of the client” or “the last known residence or business address of the client and the attorney has been unable to locate a more current address after making reasonable efforts to do so within 30 days before the filing of the motion to be relieved.”  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(d)(1).)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Counsel Philip Paul DeLuca, Esq. (“DeLuca”) moves to be relieved as counsel as to Plaintiff Nerfitte Wanzo.  Counsel properly filed a Notice of Motion and Motion (MC-051).  Counsel also filed a Declaration in Support of the Motion (MC-052) stating that “[t]here has been a breakdown in the attorney/client relationship that is irreconcilable necessitating that counsel be relieved, as it presents a conflict of interest were the representation to continue.”  (MC-052 ¶ 2.)  Counsel has also filed a Proposed Order, Form MC-053.

 

Defendant has been properly served with the moving papers.  (MC-351 pp. 5-6; MC-352 pp. 3-4; MC-053 pp. 3-4.)

 

On July 18, 2023, the Court noted the following.  (7-18-23 Minute Order.)

 

On Form MC-052, Counsel indicates that Plaintiff Wanzo has been served with the moving papers by mail at her last known address, confirmed within the past 30 days “by other means.”  (MC-052 ¶ 3.)  Counsel explains that he has requested Plaintiff’s mailing address; however, Plaintiff has not responded.  (Ibid. at ¶ 3b(1)(d).)  Thus, Plaintiff has been served by certified mail.  (Ibid.)

 

On June 29, 2023, Counsel submitted the Declaration of Greg Lowther, a paralegal at his firm, detailing his attempts to locate and serve Plaintiff.  Mr. Lowther explains that he sent Plaintiff an email on May 8, 2023, asking her to confirm her last known address; Plaintiff did not respond.  (Lowther Decl. ¶ 1, Ex. 1.)  On May 10, 2023, he emailed Plaintiff the moving papers, but she did not respond.  (Ibid. at ¶ 2, Ex. 2.)  On the same day, Mr. Lowther sent Plaintiff the moving papers by certified U.S. mail to her last known address (661 N. Harbor Blvd., Apt. 128, San Pedro, CA 90731) and a possible new mailing address he retrieved from a Google.com search (2036 W. Martin Luther King, Jr. Blvd., Los Angeles, CA 90062); Plaintiff did not respond.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4, Ex. 3.)  On May 16, 2023, the mail sent to the San Pedro address was returned to sender, indicating that Plaintiff had moved, and listing the following new address: 7426 Del Rio, Vis., Yucca Valley, CA 92284-3610.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. 4.)  Mr. Lowther emailed Plaintiff requesting that she confirm her new Yuca Valley address; however, she did not respond to his email.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6, Ex. 5.)  Mr. Lowther forwarded the moving papers to JPL Attorney Service in order to personally serve Plaintiff at her Yuca Valley address.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7, Ex. 6.)  The process server attempted to serve Plaintiff on May 20, 26, and 30, and June 2 and 5, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8, Ex. 7.)  On June 8, 2023, the process server spoke to Plaintiff’s father at the Yuca Valley address, who stated that Plaintiff was at work and was not around “much.”  (Ibid.)  The process server also left a voicemail message after attempting to contact Plaintiff by phone, but did not receive a response.  (Ibid.)  Finally, on June 29, 2023, Mr. Lowther sent the moving papers to Plaintiff via certified U.S. mail to 7426 Del Rio Vi., Yucca Valley, CA 92284-3610, as well as her email address; Plaintiff has not responded.  (Ibid. at ¶ 9, Ex. 8.)

Mr. Lowther has submitted the Summary of Service provided by JPL Process Service.  (Ibid. at ¶ 8, Ex. 7.)  The Summary lists the attempts made by the process server to personally serve Plaintiff.  (Ibid.)  The last entry, on June 8, 2023, states that the process server spoke to Plaintiff’s father, who explained that Plaintiff was at work.  (Ibid.)  However, this entry also states “PLEASE NOTE DEL RIO VISTA IS NO LONGER, THE NEW ADDRESS IS 7419 PALM AVE.”  (Ibid.)  Mr. Lowther does not address this new address and states that he served Plaintiff at 7426 Del Rio Vi., Yucca Valley, CA 92284-3610.  Thus, the Court noted that it could not discern whether Plaintiff had been properly served and continued the hearing to allow Counsel an opportunity to file supplemental papers explaining why Plaintiff was not served at the last address listed on the process server’s Summary of Service.  (7-18-23 Minute Order.)

 

            On July 20, 2023, Counsel filed the Declaration of Kayla Waymire.  Waymire explains that she is an owner of Kayway Attorney Service and the process server who attempted to serve Plaintiff.  (Waymire Decl. ¶ 1.)  She states that she was not able to serve Plaintiff at the 7426 Del Rio Vis., Yuca Valley, CA 92284-3610 address.  (Ibid.)  The address has changed and is now 7419 Palm Avenue, Yucca Valley, CA 92284, located “in a newer gated community development.”  (Ibid. at ¶ 2.)  After several attempts, Waymire was able to enter the gated community and speak to Plaintiff’s father who told her that Plaintiff is not around much and refused to accept the documents.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 3-4.)  On August 21, 2023, the Court noted that Counsel did not present sufficient evidence showing that the 7426 Del Rio Vis. And 7419 Palm Avenue addresses belong to the same location.  (8-21-23 Minute Order.)  Thus, the Court was not convinced that Plaintiff had been properly served with the moving papers and continued the hearing on the Motion one final time.  (Ibid.)

 

On August 22, 2023, Counsel filed a supplemental declaration of Greg Lowther, a paralegal at his firm.  Mr. Lowther explains that the 7426 Del Rio Vis. Address is owned by Plaintiff’s father Donald Wanzo and is part of a gated community called Desert Visa Community, which is listed under the address 7419 Palm Avenue, Yucca Valley, 92284.  (Supp. Lowther Decl. ¶¶ 3-4, Exs. 3-4.)  This is why Ms. Waymire refers to these addresses interchangeably.  (Ibid.)  Mr. Lowther also states that despite Counsel’s and his firm’s best attempts to locate Plaintiff, they have been unable to confirm contact with Ms. Wanzo.  (Ibid.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s Counsel Philip Paul DeLuca, Esq. has shown that he has attempted to contact and serve Plaintiff through various means and has not been successful.  The Court finds Counsel’s efforts to be sufficient.

 

Accordingly, Counsel Philip Paul DeLuca’s Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel as to Plaintiff Nerfitte Wanzo is GRANTED.  

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Counsel Philip Paul DeLuca’s Motion to be Relieved as Counsel as to Plaintiff Nerfitte Wanzo is GRANTED, and the Order will be signed at the hearing.  “After the order is signed, a copy of the signed order must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.”  (Cal.¿Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)  The Order on this Motion will not be effective “until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.” (Ibid.)

Case Number: 22STLC07940    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

Suresh C. Pathak v. Archana Mazra

22stlc07940

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On November 29, 2022, Plaintiff Suresh C. Pathak (“Plaintiff”), in propria persona, filed an action against Defendant Archana Mazra (“Defendant”) for breach of contract and common counts, arising out of Defendant’s alleged failure to pay Plaintiff for legal services rendered.

 

            On February 17, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion to Strike Entire Complaint. 

 

            On July 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

            The Court denied Defendant’s Motion to Strike; however, the Court, on its own motion, ordered the First Amended Complaint stricken.  (8-3-23 Minute Order.)

 

            On August 28, 2023, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to the Complaint (“Demurrer”).  Defendant also filed an Answer on the same day.

 

            No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is a pleading that may be used to test the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in the complaint.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10.)  There are two types of demurrers – general demurrers and special demurrers.  (See McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 77.)

 

General demurrers can be used to attack pleadings for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e); McKenney, 167 Cal.App.4th at 77.)  Such demurrers can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable; evidence or extrinsic matters are not considered.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 430.30, 430.70; Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the Court admits “all material facts properly pleaded” and “matters which may be judicially noticed,” but does not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].”  (Blank, 39 Cal.3d at 318.)  It gives these facts “a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (Ibid.). At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him.  (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.)  The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint.  (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.)  "If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence."  (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.)

 

Special demurrers can be used to attack the pleadings on grounds that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible, or in a contract case, for failure to allege whether a contract is oral or written.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  However, special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction civil actions and any grounds for special demurrers must be raised as affirmative defenses in the answer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 92(c).)

 

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).)  The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(2).)  Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.”  (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)  Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue.  (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227 ("Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.").)

 

III.            Discussion

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

Defense counsel states that on August 24, 2023, prior to filing the instant Demurrer, his office attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff regarding the Demurrer by phone.  (Antonyan Decl. ¶ 3.)  Attorney Ben Hillel called Plaintiff and left a voicemail around 3:45 p.m.  (Ibid.)  Attorney Hillel also sent an email to Plaintiff.   (Ibid. at ¶ 4, Ex. 1.)  On August 28, 2023, Plaintiff called defense counsel’s office and insisted that Defendant file an Answer to the Complaint; he did not state whether he would amend the Complaint.  (ibid. at ¶ 5.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant’s counsel’s declaration is sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement.

 

B.     Plaintiff’s Allegations

 

Plaintiff has filed a form complaint with the following allegations.  Around August 2015, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a written agreement whereby Plaintiff agreed to represent Defendant in Los Angeles Superior Court, Case No. 17STPB00965, consolidated with Case No. BP164651, at an hourly rate of $250.  (Compl. BC-1.)  Around December 2018, Defendant breached the agreement by failing to pay a sum of $16,971 for the legal services rendered by Plaintiff.  (Ibid. at BC-2.)  Plaintiff performed all obligations pursuant to the agreement.  (Ibid. at BC-3.)  Defendant breached the agreement and as a result, Plaintiff suffered damages in the amount of $16,971, plus interest and attorney’s fees according to proof.  (Ibid. at BC-4, BC-5.)

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff sets forth a claim for common counts.  Defendant became indebted to Plaintiff “within the last four years (1) on an open book account for money due” and “within the last four years for work, labor, services and materials rendered at the special instance and request of defendant and for which defendant promised to pay plaintiff the sum of $16,971.”  (Compl. CC-1.)  Plaintiff alleges that $16,971 is the reasonable value due and unpaid to Plaintiff despite Plaintiff’s demand, “plus prejudgment interest at the rate of 10 percent per year from December 1, 2022” and attorney’s fees by agreement or statute according to proof.  (CC-2, CC-3.)

 

C.    Defendant’s Demurrer

 

1.     First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Defendant demurs to the first cause of action for breach of contract on the grounds that the allegations fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for breach of contract and the cause of action is uncertain.  (Dem. p. 3.)  The Court notes that a special demurrer on the grounds of uncertainty is not permitted in a limited civil court.  Thus, the Court will only rule on the demurrer on the grounds that the Complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

Defendant argus that the Complaint does not plead the material terms of the agreement or attach a copy of the written agreement.  (Ibid. at p. 7.)  This “creates immense hardship on the part of the Defendant” in being able to file a complete and coherent response and to include any relevant defenses.  (Ibid. at p. 8.)  Given that Defendant does not have the written agreement in possession, Defendant cannot determine the statute of limitations, matters at issue, the dates, parties, and terms and conditions of the agreement.  (Ibid.)

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.  (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)  “A written contract may be pleaded either by its terms – set out verbatim in the complaint or a copy of the contract attached to the complaint and incorporated therein by reference – or by its legal effect. [Citations.]  In order to plead a contract by its legal effect, plaintiff must ‘allege the substance of its relevant terms.  This is more difficult, for it requires a careful analysis of the instrument, comprehensiveness in statement, and avoidance of legal conclusions.’ [Citation.]”  (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1489.)

 

            The Court finds that the Complaint does not present sufficient facts regarding the terms of the attorney-client agreement.  These terms are not alleged verbatim in the Complaint and a copy of the contract is not attached.

 

            Given that the Complaint has not been previously amended and the First Amended Complaint was ordered stricken, there is a reasonable possibility that these defects can be cured by amendment.

 

            Accordingly, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the first cause of action with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

 

2.     Second Cause of Action – Common Counts

 

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for common counts on the grounds that the allegations fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for common counts and the cause of action is uncertain.  (Dem. pp. 3-4.)  The Court notes that a special demurrer on the grounds of uncertainty is not permitted in a limited civil court.  Thus, the Court will only rule on the demurrer on the grounds that the Complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action for common counts.

 

Defendant argues that the cause of action “cannot be sustained without a written contract,” especially as California Business and Professions Code § 6148 requires attorneys to have written agreements when foreseeable expenses will exceed $1000.  (Dem. p. 9.)  Moreover, Plaintiff “makes vague references to an open book account for money due for work, labor, services and materials rendered at the special instance and request of defendant” but there are no facts alleged regarding the work and services, when payment for these services became due, and how damages have been calculated.  (Ibid.)  It appears that Plaintiff is asserting this cause of action as an alternative to the breach of contract cause of action, but he cannot do so without the necessary requirements.  (Ibid.)

 

To establish a cause of action for common counts, “[t]he only essential allegations of a common count are ‘(1) the statement of indebtedness in a certain sum, (2) the consideration, i.e., goods sold, work done, etc., and (3) nonpayment…[Citation.]”  (Farmers Ins. Exchange v. Zerin (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 445, 460.)

 

The Court finds that the allegations set forth in the Complaint are not sufficient for a cause of action for common counts.  Although Plaintiff alleges that Defendant owes $16,971 for legal services and Defendant has not paid this sum, the allegations regarding the services rendered are conclusory and do not establish a cause of action for common count.

 

            Given that the Complaint has not been previously amended and the First Amended Complaint was ordered stricken, there is a reasonable possibility that these defects can be cured by amendment.

 

            Accordingly, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the second cause of action with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Defendant Archana Mazra’s Demurrer to the Complaint’s first and second causes of action is SUSTAINED with twenty (20) days leave to amend.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Guita Sassoon v. Steven Kritanovich

23stlc01049

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On February 10, 2023, Plaintiff Guita Sassoon (“Plaintiff”) brought an action against Defendant Steven Krilanovich[1] (“Defendant”) for breach of contract and common counts, arising out of Defendant’s alleged failure to pay rent pursuant to a rental agreement between the parties.

 

On April 26, 2023, Defendant filed a Declaration of Demurring Party in Support of Automatic Extension.

 

On May 26, 2023, Defendant filed a Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.  The Court sustained the Demurrer with twenty (20) days leave to amend.  (6-29-23 Minute Order.)

 

On July 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

On August 7, 2023, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer to the FAC (“Demurrer”).

 

In response, Plaintiff attempted to file the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”); however, the SAC was rejected because Plaintiff could not file another amended complaint without leave of Court.  (8-17-23 Notice of Rejection.)

 

On August 28, 2023, the Court, on its own motion, continued the hearing on the Demurrer to September 21, 2023.  (8-28-23 Minute Order.)

 

On September 8, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Response to Demurrer.  No reply has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

A demurrer is a pleading that may be used to test the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in the complaint.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10.)  There are two types of demurrers – general demurrers and special demurrers.  (See McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 77.)

 

General demurrers can be used to attack pleadings for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e); McKenney, 167 Cal.App.4th at 77.)  Such demurrers can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable; evidence or extrinsic matters are not considered.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §§ 430.30, 430.70; Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the Court admits “all material facts properly pleaded” and “matters which may be judicially noticed,” but does not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation].”  (Blank, 39 Cal.3d at 318.)  It gives these facts “a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (Ibid.). At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him.  (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.)  The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint.  (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.)  "If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence."  (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447.)

Special demurrers can be used to attack the pleadings on grounds that the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, and unintelligible, or in a contract case, for failure to allege whether a contract is oral or written.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).)  However, special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction civil actions and any grounds for special demurrers must be raised as affirmative defenses in the answer.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 92(c).)

 

Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).)  The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(2).)  Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

When a demurrer is sustained, the Court determines whether there is a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  When a plaintiff “has pleaded the general set of facts upon which his cause of action is based,” the court should give the plaintiff an opportunity to amend his complaint, since plaintiff should not “be deprived of his right to maintain his action on the ground that his pleadings were defective for lack of particulars.”  (Reed v. Norman (1957) 152 Cal.App.2d 892, 900.)  Generally, the court will allow leave to amend on at least the first try, unless there is absolutely no possibility of overcoming the issue.  (See Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1227 ("Denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion unless the complaint shows on its face it is incapable of amendment.  [Citation.]  Liberality in permitting amendment is the rule, if a fair opportunity to correct any defect has not been given.").)

 

III.            Discussion

 

A.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

Defense counsel states that on July 21, 2023, he called Plaintiff’s counsel in order to meet and confer prior to filing the instant Motion.  (Stelnick Decl. ¶ 3.)  He was informed that Plaintiff would be filing a second amended complaint.  (Ibid.)  As of the date of the Demurrer, a second amended complaint has not been filed.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds that Defendant’s counsel’s declaration is sufficient to satisfy the meet and confer requirement.

 

B.     Plaintiff’s Allegations

 

Plaintiff has filed a form complaint with the following allegations.  Around March 2020, Plaintiff and Defendant made an oral agreement whereby Defendant would occupy “units belonging to Sasoon in the building located at 7062 Hawthorne Ave., Los Angeles, California 90028 for an agreed rental of $3600 per month.”  (FAC BC-1.)  On or about October 2020, Defendant breached the agreement by “failing to pay rentals then two months in arrears as promised, and monthly thereafter through March 2021.”  (Ibid. at BC-2.)  Plaintiff performed all obligations pursuant to the agreement.  (Ibid. at BC-3.)  Defendant breached the agreement and as a result, Plaintiff suffered damages in the amount of $25,000 in unpaid rent.  (Ibid. at BC-4.)  Plaintiff is also entitled to attorney’s fees by agreement or statute, according to proof.  (Ibid. at BC-5.)

 

Furthermore, Plaintiff sets forth a claim for common counts.  Defendant became indebted to Plaintiff “within the last four years (1) on an open book account for money due (2) because an account was stated in writing by and between plaintiff and defendant in which it was agreed that defendant was indebted to plaintiff.”  (FAC CC-1.)  Defendant also became indebted to Plaintiff “within the last two years (6) for past occupation of Plaintiff’s condominium by defendant for defendant’s benefit.”  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff alleges that $25,000.00 is the reasonable value due and unpaid to Plaintiff despite Plaintiff’s demand, “plus prejudgment interest according to proof from October 2020” and attorney’s fees by agreement or statute.  (CC-2, CC-3.)

 

C.    Defendant’s Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to the Complaint on the following grounds.

 

First, the Complaint “is vague, uncertain, ambiguous and unintelligible.”  (Dem. pp. 3-5.)  It requests damages in the amount of $25,000, which is over the jurisdictional limit in a limited civil action.  (Ibid.)  Plaintiff also demands attorney’s fees, even though there is no lease agreement attached to the FAC that provides for attorney’s fees.  (Ibid.)

 

Second, it cannot be ascertained from the pleadings whether the contract is written, oral, or implied in conduct.  (Ibid. at pp. 3-5.)  There is no indication that there is a provision allowing for attorney’s fees in the agreement.  (Ibid.)

 

D.    Plaintiff’s Opposition

 

Plaintiff opposes Defendant’s Demurrer.  Plaintiff states that the instant action is for breach of oral contract and common counts to collect unpaid rent.  (Oppos. p. 1.)  Plaintiff attempted to file a second amended amendment; however, the pleading was rejected.  (Ibid. at pp. 1-2.)

 

E.     Analysis

 

First, Defendant demurs to the FAC on the basis that it is vague, uncertain, ambiguous and unintelligible and fails to allege whether the contract is oral or written.  However, these are special demurrers that are not permitted in limited jurisdiction actions.

 

Second, Defendant demurs to the FAC on the basis that the Court lacks jurisdiction over the instant action because Plaintiff seeks damages in excess of the jurisdictional limit of a limited civil court.  Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10, a party may demur to a complaint on the basis that the court lacks jurisdiction over the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  This is a form of general demurrer that is permitted in limited civil cases.  According to Code of Civil Procedure § 85(a), an action may be brought as a limited civil case if “[t]he amount in controversy does not exceed twenty-five thousand dollars ($25,000). As used in this section, ‘amount in controversy’ means the amount of the demand, or the recovery sought, or the value of the property, or the amount of the lien, that is in controversy in the action, exclusive of attorneys’ fees, interest, and costs.”  The Court finds that Plaintiff does not seek damages in excess of the jurisdictional limit of a limited court, as the damages sought do not exceed $25,000.  Thus, this Court has jurisdiction over the matter.

 

The Court also notes that Plaintiff has previously amended the Complaint.  In order to file a second amended complaint, Plaintiff must seek leave of Court pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §§ 473(a) and 576.

 

Based on the reasons discussed above, the Court OVERRULES Defendant’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reason,

 

Defendant Steven Krilanovich’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

Case Number: 23STCP01609    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

Richard Bairos, et al. v. 7174 Melrose Avenue, LLC

23stcp01609


ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On May 11, 2023, Petitioners Richard Bairos (“Richard”) and Gary Bairos (“Gary”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed the instant Petition to Compel Arbitration and to Appoint JAMS to Conduct Arbitrator [sic] (“Petition”) against Respondent 7174 Melrose Avenue, LLC (“Melrose”).

 

            On July 13, 2023, Respondent filed a Response to the Petition.

 

            On September 14, 2023, the Court, on its own motion, continued the hearing to September 21, 2023.  (9-14-23 Minute Order.)

 

            No reply has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §1281.2, generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting¿rulings on common issues.

 

When seeking to compel arbitration, the initial burden lies with the moving party to demonstrate the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by prepondernace of evidence.  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841-42; Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021), 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 164-65.)  It is sufficient for the moving party to produce a copy of the arbitration agreement or set forth the agreement’s provisions.  (Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  The burden then shifts to the opposing party to prove by a preponderance of evidence any defense to enforcement of the contract or the arbitration clause.  (Ruiz, 232 Cal.App.4th at 842; Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  Subsequently, the moving party must establish with the preponderance of admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties.  (Ibid.)  The trial court then weighs all the evidence submitted and uses its discretion to make a final determination.  (Ibid.)  “California law, ‘like [federal law], reflects a strong policy favoring arbitration agreements and requires close judicial scrutiny of waiver claims.’”  (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 31.)

 

If the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until arbitration is completed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

The Court notes that Petitioners have not filed proof that Respondent has been served with the Petition and Notice of Hearing.  However, Respondent has filed a Response to the Petition and the Court has waived notice.  (9-14-23 Minute Order.)  Accordingly, the Court proceeds with ruling on the merits of the Petition.

 

Petitioners Richard and Gary Bairos and Respondent Melrose entered into a Standard Industrial/Commercial Multi-Tenant Lease – Net (“Lease”), attached to the Petition as Exhibit A.  (Pet. ¶ 4; Gary Bairos Decl. ¶¶ 2-4, Ex. A.)  Pursuant to the Lease Agreement, Petitioners leased the premises located at 3715 Evans Street, Los Angeles, CA 90027.  (Ibid.)  Included in the Lease Agreement is an Addendum, which contains a two-page AIR CRE Arbitration Agreement along with other addendums.  (Pet. ¶ 4; Gary Bairos Decl. ¶¶ 5-7, Exs. B-D.)  The Arbitration Agreement provides the following:

 

Except as provided in Paragraph B below, the Parties agree to resolve any and all claims, disputes or disagreements arising under this Lease, including, but not limited to any matter relating to Lessor’s failure to approve an assignment, sublease or other transfer or Lessee’s interest in the Lease under Paragraph 12 of this Lease, any other defaults by the Lessor, or any defaults by Lessee by and through arbitration as provided below and irrevocably waive any and all rights to the contrary. The Parties agree to at all times conduct themselves in strict, full, complete and timely accordance with the terms hereof and that any attempt to circumvent the terms of this Arbitration Agreement shall be null and void and of no force or effect whatsoever.

 

(Ibid., Ex. B.)  Moreover, the Arbitration Agreement provides the parties an opportunity to select an arbitrator affiliated with the Judicial Arbitration & Mediation Services, Inc. (“JAMS”) or the American Arbitration Association (“AAA”).  (Ibid.)  The Agreement states that “[i]n the event the Arbitrator is not selected as provided for above for any reason, the party initiating arbitration shall apply to the appropriate Court for the appointment of a qualified retired judge to act as the Arbitrator.”  (Ibid.)

 

            Around March 7 and 17, 2023, disputes arose between the Petitioners and Respondent and Respondent allegedly breached the Lease Agreement.  (Pet. ¶ 6.)  Petitioners argue that Respondent refuses to submit the matters to arbitration with JAMS.  (Ibid. at ¶ 7.)  On May 4, 2023, Petitioners’ counsel served a notice, as required by the Arbitration Agreement, that Petitioners intended to apply for a Court order compelling arbitration, “preferably before JAMS.”  (Ibid. at ¶ 8.)  Petitioners anticipate that Respondent will argue that the parties have agreed to arbitrate the matter before the Honorable Victor H. Person (Retired).  (Ibid. at ¶ 9.)  However, the parties have not reached an agreement and Petitioners “seek to refer the matter to JAMS because that is called for in the Lease.”  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 10-12.)  Petitioners stated that the parties discussed arbitration before JAMS or by mutual agreement, but neither mentioned AAA.  (Ibid. at ¶ 13.)  There was no selection made in the Lease itself, but Petitioners elect to have the matter arbitrated in front of JAMS.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 14-15.)  Petitioners also present that they made efforts to select a neutral arbitrator by mutual agreement; however, no agreement was reached.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 15-22.)

 

            Thus, Petitioners seek a Court order compelling arbitration of the dispute between the parties.  (Pet. p. 7.)  As the parties are unable to agree to a neutral arbitrator, Petitioners request that the Court appoint JAM to arbitrate the matter.  (Ibid.)

 

In their Response, Respondent argues that it does not refuse to arbitrate the matter.  (Response p. 2.)  However, both parties have agreed to arbitrate the matter before Judge Victor Person of ADR Services, Inc.  (Ibid.)  Thus, the Court should appoint Judge Person as the Arbitrator.  (Ibid.)  Respondent adds that, as reflected in the Arbitration Agreement, the parties did not agree in advance to arbitration before JAMS, AAA, or anyone else.  (Ibid.)  Given that the costs to arbitrate in front of JAMS or AAA are “excessive, unreasonable and unconscionable…Respondent vehemently opposes any such arbitration sponsored by either of those entities.”  (Ibid.)  Respondent argues that there is no basis for the Court to enforce binding arbitration in front of JAMS or AAA because “Respondent never agreed to either of those venues” and the Court should not “circumvent the parties’ intent to arbitrate before particular arbitrators.”  (Ibid. at p. 3.)  Thus, the Court should order arbitration before Judge Victor Person, as agreed to by the parties.  (Ibid. at p. 4.)

 

The Court finds that Petitioners have demonstrated that a valid arbitration agreement exists, the right to compel arbitration has not been waived, and there are no grounds for revocation of the agreement, or any pending litigation.  Furthermore, Respondent has expressed that it agrees to arbitration.

 

The matter at issue is the selection of a neutral arbitrator.  Having reviewed the Arbitration Agreement, the Court finds that, although the parties could have selected the venue for arbitration, they did not select any of the options listed in the Agreement.  Moreover, there is a clear dispute regarding the selection of Judge Person as the arbitrator, demonstrating that the parties have not reached an agreement.

 

According to Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.6:

 

If the arbitration agreement provides a method of appointing an arbitrator, that method shall be followed. If the arbitration agreement does not provide a method for appointing an arbitrator, the parties to the agreement who seek arbitration and against whom arbitration is sought may agree on a method of appointing an arbitrator and that method shall be followed. In the absence of an agreed method, or if the agreed method fails or for any reason cannot be followed, or when an arbitrator appointed fails to act and his or her successor has not been appointed, the court, on petition of a party to the arbitration agreement, shall appoint the arbitrator.

 

When a petition is made to the court to appoint a neutral arbitrator, the court shall nominate five persons from lists of persons supplied jointly by the parties to the arbitration or obtained from a governmental agency concerned with arbitration or private disinterested association concerned with arbitration. The parties to the agreement who seek arbitration and against whom arbitration is sought may within five days of receipt of notice of the nominees from the court jointly select the arbitrator whether or not the arbitrator is among the nominees. If the parties fail to select an arbitrator within the five-day period, the court shall appoint the arbitrator from the nominees.

 

Here, the parties have not agreed on a method of appointing the arbitrator, thus, the Court may appoint the arbitrator.  Pursuant to § 1281.6, the Court requests that the parties jointly present a list of potential arbitrators.  Once the list is presented to the Court, the Court will nominate five persons and the parties will have five days from receipt of notice to jointly select the arbitrator. If the parties do not select the arbitrator within five days, the Court will appoint the arbitrator from the list of nominees.

 

Accordingly, the Court continues the hearing on the Petition and orders the parties to submit a list of potential arbitrators.

 

IV.            Conclusion

 

For the reasons discussed above,

 

The hearing on the Petition to Compel Arbitration and Appoint JAMS to Conduct Arbitrator [sic], filed by Petitioner Richard Bairos, et al., is CONTINUED to ______________at ____ a.m. in Department 25 at the SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.  Petitioners and Defendant are ordered to file supplemental papers addressing the issues discussed herein at least 16 court days before the next scheduled hearing.  Failure to do so will result in the Petition being placed off calendar or denied.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Saini Investments LLC v. Mayra A. Martinez, et al.

23stlc01503

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On March 3, 2023, Plaintiffs Saini Investments LLC (“Saini”) and S. Arora Consulting, Inc. (“Arora”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed an action against Defendants Mayra A. Martinez (“Mayra”), Victor Martinez (“Victor”), and Rodolfo Martinez (“Rodolfo”) for breach of contract.

 

On May 8, 2023, Defendant Rodolfo, in propria persona, and Defendant Victor, in propria persona, each filed a separate Answer to the Complaint.

 

On August 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed the two instant Motions:

 

(1)   Motion to Deem Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions as Being Admitted and Request for Monetary Sanctions as to Defendant Victor Martinez (“Deem – Victor”);

(2)   Motion to Deem Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions as Being Admitted and Request for Monetary Sanctions as to Defendant Rodolfo Martinez (“Deem – Rodolfo”).

 

On August 28, 2023, the Court continued the hearing on the Motions to September 21, 2023.  (8-28-23 Minute Order.)  No opposition has been filed to either Motion.

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure 2033.280(b), failure to respond to requests for admission in a timely manner allows the requesting party to “move for an order that…the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted” by the party that failed to respond.  The requesting party’s motion must be granted by the court, “unless [the court] finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280(c).)  Since such motion is in response to failure to respond, there is no requirement to meet and confer prior to moving to deem the requests for admission admitted.  (See Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007), 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.). By failing to timely respond, the party to whom the requests are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or work product. (Code Civ. Pro. § 2033.280(a).)

 

III.            Discussion

 

A.     Motion as to Defendant Victor Martinez

 

Plaintiff moves to deem Requests for Admissions (“RFAs”), Set One, admitted as to Defendant Victor.  (Deem – Victor, p. 1.)

 

On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff served Request for Admissions, Set One, on Defendant Victor.  (Betty Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 1.)  Defendant Victor has not responded to the RFAs and no extensions have been granted.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that it has propounded requests for admission on Defendant Victor and Defendant has not provided any responses.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions as Being Admitted as to Defendant Victor Martinez is GRANTED.

 

B.     Motion as to Defendant Rodolfo Martinez

 

Plaintiff moves to deem Requests for Admissions (“RFAs”), Set One, admitted as to Defendant Rodolfo.  (Deem – Rodolfo, p. 1.)

 

On June 5, 2023, Plaintiff served Request for Admissions, Set One, on Defendant Rodolfo.  (Betty Decl. ¶ 2, Ex. 1.)  Defendant Rodolfo has not responded to the RFAs and no extensions have been granted.  (Ibid. at ¶¶ 3-4.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated that it has propounded requests for admission on Defendant Rodolfo and Defendant has not provided any responses.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions as Being Admitted as to Defendant Rodolfo Martinez is GRANTED.

 

C.    Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d)).

 

Furthermore, courts are obligated to impose monetary sanctions in cases where a “failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”  (Ibid.)  Sanctions are calculated based on “reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (Ibid. § 2023.030(a)).

 

Here, the Court grants Plaintiff’s Motions to Deem Request for Admissions as Being Admitted as to Defendants Victor and Rodolfo Martinez.  Thus, the Court must impose sanctions for Defendants’ failure to respond to the discovery requests.

 

            Plaintiff requests $850 in sanctions to be paid by Defendant Victor Martinez, as follows: two (2) hours in preparing the Motion, at an hourly rate of $395.00, plus $60 in motion filing fees.  (Deem – Victor: Betty Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

            Plaintiff requests $850 in sanctions to be paid by Defendant Rodolfo Martinez, as follows: two (2) hours in preparing the Motion, at an hourly rate of $395.00, plus $60 in motion filing fees.  (Deem – Rodolfo: Betty Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

            The Court finds that Plaintiff’s request is excessive for these simple Motions.  Accordingly, the Court grants $455 in sanctions for one (1) hour of preparing each Motion and $60 for each motion filing fee.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions as follows: $455 in sanctions to be paid by Defendant Victor and $455 in sanctions to be paid by Defendant Rodolfo.

 

IV.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

Plaintiff Saini Investments LLC’s Motion to Deem Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions as Being Admitted as to Defendant Victor Martinez is GRANTED.  Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is also GRANTED in the amount of $455, to be paid by Defendant Victor within thirty (30) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

 

Plaintiff Saini Investments LLC’s Motion to Deem Plaintiff’s Request for Admissions as Being Admitted as to Defendant Rodolfo Martinez is GRANTED.  Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is also GRANTED in the amount of $455, to be paid by Defendant Rodolfo within thirty (30) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

Case Number: 23STLC01692    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

Delbert Cannon v. Douglas Center Partners LLC, et al.

23stlc01692

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On March 14, 2023, Plaintiff Delbert Cannon (“Plaintiff”) filed an action against Defendants Douglas Center Partners LLC (“Douglas Center”), Hojas Premium Tea House, LLC (“Hojas”), Alma Rosa Ortiz aka Alma R. Ortiz aka Alma Ortiz (“Ortiz”) (collectively “Defendants”) for an alleged violation of the Unruh Civil Rights Act.

 

Defendant Douglas Center, in propria persona, filed an Answer to the Complaint on April 21, 2023.  Defendant Ortiz filed an Answer on May 16, 2023.

 

On August 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Compel Defendant Douglas Center Partners LLC’s Answers to Interrogatories (“Motion”).  No opposition has been filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A.     Special Interrogatories

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260(a).)  If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling response to the discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(b).)  Once compelled to respond, the party waives the right to make any objections, including ones based on privilege or work-product protection.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(a).)  There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020(a), 2030.290.)  No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery.  (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.)

 

Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant Douglas Partners to provide verified answers to Special Interrogatories (Set One).  (Mot. p. 1.)  Plaintiff also requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $500.  (Ibid.)

 

On June 15, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant Douglas Partners with Special Interrogatories, Set One.  (Mehrban Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A.)  Plaintiff has not received verified responses to the discovery request.  (Ibid. at ¶ 4.)  Plaintiff’s counsel sent a follow-up email to defense counsel on July 27, 2023.  (Ibid. at ¶ 5, Ex. B.)

Here, Plaintiff has demonstrated that he has propounded special interrogatories on Defendant and Defendant has not provided any responses.  Although Plaintiff was not obligated to meet and confer with Defendant, he has attempted to resolve the issue informally.

 

For this reason, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Douglas Center Partners LLC’s Answers to Interrogatories.

 

B.     Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2023.030(a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone because of that conduct.  Misuse of discovery includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d)).

 

Furthermore, § 2030.290 authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions if a party fails to respond to interrogatories.  Sanctions are calculated based on “reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”  (Ibid. § 2023.030(a)).

 

Plaintiff requests $500 in sanctions for Defendant Douglas Center Partners LLC’s failure to respond to the instant discovery request.  (Mehrban Decl. ¶ 6.)  Plaintiff’s counsel states that his hourly rate is $500.00 per hour, and he has “spent and will spend a cumulative total of no less than one hour in communicating with defense counsel regarding this motion, preparing the motion, and appearing at the hearing on the motion.”  (Ibid.)

 

Given that the Court grants Plaintiff’s Motion, Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions.  Moreover, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s request is reasonable.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $500.

 

C.    Other Orders

 

The Court makes the following additional orders.

 

The Court, on its own motion, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 436 strikes the Answer filed by Defendant Douglas Center Partners LLC.

 

Since the passage of the State Bar Act in 1927, persons may represent their own interests in legal proceedings, but may not represent the interests of another unless they are active members of the State Bar. [Citation.]”  (Hansen v. Hansen (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 618, 621.) An entity must be represented by a lawyer in legal proceedings and may not represent itself (either directly or through a non-lawyer agent) in litigation, as such an act would be the unauthorized practice of law.  (See e.g. Caressa Camille, Inc. v. Alcoholic Beverage Control Appeals Bd. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101) (corporation); Albion River Watershed Protection Ass’n v. Department of Forestry & Fire Protection (1993) 20 Cal. App. 4th 34, 37 (unincorporated association); Aulisio v. Bancroft (2014) 230¿Cal.¿App.¿4th 1518, 1519-20 (trustee for trust).)  Here, Douglas Center filed its Answer through its Managing Member Kathie Hira.  There is no indication that Hira is licensed to practice law in the State of California.  Accordingly, the Court, on its own motion, strikes the Answer filed by Douglas Center.

 

Moreover, the Court sets Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to File Proof of Service for November 30, 2023 at 9:30 a.m., in Department 25 at the Spring Street Courthouse.

 

III.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons:

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff Delbert Cannon’s Motion to Compel Douglas Center Partners LLC’s Answers to Interrogatories.  Defendant Douglas Center Partners LLC is ordered to submit verified responses, without objections, within ten (10) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

 

The Court also GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $500, to be paid within thirty (30) days of notice of the Court’s Order.

 

The Court makes the following additional orders.

 

The Court, on its own motion, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 436, STRIKES the Answer filed by Defendant Douglas Center Partners LLC.

 

Moreover, the Court sets Order to Show Cause Re: Failure to File Proof of Service of Complaint for November 30, 2023 at 9:30 a.m. in Department 25 at the SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23STLC03476    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

NYC Construction, Inc. v. Alumacovers of California, Inc.

23stlc03476

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On May 30, 2023, Plaintiff NYC Constructions, Inc. dba Teamwork Remodeling Group (“NYC Construction”) filed an action against Defendant Alumacovers of California, Inc. (“Alumacovers”) for (1) breach of contract, (2) California Commercial Code § 2201, (3) Business and Professions Code § 17538(D), (4) Business and Professions Code § 7159, and (5) unjust enrichment.

 

            On June 30, 2023, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration of Complaint and Stay Proceedings (“Motion”).  Plaintiff filed an Opposition on July 10, 2023, and Defendant filed a Reply on August 21, 2023.

 

            On September 11, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

 

II.              Legal Standard

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §1281.2, generally, on a petition to compel arbitration, the court must grant the petition unless it finds either (1) no written agreement to arbitrate exists; (2) the right to compel arbitration has been waived; (3) grounds exist for revocation of the agreement; or (4) litigation is pending that may render the arbitration unnecessary or create conflicting¿rulings on common issues.

 

When seeking to compel arbitration, the initial burden lies with the moving party to demonstrate the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by prepondernace of evidence.  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 841-42; Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021), 72 Cal.App.5th 158, 164-65.)  It is sufficient for the moving party to produce a copy of the arbitration agreement or set forth the agreement’s provisions.  (Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  The burden then shifts to the opposing party to prove by a preponderance of evidence any defense to enforcement of the contract or the arbitration clause.  (Ruiz, 232 Cal.App.4th at 842; Gamboa, 72 Cal.App.5th at 165.)  Subsequently, the moving party must establish with the preponderance of admissible evidence a valid arbitration agreement between the parties.  (Ibid.)  The trial court then weighs all the evidence submitted and uses its discretion to make a final determination.  (Ibid.)  “California law, ‘like [federal law], reflects a strong policy favoring arbitration agreements and requires close judicial scrutiny of waiver claims.’”  (Wagner Const. Co. v. Pacific Mechanical Corp. (2007) 41 Cal.4th 19, 31.)

 

If the court orders arbitration, then the court shall stay the action until arbitration is completed.  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)

 

III.            Discussion

 

Defendant seeks to a Court order compelling Defendant to arbitrate a dispute arising out of a written agreement entered into between Plaintiff and Defendant.  (Pet. pp. 3-4.)  Defendant also requests that the Court stay the proceedings pending arbitration.  (Ibid.)

As a preliminary matter, the Court notes that Defendant has filed a defective Notice of Motion that lists the wrong courthouse and judge presiding over the matter.  Accordingly, the Court continues the hearing on the Motion and orders Defendant to correct the Notice, listing the Honorable Thomas J. Griego as the judge presiding over the hearing in Department 25 of the Spring Street Courthouse, located at 312 N. Spring Street, Los Angeles, CA 90012. 

IV.            Conclusion

 

For the reasons discussed above,

 

The hearing on the Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings, filed by Defendant Alumacovers of California, Inc., is CONTINUED to October 31, 2023 at 10:30 a.m., in Department 25 at the SPRING STREET COURTHOUSE.  Defendant is ordered to file supplemental papers addressing the issues discussed herein at least 16 court days before the next scheduled hearing.  Failure to do so will result in the Motion being placed off calendar or denied.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice. 

Case Number: LAM12CG6221    Hearing Date: September 21, 2023    Dept: 25

Edward Richard Sullivan III v. Leo Pido Dano

LAM12CG6221

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                Background

 

On December 7, 2018, a money judgment was renewed in the amount of $4,001.53 in favor of Plaintiff and Assignee of Record Edward Richard Sullivan III (“Assignee”) and against Defendant and Judgment Debtor Leo Pido Dano (aka Leo Dano) (“Judgment Debtor”).

 

On July 11, 2023, Assignee, in propria persona, filed the instant Motion for an Order Allowing Costs for Enforcement of Judgment, in the amount of $265.00 (“Motion”).

 

No opposition was filed.

 

II.              Legal Standard & Discussion

 

A judgment creditor is entitled to recover the reasonable and necessary costs incurred in enforcing a judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 685.040.)  Costs authorized by § 685.040 may be claimed by filing a memorandum of costs or via noticed motion before the judgment is satisfied in full but not later than two years after the costs have been incurred.  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 685.070(b), 685.080(a).) Allowed costs include, but are not limited to, those costs outlined in § 685.070. (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080(a).)  The notice of motion must describe the costs claimed, must state their amount, and must be supported by “an affidavit of a person who has knowledge of the facts stating that to the person’s best knowledge and belief the costs are correct, and reasonable and necessary, and have not been satisfied.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080(b).)  The motion must be served personally or by mail. (Ibid.)  The court must make an order allowing or disallowing the costs to the extent justified under the circumstances of that particular case. (Code Civ. Proc., § 685.080(c).)

 

Assignee Sullivan seeks to recover costs of $265.00 incurred in enforcing the Judgment.  (7-11-23 Sullivan Decl. ¶ 1.)  The costs sought are broken down as follows (1) $65 in service fees to hire a registered process server in October 28, 2022, to serve Debtor with four Notices of Renewal of Judgment, dated November 9, 1995, November 15, 2005, November 20, 2012, and January 22, 2019; (2) $50 to serve Debtor with Notice of Court’s November 10, 2022, Ruling, and Notice of Prevailing Party’s Proposed Order, dated November 12, 2022; (3) $50 service fee for serving Debtor with February 2023, Memorandum of Costs; (5) $50 fee to serve Debtor with June 2023 Memorandum of Costs; and (6) $50 service fee for serving the instant Motion.  (7-11-23 Sullivan Decl. pp. 4-6; Tables 1-3.)

 

The Motion is timely and is supported by an affidavit from Assignee Sullivan explaining the basis for the costs incurred and attesting that the costs incurred are reasonable and necessary and have not been satisfied.  (7-11-23 Sullivan Decl. ¶¶ 4, 6-7.)

 

Having reviewed the costs sought, the Court finds that these costs are reasonable and necessary to enforce the Judgment against Judgment Debtor.  Thus, Assignee Sullivan’s Motion is GRANTED in the amount of $265.

 

III.            Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Assignee Edward Richard Sullivan III’s Motion for an Order Allowing Costs for Enforcement of Judgment is GRANTED in the sum of $265.00.
