DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00396    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION - SET ONE ADMITTED and COMPEL RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES - SET TWO; SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES - SET TWO; AND REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION - SET THREE and GRANTING MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.                INTRODUCTION

This is a sexual assault action brought by Plaintiff Jane Doe 8004 (“Plaintiff”) against Defendant Ron Thorn (“Defendant”). On February 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed this action asserting claims for (1) Sexual Assault, (2) Sexual Battery, and (3) Gender Violence. Defendant filed an answer to the complaint on September 15, 2023.

During discovery, Defendant served Plaintiff with four discovery requests: 1) Requests for Admission- Set One; 2) Form Interrogatories - Set Two 3) Special Interrogatories - Set Two and 4) Requests for Admission- Set One. Defendant has filed four motions to compel Plaintiff to serve verified responses and produce documents and seeks monetary sanctions for a total of $10,958.36. The Court will address these motions in one tentative ruling as follows:

            II.        REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS – RFA Set One

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.010.)¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared . . . .”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250(a).)  

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).) The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).) 

It is well-settled that “unsworn responses are tantamount to no responses at all.”  (Appleton v. Superior Court (1888) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636; Zorro Inv. Co. v. Great Pacific Securities Corp. (1977) 69 Cal.App.3d 907, 914.)   

Defendant served RFAs - Set One on Plaintiff on September 24, 2024, with responses due by October 28, 2024. Plaintiff’s former counsel requested a one-week extension, extending the deadline to November 4, 2024. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶¶ 2-4; Exh. A.) Despite this extension and further correspondence, Plaintiff failed to serve any responses. Defense counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter on November 5, 2024, offering an additional courtesy extension to November 12, 2024, but Plaintiff did not respond. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶¶ 7-9; Exh. B.)

As of the date of this motion, Plaintiff has not served verified responses. Plaintiff’s failure to participate in discovery obstructs the litigation process and violates her statutory obligations. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.220.)

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280(b), if a party fails to serve timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order that the matters specified in the RFAs be deemed admitted. The court must grant this motion unless verified responses are served before the hearing.

Because no verified responses have been filed as of the date of the hearing, Defendant’s motion to deem the RFAs admitted must be granted.

Accordingly, Defendant’s request to deem the RFAs admitted is GRANTED.

 

III.  INTERROGATORIES – Form Interrogatories– Set Two and Special Interrogatories – Set Two,

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(a), if a party fails to respond to interrogatories, they waive any objections, including those based on privilege or work product protection. When a party does not provide timely responses, the propounding party is entitled to move for an order compelling responses. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290(b), the Court must order the responding party to provide responses without objections.

Here, on September 24, 2024, Defendant served Form Interrogatories – Set Two and Special Interrogatories - Set Two, on Plaintiff, with responses due by October 28, 2024. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s former counsel requested and received a one-week extension, making the new deadline November 4, 2024. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶ 4.) However, Plaintiff failed to serve responses by the extended deadline. Defense counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter on November 5, 2024, granting Plaintiff a final extension to November 12, 2024, but Plaintiff again failed to respond. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶ 7; Exh. B.) As of the filing date of this motion, Plaintiff has not served any responses, and thus the motion to compel responses without objections is appropriate.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants motion to compel Form Interrogatories - Set Two and Special Interrogatories - Set Two.

 

IV.       REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION – Request for Production - Set Three

A propounding party may move to compel responses to requests for production of documents where the responding party fails to provide responses. (CCP § 2031.300.) The responding party must provide responses within thirty days after the demand is served. (CCP § 2031.030(c)(2) & (3).) The responding party waives all objections, including privilege and work product, by failing to timely respond to requests for production of documents. (CCP § 2031.300.) 

Defendant served Requests for Production - Set Three, on September 24, 2024, making responses due on or about October 28, 2024. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. A.) Plaintiff’s former counsel requested and received a one-week extension, moving the deadline to November 4, 2024. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶ 4.) On November 5, 2024, after receiving no responses, defense counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter granting a final extension to November 12, 2024. (Gutierrez Decl., ¶¶ 6-7; Exh. B.) Plaintiff, now self-represented, has failed to respond as of the filing of this motion.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendants motion to compel Request for Production – Set Three.

V.        SANCTIONS

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to deem request for admissions as admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) [the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed].) 

CCP § 2023.030 authorizes the Court to issue sanctions against a party engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process. Failure to respond to discovery, evasive responses, and objections lacking substantial justification are “misuses of the discovery process.” (CCP § 2023.010, subd. (d)-(f).) 

In this case, Plaintiff failed to provide timely, verified responses to 1) Requests for Admission- Set One; 2) Form Interrogatories - Set Two 3) Special Interrogatories - Set Two and 4) Requests for Admission- Set One. Despite multiple meet-and-confer efforts, Plaintiff has neither remedied these deficiencies nor provided any substantial justification for failing to do so. As a result, Defendant filed four motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses and requested monetary sanctions for each motion.

Defendant’s counsel seeks the following monetary sanctions: 1) $2,315.84 for Requests for Admission - Set One, reflecting 8.65 hours at $250 per hour for attorney work, 0.4 hours at $150 per hour for paralegal work, and a $93.34 filing fee; 2)  $2,183.34 for Form Interrogatories - Set Two, reflecting 8.12 hours at $250 per hour for attorney work, 0.4 hours at $150 per hour for paralegal work, and a $93.34 filing fee; 3) 2,238.34 for Special Interrogatories - Set Two, reflecting 8.34 hours at $250 per hour for attorney work, 0.4 hours at $150 per hour for paralegal work, and a $93.34 filing fee; 4) $4,220.84 for Requests for Production - Set Three, reflecting 14.59 hours at $250 per hour for attorney work, 0.5 hours at $600 per hour for senior attorney review, 1.2 hours at $150 per hour for paralegal work, and a $93.34 filing fee. The total sanctions requested amount to $10,958.36.

After reviewing the complexity of these motions and noting that no opposition has been filed, the Court finds the requested sanctions excessive. These motions are straightforward and all will be heard together. The Court concludes that awarding full sanctions for each motion is unnecessary. Instead, the Court awards reduced sanctions as follows: 6 total hours across all motions for attorney work at $250 per hour ($1,500), 1.6 total hours for paralegal work at $150 per hour ($240), and $93.34 in filing fees for each motion, totaling $373.36.

Accordingly, the Court awards a total monetary sanction against Plaintiff in the amount of $2,113.36.

VI.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 Motion by Defendant to deem Requests for Admission, Set One, admitted, and Motion to Compel responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Two; Special Interrogatories, Set Two; and  Requests for Production, Set Three, are GRANTED.

Motion by Defendant for compelling monetary sanctions is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $2,113.36.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide responses and pay sanctions within 20 days.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order.

Case Number: 23AHCV01742    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT STATE FARM’S MOTION FOR PROTECTIVE ORDER

I.          INTRODUCTION

This action arises from an automobile accident that occurred on or about April 30, 2020. On July 31, 2023, Plaintiffs Lawanda Jackson, Kayzah Jackson, and Kaylah Jackson filed a Complaint against Defendant State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance (“State Farm”) and State Farm General Insurance Company alleging causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and (3) Fraud.

On August 16, 2023, Plaintiffs Lawanda Jackson and Kayzah Jackson (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a Motion to Compel Arbitration. Subsequently, on December 5, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the operative First Amended Complaint against Defendants State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance and State Farm General Insurance Company, again alleging causes of action for: (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and (3) Fraud.

On March 29, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ Motion to Compel Arbitration. On June 14, 2024, the Court denied Plaintiffs’ Motion for Reconsideration of that ruling.

On August 28, 2024, State Farm filed a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings, which remains pending. On November 14, 2024, State Farm filed the instant Motion for Protective Order. Plaintiffs filed their Opposition on November 27, 2024, and State Farm filed its Reply on December 6, 2024.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

CCP section 2017.020 provides that “[t]he court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020(a).)  “The court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.”  (Id.) 

            A responding party may move for a protective order with respect to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, requests for admissions, and depositions and a court may make any order that justice requires to protect a party from “unwanted annoyance, embarrassment or oppression or undue burden and expense.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.420, 2030.090, 2031.060, 2033.080.)  The burden of proof is on the party seeking the protective order to show “good cause” for the order he or she seeks.  (Fairmont Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) 

            “In accordance with the liberal policies underlying the discovery procedures, California courts have been broad-minded in determining whether discovery is reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 172; Pettie v. Superior Court (1960) 178 Cal.App.2d 680, 687.) As a practical matter, it is difficult to define at the discovery stage what evidence will be relevant at trial. Therefore, the party seeking discovery is entitled to substantial leeway. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co., supra, 2 Cal.3d at p. 172.) Furthermore, California’s liberal approach to permissible discovery generally has led the courts to resolve any doubt in favor of permitting discovery. (Id. at p. 173.) In doing so, the courts have taken the view if an error is made in ruling on a discovery motion, it is better that it be made in favor of granting discovery of the nondiscoverable rather than denying discovery of information vital to preparation or presentation of the party's case or to efficacious settlement of the dispute.” (Norton v. Superior Court (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1750, 1761.) 

III.      ANALYSIS

State Farm moves for a protective order pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.420, contending that requiring its claims handler, Trish Kero, and counsel, Richard Knapp, to sit for depositions and produce documents would impose undue burden, annoyance, and expense. State Farm asserts that the information sought in these depositions is irrelevant to the pending motion for judgment on the pleadings, which is scheduled for hearing on January 31, 2025. (Motion, p.10)

State Farm filed its motion for judgment on the pleadings to resolve the case on the grounds that Plaintiffs failed to timely demand arbitration, as required by their insurance policy and Insurance Code section 11580.2. This failure, State Farm argues, which was the basis for the Court’s denial of Plaintiffs’ prior motion to compel arbitration and motion for reconsideration, bars Plaintiffs’ claims as a matter of law. According to State Farm, these prior rulings constitute an adjudication on the merits of Plaintiffs’ claims because the statute of limitations for demanding arbitration is a condition precedent to any cause of action arising under the policy. Consequently, State Farm contends that the requested depositions are unnecessary and would not impact the pending motion. (Motion, pp 9-10, Reply, p.2)

Given this, State Farm argues that the protective order is necessary to shield it from the undue burden, annoyance, and expense of conducting depositions. State Farm further contends that Plaintiffs have not demonstrated good cause to proceed with the depositions at this stage and emphasizes that, even if the motion for judgment on the pleadings is denied, Plaintiffs can seek to take these depositions at a later time, preserving the parties’ resources and preventing unnecessary costs (Reply, p. 3)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that a protective order should be denied. Plaintiffs argue that the depositions of Trish Kero and Richard Knapp are necessary to gather evidence supporting their claims of bad faith and fraud, particularly regarding representations allegedly made by Defendant’s agents about extending the arbitration deadline. Plaintiffs assert that both Ms. Kero and Mr. Knapp submitted declarations in opposition to prior motions, which Plaintiffs contend contained false or misleading statements regarding the need for a certified arbitration demand and the applicable statute of limitations. (Opposition, pp. 3-4) 

Plaintiffs further contend that these depositions are critical to rebut Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings, as the testimony of Ms. Kero and Mr. Knapp would address key factual disputes about whether Plaintiffs were misled into believing the arbitration deadline had been extended. Plaintiffs argue that their inability to depose these witnesses prior to opposing the motion for judgment on the pleadings would result in significant prejudice, as the depositions are likely to produce evidence directly relevant to their claims and defenses. (Opposition, p.5)

State Farm responds that the Court has already rejected Plaintiffs’ motion to compel arbitration and motion for reconsideration, determining that Plaintiffs failed to comply with the statute of limitations. State Farm contends these rulings constitute an adjudication on the merits, rendering the requested depositions irrelevant and unnecessary (Reply, p. 3¿)

The Court finds that State Farm has demonstrated good cause for a protective order under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.420. The Court previously denied Plaintiffs’ motion to compel arbitration and motion for reconsideration, finding that Plaintiffs failed to timely demand arbitration as required by the insurance policy and Insurance Code section 11580.2. In the motion for reconsideration, the Court specifically determined that the declarations cited by Plaintiffs did not provide new or different facts, circumstances, or law sufficient to justify reconsideration. (6/14/24 Minute Order) However, the Court makes no determination at this time as to whether those rulings constitute an adjudication on the merits, as that issue is not presently before the Court.

The Court further finds that the requested depositions of Ms. Kero and Mr. Knapp are unlikely to yield evidence relevant to Defendant’s pending motion for judgment on the pleadings, which is based on Plaintiffs’ failure to meet procedural prerequisites under the insurance policy.

Balancing the potential burden, annoyance, and expense of proceeding with these depositions and production of documents against their relevance at this stage, the Court concludes that a protective order is warranted. Given that trial is set for February 17, 2026, Plaintiffs retain the ability to re-notice the depositions should the motion for judgment on the pleadings be denied, with no prejudice to raising any future discovery issues at that time.

Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s motion for a protective order, staying the depositions of Ms. Kero and Mr. Knapp and any related document production until after the resolution of the motion for judgment on the pleadings.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            Accordingly, the Court GRANTS State Farm’s motion for protective order.

State Farm is to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV02873    Hearing Date: December 16, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING MOTION BY PLAINTIFF FOR RELIEF FROM ADMISSIONS DEEMED ADMITTED

I.          INTRODUCTION

This is a premises liability and negligence action. Plaintiff, Collean Taylor-Drennen (“Plaintiff”), brought this action on December 12, 2023, against Defendants HSR San Pasqual, LP, Bob Daignault, and Joseph Penner, alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence and (2) Premises Liability. Plaintiff contends that on January 28, 2023, she suffered injuries caused by an overgrown tree branch on Defendants’ property, which extended into a public sidewalk.

On July 31, 2024, the Court granted Defendant Joseph Penner and HSR San Pasqual, LP (collectively, “Defendants”) motion to deem admitted Request for Admission, Set One, due to Plaintiff’s failure to serve timely responses. Plaintiff’s initial responses, served on July 24, 2024, were on an incorrect pleading template from a dismissed case, resulting in procedural defects. Plaintiff filed the instant motion for relief from deemed admissions on October 11, 2024, citing inadvertence, mistake, and excusable neglect due to staff turnover in Plaintiff’s prior counsel’s office.

On November 27, 2024, Defendants filed an opposition, arguing that Plaintiff failed to establish excusable neglect and that relief would result in substantial prejudice. Plaintiff filed a reply on December 9, 2024.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A party may withdraw or amend an admission made in response to a request for admission only on leave of court granted after notice to all parties. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.300, subd. (a).) The court may permit the withdrawal only if it determines the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.300, subd. (b).) The “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect” under CCP § 2033.300 have similar meanings as those words used in CCP § 473(b). (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1419.) 

“Because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 2033.300 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief. Accordingly, the court's discretion to deny a motion under the statute is limited to circumstances where it is clear that the mistake, inadvertence, or¿neglect was inexcusable, or where it is clear that the withdrawal or amendment would not substantially prejudice the party who obtained the admission in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.” (New Albertsons, Inc., supra, 168 Cal.App.4th at 1420–21.)

 

            III.      DISCUSSION

Plaintiff seeks relief from Requests for Admissions, Set One, deemed admitted by Court order on July 31, 2024, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.300. Plaintiff argues that the deemed admissions resulted from inadvertence, mistake, or excusable neglect attributable to prior counsel. Specifically, Plaintiff contends that significant staff turnover led to confusion and disorganization, resulting in the untimely service of responses. Additionally, when responses were eventually served on July 24, 2024, they were submitted on an incorrect pleading template from a dismissed case that listed the incorrect parties. Plaintiff contends that substantive responses to the RFAs were provided before the hearing on Defendants’ motion. Plaintiff contends that current counsel diligently sought to address the matter upon assuming responsibility in the case in late August 2024. Plaintiff also contends that former counsel responsible for the mistake has since left the firm in August 2024 (Motion, pp. 3-5, Reply, p.2)

Plaintiff further argues that granting relief would not prejudice Defendants because the trial date is set for April 28, 2026, providing ample time for discovery and preparation. Conversely, denying relief would result in severe prejudice to Plaintiff, potentially foreclosing the opportunity to litigate her claims on the merits. (Motion, p.6)

In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has failed to establish excusable neglect. Defendants emphasize that the motion is unsupported by a declaration from Plaintiff's prior counsel, Erik Harper, who was responsible for the initial failure to respond and the subsequent submission of defective responses. (Opposition, pp 2-3) Defendants contends that the absence of testimony from Mr. Harper undermines Plaintiff’s claim of excusable neglect. (Id.)

Defendants also argues that Plaintiff’s failure to respond in a timely and proper manner resulted not from neglect but from deliberate gamesmanship, citing email exchanges between prior counsel. (Opposition, p.5) Moreover, Defendants claim that allowing relief at this stage would substantially prejudice Defendants, as the deemed admissions have streamlined the scope of discovery and defense strategy. (Opposition, pp.7-8)

In response, Plaintiff argues that excusable neglect has been adequately demonstrated through the declaration of current counsel, who acted promptly to address the issue upon its discovery by providing corrected responses and engaging in further meet-and-confer efforts (Reply, p. 2). Specifically, Plaintiff’s counsel served code-compliant, verified responses on November 1, 2024 (Reply, p. 4). Plaintiff further contends that Defendants will not be prejudiced because they have ample time to conduct discovery and prepare for trial, which is set for late April 2026 (Reply, pp. 4-5).

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.300, the Court may permit the withdrawal or amendment of deemed admissions if the admissions resulted from mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and if no substantial prejudice will result to the opposing party (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1420). This statute reflects California’s strong policy favoring resolution of cases on their merits. Furthermore, any doubts should be resolved in favor of the moving party to advance this policy (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979).

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiff has demonstrated excusable neglect. The failure to timely respond was the result of significant staff turnover and procedural errors by Plaintiff’s prior counsel, who has since left the firm. While the absence of a declaration from Mr. Harper, the prior counsel, is a notable omission, the detailed explanation provided by Plaintiff’s current counsel and the procedural history presented sufficiently establish that the errors were unintentional and not the result of bad faith.

Moreover, the Court finds that Defendants will not suffer substantial prejudice if relief is granted. The trial date is more than a year away, providing ample time for any necessary discovery adjustments. Given the policy under CCP section 2033.300 favoring resolution of cases on their merits, the Court concludes that granting relief is appropriate.

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

             Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion granting relief from admissions deemed admitted.

Plaintiff is to give notice.
