DEPARTMENT C LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22NWCV00487    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: C

ANITRA WASHINGTON VS. REID, ET AL.  

CASE NO.:  22NWCV00487

HEARING:  10/23/24 @ 9:30 A.M.

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

I.             Cross-Defendant Marriann Bohling’s demurrer to Cross-Complainant Ersell Reid’s First Amended Cross-Complaint is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part with 20 days leave to amend.   

 

II.            Cross-Defendant Marriann Bohling’s motion to strike portions of the First Amended Cross-Complaint is MOOT.

Moving Party to give NOTICE.

 

No Opposition filed as of October 21, 2024.

 

I.             Demurrer

 

Factual Background

 

This case arises from the purchase of real property located at 201 Reposadro Drive, La Habra Heights 90631 (the “Subject Property”) by Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Anitra Washington (“Washington”) with a $260,000.00 contribution by Defendant and Cross-Complainant Ersell Reid (“Reid”). (FAXC, ¶¶ 13-14, 32). Reid tried to buy the property by herself but failed. (FAXC, ¶ 18.) She then sought help from Washington, paying her $15,000.00. (FAXC, ¶ 19.) On August 27, 2021, the sellers executed the grant deed to both Washington and Reid. (FAXC, ¶ 24.)

 

In September 2021, Reid caught COVID-19 during the pendency of escrow and could not execute loan documents in person due to her needing to be in isolation. (FAXC, ¶¶ 25 to 27.) Her real estate agent, Cross-Defendant Alora De La Vara (“De La Vara”) of Keller Williams, suggested the following: (1) change the loan documents so that only Washington is on the title of the property; (2) have Washington sign the loan documents to keep the interest rate and allow for an instant close; (3) prepare a grant deed with both Washington and Reid on the title; (4) once Reid feels better, both she and Washington will sign the grant deed. (FAXC, ¶ 28.)  On September 21, 2021, the parties closed escrow. (FAXC, ¶ 30.)

 

Cross-Defendant Diane Luongo (“Luongo”) was the loan officer. (FAXC ¶¶ 4, 16.) Cross-Defendant Marriann Bohling (“Bohling”) was the escrow agent and notary facilitating the close of the transaction of the Subject Property. (FAXC ¶¶ 5, 17.)

 

In January 2022, De La Vara listed the subject property for sale. (FAXC, ¶ 33.)

 

Reid alleges that escrow never prepared the grant deed from Washington to Reid. (FAXC, ¶ 29.) Reid alleges that but for the Cross-Defendants, she would not have taken her name off the grant deed conveyed to her and Washington. (FAXC, ¶ 29.)

 

Reid also alleges that Washington falsely asserted that they agreed orally that Reid would buy the property from Washington when Reid could. (FAXC, ¶ 32.)

 

Reid further alleges that Cross-Defendants knew that Reid planned to take possession as an owner/buyer and refinance the subject property out of the loan in Washington’s name into Reid’s favor, and informed Reid that such a transaction would in fact be possible. (FAXC, ¶ 22.)

 

Reid sues for the following: (1) fraud; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; (3) breach of contract; (4) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200, et seq.; (5) cancellation of written instruments; (6) negligent misrepresentation and intentional misrepresentation; (7) promissory fraud; (8) fraudulent concealment; and (9) breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing.

 

Cross-Defendant Bohling demurs to the first through eighth causes of action in the First Amended Cross-Complaint on the grounds that they do not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

 

Legal Standard

 

The party against whom a complaint has been filed may object to the pleading, by demurrer, on several grounds, including that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may demur to an entire complaint, or to any causes of action stated therein. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.50, subd. (a).) The complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded. (Flynn v. Higham (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 677, 679.)

 

The burden is on the complainant to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully, to obtain an order allowing leave to amend.  (McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 78.) “Leave to amend should be denied where the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear, but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Lawrence v. Bank of America (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 431, 436.) 

 

Meet-and-Confer

 

Cross-Defendant satisfied the meet-and-confer requirements. (Decl. Loangkote, ¶¶ 3 to 6, Ex. A to C; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).)

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Bohling requests judicial notice of the following: (1) notice of ruling of Plaintiff’s motion for judgment on the pleadings in the instant action; (2) notice of entry of judgment and judgment declaring Plaintiff Anitra Washington as the legal owner of the subject property in the instant action.  

 

A court may take judicial notice of the contents of its own records. (Dwan v. Dixon (1963) 216 Cal.App.2d 260, 265.)

 

Thus, the Court grants judicial notice of the two requests.

 

Second Cause of Action – Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

Bohling demurs on the grounds that she did not owe a fiduciary duty as the escrow agent.

 

The elements of a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty are the existence of a fiduciary relationship; breach of fiduciary duty; and damages caused by the breach. (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 820.) 

 

Bohling argues that Reid was not a party to escrow; thus, Reid is not owed a fiduciary duty. Bohling further argues that even if she was, Reid decided to remove her name from the title because she was sick with COVID-19 and could not execute the documents in time to close escrow. Bohling also argues that any alleged promise to perform a future act not included in the escrow instructions are beyond the duties of an escrow officer.

 

Reid alleges that Bohling pursued personal gain or benefits, directly or indirectly, at Reid’s expense. (FAXC, ¶ 52.) Reid further alleges that Bohling failed to act as a reasonably careful escrow officer and notary would have acted under the same or similar circumstances. (FAXC, ¶ 56.) Reid was harmed as a result and Bohling’s conduct was a substantial factor in Reid’s harm. (FAXC, ¶ 56.)

 

Whether a fiduciary duty exists is generally a question of law. (Marzec

v. Public Employees’ Retirement System (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 889, 915.)

An escrow involves the deposit of documents and/or money with a third party to be delivered on the occurrence of some condition. (Summit Fin. Holdings, Ltd. v. Cont'l Laws. Title Co. (2002) 27 Cal.4th 705, 711.)  An escrow holder is an agent and fiduciary of the parties to the escrow. (Ibid.) The agency created by the escrow is limited to the obligation of the escrow holder to carry out the instructions of each of the parties to the escrow. (Ibid.) If the escrow holder does not carry out an instruction it has contracted to perform, the injured party has a cause of action for breach of contract. (Ibid.)

 

Here, Reid is suing under a breach of fiduciary duty theory, not a breach of contract theory. Despite this, Reid does not allege which escrow instructions Bohling did not follow.

 

The demurrer to the Second Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.  

 

Third Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Bohling demurs on the ground that Reid failed to identify any contract provision Bohling allegedly breached.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish the following: (1) the existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff. (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) 

 

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.) In some circumstances, a plaintiff may also plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language. (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-199.) 

 

Here, Reid does not allege whether the contract was written, oral or implied in conduct.  Also, Reid does not allege which contract provision Bohling breached. 

 

The demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Fourth Cause of Action – Unfair Business Practices

 

Bohling demurs on the grounds that Reid does not cite a law or statute that Bohling allegedly violated.

 

To plead a claim for a violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200 (“UCL”), Plaintiff must establish Defendant was engaged in an “unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice and unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising” and certain specific acts. (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17200.)

 

          Unlawful Conduct

 

“Unlawful” conduct includes any business practice or act forbidden by local, state, or federal statutes or by regulations or case law. (Munson v. Del Taco, Inc. (2009) 46 Cal.4th 661, 676.) “Unlawful” conduct claims that borrow violations of other laws and make those unlawful practices separately actionable through the UCL are generally per se violations of the unlawful prong. (Klein v. Chevron U.S.A., Inc. (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1342, 1383.)  

 

Here, Reid alleges that Bohling failed to act as a reasonably careful escrow holder would have acted under the same or similar circumstances. (FAXC, ¶ 71.) Reid does not allege any business practice or act forbidden by local, state, or federal statutes or by business regulations or case law. Thus, unlawful conduct is not sufficiently alleged.

 

          Unfair Conduct

 

There is a split in authority about what constitutes an “unfair” practice in consumer actions in unfair competition law. Some cases hold an “unfair” practice is one that offends established public policy, that is immoral, unethical, oppressive, unscrupulous, or substantially injurious to consumers, or that has an impact on the victim that outweighs defendant's reasons, justifications, and motives for the practice. (Jolley v. Chase Home Fin., LLC (2013) 213 Cal. App. 4th 872, 907.) Others hold that the public policy, which is a predicate to a claim under the “unfair” prong of the UCL, must be tethered to specific constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provisions. (Ibid.) The Court adopts the latter approach.

 

Here, as discussed above, Ried did not tether the practice to a constitutional, statutory, or regulatory provision. Thus, unfair conduct is not sufficiently alleged.

 

          Fraudulent Conduct

 

A defendant violates the fraudulent business acts or practices prong of the UCL by engaging in conduct by which “members of the public are likely to be deceived.” (See Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 211.) Courts apply an objective standard to determine whether a business practice is “likely to deceive,” assessing the potential for deception against an audience of “reasonable consumers” rather than the least sophisticated consumer. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 55.)

 

Here, Reid does not allege any fraudulent conduct.

 

Based on all the above, the demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Fifth Cause of Action – Cancellation of Written Instruments

 

Bohling demurs on the ground that Reid does not allege Bohling has interest in the subject property.

 

As section 3412 of the Civil Code provides states, a written instrument, in respect to which there is a reasonable apprehension that if left outstanding it may cause serious injury to a person against whom it is void or voidable, may, upon his application, be so adjudged, and ordered to be delivered up or canceled. A person who does not have an interest in a parcel of real property cannot bring an action to cancel a deed or mortgage regarding the property. (Osborne v. Abels (1939) 30 Cal.App.2d 729, 731.)

 

Bohling argues that Reid must allege that Bohling has an interest in the property in order to state a claim for cancellation of written instruments.  However, this argument is not supported by the authority Bohling cites – Wolfe v. Lipsy (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 633, 638. 

 

The demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action is OVERRULED. 

 

First, Sixth, Seventh, and Eighth Causes of Action – Fraud, Negligent Misrepresentation and Intentional Misrepresentation, Promissory Fraud, and Fraudulent Concealment

 

Bohling addresses all four causes of action for fraud together and demurs on the ground that Reid did not adequately plead justifiable reliance.

 

The elements of fraud are the following: (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. (Lazar v. Super. Ct. (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) This requires pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were made. (Ibid.)

 

Reid alleges that in or about September 2021, De La Varas, Luongo and Bohling knowingly made false representations to Reid, with the intent to deceive and induce Reid to act or refrain from acting in reliance upon these misrepresentations. (FAXC, ¶ 92.) The false representations made by the defendant include claiming that the title of the subject property would be recorded with Reid’s name and Reid’s financial and equitable interest would be protected, inducing Reid to make the deposit on the home knowing that they had no intention on keeping with the agreement to have escrow prepare a grant deed with Reid names on title, and it was possible to refinance the subject property out of Washington’s name. (FAXC, ¶ 92.) De La Varas, Luongo, and Bohling made these false representations to Reid in connection with the title and purchase of the subject property. (FAXC, ¶ 92.) Washington also assured Reid that title would be restored in Reid name. (FAXC, ¶ 93.) Reid justifiably relied on each Cross-Defendant's false representations and, as a result, suffered damages amounting to $450,000.00. (FAXC, ¶ 94.)

 

The Court finds that Reid did not adequately plead how, where, and by what means the representations were made. Because Reid did not sufficiently plead these facts, the Court will not address justifiable reliance, which would require the Court to evaluate the circumstances under which these statements were made. (OCM Principal Opportunities Fund, L.P. v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2007) 157 Cal. App. 4th 835, 864.)

 

Thus, the demurrer to the First, Sixth, Seventh, and Eighth Causes of Action are SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend. 

 

Uncertainty

 

Bohling does not make any arguments about uncertainty. Nonetheless, the Court will adjudicate this issue.

Demurrers for uncertainty are strictly construed, because discovery can be used for clarification, and apply where defendants cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated.  (Khoury v. Maly’s of Cal., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) 

 

The Court does not find that the allegations are so uncertain that Cross-defendant cannot reasonably determine what issues or claims are stated.

 

II.            Motion to Strike

 

Bohling moves to strike Reid’s request for costs of suit and attorney fees, specifically page 15, paragraph 18, lines 17-18; page 20, paragraph 94, lines 25 to 27; and prayer, paragraph 7, lines 6-7. Bohling also moves to strike Reid’s request for punitive damages, specifically page 16, paragraph 74, lines 11-14; prayer, paragraph 3, lines 23-26, and prayer, paragraph 6, lines 3-4.

 

Reid alleges attorney fees based on the breach of contract and fraud causes of action. (FAXC, ¶¶ 68, 94.) Because the Court sustained the demurrer as to these two causes of action, Bohling’s request is moot.

 

Reid alleges punitive damages based on the fraud, breach of contract, and breach of fiduciary duty causes of action. (FAXC, Prayer, ¶¶ 3,6.) Because the Court sustained the demurrer as to these causes of action, Bohling’s request is moot.

MARIO ANTONIO PEREZ, ET AL. v. COUNTRYWIDE MEDITERRANEAN APARTMENTS, LLC

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant MED (DE), LLC’s motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED. The Court strikes Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

Moving Party to give NOTICE.

 

This motion is unopposed as of October 21, 2024.

 

 

This is about a habitability action. Defendant MED (DE), LLC moves for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Briana Wingo and monetary sanctions and in the alternative, monetary sanctions and evidence sanctions.

 

Legal Standard

 

A Court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse after considering the totality of the circumstances: conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery. (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)  A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court can impose the ultimate sanction. (Id.)  

 

Courts have properly imposed terminating sanctions when parties have willfully disobeyed one or more discovery orders. (Los Defensores, supra, 223 Cal.App.4th at p. 390.)

 

The court can strike the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (d)(1).)

 

Discussion

 

Defendant argues that the Court may grant terminating sanctions because Plaintiff did not obey the Court’s order compelling discovery responses issued on April 30, 2024.  

 

On April 30, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motions to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set One; Special Interrogatories, Set One; and Form Interrogatories, Set One. Defendant’s counsel testifies that Plaintiff has not responded to discovery to date. (Decl. Lezon, ¶ 21.)

 

The Court may grant Defendant’s motion to dismiss because the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules. Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s discovery requests were originally due in July 2023. Plaintiff has delayed responding for more than one year. Plaintiff did not respond to Defendant’s request, did not oppose the discovery motions, and did not oppose this motion. It appears Plaintiff is no longer interested in litigating this matter.

 

Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s complaint is stricken. In its discretion, the Court does not award monetary sanctions.

Case Number: 23NWCV02474    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: C

WHITTIER APARTMENT HOMES, LLC VS. GREEN WORLD WINDOWS & DOORS CORP.

CASE NO.:  23NWCV02474

HEARING:  10/23/24 @ 10:30 A.M.

 

#12

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Green World Windows & Doors Corp.’s demurrer to the complaint is OVERRULED in part and SUSTAINED in part as set forth below. 

Moving Party to give NOTICE.

 

 

Background

Plaintiff Whittier Apartment Homes, LLC (“Plaintiff”) owns Cierra Luxury Apartments.  On August 19, 2021, Defendant Green World Windows & Doors Corp. (“Defendant”), a subcontractor, entered into an agreement with Vertical Integrity, Inc. to supply vinyl windows for Cierra Luxury Apartments. (Compl., ¶¶ 7, 8.) The work performed resulted in a glare from several windows at Cierra Luxury Apartments. (Compl., ¶ 10.) On September 7, 2023, Plaintiff sent Defendant an indemnity demand letter pursuant to Exhibit F of the subcontract agreement. (Compl., ¶ 11.) Defendant replaced several windows but did not resolve the dangerous condition. (Compl., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff sues Defendant for the following: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) negligence; and (4) declaratory relief.

 

Defendant demurs to all four causes of action on the grounds that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.

 

Legal Standard

 

The party against whom a complaint has been filed may object to the pleading, by demurrer, on several grounds, including that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may demur to an entire complaint, or to any causes of action stated therein. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.50, subd. (a).) The complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded. (Flynn v. Higham (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 677, 679.)

 

The burden is on the complainant to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully, to obtain an order allowing leave to amend.  (McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 78.) “Leave to amend should be denied where the facts are not in dispute and the nature of the claim is clear, but no liability exists under substantive law.” (Lawrence v. Bank of America (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 431, 436.) 

 

Meet-and-Confer

 

The parties have sufficiently met and conferred. (Decl. Baldino, ¶¶ 5-6; Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).)  

 

First Cause of Action – Breach of Contract

 

Defendant demurs on the grounds that it is not a party to the contract referenced in the complaint, and the allegations do not support a breach.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that it is a third-party beneficiary of the contract.

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, Plaintiff must be able to establish the following: (1) the existence of the contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach; and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff. (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant entered into the subcontract agreement with Vertical Integrity, Inc. regarding Cierra Luxury Apartments, which contains an indemnity clause. (Compl., ¶ 24.) Plaintiff does not allege that there was an agreement between it and Defendant. Plaintiff has not alleged it is a third-party beneficiary.

 

Plaintiff is required to allege it is a third-party beneficiary. (Goonewardene v ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 821.)

 

Thus, the demurrer to First Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.  Because this issue is dispositive, the Court will not adjudicate Defendant’s other arguments regarding to this cause of action.     

 

Second Cause of Action – Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

Defendant demurs on the grounds that it is not a party to the contract referenced in the complaint, and the allegations do not support a breach.

 

The implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing rests upon the existence of

some specific contractual obligation. (Racine & Laramie, Ltd. v. Dept. of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1026, 1031.) The covenant of good faith is read into contracts to protect the express covenants or promises of the contract, not to protect some public policy interest not directly tied to the contract’s purpose. (Ibid.) It is implied as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party’s rights to the benefits of the contract. (Id., at pgs.1031-1032.)

 

For the same reasons stated above, the demurrer to the Second Cause of Action is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Third Cause of Action – Negligence

 

Defendant demurs on the ground that it complied with its contractual obligations by supplying the window products as specified in the contract plans; it did not breach its contract and cannot be found negligent or responsible for any resulting defects. Defendant also demurs on the ground that because it did not breach its contract, it did not breach its contractual indemnity obligations.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that a contractor can be found negligent for installing a product pursuant to a contract if the installation was performed negligently or if the contractor knew or should have known that the installation was inherently dangerous.

 

To state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of the following: (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.) 

 

Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant owed a duty to Plaintiff to use skill, prudence, and diligence to competently and in good faith perform work on the project under the subcontract agreement and to indemnify Plaintiff as required under the agreement. (Compl, ¶ 40.) The vinyl windows resulted in a glare that caused physical damage to the premises from burning of umbrellas and other property. (Compl., ¶ 41.) Defendant agreed to replace the windows, but installed the same windows, a negligent act. (Compl., ¶ 42.) Further, Plaintiff relied on representations that the replacement windows would fix the problem, but they did not. (Compl., ¶ 42.) Plaintiff demanded that Defendant take measures to correct the problem, and Defendant responded it would not, which constitutes further negligence. (Compl., ¶¶ 43 to 44.)

 

Every contract contains a common law duty to perform the thing to be done with care, skill, reasonable expedience, and faithfulness, and the negligent failure to do so is a tort as well as a breach of contract. As the court held in El Escorial Owners’ Ass’n v DLC Plastering, Inc. (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1337, 1358, a subcontractor’s failure to ensure a watertight connection between walls constituted negligence even though it followed plans and specifications for project.

 

Thus, the demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action – Declaratory Relief

 

Defendant demurs on the ground that the fourth cause of action is not a cause of action, but a remedy and does not give rise to an independent basis for relief.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that declaratory relief is a cause of action.  

 

Plaintiff alleges that an actual controversy has arisen regarding Defendant’s duties under the subcontract agreement, including Exhibit F.  (Compl., ¶ 47.) Plaintiff contends that Defendant must indemnify Plaintiff under the subcontractor agreement based on a breach of contract theory. (Compl., ¶ 51.)

 

To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909.) Code of Civil Procedure section 1060 authorizes actions for declaratory relief under a “written instrument” or “contract.” 

 

But the declaratory relief statute should not be used to predict and determine an issue which can be determined in the main action. (Hood v. Super. Ct. (1995) 33 Cal. App. 4th 319, 324.) The object is to afford a new form of relief where needed and not to provide a litigant with a second cause of action for the determination of identical issues. (Ibid.)  Here, the declaratory relief action duplicates the breach of contract cause of action.

Further, there is no basis for declaratory relief where only past wrongs are involved. (Osseous Technologies of America, Inc. v. DiscoveryOrtho Partners LLC (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 357, 366.)  Here, Defendant seeks declaratory relief to redress past wrongs, i.e., damages owed for allegedly breaching an agreement.

 

Thus, the demurrer to Fourth Cause of Action is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

Case Number: 23NWCV03871    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: C

Lovell v. Lakewood Sheriff's Station 23NWCV03871

Case Number: 24NWCV00917    Hearing Date: October 23, 2024    Dept: C

MARTINEZ VS. FIRST CLASS VENDING, INC.

CASE NO.:  24NWCV00917, 24NWCV00919, 24NWCV00920, 24NWCV00921, and 24NWCV00922

HEARING:  10/23/24 @ 9:30 A.M.

#5 to #9

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant First Class Vending, Inc’s Motion to Consolidate is GRANTED.  Case No. 24NWCV00917 (“the Subject Action”) is hereby consolidated with Case Nos. 24NWCV00919, 24NWCV00920, 24NWCV00921, and 24NWCV00922 with the Subject Action acting as the lead case. 

All dates in Case Nos. 24NWCV00919, 24NWCV00920, 24NWCV00921, and 24NWCV00922 are hereby VACATED. The dates already set in the Subject Action remain set.

Moving Party to give NOTICE.

 

 

Plaintiffs Valeria Duarte Martinez, Cyntia Marquez Duarte, Christian Marquez Duarte, Adrianna Guerrero, and Alfonse Marquez Duarte are former employees of Defendant First Class Vending, Inc. (“Defendant”).  All are members of the same family, and all were terminated by Defendant on the same day in July 2023.  All filed suit against Defendant on the same day in March 2024 alleging race and national origin discrimination.  Defendant moves to consolidate Valeria Duarte Martinez v. First Class Vending, Inc. (24NWCV00917) (“the Subject Action”) with the four other cases for discovery, pretrial motions, and trial.

 

California Rule of Court, rule 3.350 states that a notice of motion to consolidate must: 

 

(A) List all named parties in each case, the names of those who have appeared, and the names of their respective attorneys of record; 

 

(B) Contain the captions of all the cases sought to be consolidated, with the lowest numbered case shown first; and 

 

(C) Be filed in each case sought to be consolidated. 

 

Defendant has met the above-stated procedural requirements.

Trial courts may consolidate actions involving common questions of law or fact. (CCP §1048.) Consolidating actions does not affect the rights of the parties. Consolidation’s purpose is to avoid unnecessary costs or delay, avoid procedural duplication, particularly in proof or issues common to both actions, and avoid inconsistent results by hearing and deciding common issues together. (Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485.) Deciding a motion to consolidate rests in the trial court’s sounds discretion and will not be reversed except upon a clear abuse of discretion showing. (Feliner v. Steinbaum (1955) 132 Cal.App.2d 509, 511.) Two types of consolidation are available: “(a) When actions involving a common question of law or fact are pending before the court, it may order a joint hearing or trial of any or all the matters in issue in the actions; it may order all the actions consolidated and it may make such orders concerning proceedings therein as may tend to avoid unnecessary costs or delay. (b) The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trial will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a sperate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury….” (CCP §1048.)

Each case presents its own facts and circumstances, but trial courts generally consider the following factors: (1) the timeliness of the motion: whether granting consolidation would delay the trial of any of the cases involved; (2) complexity: whether joining the actions involved would make the trial too confusing or complex for a jury; and (3) prejudice: whether consolidation would adversely affect the rights of any party (State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1956) 47 Cal.2d.428, 430-431.) In deciding a motion consolidate, a court should weigh whether the common issues predominate over the individual issues and whether any risks of jury confusion or prejudice to the parties outweigh reducing time and expense that would result from the consolidation. (Todd-Stenberg v. Shield (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 946, 978.)

In the moving papers, Defendant argues that all five actions should be consolidated because they are based on the same set of underlying facts and involve the same defendant and the same witnesses.  For example, the Complaint in the Subject Action alleges: “On or about July 11, 2023, Plaintiff’s daughter Cintya Duarte, on behalf of herself and her four family members, reported and complained to another superior, Alex Zendejas (“Zendejas”), about her supervisor’s pervasive harassment. Plaintiff was terminated the following day, along with her four family members.” (Complaint by Valerie Duarte Martinez, ¶19.) This language is repeated in each of the other four complaints, except for each Plaintiff’s relation to Cintya Duarte (e.g., daughter, sister, sister-in-law). 

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the claims are not identical because Plaintiff Valeria Duarte sues for age discrimination while the other plaintiffs do not. In addition, Plaintiff Christian Marquez Duarte filed a motion for leave to amend complaint on September 11, 2024, to allege claims of sexual harassment and negligent hiring, supervision, and retention against defendant First Class Vending, Inc, and Yesenia Sanchez. Yesenia Sanchez is only a named defendant in Plaintiff Christian Marquez Duarte’s case. Also, Plaintiff Christian Marquez Duarte’s minor brother was subject to similar claims of sexual harassment and he, too, filed a lawsuit.

 

In reply, Defendant argues that the standard is not identical claims, but common questions of law or fact. Defendant acknowledges that there is a single difference between the five pleadings, i.e. Plaintiff Valeria Duarte’s age discrimination cause of action. Defendant argues that the Court can order a separate trial of any cause of action based on Code of Civil Procedure, section 1048, subdivision (b). Defendant also argues that Plaintiff Christan Marquez Duarte’s motion for leave to file amended complaint will not be decided until June 10, 2025; thus, the new claims and new defendant are not at issue or relevant to the instant motion.  Also, the complaint against Defendant by Plaintiff Christian Marquez Duarte’s brother is not relevant because it was filed after the instant motion.

 

It is undisputed that all five cases involve the same operative facts — Plaintiffs’ termination by Defendant on or about July 12, 2023. It is also undisputed that the related cases involve the same parties and will likely involve some or all of the same witnesses. In the interests of judicial efficiency, the Court finds that the actions involve common questions of law and fact, and they should be consolidated. Therefore, the Motion to Consolidate is GRANTED. Issues of severance, bifurcation and any other related issues may be raised by the parties at a later time, if necessary.
