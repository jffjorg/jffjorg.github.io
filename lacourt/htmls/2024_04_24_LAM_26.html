DEPARTMENT 26 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS





1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling,
please email the clerk at SMCdept26@lacourt.org (and “cc” all
other parties in the same email) no later than 7:30 am on
the day of the hearing, and please notify all other parties in advance that you
will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the
subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and
the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the
tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may
nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motion, and the Court may
decide not to adopt the tentative ruling.




2. 
For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in
advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to
either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its
discretion.




3. PLEASE DO NOT USE THIS
EMAIL (SMCdept26@lacourt.org) FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE
RULING.  The Court will not read or
respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.




4. IN ORDER TO IMPLEMENT
PHYSICAL DISTANCING GOING FORWARD AND UNTIL FURTHER NOTICE, THE COURT STRONGLY
ENCOURAGES ALL COUNSEL AND ALL PARTIES TO APPEAR TELEPHONICALLY FOR NON-TRIAL
AND NON-EVIDENTIARY MATTERS.  Thus, until further
notice, Department 26 strongly encourages telephonic appearances for motion
hearings that do not require the presentation of live testimony.




 






Case Number: 22STCV10077    Hearing Date: April 24, 2024    Dept: 26

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 26

 

 

KRISTINA HOLMAN,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

KERLAN-JOBE SURGERY CENTER, LLC, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.: 22STCV10077

 

 Hearing Date: April 24, 2024

 

[TENTATIVE] order RE:

PLAINTIFF Kristina holman’S MOTION FOR ISSUE AND EVIDENTIARY SANCTIONS AGAINST DEFENDANT CEDARS-SINAI MEDICAL CENTER FOR FAILURE TO OBEY OCTOBER 24, 2023 ORDER RE TO PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET THREE

 

 

 

Background

            On March 25, 2024, Plaintiff Kristina Holman (“Plaintiff”) filed this operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) against Defendants Kerlan-Jobe Surgery Center LLC; Kerlan-Jobe , LLC; Cedars-Sinai Kerlan Jobe Institute; Cedars-Sinai Medical Center; Cedars-Sinai Medical Care Foundation; Kerlan-Jobe Orthopaedic Clinic, a Medical Group, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”); and DOES 1 through 50, inclusive, asserting eleven causes of action: (1) Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code §1102.5; (2) Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code §232.5; (3) Retaliation in Violation of California Business and Professions Code § 510; (4) Discrimination on the Basis of Age in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940, et. seq.); (5) Discrimination on the Basis of Disability in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940, et. seq.); (6) Failure to Prevent Discrimination on the Basis of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940, et. seq.); (7) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940, et. seq.); (8) Failure to Prevent Retaliation in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940, et. seq.); (9) Failure to Accommodate in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Code §12940, et. seq.); (10) Failure to Engage in a Good Faith Interactive Process  in Violation of FEHA (Gov. Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure Section 2023.030, states in pertinent part: “To the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method or any other provision of this title, the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose…[monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in the misuse of the discovery process….” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subds. (a)-(d).) “Misuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to…(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery…(g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.)

            “Nevertheless, absent unusual circumstances, such as repeated and egregious discovery abuses, two facts are generally prerequisite to the imposition of a nonmonetary sanction. There must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful.” (Lee v. Lee (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 1553, 1559.) “Even where nonmonetary sanctions are called for, they ‘”…’ should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.’ [Citations] ‘”…[¶] The sanctions the court may impose are such as are suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks, but the court may not impose sanctions which are designed not to accomplish the objects of discovery but to impose punishment.’”’ [Citations.]” (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.)

            “A trial court has broad discretion when imposing a discovery sanction.” (Lee, supra, 175 Cal.App.4th at 1559.) “In exercising this discretion [for nonmonetary sanctions], a variety of factors may be relevant, including, 1) the time which has elapsed since [the discovery requests] were served, 2) whether the party served was previously given a voluntary extension of time, 3) the number of [the discovery requests] propounded, 4) whether the unanswered questions sought information which was difficult to obtain, 5) whether the answers supplied were evasive and incomplete, 6) the number of questions which remained unanswered, 7) whether the questions which remained unanswered are material to a particular claim or defense, 8) whether the answering party has acted in good faith, and with reasonable diligence, 9) the existence of prior orders compelling discovery and the answering party’s response thereto, 10) whether the party was unable to comply with the previous order of the court, 11) whether an order allowing more time to answer would enable the answering party to supply the necessary information, and 12) whether a sanction short of dismissal or default would be appropriate to the dereliction.” (Deyo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 796-97.)

Discussion

Plaintiff seeks 11 issue and evidence sanctions on the grounds that CSMC failed to comply with the October 24, 2023 order of the Court.

CSMC Complied with the October 24, 2023 Order

The October 24, 2023 Order required CSMC to provide code complaint responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set Three, Nos. 98, 99, 100, 101, 102, 103, 104, 106, 107, 108, 110, 113, and 114, within 30 days along with a privilege log identifying every document withheld based on what specific privilege and specific basis for the redactions. Plaintiff argues CSMC produced redacted documents on the basis of overruled and unsupported objections, non-code compliant responses, and refuses to produce all responsive documents. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends she never stipulated to limit discovery to one or two responsive documents only.

In opposition, CSMC argues it timely served valid responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set Three, produced documents pursuant to the Parties’ agreement, and provided a privilege log in compliance with the October 24, 2023 Order. CSMC further asserts (1) the information it redacted in its production (but identified in its log) consists of totally immaterial but protected, private financial information that would provide CSMC’s peers an unfair competitive advantage if disclosed; (2) Plaintiff has not and cannot articulate any compelling need for the redacted information, nor can CSMC rely on the parties’ stipulated protective order given Plaintiff’s previous failure to abide by its terms; (3) Plaintiff’s issue and evidence sanctions go beyond the underlying requests and are not supported by the information Plaintiff has obtained through discovery by all named Defendants, including, responses to interrogatories, request for admissions, requests for production of documents, and other documents produced in this case; and (4) if granted, Plaintiff’s issue and evidence sanctions would result in a windfall to Plaintiff and disproportionate and unjustified punishment to CSMC.

In reply, Plaintiff contends CSMC’s objections on grounds of relevance and privacy cited in its privilege log are not akin to any statutory privilege that allows CSMC to make the same redactions to relevant documents as before. Furthermore, Plaintiff argues CSMC concedes that it continues to withhold documents and that its responses are evasive despite stating all documents will be produced as to RPD Nos. 98 and 100. Similarly, Plaintiff argues CSMC does not dispute it failed to produce all responsive documents and that the sole document produced is missing most, if not all responsive information as to RPD Nos. 99, 101, 102, 103, 106, 110, 113, and 114. Likewise, Plaintiff argues CSMC makes the same argument rejected by the Court that producing an agreement to manage the Surgery Center did not require CSMC to produce documents showing CSMC manages the Surgery Center. Additionally, Plaintiff asserts CSMC failed to show that the issue sanctions place Plaintiff in a better position. Finally, Plaintiff contends regardless of whether issue sanctions are granted, it is abuse of discretion to deny mandatory monetary sanctions upon finding that Defendants disobeyed court orders.

Background

On December 27, 2022, plaintiff Andrew Reyna (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against defendants Dreamway Express, Inc. (“Dreamway”), Sea Future Logistics, Inc. (“Sea Future”), Yuxiao Lin (“Lin”) and Suwen Pan (“Pan”) (collectively “Defendants”) alleging ten causes of action for Labor Code violations, wrongful termination, and violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200, et seq. 

Plaintiff worked for Defendants as a delivery driver from April to November 2022.  Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to pay overtime wages and provide accurate wage statements and statutorily required meal and rest periods.  (Compl., ¶¶ 24-26.)  Within weeks of making a formal OSHA complaint for unsafe work conditions, Plaintiff was terminated.  (Compl., ¶¶ 19-20.)

On March 25, 2024, attorneys Elvis Tran and Bin Li (collectively “Counsel”) filed motions to be relieved as counsel as to each of the four defendants (for a total of eight motions – four per attorney).

            On April 5, 2024, Pan filed an opposition.

            On April 10, 2024, Bin Li replied.

Legal Standard

The court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice.  (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal. App. 4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398.)

CRC, Rule 3.1362 requires (1) notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on form MC-051); (2) a declaration stating in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney-client relationship why a motion under CCP § 284(2) is brought instead of filing a consent under CCP § 284(1) (made on form MC-052); (3) service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and (4) the proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on form MC-053).

Discussion

Counsel indicate that each client was served by mail at an address that has been confirmed current within the past 30 days through a Lexis Public Records Smartlinx search.  ((Li Decl., ¶ 3; Tran Decl., ¶ 3.)  However, no proof of service is attached.

Prof. Conduct, Rule 1.16(b)(10) provides that a permissive request for withdrawal may be properly granted where an attorney believes that the tribunal will find good cause for withdrawal.  In the attorney declarations (Form MC-052), Counsel indicate that “[t]he relationship of trust and confidence essential to the attorney-client relationship[sic] has ceased to exist.” (Li Decl., ¶ 2; Tran Decl., ¶ 2.)  This is a proper basis for withdrawal.  Counsel further declare that Defendants will not be prejudiced because: (1) they were given “reasonable notice”, (2) they were advised of all upcoming deadlines and court hearings, (3) there are no unearned legal fees, and (4) Counsel provided Defendants with their client file.  (Li Decl., ¶ 2; Tran Decl., ¶ 2.)  

Defendant Pan opposes Counsel’s withdrawal. Pan contends that granting Counsel’s motions would impose an insurmountable financial burden on Defendants.  Pan contends that Counsel seeks to be relieved due to fee disputes.  (Opp’n, ¶ 5 [“he was displeased that we didn’t accept his new estimate”]; however, it is unclear which attorney Pan is referring to.)  In reply, Bin Li requests an in-camera conference to present information to support his motion to withdraw without compromising attorney-client privilege.

The Court will not order an in-camera hearing at this time. Counsel have stated that there is no trust or confidence between the parties as would be required for an attorney-client relationship. Defendant Pan alludes to a few dispute between the parties. Either ground, and certainly both, would be sufficient to warrant the granting of these motions. Counsel cannot be forced to continue to represent a client if there is a fee dispute, or where the attorney-client relationship has been broken whatever the reason.

Attorneys Bin Li and Elvis Tran are ordered to produce proof of service of the moving papers before the hearing on these motions.

Conclusion and ORDER

            The Court GRANTS the motions to be relieved as counsel for defendants, Bin Li and Elvis Tran. Counsel are ordered to file proof of service of the moving papers. Counsel are not relieved as counsel of records until they serve the Order granting their motions to be relieved on all defendants and parties, and file proof of same with the court.

            As corporate defendants cannot appear in this action without counsel, the Court sets an OSC re: legal representation of the corporate defendants on May 22, 2024, 8:30 a.m. Counsel are ordered to give notice of the OSC.
