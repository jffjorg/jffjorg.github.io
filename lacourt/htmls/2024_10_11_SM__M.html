DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SMCV00364    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Williams v. Hudson

CASE NO.:                22SMCV00364

MOTION:                  Motion for Leave to Amend to File a Second Amended Complaint

HEARING DATE:   10/11/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

If a party wishes to amend a pleading after an answer has been filed, or after a demurrer has been filed and after the hearing on the demurrer, or if he or she has already amended the pleading as a matter of course, the party must obtain permission from the court before amendment. (CCP §§ 473(a)(1), 576.)

 

Motions for leave to amend the pleadings are directed to the sound discretion of the court. “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading . . ..” (CCP § 473(a)(1); see CCP § 576.) Policy favors liberally granting leave to amend so that all disputed matters between the parties may be resolved. (See Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.) Absent prejudice to the adverse party, the court may permit amendments to the complaint “at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial.” (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761 [internal quotes omitted].) Where leave is sought to add entirely new claims, the court may grant leave to amend if the new claims are based on the same general set of facts, and the amendment will not prejudice the opposing party. (Austin v. Massachusetts Bonding & Ins. Co. (1961) 56 Cal.2d 596, 600-602; Glaser v. Meyers (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 770, 777 [holding trial court did not abuse its discretion in permitting amendment of complaint, which originally alleged constructive eviction, to allege retaliatory eviction where the new claim was based on the same general set of facts].)

 

Although denial is rarely justified, a judge has discretion to deny leave to amend if the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party. (Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530; Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490). An opposing party is prejudiced where the amendment would necessitate a trial delay along with a loss of critical evidence, added preparation expense, increased burden of discovery, etc. (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488 [leave properly denied where plaintiff sought leave on the eve of trial, nearly two years after the complaint was originally filed and gave no explanation for the delay which prejudiced defendant who did not discover or depose many of the witnesses who would support the new allegations and had not marshaled evidence in opposition of the new allegations].)

 

Procedurally, a motion for leave to amend must state with particularity what allegations are to be amended. Namely, it must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted and/or added, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number. (CRC, Rule 3.1324(a)(2)-(3).) The motion must be accompanied by a declaration specifying: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (CRC, Rule 3.1324(b).) The motion must also be accompanied by the proposed amended pleading, numbered to differentiate it from the prior pleadings or amendments. (CRC, Rule 3.1324(a)(1).) It is within the court’s discretion to require compliance with Rule 3.1324 before granting leave to amend. (Hataishi v. First American Home Buyers Protection Corp. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1454, 1469.)

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff Gwenique Williams (“Williams”) moves for leave to file a proposed Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”).

 

The current First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleges three causes of action for conversion, breach of good fair and fair dealing, and declaratory relief. Plaintiff’s proposed SAC states four causes of action for breach of oral contract, breach of duty of good faith and fair dealing, common counts for money had and received, and conversion. Thus, the SAC seeks to replace the declaratory relief cause of action with two new causes of action for breach of contract and money had. Notably, liability is set on the same general facts as alleged in the initial complaints. Williams’s breach of oral contract and common count for money had and received arise from the subject vessel transaction. Plaintiff explains that this amendment is necessary and not filed sooner because when this action was filed, her current attorney was not the attorney of record. (Sullivan Decl., ¶ 4.) When new counsel substituted into the action, he reviewed the facts and law and determined that additional causes of action should have been alleged. (¶¶5-7.) When the court sustained the demurrer with leave on June 11, 2024, counsel first requested to add new causes of action. (¶ 9.) Following the sustained demurrer, Williams filed an amended complaint on July 8, 2024. (¶ 11.) Current counsel then drafted the SAC. (¶ 12.)

 

Defendant observes that these theories should have been readily apparent when this action was filed on March 14, 2022. Defendant asserts that Plaintiff delayed over a year seeking leave to amend. Counsel first substituted in November 2023, yet failed to make the amendments

prior to the demurrer hearing and waited almost a year to propose the requested amendments.

(Crump Decl. ¶¶ 5–10.) Defendant posits that such a delay is unwarranted, unjustified, and

unreasonable. Defendant, however, does not explain any prejudice stemming from this delay. There would likely be very little prejudice to Defendant, since the amendments only expand on the legal theories and pertain to the same set of facts as the prior causes. Thus, to the extent that Plaintiff delayed in bringing the breach of contract and common counts, there is no observable prejudice to Defendant. Since Plaintiff’s delay was not prejudicial, the court must grant leave to amend.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.


Case Number: 23SMCV01693    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Webb v. Pepperdine University

CASE NO.:                23SMCV01693

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Further Responses

HEARING DATE:   10/11/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

            In the absence of contrary court order, a civil litigant’s right to discovery is broad. “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action . . . if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010; see Davies v. Superior Court (1984) 36 Cal.3d 291, 301.) Section 2017.020(a) vests the Court with authority to limit the scope of discovery if the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of the discovery sought “clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.”  CCP § 2019.030 directs the Court to consider the needs of the case, amount in controversy, and the importance of the issues at stake in the litigation, and to consider whether the discovery being sought is unreasonably cumulative or duplicative, or is obtainable by a more convenient or less expensive or less burdensome way, when deciding whether to restrict the frequency of extent of use of an authorized discovery method.

 

            CCP section 2030.220(a) requires that “[e]ach answer in a response to interrogatories shall be as complete and straightforward as the information reasonably available to the responding party permits.” Pursuant to CCP section 2030.300, a party may move to compel further responses to a form interrogatory if the other party’s answer is “evasive or incomplete.” The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections to the form and special interrogatories. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.)

 

PROCEDURAL ISSUES

 

Meet and Confer

                       

The moving party must demonstrate a “reasonable and good faith attempt” at an informal resolution of each issue presented. (CCP §§ 2016.040, 2030.300.) Plaintiff sent an email to defense counsel requesting that the parties meet and confer regarding Special Interrogatories (“SROG”) sets 1-3. (Webb Decl., Ex. F-G.) Defendant replied that it agreed to meeting and conferring regarding those sets. (Id., Ex. H.) Plaintiff apparently did not follow up with this email, or otherwise address the issues presented in this motion in the meet and confer emails. (Id.) Defendant argues that Plaintiff thus failed to meet and confer on the issues prior to filing the instant motion. Indeed, Plaintiff did not inform Pepperdine of any particular issue with the Set 2 responses until the Motion was filed. Typically, the Court would order that the parties meet and confer on the issues. However, the meet and confer efforts on the record concerning the other discovery issues demonstrate that further meet and confer efforts will not be fruitful. Thus, the Court will not require further meet and confer efforts on this motion.

 

 

 

Analysis

 

Plaintiff Breanna C. Webb moves to compel Defendant Pepperdine University to produce further responses pursuant to Plaintiff's propounded Special Interrogatories, Set Two, numbers 1-8. Pepperdine submitted objection-only responses, and therefore has the burden to show that the objections are meritorious.

 

Pepperdine objects that Set 2 was expressly directed to a non-party, Tim Perrin. SROG set 2 is specifically “directed towards Defendant employee Tim Perrin”. Further, set 2 requests that Defendant Pepperdine “HAVE TIM PERRIN PROVIDE RESPONSES TO THE FOLLOWING INTERROGATORIES.” Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.210 requires “[t]he party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory . . ..” Plaintiff thus could not demand Pepperdine to have Tim Perrin, a nonparty, respond to interrogatories. Defendant therefore did not have to respond to set 2.  Accordingly, the motion to compel further is DENIED.

 

Sanctions are mandatory. The Court must sanction any party that unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response, “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (See, e.g., CCP, § 2030.300(d).) Plaintiff made this motion despite directing the discovery to the incorrect party. Plaintiff therefore did not act with substantial justification in making this motion. Defendant presents that it incurred fees of $3,015.00 in connection with opposing the instant motion. (Onyemaobim Decl., ¶12 [9 hours at $335/hr.].) In light of the relative simplicity of the issues presented by the motion, a reasonable sanction in this instance would be $2,010.00. Accordingly, the request for sanctions is granted in the reduced total amount of $2,010.00.

 

The request for terminating sanctions in response to this motion is inappropriate. Defendant does not come close to demonstrating that Plaintiff’s discovery misconduct (incorrectly addressing a set of discovery and failing to fully meet and confer on the issues) rises to the extreme level required for terminating sanctions. (R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.)


Case Number: 23SMCV05655    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Law Officers of Payam Y. Poursalimi v. the Williams Law Group

CASE NO.:                23SMCV05655

MOTION:                  Motion to Set Aside/Vacate Default

HEARING DATE:   10/11/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

Relief under section 473(b) is either discretionary or mandatory. A motion for mandatory relief must be made no more than six months after entry of judgment and be accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to the attorney’s “mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect.” (CCP § 473(b).) The attorney affidavit of fault must contain a “straight forward admission of fault.” (State Farm Fire & Casualty Co. v. Pietak (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 600, 610.) But it need not contain an explanation of the reasons for the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence surprise or neglect. (Martin Potts & Assocs., Inc. v. Corsair, LLC (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 432, 438-441.)

Relief must be granted “unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” (Ibid.) If mandatory relief is granted, the court must “direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs” to the opposing counsel or parties. (CCP § 473(b).)

 

Where a party cannot obtain an attorney affidavit of fault, the party may seek discretionary relief under section 473(b) due to “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (CCP § 473(b).) A motion for discretionary relief must be made “within a reasonable time but in no instance exceeding six months after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (Id.) If discretionary relief is granted, the court may in its discretion order the moving party to pay the costs, including attorney fees, incurred in obtaining the default. (Rogalski v. Nabers Cadillac (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 816, 823; Vanderkous v. Conley (2010) 188 Cal.App.4th 111, 118-119.) If the motion for discretionary relief is granted, the court may order the offending attorney to pay monetary sanctions up to $1,000 to opposing parties, or up to $1,000 to the State Bar Client Security Fund, or “[g]rant other relief as is appropriate.” (CCP § 473(c)(1)(A), (B), (C).)

 

A motion for relief under section 473(b) “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted. . .” (CCP § 473(b).) However, this requirement is not jurisdictional; substantial compliance may suffice. (Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403 [finding substantial compliance where counsel offered proposed answer at motion hearing rather than serving it with moving papers].) 

 

Analysis

 

Defendants the William Law Group and Andrew Williams moves to vacate or set aside the default entered against them on June 24, 2024.  Williams explains that after the Court denied the Motion to Quash, Defendants filed a Motion for Reconsideration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure (CCP) § 1008. (Williams Decl. ¶¶ 21-25.) Defendants mistakenly believed that the submission of the motion for reconsideration would result in an extension of time to plead.  In reality, CCP section 585(b) would not preclude the clerk from entering default. This section provides in pertinent part: “if the defendant has been served other than by publication, and no answer, demurrer, notice of motion to strike ..., notice of motion to transfer ..., notice of motion to quash service of summons or to stay or dismiss the action ... or notice of the filing of a petition for writ of mandate ... has been filed ..., the clerk ... upon written application of the plaintiff, shall enter the default of the defendant.” The court clerk is entitled to rely on a document's title in determining whether it is one of the permitted responses listed. (Wisdom v. Ramirez, (1985) 177 Cal. App.3d Supp. 1, 8, disapproved of on other grounds by Janssen v. Luu, (1997) 57 Cal. App. 4th 272.) A defendant cannot claim it has filed an answer (or other responsive pleading) if the document filed is labelled otherwise because “entry of default by the clerk is a ministerial duty and that he has no authority whatever to determine the sufficiency, either as to the substance or form, of a pleading on file.” (Id.) “[T]he clerk [is] not authorized to peruse the document in question to determine whether or not it constituted an answer or should be construed as one. Instead, the clerk was entitled to rely on the document's label in making the determination whether an answer had been filed by defendant.” (Id.; see Rios v. Singh (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 871, 884-885 [motion titled “reply” did not pause entry of default when it was not titled “answer” or other permissible responsive pleading or motion, it did not contain general or specific denials of the allegations of the complaint, and it did not separately number defenses to the complaint].) Here, the motion for reconsideration is not one of the responses identified by section 585. Thus, the clerk properly entered default despite the submission of the motion for reconsideration.

 

That said, Defendants demonstrate that their default resulted from Williams’s “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect” regarding the effect of the motion for reconsideration.  Defendants do not supply any responsive pleading required for section 473(b) relief. Before granting the motion, Defendants must substantially comply with this requirement by proffering a proposed answer. If Defendants comply, then the Court would grant the motion.

 

Accordingly, the motion is tentatively GRANTED.
