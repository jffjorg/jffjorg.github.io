[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendants’ motion to quash subpoenas for production of business records

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Defendants Inter & Co. Payments, Inc., and Inter & Co., Inc. (joined by defendant Eliran Grushkowsky on September 8, 2023)

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff Forex Express Corp., d/b/a WireCash

Motion to Quash Subpoenas for Production of Business Records

The court considered the moving, joinder, opposition, and joint reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Defendants Inter & Co. Payments, Inc., and Inter & Co., Inc. (“Inter Defendants”), joined by defendant Eliran Grushkowsky (“Grushkowsky”) (collectively, “Defendants”), move the court for an order quashing the Deposition Subpoenas for Production of Business Records served by plaintiff Forex Express Corp., d/b/a WireCash (“Plaintiff”) on third parties Amazon Web Services, Inc. (“Amazon”) and GitHub, Inc. (“GitHub”).  

The court denies Defendants’ motion because (1) Defendants did not comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345, and (2) even if Defendants had complied with rule 3.1345, Defendants did not meet their burden to show that the court should quash the subpoenas.

As a threshold procedural matter, the court finds that Defendants have not complied with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345. 

“The motions that require a separate statement include a motion: [¶¶] To compel or quash the production of documents or tangible things at a deposition.”  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1345, subd. (a)(5); Weil & Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) ¶ 8:602.5 [“A motion to quash production of documents at a deposition must be accompanied by a separate statement setting forth the particular documents or demands at issue and the factual and legal reasons why production should not be compelled”].)

Because Defendants request that the court “quash the production of documents or tangible things at a deposition[,]” Defendants were required to file with the court a full and complete separate statement “that provides all the information necessary to understand each discovery request” at issue in their motion.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1345, subds. (a)(5), (c); Notice of Mot., p. 2:1-7.)  However, Defendants did not file a separate statement as required.  The court notes that a separate statement is especially necessary here, because Inter Defendants’ moving papers (1) quote only request for production number 6 in the deposition subpoenas to argue that the subpoenas’ document requests are overbroad, but (2) also seem to argue that all of the requests are overbroad.  (Mot., pp. 9:13-22 [stating that the subpoenas “broadly demand, among other things” the documents requested in request number 6 in each subpoena] [emphasis added], 10:26-28 [quoting portions of request number 6], 15:12-14 [“the requests are overbroad, duplicative, and can be obtained by less burdensome means through the Inter Defendants”] [emphasis added]; Park Decl., Exs. M, p. 90:21-23 [Deposition Subpoena to Amazon], N, p.  99:22-24 [Deposition Subpoena to GitHub].)  It is therefore unclear to the court whether Defendants contend that all or some of the document requests are overbroad.

Thus, the court exercises its discretion to deny Defendants’ motion for failing to file a separate statement as required by California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345.  (Mills v. U.S. Bank (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 871, 893 [trial court has discretion to deny discovery motion that does not comply with separate statement requirement].)

However, even if Defendants had filed a separate statement, the court finds that Defendants have not met their burden to show that the deposition subpoenas that Plaintiff served on Amazon and GitHub should be quashed.

Defendants contend that the deposition subpoenas should be quashed because (1) Plaintiff has not adequately identified its trade secrets with reasonable particularity as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.210, (2) the subpoenas are overbroad, and (3) the subpoenas seek duplicative information from non-parties that Plaintiff has or should be sought from Defendants.  The court disagrees.

First, the court finds that Plaintiff has adequately identified its trade secrets as required by Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.210.

“In any action alleging the misappropriation of a trade secret under the Uniform Trade Secrets Act . . . , before commencing discovery relating to the trade secret, the party alleging the misappropriation shall identify the trade secret with reasonable particularity subject to any orders that may be appropriate under Section 3426.5 of the Civil Code.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2019.210.)  Under this statute, “[t]he trade secret must be described ‘with sufficient particularity to separate it from matters of general knowledge in the trade or of special knowledge of those persons who are skilled in the trade, and to permit the defendant to ascertain at least the boundaries within which the secret lies.’”  (Altavion, Inc. v. Konica Minolta Systems Laboratory, Inc. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 26, 43-44.)

As noted by the parties in the opposition and reply papers, Plaintiff served on Defendants its Second Amended Identification of Trade Secrets on January 19, 2024.  (Ferrier Decl., ¶ 3; Ferrier Decl., Ex. 1.)  Plaintiff has also submitted the declaration of its expert, Dr. Philip Greenspun, in which Dr. Greenspun states that (1) Plaintiff’s Second Amended Identification “does enable a person skilled in the field to understand the trade secrets being claimed[,]” (2) he understands that Plaintiff is claiming that its trade secrets are the source code that makes up its Risk Decision Engine, SQL Database, and the eight key design concepts that the Risk Decision Engine and Database enable, and (3) the Second Amended Identification “identifies each algorithm that makes up the Risk Decision Engine” and the algorithms that are used by Plaintiff’s design concepts, such that “a skilled programmer looking at [the identification] would understand what [Plaintiff] is claiming are its trade secrets.”  (Dr. Greenspun Decl., ¶¶ 4, 22, 24-25, 29.) 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has shown that its Second Amended Identification[1] describes its trade secrets “‘with sufficient particularity to separate it from matters of general knowledge in the trade or of special knowledge of those persons who are skilled in the trade, and to permit [Defendants] to ascertain at least the boundaries within which the secret lies’” and therefore satisfies section 2019.210.  (Altavion, Inc., supra, 226 Cal.App.4th at pp. 43-44.)

Second, the court finds that Defendants have not shown that request for production number 6 (i.e., the only document request set forth in the moving papers) in the subpoenas is impermissibly overbroad.

Third, the court finds that Defendants have not met their burden to show that there exists “a threatened intrusion” on the legally protected privacy rights of third parties “that is serious.”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552; Mot., p. 13:24 [arguing that the documents demanded “may well implicate third-party privacy rights”].)

“The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.”  (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.)  Thereafter, the party seeking the information may raise “legitimate and important countervailing interests” served by the disclosure, “while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy.”  (Ibid.)  “A court must then balance these competing considerations.”  (Ibid.)  

Defendants have not submitted competent evidence establishing that request for production number 6 will violate the right to privacy of third parties.  The court finds that    (1) Inter Defendants’ counsel’s statement that Inter Defendants “likely ha[ve] legal obligations to safeguard its source code and customer information” does not adequately establish a serious, threatened intrusion on privacy, and (2) Resolution CMN 4,893 of February 26, 2021 does not establish a serious, threatened intrusion on privacy, since Defendants have not cited to any portion of the ten-page resolution establishing such an intrusion on a legally protected privacy interest.  (Park Decl., ¶ 16; Feldman Decl., Ex. F [Res. CMN 4,893]; Reply, p. 11:24-25 [generally citing Feldman Decl., Ex. F].)  The court also finds that Plaintiff’s interest in obtaining this discovery outweighs the concerns that Defendants have raised regarding the privacy interests of third parties.  Moreover, any privacy concerns that Defendants have about third parties may be addressed by designating the documents as confidential or highly confidential pursuant to the protective order entered in this action on August 28, 2023.

Fourth, the court finds that Defendants have not (1) met their burden to show that request for production of documents number 6 seeks “unreasonably cumulative or duplicative” discovery or discovery that “is obtainable from some other source that is more convenient, less burdensome, or less expensive[,]” or (2) supported their request made under Code of Civil Procedure section 2019.030 with a meet and confer declaration that satisfies section 2016.040 since the September 5, 2023 email to Plaintiff’s counsel does not show that Defendants made a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve the issues presented in connection with their request under 2019.030.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2019.030, subds. (a)(1), (b), 2016.040; Park Decl., Exs. O-P.)

ORDER

            The court denies defendants Inter & Co. Payments, Inc., Inter & Co., Inc., and joining defendant Eliran Grushkowsky’s motion to quash subpoenas for production of business records.

 

 

            The court orders plaintiff Forex Express Corp., d/b/a WireCash to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

Motion to Deem Truth of Matters in Requests for Admission Admitted

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Christopher Lemole (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) deeming admitted the truth of the matters specified in the Requests for Admission, Set One, served on defendant Dean Scott-Smith (“Defendant”), and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $961.65.

If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response, the court shall, upon motion by the propounding party, order that the matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, unless the court finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response that is in substantial compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.220.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (b), (c).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Plaintiff served Defendant with the Requests for Admissions, Set One, by mail on November 22, 2023.  (Hemming Decl., Ex. A.)  Defendant had not served responses on Plaintiff as of the date that Plaintiff filed this motion.  (Hemming Decl., ¶ 4.)  Defendant has not filed an opposition or other evidence with the court establishing that responses have since been served.

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has not served responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One, and therefore grants Plaintiff’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subds. (b), (c).)

The court grants Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280, subd. (c).)  The court finds that $811.65 ((2.5 hours x $300 hourly rate) + $61.65 filing fee) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Defendant in connection with this motion.  (Hemming Decl., ¶¶ 8-9.)

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiff Christopher Lemole’s motion to deem the truth of matters specified in requests for admission admitted.

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (b), the court orders that the truth of the matters specified in plaintiff Christopher Lemole’s Requests for Admission, Set One, served on defendant Dean Scott-Smith, is deemed admitted.

            The court orders defendant Dean Scott-Smith to pay monetary sanctions to plaintiff Christopher Lemole in the amount of $811.65 within 30 days of the date of service of this order.

            The court orders to plaintiff Christopher Lemole to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
