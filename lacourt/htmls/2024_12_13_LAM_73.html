DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV38374    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: 73

12/13/2023

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

SHAHNAZ AMIRTALESH v. ROCHELLE STERLING, et al. (19STCV38374)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Shahnaz Amirtalesh (Pro Per)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Krista Dawkins (Pyka Lenhardt Schnaider Dawkins)

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO QUASH DEFENDANTS’ SUBPOENA OF DAVID RODRIGUEZ; AND DOCUMENTS, RECORDS REPORTS AND ELECTRONICALLY STORED INFORMATION ON CASE NUMBER, RS2400426  (filed 09/30/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash Defendants’ Subpoena of David Rodriguez is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

On October 23, 2019, Plaintiff Shahnaz Amirtalesh (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Rochelle H. Sterling (“Sterling”), Beverly Hills Properties (“BHP”), The Sterling Family Trust, Susan Robman and Does 1-50 (collectively, “Defendants”).

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff’s tenancy at 240 N. Crescent Drive, Apt. 107, Beverly Hills, CA 90210 (the “Apartment”).  In her complaint, Plaintiff alleges that the Apartment landlord, BHP, failed to maintain sewage pipes in the building which caused a leak in her shower drain in October 24, 2018; that BHP delayed in granting her request to replace flood-damaged carpeting in her Apartment with laminate floors as a reasonable accommodation for her disability stemming from lung cancer; that BHP did not properly enforce COVID-19 restrictions regarding the use of the Apartment swimming pool; and further, that Apartment manager, Susan Robman, harassed and discriminated against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s religious beliefs.   

 

In the summer of 2019, Plaintiff filed complaints with the County of Los Angeles Department of Public Health (“DPH”) and the City of Beverly Hills regarding her apartment.  On September 5, 2019, the City of Beverly Hills issued a notice of violations to BHP.  On September 10, 2019, DPH issued an Official Inspection Report for violations regarding wall/ceiling floor and faucet fixtures. 

  

On October 23, 2019, Plaintiff filed her complaint against Defendants. Sterling is the owner of BHP and the Apartment and the trustee of the Sterling Family Trust.  On August 7, 2020, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging eight causes of action.  On or about October 20, 2021, the court sustained Defendants’ demurrer to the fifth cause of action in the SAC without leave to amend.  The remaining causes of action are as follows: 

C/A 1: Breach of Warranty of Habitability 
C/A 2: Negligence  
C/A 3: Nuisance  
C/A 4: Violation of Civil Code § 1942.4 
C/A 6: Violation of the California Fair Employment and Housing Act  
C/A 7: Violation of the California Unruh Civil Rights Act 
C/A 8: Breach of Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment 

 

On March 17, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Defendant Susan Robman from the case.

 

On January 24, 2023, the Court granted Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to the fourth, sixth and eighth causes of action. The motion was denied as to the first, second and third causes of action.

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Quash Defendants’ Subpoena of David Rodriguez, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves to quash the trial subpoena for David Rodriguez

·       Plaintiff argues the subpoena is untimely and was not properly served on Plaintiff.

·       Plaintiff argues the subpoena is directed at an inspector that is unrelated to this case, who was not even employee of the City of Beverly Hills in 2019, and has no personal knowledge or involvement in Plaintiff's case.

·       David Rodriguez was not listed on Defendants’ witness list.

 

Defendants filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s motion is moot

o   The subject trial subpoena would have required the witness to attend trial on October 7, 2024. That date has passed. The trial has been reset for December 8, 2025. Defendants have not yet reissued a trial subpoena to this witness.

·       David Rodriguez is a Code Enforcement Officer with the City of Beverly Hills and responds to complaints for violations of various federal, state and local laws pertaining to building code compliance and property maintenance standards and other related matters. He encountered Plaintiff on July 12, 2024 when he inspected plaintiff’s apartment because of her complaints of conditions in her apartment. Defendants intend to offer the testimony of Mr. Rodriguez for the purpose of impeaching Plaintiff

·       The subpoena was timely and properly served.

 

Plaintiff filed a reply, contending:

·       The motion is moot because trial was continued.

·       Whether the subpoena was timely served does not matter because the subpoena is unlawful and intended to harass Plaintiff

·       David Rodriguez should have been listed on Defendants’ witness list.

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a) provides: 

 

If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person. 

 

(Code of Civ. Proc., § 1987.1(a).)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Plaintiff moves to quash Defendants’ trial subpoena to David Rodriguez.

 

However, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s motion is moot. The subject trial subpoena would have required the witness to attend trial on October 7, 2024. Trial was rescheduled for December 8, 2025. Defendants have not yet reissued a trial subpoena to this witness. (Oppos., p. 2.)

In Plaintiff’s reply she does not appear to dispute that the motion is moot because the trial has been continued. (Reply, p. 3.)

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash Defendants’ Subpoena of David Rodriguez is moot and thus, DENIED.

CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s Motion to Quash Defendants’ Subpoena of David Rodriguez is DENIED.

ENCINAS, et al. v. HIKARI JAPANESE RESTAURANT, et al. (21STCV17952)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Marc Grismer (Law Office of Marc P. Grismer)

Counsel for Defendant Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC/moving party: Fang Li (Horton, Oberrecht & Kirkpatrick)

Counsel for Defendant Jhoon, Inc.: James Hart (Law Offices of Schneider & Hosmer)

Counsel for Cross-Complainant Montebello Mall Management PR, LLC: James Stanley (Law Offices of Jill A. Wood)

 

DEFENDANT MONTEBELLO TOWN CENTER INVESTORS, LLC’S MOTION FOR RELIEF OF ORDER DENYING MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT PURSUANT TO CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 473(b) (filed 11/05/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC’s Motion for Relief of Order Denying Motion for Summary Judgment Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 473(b) is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On May 13, 2021, Plaintiffs Laurie Encinas and Jesus Encinas filed this action against Defendants Hikari Japanese Restaurant, a.k.a. Sake House by Hikari, a.k.a. Hikari Sushi Bar, an unknown entity; UBS Realty Investors, LLC, a Massachusetts Limited Liability Company and Does 1 to 25.

 

On June 15, 2021, Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC (“MTCI”) was named as Doe 1.

 

On November 18, 2021, Montebello Mall Management PR, LLC (“MMMPR”) was named as Doe 6.

 

On September 7, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Notice of Death of Plaintiff Laurie Encinas.

 

On November 30, 2023, a First Amended Complaint was filed by Plaintiffs Jesus Encinas, Priscilla Pamela Chey, Jesus Encinas, Jr., Crystal Laurie Encinas, Jennifer Rose Encinas, and Jennifer Rose Encinas on behalf of the estate of Laurie Encinas.

 

On October 24, 2024, the Court denied Defendant Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

 

This is an action for premises liability arising from a trip and fall incident. Plaintiffs allege that on March 7, 2021, Laurie Encinas attempted to enter the outdoor dining area of the “Sake House by Hikari Sushi Bar” (hereinafter “Hikari Restaurant”) when she tripped and fell due to a dangerous condition, an abrupt and exposed height differential of approximately 1-inch-high where the concrete walkway and Hikari Restaurant’s outdoor patio entryway threshold met (“subject entryway”). Plaintiffs further allege that Laurie Encinas fell forward and struck her head on an umbrella stand which caused serious head injuries requiring immediate medical intervention. Plaintiffs assert causes of action for (1) Negligence and (2) Premises Liability.

 

On November 5, 2024, Defendant MTCI filed the instant Motion for Relief of Order Denying Motion for Summary Judgment, arguing:

·       Relevant procedural history

o   On April 14, 2023 Plaintiff Laurie Encinas unfortunately passed away. On November 30, 2023, a first amended complaint was filed for wrongful death and survivor damages by Laurie Encinas’ family including Jesus Encinas, Priscilla Chey, Jesus Encinas, Jr., Crystal Laurie Encinas and Jennifer Rose Encinas, individually and on behalf of the estate of Laurie Encinas (collectively, “Plaintiffs”). (See Li Decl. ¶ 3).

o   On July 11, 2023, MTCI filed its motion for summary judgment against Plaintiff’s original complaint. (See Li Decl. ¶ 4).

o   On January 30, 2024, MTCI notified all counsel that the motion scheduled on February 5, 2024 was off calendar. Given that Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint on November 30, 2023, MTCI’s motion filed on July 11, 2023 was moot because it was a motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s original complaint. In addition, MTCI intended to file a new motion for summary judgment and/or adjudication to encompass a new argument that Plaintiffs are not entitled to survivor damages such as pre-death pain and suffering pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 377.34(b). (See Li Decl. ¶ 6).

o   Instead of cancelling the reservation, MTCI continued the hearing under the reservation number 536163003873 to October 24, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

o   MTCI mistakenly and inadvertently did not cancel the reservation number 536163003873 or the hearing on October 24, 2024. Indeed, MTCI did not even file a reply to the opposition to the motion because it did not realize until the Court’s tentative ruling that the hearing was still on calendar. (See Li Decl. ¶ 7).

·       The MSJ filed on July 11, 2023 was moot because of the First Amended Complaint

o   The first amended complaint was filed by Laurie Encinas’ family alleging the wrongful death and survivor damages. An amended pleading making substantive changes supersedes the original. See State Comp. Ins. Fund v. Superior Court (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1124, 1130-1131.

·       Counsel for MTCI mistakenly and inadvertently failed to take the hearing on a moot motion for summary judgment scheduled on October 24, 2024 off calendar.

·       Therefore, the Court should allow MTCI to re-file a motion for summary judgment/adjudication on: 1) whether MTCI had a duty of care given the trivial defect doctrine; and 2) whether Plaintiffs are entitled to pre-death pain and suffering pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 377.34.

·       MTCI has not filed an answer to the FAC

o   An amended complaint that omits defendants named in the original complaint operates as a dismissal as to them.

o   In this case, Plaintiff filed the original complaint on May 13, 2021 and filed a Doe amendment naming MTCI on June 15, 2021. Plaintiff’s first amended complaint does allege Does are responsible but has not amended the first amended complaint to name MTCI as a Doe defendant

 

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Defendant’s Allegations the FAC Complaint Dismissed All Does is Moot

o   Defendant provides absolutely no authority that a Doe Defendant that was amended to name an actual defendant via Amendment to Complaint (Fictitious/incorrect Name) is dismissed by virtue of the filing of a first amended complaint.

o   In ruling on Plaintiffs’ Motion for Case Management Conference and/or Trial Setting, the Court found that MTCI was not dismissed

·       The Motion for Summary Judgment was Not Moot Because of the FAC

o   The cases relied on by Defendant to support his position are not persuasive. In those cases, the courts held the summary adjudication or judgment orders were void because the amended complaints asserted new claims, allegations, or defendants.

o   The test for what is and is not a “substantive change” should focus on whether the correction or addition might give rise to any different amount or form of liability or indicate the existence of any defense or ground for avoiding liability, not reasonably disclosed in the original complaint. Paterra v. Hansen (2021) 64 Cal. App. 5th at 530, 279 (quoting text); see also Ford v. Sup.Ct. (1973) 34 Cal. App. 3d 338, 343 - adding a new cause of action based on a different legal theory (e.g., amended complaint alleging negligent, instead of intentional, misrepresentation).

o   Here, with regard to the legal theory of liability, and the Defendants desire to file an additional Motion for Summary Judgement on whether MTCI had a duty of care given the trivial defect doctrine; which is unchanged by the filing of the FAC. The building code violation at the threshold Plaintiff tripped and fell on - that Defendant contends is a trivial defect - is the exact same defect as in the original complaint as is the FAC. Defendant, presumably knowing that new evidence is not permitted on reply, wants a second bite at the apple.

·       Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b) does not apply

o   The discretionary relief provisions of CCP § 473(b) apply to a motion for relief from a summary judgment on the grounds of mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Henderson v PG&E Co. (2010) 187 Cal. App. 4th 215, 229; Huh v Wang (2007) 158 Cal. App. 4th 1406, 1419.

o   Here, Defendant alleges that “[a]lthough MTCI intended to file a new motion for summary judgment/adjudication, MTCI had not yet filed or served the motion. MTCI mistakenly and inadvertently did not cancel the reservation number 536163003873 or the hearing on October 24, 2024.” See Motion P. 3: 23-28 and Declaration of Li, ¶ 7.

o   These allegations simply do not meet the discretionary relief provisions of CCP § 473(b).

o   It is equally irrelevant that Defendant did not file a reply to Plaintiffs’ opposition as it could not bring new evidence and it would not have mattered either way as the Court considered the moving papers, opposition thereto, declarations, affidavits, and objections to evidence.

·       Defendant fails to comply with Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b)

o   Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b) requires that “[a]pplication for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.”

o   Defendant’s motion for relief is devoid of the proposed Motion for Summary Judgment and as such the Court is required to deny this motion.

 

MTCI filed a reply, arguing:

·       MTCI seeks an order that allows it to re-file a motion for summary judgment on the trivial defect issue.

·       MTCI maintains the FAC supersedes the original complaint and mooted the MSJ

·       MTCI’s mistake meets the discretionary relief provisions of Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b). MTCI’s counsel’s mistake was essentially a calendaring mistake. In Elston, the court found that relief was properly granted “where an attorney states that he was unaware of his duty to appear or answer because his employees misplaced papers or misinformed him as to the relevant date.” (Elston v. City of Turlock (1985) 38 Cal.3d 227, 234.)

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

The discretionary relief provisions of section 473, subdivision (b) provide in pertinent part: 

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” 

 

As to discretionary relief, “[a]lthough a trial court has discretion to vacate the entry of a default or subsequent judgment, this discretion may be exercised only after the party seeking relief has shown that there is a proper ground for relief, and that the party has raised that ground in a procedurally proper manner, within any applicable time limits.” (Cruz v. Fagor America, Inc. (2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.) 

 

B.      Procedural Deficiency

As an initial matter, MTCI’s Motion fails to comply with section 473, subdivision (b).

Under section 473, subdivision (b), an application for relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.

Here, MTCI requests relief from the Court’s order denying MTCI’s Motion for Summary Judgment to Plaintiff’s original Complaint. MTCI seeks leave to re-file a Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication. However, MTCI has not filed a proposed Motion for Summary Judgment with its instant application for relief.

 

Therefore, pursuant to section 473, subdivision (b), MTCI’s motion cannot be granted based on this procedural deficiency. Nevertheless, the Court continues its discussion on the merits.

C.      Discussion

 

MTCI argues that due to counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, they had failed to take the hearing on their Motion for Summary Judgment off calendar.

 

On July 11, 2023, MTCI filed its motion for summary judgment on the trivial defect doctrine as to the original complaint. (Li Decl., ¶ 4.) The motion for summary judgment hearing was originally scheduled for September 28, 2023 at 8:30 a.m. under reservation number 536163003873. (Li Decl., ¶ 5.) The motion hearing was continued to February 5, 2024 under the same reservation. (Ibid.)

 

On November 30, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a FAC alleging wrongful death and survivor damages by Jesus Encinas, Priscilla Chey, Jesus Encinas, Jr., Crystal Laurie Encinas and Jennifer Rose Encinas, individually and on behalf of the estate of Laurie Encinas. (Li Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. B.)

 

On January 30, 2024, MTCI notified all counsel that the motion scheduled on February 5, 2024 was off calendar because the motion was now moot due to Plaintiff’s filing of the FAC. (Li Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the Motion for Summary Judgment was not moot.

 

However, courts have generally found that an amended complaint supersedes all prior complaints.  (State Compensation Ins. Fund v. Superior Court (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1124, 1130-31.)  The amended complaint furnishes the sole basis for the cause of action, and the original complaint ceases to have any effect either as a pleading or as a basis for judgment.  (Ibid.)  Since there is but one complaint in a civil action, the filing of an amended complaint moots a motion directed to a prior complaint.  (Ibid.)  Accordingly, once an amended complaint is filed, it is error to grant summary judgment or adjudication on a cause of action contained in a previous complaint.  (Ibid.) 

 

Therefore, since MTCI assumed their motion was moot, they intended to file a new motion for summary judgment and/or adjudication. MTCI contends they also intended to include a new argument that Plaintiffs are not entitled to survivor damages such as pre-death pain and suffering pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 377.34(b). (Li Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

However, MTCI’s counsel states that instead of cancelling the reservation, the hearing was mistakenly and inadvertently continued to October 24, 2024. (Li Decl., ¶ 6.) MTCI’s counsel further states she did not realize a hearing was still on calendar until she saw the Court’s tentative ruling on October 24, 2024. (Ibid.) MTCI states they did not file a reply, evidentiary objections or prepare oral argument. Thus, MTCI seeks to file a new Motion for Summary Judgment as to Plaintiffs’ FAC.

 

Based on these grounds, the Court finds that MTCI may be entitled to seek relief based on counsel’s inadvertence and mistake. However, as discussed above, MTCI’s instant motion fails to comply with section 473, subdivision (b). Therefore, MTCI’s motion is DENIED.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendant Montebello Town Center Investors, LLC’s Motion for Relief of Order Denying Motion for Summary Judgment Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 473(b) is DENIED without prejudice.

CHRISMAN v. PUENTE HILLS SUBARU, et al. (24STCV17114)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Carney Shegerian (Shegerian & Associates, Inc.)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Gene Williams (Paduano & Weintraub LLP)

 

DEFENDANT SUBARU OF AMERICA, INC.’S demurrer TO THE complaint (filed 11/15/24 and 11/19/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Defendant Subaru of America, Inc’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff Dominique Chrisman filed this employment action against Defendants Puente Hills Subaru, Bakhtiari, LLC and Subaru Corporation.

 

On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amendment to the Complaint to correct the name of Defendant Subaru Corporation to Subaru of America, Inc.

 

Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action:

1.       Discrimination in Violation of the FEHA

2.      Hostile Work Environment Harassment in Violation of the FEHA

3.      Retaliation in Violation of the FEHA

4.      Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodation in Violation of the FEHA

5.      Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process in Violation of the FEHA

6.      Failure to Prevent Discrimination, Harassment, or Retaliation in Violation of the FEHA

7.      Negligent Hiring, Supervision and Retention

8.      Wrongful Termination of Employment in Violation of Public Policy

9.      Whistleblower Retaliation

10.  Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff began working for Defendant Puente Hills Subaru (“Puente Hills”) in December of 2019. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) Plaintiff initially joined Puente Hills as a Lot Supervisor and was subsequently promoted through the ranks, ultimately assuming the role of Delivery Specialist. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff has a mental disability in which he suffers from severe anxiety and depression. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff engaged in, or Defendants believed that Plaintiff had engaged or may engage in, protected activity and conduct. (Ibid.) In 2021, Plaintiff began working under the supervision of Saman Bakhtiari, who consistently engaged in condescending, hostile, and intimidating behavior towards his employees. (Complaint, ¶ 14.) Around May of 2022, Lorenzo Serrano (“Serrano”), the general manager, requested Plaintiff purchase gift cards and bitcoin for unhappy customers using Plaintiff’s personal funds, assuring Plaintiff that Puente Hills would reimburse these expenses and provide Plaintiff with a bonus for using his own funds. Shortly after, Serrano began exerting pressure on Plaintiff for additional bitcoin purchases, asking Plaintiff to purchase thousands of dollars' worth of bitcoin with his own money. Plaintiff promptly declined, as he did not feel comfortable spending such large amounts of his own money and he had not received the bonus he was promised. Days afterwards, Serrano summoned Plaintiff to his office, where another employee, Jeffrey Salier (“Salier”), was present. Serrano and Salier were known for being good friends at the dealership. Serrano and Salier accused Plaintiff of stealing $3,500 worth of gift cards – a claim entirely devoid of merit. Following this disconcerting encounter, Serrano sent Plaintiff a suspicious text message stating, “Don’t worry about the missing cards.” Plaintiff promptly contacted chief financial officer, Omar Zeola, to report the encounter with Serrano and Salier, as well as report all purchases Serrano had asked him to make. In August of 2022, Serrano was terminated for accounting discrepancies. Salier took over as general manager, with direct oversight of Plaintiff. This transition created severe discomfort for Plaintiff, as Salier’s retaliatory behaviors and attitude were evident, including scheduling Plaintiff to come in on his days off. (Ibid.) These conditions caused Plaintiff to suffer from severe anxiety and depression and caused him to arrive late to work due to his deteriorating mental health. (Complaint, ¶ 15.) On January 3, 2023, Salier terminated Plaintiff, pretextually for tardiness. (Ibid.)

 

On November 15, 2024 and on November 19, 2024, Defendant Subaru of America, Inc. (“Subaru of America”) filed a Demurrer to the Complaint, arguing:

·       Plaintiff’s Complaint fails to state an actionable claim against Subaru of America because it does not assert facts sufficient to identify any basis upon which he contends that Subaru of America was Plaintiff’s employer or joint employer and, accordingly, there is no basis for liability on the claims alleged. Thus, Subaru of America demurs to the entire Complaint.

·       Subaru of America also demurs to the entire Complaint on the basis of uncertainty.

·       The result of Plaintiff’s failure to plead necessary facts is to leave Subaru of America unable to ascertain the nature or scope of the claims against it, necessitating dismissal of the Complaint as to Subaru of America.

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiff has adequately alleged Subaru’s liability as a joint employer under California law.

o   A party qualifies as an employer if it (a) exercises control over wages, hours, or working conditions; (b) suffers or permits work; or (c) engages in a common law employment relationship. (Martinez v. Combs (2010) 49 Cal.4th 35, 64.) Plaintiff alleges that Subaru directly or indirectly employed him and acted as an agent of Puente Hills Subaru. These allegations are sufficient to apprise Subaru of its potential liability.

o   Discovery is necessary to clarify Subaru of America’s role.

·       Plaintiff contends the Complaint is not uncertain.

o   Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and strictly construed. Ambiguities in pleadings can and should be resolved through discovery, particularly when the defendant has superior knowledge of the relevant facts. (Chen v. Berenjian (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 811, 822.) Plaintiff’s allegations are intelligible and provide sufficient notice of the claims.

 

Subaru of America filed a reply, arguing:

·       Plaintiff fails to assert any actual facts supporting an employment relationship between Subaru of America and Plaintiff.

·       Plaintiff effectively concedes that he has no evidence of an actual employment relationship between himself and Subaru of America when he argues that he needs to conduct discovery in order to establish such a relationship.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Here, counsel for Subaru of America submitted a declaration stating she discussed the grounds of the Demurrer with Plaintiff’s counsel by telephone on October 28, 2024 and in subsequent emails. (Leon Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) The Court finds the moving party satisfied its meet and confer obligations.   

 

B.      Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

C.      Joint Employer Liability

 

Subaru of America argues that the Complaint does not state facts sufficient to support joint employer liability.

 

To determine whether an organization is a joint employer, courts look to the facts surrounding the employment relationship. (Vernon v. State of California (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 114, 124-125). Factors considered include payment of salary to the employee, the location where the employee performed the work, the employer’s authority to hire and discharge the employee, and the employer’s overall control over the means and manner of the employee’s performance. (Id. at p. 125.) Further, “a person can be a joint employer without exercising direct control over the employee. If the putative joint employer instead exercises enough control over the intermediary entity to indirectly dictate the wages, hours, or working conditions of the employee, that is a sufficient showing of joint employment.” (Medina v. Equilon Enterprises, LLC (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 868, 879).

 

California law defines “employ” using three alternative tests: 1. Exercising control over wages, hours, or working conditions. 2. Suffering or permitting work. 3. Engaging in a common law employment relationship. (Martinez v. Combs (2010) 49 Cal.4th 35, 64.)

 

Here, the Complaint alleges that Defendants Subaru of America, Puente Hills Subaru, and Bakhtiari, LLC collectively employed Plaintiff and engaged in unlawful conduct that culminated in his wrongful termination. Specifically, the Complaint alleges that:

 

Defendants directly and indirectly employed Plaintiff under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (“FEHA”). (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

 

All defendants acted as agents of all other defendants in committing the acts alleged herein. (Complaint, ¶ 7.)

 

All defendants compelled, coerced, aided, and/or abetted the discrimination, retaliation, and harassment alleged in this Complaint, which conduct is prohibited under California Government Code section 12940(i). (Complaint, ¶ 4.)

 

The Court finds these allegations are not sufficient to support joint employer liability. While the Complaint alleges that Defendants collectively employed Plaintiff, there are no other allegations of Subaru of America’s control over aspects of the employment relationship, such as control over wages, hours, or working conditions.

 

Plaintiff argues in its opposition that Subaru of America’s potential involvement in overseeing policies, compliance, and training at Puente Hills Subaru supports its classification as a joint employer. In support, Plaintiff cites to Castaneda v. Ensign Group, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1015, where the court found that an entity that supplies policies, training, and oversight can be liable as an employer, even without direct control over day-to-day operations

 

However, Plaintiff does not allege in the Complaint that Subaru of America was involved in overseeing policies, compliance, and training at Puente Hills Subaru.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds Plaintiff has not sufficiently alleged joint employment liability as to Subaru of America. Thus, the demurrer to each cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

D.     Uncertainty

 

Subaru of American also demurs to the entire Complaint on the basis of uncertainty.

 

CCP section 430.10(f) provides that a pleading is uncertain if it is ambiguous and unintelligible. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f).) “A demurrer for uncertainty is strictly construed, even where a complaint is in some respects uncertain, because ambiguities can be clarified under modern discovery procedures.” (Khoury v. Maly’s of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.) “A demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that defendant cannot reasonably respond—i.e., he or she cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group) § 7:85 (emphasis in original).) “The objection of uncertainty does not go to the failure to allege sufficient facts.” (Brea v. McGlashan (1934) 3 Cal.App.2d 454, 459.) “It goes to the doubt as to what the pleader means by the facts alleged.” (Id.) “Such a demurrer should not be sustained where the allegations of the complaint are sufficiently clear to apprise the defendant of the issues which he is to meet.” (People v. Lim (1941) 18 Cal.2d 872, 882.)

 

The Court does not find that the Complaint’s causes of action are uncertain. The demurrer is not sustained on this ground.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Defendant Subaru of America, Inc’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.
