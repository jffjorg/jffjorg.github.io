(2)   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents from Potomac River Productions, LLC

The court considered the moving and opposition papers filed in connection with each motion.  No reply papers were filed.

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS FROM SKYDANCE TELEVISION, LLC

Plaintiff Dan Sackheim (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Skydance Television, LLC (“Skydance”) to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 8 and 17, and (2) awarding sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Skydance in the amount of $5,469.  (Nov. 29, 2023 Notice of Partial Withdrawal, p. 2:6-9.)

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Skydance’s further responses to Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 8 and 17 because Skydance asserts both statements of compliance with the demands, which are incomplete, and representations of its inability to comply with the demands, which are inadequate.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2).)  Skydance is required to provide a statement of compliance that complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220 or a representation of its inability to comply that complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230, but not both.

The court finds that the circumstances presented make the imposition of sanctions unjust and therefore denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Skydance.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS FROM POTOMAC RIVER PRODUCTIONS, LCC

Plaintiff moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant Potomac River Productions, LLC (“Potomac”) to provide further responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 8, 30, and 31, and (2) awarding sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Potomac in the amount of $5,469.

The court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel Potomac’s further response to Requests for Production of Documents, number 8 because Potomac asserts both a statement of compliance with the demand, which is incomplete, and a representation of its inability to comply with the demand, which is inadequate.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subds. (a)(1), (a)(2).)  Potomac is required to provide a statement of compliance that complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220 or a representation of its inability to comply that complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230, but not both.

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion to compel Potomac’s further responses to Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 30 and 31 because Potomac’s representations of its inability to comply with the demands are not inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (a)(2).)

In light of the mixed results of the court’s ruling on this motion, the court finds that the circumstances presented make the imposition of sanctions unjust and therefore denies Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Potomac.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)

ORDER

            The court grants in part plaintiff Dan Sackheim’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production of documents from Skydance Television, LLC as follows.

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the court orders defendant Skydance Television, LLC (1) to serve on plaintiff Dan Sackheim further written responses to plaintiff Dan Sackheim’s Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 8 and 17, which comply with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.210-2031.250, and (2) to produce to plaintiff Dan Sackheim all documents and things in defendant Skydance Television, LLC’s possession, custody, or control which are responsive to those requests, within 20 days of the date of this order.

            The court denies plaintiff Dan Sackheim’s request for monetary sanctions against defendant Skydance Television, LLC.

            The court grants in part plaintiff Dan Sackheim’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production of documents from Potomac River Productions, LLC as follows.

            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, the court orders defendant Potomac River Productions, LLC (1) to serve on plaintiff Dan Sackheim a further written response to plaintiff Dan Sackheim’s Requests for Production of Documents, number 8, which complies with Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.210-2031.250, and (2) to produce to plaintiff Dan Sackheim all documents and things in defendant Potomac River Productions, LLC’s possession, custody, or control which are responsive to that request, within 20 days of the date of this order.

            The court denies plaintiff Dan Sackheim’s request for monetary sanctions against defendant Potomac River Productions, LLC.

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

order to show cause re why the court should not, on its own motion, grant a motion for judgment on the pleadings

 

MOVING PARTY:                 The court, on its own motion

RESPONDING PARTY:        Plaintiff David Blank

Order to Show Cause re: Why the Court Should Not, on Its Own Motion, Grant a Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings as to Any or All of the Causes of Action Alleged in Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint, as to Any of the Plaintiffs or Defendants, on the Ground That the Court Does Not Have Jurisdiction of the Subject of the Causes of Action Because They are Subject to The Exclusive Jurisdiction of the Probate Court

            The court considered the First Amended Complaint and the “Declaration of Scott D. Fisher, Esq., in Opposition to Court’s Order to Show Cause re: Service Upon Murray J. Blank’s Successor-In-Interest and/or Personal Representative and re: Judgment on the Pleadings (Subject Matter Jurisdiction,” filed by plaintiff David Blank on December 12, 2023.

DISCUSSION

On July 26, 2023, the court set for hearing on November 1, 2023 an Order to Show Cause re: why the court should not, on its own motion, grant a motion for judgment on the pleadings as to the First Amended Complaint, filed by plaintiffs David Blank and Michael Blank, individually and as trustees of the Blank Family Trust (“Plaintiffs”) against defendants Murray J. Blank (“M. Blank”), Steven G. Blank (“S. Blank”), and Diana N. Blank (“D. Blank”) (collectively, “Defendants”), as to any or all of the causes of action alleged in Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaints, and as to any of the Plaintiffs or Defendants, on the ground that the court does not have jurisdiction of the subject of the causes of action because they are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the probate court.  (July 26, 2023 Minute Order, p. 1.)  The court ordered that any response to the Order to Show Cause re: why the court should not, on its own motion, grant a motion for judgment on the pleadings to be filed and served no later than nine court days before the hearing.  (July 26, 2023 Minute Order, pp. 1-2.)  On October 20, 2023, pursuant to stipulation of the parties, the court continued the hearing on the Order to Show Cause to December 13, 2023.  (Stipulation and Order filed October 20, 2023, p. 4.)

The court grants its own motion for judgment on the pleadings on the ground that it does not have jurisdiction over the subject matter of Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint because the causes of action alleged therein are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the probate court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subds. (b)(2), (c)(3)(B)(i).)

Plaintiffs allege the following facts in their Complaint.  Defendant M. Blank served as the initial successor trustee of the Blank Family Trust (the “Trust”).  (FAC ¶ 11.)  Over the course of one year, following the death of Jeffrey R. Blank, defendant M. Blank (1) diluted Trust assets for his personal benefit; (2) gave Trust assets, including money, to other, non-beneficiary individuals; (3) did not respond to inquiries from Plaintiffs, the Trust beneficiaries; (4) withheld Trust property from Plaintiffs, including documents necessary to operate the Trust; (5) misrepresented his status as trustee of the Trust to third parties; (6) failed to safeguard and preserve Trust assets; (7) failed to timely remit financial and other documents belonging to the Trust; and (8) removed hundreds of savings bonds from Jeffrey Blank’s safe following his death.  (FAC ¶¶ 12-13.)  M. Blank retains possession of documents belonging to the Trust, which M. Blank has refused to provide to Plaintiffs.  (FAC ¶ 13.)

“Technically, California does not have ‘probate courts.’”  (In re Michael R. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 126, 131, fn. 1.)  “Rather, probate jurisdiction is vested in the superior court.”  (Probate Jurisdiction and Venue, Cal. Prac. Guide Probate Ch. 3-B, § 3:34.)  “The term ‘probate court’ is but a convenient way of expressing the concept of a superior court sitting in exercise of its probate jurisdiction.”  (Copley v. Copley (1978) 80 Cal.App.3d 97, 107.)  The probate departments have, pursuant to state statute, exclusive jurisdiction over certain types of actions, including jurisdiction to probate and interpret a will, and over proceedings concerning the internal affairs of trusts.  (Capra v. Capra (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 1072, 1083; Prob. Code, § 17200, subd. (b)(12) [proceedings concerning the internal affairs of a trust include proceedings “Compelling redress of a breach of the trust by any available remedy”].)

The first and second causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty and aiding and abetting breaches of fiduciary duty, respectively, are based on the allegations that (1) M. Blank, in acting as trustee for the Trust, breached his fiduciary duties by engaging in the conduct set forth above, including the misappropriation and withholding of Trust assets and documents, and (2) S. Blank and D. Blank assisted M. Blank in breaching the fiduciary duties that he owed to the Trust.  (FAC ¶¶ 12, 18-19.)  The third cause of action for conversion is based on the allegation that Defendants converted property belonging to Plaintiffs and to the Trust, including by taking possession of Trust property and refusing to return such property to Plaintiffs.  (FAC ¶¶ 35-36.)  The fourth cause of action alleges that Defendants “received money that was intended to be used for the benefit of the Trust and its beneficiaries[.]”  (FAC ¶ 31.)  The eighth cause of action for negligence is based on M. Blank’s alleged breach of his duties to Plaintiffs “in performing tax and financial services for the Trust.”  (FAC ¶ 62.)  The ninth cause of action seeks an accounting due to M. Blank’s alleged misappropriation of Trust assets and collection of savings bonds.  (FAC ¶ 70.) 

The court finds that the first through fourth, eighth, and ninth causes of action, which are based on Defendants’ alleged actions in committing or assisting breaches of fiduciary duties to the Trust or the wrongful possession or misappropriation of Trust property, as set forth above, (1) seek “redress of a breach of the trust by any available remedy[,]” and (2) therefore are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the probate court.  (Prob. Code, § 17200, subd. (b)(12); Estate of Bowles, supra, 169 Cal.App.4th at p. 695.)

The fifth cause of action for claim and delivery alleges that (1) defendant M. Blank removed savings bonds from Jeffrey Blank’s safe, and (2) Plaintiffs were the intended recipients of the savings bonds.  (FAC ¶¶ 48-49.)  Similarly, the sixth and seventh causes of action are based on the allegations that Defendants took possession of money, savings bonds, and documents that belong to Plaintiffs.  (FAC ¶¶ 51, 55.)  To the extent that the causes of action are based on the alleged wrongful possession of money and documents (i.e., as alleged in support of the sixth and seventh causes of action), those facts appear to allege misappropriation of Trust property, because Plaintiffs have alleged that M. Blank gave Trust assets, including money, to his children, ex-wife, and paid certain Trust fees to himself, and that Defendants withheld documents relating to the Trust from Plaintiffs.  (FAC ¶¶ 12-13.)  

The court notes that Plaintiffs did not specifically allege that the savings bonds constitute Trust property.  (FAC ¶ 13 [M. Blank “removed hundreds of savings bonds from Jeffrey Blank’s safe following his death on November 30, 2020”].)  However, the court finds relevant and helpful to its interpretation of the First Amended Complaint the allegations set forth in connection with the ninth cause of action for an accounting, which allege that (1) M. Blank owed a duty to Plaintiffs “to avoid engaging in actions which constitute misappropriation and/or theft of Trust documents and monies, including savings bonds[,]” and (2) M. Blank took the subject “documents, monies, and savings bonds” while he was serving as trustee of the Trust.  (FAC ¶¶ 69 [emphasis added], 73.)  Thus, in reading the First Amended Complaint in its entirety, the court interprets the allegations relating to the alleged theft of savings bonds to constitute misappropriation and theft of Trust assets.  (Ibid.)

Thus, the court finds that the fifth through seventh causes of action of action seek “redress of a breach of the trust by any available remedy” since they are based on the allegations that Defendants took possession of money, documents, and savings bonds belonging to the Trust, and therefore are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of the probate court.  (Prob. Code, § 17200, subd. (b)(12); Estate of Bowles, supra, 169 Cal.App.4th at p. 695.)

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 48 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  The court finds that Plaintiffs have not met their burden to establish how they can amend their complaint to bring it within the jurisdiction of this court by failing to address, in plaintiff David Blank’s response to the court’s Order to Show Cause, what facts Plaintiffs could allege to render their complaint sufficient.  The court therefore grants the motion for judgment on the pleadings without leave to amend.  

ORDER

            The court, on its own motion, grants a motion for judgment on the pleadings without leave to amend as to plaintiffs David Blank and Michael Blank, individually and as trustees of the Blank Family Trust’s First Amended Complaint.

            The court orders that this action is dismissed.

The court orders defendants Steven G. Blank and Diana N. Blank to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion for leave to conduct limited discovery on the issue of arbitration

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                Plaintiff Jane Doe

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants Church of Scientology International, Bridge Publications, Inc., and Religious Technology Center

Motion for Leave to Conduct Limited Discovery on the Issue of Arbitration

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order permitting her to conduct limited discovery on the issue of arbitration so that she can oppose the pending motion to compel arbitration filed by defendants Church of Scientology International, Bridge Publications, Inc., and Religious Technology Center (“Defendants”).  Specifically, Plaintiff states that, “[a]mong other things, [she] seeks limited discovery on the facts and circumstances leading to Plaintiff’s signature on the document at issue and documents outlining the scope of a Scientology ‘arbitration.’”  (Notice of Mot., p. 3:21-24.)

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown good cause to conduct limited discovery necessary to oppose Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration.

First, the court finds that it has the authority to grant Plaintiff leave to obtain specified discovery.

As noted by Defendants, the court issued an order on October 27, 2023, granting Defendants’ motion to stay and ordering that this action is stayed pending resolution of their motion to compel arbitration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.4.  (Oct. 27, 2023 Order, p. 2:15-18.)  “‘The purpose of the statutory stay [required pursuant to section 1281.4] is to protect the jurisdiction of the arbitrator by preserving the status quo until arbitration is resolved.  [Citations.]  [¶]  In the absence of a stay, the continuation of the proceedings in the trial court disrupts the arbitration proceedings and can render them ineffective.  [Citation.]’”  (MKJA, Inc. v. 123 Fit Franchising, LLC (2011) 191 Cal.App.4th 643, 658.)

The court finds Aronow v. Superior Court (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 865 to be instructive, although the court acknowledges, as Defendants have pointed out, that the factual circumstances presented in Aronow are slightly different than those presented here.  Specifically, the Aronow Court considered whether “a trial court that granted a defendant’s petition to compel arbitration [has] jurisdiction to lift the stay of trial court proceedings where a plaintiff demonstrates financial inability to pay the anticipated arbitration costs[.]”  (Aronow, supra, 76 Cal.App.5th at p. 870.)  In determining this issue, the Court of Appeal noted that the defendants claimed the right to challenge the plaintiff’s in forma pauperis status and asserted the need to conduct discovery to pursue that objection.  (Id. at p. 884.)  The court “recognize[d] that interest and conclude[d], where appropriate, it can be accommodated by the trial court.”  (Ibid.)  The court further stated that, “[w]here a party to a contract with an arbitration provision opposes a motion to compel arbitration on the ground of inability to pay the costs, the moving party can ask leave to conduct limited discovery directed only to the opponent’s financial circumstances.”  (Ibid.)

The court finds that the reasoning set forth in Aronow applies to the circumstances presented by Plaintiff in the pending motion.  Plaintiff has sought leave to conduct limited discovery to oppose Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration on the ground that the subject arbitration agreement was executed as a result of duress, undue influence, and fraud.  (Mot., p. 5:17-19.)  The court finds that it may grant leave to conduct limited discovery directed only to those issues.  (Aronow, supra, 76 Cal.App.5th at p. 884; Brown v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 938, 951 [noting that the trial court continued the hearing on a motion to compel arbitration to allow the plaintiffs to conduct discovery regarding circumstances of the execution of the arbitration agreement in order to determine whether there was evidence of fraud or unconscionability].)  The court further notes that granting Plaintiff leave to conduct limited discovery does not conflict with the purpose of the statutory stay, since (1) this discovery goes to the formation of the agreement containing the arbitration provision, and therefore, (2) allowing discovery will not render any subsequent arbitration proceedings, in the event that the court grants Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration, ineffective.  (MKJA, Inc., supra, 191 Cal.App.4th at p. 658.)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiff has shown that leave to conduct limited discovery on the specified issues is warranted and necessary.

Plaintiff states that, although she was present when the arbitration agreement was signed, she does not have access to certain of Defendants’ documents to support her claims of fraud, undue influence, and duress.  For example, Plaintiff states that she does not have access to “FLAG policies regarding visitors and requiring visitors to sign such documents,” which may aid in her defenses to Defendants’ motion.  (Mot., p. 7:11-15.) 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has shown good cause to grant her leave to conduct arbitration-related discovery.

The court, however, finds that the scope of such discovery shall be limited in scope.  In Plaintiff’s notice of motion, she states that she is seeking to conduct discovery regarding, inter alia, “the facts and circumstances leading to Plaintiff’s signature on the document at issue and documents outlining the scope of a Scientology ‘arbitration.’”  (Notice of Mot., p. 3:21-24.)  In reply, Plaintiff has set forth various categories of discovery to be obtained, but Defendants have not had an opportunity to address the motion as to those specific categories.  (Reply, pp. 7:21-9:19.)

The court finds that Plaintiff has shown good cause to conduct limited discovery as to the following issues: (1) the circumstances that led to Plaintiff’s signing the agreement that contains the arbitration clause, and (2) the rules and procedures governing Scientology arbitration, but only as they relate to the arbitration agreement at issue in Defendants’ pending motion to compel arbitration.  The court therefore grants Plaintiff’s motion to conduct limited discovery as to those issues.

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiff Jane Doe’s motion for leave to conduct limited discovery on the issue of arbitration as follows.

The court orders that plaintiff Jane Doe is granted leave to conduct discovery as to the following issues in order to oppose the motion to compel arbitration filed by defendants Church of Scientology International, Bridge Publications, Inc., and Religious Technology Center: (1) the circumstances that led to plaintiff Jane Doe’s signing the agreement that contains the arbitration clause, and (2) the rules and procedures governing Scientology arbitration, but only as they relate to the arbitration agreement at issue in the pending motion to compel arbitration.

The court orders plaintiff Jane Doe to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
