ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANT KAISER FOUNDATION HEALTH PLAN, INC.’S DEMURRER

 

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT KAISER FOUNDATION HEALTH PLAN, INC.’S MOTION TO STRIKE

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Jose Flores (“Plaintiff”) alleges former Defendant Ashish Sehgal (“Sehgal”) sexually assaulted him on June 2, 2009, while administering emergency medical care at a Kaiser Permanente hospital in Panorama City, CA. (See First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶¶ 16-24.)

Plaintiff served his Complaint and Summons on a defendant Kaiser Foundation & Health Plan Inc. on September 3, 2021. The clerk of court entered judgment against Kaiser Foundation & Health Plan Inc. on November 4, 2021.

On December 17, 2021, the court approved Plaintiff’s amendment to his Complaint, correcting Kaiser Foundation & Health Plan Inc.’s name to Defendant Kaiser Foundation Health Plan, Inc. (“Defendant”).

Plaintiff attempted to have default judgment entered against Defendant several times since initially filing his action on September 2, 2021. As part of that process, on March 22, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Sehgal.

On October 4, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons. On October 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed his FAC and properly served his FAC and Summons on Defendant.

Defendant now demurrers to all Plaintiff’s causes of action and seeks to strike portions of Plaintiff’s FAC.

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On September 2, 2021, Plaintiff served his Complaint, alleging (1) Sexual Rape; (2) Sexual Battery Pursuant to Civil Code § 1708.5; (3) Assault; (4) Battery; (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (6) Professional Negligence (As to Sehgal); and (7) Professional Negligence (As to Kaiser Permanente).

On November 4, 2021, the clerk of court entered default against “Kaiser Foundation & Health Plan Inc.”

On December 17, 2021, Plaintiff amended his Complaint to correct Defendant’s name to “Kaiser Foundation Health Plain, Inc.”

On August 18, 2023, the court granted Plaintiff’s Motion to Seal.

On October 2, 2023, this matter was transferred to this Court, which has held several hearings addressing various defects in Plaintiff’s default judgment package.

On March 22, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Sehgal.

On October 4, 2024, this Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons.

On October 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed his FAC, alleging (1) Sexual Rape; (2) Sexual Battery Pursuant to Civil Code § 1708.5; (3) Assault; (4) Battery; (5) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (6) Professional Negligence; and (7) Professional Negligence.

On November 22, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Demurrer and Motion to Strike (“Motion”).

On December 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Court Order to File an Amended Pleading Including Claim for Punitive Damages.

On December 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition to Defendant’s Demurrer (“Opposition”) and Motion (“Opposition Motion”).

On December 30, 2024, Defendant filed its Replies.

On December 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Amended Request for Judicial Notice (“RJN”).

On December 31, 2024, Defendant filed an Objection to Plaintiff’s RJN (“Objection RJN”).

III.         DEMURRER

A.             Legal Standard

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589(a); see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[1] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.” (Mitchell v. California Dept. of Pub. Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007, citation omitted.) “[T]he facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

“A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245)

B.             Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), emphasis added.) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

          Defendant filed a declaration attesting to the parties’ meet and confer effort, which consisted of an emailed and mailed meet and confer letter. (See Declaration of Megan Lane filed November 22, 2024, ¶¶ 2-3, Ex. A.) Defendant has neither fulfilled its meet and confer obligations nor explained why they failed to do so. The Court will issue a ruling on Defendant’s Demurrer in the interest of judicial efficiency this time. However, the Court reminds both parties it expects compliance with their meet and confer obligations in future filings before this Court and that failure to do so could result in denial of a motion for that reason alone.

D.            Plaintiff’s Claims are not Barred by Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5 or § 340.16’s Statute of Limitations.

1.               Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5.

Defendant first argues Plaintiff’s claims are barred by Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5’s statute of limitations because the gravamen of all Plaintiff’s claims is medical malpractice. (See Demurrer, pg. 2, citing Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 336 (“Larson”.) “[T]he gravamen of his claims are professional negligence” as they arose out of Sehgal’s “rendering of professional services.” (Id. at 3.) As such, Plaintiff’s claims should have been brought three years after the date of the incident [June 3, 2009][2] or within one year after Plaintiff discovered, or should have discovered, his injury. (See id.) Defendant argues Plaintiff’s long list of reasons why he did not bring the action sooner[3] do not support a reasonable finding of delayed discovery and, in any event, Plaintiff has not alleged fraud, intentional concealment, or the presence of a foreign body such that the statute of limitations would be tolled. (See id. at 4-6.)

In Opposition, Plaintiff argues the gravamen of his claims are not merely medical malpractice but are “Civil, and Criminal, a very serious felony offense Pursuant to; California Penal Code Section 243.4 PC: Sexual Battery, Assault.” (Opposition, pg. 15.)[4],[5]

The Court does not find all of Plaintiff’s claims arise out of Sehgal’s alleged professional negligence. Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5 provides that “[i]n an action for injury or death against a health care provider based upon such person’s alleged professional negligence, the time for the commencement of action shall be three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers, or through the use of reasonable diligence should have discovered, the injury, whichever occurs first.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.5.) “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence, which would require application of MICRA.” (Larson, 230 Cal.App.4th at 347 (emphasis in original).) “Professional negligence” is “[a] negligent act or omission to act by a healthcare provider in the rendering of professional services, which act or omission is the proximate cause of a personal injury.” (Larcher v. Wanless (1976) 18 Cal.3d 656, 657-58.)

          The Court finds Larson, relied on by Defendant in support of its argument, instructive. In Larson, the plaintiff alleged the anesthesiologist handled him roughly during a preoperative checkup and in administering anesthesia (to include grabbing and twisting his arm, prying open his mouth, pushing his face). (See Larson, 230 Cal.App.4th at 351.) The Larson Court found the plaintiff “simply claims [the anesthesiologist] performed his professional services in an unnecessarily harsh and forceful manner, which amounts to a claim [the anesthesiologist] failed to meet the applicable standard of care in rendering his services.” (Id., 230 Cal.App.4th at 352.) Importantly, the “focus of Larson's claims concern the manner in which [the anesthesiologist] rendered his professional services as an anesthesiologist, not some collateral course of conduct pursued for [the anesthesiologist]'s own gain or gratification.” (Id. (emphasis added).)

          Here, however, Plaintiff claims he was admitted to “the emergency room at the Panorama City, California Kaiser hospital for hyperglycemia and anxiety” but that “[a]t some point during the physical evaluation [Sehgal] without a written or verbal consent, without explaining anything and without Plaintiff’s permission went ahead and told Plaintiff to lie on his side, Plaintiff was resting on the bed face up wearing a gown, them [Sehgal] with gloves inserted his index finger into Plaintiff’s anus for a period of time.” (FAC, ¶ 18.) This act was done despite the fact that “there was no medical or professional purpose for this act, defendants [sic] intent was sexual arousal, sexual gratification and sexual abuse…[t]his vile and despicable act was perpetrated outside of the scope of treatment and without consent with no medical purpose.” (Id. at ¶ 21; see also id. at ¶ 44 (alleging “defendants [sic] intent was sexual arousal, sexual gratification and sexual battery”).)

          Here, unlike in Larson, Plaintiff alleges Sehgal actions were collateral to his professional services as an emergency room physician and were pursued for Sehgal’s own sexual gratification. Thus, the Court does not find all of Plaintiff’s claims arose out of Sehgal’s professional negligence such that Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5 is applicable.

2.               Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16.

Defendant next argues Plaintiff’s claims are barred under Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16, as the incident occurred in 2009 when Plaintiff was over 18, occurred over 10 years prior to Plaintiff filing his Complaint, and because “plaintiff has not alleged any facts sufficient to establish that a reasonable person would not have discovered the injury or illness sooner than September 2, 2018.” (Demurrer, pg. 6.) Defendant argues Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(e) does not revive Plaintiff’s claims because Plaintiff does not adequately allege all elements of the revival statute, including that “defendant engaged in an alleged ‘cover up’.” (Id. at 7.)

In Opposition, Plaintiff argues his claims are timely under Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(a)(2) and (e). (See Opposition, pp. 5-7.)

The Court finds Plaintiff’s claims are not barred by Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16’s limitations period. “In any civil action for recovery of damages suffered as a result of sexual assault, where the assault occurred on or after the plaintiff’s 18th birthday, the time for commencement of the action shall be the later of the following: [¶] (1) Within 10 years from the date of the last act, attempted act, or assault with the intent to commit an act, of sexual assault against the plaintiff. [¶] (2) Within three years from the date the plaintiff discovers or reasonably should have discovered that an injury or illness resulted from an act, attempted act, or assault with the intent to commit an act, of sexual assault against the plaintiff.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.16(a).) The current version of Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16 became effective on January 1, 2023, and included the addition of two relevant statute of limitations revival provisions, § 340.16(b)(3) and § 340.16(e). Both provisions allow for the revival of sexual assault claims that would otherwise be barred by Code of Civil Procedure 340.16(a).

Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(b)(3) states as follows: “This section applies to any action described in subdivision (a) that is based upon conduct that occurred on or after January 1, 2009, and is commenced on or after January 1, 2019, that would have been barred solely because the applicable statute of limitations has or had expired. Such claims are hereby revived and may be commenced until December 31, 2026.”

Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(e)(1) states as follows: “Notwithstanding any other law, any claim seeking to recover damages suffered as a result of a sexual assault that occurred on or after the plaintiff's 18th birthday that would otherwise be barred before January 1, 2023, solely because the applicable statute of limitations has or had expired, is hereby revived, and a cause of action may proceed if already pending in court on January 1, 2023, or, if not filed by that date, may be commenced between January 1, 2023, and December 31, 2023.” Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(e)(2) revives claims brought by a plaintiff who alleges: “[¶] (A) The plaintiff was sexually assaulted. [¶] (B) One or more entities are legally responsible for damages arising out of the sexual assault. [¶] (C) The entity or entities, including, but not limited to, their officers, directors, representatives, employees, or agents, engaged in a cover up or attempted a cover up of a previous instance or allegations of sexual assault by an alleged perpetrator of such abuse.”

          Plaintiff claims the incident occurred in June of 2009. (See FAC, ¶¶ 16 & 40.) Plaintiff’s claims would therefore be barred by Code of Civil Procedure § 340.16(a)(1), as Plaintiff filed his Complaint after June 2019. Plaintiff must therefore have brought his claims “[w]ithin three years from the date the plaintiff discovers or reasonably should have discovered that an injury or illness resulted from an act, attempted act, or assault with the intent to commit an act, of sexual assault against the plaintiff” [§ 340.16(a)(2)] or meet the requirements of either of the relevant revival provisions, listed above.

          As currently alleged, the Court finds Plaintiff brought his claims within three years from the date he discovered or reasonably should have discovered his injuries. ¿The accrual date of a cause of action is delayed until a plaintiff both aware of their injury and its negligent cause. (See Jolly v. Eli Lilly & Co. (1988) 44 Cal.3d 1103, 1109.) A plaintiff has “reason to discover a cause of action when he or she ‘has reason at least to suspect a factual basis for its elements.’” (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 807 (citing Norgart v. Upjohn Co. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 383, 398).) “Rather than examining whether [a] plaintiff[] suspect[s] facts supporting each specific legal element of a particular cause of action, we look to whether [a] plaintiff[ has] reason to at least suspect that a type of wrongdoing has injured them.” (Id., 35 Cal.4th at 807.)¿¿ 

          Here, Plaintiff claims he “did not discover and had no reason to discover the unlawful acts until May 2021 when while examining the medical documents provided by [Defendant] upon recent request, Plaintiff understood that what [Sehgal] did was not protocol because many other doctors who also examined Plaintiff, did not suggested or performed [sic] the same test as [Sehgal] did, every other doctor simply questioned Plaintiff if he had notice any blood in his stool to which Plaintiff responded no.” (FAC, ¶ 34(e).) Plaintiff filed his Complaint on September 2, 2021, within 5 months of his discovery of Sehgal’s alleged wrongdoing. (See Complaint.) While some of Plaintiff’s alleged reasons for his delayed discovery, such as “Plaintiff wanted to forget all the events that remind him of the shameful incident” and “Plaintiff was incapacitated, suffering from mental behavior issues” do not support a reasonable inference of delayed discovery, they also do not contradict Plaintiff’s claim he didn’t discover Sehgal’s wrongdoing until May 2021. (See FAC, ¶ 34(2), (b).)

The Court’s finding is supported by Plaintiff’s claims “[Sehgal] told him everything was okay” immediately after inserting his fingers intro his rectum and “Plaintiff somehow believed what defendants did was protocol.” (FAC, ¶¶ 19, 34(c).) It is reasonable for Plaintiff’s suspicion of Sehgal’s wrongdoing to be delayed if he believed Sehgal’s actions were “protocol.” In the absence of any additional facts which would support an earlier suspicion of wrongdoing and viewing Plaintiff’s FAC in the light most favorable to Plaintiff, the Court finds Plaintiff’s claims are not barred by Code of Civil Procedure 340.16(a)(2). (See Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238 (“This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”).)

Accordingly, the Court finds Plaintiff’s claims are not barred by either Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5 or § 340.16 and declines to sustain Defendant’s Demurrer on these grounds.

E.             Plaintiff’s Properly Alleges Defendant’s Vicarious Liability for Rape, Sexual Battery, Assault, Battery, and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

Defendant argues it can only be held liable for Sehgal’s alleged intentionally tortious actions (Plaintiff’s first cause of action for rape, his second for sexual battery, his third for assault, his fourth for battery, and his fifth for intentional infliction of emotional distress) if Defendant is vicariously liable for Sehgal’s actions. (See Demurrer, pp. 8-10.) Defendant argues Sehgal was not an employee of Defendant’s but, even if he was, his alleged sexual assault of Plaintiff would not be covered in the course and scope of his employment, particularly because Plaintiff did not present for issues involving his rectum. (See id. at 8.)

The Court finds Sehgal’s status as an agent of Defendant who was acting within the course and scope of his duties is a question of fact and thus inappropriate for demurrer. “[A] principal who personally engages in no misconduct may be vicariously liable for the tortious act committed by an agent within the course and scope of the agency. Agency is the relationship which results from the manifestation of consent by one person to another that the other shall act on his behalf and subject to his control, and consent by the other so to act...” (Barenborg v. Sigma Alpha Epsilon Fraternity (2019) 33 Cal.App.5th 70, 85 (internal citations and quotations omitted).) Although employers and principals are subject to vicarious liability under the doctrine of respondeat superior for the torts of their employees and agents, the general rule is that a person who employs an independent contractor is not vicariously liable for the torts of the contractor. (See Anderson v. Chancellor W. Oil Dev. Corp. (1975) 53 Cal.App.3d 235, 239.)

Whether an individual is an employee or an independent contractor is a question of fact if it is dependent on resolution of disputed evidence or inferences. (See Fisher v. San Pedro Peninsula Hosp. (1989) 214 Cal.App.3d 590, 608–609 n.6.) It becomes one of law, however, if only one inference can be drawn from the evidence. (See Toyota Motor Sales U.S.A., Inc. v. Superior Court (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) Whether the alleged agent’s conduct was in the course and scope of his agency is also normally a question of fact, unless the operable, overt, observable facts are indisputable. (See Perez v. Van Groningen & Sons, Inc. (1986) 41 Cal.3d 962, 968.)

Here, it is clear resolution of whether Sehgal was an independent contractor or employee or whether Sehgal was acting in the course and scope of his alleged agency is a question of fact. On the face of Plaintiff’s FAC, Sehgal is an employee, a fact Defendant disputes in its Demurrer. Plaintiff also alleges Sehgal was acting “outside of the scope of treatment and without consent with no medical purpose” but that he also “was acting within the scope of his duties and/or acting for the benefit of the Kaiser at the time when he committed the negligent or wrongful act giving rise to the torts in this claim.” (See, e.g., FAC, ¶¶ 21 & 32.) It would therefore be necessary for the Court to go beyond the face of the FAC and establish uncontroverted facts by declaration or other extrinsic evidence in order settle the question of whether Sehgal was Defendant’s agent or acting within the scope of his alleged agency, an inappropriate inquiry on demurrer.

The Court will not sustain Defendant’s Demurrer on these grounds and Defendant does not provide the Court with any additional argument as to why Plaintiff’s first through sixth causes of action fail to state sufficient facts to support a cause of action.

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES Defendant’s Demurrer in its entirety.  

IV.          MOTION TO STRIKE

A.       Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (See id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (See id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (See id. at § 437.)

Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(a).) “Malice” is defined as conduct intended to cause injury to a person or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (See Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. Cal., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (See id.) “Fraud” is an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known by defendant, with intent to deprive a person of property, rights or otherwise cause injury. (See id.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.) 

B.      Defendant’s Motion to Strike Is Granted

Defendant seeks to strike Plaintiff’s punitive damages allegations and “the monetary amounts and prayer for prejudgment interest included in plaintiff's prayer for relief.” (Motion, pg. 2.) Defendant argues Plaintiff’s punitive damages allegations should be stricken for three reasons: (1) Plaintiff has not complied with Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13; (2) Plaintiff has not complied with Code of Civil Procedure § 3294(b); and (3) Plaintiff cannot allege monetary amounts pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.10. (See Motion.)

The Court finds Plaintiff’s punitive damages allegations and pled monetary amounts should be stricken (See FAC, ¶¶ 37, 56, 70, 80, and 95; see also FAC, pg. 30:7-8, 12-13.) As an initial matter, Plaintiff cannot plead a monetary amount in his FAC. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 425.10(b); see also Code Civ. Proc. § 3295(e).) Plaintiff must also seek leave of Court pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13 before alleging punitive damages against Defendant, an alleged healthcare provider (See Code Civ. Proc. § 425.13; see also FAC, ¶ 2 (“…Defendant, and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive, are and at all times relevant hereto, were hospitals, medical centers, clinics and/or healthcare facilities maintained pursuant to a license.”).)

“In any action for damages arising out of the professional negligence of a health care provider, no claim for punitive damages¿shall be included in a complaint or other pleading unless the court enters an order allowing an amended pleading that includes a claim for punitive damages to be filed. The court may allow the filing of an amended pleading claiming punitive damages on a motion by the party seeking the amended pleading and on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to¿Section 3294 of the Civil Code.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 425.13(a).) 

Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13 applies if the claim is directly related to the manner in which professional services were provided. (See Central Pathology Service Medical Clinic, Inc. v. Superior Court (1992) 3 Cal.4th 181, 192-93.) Although Plaintiff’s causes of action are not based on professional negligence for the purposes of Code of Civil Procedure § 340.5, the Court finds they are “are directly related to the manner in which professional services were rendered” for purposes of Code of Civil Procedure § 425.13. (United Western Medical Centers v. Superior Court (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 500, 504; see also Larson, 230 Cal.App.4th at 350-51 (recognizing the difference in language between Code of Civil Procedure §§ 340.5 and 425.13).)

In Opposition, Plaintiff argues he “did not request punitive damages in his FAC, but he will seek permission from the court to demand such damages.” (Opposition Motion, pg. 10.) Setting aside Plaintiff’s apparent confusion over whether he did or didn’t allege punitive damages, Plaintiff appears to acknowledge the necessity of requesting leave of Court to request them. To that end, Plaintiff has already filed a motion for leave to amend his complaint to include punitive damages which is set for hearing on March 11, 2025.

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Defendant’s Motion. In light of the Court’s ruling, the Court declines to address Defendant’s other arguments in favor of striking Plaintiff’s punitive damages allegations.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES Defendant’s Demurrer and GRANTS Defendant’s Motion to Strike.
