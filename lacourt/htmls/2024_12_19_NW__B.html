[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR RECONSIDERATION OF DEFENDANT’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT AND MODIFYING, AMENDING, OR REVOKING THE ORDER THEREOF

 

Dept. B

COMPLAINT FILED: JUNE 4, 2021

TRIAL DATE: NONE

 

I.       BACKGROUND  

This is a personal injury suit. On June 4, 2021, Plaintiff Farideh Raubitschek (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants County of Los Angeles (“County”), E & S Ring Management Corp. (“E & S”), and Does 1 to 25 alleging two causes of action: (1) premises liability; and (2) negligence. Plaintiff alleges that, on or about August 11, 2020, they tripped and fell over a hole in the sidewalk at or near 4600 Via Marina, Marina Del Rey, California, 90292, suffering injury as a result. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants’ negligent ownership or control of the subject sidewalk caused the hole.

On August 21, 2023, the County filed a motion for summary judgment (“MSJ”) on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On June 5, 2024, the case was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On August 19, 2024, the Court granted summary judgment in the County’s favor.

On September 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for reconsideration on the County’s MSJ.

On December 3, 2024, the County filed an opposition.

Plaintiff has not filed a reply. However, on December 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of appeal on the order granting summary judgment.

 

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

C.C.P. §1008(a) provides, as follows:  “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Plaintiff moves for reconsideration of the County’s motion for summary judgment based on the discovery of new, essential facts and circumstances which provide justification for the Court to reconsider, modify, amend, or revoke its prior ruling. (Motion p. 2:1-7.)

 

Jurisdiction

As a preliminary matter, the Court addresses whether it retains jurisdiction to rule upon the instant motion after Plaintiff filed a notice of appeal on December 6, 2024.

“Generally, the filing of a notice of appeal ‘divests the trial court of further jurisdiction in the cause.’” (Avenida San Juan Partnership v. City of San Clemente (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1256, 1267.) “[T]he perfecting of an appeal stays proceedings in the trial court upon the judgment or order appealed from or upon the matters embraced therein or affected thereby, including enforcement of the judgment or order, but the trial court may proceed upon any other matter embraced in the action and not affected by the judgment or order.” (CCP § 916(a).) An appeal is perfected when the notice of appeal is filed. (Kroger Co. v. Workers’ Comp. Appeals Bd. (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 952, 959.)

Here, Plaintiff filed their notice of appeal from summary judgment on December 6, 2024. The notice of appeal and instant motion both seek relief from the same ruling and, consequently, proceedings regarding relief from summary judgment are stayed in this trial court pursuant to CCP § 916. Therefore, the trial court does not retain jurisdiction to rule upon the instant motion.

IV.    CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is off calendar.

Re:                         ELISABETH RICHARDSON, ET AL. VS DAVID WEAVER, ET AL. (21STCV43996)

 

 

Petitioner Elisabeth Richardson’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

 

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Petitioner Elisabeth Richardson’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a December 3, 2020, motor vehicle accident in which Plaintiffs Elisabeth Richardson (“Petitioner”), Yasmine Hunt (“Hunt”), and Isabella Yerena (“Claimant”), by and through her guardian ad litem Petitioner (collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege that Defendants David Weaver (“Weaver”), the City of La Verne (“La Verne”) and Does 1 through 50 operated their motor vehicle in a negligent or reckless manner as to collide with Plaintiff’s vehicle and cause injuries thereof. On December 2, 2021, Plaintiffs brought suit alleging two causes of action: (1) motor vehicle negligence; and (2) general negligence.

 

On June 8, 2022, Petitioner was approved as guardian ad litem for Claimant.

 

On October 13, 2023, Petitioner filed a notice of settlement.

 

On June 7, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

 

On November 15, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability on behalf of Claimant.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $4,500.00 from the City of La Verne.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Cal. Prob. Code, §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and until it is granted, there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.) 

 

Prob. Code § 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to § 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows: 

 

“The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses (medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator), costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or incompetent person.”

 

(Prob. Code § 3601(a).) 

 

In cases pursuant to Prob. Code § 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 7.955(a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (CRC Rule 7.955(a)(2).)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $4,500.00. Of this amount, $128.61 will be used for medical expenses, $1,125.00 for attorney’s fees, and $663.70 for costs. Therefore, Claimant would receive a net settlement amount of $2,582.69. (See MC-350 Item 16.)

 

The Petition sets forth information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise pursuant to CRC Rule 7.950. (MC-350 Attachment 8.) Additionally, attorney’s fees are reasonable as they represent 25% of the total settlement amount. (MC-350 Attachment 13a.) Claimant’s counsel provides their retainer agreement as Attachment 11b(3). Costs are reasonable considering the extent of litigation. Petitioner provides the name and address of the parent and money to be delivered as Attachment 18b(5).

 

However, Petitioner fails to provide reasoning for the settlement apportionment. Petitioner and Hunt are to receive $18,000.00 and $70,000.00, respectively. (See MC-350 Item 11b(5).) Petitioner does provide a copy of the settlement agreement as Attachment 11b(6), but this agreement does not provide reasoning for the settlement apportionment. Accordingly, the Petition is denied.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES the petition to confirm minor’s compromise.

 

This is Petitioner’s second petition denied for deficiencies. Petitioner is put on notice that further deficient filings risk sanctions pursuant to CCP § 128.7 and CRC Rule 2.30.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

C.C.P. §1008(a) provides, as follows:  “When an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order and based upon new or different facts, circumstances, or law, make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order. The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (CCP § 1008(a).)

 

III.       DISCUSSION

Plaintiff moves for reconsideration of the Court’s dismissal order of Brown based on new facts and law. (Motion p. 2:2-4.)

In fact, Plaintiff acknowledges that they made these same arguments at the Order to Show Cause hearing which resulted in Brown’s dismissal. (See Rafii Declaration.) Therefore, Plaintiff fails to meet the legal standard required by CCP § 1008.

 

IV.    CONCLUSION

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

I.       BACKGROUND

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff Noah Cvengros filed this action against Defendant Sam Tucker (“Defendant”) and Does 1 through 10 alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence.

On June 6, 2024, the action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On June 12, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions.

On November 5, 2024, the instant motion was called for hearing. Defense counsel was ordered to file a declaration five days prior to the instant hearing regarding meet and confer status and status of discovery responses. (See November 5, 2024, Minute Order). The Court notes that counsel failed to do so.

The instant motion remains unopposed.

 

II.    LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 permits the Court, in its discretion, to impose the following types of terminating sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process”:

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.

(CCP § 2023.030(d).)

Conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process” includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).) “Imposition of sanctions for misuse of discovery lies within the trial court's discretion, and is reviewed only for abuse.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 991.) “The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery[.]” (Ibid. at 992.) Sanctions should be tailored to the harm created by the withheld discovery and should not be used as punishment. (Ibid.) The court should also consider whether the discovery misconduct was “willful” and whether the requesting party was prejudiced by the other’s failure to comply. (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 37.)

Lastly, courts have held that terminating sanctions are warranted when a party repeatedly fails to propound adequate discovery responses, even after the court imposed monetary sanctions. (Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrations, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093.) For example, in Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, the Court of Appeal held that five motions to compel created a record “replete with evidence of [appellant’s] failures to answer discovery requests.” (Ibid. at 279–280.)

 

III.   DISCUSSION

Defendant makes this motion on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to comply with the court’s order to provide verified responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Demand for Production of Documents by May 27, 2024. (See May 7, 2024 Minute Order.) Plaintiff also failed to pay Court-ordered sanctions in the amount of $430.00. (Korff Decl. ¶ 11.) However, Plaintiff did provide responses to Defendant’s form interrogatories, set one, special interrogatories, set one, and requests for admissions, set one. (Ibid. at ¶ 6.) Defendant filed three corresponding motions to compel further responses in relation to discovery motions. (Ibid.) They are also set to be heard on December 19, 2024.

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) Here, Plaintiff did provide responses to Defendant’s other discovery motions. While failure to comply with the Court’s May 7, 2024, order constitutes a misuse of discovery, Defendant fails to provide evidence that less severe sanctions would not produce Plaintiff’s compliance. Defense counsel also failed to file a declaration regarding efforts to meet and confer as ordered by the Court. Therefore, the Court does not find terminating sanctions appropriate at this time.

However, considering Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the Court’s May 7, 2024, order, the Court awards Defendant’s requested sanctions in the amount of $860.00. This amount includes the $430.00 in sanctions awarded in May which Plaintiff has failed to pay.

 

IV. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is DENIED but the request for additional sanctions is granted. Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel, jointly and severally, are ordered to pay defense counsel sanctions in the amount of $860.00 within 15 days of this hearing.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

I.      
BACKGROUND  

On May 2, 2022, Plaintiff Noah Cvengros (“Plaintiff”)
filed this action against Defendant Sam Tucker (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 10
alleging one cause of action for motor vehicle negligence.

On March 4, 2024, Defendant filed three
motions to compel further responses against Plaintiff, as to the following
discovery documents: (1) Form Interrogatories, Set One; (2) Requests for
Admissions, Set One; and (3) Special Interrogatories, Set One (“Discovery
Motions”). Plaintiff also requests sanctions in the total amount of $4,065
($1,355 per motion).

On June 6, 2024, the action was reassigned to
the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East,
effective as of June 24, 2024.

The motions remain unopposed.

 

II.         
LEGAL
STANDARD

Interrogatories

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 provides, in pertinent part, that
“[o]n receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move
for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that
any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is
evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under
Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those
documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit
or too general.” (CCP § 2030.300(a).) 

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45
days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified
response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding
party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party
waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2030.300(c).) 

 

Request for
Admissions

CCP § 2033.290 provides, in pertinent part, that “[o]n receipt of a
response to requests for admissions, the party requesting admissions may move
for an order compelling a further response if that party deems that either or
both of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular request is evasive
or incomplete. (2) An objection to a particular request is without merit or too
general.” (CCP § 2033.290(a).)

Unless notice of a motion to compel further responses is given within 45
days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified
response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding
party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party
waives any right to compel a further response. (CCP § 2033.290(c).)

Under California law, the objecting party has the burden of justifying
its objections when the propounding party requests that the Court order further
responses. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.)
Further, under CCP § 2023.010, it is a misuse of discovery to make unmerited
objections. (CCP § 2023.010(e).) 

 

III.      
DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer
Requirement

A motion to compel further responses shall be accompanied by a meet and
confer declaration. (CCP §§ 2030.300(b) and 2033.290(b).) Here, defense counsel
provides a declaration demonstrating diligent efforts to meet and confer with
opposing counsel. (Silva Van Vo Decl. ¶ 13; Exh. E [meet and confer letter].)
On February 9, 2024, defense counsel sent a Meet and Confer Letter to opposing
counsel, outlining the deficiencies in Plaintiff’s responses and offering
fourteen additional days for further response. (Ibid.) Defense counsel
did not receive a response nor supplemental discovery. Therefore, the Court
finds Defendant’s meet and confer efforts to be sufficient pursuant to CCP §
2030.300(b).)

 

Form Interrogatories,
Set One

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Form
Interrogatories, Set One Nos. 2.5-2.6, 6.1-6.7, 8.5, 17.1, 20.1, and
20.3-20.10.

The motion is granted. Plaintiff generally fails to provide any response
to these interrogatories, with or without objection. Each interrogatory is
related to discoverable matter. Therefore, Plaintiff is ordered to provide
verified, objection-free, supplemental responses to Form Interrogatories, Set
One, Nos. 2.5-2.6, 6.1-6.7, 8.5, 17.1, 20.1, and 20.3-20.10.

 

Requests for
Admissions, Set One

Defendant moves to compel the Plaintiff's further responses to Requests
for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 4-9 and 12-13.

The motion is granted. Plaintiff’s responses are generally riddled with
typing errors and what appear to be incoherent drafting notes. Plaintiff is
ordered to provide verified, clear, objection-free, supplemental
responses to Requests for Admissions, Set One, Nos. 4-9 and 12-13.

 

Special
Interrogatories, Set One

Defendant moves to compel the Plaintiff’s further responses to Special Interrogatories,
Set One, Nos. 2-9 and 11-14.

The motion is granted.. Plaintiff’s responses to Nos. 2-4 are generally
riddled with typing errors and what appear to be incoherent drafting notes.
Plaintiff’s responses to Nos. 5, 9, and 11, are incomplete. Plaintiff fails to
provide any responses to Nos. 6-8 and 12-14.

Plaintiff is ordered to provide verified, clear, objection-free,
supplemental responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, Nos. 2-9 and 11-14.

 

 

Monetary Sanctions

“The court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in
the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct”
under CCP § 2023.030(a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable
monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare
Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

Here, Plaintiff failed to completely and coherently respond to discovery,
constituting a misuse of the discovery process. Therefore, monetary sanctions
are appropriate.

Defendant requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $1,355 per
motion, representing a $60 filing fee, four hours preparing each motion and an
anticipated three hours for the hearing of each motion at an hourly rate of
$185. (Silva Van Vo Decl. ¶ 15.) The Court reduces monetary sanctions to $615
per motion, representing three hours of work per motion at an hourly rate of
$185 and a $60 filing fee, as each motion is largely identical, and no
oppositions were filed. Therefore, the Court awards monetary sanctions against
Plaintiff in the total amount of $1,845.
