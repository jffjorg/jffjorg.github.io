DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV29644    Hearing Date: January 21, 2025    Dept: 73

January 21, 2025

Dept. 73

Rolf M. Treu, Judge presiding

 

The Machine Group, Inc. v. NC Dynamics, LLC (21STCV29644)

 

Counsel for Cross-Defendant/moving party Nidec OKK USA Corporation (f/k/a OKK USA Corporation): Masuda, Funai, Eifert & Mitchell, LTD

Counsel for Cross-Complainant NC Dynamics, LLC: Saul Ewing, LLP

 

[Demurrer w/ motion to strike] (filed January 3, 2025)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Cross-Defendant Nidec OKK USA Corporation’s demurrer and motion to strike are DENIED AS MOOT.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 11, 2021, Plaintiff The Machine Group (“TMG”) filed this action against Defendant NC Dynamics (“NC”).

 

TMG’s Complaint alleges causes of action for breach of contract and recission. On June 3, 2024, NC Dynamics filed a Second Amended Cross Complaint (“SACC”) against TMG and Nidec OKK USA Corporation, f/k/a OKK USA Corporation (“OKK”). NC Dynamics alleges the following causes of action in its SACC: (1) breach of contract; (2) fraud in the inducement; (3) negligence; (4) intentional interference with contractual relations; (5) intentional interference with prospective economic relations; (6) negligent interference with prospective economic relations; and (7) breach of warranty of fitness for particular purpose.

 

This case concerns a contract dispute for the sale and underperformance of specialized machinery. NC alleges the following in its SACC. NC, the Defendant/Cross-Complainant, is a full-service manufacturing facility that manufactures products for aerospace companies. According to NC, NC took steps to upgrade the manufacturing process regarding an existing contract it had with Bombardier Aerostructures (“Bombardier”). As part of that process, NC requested bids from three companies to sell manufacturing equipment to NC. NC also requested the companies to conduct time studies to establish the running time for the construction of specific titanium parts for the Bombardier contract.

 

TMG, the Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant in this case, was one of the three companies to submit a bid, but the only company that offered to sell an upcoming machine from another company called OKK conducted the time study. NC contend that TMG ultimately won the bid based on a guarantee made by TMG President Richard Wackeen (“Wackeen”) that the parts would be manufactured in ten hours with the OKK machine and also upon the misrepresentation that OKK was aware that Wackeen was guaranteeing a ten hour running time. NC and TMG entered into two contracts for the purchase of two OKK machines. The machines failed to construct the parts in the contracted ten-hour running time, and multiple attempts were made to reduce the running time.

 

Because of the slow running time, NC contend that they were forced to purchase a third machine from TMG to meet its production needs for the Bombardier contract. NC further allege that TMG continued to guarantee that the OKK machines would achieve the ten-hour running time despite knowing the OKK machine could do so. NC later learned that six weeks before the parties entered into the third contract, a TMG manager named Jim McCulloch (“McCulloch”) admitted in an email that the OKK machines would never achieve a running time. McCulloch was present to witness the final run of the OKK machines, which at the lowest point exceeded 24 hours. As a result of the running time, NC contend that they lost Bombardier, and others, as a current and future customer.

 

In TMG’s Complaint, TMG alleges that NC has an unpaid balance in the amount of $1,895,150.13 on the Second Machine and Third Machine and has refused to make any further payments on these machines despite TMG’s demands.

 

On January 3, 2025, OKK filed the instant demurrer and motion to strike as to NC’s SACC.

 

No opposition has been filed, but NC has filed a Third Amended Cross-Complaint (3ACC) on January 9, 2025.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

1.      Demurrer

 

A demurrer is an objection to a pleading, the grounds for which are apparent from either the face of the complaint or a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a); see also Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) The purpose of a demurrer is to challenge the sufficiency of a pleading “by raising questions of law.” (Postley v. Harvey (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 280, 286.) “In the construction of a pleading, for the purpose of determining its effect, its allegations must be liberally construed, with a view to substantial justice between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.) The court “‘treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law . . ..’” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525.) 

 

When a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.)

 

2.      Motion to Strike

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike a pleading or any part thereof.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)¿ The court¿may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a).)¿ The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).)¿ An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., 431.10, subd. (b).)¿ The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)¿¿¿ 

 

B.      Discussion

 

OKK’s instant demurrer and motion to strike was filed in response to NC’s SACC. However, NC has now filed a 3ACC in response.

 

“A party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 472(a).)

 

Thus, the instant demurrer and motion to strike are denied as moot.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendant Nidec OKK USA Corporation’s demurrer and motion to strike are DENIED AS MOOT.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

KATHERINE AVERILLA v. ASN LONG BEACH NEPHRO CARE, et al. (23STCV20253)

 

Counsel for Moving Party/Defendant ASN Long Beach                Roxanne Bonar, David Bonar

Nephro Care LLC:                                                                                           (Bonar Law Group)

 

Counsel for Opposing Party/Plaintiff Katherine Averilla:                          Orlando F. Cabanday

                                                                                                                           (Cabanday Law Group)

 

MOTION TO STRIKE DOE 1 AND 2 AS DEFENDANTS

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Motion to Strike Doe 1 and 2 as Defendants is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 23, 2023, Plaintiff Katherine Averilla filed the instant action against Defendant ASN Long Beach Nephro Care LLC (“ASN”), and Does 1 to 50, inclusive, asserting the following causes of action:

 

1.      Retaliation in Violation of Labor Code §1102.5;

2.      Wrongful Termination In Violation Of Public Policy;

3.      Violation Of Labor Code Section 1198.5;

4.      Unpaid Wages (Labor Code § 202);

5.      Failure To Pay Overtime (Labor Code §§ 510, 1194);

6.      Failure To Provide Rest Periods (Labor Code §226.7);

7.      Failure To Provide Off-Duty Meal Period (Labor Code §§ 226.7, 512);

8.      Failure To Furnish Accurate Wage Statements (Labor Code §§ 226, 226.3, 1174, 1174.5); and

9.      Waiting Time Penalties (Labor Code §§ 226, 226.3)

 

The Complaint alleges the following. In 2022, Plaintiff was hired by Defendant as an acute dialysis coordinator. Plaintiff began asking for proper payment of wages, mandatory breaks, and time off. However, instead of doing those things and remedying violations of company policy as reported by Plaintiff, Defendant fired Plaintiff. Defendants discriminated and retaliated against Plaintiff solely because of her race, age, and attempt to obtain her proper pay.

 

On September 25, 2023, ASN filed its Answer.  

 

On December 6, 2023, Plaintiff amended the Complaint to substitute “Ngoc-Tram Gia Tran” and “Sapna Patel, MD” for the defendants sued fictitiously as Does 1 and 2, respectively (collectively, the “Doe Defendants”).

 

On December 31, 2024, ASN filed the instant motion to strike the Doe Defendants, arguing that the amendment substituting the defendants was improper for the following reasons:

 

·       Code of Civil Procedure section 474 required Plaintiff to substitute the Doe Defendants upon learning about their identities.

·       Plaintiff knew about the Doe Defendants as early as September 2023.

·       Yet Plaintiff waited until December 6, 2023, to substitute them.

·       In addition, at the January 11, 2024 Case Management Conference (“CMC”), the Court instructed Plaintiff to amend the Complaint to name the Doe Defendants and personally serve them.

·       However, Plaintiff did not serve the Doe Defendants until December 19, 2024, over a year after she knew their identities. Moreover, Plaintiff did not serve

·       Plaintiff’s delay not only violated Code of Civil Procedure section 474, but also prejudiced the Doe Defendants because the defendants did not have adequate notice to prepare their defense in time for the trial scheduled on January 21, 2025.

·       Plaintiff’s delay was not due to ignorance but rather a calculated strategy to prejudice the Doe Defendants’ ability to defend themselves.

·       Therefore, the Court should strike the untimely amendment.

 

On January 13, 2025, Plaintiff filed her opposition to the motion, arguing the following.

 

·       None of the Doe Defendants have joined or yet appeared in this action or motion, yet ASN asks the Court to strike the Doe Defendants.

·       ASN’s arguments are unpersuasive.

·       First, Code of Civil Procedure section 474 only required Plaintiff to have ignorance of the facts giving rise to the claim; the statute did not require Plaintiff to have only the knowledge of the defendants’ identities to obtain leave to amend.

o   Here, Plaintiff did not become aware that the Doe Defendants were liable under Labor Code section 558 until ASN’s counsel confirmed their liability.

o   Once Plaintiff obtained the facts forming the basis of liability, Plaintiff timely sought the doe amendment.

·       Second, at the CMC on January 11, 2024, the Court did not issue an order requiring Plaintiff to serve the Doe Defendants by a certain date.

·       Third, Plaintiff is not prohibited from serving the Doe Defendants now and ASN has not offered any authority precluding Plaintiff from seeking leave to amend at any time, even during trial. Moreover, the Doe Defendants have been served.

·       Therefore, the Court should deny the motion.

 

No reply has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for [Motion]

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: ¶ (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. ¶ (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subds. (a)-(b).)

 

B.      Meet and Confer

 

“Before filing a motion to strike …, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purpose of determining if an agreement can be reached that resolves the objections to be raised in the motion to strike.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5, subd. (a).)

 

The Court finds the meet and confer requirement satisfied. (See Declaration of Roxanne C. Bonar, ¶¶ 7-8 [testifying that the parties met and conferred].)

 

C.      Request to Strike Doe Defendants

 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 474, the fictitious name statute, states in part: ‘When the plaintiff is ignorant of the name of a defendant, he must state that fact in the complaint ... and such defendant may be designated in any pleading or proceeding by name, and when his true name is discovered, the pleading or proceeding must be amended accordingly ....’” (Fuller v. Tucker (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 1163, 1170 (“Fuller”).)

 

“The phrase ‘ignorant of the name of a defendant’ is broadly interpreted to mean not only ignorant of the defendant’s identity, but also ignorant of the facts giving rise to a cause of action against that defendant.” (Fuller, supra, 84 Cal.App.4th at p. 1170.) “‘[S]ection 474 does not impose upon the plaintiff a duty to go in search of facts she does not actually have at the time she files her original pleading. [Citation.]’ [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 1172.)

 

Here, Plaintiff argues that she did not substitute the Doe Defendants until December 6, 2023, because it was not until that time that she became aware of the facts giving rise to her claims against the Doe Defendants. (See Opposition, p. 2:15-21 [arguing that Plaintiff is asserting wage and hour claims against the defendants. In or about December 2023, ASN’s counsel stated that the company was wholly owned by the Doe Defendants. Since Labor Code section 558.1 holds owners liable for wage and hour violations, Plaintiff substituted in the Doe Defendants].)

 

“In order for [a party] to successfully argue that [a] Doe amendment was untimely, [that party] ha[s] to prove that even if [plaintiff] knew [the doe’s] identity, [the plaintiff] also knew facts giving rise to a cause of action against [the doe defendant].” (Fuller, supra, 84 Cal.App.4th at p. 1173.)

 

Here, ASN has not filed a reply disputing Plaintiff’s claim that she learned of relevant facts in or about December 2023. In addition, the Court has reviewed its Minute Order from the CMC on January 11, 2024, and notes that the Court did not give Plaintiff a deadline to serve the Doe Defendants.

 

Therefore, there is no evidence that Plaintiff unduly delayed in making the doe amendment.

 

An “amendment relates back to the filing of the original complaint unless the newly named defendant can show actual prejudice resulting from unreasonable delay in filing the amendment.” (Hazel v. Hewlett (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 1458, 1466.)

 

Here, the motion argues that the Doe Defendants have been prejudiced because they were served too close to the January 21, 2025 trial date. However, at the Final Status Conference on January 6, 2025, the Court vacated the January 21, 2025, date and set a Trial Setting Conference to take place on that date instead.

 

Therefore, the Court finds that the amendment has not prejudiced the Doe Defendants.

 

For those reasons, the motion is denied.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Motion to Strike Doe 1 and 2 as Defendants is DENIED.

BMBG Investments, LLC v. California Credit Union (23STCV17495)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff BMBG Investments, LLC/moving party: Benjamin Kiss of Fischer, Zisblatt & Kiss, LLP

Counsel for Defendant California Credit Union/opposing party: None

 

[motion TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL] (filed December 19, 2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Attorney Benjamin Kiss’s motion to be relieved as counsel is DENIED

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

This case arises from an alleged breach of contract between Plaintiff BMBG Investments, LLC (Plaintiff) and Defendant California Credit Union (Defendant). Plaintiff alleges that it agreed to lease property to Defendant in a shopping center, with Defendant agreeing to pay rent and abiding by all laws related to the tenancy. Plaintiff claims that Defendant agreed to obtain proper permits for any and all construction and/or remodeling work done on the property, and to return the premises at the end of the lease in the same condition in which Defendant received it.

 

On July 25, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant, alleging one cause of action for Breach of Contract.

 

On December 19, 2024, counsel for Plaintiff, Benjam Kiss (Mr. Kiss), filed the instant motion to be relieved as counsel.

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard

 

An attorney in an action may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other. (Code Civ. Proc., § 284(2)). Rules of Court, rule 3.1362 requires:

1. Notice of motion and motion to be directed to the client (made on the Notice of Motion and Motion to be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-051)); 

 

2. A declaration stating in general terms, and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship, why a motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2), is brought instead of filing a consent under CCP §284(1) (made on the Declaration in Support of Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-052)); 

 

3. Service of the notice of motion and motion and declaration on all other parties who have appeared in the case; and 

 

4. The proposed order relieving counsel (prepared on the Order Granting Attorney's Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel Civil form (MC-053)). 

 

The motion should be denied if withdrawal would prejudice the client.  (Rules Prof. Conduct, Rule 3-700(A)(2); Weil & Brown, Cal. Prac. Guide: Civ. Pro. Before Trial, 9:385.2 (2008); 1 Witkin Cal. Pro., Attorneys §72 (2008).) The motion should be denied if it will cause undue delay in the proceeding or cause injustice.  (Mandell v. Superior Court, (1977) 67 Cal.App.3d 1, 4.) The determination whether to grant or deny an attorney’s motion to withdraw as counsel of record lies within the sound discretion of the trial court, having in mind whether such withdrawal might work an injustice in the handling of the case. (Lempert v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1161, 1173.)  A breakdown in the attorney-client relationship (or personality clash) is grounds for withdrawal.  (Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014.) An attorney is not limited to withdrawing from a case for cause and may withdraw when withdrawal can be accomplished without undue prejudice to the client. (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 26 Cal.Rptr.2d 554, 559.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Here, Mr. Kiss has provided the Court with all the required documents. Mr. Kiss has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Counsel seeks to be relieved as counsel on the grounds that the trust and confidence essential to the attorney-client relationship has ceased to exist and irreconcilable difference have arisen between client and attorney making it unreasonably difficult to carry out the employment effectively. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).)¿ 

 

However, the final status conference and non-jury trial of this case is set for March 10, 2025 and March 26, 2025 respectively. As of the date of the hearing on the instant motion, these dates are only about two months away. Due to the potentially high risk of prejudice to Plaintiff, the Court cannot grant the instant motion. If the attorney-client relationship has deteriorated as Mr. Kiss claims, Plaintiff, as a client, must find new counsel and formally file a Substitution of Attorney form (MC-050), in order for current counsel to withdraw.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Attorney Benjamin Kiss’s motion to be Relieved as Counsel is DENIED.

 

Moving party is to give notice.

JENNIFER HENANDEZ V. STANLEY FINEMAN, ET AL. (24STCV04655)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party: Rehwald Peterson, Inc.

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party: Absolute Law Group, PC

 

Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint (filed 12/26/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs’ Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 26, 2024, plaintiff Jennifer Hernandez (Plaintiff) filed her initial complaint against Stanley Fineman (Stanley), Allan Fineman (Allan),[1] (collectively, Defendants) and Does 1 through 25. (Compl. p. 1.) She claimed the following: (1) Failure to Pay Minimum Wages in Violation of Labor Code §§ 1194 and 1197, (2) Failure to Pay Overtime Wages in Violation of the Domestic Worker Bill of Rights (Labor Code §§ 1454 and 1194), (3) Violation of Labor Code §§ 202 and 203, and (4) Violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200. (Compl. p. 1.)

 

These causes of action arise from events that occurred after Defendants hired Plaintiff in March 2017 to provide daytime caregiving services to Stanley. (Compl. ¶ 6.) Around June 2018, Defendants hired Plaintiff to work as a live-in caregiver for 24 hours, 7 days a week. (Compl. ¶ 6.) That year, Allan and his two teenagers moved into the home. (Compl. ¶ 8.) Allan made her clean up after them, forcing her to take on additional responsibilities. (Compl. ¶ 9.) Plaintiff claims Allan controlled her breaks and ability to leave the house, because he had to be there to relieve her of her duties. (Compl. ¶ 10.) In addition, Plaintiff claims she was given “$900 every 15 days,” or “$2.50 per hour for the 360 hours [she] worked during the 15-day period.” (Compl. ¶ 11.)

 

During discovery and in Allan’s response to Form Interrogatory-Employment No. 200.4, Allan claimed he was in a romantic relationship with Plaintiff during her employment. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 3.) Allan also produced recordings and texts showing evidence of abuse. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 4.) Plaintiff’s counsel, Attorney Kelly M. Haunt (Haunt), states she was not aware of the abusive behavior before filing the Complaint. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 5.) Defendants sent over their discovery responses on July 1, 2024, August 6, 2024, and August 9, 2024. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 2.)

 

Haunt and another attorney asked Plaintiff about the relationship and possible abuse. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 4.) After several months and coming to terms with the abuse, Plaintiff opened up, explaining how the relationship turned sexual in the later part of 2017 and later “toxic.” (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 5-6.) From the start of her employment, Allan would walk around the house, her place of employment, naked. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 7.) Allan would later pressure Plaintiff into sexual acts while working and would not stop until she gave in to his requests. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 5-6.) If Plaintiff refused, Allan “would either start an argument, ignore her or would make sure that her paycheck was [delayed].” (Haunt Decl. ¶ 8.) Allan would also call Plaintiff degrading names. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 9.) Haunt intends to include these facts and related causes of actions in the First Amended Complaint. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.)

 

Defendants’ discovery responses also notified Haunt that Stanley passed away on September 22, 2024. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 12.) Haunt intends to amend the First Amended Complaint to include his passing. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 12.)

 

In addition, after filing the Complaint, Haunt discovered Los Angeles Municipal Code sections 187.02 and 188.07 are applicable to the wage theft facts. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 13.) Haunt intends to bring these as an additional claim. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 13.)

 

On November 22, 2024, Haunt contacted Defendants’ counsel, Attorney John Bussman (Bussman), about filing the First Amended Complaint. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.)  Bussman said he would need to speak with Allan first. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) Haunt would follow up on November 26, December 2, December 6, and December 18. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) On December 18, 2024, Bussman said “he know[s] [Allan] will not stipulate to amend the complaint.” (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) Haunt filed this motion in response.

 

On December 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint, arguing:

·                Under the California Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), the Court should grant Plaintiff’s leave to file the First Amended Complaint. (Motion p. 2.)

·                Policy favors granting amendments. (Mesler v. Bragg Mgmt. Co. (1985) 39 Cal. 3d 290, 296.)

·                Leave to amend a complaint will be denied when there was an unreasonable delay in seeking leave, and granting leave would prejudice the defendant. (A.N. v. Cnty. of Los Angeles (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1058, 1068.)

·                Here, Plaintiff did not unreasonably delay in bringing this motion. (Motion p. 6.) Haunt communicated her intent to amend the complaint shortly after her conversation with Plaintiff. (Motion p. 6.) Plaintiff delayed in bringing this motion, because she was waiting for Defendants to stipulate to the amendment. (Motion p. 6.)

·                In addition, Defendants will not be prejudiced by the amendment, because “[t]he type of unfair surprise or additional costs that can occur when leave is sought immediately before, or during, trial just do not exist here.” (Motion p. 7.) The new causes of action and facts alleged are related to the facts found in the original Complaint. (Motion p. 7.) Everything stems from Plaintiff’s employment under the Defendants. (Motion p. 7.)

·                According to Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 531, Plaintiff can expand the scope of a complaint and add legal theories if discovery is still open. (Motion p. 6.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that:

·                Defendants believe granting leave to amend the Complaint will prejudice their rights. (Opp. p. 3.) For this reason, the motion should be denied. (Opp. p. 3.)

·                Plaintiffs proposed amended complaint “moves the goalposts” after Defendants provided a potential defense in their discovery responses. (Opp. p. 4.) Defendants claim Plaintiff should have “been aware of any alleged sexual impropriety at the time she filed her initial complaint.” (Opp. p. 4.) Plaintiff only files for leave and attempts to completely change her complaint in response to Defendant’ possible defenses. (Opp. pp. 4-5.)

·                The delay is unreasonable, because Plaintiff asks for the amendment eleven months after filing. (Opp. p. 4.) Defendants believe, according to A.N. v. City of Los Angeles, 171 Cal.App.4th 1058, “[a]llowing Plaintiff to seek leave after an unreasonable delay of nearly a year would prejudice defendant in the instant case.” (Opp. p. 4.)

In reply, Plaintiff argues:

·                Defendants’ newly asserted “defense” was not the deciding factor in the filing of this motion. (Reply p. 2.) Allan’s discovery responses and abuse prompted the necessity for asserting additional claims. (Reply p. 3.)

·                Plaintiff did not have to be “aware of any alleged sexual impropriety at the time that she filed her initial complaint, but she strategically opted to omit those allegations.” (Reply p. 3.) Plaintiff was still processing her trauma. (Reply p. 3.) In addition, there was no strategic benefit to bringing these claims now rather than in the initial Complaint. (Reply p. 4.)

·                Defendants will not be prejudiced by the amended Complaint. Plaintiff has not moved the goalpost but is exerting a claim that arose from discovery. (Reply p. 4.) Additionally, this case will not likely be ready for trial by September 2025 due to Stanley’s death. (Reply p. 4.) Defendants have not initiated probate or trust claim procedures. (Reply p. 4.)

·                Defendants do not cite any statute or case law that shows amending the complaint with nine months left before trial is prejudicial.  (Reply p. 4.) Defendants incorrectly interpret A.E v. City of Los Angeles, (2007) 171 Cal.App.4th 1154.

·                Delay alone is not a basis to deny a motion for leave to amend. (Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 564-565.)

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.      Legal Standard for Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint

 

The trial court has discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding. (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.) An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion. (Ibid.) A judge may deny a motion for leave to amend where a moving party has been dilatory in seeking leave to amend and such delay has prejudiced the opposing party. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 486, 490.) “It is difficult to understand how [a named defendant] can be prejudiced by amendment to add an additional theory of liability against it.” (Ibid.) 

 

If the party seeking the amendment has been dilatory, and the delay has prejudiced the opposing party, the Court has the discretion to deny leave to amend.  (Hirsa v. Superior Court, supra, 118 Cal.App.3d at p. 490.)  Prejudice exists where the amendment would require delaying the trial, resulting in loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation due to the reopening of discovery.  (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc. (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486-488 [“Where the trial date is set, the jury is about to be impaneled, counsel, the parties, the trial court, and the witnesses have blocked the time, and the only way to avoid prejudice to the opposing party is to continue the trial date to allow further discovery, refusal of leave to amend cannot be an abuse of discretion.”].) 

 

A motion for leave to amend must include a separate declaration specifying: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).) A motion for leave to amend must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a)(1).) Moreover, a motion to amend must state what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted or added, if any, and must set forth the page, paragraph, and line number of such allegations. (Cal. Rules of Court, rules 3.1324(a)(2)-(3).)  

 

B.      Discussion

 

1.                  Procedural Compliance

 

Plaintiff’s declaration in support of the motion satisfies the requirements under the California Rules of Court (CRC), rule 3.1324(b).  Plaintiff identifies the amendments, their effect, why they are necessary and proper, when the facts giving rise to the amendments were discovered, and the reason why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 6-14.) 

 

Plaintiff intends to add the following causes of action: “6) Violation of Los Angeles Municipal Code §§ 187.02, 188.07; 7) Sexual Harassment – Hostile Work Environment in Violation of FEHA; 8) Sexual Harassment – Quid Pro Quo in Violation of FEHA; 9) Work Environment Harassment in Violation of FEHA; 10) Discrimination on the Basis of Sex – Disparate Impact in Violation of FEHA; 11) Retaliation in Violation of FEHA; 12) Wrongful Termination (Constructive Discharge) in Violation of Public Policy; 13) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; 14) Battery; 15) Assault.” (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) Plaintiff also intends to include the facts she learned after receiving Defendants’ discovery responses as discussed above. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) The amendments’ effects are to add ten new causes of action and allege additional facts. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) 

 

Plaintiff’s amendments are necessary and proper, because Steven’s death, Plaintiff coming to terms with the abuse, and Haunt finding an additional cause of action all occurred after Plaintiff filed her Complaint and/or received Defendants’ discovery responses. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 5, 13, 14.) Through Defendants’ discovery responses, Haunt learned Steven passed away on September 22, 2024. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 5.) Steven’s death came after Plaintiff filed her Complaint on February 26, 2024. Regarding the abuse, “Plaintiff was coming to terms with the fact that [Allan’s] treatment of her was abnormal, abusive and illegal, and it took many conversations for her to open up and tell us her story.” (Haunt Decl. ¶ 5.) Allan’s discovery responses prompted Plaintiff to believe Allan had abused her. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 3, 5.) Concerning the additional wage violation cause of action, Haunt did not discover the local municipal code until after Plaintiff had filed her Complaint. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 13.) For these reasons, Plaintiff must now file to amend the complaint.

 

As discussed above, Haunt did not learn of the facts giving rise to the amendments until after she had filed the Complaint and/or received Defendants’ discovery responses on July 1, 2024, August 6, 2024, and August 9, 2024. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 2-5, 13, 14.)

 

Plaintiff did not request the amendments earlier, because Haunt and Bussman were discussing a stipulation to amend the Complaint. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.)  Bussman said he would need to speak with Allan first. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) Haunt followed up on November 26, December 2, December 6, and December 18. (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) On December 18, 2024, Bussman said that “he know[s] [Allan] will not stipulate to amend the complaint.” (Haunt Decl. ¶ 14.) At that point, Haunt moved to amend the complaint.

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff satisfies the procedural requirement under the California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).

 

Plaintiff also meets the procedural requirements under the California Rules of Court, rules 3.1324(a)(1)-(3). Plaintiff’s motion for leave provides two copies of the proposed First Amended Complaint. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 15, 16, Exhs. 1-2.) The first copy in Exhibit 1 is the “red-lined” version, which shows what allegations in the Complaint are to be deleted or added. (Haunt Decl. ¶¶ 15, 16, Exhs. 1-2.) Plaintiff also provides an Appendix of Proposed Changes, setting forth the page, paragraph, and line number of the amendments. (Motion pp. 9-10.) Thus, Plaintiff satisfies the procedural requirements under the California Rules of Court, rules 3.1324(a)(1)-(3).

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff satisfies all procedural requirements.

 

2.                  Prejudice

 

Defendants contend that granting leave to amend the Complaint will prejudice their rights. (Opp. p. 3.) Defendants allege that Plaintiff is moving goalposts by adding additional causes of action eleven months after filing the Complaint. (Opp. p. 3-4.) Defendants do not explain how amending the Complaint will cause them prejudice.

 

Allowing Plaintiff to file a First Amended Complaint at this stage would not result in undue prejudice to Defendants. A court can find prejudice when granting leave to amend would delay the trial, result in the loss of critical evidence, or increase the costs of preparation due to the reopening of discovery. (Magpali v. Farmers Group, Inc., supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at pp. 486-488.) None of those situations apply here. Trial is set for September 22, 2023. (Motion p. 3.) Defendants have over seven months to conduct discovery so there is no need to delay trial, risk critical evidence, or reopen discovery if the motion is granted. In addition, Plaintiff has discussed why she waited to bring the motion until this time and why she wishes to expand the scope of discovery. It is not to move the goalpost. Thus, granting leave to amend the Complaint will not cause prejudice.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File First Amended Complaint.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff leave to file the First Amended Complaint submitted in connection with the Motion, and the First Amended Complaint is deemed filed and served as of January 21, 2025.
