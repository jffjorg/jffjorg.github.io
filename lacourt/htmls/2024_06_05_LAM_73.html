DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCP02966    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: 73

06/05/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

SEAVEY, et al. v. SIGNATURE ENTERTAINMENT, LLC. et al. (23STCP02966)

 

Counsel for Appellants/Petitioners/opposing party:  Alan Gutman (Gutman Law), Howard King (King, Holmes, Paterno & Soriano, LLP)

Counsel for Appellees/Respondents/moving party:  David Stern, Michael Levin (Baker & Hostetler LLP)

 

MOTION TO DISMISS OR STAY (filed on 04/08/24)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Respondent’s Motion to Dismiss, or in the alternative, to Stay is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

This is an action to appeal a Labor Commissioner Award brought by Petitioners Daniel Seavey, Jonah Marais, Corbyn Besson, Jack Avery and Zach Herron (collectively “Petitioners”) against Respondents Signature Entertainment LLC (“Signature”), David Loeffler and Steve Miller.

According to the operative petition, filed August 15, 2023, Petitioners are songwriters, musicians, and recording and performing artists collectively known as the musical band “Why Don’t We.” The petition states that in or about September 2016, Petitioners entered into agreements with Signature under which Signature would retain broad authority over the band, including the right to direct and control all of the band’s recordings, personal appearances, and live performances. Signature is based out of Orange County, Florida and the each of the agreements contained a Florida choice of law provision and a forum selection provision designating Orange County, Florida, as the exclusive forum for actions related to the Agreements. None of the Petitioners lived in California when they entered into the Agreements with Signature.

On or about August 17, 2021, Signature sued Petitioners for breach of the Agreements in the County of Orange, Florida.

Petitioners filed the instant Petition to Determine Controversy seeking, an order determining that Signature violated the California Talent Agencies Act (TAA), and the Agreements between Signature and Petitioners are void.

On August 11, 2023, the Labor Commissioner found the choice of law and forum selection provisions in the Agreements controlled and dismissed the action for lack of subject matter jurisdiction.

On April 8, 2024, Respondents filed the instant Motion to Dismiss or Stay, arguing:

·       Respondents move to dismiss Petitioners’ Appeal on the grounds that the Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles is an inconvenient forum, and pursuant to the doctrine of forum non conveniens, this action may be more appropriately and justly tried in an existing lawsuit pending in the Circuit Court of Orange County in Orlando, Florida, pursuant to the parties’ agreement with a specific, mandatory forum selection clause establishing the courts in Orange County, Florida as the only forum for resolving any disputes between the parties. Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§410.30(a), 418.10.

 

Petitioners filed an opposition, arguing:

·       The Florida forum selection and choice of law provision is unenforceable because it violates California public policy

o   It would circumvent the protections of the California Talent Agencies Act

o   Respondents have the burden to show Petitioner’s unwaivable rights would not be impaired

o   Respondents do not and cannot meet their burden because dismissing this trial de novo, in favor of the Florida Action, would strip Petitioners’ unwaivable rights under California law

o   There is no basis to stay this trial de novo pending resolution of the Florida Action

·       The forum selection provision either does not apply or is ambiguous

 

Respondents filed a reply, arguing:

·       Petitioners were not California residents when they entered into the Agreement

·       Because Respondents operate out of Florida, none of their conduct falls within the territorial limits of California. As such, California law—including the Talent Agencies Act—does not apply.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Motion to Dismiss

 

“When a court upon motion of a party or its own motion finds that in the interest of substantial justice an action should be heard in a forum outside this state, the court shall stay or dismiss the action in whole or in part on any conditions that may be just.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.30, subd. (a).) With regard to a contract dispute in which the parties’ agreement contains a forum selection clause, the threshold issue in a forum non conveniens motion is “whether the forum selection clause is mandatory or permissive.” (Animal Film, LLC v. D.E.J. Productions, Inc.¿(2011) 193 Cal.App.4th 466, 471.)  A mandatory clause is ordinarily given effect without any analysis of convenience—the only question is whether enforcement is unreasonable. (Id.)  

 

The responding party on a motion to enforce a mandatory forum selection clause has the burden to establish unreasonability by showing the selected forum would be. (Intershop Communications AG v. Superior Court (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 191, 199; Cal-State Business Products & Services, Inc. v. Ricoh (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 1666, 1679) (quoting Smith, Valentino & Smith, Inc. v. Superior Court of Los Angeles (1976) 17 Cal.3d 491, 494.) "Neither inconvenience nor the additional expense of litigation in the selected forum is a factor to be considered. However, a forum selection clause will not be enforced if to do so would bring about a result contrary to the public policy of this state." (Intershop, supra, p. 199-200.) 

 

B.     Discussion

Respondents move to dismiss Petitioners’ Appeal on the grounds that this Court is an inconvenient forum, and this action may be more appropriately and justly tried in an existing lawsuit pending in the Circuit Court of Orange County in Orlando, Florida, pursuant to the parties’  agreement with a specific, mandatory forum selection clause establishing the courts in Orange County, Florida as the only forum for resolving any disputes between the parties.

a.     Whether the Florida forum selection and choice of law provision is unenforceable because it violates California public policy

 

Petitioners argue the Court should refuse to defer to the selected forum because Petitioners’ claims are based on unwaivable rights created by California statutes. Petitioners’ claims are based on the Talent Agency Act.

 

The purpose of the TAA is “remedial; its purpose is to protect artists seeking professional employment from the abuses of talent agencies.”¿ (Styne v. Stevens (2001) 26 Cal.4th 42, 50.) Under the TAA, “no person shall engage in or carry on the occupation of a talent agency without first procuring a license therefor from the Labor Commissioner.”¿ (Lab. Code, § 1700.5.)¿ A “talent agency” means “a person or corporation who engages in the occupation of procuring, offering, promising, or attempting to procure employment or engagements for an artist….”¿ (Lab. Code, § 1700.4, subd. (a).) According to the TAA, the parties can “appeal within 10 days after [the commissioner’s] determination, to the superior court where the [dispute] shall be heard de novo.”¿ (Lab. Code, § 1700.4, subd. (a).)¿¿¿ 

 

The TAA states: “In cases of controversy arising under this chapter,” for example, a person engaged in the occupation of a talent agency without a license, “the parties involved shall refer the matters in dispute to the Labor Commissioner ….”¿ (Lab. Code, § 1700.4, subd. (a) [emphasis added].)¿ According to the California Supreme Court, “this language, using the mandatory ‘shall,’ grants ‘original and exclusive jurisdiction over issues arising under the Act’ to the Labor Commissioner. [Citations.]”¿ (Villanueva v. Fidelity National Title Co. (2021) 11 Cal.5th 104, 127.)¿California law provides the California Labor Commissioner with exclusive jurisdiction to decide the first instance issues arising under the Talent Agencies Act and requires any defense first be heard by the Labor Commissioner before the parties can proceed to this Court. (Cal. Labor Code § 1700.44(a); Styne v. Stevens, (2001) 26 Cal.4th 42.)

 

It is clear that the policy behind the Talent Agencies Act is to protect artists, and that this is an important California state interest. (Marathon Entertainment, Inc. v. Blasi (2008) 42 Cal.4th 974, 984.) Courts have established that the TAA’s protections are unwaivable and cannot be circumvented by contract. (See, Buchwald, 254 Cal. App. 2d at 355, 357 (holding that “[c]learly the Act may not be circumvented by allowing language of the written contract to control” and also that California “jurisdiction may not be waived by a party”; Yoo v. Robi, 126 Cal.App.4th 1089, 1103-04 (2005) (holding that TAA relief “applies even if, as in the present case, the contract . . . contains an affirmative statement the party seeking compensation has not agreed to obtain employment for the artist”). While Respondent’s argue that Yoo is inapposite here because it does not specifically address whether parties can contract for a forum selection provision, Yoo held that the TAA's protections are legislatively enacted in order to protect California residents and, therefore, cannot be waived by contract. (Yoo, 126 Cal. App. 4th at 1103.)

Therefore, the burden is on Respondent to show that litigating this action in Florida will not diminish in any way the substantive rights afforded to Petitioners under the TAA. 

 

b.     Respondent’s Burden

 

California courts will refuse to defer to the selected forum if to do so would substantially diminish the rights of California residents in a way that violates our state's public policy." (America Online, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th 1, 12; see Intershop Communications AG v. Superior Court (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 191, 200, [127 Cal.Rptr.2d 847] (Intershop Communications) ["a forum selection clause will not be enforced if to do so would bring about a result contrary to the public policy of this state"]; CQL Products, supra, 39 Cal.App.4th at p. 1354, 46 Cal.Rptr.2d 412; Hall v. Superior Court (1983) 150 Cal.App.3d 411, 416-418, [197 Cal.Rptr. 757] (Hall ).)" (Verdugo v. Alliantgroup (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 141, 147.

 

"The party opposing enforcement of a forum selection clause ordinarily "bears the 'substantial' burden of proving why it should not be enforced.' (Global Packaging, Inc. v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1623, 1633, [127 Cal.Rptr.3d 813], original italics; Intershop Communications, supra, 104 Cal.App.4th at p. 198, 127 Cal.Rptr.2d 847.) That burden, however, is reversed when the claims at issue are based on unwaivable rights created by California statutes. In that situation, the party seeking to enforce the forum selection clause bears the burden to show litigating the claims in the contractually-designated forum "will not diminish in any way the substantive rights afforded. . . under California law." (Wimsatt v. Beverly Hills Weight etc. Internat., Inc. (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1511, 1520-1524, [38 Cal.Rptr.2d 612] (Wimsatt ); see America Online, supra, 90 Cal.App.4th at pp. 10-11, 108 Cal.Rptr.2d 699 [party seeking enforcement must "prove that enforcement of the forum selection clause would not result in a significant diminution of rights"].)" (Verdugo, supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at pp. 147-148; see G Companies Management, LLC v. LREP Arizona, LLC (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 342, 350 ["That burden, however, is reversed when the claims at issue are based on unwaivable rights created by California statutes. In that situation, the party seeking to enforce the forum selection clause bears the burden to show litigating the claims in the contractually designated forum 'will not diminish in any way the substantive rights afforded... under California law.' "].) 

In G Companies Management, LLC v. LREP Arizona, LLC, supra, 88 Cal.App.5th 342, the court held: "In Hall v. Superior Court (1983) 150 Cal.App.3d 411, 197 Cal.Rptr. 757 (Hall), this court refused to enforce a forum selection clause in a case in which two California investors had exchanged their interests in an oil and gas limited partnership in return for stock in one of their co-investors, a Utah corporation. As in this case, the parties in Hall agreed to both forum selection and choice of law provisions, which identified Nevada as the selected forum and chose its laws to govern the transaction. Although the trial court enforced the forum selection clause, this court reversed. [¶] We explained in Hall that "[w]hile 'California does not have any public policy against a choice of law provision, where it is otherwise appropriate' [citation] . . . [citation], 'an agreement designating [a 

foreign] law will not be given effect if it would violate a strong California public policy. [or] "result in an evasion of... a statute of the forum protecting its citizens.”›” "'" (Hall, supra, 150 Cal.App.3d at pp. 416-417, 197 Cal.Rptr. 757.) We then reasoned that if the pending litigation were transferred to Nevada where Nevada law would be applied, the investors would lose the benefit of California's Corporate Securities Law of 1968 (Corp. Code, § 25000 et seq.), which would otherwise govern the exchange. The investors would thus be denied California's unwaivable protections against fraud and deception in securities matters. (Hall, supra, 150 Cal.App.3d at p. 417, 197 Cal.Rptr. 757.) Consequently, the trial court erred by enforcing the provision." (88 Cal.App.5th at p. 351.) 

 

Here, Respondents have not carried their burden. Respondents fail to show the remedies Florida law provides are “adequate,” let alone that enforcing the forum selection clause would not diminish Petitioners’ rights. Respondents fail to cite any Florida authority granting Petitioners’ rights comparable to the rights the TAA establishes. (See Verdugo v. Alliantgroup, L.P., supra, 237 Cal.App.4th at p. 157 ["Simply stated, a comparison is necessary to determine whether enforcing a forum selection and choice-of-law clause would violate California's public policy embodied in its governing statutes. As explained above, a defendant seeking to enforce a mandatory forum selection clause bears the burden to show enforcement will not in any way diminish the plaintiff's unwaivable statutory rights. By definition, this showing requires the defendant to compare the plaintiff's rights if the clause is not enforced and the plaintiff's rights if the clause is enforced. Indeed, a defendant can meet its burden only by showing the foreign forum provides the same or greater rights than California, or the foreign forum will apply California law on the claims at issue."].)

 

Instead, Respondents assert that California law should not apply because Petitioners were not California residents at the time they entered into the Agreement and because Respondents operate out of Florida. However, a large part of the conduct Petitioners identified as violative of the Talent Agencies Act occurred while Petitioners were residents of California. (Levin Decl., Ex. E.) Petitioners assert that Respondents unlawfully procured performances for Petitioners without a licensed talent agent in violation of the TAA from December 2, 2016 to August 22, 2021. (Levin Decl., Ex. E.) All of the Petitioners have continuously resided in California since 2018, with the exception of Besson, who has continuously resided in California since 2019. (King Decl., Ex D, Para 2.) Petitioners assert Respondents procured 69 performances for Petitioners without a licensed talent agent from 2016 to 2017, and 135 performances for Petitioners without a licensed talent agent from 2018 to 2021. (Levin Decl., Ex. E.) Since California has an important state interest in protecting its residents and Petitioners were residents of California when the majority of the alleged violations occurred, Respondents have not met their burden of showing enforcement of the forum selection clause is not contrary to public policy and that it would not diminish Petitioners’ rights.

 

Thus, Respondents’ Motion to Dismiss is denied.

 

c.      Request to Stay

Respondents argue that in the alternative, this action should be stayed pending resolution of the Florida Action. Respondents contend that a judgment in the Florida Action will fully vindicate any interest that California might have in this dispute.

However, the Court denies Respondents’ request to stay because Respondents failed to establish that the Florida forum selection and choice of law provision is enforceable because it violates California public policy. (See Verdugo, 237 Cal. App. 4th at 162.) Since the Florida Action does not have jurisdiction to adjudicate the TAA issues, the Court does not find this as grounds to stay this matter pending the resolution of the Florida Action.

 

Respondents’ request to stay this action is denied.

 

III. CONCLUSION

 

Accordingly, Respondent’s Motion to Dismiss, or in the alternative, Motion to Stay is DENIED.
