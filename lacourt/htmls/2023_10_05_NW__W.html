DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01431    Hearing Date: October 5, 2023    Dept: W

-JANE DOE V. LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANT LOS ANGELES UNIFIED SCHOOL DISTRICT’S MOTION TO COMPEL PRODUCTION OF RECORDS, PURSUANT TO SUBPOENA, TO AMY TIERNEY, LSCW

 

Date of Hearing:        October 5, 2023                                Trial Date:       May 6, 2024

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV01431

 

Moving Party:            Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Jane Doe  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises out of allegations by Plaintiff Jane Doe that she was sexually abused by a former employee of Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”), Defendant William Paden, while he was employed by LAUSD. On September 28, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against LAUSD and Paden for (1) sexual abuse of a minor, (2) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (3) sexual harassment, (4) negligent hiring, supervision, and retention of an unfit employee, (5) breach of mandatory duty: failure to report suspected child abuse (Government Code section 815.6), (6) negligent failure to warn, train, or educate, (7) negligent supervision of a minor, and (8) negligence.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s Motion to Compel Production of Records, Pursuant to Subpoena, to Amy Tierney, LSCW is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District moves for an order compelling nonparty, Amy Tierney, LSCW, to comply with the Subpoena for Production of Business Records served on Amy Tierney pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1985.3, 1987.1, 2020.010(a)(3) and 2020.410.

 

 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records.¿ (CCP § 2020.010.)¿ A deposition subpoena may command: (1) only the attendance and testimony of the deponent, (2) only the production of business records for copying, or (3) the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things.¿ (CCP § 2020.020.)¿  

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, subdivision (a) states, “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” “[U]pon motion reasonably made by the party, judges may rule upon motions for quashing, modifying or compelling compliance with, subpoenas.” (Lee v. Swansboro Country Property Owners Ass'n (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 575, 582-583.)

 

On May 23, 2023, Defendant LAUSD served on Amy Tierney, LCSW, a subpoena for production of business records. (Ahmed Decl. ¶5.) On June 15, 2023, Defendant received a letter from attorneys on behalf of Amy Tierney, LCSW stating that the subpoena was received, but that Amy Tierney, LCSW would not produce the records absent a court order. (Ahmed Decl. ¶7.) Defendant LAUSD met and conferred with attorneys on behalf of Amy Tierney, LCSW who again stated the position that Plaintiff had not authorized release of documents and a court order would be necessary. (Ahmed Decl. ¶8.) Defendant LAUSD requested Plaintiff’s authorization for the referenced psychological records from her provider. (Ahmed Decl. ¶ 3). However, Plaintiff has refused to provide authorization for release of her records. (Ahmed Decl. ¶ 3.) Defendant LAUSD contends Defendant cannot adequately prepare the matter for Trial or evaluate Plaintiffs’ claims without information to determine the extent of Plaintiff’s alleged claimed psychological damages. Defendant LAUSD further contends the right to privacy is not absolute. Plaintiff has certainly put her mental health history at issue in this litigation by claiming that she has suffered, and is suffering, from emotional injuries. Accordingly, Defendant LAUSD contends Plaintiff’s psychological records are directly relevant to the issues raised by Plaintiff.

 

Plaintiff first objects to the motion on the grounds it is untimely. Plaintiff argues Defendant LAUSD improperly issued a second deposition subpoena on Ms. Tierney in order to reset the clock on the 60-day time limit in Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1. In the instant matter, Defendant LAUSD originally issued a subpoena to Ms. Tierney on March 2, 2023 and received her objection on March 15, 2023. According to Plaintiff, however, Defendant LAUSD realized it blew through the 60 day deadline (i.e., May 14, 2023) and issued an identical subpoena to Ms. Tierney on May 31, 2023 to attempt to "reset the clock”.

 

A motion to compel compliance with a deposition subpoena must be made within 60 days after completion of the deposition record, the date objections are served, or the date specified for production. (CCP §2025.480(b); Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032-1033.)¿ As noted above, Ms. Tierney objected to the production absent a court order on March 15, 2023. Accordingly, the record was complete when Defendant LAUSD received Ms. Tierney’s objection and LAUSD had 60 days thereafter to file its motion. This deadline was mandatory. (See Unzipped Apparel, LLC v. Bader (2007) 156 Cal. App. 4th 123, 137.)

 

The court disagrees that the deadline has passed. Although Plaintiff correctly points out that a party is not allowed to reset the clock for bringing a motion to compel response to certain types of discovery by sending out duplicative discovery requests at a later date, there is no authority—to the court’s knowledge – that a party may not direct a subsequent deposition subpoena for documents to a third party when its initial deposition subpoena was met with objection.  The court further finds that it would be contrary to public policy and common practice if a party needed to immediately file a motion to compel with the court in the face of objections to a third party deposition subpoena or forever relinquish the right to obtain such records.  It is common practice to issue multiple subpoenas to a single entity over the course of litigation, but not to pursue every one of those to final resolution in court. 

 

Next, Plaintiff argues the plain language of the subpoena at issue is way too overbroad as it amounts to seeking the proverbial kitchen sink in terms of records related to Plaintiff. Plaintiff further contends it is not only Plaintiff’s privacy rights impacted by the requested information, but also potentially the privacy rights of third parties who Plaintiff may have discussed during her treatment with Ms. Tierney.

 

The court disagrees. Although Plaintiff has a legally protected privacy interest in her medical records, psychotherapist-patient communications are discoverable if the issue of the patient's mental or emotional condition is raised by the patient. (Evid. Code 1016.) Here, Plaintiff alleges she has suffered emotional harm, humiliation, mental anguish, and emotional distress.  Defendant has established a compelling need to discover these records so as to evaluate the source and magnitude of that emotional distress.  With respect to third party privacy rights, the court agrees that Ms. Tierney may redact the names of any third parties who appear in the records, but may not redact the substance of any information provided. 

 

Accordingly, Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s Motion to Compel Production of Records, Pursuant to Subpoena, to Amy Tierney, LSCW is GRANTED.


Case Number: 23VECP00397    Hearing Date: October 5, 2023    Dept: W

SWIFT FINANCIAL, LLC AS SErvicing agent for webbank v. milky way catering, inc. et al.

 

petition to confirm arbitration award

 

Date of Hearing:        October 5, 2023                                Trial Date:       N/A 

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECP00397

 

Moving Party:            Petitioner Swift Financial, LLC  

Responding Party:     No opposition.  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is an action to confirm an arbitration award issued in Petitioner Swift Financial, LLC, as servicing agent for WebBank’s favor and against Respondents Milky Way Catering, Inc. and Karine Malakyan.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Petitioner Swift Financial, LLC’s Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Petitioner Swift Financial, LLC moves this court for an order granting the petition to confirm arbitration award on the grounds that the Defendant has been properly served with the Petition to Confirm Arbitration Award and has not timely responded to said Petition. Swift Financial makes this petition pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.

 

“Any party to an arbitration in which an award has been made may petition the court to confirm, correct or vacate the award.” (CCP §1285.) The petition must set forth (1) the substance of or have attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate, (2) the names of the arbitrators, and (3) the award and the written opinion of the arbitrators, or attach a copy.  (CCP §1285.4.)  Confirmation of the award is mandatory unless a response or petition to correct or vacate the award has been timely filed.¿ (See CCP §1286;¿Valsan Partners Limited Partnership v. Calcor Space Facility, Inc.¿(1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 809, 818.)

 

A petition to confirm an award shall be served and filed not later than four years after the date of service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner. (CCP §1288.) A court does not have jurisdiction to correct or vacate an award if the response or petition to correct or vacate the award is filed 100 days after the date of the service of a signed copy of the award on the petitioner.  (CCP §1288; Abers v. Rohrs (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 1199, 1203.)

 

In the instant matter, the Petition complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 1285.4. The arbitration award was entered by the American Arbitration Association in the State of Delaware on March 13, 2023. Petitioner has attached a copy of the agreement to arbitrate. (See Attachment 4(b).) Moreover, the petition states that George J. Tzanetopoulos conducted the arbitration (Petition ¶6) and a copy of the arbitration award and written opinion is attached to the petition.  (Petition ¶8(c), Exh. 8(c).) The copy of the arbitration award provides that the petitioner is awarded, against Respondents, jointly and severally, the total sum of $139,307.15, which represents $137,559.15 in the unpaid loan balance and loan fees, plus $1,748.00 in attorney's fees incurred in enforcing the Loan Agreement. (See Exh. 8(c).)

 

The court notes the prevailing party on a motion to confirm, correct, or vacate an arbitration award is entitled to a mandatory award of costs.  (CCP §1293.2.)  Item 10(e) of the petition requests costs and attorney fees according to proof as well as interest at the statutory rate. Because the petitioner does not provide the amounts sought, the court cannot grant the request at this time.

 

Petitioner also requests prejudgment interest under Civil Code 3289 from the date of the award, March 13, 2023 to the date of entry of judgment.

 

Accordingly, the petition for order confirming arbitrator’s award as judgment is GRANTED.

 


Case Number: 23VECV00711    Hearing Date: October 5, 2023    Dept: W

JOSEPH MENASHE, et al. v. FCA US LLC

 

PLAINTIFFs’ MOTIONs to compel defendant’s further responses to request for production of documents (Set one) and special interrogatories (Set One)

 

Date of Hearing:          October 5, 2023                                              Trial Date:       None set.   

Department:               W                                                                     Case No.:         23VECV00711

 

Moving Party:             Plaintiffs Joseph Menashe and Deborah Menahse

Responding Party:       Defendant FCA US LLC  

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a lemon law action. On February 15, 2023, Plaintiffs Joseph Menashe and Deborah Menahse filed a complaint against Defendant FCA US LLC for 1. Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act – Breach of Express Warranty; 2. Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; and 3. Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act – Civil Code §1793.2(b). On December 10, 2021, Plaintiffs acquired a 2022 Chrysler Pacifica.  Plaintiffs allege they received the vehicle with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty and developed other serious defects and nonconformities to warranty including, but not limited to the engine and its related systems, electrical system, windshield wipers, Bluetooth connection and related systems inoperable, sensor to open/close trunk inoperable, sunroof making creaking noises, excessive days out of service, and other defects.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs’ Motions to Compel Defendant’s Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents (Set One) is GRANTED.

Plaintiffs’ Motions to Compel Defendant’s Further Responses Special Interrogatories (Set One) is GRANTED, in part.

 

discussion

 

Plaintiffs Joseph Menashe and Deborah Menahse move for an order to strike Defendant FCA US LLC’s objections and compel further responses to Plaintiffs’ Special Interrogatories (Set One), Nos. 14, 20, 39, and 41 as well as further responses and production of documents to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) Requests Nos. Requests Nos. 7, 16, 20, 40, 44, 46, 50. Plaintiffs make the motions on the grounds Defendant FCA failed to provide appropriate and complete responses to the requests.

 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿(CCP § 2030.300(a).) “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (CCP § 2030.300(b).)¿In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ (CRC Rule 3.1345(a)(2).)

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive, or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310(c).) A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”  

 

Plaintiffs contend the interrogatories that are the subject of this Motion seek the identification of key witnesses, namely, those who inspected and tested, and performed warranty repairs on the Vehicle, and those who were involved with Defendant’s decision of whether or not to repurchase or replace the Vehicle at the prelitigation stage. However, Defendant’s responses asserted multiple boilerplate objections to these requests and failed to identify a single individual. Moreover, to prove their allegations, Plaintiffs requested the following categories of documents: (1) those relating specifically to Plaintiff and the subject vehicle (Request Nos. 7 and 40), (2) those relating to Defendant’s knowledge, internal investigations, analysis and publications of defects plaguing the Vehicle (Request Nos. 16, 44, 46, and 50); and (3) those relating to Defendant’s warranty and vehicle repurchase policies, procedures, and practices (Request No. 20). Again, Plaintiffs contend Defendants responded with boilerplate objections.

 

In opposition, Defendant FCA argues Plaintiffs prematurely ended the meet and confer process and filed a template motion to compel further responses to Plaintiffs’ Special Interrogatories Nos. 14, 20, 39 and 41 and Request for Production of Documents Nos. 7, 16, 20, 40, 44, 46 and 50. Defendants also contend it has expressed a willingness to supplement and now has supplemented many of the requests at issue in this motion and therefore, made Plaintiffs’ motions moot. Plaintiffs do not address the supplemental responses provided since the filing of the opposition. 

 

The court finds SROG Nos. 14 and 20 MOOT. FCA has provided supplemental responses. However, the court finds SROG Nos. 39, and 41 insufficient. FCA states for SROG Nos. 39 and 41 it “will designate a corporate representative pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure Section 2025.230.” However, that does not answer the discovery requests nor has FCA actually complied.

 

As for RPD Nos. 7, 16, 20, 40, 44, 46, and 50, the court orders FCA to produce further responses pursuant to a privilege log. FCA’s response it “will conduct a diligent search and inquiry” but failing to do so is not compliance.
