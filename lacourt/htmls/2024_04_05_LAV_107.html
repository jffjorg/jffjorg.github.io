DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23VECV02962    Hearing Date: April 5, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Harris Davis,

 

        Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

Norms, et al.,

 

          Defendants.

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    23VECV02962                107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

(1) Compel Responses to Request for Production

(2) Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

(3) Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories

(4) Compel Responses to Requests for Admission

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

(1)   Grant; grant sanctions in the reduced amount of $285.00

(2)   Grant; grant sanctions in the reduced amount of $285.00

(3)   Grant; grant sanctions in the reduced amount of $285.00

(4)   Deny; deny sanctions.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On October 25, 2021, plaintiff Harris Davis (“Plaintiff”) tripped and fell over a door mat at a restaurant owned by defendants Norms and Norms Restaurants, LLC (“Defendant” and, together with Plaintiff, “Parties”). Plaintiff thereby incurred injuries. (Complaint pp. 3–4.)

 

On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging a single cause of action for premises liability — negligence.

 

On October 18, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff with discovery requests. (Motions p. 3.) Parties agreed to multiple response deadline extensions, finally agreeing to a January 24, 2024 deadline but Defendant had not received responses as of the filing of the Motions (as defined below). (Ibid.)

 

On February 5, 2024, Defendant filed four motions to compel: a motion to compel responses to requests for production (“Motion #1), a motion to compel responses to form interrogatories (“Motion #2”), a motion to compel responses to special interrogatories (“Motion #3”), and a motion to compel responses to requests for admission (“Motion #4” and, together with Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3, the “Motions”).

 

On March 27, 2024, Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition for each of the Motions.

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

  1. Motion #1 (Requests for Production), Motion #2 (Form Interrogatories), and Motion #3 (Special Interrogatories)

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response,…[t]he party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2030.290.) And, “a party's failure to object to interrogatories within 30 days, or such additional time as extended by the court, constitutes a waiver of any right to object.” (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) Similarly, “[i]f a party to whom¿a¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed fails to serve a timely response to it,… [t]he party to whom the¿demand¿for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling¿is directed waives any objection to the demand [and]…[t]he party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the¿demand.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.300.) In the absence of any response to the discovery request, all that a motion to compel need show is that a set of¿interrogatories¿was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (See Leach v. Superior Court, supra at 905–06.) 

 

Defendant attaches to each of Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3 evidence that each such discovery request was properly served on Plaintiff’s attorney by mail on October 18, 2023. (Motions Exh. “A.”) Defendant also attaches to each of Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3 copies of e-mail correspondences between its attorney and Plaintiff’s attorney in which they agree to extend the deadline for responses to such discovery requests to January 24, 2024 as well as a copy of an e-mail sent by its attorney to Plaintiff’s attorney inquiring about the requested discovery responses after that January 24 deadline had passed. (Motions Exh. “B,” Exh. “C,” Exh. “D.”)  Finally, Defendant attaches to each of Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3 evidence that no response of any kind to any of the three discovery requests was received by that response due date or thereafter. (Stewart Decls. ¶¿10.) Thus, Defendant has met its burden of showing that Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3 are necessary.

 

Accordingly, Motion #1 is GRANTED, Motion #2 is GRANTED, and Motion #3 is GRANTED. 

 

  1. Motion #4 (Requests for Admission)

 

“If a party to whom requests for admission are directed fails to serve a timely response,… [t]he requesting party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2033.280, subd. (b).) Additionally, “[t]he party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests.” (§¿2033.280, subd. (a).)

 

In Motion #4, Defendant does not move for an order that the matters specified in the requests for admission be deemed admitted under California Code of Civil Procedure §¿2033.280, subdivision (b) but rather moves for an order to compel responses to its requests for admission under California Code of Civil Procedure §¿2030.290, subdivision (b). However, California Code of Civil Procedure §¿2030.290, subdivision (b) permits a party who has propounded discovery requests to move for an order to compel responses to only interrogatories, not to requests for admission. Finding no statutory authority for Defendant to move to compel responses to requests for admission, this court DENIES Motion #4.

 

  1. Sanctions

 

Sanctions are mandatory against the party, the attorney, or both whose failure to serve a timely response to the request necessitated the motion to compel response to interrogatories or the motion to compel response to a demand for inspection. (Cal. C. Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c); see also Cal. Rules of Court R. 3.1348(a) (the court can award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party seeking to compel discovery even though no opposition was filed, the opposition was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed). “As to the amount of¿sanctions,¿Cal. C. Civ. Proc. §¿2023.030(a)¿authorizes the trial court to impose an amount representing ‘the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result’ of a party's misuse of the discovery process.” (Parker v. Wolters Kluwer United States, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 285, 294.) 

 

Each of the Motions requests sanctions in the amount of $510.00 for a total of $2,040.00. (Stewart Decls. ¶ 11.) For each of the Motions, this is based on one hour of attorney time preparing each of the Motions and an additional hour of attorney time responding to any opposition thereto at a rate of $225.00 per hour, plus a $60.00 filing fee for each of the Motions.

 

Because this court denies Motion #4, we do not find Motion #4 to have been necessary.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the sanctions requested in Motion #4.

 

Because this court grants Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3, we find these Motions to have been necessary. However, we observe that because no oppositions were filed to any of Motion #1, Motion #2, or Motion #3, half of the attorney time for which sanctions are sought was never spent on bringing or defending these Motions. We believe that the one hour of attorney time spent preparing each of Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3 at a rate of $225.00 per hour is reasonable. We additionally find that seeking sanctions in the amount of the $60.00 filing fee in costs for each of Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3 is reasonable.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS sanctions against Plaintiff and in favor of Defendant in the reduced amount of $285.00 for each of Motion #1, Motion #2, and Motion #3, for a total of $855.00.
