Case Number: 21NWCV00512    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: C

CITIBANK N.A. v. AVETISIAN

CASE NO.: 21NWCV00512

HEARING:  January 14, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Robert Avetsian’s Motion to Deposit a Negotiable Instrument into the Court Registry Investment System is DENIED.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a debt collection action. On August 9, 2024, Plaintiff CITIBANK N.A. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Robert Avetsian, in pro per, (“Defendant”) to recover non-payment of credit card debt of $27,971.91. The Complaint alleged two causes of action for 1) Open Book Account and 2) Account Stated.

 

Non-jury trial was held on June 14, 2024 in Department P. Judgment was entered in favor of Plaintiff in the amount of $28,545.91, for damages plus costs.

 

Defendant now moves to deposit a negotiable instrument into the Court Registry Investment System to resolve the debt.

 

The Motion is unopposed as of January 10, 2025.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant seeks to deposit a negotiable instrument, a bill of exchange drawn upon by the U.S. Treasury, into the Court Registry Investment System. Defendant cites 28 U.S.C. §§ 2041 and 2042.

 

The Court finds Defendant’s Motion is inappropriate in this Court. According to Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 67, and 28 U.S.C §§ 2041 and 2042, parties may deposit funds into the Court Registry Investment System for cases involving monetary judgments, pursuant to a court order. Such statutes are applicable in federal courts, but not in state courts such as this Court. Defendant has cited no other authority regarding deposits of negotiable instruments in California state courts to satisfy a debt judgment.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Deposit a Negotiable Instrument into the Court Registry Investment System is denied.


Case Number: 22NWCV000827    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: C

KAIZOU GROUP INC. v. PAN, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV00827

HEARING:  January 14, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#7

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

I.     Cross-Complainants’ Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff Kaizou Group Inc.’s PMK is GRANTED, in part. Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc. and its counsel are ordered to pay $1,168.91 in sanctions.

II.    Cross-Complainants’ Motion to Compel Deposition of Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao is GRANTED, in part. Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao and his counsel are ordered to pay $1,968.91 in sanctions.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Background

            

This is a breach of contract action. On September 13, 2022, Plaintiff Kaizou Group Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “Cross-Defendant”) filed this action against Defendants Sang “Tony” Pan, Julie Pan (“Defendants”), and Does 1 to 100. The Complaint alleges the parties entered into an agreement (“Contract”), in which Plaintiff would provide Defendant with goods. The Complaint alleges Defendants breached the Contract by receiving the goods but failing to make payments on Plaintiff’s invoices from the period of November 24, 2021 to June 2, 2022. The total amount owed to Plaintiff is allegedly $703,484.00. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Breach of Contract and 2) Common Counts.

 

On January 24, 2024, Defendants Sang “Tony” Pan and Julie Pan (“Defendants” or “Cross-Complainants”) filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff, Shang Yang Zhao a/k/a Shangyang Zhao a/k/a Noble Zhao (“Cross-Defendants”), and Roes 1 to 20. The Cross-Complaint alleges Cross-Complainants allegedly paid for the goods in the amount of $1,081,940.00. The Cross-Complaint alleges Cross-Defendants falsified some invoices and failed to credit payment made by Cross-Complainants in the amount of $72,548.00. Cross-Complainants have allegedly overpaid in the amount of at least $305,908.00. The Cross-Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Conversion, 2) Money Had and Received, 3) Common Counts—Open Book Account, 4) Unjust Enrichment, 5) Accounting, and 6) Declaratory relief.

 

On July 17, 2024, Cross-Complainants’ counsel sent notices of deposition and request for documents for Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc.’s Personal Most Knowledgeable (PMK) and Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao, both to be held on August 8, 2024. (Park Decls., ¶ 3, Exhs. 1-2.) On July 30, 2024, Cross-Defendants’ counsel stated August 8, 2024 would not work, but that counsel would respond with an alternative date. (Park Decls., ¶ 4.) Cross-Defendants’ counsel also stated the PMK was “tentatively Huajun Zhang.” (Ibid.) On August 8, 2024, Cross-Defendants’ counsel suggested a date of August 30, 2024, which was past the discovery cutoff at the time—trial was to be held on September 20, 2024, but is now set for March 24, 2025. (Ibid.) Cross-Complainants’ counsel contacted Cross-Defendants’ counsel multiple times regarding confirmation of the August 30 deposition date, a more definite identification of the PMK, and a waiver of the discovery deadline. (Park Decls., ¶¶ 5-6, Exh. 3.) Cross-Defendants’ counsel refused to waive the discovery deadline, arguing Cross-Complainants’ counsel did not provide alternative dates prior to the deadline. (Park Decls., ¶ 6, Exh. 3.) Cross-Defendants’ counsel was otherwise unresponsive. (Ibid.)

 

Cross-Complainants now move to compel the depositions of Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc.’s PMK and Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao and to compel production of documents at such depositions. Cross-Complainants also seek sanctions.

 

No oppositions have been filed as of January 10, 2025.

 

Legal Standard

 

“If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action. . .without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd. (a).)¿ 

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion to compel deposition must be “accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (b)(2).) The parties have met and conferred, and Cross-Complainants’ counsel has been thereafter unresponsive. (See Park Decls., ¶¶ 4-6, Exh. 3.) The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has been met.

 

Discussion

 

The Court first discusses the request for production of documents at the depositions. A motion to compel deposition must “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.450 subd., (b)(1).) 

 

The Court finds Cross-Complainants failed to demonstrate specific facts showing good cause to support the production of documents. Thus, the Court denies the Motions to the Compel to the extent they demand production of documents at the depositions.

 

As to the deposition, the Court finds Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc.’s PMK and Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao failed to attend their depositions noticed for August 8, 2024. (Park Decls., ¶ 3, Exhs. 1-2.) Although Cross-Defendant suggested an alternative discovery date of August 30, 2024, that date was past the discovery cut-off deadline at the time. (Park Decls., ¶ 4.) Cross-Defendants refused to waive the discovery cut-off deadline and failed to provide alternative dates, despite Cross-Complainants attempts to meet and confer. (Park Decls., ¶ 5-6.) The Court finds Cross-Defendants failed to appear for their noticed depositions. Accordingly, the Court grants the Motions to Compel to the extent they seek to compel Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc. to identify its PMK and compel depositions of such PMK and Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao.

 

Sanctions

 

If a party deponent fails to appear or fails to produce materials designated in the deposition notice, monetary sanctions may be imposed, “unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.450, subd. (g)(1), 2025.480, subd. (j).) Sanctions may be awarded even though no opposition to the motion was filed, the opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1030(a).)

 

Cross-Complainants’ counsel seeks sanctions in total sum of $2,268.91 against each Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao and his counsel for 4 hours in preparation of this Motion and an expected 1.5 hours for the reply and hearing at a rate of $400.00, plus motion fees and e-filing fees. Cross-Complainants’ counsel seeks sanctions in total sum of $1,068.91 against each Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc. and its counsel for 2 hours in preparation of this Motion and an expected 1.5 hours for the reply and hearing at a rate of $400.00, plus motion fees and e-filing fees.

 

Because no opposition was filed, the Court reduces sanctions by .75 hours for each motion. Cross-Defendant Shang Yang Zhao and his counsel are ordered to pay $1,968.91 in sanctions. Cross-Defendant Kaizou Group Inc. and its counsel are ordered to pay $1,168.91 in sanctions.

 

 

         

 


Case Number: 22NWCV01310    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: C

AMY N., ET AL. v. AMPAC MANAGEMENT GROUP, INC., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV01310

HEARING:  January 14, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#2

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendants AMPAC Management Group, Inc. and Villa Avila II Owners Association’s Motion for Leave to Proceed with Neurophysiological (Mental) Examination of Plaintiff Amy N. is DENIED.

 

Defendants to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a premises liability action. On November 14, 2022, Plaintiff Amy N., a minor, through her Guardian Ad Litem, Concepcion Ocampo Villalobos, (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against AMPAC Management Group., Inc. and Does 1 to 30. Villa Avila II Owners Association was later added as a Defendant. The Complaint alleges Defendant was the homeowners association in charge of maintenance and repair of condominiums located at 4094 Rosemead Boulevard, in the City of Pico Rivera, CA (“Subject Premises”). The Complaint alleges that on June 19, 2021, as Plaintiff was playing on swings on the Subject Premises, the swing seat broke, causing Plaintiff to fall and suffer injuries. The Complaint alleges the two causes of action for 1) Negligence and 2) Premises Liability.

 

Defendants AMPAC Management Group., Inc. and Villa Avila II Owners Association (“Defendants”) now move for leave of Court to proceed with a neuropsychological (mental) examination of Plaintiff.

 

Legal Standard

 

“(a) If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court. 

 

(b) A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. 

 

(c) Notice of the motion shall be served on the person to be examined and on all parties who have appeared in the action.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310.) 

 

“(a) The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under Section 2032.310 only for good cause shown.”

 

(b) If a party stipulates as provided in subdivision (c), the court shall not order a mental examination of a person for whose personal injuries a recovery is being sought except on a showing of exceptional circumstances.

 

(c) A stipulation by a party under this subdivision shall include both of the following:

(1) A stipulation that no claim is being made for mental and emotional distress over and above that usually associated with the physical injuries claimed.

(2) A stipulation that no expert testimony regarding this usual mental and emotional distress will be presented at trial in support of the claim for damages.

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).) 

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion for mental examination shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b).) On December 20, 2024, Defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel requesting that Plaintiff voluntary submit to a mental examination. (Williams Decl., ¶ 5; Exh. D.) On December 27, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a response e-mail asking for a return call to discuss the examination. (Williams Decl., ¶ 6; Exh. E.) The parties later met and conferred telephonically—Plaintiff’s counsel would not agree to voluntary submission of a mental examination or to Defendants’ proposal for Plaintiff to waive all cognitive, mental, and behavioral health claims. (Williams Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff’s counsel proposed waiting a year to allow Plaintiff to determine whether she would be pursuing such claims. (Ibid.) Defense counsel contends this Motion is necessary absent an agreement by the parties. (Williams Decl., ¶ 9.) The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has been met.

 

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff has a seen a neurologist for the following injuries listed in responses to form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and questioning at her mother’s deposition: headaches, memory loss, motor-reflex decline, loss of sleep, nightmares, social impairment, school setbacks, temperament issues and hair loss. (Williams Decl., ¶ 4; Exhs. A-C.) As mentioned in the meet and confer section, Plaintiff has refused to voluntarily submit to an additional mental examination. (Williams Decl., ¶ 8.) Defendants seek an order requiring Plaintiff to submit to an additional mental examination at the office of Leah Ellenberg, Ph.D., neuropsychologist, located at 9401 Wilshire Blvd. Ste 730 Beverly Hills, CA 90212 from 9 a.m. to noon on April 2 and 3, 2025.

 

Defendants argue that there is good cause to compel Plaintiff to undergo an additional mental examination by a specialist in a different field. Defendants cite Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255 to support the contention that multiple defense examinations may be permitted on a showing a good cause. Defendants argue that a neuropsychologist examination is needed “to assess and evaluate treatment of claimed cognitive, mental, and behavioral effects of brain disorders through determination, measurement, and evaluation of Plaintiff’s claimed cognitive symptoms and the efficacy of psychological treatment of Plaintiff.” (Williams Decl., ¶ 9.) Defendants argue that the examination is limited only to areas that are needed to evaluate Plaintiff’s damages claim and is reasonably calculated to lead to discovery of admissible evidence. (Williams Decl., ¶ 10.) Further, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has placed her mental condition at issue and, if Plaintiff does not wish to submit to the examination, she must wait her claim with respect to those damages for which the examination is sought.

 

Plaintiff argues that it was first reasonably believed that Plaintiff suffered severe mental injuries as a result of the Incident. Yet, after medical assessment, it became apparent that she only suffered from garden variety mental injuries associated with the physical injuries she suffered. (Perez Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff is amendable to a stipulation pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.320, subdivision (c), that (1) Plaintiff is making no claim for mental and emotional distress over and above that usually associated with the physical injuries claimed and (2) Plaintiff will not present expert testimony at trial regarding this mental and emotional distress usually associated with the physical injuries, in support of her claim for damages. (Perez Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff has met and conferred with Defendants regarding a stipulation since May 3, 2024, but Defendants indicated they were not amendable to Plaintiff’s stipulation. (See Perez Decl., ¶ 4-8, Exh. 2.) The main point of contention is the language of the stipulations—Defendant’s stipulation uses more specific language regarding the types of mental injuries Plaintiff intends to waive, and Plaintiff’s stipulation uses more general statutory language. (Perez Decl., ¶ 6, Exhs. 1, 3.) Plaintiff has made it clear that she would only be claiming orthopedic injuries and mental and/or emotional injuries commensurate with such physical injuries. (Perez Decl., ¶ 7.) Nevertheless, the parties were not able to come to an agreement regarding the stipulation up until December 31, 2024, and Plaintiff was forced to present her opposition to the present motion. (Perez Decl., ¶ 8.) Because Plaintiff has stipulated pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.320, subdivision (c), Plaintiff argues the Motion should be denied.

 

Defendants argue in reply that exceptional circumstances warrant on order for a mental examination, despite Plaintiff’s stipulation, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.320, subdivision (b). Defendants argue it would be unfair for Plaintiff’s expert witness, a neurologist, to testify about her mental injuries without allowing Defendants to offer their own witness. In response to Plaintiff’s proposed stipulation, Defendants argue that a more specific stipulation is consistent with the intent of Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2032.320, subdivisions (b) and (c), and caselaw.

 

The Discovery Act permits a plaintiff to avoid the necessity of submitting to a mental examination by stipulating that: (1) no claim is being made for mental and emotional distress “over and above that usually associated with the physical injuries claimed”; and (2) no psychiatric testimony will be offered at trial in support of any claimed emotional distress. (See CCP § 2032.320(c).) 

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff has stipulated pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.320, subdivision (c). Plaintiff’s proposed stipulation borrows language directly from statute. (See Perez Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 3.) The Court finds this language is sufficient—the statute clearly states that if a party stipulates, the Court “shall not” order a mental examination “except on a showing of exceptional circumstances.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.320, subd. (b).) Nothing in the statute requires Plaintiff to specify exactly which mental injuries she disclaims. Defendant’s cited caselaw does not support the contention that a more specific stipulation is needed—neither Doyle v. Superior Court (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 1878 and Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008 discuss stipulations under Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.320, subdivision (c).

 

The Court finds Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, inapplicable to support a finding of good cause to warrant an additional mental examination. Shapira’s holding was that there is no numerical limit on the number of mental examinations on a showing of good cause. (Id. at p. 1255.) Yet, in that case, the plaintiff’s mental injury, brain damage allegedly caused by defendant’s improper use of medication, was severe and clearly at issue. (Id. at 1251-52.) Further, the plaintiff was examined by two psychiatrists, who would testify at trial. (Id. at p. 1254.) Here, Plaintiff has stipulated that 1) no claim is being made for mental and emotional distress over and above that usually associated with the physical injuries claimed, and 2) no expert witness will testify at trial as to those mental injuries. (Perez Decl., ¶ 4.) The Court thus finds Defendants have not offered a showing of exceptional circumstances, such as in Shapira, that warrant an order to conduct an additional mental examination.

 

Accordingly, the Court denies Defendants’ Motion for Leave to Proceed with Neurophysiological (Mental) Examination.

TRIAS v. HYUNDAI MOTOR AMERICA, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV01911

HEARING:  January 7, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#3

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Hyundai Motor America’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. The action is stayed pending arbitration.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a lemon law action. On June 22, 2023, Plaintiff Keilani C. Trias (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Hyundai Motor America, Modernity Equity Holdings Little Hills, Inc., Norm Reeves Hyundai Superstore, and Does 1 to 10. The Complaint alleges Defendants failed repair or replace Plaintiff’s 2021 Hyundai Elantra (“Subject Vehicle”). The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act-Breach of Express Warranty, 2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act-Breach of Implied Warranty, 3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act section 1793.2, and 4) Negligent Repair. These causes of action “arise out of warranty and repair obligations of HYUNDAI MOTOR AMERICA in connection with a vehicle that Plaintiff purchased and for which HYUNDAI MOTOR AMERICA issued a written warranty,” where “[t]he warranty was not issued by the selling dealership.” (Compl., ¶ 9.)

 

Defendant Hyundai Motor America (“Defendant”) now moves to compel arbitration under the Warranty and Bluelink Connected Services Agreement, and requests to stay the action.

 

Judicial Notice

 

The Court grants Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice of the Complaint but notes that judicial notice for files already in the record are unnecessary.

 

The Court grants all of Plaintiff’s Requests for Judicial Notice.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court overrules Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection to paragraph 3 and Exhibit 3 in the Declaration of Ali Ameripour and to paragraph 5 in the Declaration of Vijay Rao in support of the Motion.

Legal Standard

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; see also Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿ 

 

¿“If a court of competent jurisdiction. . .has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)¿¿ 

 

Discussion

 

Arbitration Under Hyundai’s Bluelink Service Agreement

 

Defendant argues that arbitration may also be enforced under the Bluelink Connected Services Agreement. Defendant contends that on or about May 31, 2021, Plaintiffs enrolled their vehicle in the Hyundai’s Bluelink Service through the Dealer-Assisted Enrollment process. (Rao Decl., ¶ 5.) Hyundai’s Bluelink Service refers to a connected car system that includes various functions and features. (Rao Decl., ¶ 3.) In order to enroll in Hyundai’s Bluelink services, customers must agree to the then-effective Connected Services Agreement (“CSA”). (Rao Decl., ¶ 4.) In order to enroll in Hyundai’s Bluelink services, a person would have had to click a box to acknowledge that they “read and agree[d] to the Blue Link Terms & Conditions” and then click the “Complete” button. (Rao Decl., ¶ 6.) The phrase “Terms & Conditions” included a hyperlink to the CSA. (Ibid.) As presented to Plaintiffs, the box acknowledging the Terms & Conditions would not have been “prepopulated” with a check mark. (Ibid.) Plaintiff would have had to click that box to acknowledge assent to the CSA, due to a customer not being able to successfully activate Bluelink services through the Dealer-Assisted Enrollment process unless they complete the step requiring them to click the box acknowledging they agree to the Bluelink Terms and Conditions. (Ibid.)

 

The arbitration provision in the CSA provides:

 

Hyundai and you agree to arbitrate any and all disputes and claims between us arising out of or relating to this Agreement, Connected Services, Connected Services Systems, Service Plans, your Vehicle, use of the sites, or products, services, or programs you purchase, enroll in or seek product/service support for, whether you are a Visitor or Customer, via the sites or through mobile application, except any disputes or claims which under governing law are not subject to arbitration, to the maximum extent permitted by applicable law. This agreement to arbitrate is intended to be broadly interpreted and to make all disputes and claims between us subject to arbitration to the fullest extent permitted by law. […] The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to: claims based in contract, tort, warranty, statute, fraud, misrepresentation or any other legal theory; claims that arose before this or any prior Agreement (including, but not limited to, claims relating to advertising) […]

 

(Rao Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. 2, p. 15.)

 

The Court is not persuaded that Counsel Rao has personal knowledge that Plaintiff entered into the agreement. The Court sustains Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection. Defendant thus cannot establish mutual assent.


Further, the Court finds Defendant has not met its burden of proving that Plaintiff’s claims are covered under the Bluelink Connected Services Agreement. According to Defendant, the Bluelink system refers to a connected car system that includes various functions and features. (Rao Decl., ¶ 5.) It is unclear whether any of Plaintiff’s claims involve the Bluelink system. The Complaint does not mention the Bluelink system.

 

Accordingly, arbitration is not compelled under the Bluelink Connected Services Agreement.

 

Arbitration under the Owner’s Handbook and Warranty Information

 

The 2021 Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information (“Warranty Agreement”) provides the following:

 

“PLEASE READ THIS SECTION IN ITS ENTIRETY AS IT AFFECTS YOUR RIGHTS

 

If you purchased or leased your Hyundai vehicle in the State of California, you and we each agree that any claim or disputes between us (including between you and any of our affiliated companies) related to or arising out of your vehicle purchase, use of your vehicle, the vehicle warranty, representations in the warranty, or the duties contemplated under the warranty, including without limitation claims related to the failure to conform a vehicle to warranty, failure to repurchase or replace your vehicle, or claims for a refund or partial refund of your vehicle's purchase price (excluding personal injury claims), but excluding claims brought under the MagnusonMoss Warranty Act, shall be resolved by binding arbitration at either your or our election, even if the claim is initially filed in a court of law. If either you or we elect to resolve our dispute via arbitration (as opposed to in a court of law), such binding arbitration shall be administered by and through JAMS Mediation, Arbitration and ADR Services (JAMS) under its Streamlined Arbitration Rules & Procedures.

 

We will pay all JAMS fees for any arbitration except for the initial filing fee of $250. The arbitration will be held in the city or county of your residence. To learn more about arbitration, including how to commence arbitration, you may call any JAMS office or go to www.jamsadr.org.

 

This agreement to arbitrate is intended to be broadly interpreted and to make all disputes and claims between us (including our affiliated companies) relating to or arising out of your vehicle purchase, use of your vehicle, or the vehicle warranty subject to arbitration to the maximum extent permitted by law.

 

In any arbitration, the arbitrator shall be bound by the terms of this agreement and shall follow the applicable law. […]

 

Notwithstanding the above, either you may file a lawsuit in small claims court for any claims that otherwise require binding arbitration. This agreement evidences a transaction involving interstate commerce and shall be governed by the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. §§ 1-16. Judgment upon any award in arbitration may be entered in any court having jurisdiction.

 

IF YOU PURCHASED OR LEASED YOUR VEHICLE IN CALIFORNIA, YOUR WARRANTY IS MADE SUBJECT TO THE TERMS OF THIS BINDING ARBITRATION PROVISION. BY ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS. IF YOU DO NOT AGREE WITH THESE TERMS, PLEASE CONTACT US AT OPT-OUT@HMAUSA.COM WITHIN THIRTY (30) DAYS OF YOUR PURCHASE OR LEASE TO OPT-OUT OF THIS ARBITRATION PROVISION.”

 

(Ameripour Decl., Exh. 3, pp. 13-14.)

 

Defendant argues Plaintiff agreed to be bound when Plaintiff purchased the vehicle and subsequently presented the vehicle for repair under the Warranty Agreement. Defendant further argues that Plaintiff filed this claim based on the existence of this Warranty. Thus, the arbitration agreement applies to Plaintiff’s claims. Further, it is irrelevant that plaintiff did not sign the agreement. Defendant argues that the doctrine of equitable estoppel applies where Plaintiff took advantage of the benefits in the agreement but attempts to avoid an arbitration clause due to lack of signature.

 

Plaintiff argues the agreement is unenforceable because 1) the Warranty Agreement is not properly authenticated, 2) Plaintiff did not expressly assent to the Warranty Agreement. (Trias Decl., ¶¶ 4-6.) Plaintiff argues she never relied on the Warranty Agreement in making her claims. (Trias Decl., ¶ 7.) However, as mentioned above, Plaintiff does rely on the warranty in her Complaint: “These causes of action arise out of warranty and repair obligations of HYUNDAI MOTOR AMERICA in connection with a vehicle that Plaintiff purchased and for which HYUNDAI MOTOR AMERICA issued a written warranty. The warranty was not issued by the selling dealership.” (Compl., ¶ 8.)

 

In reply, Defendant argues that Code of Civil Procedure, section § 1281.2 only requires the court to make a finding of the arbitration agreement's existence, not an evidentiary determination of its validity, citing Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219.

 

The Court initially finds an agreement to arbitrate exists, overruling Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection. “For purposes of a petition to compel arbitration, it is not necessary to follow the normal procedures of document authentication. . .[a] court is only required to make a finding of the agreement's existence, not an evidentiary determination of its validity. (Condee v. Longwood Management Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218–219.) Further, Plaintiff has not introduced evidence of its invalidity or falsity. (See ibid.)

 

The Court agrees with Defendant that arbitration is proper under the doctrine of equitable estoppel. “[A] party is not entitled to make use of [a contract containing an arbitration clause] as long as it worked to [his or] her advantage, then attempt to avoid its application in defining the forum in which [his or] her dispute. . .should be resolved.” (Felisilda v. FCA US LLC (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 486, 496.) The doctrine of equitable estoppel is meant to “prevent a party from playing fast and loose with its commitment to arbitrate, honoring it when advantageous and circumventing it to gain undue advantage.” (Metalclad Corp. v. Ventana Environmental Organizational Partnership (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1705, 1714.) Here, Plaintiff relies on the existence of the warranty in order to maintain the causes of action alleged under the Song-Beverly Warranty Act and breach of warranty claims. Accordingly, the Court determines that Defendant has met its burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement in the Warranty which covers the subject of this lawsuit.

 

“[A] nonsignatory defendant may invoke an arbitration clause to compel a signatory plaintiff to arbitrate its claims when the causes of action against the nonsignatory are ‘intimately founded in and intertwined’ with the underlying contract obligations.” (Boucher v. Alliance Title Co., Inc. (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 262, 271.) “The fundamental point is that a party may not make use of a contract containing an arbitration clause and then attempt to avoid the duty to arbitrate by defining the forum in which the dispute will be resolved.” (Id. at p. 272.) Here, Plaintiff’s claims are clearly intertwined with the Warranty Agreement, as demonstrated in the Complaint. Plaintiff thus may not avoid the arbitration provision while reaping the benefits of the Agreement. The Court finds the doctrine of equitable estoppel applies.

 

Application of the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act

 

Plaintiff argues the SBA adopts the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act’s (MMWA) preclusion of binding arbitration of warranty disputes, citing Civil Code of Procedure, section 1793.1(a)(1). Plaintiff argues that a Federal Trade Commission (FTC) regulation under the MMWA, 16 C.F.R. § 703.5, subd. (j), states that binding mandatory arbitration of any warranty claim is unlawful, even if the warranty claims arise under State law. Plaintiffs cite federal court cases supporting its contention that the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act bars enforcement of binding arbitration of warranty disputes.

 

In reply, Defendant initially note that Plaintiff does not bring a claim under the MMWA and Plaintiff’s argument is misplaced. Defendant cites Walton v. Rose Mobile Homes LLC (5th Cir. 2002) 298 F.3d 470, 478-479 and Davis v. Southern Energy Homes, Inc. (11th Cir. 2002) 305 F.3d 1268, 1274-1280, which both held warranty claims under the MMWA may be subject to valid binding arbitration.

 

The Court need not resolve the conflict between the FAA and the MMWA. The parties cite federal court cases that are merely persuasive, and not binding on this Court. The Court agrees with Defendant that Plaintiff alleges no claims under the MMWA, and this argument is misplaced. Even though the SBA incorporates the MMWA and FTC regulations interpreting the MMWA, the Court in unaware of any California caselaw that precludes arbitration based on the aforementioned FTC regulation. The Court thus cannot deny the Motion to Compel based on this argument.

 

          Unconscionability

 

Unconscionability generally includes the absence of meaningful choice on the part of one of the parties together with contract terms that unreasonably favor the other party. (Carboni v. Arrospide (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 76, 82-83.) As the party asserting unconscionability, Plaintiff has the burden of proving both procedural and substantive unconscionability. (Crippen v. Central Valley RV Outlet. Inc. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1159, 1165). Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability. (Nyulassy v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1267, 1280-81.) Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form. (Ibid.) The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms. (Ibid.) An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied. (Stirlen v. Supercuts, Inc. (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1533.) “[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.” (Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, at 114.)

 

i.             Procedural Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the Warranty Agreement’s arbitration clause is procedurally unconscionable because 1) the Agreement was a contract of adhesion, in which Plaintiff had no opportunity to negotiate, and 2) Plaintiff was not made aware of the clause or the 30-day opt out period. (Trias Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff was thus subject to oppression and surprise, constituting procedural unconscionably.

 

Defendant argues that it had no duty to highlight the arbitration provision or the opt-put provision in the Warranty Agreement. Further, Defendant argues that Plaintiff was under no obligation to purchase the Subject Vehicle, and that market choice bears on whether an agreement is adhesive.

 

The Court agrees with Defendant that it had no duty to highlight the arbitration provision or the opt-put provision in the Warranty Agreement. The Supreme Court of California has “never required, as a prerequisite to finding procedural unconscionability, that the complaining party show it tried to negotiate standardized contract provisions.” (Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, 914.) A party is “under no obligation to highlight the arbitration clause of its contract, nor [is] it required to specifically call that clause to [the consumer’s] attention. Any state law imposing such an obligation would be preempted by the FAA. (Ibid.) The Court further finds that there is a low degree of procedural unconscionability where the arbitration provision was located on page 13 of the Warranty Agreement booklet, and where the arbitration provision is prominently displayed in bold lettering in the first section of the “Hyundai Warranty Information”. (Ameripour Decl., Ex. 3, pp. 12-14.) Therefore, Plaintiff has not established procedural unconscionability.

 

ii.            Substantive Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues the arbitration clause is substantively unconscionable because 1) it is one-sided in favor of the Defendant. Plaintiff argues that, when arbitration agreements require arbitration of claims that are “realistically” only brought by one of the parties to the agreement against the other, they are substantively unconscionable as being “one-sided and harsh,” citing employment cases. Plaintiff argues that Defendant issued the warranty, and warranties are effectively one-sided promises extended by the seller to the buyer. Thus, it would never be the case that Plaintiff could compel arbitration against Defendant. The arbitration clause is thus one-sided in favor of Defendant. Plaintiff argues elements of the CSA agreement are unconscionable, but the Court need not address these arguments where it has been held the CSA does not apply to Plaintiff’s claims.

 

Defendant argues that California public policy favors arbitration. Defendant seems to address an argument that plaintiff does not make—specifically, that the arbitration clause is unconscionable with respect to discovery rules in the arbitration. The Court also notes Defendant discusses the American Arbitration Association Rules when the arbitration clause requires the parties to use JAMS. Defendant does not otherwise address Plaintiff’s arguments.

 

While the Court is empathetic to Plaintiff’s argument regarding the one-sided nature of warranties, the Court is not persuaded that such rationale applies in this context. As Plaintiff acknowledges, this rationale has “not been addressed by courts in the context of Song-Beverly Act warranty claims,” and has been mainly applied in employment claims. (Opp., p. 18, lines 1-2.) The Court also finds the arbitration clause expressly provides an opt-out provision, indicating a lack of substantive unconscionability. Therefore, the Court finds the arbitration provision is not substantively unconscionable.

 

Thus, Plaintiff has not met his burden of establishing both procedural and substantive unconscionability. Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED. The case is ordered stayed pending binding arbitration as to the entire action.

Plaintiff Samantha Ruiz’s Motion to Compel Further is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is DENIED.

This is a lemon law action. On July 10, 2023, Plaintiff Samantha Ruiz (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. The FAC alleges the following causes of action: 1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act-Breach of Express Warranty, 2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act-Breach of Implied Warranty, 3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act section 1793.2, and (4) Negligent Repair.

 

On December 29, 2023, Plaintiff propounded on VGA its Requests for Production (Set One). (Thomas Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A; Paeng Decl., ¶ 4.) VGA provided verified responses and documents in response on January 30, 2023. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. B; Paeng Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. B.) On March 8, 2024,¿Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to VGA, outlining the deficiencies in its responses to the Requests for Production (Set One). (Thomas Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. C; Paeng Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. C.) On March 13, 2024, Defendant responded to Plaintiff’s letter, refusing to respond to certain requests, including Nos. 45-46. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. D.)  In March and April 2024, the parties continued to meet and confer, through which some discovery issues were resolved—yet the issue regarding Request Nos. 45-46 remained. (Thomas Decl.,  ¶ 11, Exh. E). On April 15, 2024, the parties met and conferred telephonically—the parties agreed VGA would produce certain discovery, but that VGA would follow up with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding supplementing its responses to Request Nos. 45 and 46, among others. On July 23, 2024, this matter was transferred to new counsel for VGA. (Paeng Decl., ¶ 10.) On December 27, 2024, VGA’s counsel informed Plaintiff’s counsel that VGA will produce documents in response to Request Nos. 45-46 and requested to Plaintiff to withdraw the Motion. (Paeng Decl., ¶ 10; Exh. E.) Plaintiff declined to withdraw the Motion. (Ibid.)

 

Plaintiff now moves to compel further responses from VGA to the Requests for Production (Set One), Nos. 45-46, which relate to documents showing Defendant’s knowledge of regarding and warranty repairs performed on vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the Subject Vehicle. Plaintiff also requests sanctions.

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP Section 2031.310 allows a party who has requested production of documents and received unsatisfactory responses to move for an order compelling further responses. A motion under this section must (1) set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand and (2) be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b).) CRC Rule 3.1345 requires the moving party to file a separate statement showing good cause.

 

To establish good cause, the burden is on the moving party to show: (1) relevance to the subject matter and (2) specific facts justifying discovery. (Glenfed Develop. Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (National Union Fire Ins. Co. of Pittsburgh, Penn.) (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117; see also Kirkland v. Sup. Ct. (Guess?, Inc.) (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion¿to compel further responses to requests for production “shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under section 2016.040.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(2).)¿ The declaration must state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented in the motion.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.) On March 8, 2024,¿Plaintiff’s counsel sent a meet and confer letter to VGA, outlining the deficiencies in its responses to the Requests for Production (Set One). (Thomas Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. C; Paeng Decl., ¶ 6, Exh. C.) On March 13, 2024, VGA responded to Plaintiff’s letter, refusing to respond to certain requests, including Nos. 45-46. (Thomas Decl., ¶ 9, Exh. D.) In March and April 2024, the parties met and conferred via email and telephone, through which some discovery issues were resolved—yet the issue regarding Request Nos. 45-46 remained. (Thomas Decl.,  ¶ 11, Exh. E). On April 15, 2024, the parties agreed that VGA would follow up regarding supplementing its responses to Request Nos. 45-46. (Paeng Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. D, p. 2.) On May 6, 2024, VGA served supplemental responses and documents responsive to certain requests but did not address Request Nos. 45-46. (Thomas Decl. ¶ 12, Exh. E). Plaintiff filed this Motion two days after VGA’s supplemental responses. (Paeng Decl. ¶  9.) The Court finds the parties have met extensively regarding discovery, but that Plaintiff prematurely filed this Motion where VGA indicated it would follow up regarding Request Nos. 45-46.

 

Discussion

 

The following requests for production are at issue:

 

Request for Production No. 45: All DOCUMENTS evidencing complaints by owners of 2021 Volkswagen JETTA CITY vehicles regarding any of the complaints that the SUBJECT VEHICLE was presented to YOUR or YOUR authorized repair facilities for repair during the warranty period.

 

Request for Production No. 46: All DOCUMENTS evidencing warranty repairs to 2021 Volkswagen JETTA CITY, vehicles regarding any of the components that YOU or YOUR authorized repair facilities performed repairs on under warranty.

 

Plaintiff argues that VGA’s objections regarding overbreadth, vagueness and ambiguity, and privilege are without merit because they are stated generally. VGA’s objections pertaining to trade secrets and third-party privacy can be mitigated through the Stipulation and Protective Order in place, and through redaction. Plaintiff argues VGA’s objection to MacQuiddy v. Mercedes-Benz USA (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1045-46 is misguided because in that case, the VGA has already admitted it failed to comply with the Song-Beverly Act, and the court held plaintiff’s discovery requests were only “peripherally related” to undisputed issues. Here, VGA has not admitted fault, and Plaintiff’s requests are relevant to disputed issues. Thus, MacQuiddy does not apply. Plaintiff argues that the discovery sought by Requests for Production Nos. 45-46 are relevant because they seek information regarding VGA’s knowledge of consumer complaints regarding and warranty repairs performed on vehicles of the same year, make, and model as the Subject Vehicle. Plaintiff argues information regarding other vehicles is routinely sought in Song-Beverly Act cases to show whether VGA willfully chose not to disclose the Subject Vehicle’s defects.

 

In opposition, VGA argues that Plaintiff has failed to establish good cause for further responses. VGA argues that discovery regarding complaints and repairs of other vehicles is irrelevant to the question of whether Plaintiff is entitled to relief under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. VGA further argues that the Court should limit discovery based on the amount in controversy, which is relatively low.

 

VGA further argues that it has continued to meet and confer with Plaintiff and has provided supplemental responses. VGA argues Plaintiff demonstrates no good cause or any right to the discovery at issue. Yet VGA further argues that the Motion is moot because, “VWGoA (VGA) will have served supplemental responses and produced supplemental documents that are responsive to RFP Nos. 45 and 46” prior to the hearing. (Mot, p. 4, lines 26-28; Paeng Decl., ¶ 11.)

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues that, as of January 7, 2025, VGA has not served responses. Plaintiff argues that VGA’s substantive arguments in opposition should not be considered because VGA claims to have already produced these documents. The arguments should thus be considered waived.

 

The Court is hesitant to find this Motion moot where VGA has not yet filed proof that responses were served to Request for Production Nos. 45-46 prior to this hearing. The Court finds Plaintiff has showed good cause for producing such documents. Considering VGA has claimed it will have produced the documents prior to this hearing, the Court finds VGA is amenable to producing such documents. The Court thus agrees with Plaintiff that any substantive arguments opposing the Motion to Compel Further have been waived. (See Paeng Decl., ¶ 11.)

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production Nos. 45-46.


Sanctions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310 subdivision (h) states, “[T]he court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”

 

The Court acknowledges Plaintiff’s argument that sanctions should be awarded where VGA has delayed in providing the discovery and filed an unsuccessful opposition. But the Court declines to award sanctions where Plaintiff hastily filed the Motion without allowing VGA to respond regarding Request for Production Nos. 45-46. (See Paeng Decl., ¶¶ 7-10.) On April 15, 2024, the parties agreed that VGA would follow up regarding supplementing its responses to Request Nos. 45-46. (Paeng Decl., ¶ 7, Exh. D, p. 2.) On May 6, 2024, VGA served supplemental responses and documents responsive to certain requests but did not address Request Nos. 45-46. (Thomas Decl. ¶ 12, Exh. E). The Court finds Plaintiff filed this Motion soon after VGA provided supplemental responses without further attempt to meet and confer as to Request Nos. 45-46. Under these circumstances, the Court finds an award of sanctions would be unjust.

Background

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 377.31 provides that the decedent’s personal representative or, if none, the decedent’s successor in interest may continue a decedent’s pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.30; see Adams v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 71, 78-79.) A successor in interest is the beneficiary of the decedent’s estate or other successor in interest who succeeds to a cause of action or to a particular item of the property that is the subject of the cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.11.) Section 377.33 provides that the court in which an action is continued may make any order concerning parties that is appropriate to ensure proper administration of justice, including the appointment of the decedent’s successor in interest as a special administrator or guardian ad litem.¿¿ 

¿ 

Section 377.32 provides that a person who seeks to commence such an action as the decedent’s successor in interest must file an affidavit or declaration providing certain information, including the decedent’s name, date and place of decedent’s death, and statements regarding whether the estate has been administered and that the affiant or declarant is the successor in interest on decedent’s claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (a).) A certified copy of the decedent’s death certificate must also be attached to the affidavit or declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 377.32, subd. (c).)¿

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff Marielena Cazarez submits a declaration in support of this motion stating the following:

4.    August J. Gomez is Decedent’s successor-in-interest, as defined in Code of Civil Procedure section 377.1, and Marilena Cazarez is the Guardian ad litem of August J. Gomez. (Cazarez Decl., ¶ 6.) Decedent died without a will. (Cazarez Decl., ¶ 6.)

5.    No other person has a superior right to commence the action or proceeding or to be substituted for the decedent on the pending action or proceeding. (Cazarez Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

A certified copy of Decedent’s death certificate is attached to the declaration. (Cazarez Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 1.)

 

The Court finds that Decedent died intestate and was unmarried at the time of his death. (Cazarez Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. 1.) Under Probate Code section 6402, subdivision (a), August J. Gomez qualifies as Decedent’s successor-in-interest as his son.

 

Having met all the requirements set forth in Code Civ. Proc., § 377.11, Plaintiffs’ Motion to Substitute Marielena Cazarez ad litem for August J. Gomez, in place of deceased Plaintiff, Gustabo Gomez, is granted.¿¿ 


Case Number: 24NWCV00580    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: C

SOLIS v. DAVIS, ET AL.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00580

HEARING:  January 14, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#11

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Hope Vega’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is GRANTED, on the condition that Defendant pays for Plaintiff’s arbitration fees.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a legal malpractice action. On February 23, 2024, Plaintiff Sara Solis (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Vincent W. Davis and Joyce Hope Vega (“Defendants”). Defendants substituted in as Plaintiff’s counsel in a separate proceeding in 2019. (Complaint, ¶ 25.) Plaintiff alleges that during that proceeding, Defendants sought multiple continuances of trial extending the five-year statute deadline and failed to make timely filings ultimately leading to the Court dismissing Plaintiff’s complaint with prejudice. (Complaint, ¶¶ 38-55.) The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: (1) Professional Negligence, (2) Breach of Contract, (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (4) Conversion.

 

Both Defendants filed Motions to Compel Arbitration. On December 18, 2024, the Court heard Defendant Davis’ Motion. The Court ruled that Defendant could elect to pay Plaintiff’s share of the arbitration fees or waive the right to arbitrate Plaintiff’s claim. (See 12/18/24 Minute Order.)

 

Defendant Hope Vega (“Defendant”) now moves to compel arbitration.

 

Legal Standard

 

Parties may be compelled to arbitrate a dispute upon the court finding that: (1) there was a valid agreement to arbitrate between the parties; and (2) said agreement covers the controversy or controversies in the parties’ dispute.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2; see also Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004)¿118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.) A party petitioning to compel arbitration has the burden of establishing the existence of a valid agreement to arbitrate and the party opposing the petition has the burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to its defense. (Banner Entertainment, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1998) 62 Cal.App.4th 348, 356-57.)¿ 

 

¿“If a court of competent jurisdiction. . .has ordered arbitration of a controversy which is an issue involved in an action or proceeding pending before a court of this State, the court in which such action or proceeding is pending shall, upon motion of a party to such action or proceeding, stay the action or proceeding until an arbitration is had in accordance with the order to arbitrate or until such earlier time as the court specifies.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.4.)¿¿ 

 

Discussion

 

          Valid Agreement

 

The Court first addresses the issue of whether Defendant, as a non-signatory, can enforce the arbitration agreement against Plaintiff. Defendant argues that the equitable estoppel doctrine applies where Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant Hope Vega are inextricably intertwined with her claims against Defendant Davis. Defendant is an associate attorney for Defendant Davis and worked on the case at the center of the legal malpractice claim. (Hope Vega Decl., ¶¶ 3, 5.) Plaintiff does not dispute this argument in the opposition.

 

The Court finds that Defendant can enforce the arbitration agreement as a non-signatory based on equitable estoppel doctrine. The equitable estoppel exception applies where “the causes of action against the nonsignatory are intimately founded in and intertwined with the underlying contract obligations” and “where the claims are based on the same facts and are inherently inseparable from the arbitrable claims against signatory defendants.” (Garcia v. Pexco, LLC (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 782, 786.) Here, Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant Hope Vega are intertwined with Plaintiff’s claims against Defendant Davis, the signatory to the arbitration agreement. Defendant Hope Vega is an associate attorney for Defendant Davis and worked on the case at the center of the legal malpractice claim. (Hope Vega Decl., ¶¶ 3, 5.) The Court finds the claims concern common issue of fact and law. Defendant Hope Vega may thus enforce the arbitration agreement as a non-signatory.

 

Under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, Arbitration Clauses are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.) The party moving to compel arbitration must establish the existence of a written arbitration agreement between the parties. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 1281.2.) In ruling on a motion to compel arbitration, the court must first determine whether the parties actually agreed to arbitrate the dispute, and general principles of California contract law help guide the court in making this determination. (Mendez v. Mid-Wilshire Health Care Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 534, 541.)

 

“With respect to the moving party’s burden to provide evidence of the¿existence¿of an agreement to arbitrate, it is generally sufficient for that party to present a copy of the contract to the court. (See¿Condee v. Longwood Management Corp.¿(2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 218; see also Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“A petition to compel arbitration or to stay proceedings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.2 and 1281.4 must state, in addition to other required allegations, the provisions of the written agreement and the paragraph that provides for arbitration. The provisions must be stated verbatim, or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference”].) Once such a document is presented to the court, the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges. [Citation.]” (Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC¿(2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.)

 

Here, Defendant attached the Attorney-Client Fee Agreement (“Agreement”) entered between Plaintiff and Defendant Davis on September 30, 2019. (Hope Vega Decl., ¶ 4, Exh. A.) The Agreement contains an arbitration provision in section 11 as follows:

 

11. ARBITRATION OF FEE OR OTHER DISPUTES:

 

11.1 Any dispute between Client and Attorney as to the reasonable value of services under this Agreement shall be arbitrated under the provisions of the State Bar Fee Arbitration Program by the Los Angeles County Fee Arbitration Program.

 

1.2 Any dispute (other than one concerning legal fees only), including, but not limited set forth in section 6200-6206 of the California Business and Professions Code, disputes as to the quality of Attorneys’ services, breach of fiduciary duties, fraud or any other aspect of the attorney-client relationship, will be determined by submission to binding arbitration as provided by California law, and not by a lawsuit or resort to court process, except as California law provides for judicial review or arbitration proceedings. Client may also have the right, under California law to mandatory nonbinding arbitration before a binding arbitration. If Client is entitled to and asks for, nonbinding arbitration, and Client and/or Attorney is/are unhappy with the result, it will be followed by binding arbitration. All parties to this Agreement, by entering into it, are giving up their rights to have any dispute decided in a court of law before a judge or jury and instead, are accepting the use of arbitration

11.3 By signing this Agreement, Client is consenting to binding arbitration of any and all disputes.

 

The Court finds Defendant has proven the existence of a valid, signed arbitration agreement with Plaintiff that covers the claims in dispute. In opposition, Plaintiff does not dispute the existence of an arbitration agreement. Rather, Plaintiff argues that due to her limited financial means, this agreement is unconscionable and unenforceable unless Defendants pay all arbitration fees.

 

Unconscionability

 

Plaintiff argues that the Agreement is unconscionable because it denies her of a forum to litigate her claims. In support of this argument, Plaintiff relies on Roldan v. Callahan & Blaine (2013) 219 Cal.App.4th 87 and Aronow v. Superior Court (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 865. In Roldan, the California Court of Appeals reviewed a trial court decision to deny a motion seeking an order to compel a defendant to advance the upfront cost of an arbitration forum. (Roldan, 219 Cal.App.4th at p. 89.) The Roldan court declined to reach the issue of whether arbitration agreements are unenforceable based on a plaintiff’s ability to pay but concluded that plaintiffs who were granted permission to proceed in forma pauperis in trial court can be excluded from the obligation to pay arbitration fees. (Id. at 95.) Therefore, the court gave the defendant a choice—“if the trial court determines that any of these plaintiffs is unable to share in the cost of the arbitration, [defendant] can elect to either pay that plaintiff's share of the arbitration cost and remain in arbitration or waive its right to arbitrate that plaintiff's claim.” (Id. at 96.) In reaching this conclusion, the court reasoned “to rule otherwise might effectively deprive [plaintiffs] of access to any forum for resolution of their claims against [defendant].” (Ibid.)

 

The Court of Appeals in Aronow affirmed Roldan, finding that a trial court should determine the issue of arbitrator fee payment before commencement of arbitration, and, in the situation plaintiff is unable to pay, to offer defendant to either pay costs or waive the right to arbitrate. (Aronow, supra, 76 Cal.App.5th at p. 884-86.) The Court in Aronow suggested a fee waiver request could function to determine a Plaintiff’s ability to pay arbitration costs:

 

“In response to a petition to compel arbitration, a litigant who claims financial inability to pay the arbitrator's fee could submit the Judicial Council application form and the financial statement prescribed by Government Code section 68633, subdivisions (c)(1) and (c)(2). The court may delegate to the clerk the authority to grant applications for an initial fee waiver that meet the standards of eligibility and application requirements set forth in Sections 68632 and 68633. (Gov. Code, § 68634, subd. (d).) Where a party seeking relief from paying the arbitrator's fee is one of the persons enumerated in Government Code section 68632’s list of persons eligible to proceed without paying court fees and costs, the decision would be ministerial.”

 

(Id. at 884.)(Internal citations omitted.)

 

Here, Plaintiff is not opposed to arbitration and only takes issue with the associated fees. Plaintiff has already submitted the Judicial Council application, and this Court granted a fee waiver based on Plaintiff’s economic status. (Request to Waive Court Fees, 02/23/2024.) Prior to filing this case, Plaintiff’s counsel requested Defendants pay the arbitration fees, but did not receive a response. (Moore Decl., ¶ 2; Exh. 1.) Plaintiff is willing to submit to arbitration if Defendants agree to pay the costs. (Moore Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

In reply, Defendant argues that Plaintiff is required to pay her pro rate share of the arbitration fees. In the alternative, Defendant argues Plaintiff should seek a fee waiver for arbitration.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff is not opposed to arbitration but is opposed to paying her share of fees due to her indigent status. The Court grants Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration, on the condition that Defendant pays for arbitration fees.


Case Number: 24NWCV00620    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: C

FERRILL v. NEWSOM

CASE NO.: 24NWCV00620

HEARING:  January 14, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a quiet title action. On February 28, 2024, Plaintiff Patrick S. Ferrill filed this action against Defendant Terri Newsom as personal representative of the Estate of Karrinne Dodd (“Decedent”) and Does 1 to 10. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff recently found and recorded a Quit Claim Deed, dated February 13, 2013, that granted the Decedent’s residential property located at 15406 Dumont Ave, Norwalk, CA 90650, legally designated as LOT 96 OF TRACT NO. 16224, IN THE CITY OF NORWALK, COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES, STATE OF CALIFORNIA, AS PER MAP RECORDED IN BOOK 388, PAGE 6-12, IN THE OFFICE OF THE COUNTY RECORDER OF SAID COUNTY (“Subject Property”) to Plaintiff. The Complaint alleges the Estate of Decedent is currently being probated according to a fraudulent will in Probate Case No. 21STPB08875. The Complaint alleges one cause of action to quite title to the Subject Property in favor of Plaintiff.

 

On March 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for preliminary injunction restraining Defendant from selling, disposing of, interfering, or committing waste upon the Subject Property. On March 14, 2024, the Court denied the ex parte application, finding that the Probate Court has complete jurisdiction over claims to property of the estate. (See 3/14/24 Minute Order.) In response to Plaintiff’s argument that the matter should be heard in this Court because the Probate Court found Plaintiff had no standing, this Court found Plaintiff’s only remedy is to appeal that decision, not to file an action in this Court. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant now demurs to the Complaint based on the grounds that the Court has no subject matter jurisdiction over the action to quiet title.

 

 

 

 

Legal Standard

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

  

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.)

 

Judicial Notice

 

The Court grants Defendant’s Request for Judicial Notice of the Probate Court action, entitled Estate of Karrinne Dodd, Case No. 21STPB08875 (“Probate Action”).

 

Meet and Confer

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a) requires the parties to meet and confer “in person or by telephone.” Defense counsel did not address meet and confer efforts in the declaration in support of the Demurrer. Although the Court finds the meet and confer requirement has not been met, the Court will assess the merits of the Demurrer in interests of judicial efficiency.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant argues, in line with the Court’s March 13, 2024 Minute Order, that this Court has no jurisdiction over this action to quiet title. Defendant argues that the Probate Action was filed on September 13, 2021, before this action. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 1.) On December 15, 2023, the Probate Court granted an order confirming sale of the Subject Property. (Orloff Decl., ¶ 1, Exh. A.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the probate court did not have jurisdiction over the property because it transferred to Plaintiff by deed in 2013, as alleged in the Complaint. (Compl., ¶ 12, Exh. A.) Plaintiff argues the probate court only has jurisdiction limited to property that is part of the estate at the time of decedent’s death. Plaintiff argues that the Probate Court has determined Plaintiff does not have standing, but that Plaintiff is not barred from seeking equitable relief in Civil Court.

 

In reply, Defendant objects on the grounds that the opposition is untimely—the opposition was due on December 31, 2024, but was filed on January 2, 2025. Defendant argues that the issue of whether the Subject Property was transferred in 2013 should properly be heard by the Probate Court, because such Court has jurisdiction to determine whether real property is part of an estate. Plaintiff should seek relief from the Probate Court, not from this Court.

 

“It is the general rule in California that the superior court sitting in probate has exclusive jurisdiction to determine the persons and share that each has in the estate of a decedent.” (Howard v. Bennett (1942) 53 Cal.App.2d 546, 548.) The Probate Court has complete and exclusive jurisdiction over property of the estate, all persons claiming interests in the decedent’s estate, and all matters involved in a complete and effective administration of the estate, which include the probate of wills. (See Prob. Code, § 7050; see also Morrison v. Kaufman (1945) 68 Cal.App.2d 280, 281; Colden v. Costello (1942) 50 Cal.App.2d 363, 370.)

 

The Court sustains the demurrer on the grounds that this Court has no jurisdiction over this matter under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (a). The Probate Court has exclusive jurisdiction over claims of interest in the decedent’s estate. (See Prob. Code, § 7050.)

 

Consistent with the Court’s decision on Plaintiff’s ex parte application, the Court finds the only appropriate remedy for Plaintiff is to seek appellate relief from the Probate Court’s decision that Plaintiff lacked standing, not to file a new action in this Court. (See 3/14/24 Minute Order.)

 

Accordingly, the Court sustains Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint without leave to amend.


Case Number: 24NWCV02787    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: C

SIMIN LP v. WELLS INTERNATIONAL MARKETING GROUP, INC.

CASE NO.: 24NWCV02787

HEARING:  January 14, 2024 @ 10:30 a.m.

JUDGE:  RAUL A. SAHAGUN

 

#12

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Wells International Marketing Group, Inc.’s Demurrer to the FAC is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is an unlawful detainer action. On August 27, 2024, Plaintiff Simin LP (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendant Wells International Marketing Group, Inc. (“Defendant”).

 

On November 13, 2024, the Court heard Defendant’s Demurrer to the Complaint. The main issue was whether the Complaint properly alleged service of the Three-Day Notice (“First Notice”). The Court found that while Notice was not defective on its face, the Complaint failed to allege proper service in accordance with either the terms of the lease of Code of Civil Procedure 1162, subdivision (b)(3). The Court sustained the Demurrer with leave to amend within 10 days.

 

On November 22, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC). The FAC was amended to allege facts stating service of the First Notice was proper under Code of Civil Procedure 1162, subdivision (b)(3) (i.e., the First Notice was posted on the property and send via regular mail). (FAC, ¶¶ 9(a), (b), Exhs. 2-3.) The FAC also contained allegations regarding second Three-Day Notice served on November 15, 2024 (“Second Notice”). (FAC, ¶¶ 9(c)-(e), Exhs. 4-6.) The Second Notice was served in the same manner as the First Notice and was also sent via overnight mail. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant now demurs to the FAC on the grounds that it fails to allege fact sufficient to state a cause of action for unlawful detainer and is uncertain.

 

On December 12, 2024, Plaintiff applied ex parte to advance the hearing on the Demurrer and requested for application to be treated as the opposition. The Court granted the application and advanced the hearing to today’s date.

 

 

 

Legal Standard

 

As a general matter, in a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  

  

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245.) 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1161, subdivision (2), a tenant is guilty of unlawful detainer under the following circumstances:

 

“When the tenant continues in possession, in person or by subtenant, without the permission of the landlord, or the successor in estate of the landlord, if applicable, after default in the payment of rent, pursuant to the lease or agreement under which the property is held, and three days’ notice, excluding Saturdays and Sundays and other judicial holidays, in writing, requiring its payment, stating the amount that is due, the name, telephone number, and address of the person to whom the rent payment shall be made, and, if payment may be made personally, the usual days and hours that person will be available to receive the payment. . .”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 1161, subd. (2).)

 

Accordingly, the basic elements of unlawful detainer for nonpayment of rent are the following: 1) the tenant is in possession of the premises, 2) that possession is without permission, 3) the tenant is in default for nonpayment of rent, 4) the tenant has been properly served with a written three-day notice, and (5) the default continues after the three-day notice period has elapsed. (KB Salt Lake III, LLC v. Fitness Internat., LLC (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 1032, 1045.)

 

Meet and Confer

 

The meet and confer requirement for demurrers does not apply to unlawful detainer proceedings. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (d)(2).)

 

Discussion

 

Defendant’s primary argument is that the FAC fails to state a claim because it contains no allegations that it served a valid three-day notice prior to the filing of this lawsuit. The First Notice was served on Defendant by posting a copy at the Property and then mailing it to Defendant via regular mail. The First Notice was not served properly according to the Lease, which requires “all notices required or permitted by this lease or applicable law shall be in writing and may be delivered in person or may be sent by overnight delivery service guaranteeing next day delivery.” (Paragraph 23 of the lease agreement, attached to FAC, Exh. 1).The Second Notice was served after, the original Complaint was filed—thus, Plaintiff cannot rely on the Second Notice because the facts necessary for an unlawful detainer claim must occur prior to the filing of the lawsuit. Further, Defendant argues that Plaintiff cannot rely on the First Notice because it was expressly canceled out by the language of the Second Notice, which states, “You are further notified that this Notice supersedes all Notices to Pay Rent or Surrender Possession of Premises or other default notices dated prior to the date of this Notice with respect to the above-referenced Premises, but this Notice is served in conjunction with and in addition to any and all notices of same date.” (FAC, Exh. 4.)

 

Defendant further argues that the FAC is uncertain and ambiguous as to which three-day notice the action was filed upon. Lastly, Defendant’s argue the FAC is uncertain because it asserts a breach of contract claim for compensatory damages, which are not available in an action for unlawful detainer.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendant cites no legal authority to support its contention that the Second Notice cannot be grounds for the FAC. Plaintiff argues the Amended Complaint was filed more than three days after service of the Second Notice and is thus not premature. Plaintiff further argues the FAC adequately alleges the method of service for each notice—the First Notice complies with Code of Civil Procedure section 1162, subdivision (b)(3), and the Second Notice complied with Code of Civil Procedure section 1162, subdivision (b)(3) and the Lease. Further, Plaintiff does not seek to allege a separate claim for breach of contract. Moreover, the remedies Plaintiff seeks—recovery of possession, past-due rent, forfeiture of the lease, and damages related to the tenant's holding over—are consistent with unlawful detainer action.

 

In reply, Defendant argue that a valid three-day notice is a prerequisite to filing an unlawful detainer action. Defendant reiterates that Plaintiff’s amended allegations regarding the Second Notice could not have grounded the first complaint because they did not occur when the complaint was originally filed.

 

The Court will discuss the following issues: 1) whether the notices were properly served and whether the FAC may rely upon the Second Notice, 2) whether the FAC is uncertain, and 3) whether the FAC improperly asserts a breach of contract claim.

 

Breach of Contract Claim

 

The Court initially finds that the FAC does not assert a separate breach of contract claim. The FAC only seeks recovery of possession, past-due rent, forfeiture of the lease, and damages related to the tenant's holding over. (FAC, ¶ 16.) The Court finds such remedies are awardable in an unlawful detainer suit. The Court may not sustain the demurrer on these grounds.

 

Service of the Notices and Reliance on the Second Notice

 

The parties agreed an alternative notice requirement via the lease agreement. In commercial tenancies, parties can “lawfully agree to notice requirements different from. . .those contained in [the Code of Civil Procedure].” (Folberg v. Clara G.R. Kinney Co. (1980) 104 Cal.App.3d 136, 140.) Here, the lease agreement provides that “[a]ll notices required or permitted by this [l]ease or applicable law shall be in writing and may be delivered in person (by hand or by courier) or may be sent by overnight delivery service guaranteeing next day delivery, with postage prepaid, and shall be deemed sufficiently given if served in a manner specified in this Paragraph 23.” (Compl., Exh. 1 at ¶ 23.1.)

 

As to the First Notice, the Court finds that service did not comply with the lease agreement. The First Notice was served on Defendant by posting a copy at the Property and then mailing it to Defendant via regular mail. (See FAC, ¶ 9(a)-(b), Exh. 1.) Plaintiff admits that the First Notice complied with Code of Civil Procedure section 1162, subdivision (b)(3) but not the lease agreement, which requires notice to be served by “overnight delivery service” and not regular mail. (See Opp. p. 10, line 2.) The Court finds the First Notice was improperly served and thus, cannot serve as the basis for the FAC.

 

The next issue is whether Plaintiff can rely on the Second Notice, which was served after this action was filed and alleged in the FAC. The Court initially finds that the Second Notice was properly served in accordance with the lease agreement—it was posted at the property and mailed via regular and overnight mail. (FAC, ¶ 9(c)-(d).)

 

While service was proper, the Court finds that Plaintiff may not rely on the Second Notice in the FAC. Plaintiff argues that the FAC, as an amended Complaint, supersedes all prior complaints—thus, Plaintiff can rely on the Second Notice as properly alleged in the FAC. The Court disagrees. Because Plaintiff filed a new three-day notice after the original Complaint was filed, the FAC is technically not an amended pleading but a “supplemental pleading” which alleges facts occurring after the original pleading was filed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 464, subd. (a); see also California Farm & Fruit Co. v. Schiappa-Pietra (1907) 151 Cal. 732, 742-743 (“The complaint, whether original or amended, can properly speak only of things which occurred either before or concurrently with the commencement of the action. But the office of the supplemental complaint is to bring to notice things occurring after the commencement of the action. . .”) (superseded by statute on unrelated issue).) Case law provides that a supplemental pleading cannot cure a premature lawsuit. (Radar v. Rogers (1957) 49 Cal.2d 243, 247 (“The general rule is that where an action is prematurely brought, and the original complaint must fall, a supplemental complaint has no place as a pleading.”).) Further, a landlord generally need not file a supplemental unlawful detainer complaint against an original defendant since the facts necessary to support eviction must have occurred prior to filing of the UD complaint. (See Friedman, et al., Cal. Practice Guide: Landlord —Tenant (The Rutter Group 2024), ¶ 8:140.1.) Here, because the original Complaint was premised on an improperly served three-day notice, the Complaint was prematurely brought. Plaintiff cannot rely on a supplemental complaint to “aid an original complaint which was filed before a cause of action had arisen.” (Ibid.)

 

The Court sustains the Demurrer based on Plaintiff’s misguided reliance on a supplemental pleading to “cure” the premature lawsuit, where the First Notice was defective and the facts necessary to support the action must have occurred prior to filing of the complaint.

 

          Uncertainty

 

Defendants further demur on the grounds that the FAC is uncertain as to which notice Plaintiff is relying on. On the face of the FAC, Plaintiff relies on both the First and Second Notices—the opposition confirms this. (Opp., p. 9, lines 23-24.) Yet Defendant points out that the First Notice was explicitly withdrawn by the language of the Second Notice, which states the following: “You are further notified that this Notice supersedes all Notices to Pay Rent or Surrender Possession of Premises or other default notices dated prior to the date of this Notice with respect to the above-referenced Premises.” (FAC, Exh. 4.)

 

Where there are inconsistencies between the notice attached to the complaint and allegations in the complaint, the complaint may be subject to special demurrer for uncertainty or general demurrer for failure to state a cause of action. (See Dodd v. Citizens Bank of Costa Mesa (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1624, 1627; Friedman, et al., Cal. Practice Guide: Landlord —Tenant (The Rutter Group 2024), ¶ 8:225.5.) Further, “facts appearing in exhibits attached to the complaint will also be accepted as true and, if contrary to the allegations in the pleading, will be given precedence. (Dodd, 222 Cal.App.3d at p. 1627.)

 

The Court finds that the Second Notice, which states that it supersedes the First Notice, must be taken as true. The Court agrees with Defendant that the language in the Second Notice is inconsistent with the allegations in the FAC, and thus, a demurrer lies. Further, as discussed earlier, the FAC may not rely on the Second Notice to cure a defect in the First Notice.

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Court sustains the Demurrer to the FAC without leave to amend.
