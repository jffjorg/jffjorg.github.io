DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV05062    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

VIG PRIVATE LENDING

 

         vs.

 

ILAN KENIG

 

 Case No.:  24STCV05062  

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 23, 2024

 

Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

 

On 2/29/2024, Plaintiff VIG Private Lending, Inc (Plaintiff) filed an unlawful detainer action against Defendant Avivit Kenig (Defendant). On 4/24/2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended unlawful detainer complaint. (FAC).

 

            On 8/30/2024, Plaintiff moves for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of its Complaint.

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff moves for summary judgment, or alternatively, summary adjudication, of the following issues:

 

1.      Plaintiff is the owner of the property at 1355 Berea Place, Pacific Palisades, CA 90272, County of Los Angeles (the “Property”);

 

2.      Plaintiff properly served Defendants with a valid notice to quit on March 12, 2024; and

 

3.      The daily fair market value of the property at the Property is at least $1,000.00.

 

Plaintiff argues that it is entitled to judgment because there are no materials facts in dispute. More specifically, Plaintiff argues that neither title, service of Notice, market value, or affirmative defenses are in dispute here.

 

As for title, where, as here, the unlawful detainer action is brought under CCP section 1161a, a plaintiff must prove his acquisitions of title by purchase at the sale. Under CCP § 1161a, unlawful detainer lies “(3) Where the property has been sold in accordance with Section 2924 of the Civil Code, under a power of sale contained in a deed of trust executed by such person, or a person under whom such person claims, and the title under the sale has been duly perfected.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1161a.) Thus, the only issue before this court is compliance with Section 2924. No other section is relevant for the purposes of determining immediate right to possession in this action.

 

In a nonjudicial foreclosure sale, a trustee exercises a power of sale contained in a deed of trust. The purchaser at a nonjudicial foreclosure sale takes title by a trustee’s deed. (Nguyen v. Calhoun (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 428, 441.) When the deed that the trustee delivers to the buyer at a foreclosure sale recites that all procedural requirements for the default and sale notices have been met, a rebuttable presumption that these notice requirements have been satisfied applies. If the buyer is a bona fide purchaser, the presumption becomes conclusive. (Civ. Code § 2924.)

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted evidence that a foreclosure sale was held on January 17, 2024, at which Yarden Consulting LLC (Yarden) purchased the Property and subsequently received title pursuant to a Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale. (UMF ¶ 1.) Plaintiff submitted evidence that it is the authorized agent of Yarden regarding the Property, with authority to, inter alia, bring the instant action on Yarden’s behalf. (UMF ¶2.)

 

Plaintiff submitted evidence that the Trustee’s Deed upon Sale states, “All requirements of law regarding the mailing of copies of notices or the publication of a copy of the Notice of Default or the personal delivery of the copy of the Notice of Default and the posting and publication of copies of the notice of a Sale have been complied with.” (RJN #1). Therefore, unless Plaintiff had actual knowledge or constructive notice of some irregularity in the sale proceedings at the time of the sale, this statement is conclusively binding on Defendant as against Plaintiff.

 

Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendant to show any purported irregularity in the sale proceedings at the time of the sale.

 

Defendant did not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this issue. 

 

As for Service of Notice, service carried out by a registered process server, as here, “establishes a presumption, affecting the burden of producing evidence, of the facts stated in the return.” (Evid. Code § 647.) “The effect of a presumption affecting the burden of producing evidence is to require the trier of fact to assume the existence of the presumed fact unless and until evidence is introduced which would support a finding of its nonexistence, in which case the trier of fact shall determine the existence or nonexistence of the presumed fact from the evidence and without regard to the presumption.” (Evid. Code § 604.)

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted evidence that:

 

-         On March 12, 2024, the notice to quit in this action was served upon Defendants by a registered process server. (UMF ¶ 3.)

 

Plaintiff’s evidence supports a reasonable inference that Defendant was properly served in this case. Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendant to show a triable issue as to service. Notably, the simple denial of service is insufficient to overcome the statutory presumption of Evidence Code section 647. (Palm Property Investments, LLC v. Yadegar (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 1219, 1428.) Defendant must produce affirmative evidence sufficient to both rebut and overcome the presumption of service. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant did not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this issue. 

 

As for market value, pursuant to Evidence Code section 813(a), “The value of property may be shown only by the opinions of any of the following: (1) witnesses qualified to express such opinions; (2) the owner or the spouse of the owner of the property or property interest being valued; or (3) an officer, regular employee, or partner designated by a corporation, partnership, or unincorporated association that is the owner of the property or property interest being valued, if the designee is knowledgeable as to the value of the property or property interest.”

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted a declaration by a qualified expert witness regarding the market value of the Property. Vladimir Valsky is a real estate professional with over 10 years of experience managing similar rental properties. He is familiar with residential property valuation in Pacific Palisades, California. According to Mr. Valsky, the Property’s fair market monthly rental value is $39,500.00, and its daily rental value of at least $1,000.00.

 

Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendant to disclose a triable issue as to market value.

 

Defendant did not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this issue. 

 

 

Finally, as for affirmative defenses, Plaintiff submitted evidence that prior to this action, Defendants repeatedly stated, including in sworn declarations under oath, that they are not occupants and would not occupy the Property. (UMF, ¶¶  12-18, see ¶¶  14 and 16 for sworn declarations.) As such, Plaintiff contends that:

           

Therefore, it is undisputed that defendants are unlawful holdover occupants following a foreclosure sale, not tenants. Consequently, the purported affirmative defenses asserted by Defendants are inapplicable because those affirmative defenses apply to tenants, including but not limited to the not breach of the warranty of habitability, repair and deduct, California Tenant Protection Act of 2019 (AB 1482), Los Angeles Rent Stabilization Ordinance (“LARSO”), and City of Los Angeles Just Cause for Eviction Ordinance (LAMC §§ 165.00, et seq.)

 

            (MSJ, 9: 10-15.)

 

            Moreover, Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s tenancy claims are contradictory and thus do not create a triable issue. (See, e.g., California Medical Assn. v. Aetna Health of California Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1075, 1095 ([“a party opposing summary judgment cannot create an issue of fact by a declaration that contradicts the party's prior discovery responses”]; Minish v. Hanuman Fellowship (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 437, 449 [a party may not defeat summary judgment by means of declarations or affidavits which contradict that party's deposition testimony or sworn discovery responses]

 

Here, Defendant claims in discovery that they are tenants of the Property’s former owner, non-party 1355 Berea LLC pursuant to a written lease. Moreover, Defendant claims their purported tenancy pursuant to said lease began March 21, 2023. However, Plaintiff submitted evidence that three weeks after this purported lease start date, Defendants signed a loan application under oath on April 11, 2023 that contradicts this claim.

 

Accordingly, the burden shifts to Defendant to disclose a triable issue as to market value.

 

Defendant did not oppose the motion, and thus did not raise a triable issue as to this issue. 

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

 

It is so ordered.

TENATIVE RULING

 

ALADDIN DINAALI

 

         vs.

 

INTERLACED SOCIAL LLC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  24STCV19026

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 23, 2024

 

 

Plaintiff’s motion for a preliminary injunction is DENIED.

 

            On 7/30/2024, in properia persona Plaintiff Aladdin Dinaali filed suit against Interlaced Social, LLC, Augustin Pulido, and Kenneth Barlow, alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) unfair business practices; (3) fraud; (4) fraud; (5) fraud; (6) negligence; (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (8) negligent infliction of emotional distress; and (9) common counts. 

 

            On 9/23/2024, Plaintiff moved for a preliminary injunction.

 

Discussion

 

            Plaintiff’s motion advances two grounds for a preliminary injunction.

 

One, Plaintiff argues he is entitled to a preliminary injunction on the grounds that Defendants are conducting business in California without proper registration deliberately evading service of legal documents such as summons and complaints. However, Plaintiff submits no supporting evidence of this, nor does Plaintiff provide legal authority to show this can be the basis of a preliminary injunction.

 

            Second, Plaintiff argues that a preliminary injunction should be granted because Doublelist, a dating site owned by Interlaced Social, is running a prostitution operation. However, Plaintiff has not submitted any evidence which could show that any harm to Plaintiff could not be satisfied by monetary damages, nor has Plaintiff proposed a constitutionally permissible injunction whereby this Court could shut down the DoubleList dating website, or could refer individuals for criminal prosecution at the pleading stage.

 

            Accordingly, the Court has no legal authority before it by which to grant an injunction. (“This failure to cite pertinent legal authority is enough reason to reject the argument”); Akins v. State of California (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1, 50, 71 (contention waived by failure to cite legal authority).

 

            The Court urges Plaintiff to consult with an attorney to pursue these claims.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for a preliminary injunction is denied.

 

It is so ordered.

CALIFORNIA DEPARTMENT OF EDUCATION

                          

         vs.

 

GOLDEN DAYS SCHOOLS, INC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  BC705147

[Consolidated with 19STCV12595]

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 23, 2024

 

 

            Plaintiff CDE’s motion for attorney fees and costs is GRANTED. Plaintiff is awarded $1,517,311.25 in attorney fees, and $34,781.94 in costs.

 

On 5/4/2018, Plaintiff California Department of Education (Plaintiff) filed suit against Golden Day Schools, Inc. and Clark Parker, alleging breach of contract and declaratory judgment.

 

            Now, CDE seeks $1,517,311.25 in attorney fees, and $34,781.94 in costs, pursuant to Civil Code section 1717.

 

            The motion is unopposed.

 

Legal Standard

 

The party claiming attorneys’ fees must establish entitlement to such fees and the reasonableness of the fees claimed.  (Civic Western Corporation v. Zila Industries, Inc. (1977) 66 Cal.App.3d 1, 16.) “Except as attorney’s fees are specifically provided for by statute, the measure and mode of compensation of attorneys and counselors at law is left to the agreement, express or implied, of the parties[.]” (CCP § 1021.)

 

“It is well established that the determination of what constitutes reasonable attorney fees is committed to the discretion of the trial court, whose decision cannot be reversed in the absence of an abuse of discretion.”  (Melnyk v. Robledo (1976) 64 Cal.App.3d 618, 623.)  In exercising its discretion, the court should consider a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in handling the matter, the attention given, the success or failure, and the resulting judgment.  (Id.)

 

In determining what constitutes a reasonable compensation for an attorney who has rendered services in connection with a legal proceeding, the court may and should consider the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required and the skill employed in handling the litigation, the attention given, the success of the attorneys’ efforts, their learning, their age, and their experience in the particular type of work demanded the intricacies and importance of the litigation, the labor and necessity for skilled legal training and ability in trying the cause, and the time consumed. (Stokus v. Marsh (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 647, 657.)

 

In determining the proper amount of fees to award, courts use the lodestar method.  The lodestar figure is calculated by multiplying the total number of reasonable hours expended by the reasonable hourly rate.  “Fundamental to its determination … [is] a careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney … in the presentation of the case.”  (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 48 (Serrano III).)  A reasonable hourly rate must reflect the skill and experience of the attorney.  (Id. at 49.)  “Prevailing parties are compensated for hours reasonably spent on fee-related issues.  A fee request that appears unreasonably inflated is a special circumstance permitting the trial court to reduce the award or deny one altogether.”  (Serrano v. Unruh (1982) 32 Cal.3d 621, 635 (Serrano IV); see also Weber v. Langholz (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1578, 1587 (“The trial court could make its own evaluation of the reasonable worth of the work done in light of the nature of the case, and of the credibility of counsel’s declaration unsubstantiated by time records and billing statements.”)

 

Reasonable attorney fees should be based on an objective standard of reasonableness, i.e., the market value of services rendered, not on some notion of cost incurred. (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1090.)  The value of legal services performed in a case is a matter in which the trial court has its own expertise.  (Id. at 1096.)  The trial court may make its own determination of the value of the services contrary to, or without the necessity for, expert testimony.  (Id.)  The trial court makes its determination after consideration of a number of factors, including the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.  (Id.)

 

Discussion

 

            CDE seeks $1,517,311.25 in attorney fees, and $34,781.94 in costs, pursuant to Civil Code section 1717.

 

            Here, the relevant contracts on which the Department has sued Golden Day and the Parkers all incorporate “Standard Terms and Conditions.” (Heyn Decl. ¶ 12.) The Standard Terms and Conditions provide, “The CDE shall recoup any payments made for costs which were not reasonable and necessary. The amount that is recouped shall be the excess payment over the reasonable or fair market value, or one hundred percent (100%) of the cost, if the cost was not necessary. The CDE may elect to recover any costs of recoupment, including collection services or attorney fees.” (App., Ex. 1, at p. 31, § V.I.)

 

            California Code of Regulations, title 5, section 18038, provides:

 

(a)   The State Department of Education shall recoup any payments made for costs which were not reasonable and necessary. The amount that is recouped shall be the excess payment over the reasonable or fair market value, or one hundred percent (100%) of the cost, if the cost was not necessary.

 

(b)  The State Department of Education may elect to recover any costs of recoupment, including collection services or attorney fees.

 

As prevailing party under the childcare contracts and under section 18038, the Department is entitled to recover its attorneys’ fees. Notably, CDE is not seeking attorney fees related to the audit. Rather all fees and costs cost relate to the OAH proceedings to contest the audit, these consolidated actions, and the three bankruptcies that Golden Day and Parker filed:
