DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV01578    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: E

Los Angeles Superior Court

825 MAPLE AVENUE l TORRANCE l CALIFORNIA l 90503

 

HONORABLE ALAN B. HONEYCUTT

DEPARTMENT E – TENTATIVE RULING

 

v Case Name:

Wise v. Macy’s, Inc. et al.

v Case Number:         

23TRCV01578

v Hearing Date:       

November 7, 2024

v Trial Date:

April 8, 2025

v Notice Sufficiency:

OK / Unopposed (after Amended Notice; see below)

 

 

 

 

 

 

Motion(s):

Motion for Summary Judgment / Adjudication

Movant(s):

Defendant Schindler Elevator Corporation

Opposing Party(ies):

Plaintiff Aurelia Wise

 

 

 

Defendant’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 17, 2023, Plaintiff Aurelia Wise (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendants Macy’s, Inc., Macy’s Retail Holdings, LLC, and Does 1-25. On November 1, 2023, Plaintiff amended her complaint to substitute Defendant Schindler Elevator Corporation (“Defendant”), the moving party here, for fictitiously-named Doe 1.

 

Plaintiff’s complaint asserts two (2) causes of action: 1. Premises Liability and 2. General Negligence. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that on March 20, 2022, she sustained injuries as a result of a slip and fall accident on a stopped escalator in a Macy’s store.

 

On November 28, 2023, Defendant filed its answer.

 

On July 30, 2024, Defendant moved for summary judgment and/or adjudication of both causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint. On August 14, 2024, Defendant refiled its motion due to a notice defect, properly setting the hearing at least seventy-five (75) days after filing.

 

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed her opposition. On October 7, 2024, Defendant replied.

 

 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

Plaintiff filed no evidentiary objections. Instead, she filed a Request for Judicial Notice asking the Court take notice of its own prior ruling on objections to Defendant’s evidence in support of its motion.

 

Judicial notice of the Court’s own rulings is unnecessary. The prior ruling also not “relevant”, in the evidentiary sense, to Defendant’s motion or Plaintiff’s opposition. The request is denied.

 

Plaintiff has asserted no objections. If she intended to do so via reference to a prior ruling, she has not followed the Rules of Court. (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1354(b).) It is improper and ineffective to force Defendant and the Court to cross-reference prior rulings to ascertain Plaintiff’s objections.

 

Nonetheless, for the sake of argument, the Court adopts its ruling on all relevant prior objections. Excluding the evidence in question does not affect the result.

 

Defendant asserted no objections to Plaintiff’s evidence.

  

LEGAL STANDARD

  

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to enable summary dismissal without the need for trial where an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Ritchfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Summary judgment is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(c); Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)  

 

A defendant may move for summary judgment/adjudication if it contends that the action or individual cause of action has no merit. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(a), (f).) The moving party has the initial burden of proof to demonstrate with admissible evidence the action or cause of action lacks merit.  (Id., § 437c(a).) A cause of action has no merit if either (1) one or more of the elements of the cause of action cannot be separately established, even if that element is separately pleaded or (2) defendant establishes an affirmative defense to that cause of action.” (Id., § 437c(o); Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 853.) Once the moving party has met the burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material facts exists as to a cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(o)(2).)

 

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Defendant moves for summary judgment or adjudication on the following grounds:

 

             (1) Defendant did not control the premises where Plaintiff’s accident took place, a Macy’s store (“the Premises”);

 

             (2) Defendant was not negligent because there was no dangerous condition at the scene of Plaintiff’s accident;

 

             (3) Defendant had no duty to warn of any dangerous condition that may have existed, because it was open and obvious;

 

             (4) Plaintiff was fully aware and warned about any dangerous condition;

 

             (5) “Defendant was not negligent because Defendant did not cause the incident”, as framed in Defendant’s notice; and

 

             (6) Plaintiff caused her own injuries.

  

Premises Liability

 

Premises liability shares its elements with negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages. (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) Premises liability is simply a species of negligence “grounded in the possession of [a] premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, quotation marks omitted.) The theory relies on a crucial premise: that the defendant in a premises liability action owns or controls the property on which a plaintiff’s injury took place. (Martin v. Gladston (2023) 96 Cal.App.5th 681, 689 [collecting precedents]; Kinsman v. Unocal Corp. (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659, 672 [“proper test” is reasonable care in “management of [one’s] property”]; Soto v. Union Pacific Railroad Co. (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 168, 177.)

 

Defendant neither owned nor controlled the Premises, that is, the Macy’s in which Plaintiff’s injury took place. The fact that “the escalator was lockout / tagout by Schlinder [sic]” (Pl. Resp. to DSMF 9) does not show Defendant owned or controlled any portion of the Premises, including the escalator. Plaintiff does not define “lockout / tagout” in her opposition, nor does she dispute Kay Donovan’s attestation that “Schindler never controlled the layout of the Macy’s store ... the location of the escalators and elevators at the Premises, the condition of the Premises, [or] the actions of the staff at the Premises[.]” (Donovan Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Defendant negates Plaintiff’s claim that Defendant owned or controlled any portion of the premises, and Plaintiff raises no triable issue. Plaintiff’s premises liability claim fails.

 

Negligence

 

As noted, the elements of negligence are duty, breach, causation, and damages. (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.)

 

Defendant’s arguments go to the first through third elements: (1) that, assuming it had a duty to maintain the escalator in safe condition, it did not breach that duty; (2) that it had no separate duty to warn Plaintiff about any dangerous condition on the escalator; if it did have such a duty, it warned her as required and she received that warning; and (3) if it breached either purported duty, Plaintiff cannot show those breaches caused her injuries – in fact, the evidence demonstrates she caused her injuries herself.

 

1. Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue that the escalator was a dangerous condition, or that Schindler had a duty to warn of such condition.

 

First: liability for a dangerous condition on a premises attaches to the landowner on a theory of premises liability. (Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 1200, 1207 [“landowners are liable for injuries caused by a lack of due care” (italics added)].) As discussed above, Defendant is entitled to adjudication of Plaintiff’s cause of action for premises liability because Defendant has demonstrated, as a matter of law, that it did not own or control the Premises.

 

Second: even if Defendant owned or controlled the Premises – and did not – the dangerous condition Plaintiff relies on was open and obvious, making it an “exception to the general duty of care rule”. (Cf. Jones v. Awad, supra, 39 Cal.App.5th, at p. 1208 [no liability if purported dangerous condition were “the step in general”].) Plaintiff does not identify any particular dangerous condition that caused her injury, except for the stopped escalator. The parties agree the escalator required a right handrail replacement. But as Plaintiff describes her accident, she “had her left hand on the railings [sic]” and “fell after the escalator caught her foot”. (Opp., 7:13-16, italics added.) Because she does not identify any specific dangerous condition, she must rely on the escalator’s condition as a whole, that is, that was stopped pending repairs. That condition was open and obvious.

 

Defendant negates Plaintiff’s claim that it owed her a duty to repair or warn about the condition of the escalator. Plaintiff raises no triable issue.

 

            2. Plaintiff has not raised a triable issue that the escalator caused her damages.

 

The evidence Plaintiff offers in support of her opposition to Defendant’s motion is nearly identical to the evidence she offered in opposition to its co-defendants’ motion, which the Court heard and granted on October 17, 2024. The only distinction between the evidence is her inclusion here of pages 38 and 44 of her deposition transcript. Neither page contains further facts that might raise a triable issue as to causation.

 

On October 17, 2024, the Court ruled that the evidence in question established that “Plaintiff does not possess evidence to establish that Macy’s caused her injuries”, which carried Macy’s initial burden to negate the question of causation. (10-17-2024 Minute Order, at p. 5.) The reasoning applies equally here: Plaintiff admitted she doesn’t know what happened when she fell and she has no evidence of malfunction or debris that caused her injury.

 

To raise a triable issue in her opposition to Macy’s motion, Plaintiff “only offer[ed] her deposition transcript”, and that did not raise a triable issue. The addition of two new pages of her deposition does not contribute to her showing on that score. She has not raised a triable issue as to causation in her opposition to this motion either.

 

            3. Conclusion

 

Defendant negates Plaintiff’s showing of duty and causation, and she does not raise a triable issue as to either. Both of her causes of action fail, and Defendant is entitled to summary judgment. Ancillary points in Defendant’s motion – e.g., whether Plaintiff was aware of the dangerous condition, whether she caused her own injuries, and whether a stopped escalator constitutes a dangerous condition – need not be discussed.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Defendant’s motion is granted. Defendant is ordered to submit a proposed form of judgment within ten (10) days of this order.
