ORDER RE:

 

MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED

 

 

Defendant M Stephen Cho Esq., (erroneously sued as Stephen Cho) (herein, “Cho”) moves or an order deeming admitted Requests for Admissions served on November 11, 2023, and granting monetary sanctions against Plaintiff Janet Haywood and her counsel of record Ms. Joyce Vega, Esq., jointly and severally, in the amount of $2,160.00. 

Cho’s motion will be continued to a new date as set forth below. NO HEARING WILL TAKE PLACE ON FEBRUARY 2, 2024.

Pursuant to the Court’s power to “amend and control its process and orders so as to make them conform to law and justice” (Code Civ. Proc., § 128, subd. (a)(8)), the Court orders the parties in this case to participate in an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”). (Note:¿The Court’s policy regarding IDCs appears in the Courtroom Information available in Dept. 50 and on the Court’s website.)¿Lead or other designated counsel for the parties with full authority are ordered to participate in person in an IDC. After consulting with opposing counsel regarding available dates, Cho must make a prompt reservation for the IDC using the Court’s online reservation system. Cho must file Dept. 50’s one-page IDC form in the department seven days prior to the IDC, and the responding parties may file the same form in the department setting forth a response three days prior to the IDC.  

Once Cho has confirmed an IDC date, Cho must use the Court’s online reservation system to continue the motion to a post-IDC discovery hearing date. The parties are ordered to have with them whatever materials are needed to make the IDC session productive and successful. Prior to the IDC date, lead or other designated counsel for the parties, with full authority, are to meet and confer, in person or via telephone in a further attempt to resolve as many of the issues as possible before the IDC. (See CRC Rule 3.670, subd. (f)(2).) If the parties resolve their discovery disputes before the IDC date, Cho is ordered to take both the IDC and the motion off calendar as soon as possible. 

Cho is ordered to provide notice of this Order. 

 

 

DATED:  February 2, 2024                            ________________________________

Hon. Teresa A. Beaudet

Judge, Los Angeles Superior Court

Background

Plaintiff Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”) filed this action on December 23, 2021 against Defendants Ramy Elias, M.D. (“Elias”); Ramy Elias, M.D., Inc.; Center for Advanced Orthopedics and Sports Medicine, LLC; Cerritos Surgery Center, LLC; Afshin A. Mashoof, M.D., Inc.; Lakewood Orthopaedic Surgical and Medical Group (“Lakewood”); E.W. Wassef, M.D., Inc. (“E.W. Wassef”); CCNI Incorporated (“CCNI”); and Andrew Wassef, M.D., Inc. (“Andrew Wassef”).

            On November 29, 2022, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action for (1) sexual battery in violation of Civil Code § 1708.5, (2) battery, (3) domestic violence in violation of Civil Code § 1708.6, (4) assault, (5) sexual harassment, (6) gender and sex discrimination in violation of Government Code § 12940(A), (7) gender violence (Civil Code § 52.4), (8) sexual harassment by a physician (Civil Code §§ 51.9, 52), (9) professional negligence/medical malpractice, (10) failure to prevent discrimination and harassment, (11) violation of Bane Act, (12) unlawful retaliation in violation of Labor Code §1102.5, (13) violation of Labor Code § 230(f), (14) fraud, and (15) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

On March 7, 2022, Elias filed a Cross-Complaint against a number of Cross-Defendants. On December 5, 2022, Elias filed the operative Second Amended Cross-Complaint, asserting causes of action for (1) fraud, (2) attempted extortion, (3) slander per se, (4) conversion, (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress, and (6) conspiracy to commit fraud.¿¿ 

On June 12, 2023, Lakewood, E.W. Wassef, CCNI, and Andrew Wassef filed a Cross-Complaint against Elias and Ramy Elias, M.D., Inc., asserting causes of action for (1) express indemnity and (2) equitable indemnity.

The trial date in this action is currently set for March 6, 2024.

Plaintiff and Elias, Ramy Elias, M.D., Inc., Afshin A. Mashoof, M.D., Inc., Center for Advanced Orthopedics and Sports Medicine, Inc., and Cerritos Surgery Center, LLC (collectively, the “Moving Parties”) now move for a “continuance of the trial date for the instant action from March 6, 2024 to July 22, 2024 or to a date as soon thereafter as is feasible for hearing based on the court’s calendar and the schedule of the parties’ counsel.” Lakewood, E.W. Wassef, CCNI, and Andrew Wassef (collectively, the “Lakewood Defendants”) oppose.

Discussion

“The court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (c).) “In ruling on a motion or application for continuance, the court must consider all the facts and circumstances that are relevant to the determination.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (d).)

The Moving Parties assert that here, good cause exists for a continuance of the trial.

In his supporting declaration, Plaintiff’s counsel states that “[a]s part of gathering documents in preparation for trial in this matter, I have tried to obtain Plaintiff’s medical records from all of her therapists and medical providers. I have not succeeded in obtaining all of her records, however, despite multiple inquiries, from the medical school she currently attends. I need Plaintiff’s complete medical records to proceed with this case but the discovery cutoff window for the March 6, 2024 trial in this action is rapidly approaching and I do not have all of her records despite promises by her medical school to send me all of her records.” (Okadigbo Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that “Plaintiff will want the opportunity to introduce and rely on all of her medical records that bear on her damages claims in this matter, including records that have not yet been produced by her medical school.” (Okadigbo Decl., ¶ 4.)

The Moving Parties note that circumstances that may indicate good cause for a trial continuance include “[a] party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (c)(6).)

Plaintiff’s counsel further states that “[i]n email communications with counsel for Ramy Elias, M.D., Tina Starr, on or about November 30, 2023, Ms. Starr conveyed to me that Defendant Ramy Elias’ counsel…will not be able to complete the deposition of Plaintiff without all of her medical records. As such, Plaintiff’s deposition remains to be completed and will not be completed prior to the close of discovery if obtaining all of her records remains a condition for the completion of her deposition.” (Okadigbo Decl., ¶ 4.) The Moving Parties assert that “[a]s such, good cause further exists in that the inability to complete Plaintiff’s deposition due to not possessing Plaintiff’s medical records constitutes a ‘significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case as a result of which the case is not ready for trial’ under Rule 3.1332(c)(7) of the California Rules of Court.” (Mot. at p. 3:20-24.)[1]

In their opposition to the instant motion, the Lakewood Defendants assert that “Plaintiff has delayed completing her production of the requested documents for nearly two years based on an unsupported and unbelievable contention that Plaintiff is ‘unable’ to obtain her own medical records. Plaintiff has provided no evidence of ‘best efforts’ to secure the information.” (Opp’n at p. 3:18-20, emphasis omitted.) But as set forth above, Plaintiff’s counsel states in his declaration under penalty of perjury that he has “tried to obtain Plaintiff’s medical records from all of her therapists and medical providers,” and that he has “not succeeded in obtaining all of her records, however, despite multiple inquiries, from the medical school she currently attends.” (Okadigbo Decl., ¶ 3.)

            In his declaration in support of the motion, Plaintiff’s counsel also states that “[r]ecently, the parties have also agreed to participate in a mediation, hopeful that the case will resolve without the need for trial. Towards this end, the parties have agreed to a mediator – Sharyl Garza of the Garza Firm - and to a mediation date of February 1, 2024.” (Okadigbo Decl., ¶ 5.) Plaintiff’s counsel asserts that “the parties need to have the trial date continued until at least July 22, 2024 to allow them to participate in mediation without the concern of having to increase their litigation costs and expenses by conducting depositions prior to the discovery cutoff and, thereby, potentially increasing the costs of settlement prohibitively.” (Ibid.) The Moving Parties assert that “[g]ood cause further exists in that settlement between the parties has a greater prospect of achievement if the parties are able to suspend the incurrence of further litigation costs and expenses by refraining from conducting further depositions and discovery related matters while focusing on settlement efforts.” (Mot. at p. 4:4-7.) This does not appear to be disputed by the Lakewood Defendants

            Plaintiff’s counsel also states in his supporting declaration that “I have tried over the past two months to extract availability dates for several witness depositions, including Ramy Elias, M.D., Ranya Elias (who Defendant Ramy Elias lists as a percipient witness to his slander crossclaim against Plaintiff) and Stephanie Miramontes (who they claimed they might represent). As of yet, Ramy Elias’ counsel has not provided me availability dates for any of these depositions…If Plaintiff is not able to depose these individuals, it would naturally put Plaintiff in a detrimental position with respect to proceeding to trial.” (Okadigbo Decl., ¶ 6.)

            In the opposition, the Lakewood Defendants assert that “[t]he parties already agreed, and the Court already ordered, that depositions will be completed in February in advance of the March trial date.” (Opp’n at p. 2:11-12.) The Lakewood Defendants note that on January 11, 2024 a hearing was held on Plaintiff’s ex parte application for an order shortening time on the hearing of the joint motion to continue trial date. The Court’s January 11, 2024 minute order provides, inter alia, that “[t]he Ex Parte Application Order Shortening Time for Hearing on Motion to Continue Trial Date filed by Jane Doe on 01/10/2024 is Denied. The parties have agreed to take the remaining depositions after the discovery cut-off and to extend the expert designation date and the IME date to a mutually agreeable date in February after the mediation.”

            In the reply, Plaintiff counters that “[g]ood cause exists to continue the trial date to enable the parties to complete all remaining depositions without having to, at the same time, prepare for trial in the roughly one-month window of time between the scheduled mediation date of February 1, 2024 and the trial date of March 6, 2024.” (Plaintiff’s Reply at p. 2:10-12.) Plaintiff also asserts in her reply that “[w]hen the Court…entered in its order a plan for conducting all of these depositions in February, Plaintiff’s counsel still could not anticipate that counsel for all Defendants would be unable to provide enough availability dates to accomplish completing all of the parties’ anticipated depositions. Plaintiff will clearly suffer prejudice if the trial date is not continued due to being hamstrung by the claimed unavailability of Elias’ counsel for depositions or by their refusal to provide availability dates for the completion of the depositions Plaintiff wishes to conduct.” (Reply at pp. 5:28-6:6.)

            The Lakewood Defendants also assert in the opposition that “[t]he backlog of remaining depositions is a problem of Plaintiff’s own making.” (Opp’n at p. 3:3.) The Lakewood Defendants assert that “[t]here is no reason why Plaintiff did not take the depositions sooner. This case has been pending for 25 months since December 2021… If Plaintiff believed the Elias Defendants were being obstructive, the proper course would have been to set an IDC or to file a discovery motion well before the eve of trial.” (Opp’n at p. 3:6-13, emphasis omitted.) The Lakewood Defendants note that California Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subdivision (b) provides, inter alia, that “[t]he party must make the motion or application as soon as reasonably practical once the necessity for the continuance is discovered.” Plaintiff argues in the reply that “contrary to the Lakewood Defendants’ assertions to the contrary, Plaintiff has been diligent in conducting discovery. Thus far, Plaintiff has conducted the depositions of Dr. Ezzat Wassef, the Managing Partner of Lakewood, and Dr. Afshin Mashoof, Defendant Ramy Elias’ partner at the Center for Advanced Orthopedics and Sports Medicine and Cerritos Surgery Center. Each time Plaintiff’s counsel has moved to conduct additional depositions, he has been thwarted by counsel for the Elias Defendants…” (Reply at p. 5:15-20.)

            The Lakewood Defendants also assert in the opposition that “[t]his case has been pending for over two years and trial already has been continued,” and that “the Lakewood Defendants…will continue to be saddled with increased legal expenses if the case is allowed to drag on.” (Opp’n at p. 4:6-7, 4:16-17.) The Moving Parties acknowledge that “[t]he parties have previously requested, and were granted, a continuance of the initial trial date. As such, this is the second request for an extension in his matter.” (Mot. at p. 2:24-25.) However, as noted by Plaintiff in her reply, “[a]lthough the Lakewood Defendants maintain that they will prejudiced by having to wait longer for trial, they have provided no evidence that they will incur additional litigation costs or expenses they did not already stand to expend…” (Reply at p. 6:11-14.)

            Based on a consideration of the arguments and evidence presented by the parties, the Court finds that the Moving Parties have demonstrated good cause for a brief trial continuance.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Moving Parties motion is granted.
