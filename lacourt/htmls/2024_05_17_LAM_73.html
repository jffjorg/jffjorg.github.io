DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV23647    Hearing Date: May 17, 2024    Dept: 73

5/17/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

MARCHEL GABRIELLE AUGUSTE v. THOMAS WESLEY PENTZ (21STCV23647) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Allison Schulman; Christina Coleman 

Counsel for Defendant/moving party:  Bryan Freedman; Brian Turnauer; Tamar Yeghiayan 

 

MOTION TO COMPEL INDEPENDENT MEDICAL EXAMINATION (filed 3/05/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant Pentz’s Motion to Compel Independent Medical Examination is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On June 25, 2021, Plaintiff Marchel Gabrielle Auguste (“Plaintiff” or “Auguste”) filed a Complaint (the “Auguste Lawsuit”) against Defendant Thomas Wesley Pentz (“Defendant” or “Pentz”), alleging causes of action for (1) Sexual Battery; (2) Gender Violence; (3) Violation of the Ralph Civil Rights Act; (4) Violation of the Tom Bane Civil Rights Act; (5) Action Based on Civil Code § 1708.85; (6) Intentional Intrusion Into Private Affairs; (7) Battery; (8) Assault; (9) Defamation; (10) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; and (11) Fraud.  

  

Auguste has sued alleging, among other things, the following: (i) Pentz communicated with her when she was a minor; (ii) groomed her; (iii) gave her an STD; (iv) ejaculated inside her; (v) raped her (all contradicted by her own communications); (vi) defamed her and (vii) committed revenge porn against her (“Auguste Lawsuit”). Auguste alleges that all the aforementioned acts caused her severe emotional distress.

 

On September 14, 2021, the Court ruled this case and Thomas Wesley Pentz v. Marchel Gabrielle Auguste, Los Angeles Superior Court Case No. 21STCV16321, were related within the meaning of California Rules of Court, rule 3.300(a).

 

On March 5, 2024, Defendant Pentz filed a Motion to Compel Independent Medical Examination of Plaintiff, arguing: 

·       Auguste has placed her mental condition in controversy in this case through her allegations and testimony of serious ongoing emotional distress, including anxiety and depression. As such, there is good cause for the Court to order an Independent Mental Examination of Auguste pursuant to §§2032.020(a) and 2032.310(a)

·       Auguste’s social media postings within the last six-months show that her mental state should be questioned. For example, Auguste has stated in videos posted on her social media that she is experiencing anxiety, stress, sleeplessness, etc.

·       Auguste’s supplemental complaint and her verified interrogatory responses detail Auguste’s claimed mental and emotional damages, thus putting her mental condition in controversy

·       Defendant Pentz attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff’s counsel but those efforts were unsuccessful

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.               Legal Standard

 

The Civil Discovery Act authorizes discovery in the form of a physical or mental examination of a party whose physical or mental condition is in controversy in the action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.020, subd. (a).) If the action is one for personal injuries, the defendant may demand exactly one physical examination of a plaintiff without leave of court. (Id., § 2032.220, subd. (b).) The examination may not include “any diagnostic test or procedure that is painful, protracted, or intrusive” and must be conducted “within 75 miles of the residence of the examinee. (Id., § 2032.220, subd. (a).) 

 

A demand for an examination that exceeds the dictates of Code of Civil Procedure sections 2032.210 to 2032.260 requires the Court’s approval. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).) Thus, a second physical examination, or any mental examination require the Court’s approval. (Ibid.) A motion for an examination “shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination” and include a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (b).) The court may only grant such leave for good cause, so as to protect the examinee from “excessive and unwarranted intrusions.” (Id., § 2032.320, subd. (a); Sporich v. Superior Court (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 422, 428.) 

 

“Good cause is established by facts that ‘appear in the record as a demonstrable reality.’ [Citation.] Mere speculation, standing alone, will not suffice.” (Sporich v. Super. Ct., supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at 428, quoting People v. Gates (1987) 43 Cal.3d 1168, 1199.) For example, without further showing, an attorney’s opinion that an examination is necessary does not provide good cause. (Ibid.) 

 

But otherwise, the statute is “silent on both the nature of good cause and the manner in which it is to be shown.” (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court In and For Merced County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 388.) Generally, statutes relating to discovery procedures, including examinations, “should be liberally construed in favor of disclosure.” (Harabedian v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 195 Cal.App.2d 26, 31.) Yet simultaneously, “cumulative discovery is prohibited,” as is discovery requested without knowing precisely what one seeks, in an attempt to “obtain all possible information for the purposes of his case,” i.e., fishing. (Sporich v. Super. Ct., supra, 77 Cal.App.4th at 428; Greyhound Corp. v. Super. Ct. In and For Merced Cty., supra, 56 Cal.2d at 384.) 

 

B.               Discussion

 

Defendant moves to compel Plaintiff to undergo an independent medical examination performed by psychiatrist Dr. Barbara Justice. (Thomas Mot. Compel Independent Med. Exam. at Decl. McCabe ¶ 3.)

 

Defendant asserts Auguste has placed her mental condition in controversy in this case. In Auguste’s Supplemental Complaint she alleges “extreme emotional distress.” (Suppl. Compl. ¶ 31, 39, 126.) Plaintiff states that “as a result of Defendants’ actions, Plaintiff has suffered and will continue to suffer general and special damages, including severe and profound pain and emotional distress, anxiety, depression, headaches, tension, and other physical ailments, as well as medical expenses, expenses for psychological counseling and treatment.” (Id. ¶ 38.) Auguste’s verified interrogatory responses also detail her emotional distress damages and contend that she will require ongoing treatment. (Yeghiayan Decl, ¶4-9, Ex. A, p. 19.) Based on Auguste’s allegations of serious emotional distress as stated in her Supplemental Complaint and discovery answers, the Court finds Defendant has good cause to take a psychiatric examination of Auguste.

 

Auguste does not oppose the motion.  ¿“A party who has not timely filed written opposition to a motion…may not be afforded an opportunity to offer oral argument at the hearing.” (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)¿ “The failure to file opposition creates an inference that the motion or demurrer is meritorious.”¿ (Ibid.)¿ “In such cases, the court may hear argument limited to a request for a continuance of the hearing in order to afford an opportunity for written opposition.”¿ (Ibid.) “[T]he rule is patently intended to prevent the introduction of legal theories without prior notice to opposing counsel and the court.”¿ (Ibid.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Defendant Pentz’s Motion to Compel Independent Medical Examination.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Defendant Pentz’s Motion to Compel Independent Medical Examination is GRANTED.

YEDAM, INC., et al. v. DANIEL LEE, et al. (21STCV30828)

 

Counsel for Cross-Complainants/opposing party:  Dale Park (Law Offices of Dale J. Park)

Counsel for Cross-Defendants/moving party: Frederick Lee (Frederick W. Lee Law Firm APC)

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER ANSWERS TO FORM INTERROGATORIES (SET ONE) (filed on 01/22/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The request to compel further responses is granted, in part, and denied, in part, as set forth below.  Further responses are due within 30 days.  Sanctions are denied. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 20, 2021, Plaintiffs Yedam, Inc. (“Yedam") and Eun Kyung Choi (“Choi") brought this breach of loyalty and unfair business practices action against Daniel N. Lee (“Lee") and Jin Sook Lee (together, “the Lees"), and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“DM Cosmetics").

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) asserting 5 causes of action for: (1) Breach of Duty of Loyalty; (2) Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (3) Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Advantage; (4) Unfair Business Practices; and (5) Declaratory Relief.

 

The FAC alleges the following. Yedam imports and purchases products including candles and scented oils that are sold at various retail locations such as department stores in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 9.) On or about February 2017 Defendant Daniel Lee began working for Yedam in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 10.) In his capacity as an employee, Defendant Daniel Lee was significantly involved in dealing with: a) manufacturers that produced candles, scented oils or related products; b) the manufacturers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; c) the buyers at the department stores in Korea that purchased such products from Yedam; d) the buyers’ decision makers and the purchase terms such as price; and e) the retail locations in both the United States of America and South Korea where such items were sold at. (FAC, ¶ 22.) Notwithstanding his duty of loyalty, Defendant Daniel Lee, in conjunction with his wife Defendant Angela Lee, have misused Yedam’s proprietary information in order to enrich themselves financially. (FAC, ¶ 24). Defendants’ actions have caused Buyers for Department Stores in South Korea to cease purchasing products from Plaintiff Yedam and/or have caused the closure of retail locations in South Korea. (FAC, ¶ 26).

 

The Lees and DM Cosmetics, Inc. (“Cross-Complainants" or “Defendants”) cross-complained against Yedam, Choi, Olive House LLC, and Lamis Tania, LLC (“Cross-Defendants"). On August 10, 2023, Cross-Complainants filed their third amended cross-complaint (“TACC”) asserting 8 causes of action against Cross-Defendants: (1) Breach of Oral Contract I; (2) Breach of Oral Contract II; (3) Breach of Oral Contract III; (4) Breach of Oral Contract IV; (5) Misappropriation of Trade Secrets; (6) Trademark Infringement; (7) Unfair Competition in Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code §17200 et seq. and California Trademark Law; and (8) Declaratory Relief.

 

The TACC alleges the following. Prior to 2017, Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Choi owned Yedam. (TACC, ¶ 11.) Yedam was experiencing serious financial problems. (Ibid.) In February of 2017, Choi approached Defendant/Cross-Complainant Lee asking for help. (Id., ¶ 12.) Choi proposed Lee serve as Yedam’s general manager, whereby Lee would oversee the Korean market and Choi would oversee the Chinese market. (Ibid.) In exchange, Lee would be paid a $10,000 per month salary. (Ibid.) In addition, Choi would give Lee 49% ownership of Yedam if Lee could help Yedam “overcome [its] financial hardship and become profitable." (Ibid.) Lee agreed and moved from the US to Korea to serve as Yedam’s general manager. (Id., ¶ 13.) To help Yedam solve its financial issues, Lee loaned Yedam $70,000.00 in operating capital. (Id., ¶ 14.) Choi promised that the $70,000.00 would be paid back when Yedam become profitable, along with 7% interest per annum from April 1, 2017. (Id., ¶ 12.) Lee successfully managed Yedam thereafter, and Yedam went from net losses in 2016 and 2017 to net profits of over a million dollars per year by the end of 2019. (Id., ¶ 15.) However, in July of 2019, Yedam faced supply chain issues when the “Milk House Candle" company in the United States refused to renew its exclusive distribution contract with Yedam. (Id., ¶ 16.) In response, the Lees created a new brand of merchandise named “Dani Mackenzie" under DM Cosmetics, Inc. (Id., ¶ 17.) The Lees trademarked Dani Mackenzie in the United States in May of 2020. (Id., ¶ 18.) Around that same time, Choi proposed that Yedam purchase Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics in exchange for Yedam having the right to use the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 19.) Choi told the Lees that there was no need to have a written licensing and distribution agreement because Choi would transfer 49% of Yedam’s ownership interest to Lee by the end of December 2020. (Id., ¶ 20.)

 

Dani Mackenzie became highly popular in Korea, and Yedam’s sales and profit soared to the point that Yedam was valued at over $50 million. (Id., ¶ 22.) On December 20, 2020, Lee demanded that Choi transfer 49% of Yedam to him. (Id., ¶ 23.) Lee also demanded that Choi and Yedam repay the $70,000 operating capital loan with 7% interest from April 1, 2017. (Ibid.) However, Choi terminated Lee from Yedam shortly thereafter and refused to convey the 49% ownership share. (Id., ¶ 25.) Further, Choi and Yedam stopped purchasing Dani Mackenzie merchandise from DM Cosmetics and started using other suppliers to manufacture products bearing the Dani Mackenzie trade name. (Id., ¶ 28.) On March 18, 2021 and April 14, 2021, Cross-Complainants demanded that Cross-Defendants cease and desist manufacturing, supplying and/or selling products under the Dani Mackenzie name. (Id., ¶ 32.)

 

On January 22, 2024, Cross-Complainants/Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel Further Answers to Form Interrogatories (Set One), arguing:

·       Defendants move to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, No. 2.3, 2.4, 2.5, 2.6, 2.11, 2.12, 4.1, 4.2, 6.1, 9.1, 9.2, 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2, 17.1, 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6.

·       Plaintiff Choi provided inadequate, evasive and frivolous responses and objections

·       Defendants requests that the Court award monetary sanctions against Choi and her attorney of record in the sum of $2,700.00.

Plaintiffs filed an opposition, arguing:

·       Plaintiffs have properly responded to the “incident” form interrogatories

o   Defendants did not provide a particular definition to the term “incident”

o   Form Interrogatories Nos. 9.1 series (entitled Other Damages) and the 12.1 series (entitled Investigation - General) incorporate the term “incident” and are objectionable

o   Nevertheless, Plaintiff provided substantive responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 2.6, 2.11, 2.12, 4.1,4.2, 6.1, 12.2 - 12.6, 13.1 and 13.2

·       Defendants did not designate a trade secret

o   Given Defendants’ usage of Form Interrogatories and the definition therein of "incident" it is impossible to separate discovery related on the one part to the trade secret claim and accompanying claims from other claims. Defendants must first make a compliant trade secret designation and then propound discovery that does not overlap between trade secret claims and other matters

·       Regarding Interrogatory 17.1, Defendants did not attach the Request for Admissions or responses, thus they failed to carry their evidentiary burden

·       Sanctions should be awarded to Plaintiffs in the amount of $6,682.50

 

Defendants filed a reply arguing:

·       The TACC amply describes the circumstances and events giving rise to the present action, which Section 4 of the Interrogatories incorporated into the definition of “incident.”

·       Plaintiffs’ argument that Defendants’ didn’t identify a trade secret is disingenuous as the claims in this action go beyond simply trade secrets

·       Regarding Interrogatory 17.1, Plaintiffs argument that Defendants should have attached the Requests for Admissions with the instant motion is frivolous as Defendants served the Requests for Admissions on Choi with the Form Interrogatories

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by propounding to any other party to the action written interrogatories to be answered under oath.”¿ CCP § 2030.010(a).¿ “Within 30 days after service of interrogatories, the party to whom the interrogatories are propounded shall serve the original of the response to them on the propounding party, unless on motion of the propounding party the court has shortened the time for response, or unless on motion of the responding party the court has extended the time for response.”¿ CCP § 2030.260(a).¿ 
¿ 

“The party to whom interrogatories have been propounded shall respond in writing under oath separately to each interrogatory by any of the following: (1) An answer containing the information sought to be discovered. (2) An exercise of the party's option to produce writings. (3) An objection to the particular interrogatory.”¿ CCP § 2030.210(a).¿ “If the responding party does not have personal knowledge sufficient to respond fully to an interrogatory, that party shall so state, but shall make a reasonable and good faith effort to obtain the information by inquiry to other natural persons or organizations, except where the information is equally available to the propounding party.”¿ CCP § 2030.220(c).¿ 

¿ 

“On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete. (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate. (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(a).¿ “A motion under subdivision (a) [of CCP § 2030.300] shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(b).¿ In addition, a separate statement is required.¿ California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 3.1345(a)(2).¿ 

¿ 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the propounding party waives any right to compel a further response to the interrogatories.”¿ CCP § 2030.300(c).¿“[T]he time limitations set forth in section 2030 and section 2034, subdivision (a) [are] mandatory and [when] not met, the trial court's order is in excess of its jurisdiction.”¿(Vidal Sassoon, Inc. v. Superior Court¿(1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 681, 685.)

 

a.     Interrogatory Nos. 2.5

 

Defendants argue that further responses to Form Interrogatories No. 2.5 Set One, must be compelled. Request No. 2.5 requests the following:  

 

“State:

(a) your present residence ADDRESS;

(b) your residence ADDRESSES for the past five years; and

(c) the dates you lived at each ADDRESS”

 

Here, Choi objects to the information requested and states that it violates the right to privacy. To the extent Choi asserts a privacy privilege, she must submit a privilege log for the determination of the applicability of such privilege. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240(c)(1) [“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.”].) Choi has not submitted a privilege log and the Court finds this background information may lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 2.5 is GRANTED.

 

b.     Interrogatory Nos. 2.3, 2.4, 2.6, 2.11, 2.12, 4.1, 4.2, 6.1, 9.1, 9.2, 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1, 13.2,

 

Form Interrogatory Nos. 2.3, 2.4, 2.6, 2.11, 2.12, 4.1, 4.2, 6.1, 9.1, 9.2, 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1 and 13.2 seek information regarding the “Incident,” which is defined as “The circumstances and events surrounding the alleged accident, injury, or other occurrence or breach of contract giving rise to this action or proceeding.” (Park Decl., Exh. 1, p. 1.) 

 

Choi objects on the grounds that the interrogatories are vague and ambiguous as to the term “Incident.”

 

The Court agrees that the interrogatories are vague and ambiguous, as it is unclear what the “Incident” or occurrence giving rise to this action refers to. The TACC references multiple oral contracts and there appears to be multiple occurrences that gave rise to this action. Without more context as to the information Plaintiff seeks, the Court is unable to fashion a definition for “Incident.” As such, the Court orders the parties to meet and confer regarding the definition for “Incident.” 

 

Thus, Defendants’ motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatory Nos. 2.3, 2.4, 2.6, 2.11, 2.12, 4.1, 4.2, 6.1, 9.1, 9.2, 12.1, 12.2, 12.3, 12.4, 12.5, 12.6, 13.1 and 13.2 is GRANTED, subject to the parties’ revised definition for “Incident.”

 

c.      Interrogatory Nos. 17.1

 

Defendants argue that further responses to Form Interrogatories No. 17.1, Set One, must be compelled. Request No. 17.1 requests the following:  

 

“Is your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories an unqualified admission? If not, for each response that it is not an unqualified admission:  

a.               State the number of the request;  

b.               State all facts upon which you base your response;  

c.                State the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and  

d.               Identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each DOCUMENT or thing.”

 

Defendants assert that Choi failed to admit or deny as to Request for Admission Nos. 10, 11, 14, 16 and 17, and only responded with objections as to those requests. In addition, Defendants argue Choi admitted to Request for Admission Nos. 1 and 2, but they were not unqualified admissions because Choi qualified them with objections. Thus, Defendants argue that Request for Admission Nos. 1, 2, 10, 11, 14, 16 and 17 should have been included in Choi’s response to this interrogatory.

 

Here, Defendants did not provide their Request for Admissions or Choi’s responses to the Request for Admissions at issue. Therefore, the Court is unable to make a determination as to whether Choi should have included these Requests for Admissions in her response to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1. Defendants have not shown that Choi’s response to Interrogatory No. 17.1 is not code compliant.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 17.1 is DENIED.

 

d.     Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6

 

Form Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6 reference “agreements alleged in the pleadings.” (Park Decl., Exh. 1, p. 8.) 

 

Choi responded to Form Interrogatory Nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, by stating “there are no agreements as alleged by Propounding Party.” (Park Decl., Exh. 2, p. 10.) However, the Propounding Party has alleged the parties entered into agreements in the TACC. The TACC asserts causes of action for the breach of four oral contracts and alleges the parties entered into these oral agreements. Choi responded to Form Interrogatory Nos. 50.5 and 50.6 by referencing “the first four causes of action” in the TACC. However, the interrogatories inquire as to the agreements and not the causes of action. The information sought related to the alleged oral agreements is relevant to Defendants’ claims. Therefore, the Court finds that a further response is required.

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendants’ motion to compel further responses to form interrogatory nos. 50.1, 50.2, 50.3, 50.4, 50.5, 50.6 is GRANTED.

 

B.     Sanctions

 

The court may impose sanctions against any party for engaging in conduct constituting a “misuse of the discovery process.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030 (a).)¿Misuse of the discovery process includes “failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010 (d).) 

 

Defendants request that the Court award monetary sanctions against Choi and her attorney of record in the sum of $2,700.00. In opposition, Choi requests sanctions of $6,682.50 on grounds that Defendants brought this motion without substantial justification.¿ 

 

Here, Defendants’ motion is granted and denied in part. The Court does not exercise its discretion to impose sanctions.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

The request to compel further responses is granted and denied in part.

 

The parties shall meet and confer and define the term “Incident” within 10 days of the Court’s order.  

 

Plaintiff Choi shall serve supplemental responses within 30 days of the Court’s order.

 

Sanctions are denied.

LETESHIA DRISDOM, et al. v. JOSE MORENO III, et al. (22STCV28248) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Haig B. Kazandjian (Haig B. Kazandjian Lawyers, APC)

Counsel for Defendant/opposing party:  Barry Reagan (Slaughter, Reagan & Cole, LLP) Michael Halvorsen (Phillips, Spallas and Angstadt LLP) 

 

PETITION TO CONFIRM MINOR’S COMPROMISE (filed 3/28/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

The Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise is GRANTED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On August 30, 2022, Plaintiffs Leteshia Drisdom and Marshawn Hemmans, a minor by and through his guardian Leteshia Drisdom, filed this action against Defendants Jose Moreno III; 802 Anderson Road LLC; West 54th Street LLC; Jose Moreno, as Trustee under the Moreno Family Trust dated November 3, 2016; and Martha C. Moreno, as Trustee under the Moreno Family Trust dated November 3, 2016.

 

Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint on March 7, 2023, alleging causes of action for (1) Private Nuisance; (2) Negligence; (3) Breach of Warranty of Habitability; (4) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; (5) Breach of Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (6) Violation of CA Civil Code Section 1942.4 Wrongful Rent Collection; and (7) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.  

  

The FAC alleges that Plaintiffs seek monetary damages against Defendants for the improper and negligent maintenance of Plaintiffs’ leased premises, commonly referred to as 2315 W 54th St. Unit #4, Los Angeles, CA 90043. (FAC, ¶ 1.) Plaintiffs allege uninhabitable conditions of the rental property and claim slum housing conditions including: defective air conditioning, lack of water, lack of water pressure, lack of hot water, deteriorating and/or cracked ceilings, plumbing issues, defective ventilation, unsanitary refuse areas/bins, and damaged, cracked, and deteriorated common area walls/ceiling. (FAC, ¶ 2.)

 

The parties reached a settlement agreement in this case. (See Court’s 3/27/24 Minute Order.)

 

On March 28, 2024, Parent and Guardian Ad Litem Letishia Drisdom (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition to Approve Compromise of Pending Action (the “Petition”) on behalf of Claimant Marshawn Hemmans (Claimant”) 

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

“When a minor, a person who lacks legal capacity to make decisions, or a person for whom a conservator has been appointed is a party, that person shall appear either by a guardian or conservator of the estate or by a guardian ad litem appointed by the court in which the action or proceeding is pending, or by a judge thereof, in each case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(1).) 

 

The Court has reviewed the ¿Petition to Approve Compromise of Disputed Claim of a Minor. Defendants have offered to settle this matter in the total amount of $75,000. The Court has considered the Declaration of Petitioner Letishia Drisdom and the Declaration of Haig Kazandjian, Plaintiffs’ counsel, in support of the fee request. Of the total settlement payment of $75,000, the total settlement amount for Claimant/ Marshawn Hemmans is $5,000. Kazandjian states “the apportionment of the settlement payments between the Claimant and the other named Plaintiff is based on the damages claimed by each Plaintiff. The adult Plaintiff Leteshia Drisdom has claims for rent abatement that arose from the tenancy at the subject property as detailed in the complaint. The rent abatement claims make up a majority of the claimed damages.” (Kazandjian Decl., ¶ 6.) The petition states that Claimant suffered emotional distress damages and loss of value of their leasehold. (Petition, ¶ 6.) Attorney fees for Claimant/Marshawn Hemmans is based on the contingency fee agreement of 25%. (Kazandjian Decl., ¶ 9.) Thus, the total settlement balance, after attorney fees are deducted, for Claimant/Minor Marshawn Hemmans is $3,750.00. The Court finds that on the current record, and in consideration of the factors set forth in Cal Rules of Court, Rule 7.955(b), a fee of 25% of the gross settlement is reasonable.  The Court finds that the settlement is reasonable, and based thereon, approves and grants the petition. 

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS the Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise.

 

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

The Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise is GRANTED.
