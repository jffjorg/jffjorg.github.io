DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 23LBCV01401    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S27

This Court has, in the past year, seen a dramatic increase in motions to compel further responses in lemon law cases.  Similar issues are presented on all of the motions, and the attorneys for both parties fail and refuse to meet and confer in good faith in light of this Court’s prior rulings in order to resolve issues relating to each case.  The Court’s docket has been inundated with these motions, with discovery motions in lemon law cases often taking up more than five motion hearing slots in a week. 

 

The Court finds Counsel, with the assistance of this Court’s prior rulings, can easily resolve the issues on these discovery motions.  The Court has ruled on discovery motions concerning the instant defendant, General Motors, in the following cases (among others):

23LBCV00159

23LBCV00489

22LBCV00586

23LBCV00181

22LBCV00761

22LBCV00986

23LBCV00126

22LBCV00456

22LBCV00475

22LBCV00130

22LBCV00456

22LBCV00415

21LBCV00261

21LBCV00227

 

These motions are made more difficult because GM, in its opposing separate statement, makes the same argument concerning each and every discovery item at issue, rather than tailoring its argument to the specific discovery item in dispute. 

 

The Court has read and considered Volkswagenwerk Aktiengesellschaft v. Superior Court (1981) 122 Cal.App.3d 326, 331-334, wherein the Court of Appeals affirmed the trial court’s award of sanctions against BOTH attorneys on the case, which sanctions were ordered payable to the other party directly.  The Court finds the attorneys in these lemon law cases are acting in a way that does not benefit their clients, constantly incurring attorneys’ fees in connection with discovery disputes when they are already aware of how the Court will rule on the motions. 

 

The hearing on this motion is continued to Tuesday, 7/16/24 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27.  The Court expects Counsel to meet and confer, with this Court’s prior rulings in mind (rulings by other trial courts are not relevant and should not be used in the meet and confer discussion).  If Counsel are unable to resolve all of their issues prior to the continued hearing on the motion, they must file a joint statement of items that remain in dispute.  The joint statement must list each discovery item in dispute, the most current response to that item, and each party’s statement of why a further response should or should not be ordered.  The joint statement must make arguments relevant only to the item in dispute, not to the other items, so the Court can easily and quickly rule on each item. 

 

If the parties resolve their issues, they must take the motions off calendar using the online reservation system.  If they are unable to resolve their issues, they must also file short (no more than five pages) briefs and declarations detailing their efforts to meet and confer and making any argument against imposition of sanctions that they wish to articulate. 

 

Joint statement, briefs, and declarations are due at least two weeks prior to the continued hearing date. 

 

The Court notes that the FSC and trial in this case are scheduled for 7/19/24 and 7/22/24 respectively.  The Court is hopeful that the FSC and trial dates can remain in place, but will consider briefly continuing them if the parties are unable to swiftly resolve their discovery dispute. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative.  If the parties do not submit on the tentative, they should arrange to appear remotely.


Case Number: 23LBCV02017    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Damon Scott Stansbury filed this action against Defendants, Harborlight Landing, LLC, et al. for damages arising out of an incident that occurred on 1109/22.  Specifically, Plaintiff alleges Defendant, Anders Stefan Thene, assaulted and battered him at a marina owned, operated, managed, controlled, and/or otherwise occupied by the remaining defendants. 

 

2.     Motion to Quash

  1. Parties’ Positions (original papers)

Defendants, Hilton Worldwide Holdings, Inc., Hilton Franchise Holding LLC and Hilton Domestic Operating Company, Inc. (hereafter collectively referred to as “Hilton”) move to quash service of the summons and complaint on them, contending the Court has neither general nor specific jurisdiction over them, and therefore the action against them should be dismissed.

 

Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, contends the incident occurred on property owned by the Maya Hotel, which is the moving defendants’ franchisee.  Plaintiff contends the franchise agreement provides numerous standards the franchisee is required to uphold, thus showing the moving defendants retain control over the property.  Plaintiff contends the moving defendants have purposely availed them of the benefits of this jurisdiction by virtue of their franchise relationships, and contends the moving defendants’ acts in the forum state are directly relevant to this lawsuit, which alleges Maya Hotel staff, in keeping with the moving defendants’ training standards, failed to protect Plaintiff from the assault that occurred on the property.  Plaintiff, in the event the Court is inclined to grant the motion, asks for a continuance to conduct jurisdictional discovery.

 

In reply, Defendant argues Plaintiff failed to meet his burden to show jurisdiction is proper because the agreement upon which he relies is only an exemplar agreement, and not the agreement that binds the franchisor and franchisee in this case.  It argues the cases permitting imposition of jurisdiction based on a franchisor/franchisee relationship have involved an additional factual showing linking the agreement to the claims of the case.  It argues Plaintiff has not made a showing linking training or lack of training to this case because he has used only an exemplar agreement, and the exemplar agreement does not have the level of training specificity that would be required to show a link between the purported minimum contacts and the incident that forms the basis of this lawsuit.

 

  1. Prior Hearing

The Court was originally scheduled to hear this motion on 3/28/24.  The Court continued the hearing date to permit Plaintiff to conduct jurisdictional discovery.

  1. Supplemental Papers

Plaintiff, in his supplemental opposition to the motion, argues the franchise agreement between Moving Defendant and Hotel Maya contains an extensive “standards” section that include training and training materials, and that permit Defendant to terminate the franchise agreement in the event of non-compliance.  He contends Defendant is subject to jurisdiction in light of the franchise agreement.  In the alternative, he seeks leave to conduct additional discovery as to the management and operation of the hotel, including training of employees. 

 

Defendant, in supplemental reply, contends the opposition should be disregarded because it was untimely filed.  It contends a franchise agreement with general provisions governing standards applicable to all Hilton hotels to ensure the Hilton name and brand are not diluted is insufficient to support imposition of jurisdiction. 

 

  1. Initial Note re: Opposition

In light of the Juneteenth holiday, Plaintiff’s supplemental opposition papers were due on or before 6/13/24.  As Defendant correctly notes in reply, they were untimely filed and served on 6/17/24.  Because dismissal is a harsh remedy, the Court has read and considered the opposition papers.  Plaintiff is admonished to ensure timely filing and service of all future papers.

 

  1. Redacted Agreement

Plaintiff filed a redacted copy of the subject franchise agreement.  Plaintiff also quoted the agreement at length in the supplemental opposition papers, but blacked out the provisions of the agreement on which he relies.  Plaintiff did not file a motion to seal, and did not follow the procedure in CRC 2.551(b)(3) to lodge agreements subject to confidentiality agreement or protective order without seeking a sealing order.  This is not a situation where the Court can simply review the redacted points and authorities and redacted agreement and rule on the motion; Plaintiff contends the redacted portions of the agreement are the evidence that support his jurisdictional argument.

 

  1. Continuance

Pursuant to Elkman v. National States inc. Co. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1313, the burden of proof is on the plaintiff to show, by a preponderance of the evidence, that the defendant had sufficient minimum contacts with the forum state to justify imposition of personal jurisdiction over it. 

 

 

Pursuant to HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.Ap.4th 1160, 1173, the trial court has discretion to continue the hearing on a motion to quash to permit the plaintiff to conduct jurisdictional discovery. 

 

Plaintiff, in his supplemental opposition, indicates there is outstanding discovery, including written discovery and depositions, that has not been completed.  He seeks such a continuance in the opposition, and Defendants do not contest the request in reply. 

 

The hearing on the motion is continued for 90 days, to Tuesday, 10/01/24 at 8:30 a.m. in Department S27.  Plaintiff is permitted to conduct jurisdictional discovery ONLY in support of his case against the moving defendants during this time (Plaintiff’s rights to conduct other discovery as to other defendants are not affected by this ruling).  The parties must file supplemental opposition and supplemental reply per Code.

 

If the parties want the Court to rely on documents that they believes are confidential, the parties must follow the procedures set forth in the Rules of Court to have the documents sealed.  They must lodge an unredacted copy of all documents on eCourt so the Court can review the unredacted documents prior to the hearing.

 

Moving Defendants are ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV02156    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Tracy McCrackin filed this action against Defendants, Uber Technologies, Inc. and William Doe for negligence, IIED, NIED, negligent hiring, supervision, and retention, violation of the Bane Act, and violation of the Ralph Act.  Plaintiff alleges William Doe, a driver for Uber, picked her up, but instead of going south to Long Beach as requested and instructed on the maps app, William began driving north on the freeway away from her destination.  She alleges that she repeatedly asked William to let her out of the car, but he refused.  She called her husband, who also asked him to let her out of the car, but he continued to refuse.  Ultimately, William exited the freeway in Carson, turned around, dropped Plaintiff at the Aquarium (not her chosen destination), and sped off. 

 

  1. Motion to Compel Arbitration

a.     Parties’ Positions

Uber moves to compel arbitration, contending Plaintiff agreed to the terms of service in 2016 and again in 2021, and both times the terms of service including an arbitration provision. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  She argues there is a factual dispute as to whether she signed the 2016 agreement.  She argues the 2021 agreement cannot be enforced because the agreement excepts claims for gender-related violence, which is what she asserts in this case.  Finally, she contends there is a risk of inconsistent rulings if the motion is granted, because she will have to arbitrate her claims against Uber but litigate her claims against William. 

 

Uber, in reply, indicates it will proceed based solely on the 2021 agreement, which Plaintiff does not dispute accepting.  It indicates any determination as to whether the 2021 agreement should be enforced or not is delegated to the arbitrator.  If the Court is inclined to interpret the 2021 agreement, it contends the agreement excepts only claims for gender-related assault and harassment, not for kidnapping, which is alleged in the complaint.  Finally, it contends §1281.2(c) cannot be applied to preclude arbitration because the parties’ agreement requires interpretation under the FAA, which does not have an exception for potential conflicting rulings.

 

b.     Arbitration Agreement

Plaintiff contends she did not accept the 2016 agreement.  Uber, in reply, indicates it will proceed solely based on the 2021 agreement, which Plaintiff does not deny accepting by agreeing to the terms of service.  The Court, therefore, finds Plaintiff agreed to the 2021 arbitration provision when she agreed to the 2021 terms of service. 

 

c.     FAA or State Law

Plaintiff argues state law precludes enforcement of the arbitration agreement (see discussion below).  Thus, the Court must determine whether state law or the FAA applies.  §2 of the parties’ agreement provides that the FAA applies. 

 

Even where there is no evidence that the parties' dealings involve interstate commerce, the parties may elect to have the FAA govern enforcement of their agreement, thereby preempting application of state law.  Victrola 89, LLC v. Jaman Properties 8 LLC (2020) 46 Cal.App.5th 337, 354-355.  Thus, the Court finds the FAA, not state law, governs the parties’ relationship. 

 

d.     Arbitrator or Court Determination of Interpretation of Agreement

Plaintiff contends the 2021 agreement does not require her to arbitrate her claims against Uber because the 2021 agreement exempts claims for gender-related violence.  She cites to a provision in the agreement, §2(b), which provides that the agreement does not require arbitration of claims involving sexual assault or sexual harassment. 

 

Uber contends the arbitrator must interpret this provision and determine whether or not Plaintiff’s claims are arbitrable.  Uber cites to §2 of the agreement, which makes clear that the arbitrator has the exclusive authority to resolve the threshold issue of arbitrability. 

 

An arbitration clause that “clearly and unmistakably” empowers the arbitrator to decide what matters are arbitrable will doubtless be given effect. (E.g., “The Arbitrator, and not any federal, state, or local court or agency, shall have exclusive authority to resolve any dispute relating to the interpretation, applicability, enforceability or formation of this Agreement including, but not limited to any claim that all or any part of this Agreement is void or voidable.”)   Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson (2010) 561 US 63, 66, 68-69, fn. 1; B.D v. Blizzard Entertainment, Inc. (2022) 76 Cal.App.5th 931, 957-958. 

 

Notably, the U.S. Supreme Court resolved the issue for cases decided under the FAA, holding that where an arbitration agreement clearly and unmistakably delegates to an arbitrator the determination of arbitrability of a claim or a dispute, there is no exception allowing a court to ignore that arbitration provision just because the court agrees with the opposing party's contention that the moving party's assertion of arbitrability is “wholly groundless.” That exception is inconsistent with the FAA (citing Rent-A-Center, West, Inc. v. Jackson (2010) 561 US 63, 68-70). Arbitration is a creature of contract, and the question of who decides arbitrability is a question of contract that the FAA allows parties to determine. The Supreme Court abrogated contrary federal cases. 

 

Thus, the Court finds the arbitrator must determine arbitrability. 

 

The Court notes, but does not decide, that Plaintiff’s complaint does not appear to sound in sexual assault or sexual harassment, but instead in kidnapping.  The Court does not wish to minimize the experience, but the experience described in the complaint does not lend itself to the conclusion that Plaintiff was assaulted or harassed based on her gender. 

 

e.     CCP §1281.2(c)

Plaintiff’s final contention is that the Court should deny the motion because requiring her to arbitrate her claims against Uber and litigate her claims against William could result in conflicting rulings. As Uber correctly notes in reply, the FAA does not have a provision akin to §1281.2(c), and any agreement interpreted under the FAA must be enforced despite the risk of inconsistent rulings.  See Victrola 89, LLC, supra, at 346-347. 

 

f.      Conclusion

Uber successfully established there is a binding arbitration agreement between Plaintiff and Uber.  Plaintiff failed to show a defense to the agreement.  The motion to compel arbitration is therefore granted.  The action against Uber is stayed pending the outcome of arbitration. 

 

Uber is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 23LBCV02241    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Complaint

Plaintiff, Michael Brown filed this action against Defendants, Community Loan Servicing, LLC and Nationstar Mortgage, LLC (erroneously sued and served as Community Loan Services Right Path Servicing) on 11/22/23.  The complaint includes a cause of action for constructive fraud and related claims.  The complaint arises out of Defendants’ servicing of Plaintiff’s loan and attempts at foreclosure on the loan.

 

2.     Demurrer

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Procedural History

Defendants filed their demurrer on 3/21/24, setting it for hearing on 8/20/24.  Thereafter, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend to add a third defendant, Rushmore, to the complaint, but expressly indicated it would still rule on the demurrer.  The Court rescheduled the demurrer for hearing on 6/27/24. 

 

On 5/13/24, Plaintiff filed opposition to the original demurrer.  On 5/20/24, Defendants filed an amended demurrer, which merely reflects the fact that Plaintiff is now suing three entities instead of two.  Plaintiff has not filed opposition to the amended demurrer, but it appears he intends his opposition to the original demurrer to serve as his opposition to the amended demurrer.

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he and Plaintiff met and conferred many times prior to the filing of the demurrer, but were unable to resolve the issues presented by way of the demurrer.  The meet and confer requirement is therefore satisfied. 

 

  1. First Cause of Action, Constructive Fraud

Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for constructive fraud, contending the parties are not in a fiduciary relationship and therefore no claim for constructive fraud can be pled.  Defendants cite numerous cases holding that lenders and borrowers are not in a fiduciary relationship.  See, for example, Nymar v. Heart Fed. Sav. & Loan Ass’n (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1089, 1093. 

 

Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, argues Defendants were not lenders, but instead loan servicers, especially when providing a loan modification, do stand in a fiduciary relationship with the persons seeking modification.  Plaintiff does not cite any case so holding, and instead cites the general law regarding fiduciary duties. 

 

Defendants, however, addressed this argument in their moving papers.  They noted that numerous federal courts have held that loan servicers do not owe fiduciary duties to borrowers.  See, for example, Shepherd v. Am. Home Mortg. Servs. (E.D. Cal.) 2009 LEXIS 108523. 

 

While neither party cites any on-point California state law authority, the Court finds the reasoning of the various federal cases cited to be compelling.  If a bank or lender does not owe a fiduciary duty, then it seems reasonable that a servicer’s duties would also not be fiduciary in nature.  Absent a fiduciary duty, a claim for constructive fraud fails.  See Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1249-50. 

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action for constructive fraud is sustained.  Because this is a purely legal issue, leave to amend is denied.

 

  1. Promissory Estoppel

Plaintiff’s second cause of action is for promissory estoppel.  Defendants demur to the cause of action on two grounds.  First, they contend the complaint fails to allege any promise with sufficient specificity for them to respond.  Second, they contend any promise alleged was made by Community, but not by Nationstar or Rushmore, such that the cause of action fails against them.

 

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend on the first ground.  The second cause of action merely indicates there was a promise with no intent to perform, but fails to indicate what the promise was.  The opposition is also silent in this regard.  It appears the promise was the modification.  Notably, the complaint purports to attach various exhibits, but none are attached.  Plaintiff must amend his complaint to clearly state, in the body of the second cause of action, what promise was made, by whom, when, and how he relied on that promise.  If the promise was made in writing, he must attach as an exhibit to the amended complaint the subject writing.

 

The demurrer on the second ground is overruled.  Defendants do not brief the issue, but it appears clear that any subsequent loan servicer would step into the shoes of a prior servicer, and would be bound by any promises made by that prior servicer.  If Defendants believe this is not a correct statement of the law, they must adequately brief the issue in a pleading challenge directed at Plaintiff’s amended complaint.

  1. Negligence

Plaintiff’s third cause of action is for negligence.  Defendants contend the cause of action fails because they have no duties, outside of those created by contract, to their borrowers.  Additionally, they contend Plaintiff has not alleged the breach of any duty owed to him. 

 

Defendants cite Sheen v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 905, 915 to support their position that lenders do not owe a duty of care to borrowers outside of the scope of the parties’ contract.  Plaintiff cites nothing to the contrary in opposition.  He argues loan servicers should not be allowed to abuse their position.  The Court agrees.  The Court finds, however, that the relationship is governed by the original loan agreement and any subsequent modification thereto, and any cause of action would sound in contract, not in negligence.  If Plaintiff can allege Defendants breached a term of the contract running between them and Plaintiff, then he can state a claim.  He cannot, however, state a claim sounding in negligence, as there is no duty between Defendants and Plaintiff outside the four corners of the parties’ contract(s).  The demurrer is therefore sustained.  Leave to amend is denied as to negligence, but granted as to breach of contract if Plaintiff can plead the terms of a specific contract that were breached.

 

  1. Intentional Misrepresentation

Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is for intentional misrepresentation. 

 

The elements of fraud are: (1) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); (3) intent to defraud or induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) damages.  (See Civil Code §1709.)  Fraud actions are subject to strict requirements of particularity in pleading.  Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal. 3d 197, 216.

Since allegations of fraud involve a serious attack on character, fairness to the defendant demands that he should receive the fullest possible details of the charge in order to prepare his defense.  Accordingly the rule is everywhere followed that fraud must be specifically pleaded, such that: (a) General pleading of the legal conclusion of “fraud” is insufficient; the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged; and (b) Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner (i.e., factually and specifically), and the policy of liberal construction of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked to sustain a pleading defective in any material respect.  Id.  

 

Defendants, in their demurrer, correctly note that Plaintiff failed to plead the claim for misrepresentation with the requisite specificity.  Plaintiff, in opposition to the demurrer, argues the modification itself was the misrepresentation because Defendants then never honored the modification.  As noted above, Plaintiff purported to attach the modification as an exhibit to his complaint, but there are no exhibits to the complaint.  To the extent Plaintiff is alleging the modification itself is the fraud, Plaintiff must attach a copy to his amended complaint.

 

Plaintiff next argues Defendants have been “too many misrepresentations to recount.”  Plaintiff must plead those misrepresentations with the requisite specificity, detailed above, if he wishes to state a claim based on those misrepresentations. 

 

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.

 

  1. BPC §17200

Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is for violation of BPC §17200, et seq.  Defendants correctly note that the cause of action for violation of BPC §17200 fails because Plaintiff has not stated any substantive claim against them.  Plaintiff does not address this argument in opposition to the demurrer.  The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend for the reasons discussed above.

 

  1. Violation of Civil Code §§2924.11 and 2924.17

Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action is for violation of Civil Code §§2924.11 and 2924.17.  §2924.11 precludes a mortgage servicer or related entity from initiating foreclosure proceedings if a foreclosure prevention alternative is approved in writing prior to recordation of a notice of default. 

 

Defendants correctly note that nothing Plaintiff alleges they did constitutes a violation of the section.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants refused to review evidence and refused payments.  Plaintiff does not, however, allege Defendants initiated foreclosure proceedings.  The demurrer is therefore sustained.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiff can allege an actual violation of the section.

 

Plaintiff concedes his claim under §2924.17 fails, and therefore the demurrer to that cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  Plaintiff indicates he intended to use under Penal Code §15610.30.  Plaintiff may amend to state a claim if he is able to do so. 

 

  1. Conclusion

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained with leave to amend in part and without leave to amend in part as set forth fully above.  Plaintiff must file an amended complaint within twenty days.  Defendants must file a responsive pleading within the statutory time thereafter. 

 

3.     Ex Parte Application

On 2/26/24, Plaintiff filed an ex parte application for a temporary restraining order and an OSC re: preliminary injunction.  On 2/28/24, the Court granted the application and scheduled a hearing on the request for a preliminary injunction for 4/26/24.  The Coutt ordered the parties to submit briefs by 4/12/24.

 

4.     Motion for Preliminary Injunction

a.     Law Governing Issuance of a Preliminary Injunction

“In deciding whether to issue a preliminary injunction, a court must weigh two ‘interrelated’ factors: (1) the likelihood that the moving party will ultimately prevail on the merits and (2) the relative interim harm to the parties from issuance or nonissuance of the injunction. Common Cause v. Board of Supervisors (1989) 49 Cal.3d 432, 441-442.  Appellate review is limited to whether the trial court's decision was an abuse of discretion.”  Butt v. State of California (1992) 4 Cal.4th 668, 677-678.

 

b.     RJN

On 4/12/24, Defendants filed a request for judicial notice of the complaint, deed of trust, and ex parte application papers.  The RJN Is granted. 

 

c.     Parties’ Positions

Plaintiff seeks a preliminary injunction precluding Defendants from going forward with any sale of his property.  Plaintiff contends he entered into a loan modification agreement with Defendants’ predecessor, and Defendants are wrongfully refusing to honor the agreement.  plaintiff contends he will suffer irreparable harm if his home is sold. 

 

On 4/12/24, Defendants filed points and authorities concerning Plaintiff’s request for relief.  Defendants correctly note that Plaintiff’s entire action is premised on his allegations that his prior loan servicer, Community, entered into a loan modification agreement with him in September of 2020, and that Defendants, the current loan servicers, are not honoring the terms of the loan modification agreement.  Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not provided a copy of the loan modification agreement with his moving papers.    

 

Plaintiff filed reply papers the day before the original hearing on this motion.  The Court was not able to review the papers prior to the hearing, and therefore continued the hearing to consider all papers presented.  Plaintiff in his reply, contends he did provide a copy of the modification agreement, as well as all related documents, with his original application. 

 

b.     Analysis

The Court has reviewed the documents submitted with the original application.  Notably, the documents were not authenticated.  The first attached exhibit appears to be a modification agreement between Bayview (Community’s predecessor) and Plaintiff, dated 9/01/20.  The modification agreement provides for monthly payments in the amount of $853.33 beginning on 9/01/20 and continuing until 8/01/60.  The agreement is not signed by Bayview and is only signed by Plaintiff.  It appears, however, to have been professionally drafted, so the Court can only presume that it was drafted by Bayview. 

 

Plaintiff then provides copies of monthly payments in the amount of $1106.23 made between May of 2020 and July of 2022.  It appears, per Exhibit 3, that the difference between $853.33 and $1106.23 was the property taxes that were being impounded into the monthly payment. 

 

Plaintiff then provides documents as Exhibits 4-5 that the Court cannot clearly determine the purpose of.  It appears these documents are to show Plaintiff attempted to continue to make his required monthly payments, but they were not accepted.  Plaintiff must be prepared, at the hearing, to explain what these documents are and what they show. 

 

If the Court is satisfied, after the hearing, with Plaintiff’s showing that Defendants are in breach of their obligations under the modification agreement, the Court will grant the request for a preliminary injunction precluding Defendants from initiating foreclosure proceedings.  The Court notes that the balancing of harms clearly weighs in favor of Plaintiff, who will lose his home if Defendants are allowed to foreclose. 

 

3.         Case Management Conference

The parties are reminded that there is a CMC on calendar today concurrently with the hearing on the demurrer and motion for preliminary injunction.  The Court asks the parties to make arrangements to appear remotely at the CMC and hearing on the demurrer and motion.
