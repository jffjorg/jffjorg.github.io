DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV00808    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: H

Murillo v. Dr. Privitera’s Holistic Clinic, et al., Case No. 21PSCV00808


ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT’S MOTIONS

The Court DENIES Defendant Privitera’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings and Amended Motion for Disqualification.


Background 

In this employment discrimination case, Plaintiff Malissa Murillo (“Plaintiff”) alleges that while working for Defendants Dr. Privitera’s Holistic Clinic (the “Clinic”), Alternative Endeavors, Inc. (“Endeavors”), and Anakin Mark Privitera (“Privitera”) (collectively, “Defendants”) as an office assistant at the Clinic, Privitera made lewd and vulgar comments in her presence on a repeated basis and made sexual advances to her.

On October 5, 2021, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Defendants and Does 1-40 for (1) discrimination based on sexual harassment in violation of the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) and (2) failure to timely pay wages owed.

On December 23, 2021, the Clerk entered default against Endeavors. On April 18, 2022, the Clerk entered default against the Clinic.

On April 24, 2023, the court granted Endeavors’ motion to set aside default and motion to quash service of summons.

A Mandatory Settlement Conference is set for October 3, 2024. The Final Status Conference is set for October 30, 2024. The Court Trial is set for November 8, 2024. 

Pending before the Court are Privitera’s motions for judgment on the pleadings and for disqualification of plaintiff’s counsel. For the reasons stated herein, both motions are denied.

 

1. Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings 

Legal Standard 

The rules governing demurrers are generally applicable to a motion for judgment on the pleadings. (Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999; Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (d) [“The grounds for motion. . . shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. Where the motion is based on a matter of which the court may take judicial notice. . ., the matter shall be specified in the notice of motion, or in the supporting points and authorities, except as the court may otherwise permit”].)

 

A motion by a plaintiff may only be made on the grounds “that the complaint states facts sufficient to constitute a cause or causes of action against the defendant and the answer does not state facts sufficient to constitute a defense to the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c)(1)(A).) A motion by a defendant may only be made on the grounds that (1) “[t]he court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the complaint” or (2) “[t]he complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (c).)
 

Discussion

“Defendants” move the court, per Code of Civil Procedure section 438, for judgment on the pleadings, on the basis that Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Privitera.

As an initial matter, the Court notes that, while the motion purports to have been filed by “Defendants,” it will be construed as having been brought by Privitera only, inasmuch as the Clinic is in default and Endeavors, as a corporation, must be represented by counsel (Caressa Camille, Inc. v. Alcoholic Beverage Control Appeals Bd. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101 [“As a general rule, it is well established in California that a corporation cannot represent itself in a court of record either in propria persona or through an officer or agent who is not an attorney.”]). Privitera is not a licensed attorney. Accordingly, the motion must be construed as seeking relief as to Privitera only.

The Court also notes that the reply brief filed September 13, 2024 indicates that “Defendants” are also moving to dismiss the matter for improper service. The Court declines to entertain this request because it is beyond the scope of the moving papers. The Court also declines to review the exhibits attached to the reply brief as improper for consideration on a motion for judgment on the pleadings.

Although a nonstatutory motion “may be made at any time either prior to the trial or at the trial itself” (Stoops v. Abbassi (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 644, 650 [quotation marks and citation omitted]), a statutory motion cannot be made after entry of a pretrial conference order or 30 days before the initial trial date, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (e).)

 

The initial trial date in this matter was July 14, 2023. The instant motion was filed nearly a year afterwards, on July 5, 2024. The Court denies Privitera’s statutory motion as untimely.

 

2. Amended Motion to Disqualify

Legal Standard

Rule 1.7 of the California Rules of Professional Conduct addresses conflicts of interest and explains when a lawyer must obtain informed written consent or disclose a conflict of interest to a client. For instance, “[a] lawyer shall not, without informed written consent from each client . . . represent a client if the representation is directly adverse to another client in the same or a separate matter” or “if there is a significant risk the lawyer’s representation of the client will be materially limited by the lawyer’s responsibilities to or relationships with another client, a former client or a third person, or by the lawyer’s own interests.” (Cal. R. Prof. Conduct [“CRPC”] 1.7.) 

“Whether an attorney should be disqualified is a matter addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court.” (National Grange of Order of Patrons of Husbandry v. California Guild (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 706, 713 [quotations and citation omitted].) 

 

Discussion

Privitera moves the court for an order disqualifying Plaintiff’s counsel Thomas Byrnes (“Byrnes”) and Marcy Railsback (“Railsback”).[FN1]

At the outset, the Court notes that it declines to consider the parties’ September 13, 2024 filings relating to this motion. Specifically, the Court declines to consider: (1) Plaintiff’s 16-page document entitled “Objections to ‘Defendant’s Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to Defendant’s Motion to Disqualify Plaintiff’s Counsel’ and Request that Motion and ‘Reply’ be Stricken, and Privitera Admonished and Instructed” and (2) Privitera’s 107-page document entitled “Amended Defendant’s Reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to Motion for Disqualification; Points and Authorities in Support; Request for Exception to Submit an (Amended) Reply Due to New Facts that Have Arisen.” Both filings are not authorized by Code, were not permitted by the Court, and constitute an improper attempt to circumvent the page limitations of the California Rules of Court.

Turning to the merits of Privitera’s motion, Privitera has not demonstrated that Byrnes’ and/or Railsback’s representation of Plaintiff in this matter constitutes any conflict of interest contemplated by the California Rules of Professional Conduct.[FN2] Moreover, while Privitera appears to argue that Byrnes has blackmailed him or otherwise acted inappropriately, Privitera’s contention is not supported by any declaration. The Court declines to consider Exhibits 1-4 attached to the motion as they have not been sufficiently identified and authenticated.

The Court also declines to consider any new authority and/or argument raised for the first time in Privitera’s reply. (American Drug Stores, Inc. v. Stroh (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1446, 1453 [“Points raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive the respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument”]; Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1537 [“[N]ew evidence is not permitted with reply papers”].)

Privitera’s motion is denied.


[FN1] While Privitera’s notice of motion is limited to seeking disqualification of Byrnes, in his accompanying memorandum of points and authorities, he appears to seek disqualification of Railsback as well. Notwithstanding California Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(a), which requires that the notice of motion state the “nature of the order being sought and the grounds for issuance of the order,” the Court will construe the motion as seeking disqualification of both Byrnes and Railsback, as Plaintiff has failed to articulate any prejudice by this omission in the notice.

[FN2] Privitera repeatedly cites to Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 3-310; however, Rule 3-310 has been superseded by Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.7, which went into effect on November 1, 2018. Rule 1.7 is entitled “Conflict of Interest: Current Clients.”
