DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24VECV00571    Hearing Date: November 22, 2024    Dept: 107

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Asha Enterprises, LLC,

Plaintiff,

 

      

   v.

 

Wilmington Savings Fund Society, FSB, et al.,

Defendants.

 

 

 

   Case Number             Department

    24VECV00571             107

 

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens 

 

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:

(1) Grant Motion; (2) Grant Wilmington’s attorneys’ fees request in the sum of $9,556.50.

                                               

  1. BACKGROUND

 

  1. Factual Background

 

The following factual background is drawn from the first amended complaint.

 

On March 15, 2022, Asha Enterprises, LLC (“Plaintiff”) purchased property located at 4252 Tosca Road, Woodland Hills, CA 91364 (the “Subject Property”). (First Amended Complaint, “FAC” ¶ 8.) On or about April 6, 2022, Plaintiff borrowed $846,000.00 from Conventus, LLC. (FAC ¶ 9.) To secure the promissory note, Plaintiff executed a deed of trust to Servicelink Title Company, as trustee, for the benefit of Conventus, LLC, the beneficiary. (FAC ¶ 9.) On March 17, 2023, Conventus, LLC assigned the deed of trust to Defendant Wilmington Savings Fund Society, FSB (“Wilmington”). (FAC ¶¶ 2, 10.)

 

On April 1, 2023, the promissory note matured, and Plaintiff, through its manager and member, Ardeshir Behdinan (“Behdinan”) asked Conventus for an extension of time to pay back the loan. (FAC ¶¶ 11-12.)  On May 29, 2023, Wilmington substituted Defendant California TD Specialists as substitute trustee. (FAC ¶¶ 3, 14.)

 

On June 9, 2023, Defendant California TD Specialists executed a notice of default and election to sell under the deed of trust. (FAC ¶ 15.) A few days later, Defendant California TD Specialists recorded the notice of default. (FAC ¶ 15.) On June 20, 2023, Behdinan, on Plaintiff’s behalf, sought another extension of the loan. (FAC ¶ 16.)

 

On August 15, 2023, Micelle Guillen of Defendant New York Mortgage Trust, Inc. (“NYMT”) emailed Plaintiff stating the lender would reinstate the loan and extend the maturity note to October 1, 2023, if Plaintiff paid $37,755.30 and executed documentation. (FAC ¶¶ 4, 18.)

 

On September 19, 2023, Defendant California TD Specialists recorded a notice of trustee’s sale which provided notice of an October 11, 2023, trustee’s sale of the Subject Property. (FAC ¶ 20.) On October 3, 2023, Conventus told Behdinan the loan was assigned to NYMT. (FAC ¶ 20.) The next day, Guillen told Behdinan the offer was no longer available, and an expedited payoff demand was ordered. (FAC ¶ 21.) NYMT’s representatives told Behdinan the sale would be postponed from October 11, 2023, to October 24, 2023, to allow Plaintiff to close the sale to a third party and pay off the loan through the sale escrow. (FAC ¶ 22.) On or about October 11, 2023, Behdinan sent Defendants the settlement statement on the pending escrow sale. (FAC ¶ 23.)

 

On October 24, 2023, representatives of California TD Specialists and NYMT told Behdinan the sale would be postponed to October 31, 2023, so Plaintiff could close the Subject Property’s sale. (FAC ¶ 24.) However, the trustee’s sale went forward on October 24, 2023, and Wilmington was the winning bidder with a $910,057.51 bid. (FAC ¶ 24.)

 

Behdinan asked Defendants for the right to redeem the property, but Defendants refused. (FAC ¶ 25.) Plaintiff alleges that at all times it “was ready, willing and able to tender the amount of the payoff, and had an investor who had already tendered funds to Stewart Title for purchase of Property with instructions to pay all liens, including that of Defendants from such funds” but Defendants “wrongfully failed and refused to provide the necessary and indispensable information to escrow or continue the trustee’s sale to October 31, 2024 to allow the escrow to close despite their representations that they would cooperate.”  (FAC ¶ 26.) Plaintiff alleges that “[i]f the Defendants had not made those representations, Plaintiff would have filed a voluntary petition for reorganization under Chapter 11 of the Bankruptcy Code to preserve the Property and permit the sale escrow to close with Bankruptcy Court approval.” (FAC ¶ 26.)

 

  1. Procedural Background

 

On February 8, 2024, Plaintiff Asha Enterprises, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Wilmington, California TD Specialists, New York Mortgage Trust, Inc. (“NYMT”), all persons unknown, claiming any legal or equitable right, title, estate, lien, or interest in the property described in the complaint adverse to Plaintiff’s claims, or any cloud thereto, and Does 1 through 10 alleging six causes of action for set aside foreclosure sale, quiet title, cancel trustee’s deed, declaratory and injunctive relief, negligent misrepresentation, and intentional misrepresentation.

 

On April 17, 2024, Defendants Wilmington and NYMT demurred to Plaintiff’s complaint. On June 12, 2024, this court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend. (06/12/24 Minute Order p. 6.)

 

On June 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint against Defendants Wilmington, California TD Specialists, NYMT, and Does 1 through 10 (“Defendants”), alleging two causes of action for quiet title and cancellation of trustee’s deed.

 

On October 18, 2024, Wilmington filed a motion to expunge lis pendens (the “Motion”). On November 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On November 14, 2024, Wilmington filed a reply.

 

  1. LEGAL STANDARD

 

“[A] lis pendens is a recorded document giving constructive notice that an action has been filed affecting title or right to possession of the real property described in the notice. Its effect is that anyone acquiring an interest in the property after the action was filed will be bound by the judgment…. Once a lis pendens is filed, it clouds the title and effectively prevents the property's transfer until the litigation is resolved or the lis pendens is expunged.” (BGJ Associates, LLC v. Superior Court (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 952, 967.)¿ 

¿ 

“At any time after notice of pendency of action has been recorded, any party, or any nonparty with an interest in the real property affected thereby, may apply to the court in which the action is pending to expunge the notice…. Evidence or declarations may be filed with the motion to expunge the notice. The court may permit evidence to be received in the form of oral testimony, and may make any orders it deems just to provide for discovery by any party affected by a motion to expunge the notice. The claimant shall have the burden of proof under Sections 405.31 and 405.32.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.30.)¿The court shall order the expungement if it finds the pleading on which the lis pendens was based “does not contain a real property claim,” or if it finds the party who filed the pleading “has not established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§¿405.31, 405.32.) “The party opposing a motion to expunge a lis pendens has the burden to show that a real property claim has been alleged and has probable validity based upon a preponderance of the evidence.” (Nunn v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 346, 363.)¿ 

 

  1. THE MOTION, OPPOSITION, AND REPLY

 

  1. Wilmington’s Arguments

 

Wilmington argues Plaintiff cannot establish a probable validity of its causes of action for the following reasons. First, the email communications show that Conventus and NYMT offered Plaintiff loan extensions, but Plaintiff did not accept the offers. (Motion p. 15.) Second, Plaintiff received multiple payoff demands. (Motion p. 16.) Third, Plaintiff did not forward the payoff demand to the escrow entity and the purported lender did not tender any funds to escrow. (Motion p. 16.) Fourth, an oral agreement to postpone a trustee sale is not enforceable under the Statute of Frauds. (Motion pp. 17-18.) Fifth, the Estimated Settlement Statement attached to the first amended complaint as Exhibit 10 contains false buyer/borrower information because the purported buyer, Ember Holdings, LLC, does not exist. (Motion p. 18.) Finally, Wilmington argues that Plaintiff cannot overcome the presumption of sale validity because there was no irregularity in the trustee sale procedure, and the lis pendens is defective because it was not recorded with this court. (Motion pp. 19-20.)

 

Wilmington further argues that the lis pendens is defective because it was not filed with this court. (Motion p. 20.)

 

To support its Motion, Wilmington submits exhibits and declarations by California TD Specialists’ Trustee Sale Officer, Janina Hoak, NYMT’s Vice President, David Pourdavoudi, and Defendants NYMT’s and Wilmington’s counsel, Kathy Shakibi. (Hoak Declaration ¶ 1; Pourdavoudi Declaration ¶ 1; Shakibi Declaration ¶ 1.)

 

  1. Plaintiff’s Opposition

 

Plaintiff argues it has demonstrated the existence and probable validity of its quiet title cause of action because the first amended complaint details Plaintiff’s title and its basis, the complaint identifies and addresses the adverse claims against which Plaintiff is seeking a title determination, and the first amended complaint contains a detailed description of the property, the specific date as of which the determination is sought, and a prayer for determination of Plaintiff’s title. (Opposition pp. 3-4.)

 

Plaintiff also argues it has demonstrated the existence and probable validity of its cancellation of trustee’s deed cause of action because the trustee’s sale did not result in a sale to a bona fide purchaser, it merely reverted to the assignee of the original note, Defendants engaged in fraudulent and willfully oppressive conduct by strategically delaying the transmittal of the payment demand, and Plaintiff was harmed by Defendants’ actions. (Opposition p. 5.)

 

To support its burden, Plaintiff relies on the allegations in the verified first amended complaint. (Opposition pp. 4-5.) The only evidence Plaintiff submits with its opposition is a declaration by Plaintiff’s counsel, Mario Iskander, discussing the attorneys’ fees in this case. (Iskander Declaration ¶¶ 1, 4-5.)

 

  1. Wilmington’s Reply

 

Wilmington argues Plaintiff failed to address its arguments and evidence, Plaintiff did not commence this action in good faith and for a proper purpose, and the lis pendens is defective because it was not filed with this court. (Reply pp. 3-9.)

 

  1. DISCUSSION

 

  1. Service

 

“Any notice of pendency of action shall be void and invalid as to any adverse party or owner of record unless the requirements of Section 405.22 are met for that party or owner and a proof of service in the form and content specified in Section 1013a has been recorded with the notice of pendency of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.23.) Code of Civil Procedure section 405.22 provides that “[i]mmediately following recordation, a copy of the notice shall also be filed with the court in which the action is pending.”

 

Plaintiff has not submitted a copy of the lis pendens with this court. Thus, Plaintiff failed to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 405.22.

 

  1. Real Property Claim 

 

A “‘[r]eal property claim’ means the cause or causes of action in a pleading which would, if meritorious, affect (a) title to, or the right to possession of, specific real property or (b) the use of an easement identified in the pleading, other than an easement obtained pursuant to statute by any regulated public utility.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 405.4.)¿“In making [a determination as to whether a real property claim is being asserted], the court must engage in a demurrer-like analysis. Rather than analyzing whether the pleading states any claim at all, as on a general demurrer, the court must undertake the more limited analysis of whether the pleading states a real property claim. Review ‘involves only a review of the adequacy of the pleading and normally should not involve evidence from either side, other than possibly that which may be judicially noticed as on a demurrer.’” (Park 100 Investment Group II, LLC v. Ryan (2009) 180 Cal.App.4th 795, 808–809, internal citations omitted.)¿“Therefore, review of an¿expungement¿order under¿section 405.31¿is limited to whether a real property claim has been properly pled by the claimant.” (Kirkeby v. Superior Court (2004) 33 Cal.4th 642, 648.)

 

Plaintiff seeks quiet title to the Subject Property against Wilmington and other non-moving defendants and Plaintiff seeks to cancel and vacate the trustee’s deed issued because of the October 24, 2023, trustee’s sale. (FAC Prayer for Relief ¶¶ 1, 3.)

 

Plaintiff’s claims for quiet title and cancellation of a trustee’s deed would, if meritorious, affect title to or right to possession of the Subject Property. (FAC Prayer for Relief ¶¶ 2-3.) Thus, Plaintiff’s first amended complaint alleges a real property claim.

 

  1. Probable Validity of the Claim 

 

“The trial court must order a¿lis¿pendens¿be¿expunged¿if the claimant fails to establish by a preponderance of the evidence the¿probable¿validity¿of¿the real property claim. Unlike other motions, nonmoving parties must demonstrate the existence or¿probable¿validity¿of their real property claims. Probable¿validity¿’means that it is more likely than not that the claimant will obtain a judgment against the defendant on the claim.’ Thus, when presented with a pretrial motion to¿expunge¿a¿lis¿pendens, the court must grant the motion unless the claimant establishes ‘by a¿preponderance of the evidence¿the¿probable¿validity¿of the real property claim.’” (De Martini v. Superior Court (2024) 98 Cal.App.5th 1269, 1278–79, internal citations omitted, emphasis original.) “In contrast to such demurrer-like review of whether the ‘pleading’ states ‘a real property claim,’ section 405.32 provides an entirely separate ground of attack in the trial court on a lis pendens notice, an evidentiary hearing on the probability the proponent will be able to establish a valid real property claim.” (BGJ Associates, LLC v. Superior Court, supra, 75 Cal.App.4th at 956–57.)¿ 

 

A complaint alleging a cause of action for quiet title must be verified and include: (1) a description of the property; (2) plaintiff’s title as to which a determination is sought and the basis of that title; (3) “[t]he adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought”; (4) date as of which the determination is sought; and (5) a prayer for determination of title. (Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.)

 

The “elements of an equitable cause of action to set aside a foreclosure sale are:  (1) the trustee or mortgagee caused an illegal, fraudulent, or willfully oppressive sale of real property pursuant to a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust; (2) the party attacking the sale (usually but not always the trustor or mortgagor) was prejudiced or harmed; and (3) in cases where the trustor or mortgagor challenges the sale, the trustor or mortgagor tendered the amount of the secured indebtedness or was excused from tendering.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A. (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 89, 104.)

 

Plaintiff alleges that “[b]etween October 11[,] 2023 and October 24, 2023, . . . Plaintiff . . . made multiple phone calls to Defendants and sent emails to facilitate the payoff of the loan through escrow. However, Defendants began to ignore [its] calls and emails.”  (FAC ¶ 23.)

 

Wilmington submits California TD Specialists’ comment log documenting communications regarding the loan between October 5, 2023, to October 24, 2023. (Ex. 15; Hoak Declaration ¶ 8.) The comment log shows that Michelle Guillen at NYMT requested a payoff on October 12, 2023. (Ex. 15; Hoak Declaration ¶ 11.) And, on October 18, 2023, Guillen requested a reduced payoff in the sum of $905,000, and NYMT approved the request. (Ex. 15; Hoak Declaration ¶¶ 12-13.) Wilmington also submits records showing that, on October 19, 2023, Guillen sent Plaintiff the demand reflecting the reduced amount. (Pourdavoudi Declaration ¶ 30; Ex. 14.) However, the payoff was not received on October 23, 2024, and “NYMT instructed to proceed with foreclosure.” (Hoak Declaration ¶ 15.)

 

Wilmington’s evidence shows that NYMT, through Guillen, were corresponding with Plaintiff during the period Plaintiff alleges Defendants were ignoring his calls and emails and that Guillen facilitated and communicated to Plaintiff a lower payoff during that period. Accordingly, Plaintiff has not shown, by a preponderance of the evidence, that Defendants ignored Plaintiff and failed to facilitate the payoff of the loan.

 

Plaintiff also alleges that Plaintiff “was told by the representatives of [California TD Specialists] and NYMT on behalf of [Wilmington] that the sale would be postponed to a date after October 31, 2023 to allow Plaintiff’s sale of the [Subject] Property to close.” (FAC ¶ 24.)

 

“The statute of frauds provides that any agreement pertaining to ‘the sale of real property, or of an interest therein’ is invalid unless it is memorialized in writing and signed by the party to be charged.” (Granadino v. Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 411, 415.) “A mortgage or deed of trust is subject to the statute of frauds” and “[a]n agreement that modifies a contract subject to the statute of frauds is likewise subject to the statute of frauds.” (Id. at p. 416.) “A gratuitous oral promise to postpone a sale of property pursuant to the terms of a trust deed ordinarily would be unenforceable under [Civil code] section 1698.” (Id.) In Granadino, the Court held that the trial court did not err by finding the appellants’ claim was barred by the statute of frauds because “the alleged promise that no foreclosure sale was scheduled is unenforceable absent a signed writing by Wells Fargo [and i]t is undisputed that no such writing exists.” (Id.) 

 

Plaintiff does not allege there exists, nor submit, any writing showing Defendants agreed to postpone the sale until October 31, 2023. (FAC ¶ 24.)

 

An exception to the general rule stated above applies when the borrower relies on a promise to postpone the foreclosure sale if the borrower procures a solvent buyer. (See Nguyen v. Calhoun (2003) 105 Cal.App.4th 428, 450.) However, even assuming that the exception applies here, Wilmington submits a declaration by NYMT’s Vice President stating “NYMT’s business records reflect that no one at NYMT, or on behalf of NYMT, orally represented to anyone that the trustee sale of October 24, 2023, would be postponed.” (Pourdavoudi Declaration ¶ 34.) And Wilmington submits a declaration by California TD Specialists’ Trustee Sale Officer stating, “TD’s Comment Log reflects that no one at TD or on behalf of TD, orally or otherwise, represented that the trustee sale of October 24, 2023, would be postponed.” (Hoak Declaration ¶ 18.) 

 

This court finds that Plaintiff’s allegation Plaintiff “was told by the representatives of [California TD Specialists] and NYMT on behalf of [Wilmington] that the sale would be postponed to a date after October 31, 2023” is insufficient to satisfy Plaintiff’s burden. (See FAC ¶ 24.) Accordingly, Plaintiff has not shown, by a preponderance of the evidence, that Defendants promised to postpone the sale until October 31, 2023.

 

Finally, Plaintiff alleges it “was ready, willing and able to tender the amount of the payoff, and had an investor who had already tendered funds to Stewart Title for purchase of Property with instructions to pay all liens, including that of Defendants from such funds” and the “tender was made to escrow in conjunction with Plaintiff’s calls and requests to Defendants to send a payoff demand to escrow.” (FAC ¶ 26.)

 

Wilmington submits evidence showing its counsel subpoenaed Stewart Title of California’s escrow file. (Shakibi Declaration ¶ 7.) Wilmington argues the file “does not include the payoff demand that NYMT had forwarded to [Behdinan] and instructed [Behdinan] to forward it to escrow[,]” “does not show any funds tendered to escrow by any party[,]” “does not include the Purchase Agreement that [Behdinan] had forwarded to NYMT showing a closing date of October 20, 2023[,]” and “does not show that escrow had progressed much beyond opening.” (Motion p. 17.)

 

This court finds that Wilmington’s evidence is persuasive and therefore finds that Plaintiff has not shown, by a preponderance of the evidence, that it was ready, willing, and able to tender the amount of the payoff or that its investor tendered funds to Stewart Title.

 

Thus, Plaintiff has not established, by a preponderance of the evidence, the probable validity of its real property claims.

 

  1.  ATTORNEY’S FEES 

 

California Code of Civil Procedure section 405.38 reads, “[t]he court shall direct that the party prevailing on any motion under this chapter be awarded the reasonable attorney's fees and costs of making or opposing the motion unless the court finds that the other party acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of attorney's fees and costs unjust.” “Substantial justification is justification that is ‘clearly reasonable because it is well-grounded in both law and fact.’” (Pollock v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1348, 1359.) 

 

Wilmington is the prevailing party. Plaintiff has not submitted adequate evidence establishing, by a preponderance of the evidence, the¿probable¿validity¿of¿Plaintiff’s real property claims. This court also finds that Plaintiff has not acted with substantial justification and no other circumstances make imposing attorneys’ fees in this case unjust.

 

Wilmington seeks $9,556.50 in attorneys’ fees. (Reply p. 9; Motion p. 20.) This includes 22.2 hours preparing the Motion and 5.5 hours reviewing the opposition, preparing a reply, preparing for and attending the hearing, and preparing the proposed order, at $345.00 per hour. (Shakibi Declaration ¶¶ 13-14.)  Counsel’s declaration includes a comprehensive breakdown of how counsel spent these 22.2 hours.

 

 

Given the specialized nature of the issues raised by the Motion and Wilmington’s thorough briefing of facts and law, this court finds that the attorneys’ fees are reasonable and the rates at which the hours are billed are reasonable.

 

Thus, this court grants Wilmington’s attorneys’ fees request in the requested amount of $9,556.50.

 

  1. CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, this court grants Wilmington’s Motion. This court also grants Wilmington’s request for attorneys’ fees in the sum of $9,556.50, to be paid by Plaintiff to Wilmington within thirty (30) days’ notice of this court’s order.

Defendant Karen C. Brown-Bakerman obtained (“Defendant”) two loans from Plaintiff Navy Federal Credit Union (“Plaintiff”). (Complaint pp. 3-4.) On or about December 27, 2023, Defendant failed to make payments on the loans. (Complaint pp. 3-4.) On November 6, 2024, this court denied Plaintiff’s default application packet.

 

On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff submitted additional documents supporting its default application packet.

 

II.             LEGAL STANDARD

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800 sets forth the requirements for default judgments. In pertinent part, the rule dictates that a party must use form CIV-100 and file the following documents with the clerk: (1) except in unlawful detainer cases, a brief summary of the case identifying the parties and the nature of plaintiff's claim; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a declaration of nonmilitary status for each defendant against whom judgment is sought; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment against specified parties under Code of Civil Procedure section 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1800(a)(1)-(9).)

 

III.           DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff seeks default judgment against Defendant in the sum of $47,844.14.

 

Plaintiff’s default judgment application is not compliant with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800.

 

The court rejected Plaintiff’s default judgment application packet on November 6, 2024, stating:

 

Plaintiff seeks default judgment against Defendant in the sum of $47,844.14. For the following reasons, Plaintiff’s default judgment application is not compliant with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1800. Plaintiff has not submitted a summary of the case. Plaintiff’s submitted exhibits do not support the damages amount. Plaintiff seeks damages based on two loans. The first loan’s balance totals $7,758.09. (Ex. A; Leroy Declaration ¶¶ 5, 7.) Leroy’s declaration cites Exhibit A, which supports the damages amount sought for the first loan. (Ex. A; Leroy Declaration ¶ 5.) The second loan balance purportedly totals $37,805.59. (Leroy Declaration ¶¶ 6-7.) Leroy’s declaration cites Exhibit B to support the second loan’s balance. (Leroy Declaration ¶ 6; Ex. B.) Exhibit B shows that the second loan’s payments originally totaled $59,077.31, but Exhibit B does not show that the current balance on the second loan is $37,805.59. (Ex. B.)

 

(Notice of Rejection of Electronic Filing filed 11/06/24.)

On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff submitted an exhibit showing the outstanding loan balance is $37,805.59. (Ex. B p. 4.) However, the declaration submitted with that exhibit does not authenticate that document. (Leroy Declaration ¶ 6.) Moreover, the Leroy declaration submitted on November 20, 2024, is dated October 2, 2024, and therefore appears to be the same Leroy declaration Plaintiff submitted with its first default judgment application packet.

 

IV.           CONCLUSION

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s default judgment application is denied.
