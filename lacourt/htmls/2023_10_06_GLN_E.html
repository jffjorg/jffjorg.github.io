DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21PSCV01086    Hearing Date: October 6, 2023    Dept: E

Hearing Date: 10/06/2023 – 10:30am Case No: 21PSCV01086 Trial Date: 12/26/2023 Case Name: ERIC CHEN, a natural person and CINDY CHOU, a natural person v. JAMES WU, a natural person; USA LIFE MANAGEMENT CORPORATION, a California corporation; CHIHUANG WU, a natural person; PIHUI WU, a natural person; and DOES 1-100 inclusive

4 - TENTATIVE RULINGS ON (3) MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES & (1) MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs, Eric Chen and Cindy Chou, filed a Complaint on 12/30/2021. Plaintiffs allege four causes of action against Defendants – James Wu, a natural person; USA Life Management Corporation, a California Corporation; ChiHuang Wu, a natural person; and PiHui Wu, a natural person.

The causes of action in the Complaint are: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Specific Performance, (3) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and (4) Fraud. The first and second causes of action are alleged against ChiHuang Wu, PiHui Wu, and James Wu. The third and fourth causes of action are alleged against James Wu

Plaintiffs allege that Defendants ChiHuang Wu and PiHui Wu are husband and wife who are the owners of the Subject Property at 18932 Amberly Place, Rowland Heights, CA 91748 which is the subject of this litigation. (Compl. ¶4.)[Plaintiffs’ Complaint refers to the Wus as Defendants ChiHuang and PiHui. Plaintiffs’ Complaint refers to James Wu as James.

Plaintiffs allege they reside next door to the Subject Property. (Compl. ¶10.) Plaintiffs allege that Mrs. Wu contacted Plaintiff Cindy Chou and indicated they would be interested in selling the house to Plaintiffs. (Compl. ¶11.) Plaintiffs allege that when Mrs. Wu contacted Cindy Chou about selling the house, Mrs. Wu indicated that her son, James, would oversee everything about the sale and would make all decisions regarding the handling of the sale. (Id.)

Plaintiffs allege they discussed potentially having James Wu act as their broker based on their relationship with the Wus and to make for an easier sale process with James being able to benefit from the sale and to reduce costs to the Wus. (Compl. ¶12.)

Plaintiffs allege they put in an offer to purchase the Subject Property for $1.49 million, the offer was for a 45-day escrow, and that James would act as a dual broker/agent to reduce the costs to sellers of the Subject Property. (Compl. ¶13.)

Plaintiffs allege that James congratulated Plaintiffs that their offer had been accepted by the Wus for the purchase price of $1.49 million. (Compl. ¶14.) Plaintiffs allege that even though the deal had been agreed upon, James informed Plaintiffs that the real estate rules required him to also list

the Subject Property on the Multiple Listing Service just as a formality. (Compl. ¶16.) Plaintiffs allege that the Wus decided to not go forward with the agreement because the Wus decided to sell the property to another buyer for a higher price. (Compl. ¶19.)

These four motions deal with one motion to compel responses, and three motions to compel further responses.

MOTION 1 – Compel Responses, RFP, Set Two, No. 44

RELIEF REQUESTED

Plaintiff, Eric Chen, moves this Court for an order to compel Defendant, James Wu, to provide an initial response to request no. 44 of Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set Two.

Plaintiff will also move the Court to impose monetary sanctions against James Wu and his counsel of record, Chiao & Wu, LLP and Lyle R. Mink, Esq., in the amount of $912.50, jointly and severally, for their misuse of the discovery process and for forcing Plaintiff to make this Motion. Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §§2023.040, 2023.030, 2031.300 (c), and 2023.010 (d), and (h).

Procedural

Moving Party: Plaintiff, Eric Chen Responding Party: No Opposition

LEGAL STANDARD – COMPEL RESPONSES, INSPECTION DEMANDS

Within 30 days after service of a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the party to whom the demand is directed shall serve the original of the response to it on the party making the demand, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared in the action, unless on motion of the party making the demand, the court has shortened the time for response,

or unless on motion of the party to whom the demand has been directed, the court has extended the time for response. (CCP §2031.260(a).)

If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand. (CCP §2031.300(b).)

If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, the party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010). (CCP §2031.300(a).) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280. (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (CCP §2031.300(a)(1)-(2).)

Unlike a motion to compel further responses, a motion to compel responses is not subject to a 45-day time limit, and the propounding party does not have to demonstrate either good cause or that it satisfied a “meet and confer” requirement. (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404 citing Weil and Brown, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2006) ¶¶8:1137 to 8:1144, pp. 8F-59 to 8F-60, ¶¶ 8:1483 to 8:1489, pp. 8H-29 to hH-30 (Weil & Brown).)

LEGAL STANDARD – COMPEL FURTHER – REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION

Under CCP § 2017.010, “any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action..., if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” The Section specifically provides that “[d]iscovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action,” and that discovery “may be obtained of the identity and location of persons having knowledge of any discoverable matter, as well as of the existence, description, nature, custody, condition and location of any document, electronically stored information, tangible thing, or land or other property.”

CCP § 2031.310(a) provides that a party demanding a document inspection may move for an order compelling further responses to the demand if the demanding party deems that:

“(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

TENTATIVE RULING MOTION 2- RFPs 34, 35, and 36

Under CCP § 2031.310 (b)(1), “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.”

“In the more specific context of a demand for production of a tangible thing, the party who asks the trial court to compel production must show “good cause” for the request—but unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

Here, the Court isn’t entirely clear how Plaintiff is alleging the documents he requests are relevant to the instant action.

Plaintiff asserts several reasons as to why further responses should be compelled. Below, the Court lists a few reasons that Plaintiff asserts he is entitled to further responses to RFPs 34, 35, and 36:

1. James Wu was a dual agent to his parents, Spouses Wu (sellers) and Plaintiffs (buyer) for the sale of the Property. James negotiated with Plaintiffs through text messages and email communications to finalize the material elements of the contract.

2. The requested documents are relevant and are reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence because it would show James Wu’s custom and practice in dealing with other individuals for the sale/purchase of property whether he finalizes the material elements of the contract and have the other party sign the California form thereafter. In view thereof, Plaintiffs should be able to examine documents of other real estate transactions that James Wu did including communications through text messages and email communications to finalize the material terms of the agreement.

3. Plaintiff offered to limit the time period to five (5) years, which is 2018 to 2023. Plaintiff is likewise not seeking communications between James and his attorney, but only communications between James Wu and his clients regarding real-estate transactions. As a result of his objection, James Wu should be able to produce a privilege log. Cal Code Civ Proc § 2031.240.

4. Although Plaintiff is entitled to the information of James Wu’ clients, Plaintiff proposed that personal information be redacted as Plaintiff’s main objective for obtaining such documents is to determine James Wu’s custom and practice.

5. The requested documents would facilitate the ascertainment of the truth and the just resolution of Plaintiff’s claims against James Wu because Plaintiff would be able to examine James Wu’s custom and practice in dealing with real estate transactions, and would be able to evaluate if James’ actions towards Plaintiffs for the sale of the Property is similar his other transactions.

(Pl. Sep. Stmt. p. 2-8.)

As can be seen from above, it is difficult to determine how the Plaintiff is attempting to establish relevancy/good cause.

If Plaintiff had explained why a specific request provides information that is needed to establish a specific element of a specific cause of action in the Complaint, the relevancy would be apparent to the Court. Plaintiff does not do that.

The Court is left to guess as to how each request is relevant because of Plaintiff’s somewhat vague explanations as to relevancy in the context of the instant action.

As another example, as to number 2 above, where Plaintiff argues the requests are relevant in a conclusory fashion, Plaintiff notes how “…it would show James Wu’s custom and practice in dealing with other individuals for the sale/purchase of property whether he finalizes the material elements of the contract and have the other party sign the California form thereafter.” As can be seen from that explanation, Plaintiff makes no attempt to explain how or what in the Complaint

“James Wu’s custom and practice in dealing with other individuals for the sale/purchase of property whether he finalizes the material elements of the contract and have the other party sign the California form” is relevant to in this action. Plaintiff provides no explanation as to what element this would satisfy in a specific cause of action, and Plaintiff makes no explanation as to what cause of action this information is even relevant to.

The Court notes it is not saying that Plaintiff must explicitly state how the request would satisfy a specific element of a specific cause of action, because there may be scenarios where the request relates to settlement, damages, or something else that is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. However, as to all these requests, Plaintiff’s unclear explanations as to relevancy/good cause leave the Court to guess what these requested documents are legally relevant to in this action, and thus no fact specific showing of relevancy is established.

The Court tentatively DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to RFPs 34, 35, and 36, since the burden is on the moving party to establish good cause.

I anticipate spending another 1.0 hour reviewing the opposition and 1.0 hour appearing at the hearing. There will be an additional amount of $550.00. Also, there is a filing fee in the amount of $60.00. Thus, Plaintiff is entitled to the attorneys’ fees and costs in the total amount of $802.50.” (Decl. Quijano ¶11.)

The issue of sanctions will be taken up at the hearing.
