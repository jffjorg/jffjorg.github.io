Case Number: 23PSCV01379    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: H

A.A. v. Ortega, Case No. 23PSCV01379

ORDER ON DEFAULT JUDGMENT APPLICATION

 

Plaintiff A.A., a minor by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Sherry Guia’s Application for Default Judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Background 

This is a dog bite case. On May 5, 2023, Plaintiff A.A., a minor by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Sherry Guia (“Plaintiff”), filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Eileen Ortega (“Defendant”) and Does 1-10 for (1) negligence, (2) common law strict liability based on known dangerous propensities, and (3) statutory strict liability based on the dog bite statute.

On June 25, 2024, the Clerk entered default against Defendant.

Pending before the Court is Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment.

Discussion

 

Plaintiff’s Application for Default Judgment is denied without prejudice. The

following defects are noted:

Plaintiff has failed to provide the Court with a full and complete default prove-up package in compliance with California Rules of Court Rule 3.1800. On October 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a four-page document entitled “Amended Application for Default Judgment; Declarations of Alexis Galindo and Sherry Guia; Exhibits” (“Amended Application”), a five-page document entitled “Amended Declaration of Alexis Galindo ISO Plaintiff’s Default Judgment Application” (“Amended Galindo Declaration”), and a three-page proposed judgment; no other documents were filed. The Amended Application does not attach a declaration from Sherry Guia or any exhibits. The Amended Galindo Declaration references Exhibits A-H as being attached, but there are no such exhibits. The Court declines to evaluate piecemeal submissions.

Accordingly, the application is denied without prejudice. Plaintiff should submit a revised default prove-up application that addresses the foregoing issues.

  

Case Number: 23PSCV03055    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: H

Colindres v. CalPortland Construction et al., Case No. 23PSCV03055

ORDER ON DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Defendants CalPortland Construction and CalPortland Company’s Motion for Summary Judgment or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication is GRANTED.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiffs Sylvia Colindres, Elijah Colindres, Gabrielle Colindres, Makayla Colindres, by and through her guardian ad litem, Sylvia Colindres, and the Estate of Kenneth Colindres, by and through its administrator, Sylvia Colindres, (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) allege that Kenneth Colindres (the “decedent”) was killed on January 17, 2023, when his vehicle collided head-on with a semi-truck. 

On October 3, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against CalPortland Construction and Does 1-100 for (1) wrongful death—dangerous condition of private property and (2) survival. 

On February 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an “Amendment to Complaint,” wherein CalPortland Company was named in lieu of Doe 1.

Pending before the Court is the motion for summary judgment or, in the alternative, summary adjudication filed by CalPortland Construction and CalPortland Company (collectively, “Defendants”). As explained below, the motion is granted as to CalPortland Construction because CalPortland Construction lacks ownership, lease, occupation, or control of Cement Plant Road, and the motion is granted as to CalPortland Company because Plaintiffs’ causes of action against CalPortland Construction are subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of California’s worker’s compensation system.


II.      Legal Standard

“A party may move for summary judgment. . . if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (a)(1).) 

“The motion for summary judgment shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).)

“On a motion for summary judgment[/adjudication], the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) “A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) 

“A defendant or cross-defendant has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action. . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action. Once the defendant. . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff or cross-complainant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)

III.     Discussion

Defendants move the Court for an order granting summary judgment in their favor and against Plaintiffs; in the alternative, Defendants seek summary adjudication as follows:

Issue No. 1: Plaintiffs’ first cause of action (i.e., for Wrongful Death—Dangerous Condition of Private Property) fails as to CalPortland Company because it is subject to the exclusive jurisdiction of California’s worker’s compensation system;

Issue No. 2: Plaintiffs’ second cause of action (i.e., for Survival) fails as to CalPortland Company for the same reason as Issue No. 1;

Issue No. 3: Plaintiffs’ first cause of action fails as to CalPortland Construction because Plaintiffs cannot prove the necessary element of ownership, possession or control of the subject private property; and

Issue No. 4: Plaintiffs’ second cause of action fails as to CalPortland Construction for the same reason as Issue No. 3.

At the outset, the Court summarily grants the motion as to CalPortland Construction because it is essentially undisputed that CalPortland Construction “did not own, lease, occupy or control Cement Plant Road where the [collison] occurred as of the date of the [collision].” (UMF No. 3.) Nothing proffered by Plaintiffs in response to this fact has anything to do with CalPortland Construction’s lack of ownership, lease, occupation, or control of Cement Plant Road. Further, Plaintiffs expressly state that they “do not oppose the motion as to Defendant CalPortland Construction” (Opp., p. 2, fn. 1). The remaining analysis thus pertains only to CalPortland Company.

CalPortland Company contends that Plaintiffs’ claims against it are barred pursuant to the workers’ compensation exclusivity rule. The California Constitution vests the Legislature with the power “to create, and enforce a complete system of workers’ compensation.” (Cal. Const., Art. XIV, § 4). “The Workers’ Compensation Act invests the [Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board] with exclusive jurisdiction over disputes regarding an employee’s right to compensation or an employer’s liability. (Lab. Code, § 5300, subds. (a) and (b) . . .).” (Mitchell v. Scott Wetzel Services, Inc. (1991) 227 Cal.App.3d 1474, 1478 [emphasis added]).

Workers’ compensation liability “exist[s] against an employer for any injury sustained by his or her employees arising out of and in the course of the employment and for the death of any employee if the injury proximately causes death.” (Lab. Code § 3600, subd. (a).) Specifically, workers’ compensation liability exists “in those cases where the following conditions of compensation concur: (1) Where, at the time of the injury, both the employer and the employee are subject to the compensation provisions of this division. (2) Where, at the time of the injury, the employee is performing service growing out of and incidental to his or her employment and is acting within the course of his or her employment. (3) Where the injury is proximately caused by the employment, either with or without negligence.” (Id.)

“[I]njuries sustained by an employee while going to or from his place of work upon premises owned or controlled by his employer are generally deemed to have arisen out of and in the course of the employment.” (Pacific Indem. Co. v. Industrial Acc. Commission (1946) 28 Cal.2d 329, 336.)

Labor Code section 3602 provides that “[w]here the conditions of compensation set forth in Section 3600 concur, the right to recover compensation is, . . . the sole and exclusive remedy of the employee or his or her dependents against the employer.”

The undisputed facts establish that (1) on January 17, 2020, while driving southbound on Cement Plant Road in Kern County, the decedent collided head-on with a vehicle operated by Francisco Aristides Argueta, resulting in the decedent’s death (the “subject incident”) (UMF No. 1); (2) Cement Plant Road, where the subject incident occurred, is private property owned, operated, and controlled by CalPortland Company (UMF No. 2)[FN1]; (3) at the time of the subject incident, the decedent was an employee of CalPortland Company and was driving on Cement Plant Road toward CalPortland Company’s Mohave Cement Plant on his way to report for work (UMF Nos. 6 and 7); and (4) at the time of the subject incident, Cement Plant Road was the only paved road available to individuals (including employees) seeking to access the Mojave Cement Plant (UMF No. 8). Based on these undisputed facts, the Court concludes that Plaintiffs’ two causes of action alleged against CalPortland Company arise out of and in the course of the decedent’s employment.

Plaintiffs argue that CalPortland Company should be equitably estopped from claiming the worker’s compensation exclusivity defense bars Plaintiffs’ claims. However, subject matter jurisdiction cannot be bestowed upon a tribunal otherwise lacking such jurisdiction by equitable estoppel. “The only point of jurisdictional concurrency between the [Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board] and civil courts is jurisdiction to determine jurisdiction. Once determined, jurisdiction is exclusive, not concurrent, and civil courts have no power to grant relief in matters concerning the workers’ compensation remedy.” (Hughes v. Argonaut Ins. Co. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 517, 527; Scott v. Industrial Acc. Commission (1956) 46 Cal.2d 76, 83 [“If at the time of the accident there was no workmen’s compensation coverage, then the commission is without jurisdiction to grant relief, and if there was such coverage then the superior court is without jurisdiction and must leave the parties to pursue their remedies before the commission.”) “[S]ubject matter jurisdiction cannot be conferred by waiver, consent or estoppel upon a tribunal otherwise lacking such jurisdiction.” (Hughes, supra, 88 Cal.App.4th at 527). “There is no ‘equitable claim’ exception to the [Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board]’s exclusive jurisdiction.” (Id. at 531).

The motion is granted on this basis.[FN2]

In light of the Court’s ruling granting summary judgment to Defendants, the Court vacates the Final Status Conference set for August 26, 2025 and the Jury Trial set for September 9, 2025.


[FN1] While Plaintiffs technically “dispute” UMF No. 2, nothing proffered in response to this fact has anything to do with Company’s ownership, operation, and control of Cement Plant Road.

[FN2] The Court declines to rule on Defendants’ evidentiary objections as they are not material to the disposition of this motion.

Case Number: 23PSCV03386    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: H

Chinese American Parent Association of Walnut v. Hsu, et al.,
Case No. 23PSCV03386

 

ORDER ON DEMURRER

Defendants Joy Shui Hsu, Teresa Chen, and Walnut High School Chinese American Parent Association, Inc.’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s Complaint is SUSTAINED, with 20 days’ leave given to amend.

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiff Chinese American Parent Association of Walnut (“Plaintiff” or the “Association”) alleges as follows:

          The Association was organized in 2000 as a nonprofit organization to provide educational support to the parents and students of Walnut schools, including its high school. Since its inception, the Association has been doing its charitable business under the names of “Chinese American Parent Association of Walnut,” “CAPA of Walnut,” “CAPA,” “Chinese American Parent Association of Walnut High School,” and its Chinese transliteration (collectively, “Association Trademarks”). On May 19, 2023, Joy Shui Hsu (“Hsu”) filed Articles of Incorporation with the state, forming a nonprofit organization with the name of “Walnut High School Chinese American Parent Association Inc.” (“Walnut Parent Association”). Hsu and Teresa Chen (“Chen”) are the CEO and CFO of Walnut Parent Association, respectively.

          On or about July 5, 2023, during the opening date of membership recruitment at Walnut High School, the Association discovered that Hsu and Walnut Parent Association set up a booth immediately adjacent to the Association’s booth in the library and used one or more of the Association Trademarks to solicit donations and membership enrollments. Hsu, Chen, and Walnut Parent Association have advised visitors that the Association had tax troubles with the state and would soon be dissolved and that Walnut Parent Association was sanctioned by the state and Internal Revenue Service. 

          During the July 5, 2023 recruitment event, the Association obtained a copy of Walnut Parent Association’s flyer, which promoted a “pay to play” program soliciting donations with the promise of a premier lunch pass that would enable students who donated $1,000.00 or more to Walnut Parent Association to cut in front of other kids in the lunch line. On or about July 10, 2023, representatives of the Association met with Walnut High School’s principal, Ryan Maine (“Maine”), during which time Maine advised that Walnut Parent Association was approved by the Walnut Valey Unified School District (the “District”) and that Tony Torng (“Torng”), an elected member of the Board of Trustees for District, had personally introduced Hsu and Walnut Parent Association to him as a new approved organization at Walnut High School. The Association has not found any record reflecting that the District approved Walnut Parent Association. 

On November 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Hsu, Chen, Walnut Parent Association, Torng, and Does 1-25 for (1) common law trademark infringement, (2) civil conspiracy to commit trademark infringement, (3) unfair competition (Bus. & Prof. Code §§ 17200 et seq.), and (4) common law unfair competition.

Pending before the Court is Hsu, Chen, and Walnut Parent Association’s demurrer to the complaint. For the reasons stated herein, the demurrer is sustained, with 20 days leave to amend.

 

II.      Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

 

III.     Discussion 

Hsu, Chen, and Walnut Parent Association (hereinafter collectively, “Defendants”) demur, per Code of Civil Procedure sections 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to the first through fourth causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint, on the basis that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and are uncertain. 

At the outset, the Court notes that Defendants’ demurrer is accompanied by declarations from Hsu, made both individually and on behalf of Walnut Parent Association, and from Chen. The Court declines to consider the declarations as improper on demurrer.

          A.       Alter Ego Allegations

Defendants assert that Plaintiff has insufficiently pled alter ego. The Court agrees.

“[T]wo conditions must be met before the alter ego doctrine will be invoked. First, there must be such a unity of interest and ownership between the corporation and its equitable owner that the separate personalities of the corporation and the shareholder do not in reality exist. Second, there must be an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.” (Sonora Diamond Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 523, 538.)

“To recover on an alter ego theory, a plaintiff need not use the words ‘alter ego,’ but must allege sufficient facts to show a unity of interest and ownership, and an unjust result if the corporation is treated as the sole actor.” (Leek v. Cooper (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 399, 415 [emphasis added].)

Here, Plaintiff cites conclusions of alter ego, with no supporting facts. Defendants’ demurrer is sustained on this basis. Moreover, as explained in more detail below, the individual causes of action are also deficient.

 

          B.       First Cause of Action (Common Law Trademark Infringement)

To state a claim for common law trademark infringement, a plaintiff must allege “[1] the existence of prior and continuous use of a trade name in such a way that a secondary meaning is acquired, [2] subsequent use of a confusingly similar trade name by the defendant, and [3] likelihood of confusion in the minds of the public of the defendant’s business as that of, or as one affiliated with, the plaintiff.” (Sunset House Distributing Corp. v. Coffee Dan’s, Inc. (1966) 240 Cal.App.2d 748, 753.)

Plaintiff fails to set forth facts establishing prior and continuous use with secondary meaning. Plaintiff also has failed to identify when, where, or how the Association Trademarks were employed in a manner likely to cause confusion among the public. Further, while Plaintiff alleges that it “has received numerous complaints from the public questioning who was the real CAPA [Chinese American Parent Association] at Walnut High School” (Complaint, ¶ 29), Plaintiff has not pled any facts as to when and what complaints were made.

Defendants’ demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained.

 

          C.       Second Cause of Action (Trademark Infringement Conspiracy)

“The elements of a civil conspiracy are (1) the formation and operation of the conspiracy; (2) the wrongful act or acts done pursuant thereto; and (3) the damage resulting.” (Mosier v. Southern California Physicians Ins. Exchange (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1022, 1048.)

Plaintiff fails to set forth facts suggesting that Defendants engaged in concerted action for the purpose of infringing on the Association Trademarks. Plaintiff also fails to allege specific resulting damages. Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action is sustained.


          D.      Third Cause of Action (Unfair Competition under Bus. & Prof. Code)

“Business and Professions Code section 17200. . . establishes three varieties of unfair competition-acts or practices which are unlawful, or unfair, or fraudulent.” (Podolsky v. First Healthcare Corp. (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 632, 647.) “Because the statute is framed in the disjunctive, a business practice need only meet one of the three criteria to be considered unfair competition.” (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1471.)

“Unlawful business acts or practices within the meaning of the UCL include anything that can properly be called a business practice and that at the same time is forbidden by law. A practice is forbidden by law if it violates any law, civil or criminal, statutory or judicially made, federal, state or local. By extending to business acts or practices which are unlawful, the UCL permits violations of other laws to be treated as unfair competition that is independently actionable. Even if the violation of another law does not create a private right of action, if the violation constitutes unfair competition, it is actionable.” (Id. at 1474-1475 [quotations and citations omitted].)

“A business practice is unfair within the meaning of the UCL if it violates established public policy or if it is immoral, unethical, oppressive or unscrupulous and causes injury to consumers which outweighs its benefits.” (Id. at 1473.) “A fraudulent business practice is one which is likely to deceive the public. . . [t]he determination as to whether a business practice is deceptive is based on the likely effect such practice would have on a reasonable consumer. (Id. at 1471.)

Plaintiff fails to allege any unlawful, unfair or fraudulent business act or practice, for the reasons identified above. Defendants’ demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained.


          E.       Fourth Cause of Action (Common Law Unfair Competition)

“The common law tort of unfair competition is generally thought to be synonymous with the act of “passing off” one's goods as those of another. The tort developed as an equitable remedy against the wrongful exploitation of trade names and common law trademarks that were not otherwise entitled to legal protection.” (Bank of the West v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1254, 1263.)

Plaintiff fails to allege facts reflecting Defendants’ “passing off” their services as those of Plaintiff’s. Defendants’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is sustained.

 

IV.     Conclusion

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ demurrer is sustained in its entirety. The Court grants Plaintiff 20 days’ leave to amend the complaint. (City of Stockton v. Superior Ct. (2007) 42 Cal. 4th 730, 747 [“[L]eave to amend is liberally allowed as a matter of fairness, unless the complaint shows on its face that it is incapable of amendment.”].) 

Case Number: 24PSCV01622    Hearing Date: November 14, 2024    Dept: H

Commercial Credit Group Inc. v. KPW Trucking, Inc., et al.,
Case No. 24PSCV01622

 

ORDER ON PLAINTIFF’S APPLICATIONS FOR WRIT OF POSSESSION

          1.       Plaintiff Commercial Credit Group Inc.’s APPLICATION FOR WRIT OF POSSESSION AFTER HEARING [i.e., as to Contract 1] is granted as to Defendant KPW Trucking, Inc., with no bond. The Court is inclined to deny the writ as to the remaining defendants given their limited roles as guarantors. The Court will hear further from Plaintiff’s counsel in this regard at the hearing.

          2.       Plaintiff Commercial Credit Group Inc.’s APPLICATION FOR WRIT OF POSSESSION AFTER HEARING [i.e., as to Contract 2] is granted as to Defendant KPW Trucking, Inc., with no bond. The Court is inclined to deny the writ as to the remaining defendants given their limited roles as guarantors. The Court will hear further from Plaintiff’s counsel in this regard at the hearing.


I.        Background 

Plaintiff Commercial Credit Group Inc. (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows: 

          On December 30, 2022, KPW Trucking, Inc. (“KPW”) entered into a contract (“Contract 1”) by which Plaintiff financed KPW’s acquisition of a 2023 Volvo VNL64T760 Sleeper Tractor with Detroit DD13 425HP Engine and Automatic Transmission, Serial Identification No. 4V4NC9EH4PN611697 (“Equipment 1”). On September 13, 2022, KPW entered into another contract (“Contract 2”) by which Plaintiff financed KPW’s acquisition of a 2017 Hyundai Dry Van Trailer, VIN #3H3V532C6HT324085 (“Equipment 2”). KPW defaulted under Contract 1 and  Contract 2 by failing to make installment payments and to maintain physical damage insurance. Contract 1 and Contract 2 were guaranteed by Keping Wang (“Wang”), Mo Li (“Li”), LM Logistic, Inc. (“LM Logistic”), and W.K.P. Logistics, Inc. (“W.K.P. Logistics”). 

On May 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against KPW, Wang, Li, LM Logistic, W.K.P. Logistics (collectively, “Defendants”), and Does 1-100 for (1) possession of personal property as to Contract 1 and (2) possession of personal property as to Contract 2.

Pending before the Court are Plaintiff’s applications for writ of possession, whereby Plaintiff seeks to recover Equipment 1 and Equipment 2.

 

II.      Discussion

          A.       Writ of Possession (Equipment 1)

Plaintiff applies for a writ of possession after hearing pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 512.010 et seq. as against Defendants to recover Equipment 1.

“Claim and delivery is a remedy by which a party with a superior right to a specific item of personal property (created, most commonly, by a contractual lien) may recover possession of that specific property before judgment.” (Waffer Internat. Corp. v. Khorsandi (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 1261, 1271.) 

No writ of possession may issue, except after a hearing on a noticed motion (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.020, subd. (a)) or by ex parte application satisfying the conditions of section 512.020, subdivision (b).  Prior to the section 512.020 hearing, the defendant must be served with all of the following: (a) A copy of the summons and complaint; (b) A Notice of Application and Hearing; (c) A copy of the application and any affidavit in support thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.030, subd. (a).) “If the defendant has not appeared in the action, and a writ, notice, order, or other paper is required to be personally served on the defendant under this title, service shall be made in the same manner as a summons is served under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 413.10) of Title 5.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.030, subd. (b).) 

The “Notice of Application and Hearing” must inform the defendant of all of the following: (a) A hearing will be held at a place and at a time, to be specified in the notice, on plaintiff's application for a writ of possession; (b) The writ will be issued if the court finds that the plaintiff's claim is probably (not actually) valid and the other requirements for issuing the writ are established; (c) If the defendant desires to oppose the issuance of the writ, he must file with the court either an affidavit providing evidence sufficient to defeat the plaintiff's right to issuance of the writ or an undertaking to stay the delivery of the property in accordance with section 515.020; (d) The notice must contain the following statement: “If you believe the plaintiff may not be entitled to possession of the property claimed, you may wish to seek the advice of an attorney. Such attorney should be consulted promptly so that he may assist you before the time set for the hearing.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.040.)

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed five proofs of service, which reflected that Defendants had each been served via substitute service with, inter alia, the summons, complaint, the and the application for writ of possession as to Equipment 1 on October 8, 2024 and that the foregoing documents had thereafter been mailed to Defendants on October 9, 2024. The Court determines that adequate notice was provided to Defendants.

Turning to the merits of the application, the Court must make its determinations upon the basis of the pleadings and other papers in the record; upon good cause shown, the Court may receive and consider additional evidence and authority produced at the hearing or may continue the hearing for the production of such additional evidence, oral or documentary, or the filing of other affidavits or points and authorities. (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.050.) The Court must issue writ of possession if the plaintiff establishes “the probable validity of his claim” to possession of the property and the undertaking requirements of section 515.010 are satisfied. (Code Civ. Proc., § 512.060; RCA Service Co. v. Superior Court (1982) 137 Cal.App.3d 1, 3.) “Probable validity” exists where it is more likely than not that the plaintiff will obtain a judgment against the defendant on that claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 511.090; RCA Service Co., supra, 137 Cal.App.3d at 3.) 

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are (1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.) The elements of a cause of action for breach of guaranty are “(1) there is a valid guaranty, (2) the borrower has defaulted, and (3) the guarantor failed to perform under the guaranty.” (Gray1 CPB, LLC v. Kolokotronis (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 480, 486.)

Probable validity, in this case, has been established with respect to KPW, pursuant to the application, which is executed under penalty of perjury by Plaintiff’s Assistant Vice President of Operations, Michael Mikulan (“Mikulan”), as well as by Mikulan’s declaration and the exhibits attached thereto.

 

Mikulan attests, and/or the relevant documents reflect, as follows: 

          On December 30, 2022, KPW entered into Contract 1 by which Plaintiff financed KPW’s acquisition of Equipment 1. (Mikulan Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.) Contract 1 was guaranteed by Wang, Li, LM Logistic, and W.K.P. Logistics. (Id.) Plaintiff is, and has been since the date of Contract 1, the legal owner of Equipment 1. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. B.) KPW failed to make the July 7, 2023 payment to Plaintiff called for in Contract 1 and has failed to make all subsequent payments. (Id., ¶ 7). Per the terms of Contract 1, Plaintiff has the right to accelerate payments and demand surrender of Equipment 1 upon default, including the failure to make any payments. (Id.) Plaintiff is informed and believes that KPW has not maintained physical damage insurance for the protection of Equipment 1, as required by Contract 1. (Id., ¶ 8.)

          Upon information and belief, and subject to an inspection of Equipment 1, the  market value of Equipment 1 is $151,750.00. (Id., ¶ 9, Exh. C.) The balance on Equipment 1 is $164,374.37 as of March 27, 2024. (Id.) The balance continues to accrue interest and fees. (Id.) The equity in Equipment 1 is, at most, -$12,624.37. (Id.) The reasonable value of Equipment 1 is, in Mikulan’s opinion based on his years of experience in the equipment finance industry, $180.50/day, which Plaintiff has suffered since July 7, 2023. (Id., ¶ 10.) Equipment 1 has not been taken for a tax assessment or fine pursuant to a statute or seized under an execution against Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 11). 

          Mikulan has probable cause to believe that Equipment 1 is located at 5400 G St., Chino, CA 91710 or 13350 11th St., Chino, CA 91710, as Plaintiff’s agents have observed Equipment 1 at both addresses. (Id., ¶ 12.) 

While, based on the above showing, the Court is persuaded that a writ of possession should issue as to KPW, it is unclear to the Court why a writ of possession should issue as to Wang, Li, LM Logistic, and W.K.P. Logistics, inasmuch as their role appears limited to that of guarantors and there is no indication that Equipment 1 is in their possession. The Court will hear further from Plaintiff’s counsel in this regard at the time of the hearing.

A bond must be posted as part of the undertaking unless the Court finds that the defendant has no interest in the property. (Code Civ. Proc., § 515.010, subd. (b).) The undertaking must be in an amount equal to twice the value of the defendant’s interest in the property (market value less all liens, etc.).  (Id.) Defendant’s interest is based on the market value of the property less (1) any amount due and owing on any conditional sales contract or security agreement, (2) all liens and encumbrances on the property, and (3) any other factors to determine the defendant’s interest in the property. (Id.) 

The application is granted as to KPW, with no bond. The Court determines that, since the balance owed is substantially more than the fair market value of Equipment 1, KPW has no interest in Equipment 1, and therefore Plaintiff is not required to post an undertaking.
   

          B.       Writ of Possession (Equipment 2)

Plaintiff applies for a writ of possession after hearing pursuant to Code of Civil

Procedure sections 512.010 et seq. as against Defendants to recover Equipment 2.

On October 15, 2024, Plaintiff filed five proofs of service, which reflected that Defendants had each been substitute served with, inter alia, the summons, complaint, and the application for writ of possession as to Equipment 2 on October 8, 2024 and that the foregoing documents had thereafter been mailed to Defendants on October 9, 2024. The Court determines that adequate notice was provided to Defendants. 

Turning to the merits, probable validity, in this case, has been established with respect to KPW pursuant to the application, which is executed under penalty of perjury by Mikulan, as well as by Mikulan’s declaration and the exhibits attached thereto. 

Mikulan attests, and/or the relevant documents reflect, as follows:

          On September 13, 2022, KPW entered into Contract 2, by which Plaintiff financed KPW’s acquisition of Equipment 2. (Mikulan Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. A.) Contract 2 was guaranteed by Wang, Li, LM Logistic and W.K.P. Logistics. (Id.) Plaintiff is, and has been since the date of Contract 2, the legal owner of Equipment 2. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. B.)

          KPW failed to make the July 17, 2023 payment to Plaintiff called for in Contract 2 and has failed to make all subsequent payments. (Id., ¶ 7). Per the terms of Contract 2, Plaintiff has the right to accelerate payments and demand surrender of Equipment 2 upon default, including the failure to make any payments. (Id.) Plaintiff is informed and believes that KPW has not maintained physical damage insurance for the protection of Equipment 2, as required by Contract 2. (Id., ¶ 8.)

          Upon information and belief, and subject to an inspection of Equipment 2, the market value of Equipment 2 is $25,000.00. (Id., ¶ 9, Exh. C.) The balance on Equipment #1 is $26,383.46 as of March 27, 2024. (Id.) The balance continues to accrue interest and fees. (Id.) The equity in Equipment 2 is, at most, -$1,383.46. (Id.) The reasonable value of Equipment 2 is, in Mikulan’s opinion based on his years of experience in the equipment finance industry, $35.50/day, which Plaintiff has suffered since July 17, 2023. (Id., ¶ 10.) Equipment 2 has not been taken for a tax assessment or fine pursuant to a statute or seized under an execution against Plaintiff. (Id., ¶ 11).

          Mikulan has probable cause to believe that Equipment 2 is located at 5400 G St., Chino, CA 91710 or 13350 11th St., Chino, CA 91710, as Plaintiff’s agents have observed Equipment 2 at both addresses. (Id., ¶ 12.)

While the Court is persuaded that the writ should issue as to KPW, it is again unclear to the Court why a writ of possession should issue as to Wang, Li, LM Logistic, and W.K.P. Logistics, inasmuch as their role appears limited to that of guarantors and there is no indication that Equipment 2 is in their possession. The Court will hear further from Plaintiff’s counsel in this regard at the time of the hearing.

The application is granted as to KPW, with no bond. The Court determines that, since the balance owed is substantially more than the fair market value of the Equipment 2, KPW has no interest in Equipment 2, and therefore Plaintiff is not required to post an undertaking.
