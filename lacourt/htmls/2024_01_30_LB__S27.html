DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 19LBCV00551    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: S27

On 6/30/23, Defendants, and all of them, filed a motion for judgment on the pleadings directed at Plaintiff’s complaint.  Specifically, Defendants, by way of the motion, seek judgment on the pleadings on the first cause of action.  On 8/22/23, the Court heard and granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend.  On 8/24/23, Plaintiff filed its operative First Amended Complaint.

 

On 9/26/23, Defendants, and each of them, filed a demurrer to the FAC.  The demurrer attacks the first cause of action, which remains unchanged from the original complaint.  It also attacks the third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, and eighth causes of action in the amended complaint.  The hearing on the demurrer is scheduled for 2/08/24, slightly more than a week after the hearing on this MJP.

 

The Court notes that, in its 8/16/23 ruling on the motion to amend, it stated that it could and would rule on the pending motion for judgment on the pleadings despite its ruling on the motion to amend.  That was, however, before Defendants filed a demurrer to the FAC, which demurrer advances substantially the same arguments advanced in connection with the motion for judgment on the pleadings.

 

The Court finds it is in the interest of justice to take the MJP off calendar and to hear all pleading challenges when it hears and rules on the demurrer on 2/08/24.  The Court therefore takes the MJP off calendar at this time. 

 

Defendants are ordered to give notice. 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.

Case Number: 22LBCV01066    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: S27

1.             Facts

Plaintiff, Mary Wilson filed this action against Defendant, Rodger Wilson for damages arising out of a partnership agreement the parties signed during the course of their divorce proceedings.  The operative complaint is the FAC, which was filed on 2/09/23.  It includes causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty, elder abuse, breach of contract, conversion, unjust enrichment, accounting, constructive trust, and violation of Penal Code §496(c).

 

2.             Motions to Compel

Plaintiff filed motions to compel further responses to FROGs, SROGs, and RPDs on 10/03/23, setting them for hearing on 1/30/24.  On 1/17/24, Defendant filed timely opposition to the motions.  Defendant, in opposition, provides evidence that he has provided supplemental responses to all outstanding discovery after meeting and conferring with Plaintiff, such that the motions are now moot. 

 

Any reply to the opposition was due on or before 1/23/24.  The Court has not received timely reply papers and will not consider late-filed reply papers.  The Court finds the motions are substantively moot. 

 

The only outstanding issue is imposition of sanctions.  Plaintiff seeks imposition of sanctions in the amounts of $3210 (FROGs), $2760 (RPDs), and $4560 (SROGs). 

 

Each of Plaintiff’s attorney’s meet and confer declarations avers that Counsel sent a meet and confer letter on 9/13/23.  While the declarations are silent on this issue, the notice of motion indicates Defendant failed to respond to the attempt to meet and confer.  Notably, Defense Counsel’s declaration in opposition to the motion is silent concerning any attempt to meet and confer between 9/13/23 and the filing of the motions, on 10/03/23.  Defense Counsel declares he commenced meeting and conferring “following the filing of the within Motion.”  It was the obligation of Defense Counsel to meet and confer BEFORE the motions were filed, so as to avoid the need for the filing of the motions.  That said, Plaintiff’s meet and confer letter merely proposed further responses by 9/18/23 and documents by 10/01/23, and did not propose any further communication or attempts to meet and confer, and there was apparently no attempt to follow up prior to filing the motions. 

 

On balance, the Court finds both parties could have engaged in more extensive meet and confer efforts in order to avoid the need for the filing of the motions.  In light of the lack of reply papers, the Court is inclined not to impose sanctions at this time.

Case Number: 23LBCV00310    Hearing Date: January 30, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Complaint

Plaintiffs, Monet Duffen, et al. filed this action against Defendant, Matthew Berlign for damages arising out of an automobile accident.  The accident occurred on 1/28/21.  Plaintiffs filed their complaint on 2/17/23.    

 

2.     Demurrer

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares the parties met and conferred but were unable to resolve the issue of whether the statute of limitations bars the action.  The demurrer is therefore ripe for determination on its merits. 

 

  1. Request for Judicial Notice

Defendants seek judicial notice of Plaintiffs’ complaint, Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint, and the fact that there is no summons with the FAC.  The RJN is unnecessary.  The Court evaluates the operative complaint and all documents in the file when evaluating a demurrer. 

 

  1. Statute of Limitations

i.              Parties’ Positions

Defendant argues the entire complaint is barred by the two-year statute of limitations.  Plaintiffs argue the demurrer should be overruled because (a) the complaint was timely filed, (b) the amended complaint relates back to the original complaint, and (c) the SOL was equitably tolled.

 

ii.             Initial Note

The proof of service of the opposition to the demurrer is unsigned and undated, and has various blanks not filled in.  Plaintiffs’ attorney’s inattention to detail is made manifest throughout the opposition papers.  The Court asks Counsel to ensure appropriate attention to detail in the future in connection with this and other matters.

 

iii.            Timely Filing

Plaintiffs’ primary contention is that their complaint was timely filed within two years.  Plaintiffs provide the declaration of their attorney and various exhibits thereto.  The declaration and exhibits establish that Plaintiffs first filed their complaint on 1/30/23.  The Clerk rejected the filing because the address of the intersection and the zip code in the addendum did not match.  The rejection was dated 1/30/23 at 2:03 p.m.  The documents were resubmitted on 2/08/23 at 12:10 p.m. and rejected “per conversation with attorney’s office” the same day at 2:10 p.m.  The documents were resubmitted the same day at 4:43 p.m. and were rejected the following motion (2/09/23) at 8:35 a.m. because the documents were not text-searchable and Steven Johnson was improperly named in the complaint.  The reject notice also noted that the type of action on the addendum must match the type of action on the portal.  The documents were resubmitted on 2/09/23 at 2:25 p.m. and were rejected on 2/17/23 at 1:33 p.m., again because Johnson was improperly named and the type of action on the addendum did not match the type of action on the portal.  On 2/17/23, Plaintiffs filed their summons, complaint, and related documents, all of which were accepted for filing.    

 

CRC 1010.6(e)(4)(E) provides, “If the clerk of the court does not file a complaint or cross complaint because the complaint or cross complaint does not comply with applicable filing requirements or the required filing fee has not been paid, any statute of limitations applicable to the causes of action alleged in the complaint or cross complaint shall be tolled for the period beginning on the date on which the court received the document and as shown on the confirmation of receipt described in subparagraph (A), through the later of either the date on which the clerk of the court sent the notice of rejection described in subparagraph (C) or the date on which the electronic filing service provider or electronic filing manager sent the notice of rejection as described in subparagraph (D), plus one additional day if the complaint or cross complaint is subsequently submitted in a form that corrects the errors which caused the document to be rejected. The party filing the complaint or cross complaint shall not make any change to the complaint or cross complaint other than those required to correct the errors which caused the document to be rejected.”

 

Plaintiffs did not act in a way that tolled the statute of limitations per the Rules of Court.  This is so for two reasons.  First, this is so because the statute was only tolled if Plaintiffs corrected the defect listed in the rejection.  Plaintiffs were instructed more than once that Johnson should not be named and that the type of action in the portal had to match the type of action in the addendum.  Additionally, this is so because Plaintiffs were required to re-submit the next day.  The first rejection of the documents was on 1/30/23, but Plaintiffs did not attempt to re-file until 2/08/23. 

 

iv.            Relation Back

Plaintiffs’ second argument is that the action relates back to the filing of the original complaint, which was filed on 1/30/23.  The problem with this argument is that, as detailed above, the original complaint was NOT filed on 1/30/23.  It was submitted on 1/30/23 and was rejected, and the defects were not timely cured.  This argument therefore also fails.

 

v.             Equitable Tolling

Plaintiffs’ final argument is that the statute of limitations was equitable tolled.  Specifically, Plaintiffs contend they had been working with Defendant’s insurance carrier, Progressive, which had accepted liability for the accident and was negotiating Plaintiffs’ claim. 

 

"California applies equitable tolling if before a plaintiff filed a case the plaintiff gave the defendant timely notice of the claim through a prior civil action, through an administrative proceeding, or through a grievance procedure.”  Addison v. State of California (1979) 21 Cal.3d 313; Elkins v. Cere (1974) 12 Cal.3d 410, 414; McDonald v. Antelope Valle Community College Dist. (2008) 45 Cal.4th 88, 111-112.  The three elements of equitable tolling are (a) timely notice, (b) lack of prejudice, and (c) reasonableness and good faith.  Addison at 319. 

 

In Collier v. City of Pasadena (1983) 142 Cal.App.3d 917, 924, the Court held:

The timely notice requirement essentially means that the first claim must have been filed within the statutory period. Furthermore the filing of the first claim must alert the defendant in the second claim of the need to begin investigating the facts which form the basis for the second claim. Generally this means that the defendant in the first claim is the same one being sued in the second. As an example, a workers' compensation claim equitably tolls a personal injury action against that same employer for injuries sustained in the same incident. But under ordinary circumstances that workers' compensation claim would not equitably toll a personal injury action against a third party who might also be liable for the injury. (Freeman v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1975) 14 Cal.3d 473 [121 Cal.Rptr. 477, 535 P.2d 341]; but see Tu-Vu Drive-In Corp. v. Davies, supra, 66 Cal.2d 435 where a claim against a third party tolled the statute of limitations against an attorney defendant in a wrongful execution case.)

 

Plaintiffs herein did not file any prior lawsuit against Defendant.  They pursued an insurance claim with Defendant’s insurer.  Plaintiffs fail to cite any authority applying the doctrine of equitable estoppel under such circumstances.  Absent such authority, the Court is not inclined to extend the doctrine to circumstances where an insurance claim is pending but no timely lawsuit has been filed. 

 

vi.            Conclusion

The demurrer is sustained on the ground that the statute of limitations bars the action.  Because this is a purely legal issue, leave to amend is denied. 

 

3.     Motion to Strike

Defendant’s motion to strike is moot in light of the ruling on the demurrer.
