DEPARTMENT 205 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV01996    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: 205

Superior Court of California 

County of Los Angeles – West District 

Beverly Hills Courthouse / Department 205 

 

 

MATTHEW WINTER, 

  

Plaintiff, 

v. 

 

ASAF GLAZER, et al.,  

 

Defendants. 

 

  Case No.:  23SMCV01996 

  

  Hearing Date:  November 26, 2024 

  

 

  [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: 

  DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL  

  RESPONSES TO REQUEST FOR  

  DOCUMENTS (SET THREE) AND  

  REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS  

 

 

 

 

 

BACKGROUND 

 

This action arises out of a car accident.  Plaintiff Matthew Winter’s car was struck by a car driven by Defendant Asaf Glazer (“Moving Defendant”), who was attempting to make a left turn from a private driveway.  Plaintiff alleges Glazer carelessly and negligent struck his car which was proceeding straight and had the right of way.   

At the time of the accident, Plaintiff claims Glazer was performing job duties for the joint business venture between Defendants David Asulin, JRG Realtors, Inc. and Mr. Build Home Improvement Company.  Plaintiff alleges these Defendants are vicariously liable for the actions of Glazer.   

On December 26, 2023, Defendant served Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), Set Two, seeking “all text messages you sent or received on YOUR cellular telephone on the day of the INCIDENT” (RFP No. 21) and “all phone calls you made or received on YOUR cellular telephone on the day of the INCIDENT” (RFP No. 22). (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 4.)  On April 24, 2024, the Parties participated in an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) before this Court. (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 5.)  During the IDC, Plaintiff agreed to search for and produce evidence of phone calls and/or text messages within the thirty (30) minutes preceding the subject accident. (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 8.)  On May 30, 2024, Plaintiff provided further verified responses to RFP Nos. 21 and 22, indicating that Plaintiff does not possess any responsive documentation within his care and custody, and accordingly, no documents will be produced.  (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 10.)  

Defendant filed three (3) motions to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to RFP Nos. 21 and 22.  (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 11.)  On September 24, 2024, this Court heard Defendant’s Motions to Compel on their merits, concluding that: “Moving Defendant’s declaration does not state specific facts supporting a good cause showing.  In any event, even assuming good cause was shown, Moving Defendant does not explain why Plaintiff’s responses that he has no documents to produce is evasive and not code-compliant.”  (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 13.)  

Two days later, on September 26, 2024, Defendant served his Request for Documents (Set Three) asking Plaintiff to “[p]roduce YOUR cell phone records on October 21, 2022, between 8:16 AM to 9:16 AM.”  The incident occurred at 8:46 a.m., so the request seeks cell phone records 30 minutes before and 30 minutes after the incident.  Defendant claims Plaintiff did not respond to the request.  Plaintiff represents it served a response on October 31, 2024.             

This hearing is on Defendant’s motion to compel a response to the document request.  Defendant initially argued that Plaintiff failed to respond at all.  In his reply, Defendant now argues, Plaintiff failed to timely respond, and therefore, has waived any objection and must produce the requested documents.   

 

 

MEET AND CONFER 

Unlike a motion to compel a “further” response, a motion to compel an initial response does not have a meet and confer requirement.  (Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.)  Here, however, there was a response (Ex. 1 to Mihalic Decl.), and therefore Defendant was obligated to serve a meet and confer declaration.  By failing to do so, Defendant’s motion is procedurally defective.   

LEGAL STANDARD 

Where there has been no timely response to a demand to produce documents, the demanding party may seek an order compelling a response.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300 (b).)  A party’s failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  (Id. at¿§ 2031.300 subd. (a).) 

There is no deadline for a motion to compel initial responses, and the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  (See generally¿Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.)  Under California Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(b), a separate statement is not required when no response has been provided. 

DISCUSSION 

Unless otherwise ordered or specifically provided by law, all moving papers and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least sixteen (16) court days before the hearing. Cal. Code Civ. Pro. § 1005(b).  However, if the notice is served by mail, the required 16-day period of notice before the hearing shall be increased by five calendar days if the place of mailing and the place of address are within the State of California.  (Id.)  

Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel on November 1, 2024.  Sixteen (16) court days from November 1, 2024 is November 26, 2024.  Nevertheless, Defendant filed the instant motion with an original hearing date of November 21, 2024.  Accordingly, Defendant filed his moving papers and supporting papers thirteen (13) court days before the hearing, in violation of Code of Civil Procedure § 1005(b). 

To cure this defect, Defendant continued the hearing date on his motion to compel to November 26, 2024.  But Defendant did not continue the hearing date until November 12, 2024 – five (5) days after Plaintiff’s Opposition to this Motion was due.  (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 7.)  Thus, Defendant did not give Plaintiff the required 16 court days notice in advance of the new hearing date.   

Moreover, as Plaintiff responded to the requests (albeit untimely), Defendant’s motion is one for further responses which in this Department, has additional procedural requirements than a motion to compel initial responses, including (1) the filing of a joint statement setting out the request, the response, and a short statement on each party’s position and (2) attending an informal discovery conference.  Defendant failed to comply with these additional requirements.   

Plaintiff seeks sanctions against Defendant for misuse of the discovery process, by repeatedly propounding the same discovery request for Plaintiff’s cell phone records.  The Court concludes that sanctions are warranted.   

Section 2023.030 states that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.”    

Misuses of the discovery process include making a discovery motion unsuccessfully and without substantial justification.  (Cal. Code Civ. Pro. § 2023.030.)  “Substantial justification” means clearly reasonable justification that is well grounded in both law and fact.” (Padron v. Watchtower Bible & Tract of New York, Inc. (2017) 16 Cal.App.5th 1246, 1269.)   

The misuse of discovery process also includes failing to meet and confer in good faith to resolve a discovery dispute when required by statute to do so.  (Michaely v. Michaely (2007) 150 Cal.App.4th 802.) 

Here, Defendant has previously served substantially identical discovery requests to which Plaintiff has served verified, code-compliant responses.  Defendant moved to compel further responses to those previous, substantially identical discovery requests, which the Court denied for lack of good cause and failure to show Plaintiff’s verified responses were not code-compliant.  The next day, Defendant served substantially identical discovery requests with slightly different wording.  Defendant then filed a motion to compel without meeting and conferring and further indicating that Plaintiff had not responded to Defendant’s discovery requests, when Plaintiff in fact had done so.  On these facts, the Court finds Defendant cannot demonstrate substantial justification for his actions.   

In determining the appropriate amount of¿sanctions, the Court¿starts¿with the¿lodestar¿which is the reasonable hourly rate multiplied by the reasonable hours spent.¿(PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1096; see also¿Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)  Plaintiff’s counsel has been practicing law for 3 years, and he does not charge an hourly rate to his clients, but contends the value of his services is $300 per hour.  (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 20.)  Counsel represents that a higher hourly rate of $325 was approved as reasonable in the United States District Courts for the Southern District of Florida and the Eastern District of New York.  (Mihalic Decl. ¶ 21.)  On these facts, the Court concludes counsel’s hourly rate of $300 is reasonable. 

Plaintiff seeks sanctions for 12.75 hours of work.  But only 9.25 of these hours relate to the instant motion.  The remainder relate to another motion (a motion for protective order) and meet and confer attempts related to prior discovery requests.  The Court will only award fees associated with this motion to compel.  Accordingly, the Court awards sanctions of $2,775. 

 

CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s motion to compel and for sanctions.  The Court awards sanctions of $2,775 in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant and his counsel, jointly and severally. 

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

BACKGROUND 

 

This case arises from a motor vehicle v. pedestrian accident on July 25, 2023.  Plaintiff Allen Ashkenazi was in a crosswalk with a green pedestrian light, when Defendant Drewella McLendon made a left turn crossing over the crosswalk and severely injuring Plaintiff.  At the time of the accident, McLendon was driving in the course and scope of her employment with co-defendants Stepping Stones Group (“Stepping Stones”) and Center for Behavioral Education and Social Therapies (“CBEST”).   

On August 27, 2024, Plaintiff propounded his special interrogatories, set three (“SROGs”), and demand for production of documents, set 3 (“RFPs”) on each Defendant.  (Ex. 1 to Bohrer Decl.)  As such, responses were due no later than September 30, 2024.   

On October 7, 2024, Plaintiff reminded Defendants of the overdue responses.  (Ex. 2 to Bohrer Decl.).  Defense counsel claimed to have overlooked the discovery requests.  Plaintiff’s counsel than re-sent the requests with the admonition: “Because I'm a nice guy, and understand that things inadvertently get missed, I will not rush into motionville (again) if vou can give me a reasonable and agreed-to time frame for responses.”  (Ex. 3 to Bohrer Decl.)  On October 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed four motions to compel, one for each discovery request to each Defendant.   

Over a month after the responses were due and 15 days after the motions were filed, Defendants served their responses on November 1, 2024.  Accordingly, the motions to compel initial responses are moot, and the only issue remaining for this Court is whether sanctions should be awarded.   

DISCUSSION 

Interrogatories 

Plaintiff requests sanctions based on Defendant’s failure to timely respond to Plaintiff’s SROGs.  Plaintiff relies on Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 which states: “The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

Although § 2030.290 imposes sanctions only when a party unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, and Defendant is not opposing the motion to compel (and indeed provided discovery responses to all the interrogatories), Local Rule 3.1348 allows the Court to impose sanctions “in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” 

Moreover, the Court may award sanctions for discovery abuses which include the failure to timely respond to discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§¿2023.010 subd. (d),¿2023.030).)  “Courts are authorized to exercise their discretion to award discovery sanctions for abuse of the discovery process.”  (NewLife Sciences, LLC v. Weinstock (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 676, fn. 10 (citing Code Civ. Proc., §§¿2023.010,¿2023.030).) 

Defendants argue that no sanctions should be imposed because they provided responses.  There is no explanation as to why the responses were untimely.  On these facts, the Court concludes that Defendant has not shown substantial justification for their untimely responses or that the circumstances would make the imposition of sanctions unjust.   

In determining the amount of sanctions, the Court starts with the lodestar which is the reasonable hourly rate multiplied by the reasonable time spent.  Counsel represents his regular billing rate is $675.  He provides no further explanation on his experience or that his rate has been approved in other cases.  Accordingly, the Court uses a reasonable hourly rate of $250.  As to the amount of hours expended, counsel represents he spent three hours for the two motions to compel SROGs.  Counsel also attests he expects to spend one hour in reviewing the opposition and preparing the reply as to each motion, and he expects to spend three hours of travel time and one hour to attend the hearing.   

Counsel acknowledges that he can only seek the travel and hearing time once as he will only be attending one hearing on all four motions he has filed.  Also, Plaintiff has only filed one omnibus opposition, and accordingly, the Court will award only one hour total for all time spent to review the opposition and prepare the consolidated reply.  Further, the Court concludes that the three hours of travel time is excessive and will only award one hour of travel time.  In sum, the Court will award 8.5 hours to prepare the two motions to compel SROGs (or $2,125) plus motion fees ($162.96) for a total of $2,287.96.  

Document Demands 

Plaintiff requests sanctions based on Defendant’s failure to timely respond to Plaintiff’s RFPs.  Plaintiff relies on Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.300 which states: “(c) Except as provided in subdivision (d), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” 

Although § 2030.290 imposes sanctions only when a party unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel, and Defendant is not opposing the motion to compel (and indeed provided discovery responses to all document demands), Local Rule 3.1348 allows the Court to impose sanctions “in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” 

Moreover, the Court may award sanctions for discovery abuses which include the failure to timely respond to discovery.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§¿2023.010 subd. (d),¿2023.030).)  “Courts are authorized to exercise their discretion to award discovery sanctions for abuse of the discovery process.”  (NewLife Sciences, 197 Cal.App.4th at fn. 10 (citing Code Civ. Proc., §§¿2023.010 2023.030).) 

In determining the amount of sanctions, the Court uses the lodestar and a reasonable hourly rate of $250.  As to the amount of hours spent, counsel represents he spent three hours on the two motions to compel RFPs.  Counsel also attests he expects to spend one hour in reviewing the opposition and preparing the reply as to each motion, and he expects to spend three hours of travel time and one hour to attend the hearing.  Counsel acknowledges that he can only seek the travel and hearing time once as he will only be attending one hearing on the six motions to compel he has filed.  Also, Plaintiff has only filed one omnibus opposition to the four motions to compel, and accordingly, the Court will award only one hour total for all time spent to review the opposition and prepare the reply, which time is already accounted for in the sanctions awarded for the motion to compel SROGs.  In sum, the Court will award three hours to prepare the two motions to compel (or $750) plus motion fees ($162.96) for a total of $912.96. 

Issue and Evidentiary Sanctions 

In addition to monetary sanctions, Plaintiff also seeks issue and evidentiary sanctions.  Plaintiff argues that issue and evidence sanctions are warranted because to date, Plaintiff has filed 10 motions to compel responses, and Defendants’ repeated failures to timely respond has stymied Plaintiff’s efforts to seek discovery.  Plaintiff, however, does not specify what issues he seeks to be admitted or evidence he seeks to preclude.  Accordingly, the Court denies the request for issue and evidentiary sanctions.  

CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s motions to compel as moot and GRANTS IN PART and DENIES IN PART the request for sanctions.  The Court will award sanctions in the amount of $3,200.92 jointly and severally against Defendant and its counsel’s law firm Zelm, Erlich & Mack.
