DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV12295    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

July 3, 2024

CASE NUMBER

20STCV12295

MOTION

Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

MOVING PARTY

Plaintiff Sujey Tinoco

OPPOSING PARTY

None

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Sujey Tinoco (“Plaintiff”) prevailed at trial against Defendant Teresa Medical Center, Inc. (“Defendant”).  Plaintiff moves for attorney fees.  Defendant has not opposed the motion.

 ANALYSIS

In actions under the Fair Employment and Housing Act, “the court, in its discretion, may award to the prevailing party . . . reasonable attorney's fees . . . .”  (Gov. Code, § 12965, subd. (b).)  The court finds Plaintiff is entitled to attorney fees as the prevailing party against Defendant.  The prevailing party must seek an award of attorney fees through a noticed motion and bears the burden of proof to justify the amount sought. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5)(A).)

In the instant matter, Plaintiff contends her attorneys billed $733,130.65 in attorney fees. This lodestar figure is based on billing at hourly rates of up to $1,000 for attorneys and $300 for paralegals. “The reasonable market value of the attorney's services is the measure of a reasonable hourly rate. This standard applies regardless of whether the attorneys claiming fees charge nothing for their services, charge at below-market or discounted rates, represent the client on a straight contingent fee basis, or are in-house counsel.” (Chacon v. Litke (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 1234, 1260, internal quotations and citations omitted.) Based on the court’s review of the case file, including Plaintiff’s detailed summary of the services performed and by whom, the court concludes a collective $500 per hour is a reasonable rate for attorneys and $200 per hour is a reasonable rate for paralegals engaged in similar litigation in the Los Angeles area, and these hourly rates amply compensate Plaintiff’s counsel for their work on this case.

The court declines to award compensation for 23.6 hours of work performed by law clerks and an investigator, which the court views as part of the overhead of running a law office.  In addition, the court notes there are entries by attorneys and paralegals described as “developed case strategy,” and there are also many entries related to conferences in which multiple attorneys and paralegals were involved.  Thus, while solely considering the multiple entries concerning developing case strategy and not those related to conferences, the court deducts 56 hours of attorney time and 17 hours of paralegal time.  This amounts to a deduction of $31,400 (for attorneys, 56 hours multiplied by $500 equals $28,000, and for paralegals, 17 hours multiplied by $200 equals $3,400). 

The court, therefore, adjusts the lodestar figure to $472,465 and grants Plaintiff’s motion for an award of attorney fees in that amount.  This figure represents $403,425 for attorney time calculated by multiplying 806.85[1] hours by $500 and $100,440 for paralegal time calculated by multiplying 502.20 hours by $200, for a total of $503,865, and then deducting $31,400 from that amount.  The court declines to award a multiplier.

Plaintiff also seeks costs and expenses.  As required, Plaintiff submitted a cost memorandum on Judicial Council Form MC-011, pursuant to Rules of Court, rule 3.1700(a).  Plaintiff is awarded costs in the amount of $24, 837.28.  This amount represents a deduction from Plaintiff’s cost bill of $1,514.10 which Plaintiff designated as “Other” without an explanation of the costs or expenses to be attributed to it.

Plaintiff is to give notice of, and file proof of service concerning, this order. 


[1]The court’s tabulation of the number of attorney hours billed in the case is 806.85.  But the summary of time billed Plaintiff attached to her motion indicates that amount is 892.1 hours.  It appears the discrepancy results from an error in the total amount of hours attributed to attorney Sylvia Panosian at the end of the summary.  It indicates she billed a total of 87.2 hours when it should have been 2 hours, given the information Plaintiff provides.

CASE NUMBER

23STCV18072

MOTIONS

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendants AQP Property Management and Station 11, LLC

OPPOSING PARTIES

Plaintiffs Maria Victoria Martinez and Farid Martinez

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Maria Victoria Martinez (“Victoria”) and Farid Martinez assert habitability violations against Defendants AQP Property Management and Station 11, LLC.  Defendants demur to the fifth, eighth, and ninth causes of action in Plaintiffs’ first amended complaint.  Defendants also move to strike portions of the first amended complaint.  Plaintiffs oppose the motions. 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations of the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action is under the Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (“Rosenthal Act”).  That provision regulates the collection of consumer debts incurred to acquire property, services, or money for personal, family, or household use on credit.  (Civ. Code, § 1788.2, subds. (b), (e), (f), (h).)  The Rosenthal Act applies to persons and entities “who, in the ordinary course of business, regularly, on behalf of that person or others engage in debt collection,” or who compose and sell forms, letters, and other collection materials.  (Civ. Code, § 1788.2, subds. (c), (g).)  

In this case, Plaintiffs allege Defendants are debt collectors because Defendants collected utility payments from Plaintiffs, and Plaintiffs used utilities on credit.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 121-122.)  However, Plaintiffs allege Defendants fraudulently charged Plaintiffs for electricity and gas, for which Plaintiffs had already paid.  (Id., ¶¶ 52-56.)  Plaintiffs, therefore, do not allege Defendants were collecting payment for purchases Plaintiffs made on credit, but rather Defendants were wrongfully overcharging Plaintiffs.  Further, Plaintiffs do not allege Defendants regularly engaged in this scheme, such that it is part of the ordinary course of Defendants’ business.  Plaintiffs’ citation to cases in support of their contention is unavailing.  (See, e.g., Calvary SPV I, LLC v. Watkins (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 1070, 1085 [debt at issue based on use of a personal gasoline credit card]; Davidson v. Seterus, Inc., (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 283, 296-297 [court addressed the issue of whether collecting mortgage debt, not paying for utilities, falls under the Rosenthal Act]; Gouskos v. Aptos Village Garage, Inc. (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 754, 759 [court found the automobile repair transaction at issue did not constitute a consumer credit transaction].)  In the instant matter, Defendants’ alleged conduct does not fall within the Rosenthal Fair Debt Collection Practices Act.

Plaintiffs’ eighth cause of action is under the Unruh Civil Rights Act (“Unruh Act”).  To state a cause of action under the Unruh Act, Plaintiff must allege Defendants are business establishments that intentionally denied Plaintiff full and equal accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, or services based on Plaintiff’s sex, race, color, religion, ancestry, national origin, disability, medical condition, marital status, or sexual orientation. (Civ. Code, § 51, subd. (b).)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants discriminated against Victoria because of her gender and national origin by pressuring her to leave the property.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 146.)  These allegations suffice for pleading purposes.  The essence of Defendants’ argument is that they do not believe Plaintiffs’ allegations.  Plaintiffs’ credibility is not at issue on demurrer.  Defendants may obtain further information regarding the veracity of Plaintiff’s allegations during discovery. 

Plaintiffs’ ninth cause of action is for housing discrimination under the Fair Employment and Housing Act.  It is unlawful “[f]or the owner of any housing accommodation to discriminate against or harass any person because of the race, color, religion, sex, gender, gender identity, gender expression, sexual orientation, marital status, national origin, ancestry, familial status, source of income, disability, or genetic information of that person.” (Gov. Code, § 12955, subd. (a).)  Again, Plaintiffs allege Defendants discriminated against Victoria based on her gender and national origin.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 158.)  These allegations suffice for pleading purposes.

Defendants also move to strike the prayer for punitive damages. To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294. (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) In that regard, a plaintiff must allege the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166, internal citations and footnotes omitted.)

In the instant matter, Plaintiffs allege, “Defendant’s acts were willful, malicious, and oppressive, amounting to despicable conduct that subjected Plaintiffs to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of their rights, so as to entitle Plaintiffs to an award of punitive and exemplary damages in an amount to be determined at trial.”  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 100.)  Plaintiffs do not allege specific facts to support these conclusions.  Accordingly, the Court grants the motion to strike with leave to amend.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ fifth cause of action is sustained with leave to amend.  The demurrer is otherwise overruled.

The Court grants Defendants’ motion to strike the punitive damages allegations with leave to amend.

Plaintiffs are to file an amended complaint within 10 days of the issuance of this ruling.

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

July 3, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV27343

MOTION

Motion to Compel Arbitration

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Hollywood Inn, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Luis Hernandez

 

MOTION

              Defendants Hollywood Inn, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves to compel arbitration of the claims Plaintiff Luis Hernandez (“Plaintiff”) asserts against Defendant.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.  Plaintiff also filed an amended complaint.  Plaintiff had the right to amend his complaint without leave of court before Defendant responded to the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 472.)  However, Plaintiff did not file the amended complaint until after Defendant had filed and served this motion to compel arbitration.  As such, the Court disregards the first amended complaint.  

ANALYSIS

The moving party on a petition to compel arbitration “bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by a preponderance of the evidence, while a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.  The trial court sits as the trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, and any oral testimony the court may receive at its discretion, to reach a final determination.”  (Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Defendant presents an arbitration agreement that Plaintiff signed when Plaintiff worked for Defendant.  The arbitration agreement provides that Plaintiff and Defendant agree to “utilize binding individual arbitration to resolve all disputes that might arise out of or be related in any way to [Plaintiff’s] employment by [Defendant].”  (Declaration of Atul Madhav in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit A.)  Accordingly, Defendant meets its burden to show an agreement to arbitrate covers Plaintiff’s claims. 

The arbitration agreement Plaintiff signed specifies the Federal Arbitration Act governs.  (Declaration of Atul Madhav in Support of Motion to Compel Arbitration, Exhibit A.)  A choice of law provision in an arbitration agreement is enforceable.  (See Coopers & Lybrand v. Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 524, 538.)  As such, the Federal Arbitration Act applies. 

Plaintiff argues the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021, which became law on March 3, 2022, bars arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims.  This law amends the Federal Arbitration Act to provide, “at the election of the person alleging conduct constituting a sexual harassment dispute or sexual assault dispute, or the named representative of a class or in a collective action alleging such conduct, no predispute arbitration agreement . . . shall be valid or enforceable with respect to a case which is filed under Federal, Tribal, or State law and relates to the sexual assault dispute or the sexual harassment dispute.”    (9 U.S.C. § 402(a).)  For purposes of this law, a sexual assault dispute is “a dispute involving a nonconsensual sexual act or sexual contact . . . , including when the victim lacks capacity to consent . . . ,” and a sexual harassment dispute is “a dispute relating to conduct that is         alleged to constitute sexual harassment under applicable Federal, Tribal, or State law.  (9 U.S.C. § 401(3)-(4).)  The law applies to “any dispute or claim that arises or accrues on or after the date of enactment of this Act.”  (9 U.S.C. § 401 note.) In this case, Plaintiff’s claims arose after May 2, 2022.  (Complaint, ¶ 9.)  Accordingly, the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 applies to this case. 

In the operative complaint, however, Plaintiff does not assert a claim for sexual harassment or sexual assault.  Further, even if the Court were to consider Plaintiff’s unauthorized first amended complaint, Plaintiff does not assert a claim for sexual harassment because in order to do so, Plaintiff must allege Defendant subjected Plaintiff to “sexual propositions, unwarranted graphic discussion of sexual acts, and commentary on the employee's body and the sexual uses to which it could be put . . . ,” or that “an employer created a hostile environment for an employee because of that employee's sex.”  (Mogilefsky v. Superior Court (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1415.)  In this matter, Plaintiff alleges Defendants refused to permit Plaintiff’s boyfriend to move in with him at the housing Defendants provided for him.  (First Amended Complaint, ¶ 36.)  That is discrimination against Plaintiff “in compensation or in terms, conditions, or privileges of employment . . . ,” not harassment.  (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (a).)  Accordingly, the Ending Forced Arbitration of Sexual Assault and Sexual Harassment Act of 2021 does not bar arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to arbitrate is granted.  This case is stayed pending arbitration.  The Court sets an order to show case re: status of arbitration to be heard on January 9, 2025.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
