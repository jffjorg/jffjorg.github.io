DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 19STCV08965    Hearing Date: September 19, 2023    Dept: 56

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

ERIK KITTER, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

 

KEUM NAM CHANG, et al.,                                                           

                        Defendants.   

 

      CASE NO.: 19STCV08965

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: MOTIONS TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL

 

Date:  September 19, 2023

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

Jury Trial: April 8, 2024

 

 

AND CONSOLIDATED ACTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY: Brett G. Hampton (“Hampton”)

 

Hampton seeks to be relieved as counsel for Defendants Keum Nam Chang (“Chang”) and GJY Property Investment, LLC (“GJY”) (collectively, “Defendants”). The motions to be relieved as counsel (collectively, the “Motions”) that Hampton filed on August 17, 2023 are compliant with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362.  The Motions are unopposed.  Any opposition papers were required to have been filed and served at least nine court days before the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure section 1005, subdivision (b).[1]

 

 

DISCUSSION

The court has discretion on whether to allow an attorney to withdraw, and a motion to withdraw will not be granted where withdrawal would prejudice the client.  (Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915.) 

 

In support of the Motions, Hampton declares there has been an irreconcilable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship that prevents the representation from being able to proceed.  The Court finds this to be an adequate basis for withdrawal.  For this reason and because they are unopposed, the Court GRANTS the Motions.  (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)  As a business entity, GJY must be represented by counsel.  (See Caressa Camille, Inc. v. Alcoholic Beverage Control Appeals Bd. (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 1094, 1101.)  The Court schedules an OSC regarding GJY’s representation on October 10, 2023 at 8:30 a.m. in this department.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the Court at SMC_DEPT56@lacourt.org as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion will be placed off calendar. 

 

     Dated this 19th day of September 2023

 

 

 

 

Hon. Holly J. Fujie

Judge of the Superior Court

 

 

 


[1] On September 1, 2019, GJY filed a Notice of Bankruptcy Stay based on its initiation of bankruptcy proceedings.

            RESPONDING PARTY: Defendants Michael Theodore and Casa Theodore, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”)

 

The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

 

BACKGROUND

This action arises out of a dispute over the enforcement of a previously issued judgment. The currently operative fourth amended complaint (the “4AC”) alleges: (1) statutory voidable transfers; and (2) common law voidable transfers. 

 

           

 

On March 10, 2023, the Court granted Plaintiff’s motion to compel responses to Requests for Production (“RFPs”), Set Five and denied Defendants’ motion for protective order that argued that they should not be required to respond to the same set of RFPs.   

 

On April 5, 2023, Defendants filed a motion for relief from waiver of objections to the RFPs addressed in the March 10, 2023 order.  Two days later, on April 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to the RFPs that argued that the objections asserted in Defendants’ responses were meritless.  The Court heard both of these motions on June 22, 2023 and issued a minute order granting Defendants relief from waiver of objections.  Based on the finding that Defendants were relieved of their waiver of the right to assert objections to the RFPs, the Court determined that the Plaintiff’s motion to compel further was moot and did not consider its arguments on the merits of Defendants’ asserted objections.

 

On July 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed a motion to compel further responses to the RFPs (the “Motion”).  The instant Motion concerns substantially the same RFPs as the April 7, 2023 filing. 

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

            Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections are OVERRULED in their entirety.

 

 

 

 

 

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

Under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 2031.310, a motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (CCP § 2031.310, subd. (c).)  A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand.  (See CCP § 2031.310 subd. (b)(1); Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 234-35.)  To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.  (Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.) 

 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections.  (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)  The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  (CCP § 2017.020, subd. (a).)  To support an objection to a request for production of documents, the objecting party must establish the validity of its objections with supporting facts in order to meet its burden.  (Southern Pac. Co. v. Superior Court (1969) 3 Cal.App.3d 195, 198.) 

 

Meet and Confer

            As a preliminary matter, the Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

 

Timeliness

Unless notice of the motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.  (CCP § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

 

            It is undisputed that the instant Motion concerns discovery responses that Defendants provided on March 22, 2023.  Defendants argue that the Motion is not timely because it was filed more than 45 days after they provided their responses.  Due to the procedural circumstances and oscillating motion practice regarding discovery in this case, which most recently culminated in the Court, on its own motion, removing Plaintiff’s April 7, 2023 motion to compel further from the calendar, the Court finds that the instant Motion is not time-barred.  The Court’s March 10, 2023 order directed Defendants to submit responses without objections to the RFPs, and although Defendants subsequently sought relief from waiver of objections, before the Court granted them that relief on June 22, 2023, their responses were not compliant with a court order.[1]  The Court’s June 22, 2023 order determined that Defendants were entitled to relief from a blanket waiver of objections without a consideration of the legal merits of individual objections.  Plaintiff never withdrew his April 7, 2023 motion or otherwise conceded the arguments raised therein.  The Court will therefore consider the merits of the instant Motion.

 

Privacy/Privilege

Tax returns are privileged from disclosure.  (Strawn v. Morris, Polich & Purdy, LLP (2019) 30 Cal.App.5th 1087, 1098.)  The purpose of the privilege is to encourage voluntary filing of tax returns and truthful reporting of income, and thus to facilitate tax collection.  (Id.)  The tax return privilege is not absolute and will not be upheld when: (1) the circumstances indicate an intentional waiver of the privilege; (2) the gravamen of the lawsuit is inconsistent with the privilege; or (3) a public policy greater than that of the confidentiality of tax returns is involved.  (Id.)  In situations where the third exception applies, disclosure may be ordered when the party requesting disclosure establishes: (1) the defendant has refused to produce relevant nonprivileged financial records or has produced only meaningless and unreliable financial information in response to punitive damage discovery; (2) the defendant has engaged in a pattern of improperly obstructing efforts to obtain financial records through means that do not implicate the privilege and it is reasonable to assume this pattern of conduct will continue; and (3) less intrusive methods to obtain the financial records have been unsuccessful.  (Li v. Yan (2016) 247 Cal.App.4th 56, 67.)  Judgment debtor examinations serve an important function in our judicial system that may serve as a predicate for a court requiring the disclosure of tax records.  (See id. at 66-67.)

 

The Motion details the ongoing discovery disputes regarding Defendants’ financial information that sufficiently demonstrates that public policy concerns outweigh Defendants’ privacy interests in their financial records.  Defendants attempt to distinguish the authority cited in the Motion by noting that this is a separate action rather than an attempt to enforce the judgment entered in a previous action, but their arguments do not address the underlying policy concerns.  As a result, Defendants’ arguments are not convincing, particularly since initiating a separate action is a permissible alternative to enforce a judgment.  (See, e.g., Berger v. Varum (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 1013, 1016-17.)  Furthermore, Defendants have repeatedly evaded their discovery obligations, even when faced with court orders requiring them to cooperate with Plaintiff’s requests. 

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS the Motion.  Defendants are ordered to submit supplemental responses to the RFPs detailed in the Separate Statement filed in support of the instant Motion within 20 days of the date of this order. 

 

Monetary Sanctions

            In connection with the Motion, Plaintiff requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $4,061.  (Declaration of Lyle R. Mink (“Mink Decl.”) ¶ 42.)  This amount represents: (1) three hours preparing the moving papers at a rate of $500 per hour; (2) an anticipated two hours preparing reply papers and three hours attending the hearing; and (3) a filing fee in the amount of $61.65.  (Id.) 

 

The Court exercises its discretion and awards Plaintiff monetary sanctions in the reasonable amount of $1,561.65, which represents three hours preparing the Motion at a rate of $500 per hour and $61.65 in filing fees.  (Moran v. Oso Valley Greenbelt Assn. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 1029, 1034.)  Defendants and counsel are jointly responsible for paying this amount within 20 days of the date of this order.

Case Number: 23STCV03131    Hearing Date: September 19, 2023    Dept: 56

 

 

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

ISIGN INTERNATIONAL, INC.,

 

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

THIEN PHAM, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

      CASE NO.:  23STCV03131

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: DEMURRER

 

Date: September 19, 2023

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

Judge: Holly J. Fujie

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Thien Pham (“Moving Defendant”)[1]

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff

 

The Court has considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.[2]

 

BACKGROUND

            This action arises out of an employment relationship.  The Complaint alleges: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (3) fraud and deceit; (4) breach of fiduciary duty; (5) constructive fraud; (6) conversion; (7) misappropriation of trade secrets in violation of Civil Code section 3462; (8) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200; and (9) declaratory relief.

In relevant part, the Complaint alleges: In around 2016, Moving Defendant began working for Moving Defendant as a director and Chief Technology Officer (“CTO”).  (Complaint ¶ 6.)  In around July 2021, in response to issues with Moving Defendant’s job performance, Plaintiff and Moving Defendant entered into a written agreement (the “Employment Agreement”), which provided that Moving Defendant would no longer serve as a director and specified parameters on Moving Defendant’s conduct during the employment relationship.  (See id.)  The provisions of the Employment Agreement restricted Moving Defendant from engaging in other business endeavors that would conflict or interfere with the performance of his job duties.  (Id.)  Under the Employment Agreement, Moving Defendant was also prohibited from using or sharing Plaintiff’s confidential information, such as its proprietary cybersecurity technologies.  (See Complaint ¶¶ 6-9.) 

 

Between late 2021 and September 2022, when two of Plaintiff’s officers, Wayne Taylor (“Taylor”) and Steaphan Weir (“Weir”) would ask for updates about potential business opportunities, Moving Defendant informed them that he was not aware of any.  (See Complaint ¶ 12.)  In late 2022, however, Taylor and Weir learned that Moving Defendant had agreed to begin a separate business relationship with some of Plaintiff’s former potential clients.  (Complaint ¶ 13.)  At this time, Plaintiff also learned that Moving Defendant had shared Plaintiff’s proprietary information in furtherance of establishing his separate relationships with these third parties.  (See id.)  After learning of Moving Defendant’s indiscretions, Plaintiff conducted an investigation and learned that Moving Defendant had also lied about the web data hosting vendor that Plaintiff engaged at his recommendation; rather than being a disinterested third party providing a direct service, Plaintiff learned the organization had contracted out its hosting responsibilities and that Moving Defendant was financially profiting from the arrangement.  (See Complaint ¶¶ 7, 14.) 

 

Moving Defendant filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) to the first, second, third, fifth, and seventh causes of action on the grounds that the Complaint fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action.

 

DISCUSSION

Meet and Confer

The meet and confer requirement has been met.

 

Legal Standard

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)  The court accepts as true all material factual allegations and affords them a liberal construction, but it does not consider conclusions of fact or law, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or judicially noticed facts.  (Shea Homes Limited Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254.)  With respect to a demurrer, the complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded.  (Rodas v. Spiegel (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 513, 517.)  A demurrer will be sustained without leave to amend if there exists no reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) 

 

 

First and Second Causes of Action

The elements of a claim for breach of contract are: (1) the contract; (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) the defendant’s breach; and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.  (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

 

            Moving Defendant argues that the Complaint fails to allege a breach of contract or breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing because it neither quotes the Employment Agreement verbatim nor incorporates a copy of the document.[3]  This argument is not persuasive.  Under California law, a plaintiff may plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.  (See Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Insurance Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-99.)  Moreover, despite Moving Defendant’s arguments to the contrary, the Complaint sufficiently alleges the legal effect of the Employment Agreement—the limitations it imposed on Moving Defendant’s ability to pursue independent relationships for his personal gain—and how Moving Defendant’s conduct deviated from those limitations.  The Court therefore OVERRULES the Demurrer to the first and second causes of action.

 

Third Cause of Action

The elements of intentional misrepresentation are: (1) misrepresentation; (2) knowledge of falsity; (3) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage.  (Golden Eagle Land Investment, L.P. v. Rancho Santa Fe Assn. (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 399, 428.)  The elements of a cause of action for fraud based on concealment/omission are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact; (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff; (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.  (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198.) 

 

Each element of fraud-based claims element must be alleged with particularity.  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  The facts required to be pled are facts that show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.  (Id.)  Less specificity is required to allege fraud when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy.  (Tenet Healthsystem Desert, Inc. v. Blue Cross of California (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 821, 838.)

 

            The Court finds that the allegations of the Complaint are sufficient to satisfy the pleading requirements for fraud-based claims.  The Complaint alleges the specific conduct underlying the claim, and the nature of the allegations suggests that the details of the conduct are necessarily in Moving Defendant’s control.  The Court therefore OVERRULES the Demurrer to the third cause of action.

 

 

 

Fifth Cause of Action

Constructive fraud is a unique species of fraud applicable only to a fiduciary or confidential relationship.  (Assilzadeh v. California Federal Bank (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 399, 415.)  Most acts by an agent in breach of his fiduciary duties constitute constructive fraud.  (Id.)  The elements of a constructive fraud cause of action are: (1) a fiduciary or confidential relationship; (2) nondisclosure (breach of fiduciary duty); (3) intent to deceive; and (4) reliance and resulting injury.  (Prakashpalan v. Engstrom, Lipscomb & Lack (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1131.) 

 

The Demurrer does not distinguish the constructive fraud claim from the fraud and deceit claim or challenge the claim on a basis other than the specificity of the Complaint’s fraud-based allegations.  The Court finds that the Complaint sufficiently alleges constructive fraud based on the fiduciary duty Moving Defendant owed Plaintiff as its CTO and his intentional actions that contravened that duty.  The Court therefore OVERRULES the Demurrer to the fifth cause of action. 

 

Seventh Cause of Action

The elements of a cause of action for misappropriation of trade secrets under the California Uniform Trade Secrets Act (“CUTSA”) are: (1) the plaintiff’s possession of a trade secret; (2) the defendant's misappropriation of the trade secret, meaning its wrongful acquisition, disclosure, or use; and (3) resulting or threatened injury to the plaintiff.  (Silvaco Data Systems v. Intel Corp. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 210, 220, disapproved of on other grounds by Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310.)  Under CUTSA, a trade secret has four elements: (1) it must be comprised of “information”; (2) it must not be “generally known”; (3) it must derive “independent economic value” from the fact that it is a secret; and (4) it must be the subject of “reasonable” efforts to “maintain its secrecy.”  (Civ. Code § 3426.1, subd. (d); see Gemini Aluminum Corp. v. California Custom Shapes, Inc. (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 1249, 1263.)  A misappropriation includes disclosure or use of a trade secret of another without consent.  (See Civ. Code § 3426.1, subd. (b).)  

 

Under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 2019.210, in an action alleging trade secret misappropriation under CUTSA, before commencing discovery relating to the trade secret, the party alleging the misappropriation shall identify the trade secret with reasonable particularity.  (CCP § 2019.210 (emphasis added).)  Plaintiffs are therefore generally not required to spell out the details of their trade secrets in pleadings but must instead describe the subject matter of the trade secrets with sufficient particularity to separate them from matters of general knowledge in the trade or of special persons who are skilled in the trade to permit the defendant to ascertain the boundaries of the alleged secret.  (See Albert’s Organics, Inc. v. Holzman (N.D. Cal. 2020) 445 F.Supp.3d 463, 472.) 

 

Moving Defendant argues that the Complaint fails to identify Plaintiff’s alleged trade secrets with sufficient particularity.  The Complaint alleges categories of Plaintiff’s proprietary and confidential information that sufficiently defines the contours of its allegedly misappropriated trade secrets.  (See Complaint ¶ 9.)  The Court finds that this is sufficient for the purpose of pleading, and that any ambiguities may be clarified by the discovery disclosures required under CCP section 2019.210.  The Court therefore OVERRULES the Demurrer to the seventh cause of action.

 

Venue

            Moving Defendant briefly contends that the Complaint does not establish that jurisdiction or venue are proper.  This contention is not supported by reasoned argument or citations to authority, and the Court thus finds Moving Defendant has waived this point.  (See Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)  

 

            The Court therefore OVERRULES the Demurrer in its entirety.  Moving Defendant is ordered to file a responsive pleading within 20 days of the date of this order.
