DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV05453    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: W

S S v. DAVID NIKNIA, et al.

 

Plaintiff’s motion to lift stay

 

Date of Hearing:        October 12, 203                    Trial Date:       None set.

Department:               W                                               Case No.:        22STCV05453

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 14, 2022, plaintiff SS (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants David Niknia (“Defendant”), Panorama Medical Group (“PMG”), and Ray Salari, M.D. (“Salari”). Plaintiff asserts causes of action for negligence, sexual assault and battery, sexual harassment in a defined relationship, negligent hiring, training, supervision, and retention, and intentional infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant is a licensed physician’s assistant and the chief financial officer and corporate secretary for PMG, which operates a medical practice. (Compl., ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges that on October 14, 2021, Defendant inappropriately, and for no medical purpose, touched her inner thighs, buttocks and vagina and his actions constituted a physical and sexual assault and battery on Plaintiff. (Compl., ¶¶ 27-29.) Plaintiff also alleges that PMG and Salari failed to properly investigate, screen, train, or supervise its employees as to prevent sexual harassment and abuse in their business. (Compl., ¶¶ 18-25.)

 

On May 11, 2022, the instant action was stayed for a period of six months due to Defendant Niknia’s ongoing criminal case against him involving Plaintiff and a violation of Penal Code section 289(D)(3), forcible penetration of a victim with a foreign object.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiff’s Motion to Lift Stay is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff SS moves to lift the stay of proceedings on the grounds Plaintiff SS will be prejudiced if the stay remains and Plaintiff SS cannot conduct discovery against Defendant Niknia.

 

“The Constitution does not ordinarily require a stay of civil proceedings pending the outcome of criminal proceedings [Citations.] … ‘Nevertheless, a court may decide in its discretion to stay civil proceedings ... “when the interests of justice seem [ ] to require such action.” ‘ Id. at 1375 (quoting United States v. Kordel, 397 U.S. 1, 12 n. 27, 90 S.Ct. 763, 769 n. 27, 25 L.Ed.2d 1 (1970)).” (Keating v. Office of Thrift Supervision (9th Cir. 1995) 45 F.3d 322, 324.)  

 

The California Court of Appeal adopted this approach in Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 876, 885. The California Court of Appeal adopted a five factor test from Keating for evaluating if the interest of justice requires a stay to a civil action while a criminal action is proceeding. The tests weighs the following factors: (1) the interest of the plaintiffs in proceeding expeditiously with this litigation or any particular aspect of it, and the potential prejudice to plaintiffs of a delay; (2) the burden which any particular aspect of the proceedings may impose on defendants; (3) the convenience of the court in the management of its cases, and the efficient use of judicial resources; (4) the interests of persons not parties to the civil litigation; and (5) the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal litigation. (Avant! Corp. v. Superior Court, supra, 79 Cal.App.4th at p. 885.)  

 

Plaintiff SS argues almost fourteen months have passed since the first stay of proceedings ordered May 11, 2022. As a result of the stay, Plaintiff SS has not been able to conduct any written or oral discovery to Defendant Niknia for over a year. Plaintiff SS further argues the Keating factors weigh in favor of denying Defendant Niknia’s second motion for stay. Specifically, Plaintiff SS contends a trial would not be set until 2025 if SS needs to wait for Defendant Niknia’s criminal proceeding to conclude. As for the second factor, Plaintiff argues Defendant Niknia would suffer little if any burden in having to respond to discovery. Defendant Niknia can maintain his Fifth Amendment rights and can object based upon his Fifth Amendment to questions that may criminally implicate him. Regarding the third factor, Plaintiff SS argues there would be little, if any, inconvenience to the court if SS is entitled to proceed with discovery and litigation. Plaintiff SS does not address the fourth factor. Lastly, the fifth factor analyzes the interest of the public in the pending civil and criminal actions. Plaintiff argues the public has an interest to not only ensure that Defendant Niknia is charged and convicted for this act, but the public has an interest in ensuring that plaintiff SS is compensated for her serious physical and emotional injuries.

 

In opposition, Defendant Niknia argues he shouldn’t have to choose between sacrificing his constitutional rights or sacrificing his opportunity to defend himself against claims that could result in a significant civil judgment. Moreover, Defendant Niknia argues the Keating factors weigh in his favor. Defendant Niknia argues the first factor weighs in his favor as another six-month stay is not going to unduly prejudice Plaintiff. As for the second factor, as noted above, Defendant Niknia argues he will be faced with a choice to either waive his Fifth Amendment rights or defend the civil action. If Niknia chooses to assert the Fifth Amendment privilege, he will severely inhibit his defense to Plaintiff’s claims, potentially ceding to a civil judgment amounting to hundreds of thousands of dollars. Regarding the third factor, Defendant Niknia argues the continued stay will also negate or minimize the need for extensive disputes and motion practice regarding the boundaries of Niknia’s rights to invoke his Fifth Amendment privilege. As for the fourth factor, Defendant Niknia contends criminal authorities have an interest in allowing the criminal case to proceed, unimpeded by this civil action. Lastly, Defendant Niknia argues the fifth factor favors a continued stay. Defendant Niknia contends the interest of the public would undoubtedly weigh in favor of a continued stay, as the public interest is better served by the completion of a criminal case unfettered by the inevitable civil discovery disputes that will impede Defendant's attempt to resolve the criminal case. Defendant Niknia also argues his age is not a sufficient basis to lift the stay. Specifically, Plaintiffs are unaware of Defendant Niknia’s health condition and moreover, Plaintiff offers no authority that this is a sufficient basis to lift a stay.

 

Based on the arguments put forth, the court finds that it is in the interest of justice to lift the stay and allow the civil action to proceed.  As noted above, Defendant Niknia can invoke his Fifth Amendment privilege if necessary. The court further notes because of the uncertain, extended timeline in the criminal proceedings against Defendant Niknia, Plaintiffs will suffer “prejudice resulting from the loss of evidence, including the inability of witnesses to recall specific facts, or the possible death of a party.”  (Clinton v. Jones (1997) 520 U.S. 681, 707-708.) Current discovery against Defendant Niknia is necessary to look into more witnesses to Defendant Niknia’s alleged modus operandi of sexually assaulting female patients.

 

Therefore, after considering the particular circumstances and competing interests involved in this case, the court grants Plaintiffs’ motion to lift the stay.  


Case Number: 22VECV02109    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: W

YVONNE JOHNSON V. SAFEBUILT INSURANCE COMPANIES, ET AL.

 

DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER WITH AND WITHOUT MOTIONS TO STRIKE

 

Date of Hearing:        October 12, 2023                              Trial Date:       N/A 

Department:              W                                                        Case No:         22VECV02109

 

Moving Party:            Golden State Claims Adjusters and United Specialty Insurance Company

Responding Party:     Plaintiff Yvonne Johnson

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff Yvonne Johnson brought a prior action (via a cross-complaint) against defendant United Specialty Insurance Company’s insured, Arina Builders, who she claimed had damaged her property.  That action resulted in a judgment in plaintiff’s favor for $261,120.  Plaintiff subsequently sought attorney’s fees against the insured, which were granted.  The judgment and the fees were not paid.  Plaintiff than brought the instant suit against the insurance company (United), and Golden State Claims Adjusters (GSCA), a third party claims administrator, seeking the full amount of the judgment and attorneys fees.  The first six causes of action  in the First Amended Complaint are against United only, for 1) Judgment Creditor’s action against the Insurer; 2) Failure to Pay a Covered Claim; 3) Unreasonable Failure to Defend; 4) Bad Faith Failure to Investigate a Claim; 5) Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing; and 6) Violation of Bus. & Prof. Code Section 17200, et seq.  Plaintiff also stated claims against GSCA and United for Intentional and Negligent Misrepresentation (the Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action).

 

Defendant GSCA filed a demurrer to the First Amended Complaint.  Defendant United filed a demurrer and motion to strike.

 

II. [TENTATIVE] RULING

 

Defendant Golden State Claims Adjusters’ Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is Sustained with Leave to Amend.

 

Defendant United’s Demurrer is Sustained in Part without leave to amend, overruled in Part and Sustained with leave to amend in part.  The Motion to Strike the First Amended Complaint is granted in part.

 

III. DEFENDANT GSCA’s DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

            A. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

Defendant Golden State Claims Adjusters (GSCA) requests judicial notice of the cross-complaint filed by Johnson in Glendale Acceptance v. Johnson, Case No. BC624204.  The court grants the request for judicial notice. 

 

            B.  DISCUSSION

 

Defendant GSCA demurs to the 7th and 8th Causes of Action on the ground that they lack the requisite specificity for a fraud claim.  GSCA also generally demurs to the complaint on the grounds that defendant did not owe plaintiff a duty for inadequate claims handling as a  matter of law.

 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings, i.e., it raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, petition, answer, or cross-complaint).  (C.C.P. §§422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [citing text]; Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388 [citing text].) A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at pg. 994 [citing text].)

 

The elements of a cause of action sounding in fraud include (1) a false representation;

(2) the speaker's knowledge of the falsity of the representation; (3) an intent to defraud; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage. (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.) "Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant to understand fully the nature of the charge made." (Roberts v. Ball, Hunt, Hart, Brown & Baerwitz (1976) 57 Cal.App.3d 104, 109.) "This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which 'show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered."' (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) "The requirement of specificity in a fraud action against a corporation requires the plaintiff to allege the names of the persons who made the

allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written." (Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.)

 

The court agrees with defendant that the allegations of intentional and negligent misrepresentation are insufficient.  The FAC fails to allege that the representations were false, known to be false, that there was an intent to defraud or that plaintiff justifiably relied to her detriment on any statement, and how she was damaged by these alleged statements.  The opposition brief, rather than clarifying these issues, merely highlighted the inadequacy of the pleading and current theory of the case,

 

GSCA separately argues that plaintiff cannot allege a breach of duty claim against it because a third party claims adjuster owes no duty of care to a third party claimant.  In the instant case, plaintiff alleges that the claim was provided to GSCA and GSCA acknowledged its receipt of the claim; that GSCA somehow bungled the investigation and that “defendants” wrongfully failed to pay the claim and the attorneys fees, which they apparently deemed not covered by the policy.  The court agrees with defendant that California law clearly holds that third-party claims handlers are not liable for bad faith or breach of contract

for failing to pay claims or properly handle claims. (Gruenberg v. Aetna Ins. Co. (1973) 9 Cal.3d 566, 576; Cooper v. Equity General Ins. (1990) 219 Cal.App.3d 1252, 1258-1260.) There is ordinarily no contractual relationship between the injured party and the claims administrator.   Because any contractual relationship in the insurance context lies with the insurer (i.e., the entity that issued the insurance contract), a cause of action for bad faith can only be asserted against the insurer, not the third-party claims handler. (Ibid.) Moreover, adjusters hired by an insurer to investigate and administer a claim cannot be sued for negligence because third-party adjusters owe no duty of  care to a claimant or anyone other than the insurance company for whom they are working. (Sanchez v. Lindsey Morden Claims Services, Inc. (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 249, 256.)  With these principles in mind, the court cannot rule out that plaintiff might be able to allege a claim for intentional misrepresentation against GSCA, but it has not done so in the FAC. 

 

The court sustains the demurrer with leave to amend as to the Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action. 

 

IV.   DEFENDANT UNITED’s DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Defendant United demurs to the Third and Fourth Causes of Action on the grounds that such claims cannot be brought by a third party claimant; they are personal to the insured and can only be brought by the insured or their assignee.  Defendant is correct that only an insured may sue its insurer for bad faith, because such a claim is founded upon the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing inherent in  the contract of insurance, a contract that exists only between the insurer and the insured. (Austero v. Nat'l Cas. Co. (1976) 62 Cal.App.3d 511, 515.)  As a result, courts have consistently held that "an alleged breach of the covenant does not give rise to a cause of action by third party claimants" such as Plaintiff. (Coleman v. Gulf Ins. Group (1986) 41 Cal.3d 782, 795.) Instead, an "injured claimant may proceed directly against the insurer only to the extent that the insured party has assigned the claimant his cause of action . . . ." (Ibid; see also Murphy v. Allstate Ins. Co. (1976) 17 Cal.3d 937, 940-942). There is no assignment alleged here.  The court therefore sustains without leave to amend the demurrer to the Third and Fourth Causes of Action. 

 

Defendant also demurs to the First and Second Causes of Action on the ground that plaintiff cannot prevail on claims against it for failure to pay the judgment because the policy expressly exempted unlicensed contractors and Arina Builders was previously alleged by plaintiff to be an unlicensed contractor.  The court does not believe the issue of coverage can be determined at the demurrer stage and therefore overrules the demurrer to the First and Second Causes of Action.

 

As for defendant United’s demurrer to the Seventh and Eighth Causes of Action, the court agrees that plaintiff has not adequately alleged a false statement,  knowledge of its falsity, intent, detrimental reliance and damages.  The court sustains the demurrer to these two causes of action with leave to amend. 

 

V.  DEFENDANT UNITED’S MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Defendant United moves to strike all allegations regarding United’s purported bad

faith conduct that occurred prior to entry of the judgment in the underlying action. Defendant argues such allegations are irrelevant and improper because Plaintiff has no standing to pursue claims regarding alleged bad faith conduct that occurred prior to the entry of the judgment.  Such claims can only be brought by United’s insured, Arina Builders.   

 

Defendant also seeks to strike three paragraphs of allegations that United violated the Fair Claims Settlement Practices Act (“FCSPA”), on the ground there is no private right of action for such violations, and plaintiff lacks standing to pursue claims regarding any conduct that occurred prior to entry of the underlying judgment.

 

Finally, United seeks to strike four  paragraphs of allegations that United waived terms of the policy and is estopped from asserting coverage exclusions. Defendant argues that such allegations do not conform to California law, as “Plaintiff’s FAC does not plead, and cannot possibly plead, that USIC intentionally relinquished rights under the policy, nor is there basis from which Plaintiff could allege conduct by USIC intended to be relied upon by the insured or detrimental reliance by the insured.”

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 435, subdivision (b)(1), provides, “Any party, within the

time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof . . . .” “The Court may…. (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading . . . [or] (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code of Civ. Proc., § 436.) An “irrelevant matter” is (1) an allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense, (2) an allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense, or (3) a demand for judgment requesting relief not support by the allegations of the complaint. (Code of Civ. Poc., § 431.10, subd. (b).) “The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take

judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.)

As noted above, a third party claimant may not bring a claim for bad faith against an insurance company unless they have been assigned the right to do so by the insured, which is not alleged here.

 

An exception exists in situations where a third-party claimant has obtained a judgment

against an insured. When that happens, "[A]n action may be brought against the insurer on the policy and subject to its terms and limitations, by such judgment creditor to recover on the judgment." (Ins. Code, § 11580, subd. (b)(2).) Insurance Code section 11580 "makes the judgment creditor a third party beneficiary of the insurance contract between the insurer and the insured." (Murphy, supra, 17 Cal.3d at 943.) At that point, the third-party claimant as a third-party beneficiary has a direct right of action against the carrier. (Hand v. Farmers Ins. Exch. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 1847, 1858.) Accordingly, any allegations that relate to conduct by the insurer prior to judgment are improper and must be stricken.  Specifically, the court strikes paragraphs 21-30 from the FAC. 

 

With respect to the request to strike the allegations that United violated the FCSPA, the court agrees that those allegations are irrelevant as FCSPR does not create a private civil cause of action, and a third-party action cannot be premised on purported violations of the FCSPR. (Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Companies (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287, 304-305.) Accordingly, the court strikes the following allegations from the FAC:

 

40. DEFENDANTS failed to conduct a full, fair, prompt, and thorough investigation

of PLAINTIFF’s CLAIM. DEFENDANTS were required to accept or deny PLAINTIFF’s

CLAIM within forty (40) days of receiving the CLAIM in accordance with 10 Cal. Code.

Reg. §2695.7(b)(4). PLAINTIFF’s first sent notice of CLAIM to INSURER on February 25,

2020 and INSURER was required to accept or deny by April 5, 2020. PLAINTIFF’s last

notice of CLAIM to INSURER on December 7, 2020 and INSURER was required to accept

or deny by January 16, 2021.

 

65. PLAINTIFF claims that DEFENDANTS acted unreasonably, that is, without proper

cause, by failing to conduct a proper investigation of her CLAIM. DEFENDANTS are

obligated to comply with California’s Fair Claims Settlement Practices Regulations, 10 Cal.

Code. Reg. §2695.7(b) titled “Standards for Prompt, Fair and Equitable Settlements” which

requires that “[u]pon receiving proof of claim, every insurer, except as specified in

subsection 2695.7(b)(4), [not applicable to this POLICY] shall immediately, but in no event

more than forty (40) calendar days later, accept or deny the claim, in whole or in part. The

amounts accepted or denied shall be clearly documented in the claim file unless the claim has been denied in its entirety.”

 

66. DEFENDANTS failed to conduct a full, fair, prompt, and thorough investigation of

PLAINTIFF’s CLAIM. DEFENDANTS were required to accept or deny PLAINTIFF’s

CLAIM within forty (40) days of receiving the CLAIM in accordance with 10 Cal. Code.

Reg. §2695.7(b)(4). PLAINTIFF’s first sent notice of CLAIM to INSURER on February 25,

2020 and INSURER was required to accept or deny by April 5, 2020. PLAINTIFF’s last

notice of CLAIM to INSURER on December 7, 2020, and INSURER was required to accept

or deny by January 16, 2021.

 

Finally, defendant seeks to strike all of the allegations that defendant United has waived or should be estopped from denying coverage for the attorneys fees sought by plaintiff.  The court agrees with defendant that the case law does not allow such a claim to be brought by a third party for any conduct that occurred prior to entry of judgment.  As currently plead, the FAC appears to base its waiver and estoppel claims on conduct that occurred prior to the entry of judgment.   The court will sustain the demurrer on these grounds, but will grant leave to amend.  Any conduct that allegedly constitutes a waiver or release must have occurred after entry of judgment, and plaintiff must also plead all other elements to justify these two theories.   

 


Case Number: 23VECV01079    Hearing Date: October 12, 2023    Dept: W

ELLEN S. DURANT, et al. v. CONSTANCE ADLER GALLOWAY, et al.

 

defendant urban bilial’s demurrer to the complaint and defendant constance adler galloway’s motion to set aside default

 

Date of Hearing:        October 12, 2023                              Trial Date:       None set.

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        23VECV01079

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 10, 2023, Plaintiffs Ellen S. Durant and David E. Durant filed a complaint against Defendants Constance Adler Galloway, Hanibal Design Carlisle, and Urban Bilial Carlisle for breach of contract, intentional misrepresentation, and financial elder abuse. Plaintiffs allege Ellen, who is 89 years old, purchased a 3-bedroom condominium. David is Ellen’s son. Plaintiffs further allege Constance befriended Ellen and Ellen considered Constance like a daughter. Constance informed Ellen she could act as a property manager for the condominium. Plaintiffs allege in 2020, Constance signed a lease with Defendants Hanibal and Urban to rent one of the bedrooms in the condominium. In 2021, Plaintiffs decided to sell the property. Shortly thereafter, the friendship between Ellen and Constance deteriorated. Plaintiffs allege then Constance orchestrated a police action, where police disrupted Ellen’s convalescence and quiet enjoyment of the Property. Moreover, Constance accepted rent from Defendants Hanibal and Urban against Plaintiffs’ wishes. Plaintiffs wired $9,000 to Defendants Hanibal and Urban to have them vacate the property; however, Hanibal and Urban have failed to vacate the property.

 

Request for entry of default against Defendant Hanibal Design Carlisle was entered on August 31, 2023.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

I.                    Defendant Urban Bilial Carlisle’s Demurrer to the Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND

II.                  Defendant Constance Adler Galloway’s Motion to Set Aside Default is GRANTED

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                    DEFENDANT URBAN BILIAL CARLISLE’S DEMURRER TO THE COMPLAINT

 

Defendant Urban Bilial Carlisle demurs to Plaintiffs’ complaint on the grounds the first cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against Defendant Urban.

 

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings, i.e., it raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, petition, answer, or cross-complaint).  (C.C.P. §§422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [citing text]; Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388 [citing text].) 

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at pg. 994 [citing text].) 

 

Defendant Urban first demurs to the complaint on the grounds Plaintiffs fail to attach the contract or cite the provisions of the contract verbatim. The court is unpersuaded by Defendant’s argument that the terms of a written agreement must be set forth in verbatim in the complaint. Defendant cites Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394 to support his contention. However, the quoted portion by Defendant is in reference to Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452. Since Otworth, the California Supreme Court has held that “[i]n an action based on a written contract, a plaintiff may plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Insurance Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198-99.) Moreover, Miles held “plaintiff's failure either to attach or to set out verbatim the terms of the contract was not fatal to his breach of contract cause of action.” (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 402.) The court in Miles found the plaintiff alleged the basic elements of a breach of contract claim. Similarly here, Plaintiff has alleged Plaintiffs and Defendants Hanibal and Urban entered into a vacate agreement whereby Plaintiffs were to wire $9,000 and Defendants were to vacate; however, after Plaintiffs wired the money, Defendants failed to vacate the unit. The court does agree, however, the complaint fails to allege whether this is an oral or written agreement as required under 430.30(g).

 

Accordingly, Defendant Urban’s demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

II.                  DEFENDANT CONSTANCE ADLER GALLOWAY’S MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT

 

Defendant Constance Adler Galloway moves the court to set aside the default entered against Galloway on the grounds the default of Defendant Galloway was done in bad-faith and for the sole purpose of wasting judicial resources as counsel for Plaintiffs had actual notice that service of process was not valid at Defendant Galloway’s daughter’s residence as Defendant Galloway never resided there and sought default although in contact with Defense counsel.

 

Section 473(d) provides that “[t]he court may . . . on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(d).) A default judgment is void against a defendant who was not served with a summons in the manner prescribed by statute. (See Sakaguchi v. Sakaguchi (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 852, 858.) Relief pursuant to section 473(d) may be made at any time. (See Manson, Iver & York v. Black (2009) 176 Cal.App.4th 36, 42.) 

 

Defendant Galloway contends the service was improper because Defendant Galloway did not reside at the residence where substituted service occurs. Moreover, Defense counsel was in contact with Plaintiff’s counsel regarding the issues with service. Had the parties properly and openly communicated with each other this could have been avoided.  (See Smith v. Los Angeles Bookbinders Union No. 63 (1955) 133 Cal.App.2d 486, 500 [“The quiet speed of plaintiffs’ attorney in seeking a default judgment without the knowledge of defendants’ counsel is not to be commended.”] disapproved on other grounds by MacLeod v. Tribune Pub. Co. (1959) 52 Cal.3d 536.)

 

Despite receiving correspondence from Defense counsel inquiring about service, Plaintiff has not done anything regarding the request for default entered. As noted above, Defendant contends he has never resided at his daughter’s residence where substituted service occurs.

 

Accordingly, Defendant Galloway’s unopposed motion to set aside default is GRANTED.
