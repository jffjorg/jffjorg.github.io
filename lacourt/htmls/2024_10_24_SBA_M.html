Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka Thursday, October 24, 2024

Department M    Calendar No. 17  

 

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

Sylvia Garten v. Frank James Corbett, et al.  

19TRCV00625

1.      Sylvia Garten’s Motion for Order Compelling Answers to First Set of Judgment Debtor Interrogatories to Renee M. Smith and Request for Sanctions

2.      Sylvia Garten’s Motion for Order Compelling Answers to First Set of Judgment Debtor Interrogatories to Frank James Corbett and Request for Sanctions

3.      Sylvia Garten’s Motion for Order Compelling Answers to First Set of Judgment Debtor Requests for Production of Documents to Renee M. Smith and Request for Sanctions

4.      Sylvia Garten’s Motion for Order Compelling Answers to First Set of Judgment Debtor Requests for Production of Documents to Frank James Corbett and Request for Sanctions  

 

TENTATIVE RULING


            Sylvia Garten’s Motions to Compel Answers to First Set of Judgment Debtor Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents from Frank James Corbett and Renee M. Smith and Requests for Sanctions are granted.

 

            Background

 

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on July 23, 2019. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff is the owner of real property located at 209 Camino De Las Colinas, Redondo Beach, Los Angeles County, California, which is legally described as follows: 7514002020, 209 Camino De Las Colinas, Torrance, Tract #10300 Lot 32 Blk H. Defendants are the owners of other real property commonly known as 213 Camino, De Las Colinas, Redondo Beach, Los Angeles 5 County, California, which property is adjacent to Plaintiff’s property, and which is legally described as follows: 7514002019, 213 Camino De Las Colinas, Torrance, Tract#10300 Lot 31 Blk H. Defendants have engaged in improper, non-authorized, and non-permitted construction on their property which has caused a nuisance to Plaintiff. On August 25, 2023, the Court entered an amended judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendants.

 

            Motions to Compel

 

            CCP § 2030.290 states: “If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply…The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (CCP § 2030.290(b).) 

 

            CCP § 2031.300 states: “If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, the following rules shall apply: The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”  (CCP § 2031.300(b)).

 

               Pursuant to CCP § 708.020, a judgment creditor may propound written interrogatories to a judgment debtor.  Interrogatories are enforced in the same manner as interrogatories in a civil action. CCP § 708.020(c).  Thus, the provisions of CCP § 2030.290 apply equally to a judgment creditor’s interrogatories.

 

            Pursuant to CCP § 708.030, a judgment creditor may propound a demand for production and inspection of documents to a judgment debtor.  Demands for production of documents are enforced in the same manner as inspection demands in a civil action. CCP § 708.030(c).  Thus, the provisions of CCP § 2031.300 apply equally to a judgment creditor’s demand for production of documents.

 

            Judgment creditor/Plaintiff served upon judgment debtors/Defendants first set of judgment debtor interrogatories and judgment debtor requests for production of documents on July 10, 2024. (Decls., Michelle R. DeMason, ¶¶ 3-5.)  Plaintiff contends that no responses were served to the discovery requests.  (Id.) 

 

            Thus, the motions to compel responses to first set of judgment debtor interrogatories and judgment debtor requests for production of documents are granted.

 

            Judgment debtors are ordered to serve responses, without objections, within 10 days of this date.

 

            Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff’s requests for monetary sanctions are granted.

 

        Defendants and Defendants’ counsel are sanctioned the total amount of $2,240.00 which was calculated as follows: 5 hours to prepare and appear at $400 per hour, plus 4 filing fees at $60.00 each. Thus, Defendant Renee M. Smith and her counsel are sanctioned the total amount of $1,120 and Defendant Frank James Corbett and his counsel is sanctioned the total amount of $1,120.00.

 

            Sanctions are payable within 30 days of this date.

 

            Plaintiff/judgment creditor is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Epps & Coulson, LLP v. Stuart Lee Zubrick, et al.    

23TRCV01317

1.      Stuart Lee Zubrick’s Motion to Expunge Notice of Attorney’s Lien     

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING


Stuart Lee Zubrick’s Motion to Expunge Notice of Attorney’s Lien is denied.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on April 26, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Defendant owes Plaintiff for legal services rendered. On July 13, 2023, Defendant filed a Cross-Complaint. Cross-Complainant alleged the following causes of action based on Cross-Defendant’s representation of Cross-Complainant in the prior legal action: 1. Fraud; 2. Breach of Fiduciary Duty; 3. Breach of Contract; 4. Slander of Title; 5. Cancellation/Expungement of Lien and Quiet Title/Injunctive Relief.

 

Requests for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant and Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice are granted.

 

Motion to Expunge Lis Pendens

 

“At any time after notice of pendency of action has been recorded, any party, or any nonparty with an interest in the real property affected thereby, may apply to the court in which the action is pending to expunge the notice. . . Evidence or declarations may be filed with the motion to expunge the notice. The court may permit evidence to be received in the form of oral testimony, and may make any orders it deems just to provide for discovery by any party affected by a motion to expunge the notice. The claimant shall have the burden of proof under Sections 405.31 and 405.32.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 405.30.

 

“In proceedings under this chapter, the court shall order the notice expunged if the court finds that the pleading on which the notice is based does not contain a real property claim. The court shall not order an undertaking to be given as a condition of expunging the notice where the court finds the pleading does not contain a real property claim.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 405.31.  “In proceedings under this chapter, the court shall order that the notice be expunged if the court finds that the claimant has not established by a preponderance of the evidence the probable validity of the real property claim.”   Code Civ. Proc., § 405.32.

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 405.4 states: “‘Real property claim’ means the cause or causes of action in a pleading which would, if meritorious, affect (a) title to, or the right to possession of, specific real property or (b) the use of an easement identified in the pleading, other than an easement obtained pursuant to statute by any regulated public utility.” An action for money on a contract is not a real property claim even if it has some relation to real property. Ziello v. Superior Court (1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 321, 332.

 

“The court shall direct that the party prevailing on any motion under this chapter be awarded the reasonable attorney's fees and costs of making or opposing the motion unless the court finds that the other party acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of attorney's fees and costs unjust.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 405.38.

 

The Court has presented the legal authorities listed above only because Defendant’s motion is purportedly based on the authority set forth in CCP § 405.30 et seq. (Notice of Motion, page 1, line 27 to page 2, line 5.)  Defendant then proceeded to list two real properties at issue on page 2 of the notice of motion. However, the motion itself does not appear to be a motion to expunge a notice of pendency of action (lis pendens), but, instead, a purported motion to expunge an attorney’s lien. In fact, moving party does not even attempt to claim that a notice of pendency of action was filed by Plaintiff, but apparently (although the Court can only speculate) set forth the authorities stated above dealing with notices of pendency of action in the event that Plaintiff contends that the attorney’s lien is a lis pendens. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities, page 4, line 28.)  However, Plaintiff does not state that this is the case at all specifically stating: “Coulson didn’t file a lis pendens.” (Opposition, page 2, line 3.)

 

Thus, there is no proper procedural authority set forth that would act as a procedural mechanism to expunge an “attorney lien.” Instead, it appears that Defendant filed a motion utilizing inappropriate authority to attempt to obtain a speedy adjudication of this action without a proper adjudicative motion, such as a motion for summary judgment, or without the most well-known method to adjudicate cases on the merits – a trial. Defendant sets forth arguments why he believes that an attorney’s lien is not proper but, of course, Defendant will have the ability to fully present his defenses at the time that the action is adjudicated to conclusion.

 

The Court notes that Defendant primarily relied upon two cases to support his substantive argument that Plaintiff must file and prevail in a separate independent action: Valenta v. Regents of University of California (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 1465 and Hansen v. Jacobsen (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 350. In Valenta, the moving party proceeded on what the Court termed a “motion to terminate the liens.” The Court of Appeal did not provide any explanation as the specific procedural mechanism that would authorize such a motion. The instant Court is not aware of any such procedural mechanism and cannot proceed to rule on such a motion without a showing that there is a specific mechanism that would authorize a “termination of an attorney’s lien.” In Hansen, the party seeking an adjudicative ruling on the lien proceeded under a very familiar motion – a motion to strike.

 

In any event, the substantive arguments made by Defendant also appear to be misplaced. The arguments made by Defendant would perhaps have been appropriately made by Defendant in the underlying action in which the attorney lien was filed. However, the instant action itself is the independent and separate action that Defendant believes must be filed prior to enforcing the lien. Of course, the instant independent action, according to Plaintiff’s argument, must be adjudicated on the merits to its conclusion prior to a determination as to whether the attorney’s lien can be enforced. Presumably, this is exactly why Plaintiff filed the instant independent action.

 

Thus, for the foregoing reasons, the motion to expunge attorney’s lien is denied.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Jan Building, Inc. v. Padilla’s Company, Inc., et al.   

23TRCV02558

1.      Padilla’s Company, Inc., et al.’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

2.      Padilla’s Company, Inc., et al.’s Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint  

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING


Padilla’s Company, Inc.’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Padilla’s Company, Inc.’s Motion to Strike Portions of First Amended Complaint is deemed moot.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff’s Complaint was filed on August 7, 2023 and the First Amended Complaint was filed on May 28, 2024. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff is the general contractor for a construction project (“Project”) located at 1700 S. Pacific Coast Highway, Redondo Beach, California. Plaintiff entered into sub-contracts with Padilla’s Company, Inc. for rough grading and shoring, and, later, for concrete masonry. Defendant failed to adequately perform the contracts and failed to have the proper licensing to perform the contracts.   Plaintiff alleges the following causes of action: 1. Disgorgement (B&P 7031(b)); 2. Disgorgement (B&P 7031); 3. Fraud; 4. Negligence; 5. Unlawful, Fraudulent, and Unfair Business Practices (B&P 17200 et seq.); 6. Breach of Contract; 7. Breach of Contract.

 

            Meet and Confer

             

            Defendant filed a meet and confer declaration in sufficient compliance with both CCP § 430.41 and CCP § 435.5.  (Decl., David. A. Carnie.)

 

            Demurrer


A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law and raises only questions of law. (Schmidt v. Foundation Health (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 1702, 1706.)  In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  The Court may not consider contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law.  (Moore v. Conliffe (1994) 7 Cal.App.4th 634, 638.) Because a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint, the plaintiff must show that the complaint alleges facts sufficient to establish every element of each cause of action.  (Rakestraw v. California Physicians Service (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.)  Where the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, courts should sustain the demurrer.  (C.C.P., § 430.10(e); Zelig v. County of Los Angeles (2002) 27 Cal.App.4th 1112, 1126.)

Sufficient facts are the essential facts of the case "with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficiently specific to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source, and extent of his cause of action.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 643-644.) "Whether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer."  (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609–610.)  Under Code Civil Procedure § 430.10(f), a demurrer may also be sustained if a complaint is “uncertain.” Uncertainty exists where a complaint’s factual allegations are so confusing they do not sufficiently apprise a defendant of the issues it is being asked to meet.  (Williams v. Beechnut Nutrition Corp. (1986) 185 Cal.App.3d 135, 139, fn. 2.)

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for fraud for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and uncertainty. CCP § 430.10(e)(f).    

            Third Cause of Action for Fraud

            The demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend. Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to state a cause of action.

 

            “A complaint for fraud must allege the following elements: (1) a knowingly false representation by the defendant; (2) an intent to deceive or induce reliance; (3) justifiable reliance by the plaintiff; and (4) resulting damages.”  Service by Medallion, Inc. v. Clorox Co. (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1807, 1816. “[T]he elements of an action for fraud and deceit based on a concealment are: (1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he has known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage.” Boschma v. Home Loan Center, Inc. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 230, 248.

 

            “Every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in the proper manner and the facts constituting the fraud must be alleged with sufficient specificity to allow defendant to understand fully the nature of the charge made.”  Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157.  Plaintiff must state facts which “show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.

 

“[C]onduct amounting to a breach of contract becomes tortious only when it also violates a duty independent of the contract arising from principles of tort law. An omission to perform a contract obligation is never a tort, unless that omission is also an omission of a legal duty.” Erlich v. Menezes (1999) 21 Cal.4th 543, 551 (internal citations and quotations omitted.)

 

            Plaintiff has failed to allege specific facts as to the element of justifiable reliance. Plaintiff relies upon written representations made in the sub-contracts itself with respect to the licensure status of Defendant. However, as noted above, failure to comply with terms in the contract, which are specifically set forth with respect to the licensure status of Defendant, may support a cause of action for breach of contract but does not support a tort unless an independent duty arises separate and apart from the contract. That independent duty could be derived via a properly stated fraud cause of action. However, here, mere conclusions are set forth with respect to justifiable reliance.

 

For example, the alleged representations must have been made, according to Plaintiff’s theory, in writing, on October 5, 2020 and May 31, 2021 when the Vice President of Padilla signed the sub-contracts. However, it is unclear and not stated when the signatory for Plaintiff signed the sub-contracts. If the signatory signed prior to these dates, it appears unlikely that Plaintiff could have justifiably relied in any manner upon those representations prior to entering into the agreements. There are no specific facts to indicate that the signatory for Plaintiff actually reviewed and read the purported written representation made by Defendant and, in justifiable reliance upon the representation, then proceeded to execute the agreement. In addition, the Court notes that Plaintiff still has not stated specific facts as to where the representations were made.  

 

            Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is sustained with 20 days leave to amend.  

 

            Motion to Strike 

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  CCP § 436(a).  The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.  CCP § 436(b).  The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws.  CCP § 436.  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP § 437.

 

Defendant moves to strike the following paragraph of the Complaint: “Paragraph 48, 10:11-14 – “Defendants acts as alleged herein were malicious, fraudulent and/or oppressive and were undertaken in the conscious disregard of the rights of Plaintiff. Defendants’ actions entitle Plaintiff to an award of punitive damages in an amount appropriate to punish to make an example of Defendants, according to proof.”  (Notice of Motion, page 2, lines 3-6.)

 

The motion to strike is moot upon the sustaining of the demurrer to the third cause of action.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
