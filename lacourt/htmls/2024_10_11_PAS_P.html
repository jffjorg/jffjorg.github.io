DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00796    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET TWO

I.        INTRODUCTION

          On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff Elena Rubio ("Plaintiff") filed a lawsuit against Defendant American Honda Motor Company ("Defendant") asserting three causes of action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act: (1) breach of express warranty, (2) breach of implied warranty, and (3) violation of section 1793.2 of the Act. The claims concern a 2018 Honda Accord that Plaintiff purchased in October 2018, alleging the vehicle had serious defects which worsened over time. Despite multiple repair attempts, Defendant allegedly failed to conform the vehicle to its warranty obligations.

On March 6, 2024, Plaintiff propounded Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two. On April 5, 2024, Defendant responded with verified "boilerplate objections." On April 22, 2024, Defendant provided additional responses, reiterating the same objections. As a result, Plaintiff asserts that Defendant has failed to produce any of the requested responsive documents.

This motion is now before the Court, with Plaintiff seeking to compel further production of documents from Set Two, which are relevant to the claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“SBA”)

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          A party responding to requests for inspection must either provide a statement of compliance, represent that it lacks the ability to comply, or object. Code Civ. Proc. §2031.210.

          - If a party responds with a statement of compliance, the statement must indicate whether production will be allowed in whole or in part and that all documents in the responding party’s possession, custody or control to which no objection is being made will be included in the production. Code Civ. Proc. §2031.220.

          - If a party responds that a particular demand cannot be complied with it must include a representation that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry have been made and that the inability to comply is because the item has never existed, has been lost, stolen, or misplaced, or has never been in or is no longer in the responding party’s possession, custody, or control; such response must also identify the name and address of any person or entity known or believed to have possession, custody, or control of the item or category of item.  Code Civ. Proc. §2031.230.

          - If a response includes objections, a privilege log identifying documents being withheld must be provided. Code Civ. Proc. §2031.240.

          When a party propounding demands for inspection deems responses to the responses to be incomplete or evasive, or deems objections to be without merit, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses. Such motion must set forth facts showing good cause for the discovery, be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, and include a separate statement. Such motion must also be made within 45 days of verified responses or supplemental responses, or on or before any specific later date the parties have agreed to in writing. Code Civ. Proc. §2031.310(a)-(c).

          Code Civ. Proc. §2031.310 (h) provides for the imposition of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes such a motion unless the court finds that the one subject to sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.

 

III.     ANALYSIS

A.   Position of the Parties

Moving Party’s Position

Plaintiff brings this Motion pursuant to California Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2031.310, and 2031.320, on the grounds that Defendant´s objections are not Code-compliant, and that Defendant failed to provide adequate responses to Plaintiff’s RPDs, which seek documents relevant to the asserted SBA causes of action.

Responding Party’s Opposition

          Defendant argues that its objections are necessary based on the phrasing of the requests because Plaintiff includes qualifying phrases. asserting that the requests are overbroad and vague, particularly because they include qualifying terms such as "concerning," "referring," or "relating to." Defendant asserts that it provided responses to 39 of 55 requests, making its objections and responses code-compliant under Code of Civil Procedure. Defendant also contends the requests are irrelevant, as they seek information unrelated to the specific vehicle at issue, such as data on other vehicles and customer complaints. Furthermore, Defendant raises concerns over privacy and confidentiality, arguing that the requests improperly seek trade secrets and proprietary information. Lastly, Defendant claims that Plaintiff has failed to show good cause for producing additional documents.

 

B.    Meet and Confer

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating, “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿ 

Here, on April 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant’s counsel a letter in an attempt to meet and confer in good faith regarding Defendant’s responses to Plaintiff's Request for Production of Documents, Set Two. (Derderian Decl. ¶ 19; Ex. 5.) In this letter, Plaintiff’s counsel explained the relevance and importance of each request in Plaintiff's Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, and supported this position with statutory and case law to demonstrate that good cause exists to produce the requested documents. (Id.) On April 22, 2024, Defendant provided further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production, Set Two, following its initial objections-only responses. (Tahsildoost Decl. ¶ 10, Ex. F.)

On May 23, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel served a further meet and confer letter addressing Defendant’s discovery responses. Plaintiff’s counsel provided detailed explanations as to why the documents sought are both relevant and probative. Specifically, the requested documents are directly related to the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (“SBA”) claims, including those involving: (1) Defendant’s policies and procedures for handling Song-Beverly cases and the subject vehicle (Request Nos. 1-9); and (2) Defendant’s internal investigations of the defects exhibited in the subject vehicle, including electrical defects (Request Nos. 10-32) and suspension defects (Request Nos. 33-55). (Derderian Decl. ¶ 20.)

Therefore, the court notes the meet and confer requirement has been met.

 

C.    Legal analysis

Plaintiff’s Set Two contains 55 requests. Of these, Defendant objects to 16 requests. Defendant claims to have responded to 39 of Plaintiff’s requests by either agreeing to produce documents or stating that it was unable to comply after a diligent search and reasonable inquiry. (Opposition, p. 2.)

Defendant asserts that, for Request Nos. 6, 11, 12, 13, 18, 19, 25, 27, 30, 32, 34, 35, 36, 41, 42, 48, 50, 52, 53, and 55, it agreed to produce responsive documents in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.220.

As to Request Nos. 5, 7, 10, 14, 16, 22, 23, 24, 28, 33, 37, 39, 45, 46, 47, and 51, Defendant responded that it was unable to comply with each request because, after a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, no responsive documents were found, or the request was otherwise inapplicable to Defendant. This response is in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230.

Accordingly, the requests that remain in dispute are Request Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, and 54.

Defendant objects to Plaintiffs Request For Production of Documents, Set Two, Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, and 54. Defendant objected to each request on the bases of relevance, overbreadth, undue burden and trade secret privilege. 

Request for Production Nos.1-9; (which 1-4 and 8-9 are in dispute)

Plaintiff Requests Nos. 1-4, 8-9 seek documents relating to Defendant’s policies and procedures for handling SBA cases and the subject vehicle. Specifically, Plaintiff states that these requests seek documents regarding Defendant’s written warranties and its policies and procedures for addressing customer complaints, as well as whether Defendant fulfills its affirmative duties to repurchase defective vehicles under the SBA. Plaintiff asserts that these requests are crucial to Plaintiff’s claim for civil penalties, and to demonstrate that Defendant’s awareness of a prevalent defect that neither Defendant nor authorized repair facilities were able to repair. (Derderian Decl. ¶ 21; Motion, pp. 5-6.) Plaintiff further asserts that these requests focus on documents and information relating to the 2018 Honda Accord, VIN 1HGCV1F34JA244110, which is the subject of this lawsuit.

 

Requests for Production 10-55 (which 

Plaintiff’s Requests for Production Nos. 10-55 seek documents relating to Defendant’s internal investigations into the defects exhibited in the subject vehicle, specifically focusing on electrical defects. Plaintiff contends that these requests seek documents reflecting Defendant’s internal assessments and knowledge of the electrical issues and warranty records related to the subject vehicle and other vehicles experiencing similar defects. Plaintiff asserts that these requests define the defects based on the symptoms and repairs documented in the subject vehicle’s repair orders. Plaintiff argues that these documents are essential to establishing "willfulness" by demonstrating Defendant's knowledge of the alleged defects—either through evidence that the subject vehicle was not repaired after a reasonable number of attempts or through internal investigations and records showing that other consumers experienced similar problems. (Derderian Decl. ¶ 22; Ex. 6.)

As stated above, Defendant objects to Plaintiffs Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, Nos. 1, 2, 3, 4, 8, 9, 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, and 54. Defendant objected to each request at issue on the bases of relevance, overbreadth, undue burden and trade secret privilege. 

 

a.               Scope/Relevance

Discovery is relevant if it is admissible as evidence, or “appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.)¿¿ 

b.     Improper/Undue Burden

Plaintiffs further argue that Defendant’s objections to their first set of RFPs on the basis that they are unduly burdensome and oppressive are meritless without any additional showing of any such undue burden or oppression. The party objecting to a discovery request on this basis bears the burden of supplying evidence of “the quantum of work required.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 549.)¿¿ 

 

c.     Overbreadth

To the extent that the court considers this objection, the court notes that the standard for overbreadth is to be ambiguous to the point of being unintelligible, which is not met here. (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)¿ 

d.     Trade Secrets

“‘Trade secret’ means information, including a formula, pattern, compilation, program, device, method, technique, or process, that: (1) Derives independent economic value, actual or potential, from not being generally known to the public or to other persons who can obtain economic value from its disclosure or use; and (2) Is the subject of efforts that are reasonable under the circumstances to maintain its secrecy.” (Civ. Code § 3426.1, subd. (d).)¿ 

 

Nos.1 and 2

In response to most of the discovery requests at issue, Defendant did substantively respond. Here, for request No.1, Defendant asserts that “it was unable to comply with the request because, pursuant to a diligent search and reasonable inquiry, AHM had no responsive documents, and none had ever existed.” The Court finds this is compliant with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.230. 

 

As for No.2, Defendant “agreed to produce responsive documents pertaining to Plaintiff and/or the Subject Vehicle.”  To the extent that such documents have not yet been produced, the Court orders Defendant to produce them.

No. 3

Defendant objects to this request on the grounds that it is overbroad, vague, and ambiguous. However, to the extent the Court considers this objection, the Court notes that the standard for overbreadth requires ambiguity to the point of being unintelligible, which is not satisfied here. (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.) Accordingly, Defendant is ordered to provide a further response. 

 

No. 4

Defendant objects to this request on the grounds of irrelevance, vagueness, and ambiguity, specifically arguing that the term "recall policy and procedure" is not defined and is therefore vague and ambiguous, making the information sought indeterminable. (Opposition to Plaintiff’s Separate Statement.) However, to the extent the Court considers this objection, the Court notes that the standard for overbreadth requires ambiguity to the point of being unintelligible, which is not the case here. (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.) Additionally, the information sought is relevant, as Plaintiff is seeking documents related to Defendant’s policies and procedures for handling Song-Beverly Act cases, which is directly at issue in this action. Therefore, Defendant is ordered to provide a further response.

 

Nos 8-9

Request No. 8 seeks all documents used since 2011 to evaluate consumers’ requests for repurchases pursuant to the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. For this request, Defendant states that it voluntarily agreed to produce what it was not otherwise obligated to “for purposes of promoting the cooperative exchange of information between the parties.” (Opposition to Plaintiff SS, p. 9).  To the extent that such documents have not yet been produced, the Court orders Defendant to produce them.

Request No. 9 seeks information regarding Defendant’s "warranty buyback reduction goals." Defendant objected to this request, arguing that it is irrelevant to any element of Plaintiff’s case under the statutory framework of the Song-Beverly Act. Specifically, Defendant asserts that this information has no bearing on whether Plaintiff’s vehicle exhibited a nonconformity that substantially impaired its use, value, or safety, nor whether Defendant was unable to repair the vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts. Additionally, Defendant objects on the grounds that the request seeks information that violates the right to privacy or is confidential, commercially sensitive, proprietary, or a trade secret. Defendant further objects to the extent that this request seeks responses on behalf of any other entity. The Court agrees with Defendant that this request exceeds the scope of permissible discovery and may encroach upon confidential information. Therefore, Plaintiff’s Request No. 9 is DENIED.

 

Nos 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, 54

Defendant raises several arguments and groups them together for Requests for Production Nos. 10-55, including Nos. 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, and 54, which are at issue here. Defendant first argues that the requests are overbroad and vague, particularly due to the use of terms like "concerning," "referring," or "relating to" without sufficient particularity, which fail to describe the requested documents with the specificity required by the Code of Civil Procedure.

Next, Defendant contends that these requests are irrelevant because they seek information about other vehicles, customer complaints, or defects unrelated to Plaintiff’s vehicle. Defendant asserts that this information does not pertain to the specific claims under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, which is focused on whether Plaintiff’s vehicle exhibited a nonconformity that substantially impaired its use, value, or safety.

Defendant also raises concerns regarding privacy, particularly with Requests Nos. 15-55, which may involve the disclosure of trade secrets, proprietary information, and other confidential data. As a result, Defendant argues that these requests exceed the permissible scope of discovery and should be denied.

Here, the Court notes that the parties may stipulate to a protective order to safeguard against the disclosure of any claimed private, trade secret, or confidential proprietary information. To the extent that the Court considers Defendant's objection based on privacy and confidentiality, the Court observes that the standard for overbreadth requires ambiguity to the point of being unintelligible, which is not the case here. (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)

Moreover, the Court finds that Defendant has not sufficiently substantiated its claim of undue burden in producing the requested documents. The Court also agrees with Plaintiff that the requests for production relating to the disputed requests are essential to demonstrate "willfulness" specifically regarding whether the manufacturer had knowledge of the alleged defects. This knowledge could be evidenced by the fact that the subject vehicle was not repaired after a reasonable number of attempts or through internal investigations and warranty records showing that other consumers experienced similar issues.

Accordingly, the Court compels Defendant to provide further responses to Requests for Production Nos. 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, and 54.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Plaintiff’s motion to compel Further Responses to Request for Production, Set Two is GRANTED IN PART as to Request for Production Nos. 2, 3, 4, 8, 15, 17, 20, 21, 26, 29, 31, 38, 40, 43, 44, 49, and 54.

Defendant is ordered to provide further code complaint responses within 20 days of this order.

Case Number: 23AHCV01038    Hearing Date: October 11, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANTS' DEMURRER TO SECOND AMENDED COMPLAINT

I.         INTRODUCTION

In this medical malpractice action, Plaintiff Laurie Moore ("Plaintiff") alleges that on May 6, 2022, Defendants Luisana Gomez, Ramachandran Srinivasan, Howard Sofen, and Complete Care Community Health Center provided her with medical care. Plaintiff claims that the medical care she received from Defendants was deficient, leading to significant harm and injury. Specifically, Plaintiff asserts that Defendants negligently prescribed Plaquenil for her psoriatic arthritis, which resulted in serious adverse health conditions. On November 15, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed Howard Sofen from the case. 

On May 31, 2024, the Court issued an order sustaining Defendants Luisana Gomez, Ramachandran Srinivasan and Complete Care Community Health Center (collectively “Defendants”) demurrer as to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). In response, on June 21, 2024, Plaintiff filled her Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) asserting 1) Medical Negligence and 2) Statutory Violations.

            On July 24, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer as to the SAC, arguing that the second cause of action for statutory violations fails to state a cognizable cause of action. Plaintiff filed an Opposition on September 26, 2024, and Defendant filed a Reply on October 3, 2024. 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

In this instance, the Court finds that Defendants sufficiently complied with the meet-and-confer requirements. The Declaration of Jacob S. Rosenberg is offered in support of counsel's compliance with Code of Civil Procedure Section 430.41, which requires parties to meet and confer before filing a demurrer. Rosenberg declares that he met and conferred with Plaintiff’s counsel on July 17, 2023, regarding the issues raised in Defendants’ demurrer. Plaintiff’s counsel responded the same day, disputing the grounds for the demurrer. (Rosenberg Decl. ¶ 5).

Accordingly, the Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been satisfied.

MOVING PARTY’S ARGUMENT

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s second cause of action for Statutory Violations under the California Physician Assistant Practice Act fails because the Act does not provide a private right of action, and any alleged violations should be incorporated into the medical negligence claim. Defendants also argue that the Second Cause of Action is uncertain.

Defendants do not demur as to the first cause of action for Medical Negligence.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

III.      ANALYSIS

A.    General Demurrer – Failure to State Facts Sufficient to Constitute a Cause of Action

Defendants demur to the second cause of action for statutory violations, arguing that Plaintiff has failed to demonstrate a private right of action under the California Physician Assistant Practice Act. (Demurrer, pp. 12-17).  Plaintiff, however, seeks to hold Defendants liable for violations of this Act, as set forth in Division 2, Chapter 7.7 of the Business & Professions Code (§ 3500, et seq.), as well as the California Physician Assistant Regulations under Title 16, Division 13.8 of the California Code of Regulations (§ 1399.500, et seq.) (SAC, p. 7). Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants violated several provisions, including California Code of Regulations, Title 16, §§ 1399.540, 1399.541, 1399.545, 1399.546, and 1399.547 (SAC, p. 8).

The relevant statutes and regulations govern specific duties on a physician assistant and his or her supervising physician. The legislative intent of the Act is to “encourage the effective utilization of the skills of physicians and surgeons, and physicians and surgeons and podiatrists practicing in the same medical group practice, by enabling them to work with qualified physician assistants to provide quality care.” (Business & Professions Code § 3500).

In the SAC, Plaintiff cites several provisions of the California Code of Regulations. Specifically, Title 16, §§ 1399.540, 1399.541, 1399.545, 1399.546, and 1399.547 (SAC, p. 8). § 1399.540(c) requires physician assistants to consult with or refer patients to a licensed physician when a task exceeds their competence. Similarly, § 1399.541(e) imposes duties on physician assistants to instruct and counsel patients on matters related to their physical and mental health. §1399.545 mandates that supervising physicians must be available and maintain responsibility for the medical services provided by the physician assistant. §1399.546 requires physician assistants to document the supervising physician for each episode of patient care, while § 1399.547 requires notification to patients that the physician assistant is licensed and regulated by the board.

Here, Plaintiff pleads that Defendants violated these regulations by failing to properly supervise the care provided by Defendant Gomez, a physician assistant. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant Dr. Srinivasan failed to evaluate Gomez’s competency and skill (SAC, ¶33a, p. 8), This allegation directly violates §1399.546. Furthermore, Plaintiff asserts that both Defendants Dr. Srinivasan and Health Center failed to adequately supervise Gomez’s care of Plaintiff between March 8, 2022, and approximately April 12, 2023, which would violate § 1399.540(c). These failures allegedly misled Plaintiff into believing that she was receiving care from a licensed medical doctor, constituting a violation of § 1399.547. Additionally, Plaintiff contends that Defendants did not provide proper notification that Gomez was a licensed physician assistant, and that Gomez improperly prescribed medication in violation of § 1399.541 (SAC, pp. 7-9). Given these allegations, The Court finds that Plaintiff has provided sufficient factual allegations sufficient to support a finding that the facts are plead with particularly.

Additionally, Defendants cite Lopez v. Ledesma (2022) 12 Cal.5th 848, a Supreme Court of California case concerning the scope of a physician assistant's practice under MICRA's cap on non-economic damages. However, this case is not controlling here, as the Plaintiff is not asserting MICRA applicability but rather claims under the California Physician Assistant Practice Act. Defendants argue that the Act is improperly used here as a means to circumvent non-economic damage limits, citing that it was not intended to be a separate cause of action (Demurrer, p. 9).

This argument is unpersuasive. The Plaintiff is not precluded from asserting a cause of action based on alleged violations of the Physician Assistant Practice Act. For purposes of ruling on the demurrer, the Court must determine whether the Plaintiff’s SAC sufficiently alleges that Defendants violated the Act.

Defendants argue that there is no private right of action under the Act, and any statutory violations should be incorporated into the medical negligence claim. (Reply, p.5) However, Plaintiff argues that Defendants have cited no case law that specifically prohibits Plaintiff from pleading statutory violations of the Act as a separate cause of action. (Opposition, p.18)

After conducting its own research, the Court has not found any appellate court decisions directly addressing this issue or finding that such a cause of action cannot proceed. Thus, the Court intends to allow Plaintiff to allege a cause of action for statutory violation under the doctrine of negligence per se.

The Court finds that while negligence per se is not a separate cause of action (see Johnson v. Honeywell International, Inc. (2009) 179 Cal.App.4th 549, 555; Millard v. Biosources, Inc. (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1353 n.2.), it is a separate theory of negligence upon which recovery may be based. (See e.g., Randi W. v. Muroc Joint Unified School Dist. (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1066, 1086-87.)  Therefore, the Court recognizes that negligence per se may serve as a basis for Plaintiff’s recovery.

Given that such a claim is not expressly prohibited, the Court will permit Plaintiff to proceed on this alternate negligence per se theory.

Accordingly, the Court finds that the SAC pleads sufficient facts to proceed on this cause of action and the demurrer is overruled.

IV.       ORDER

            The Court OVERRULES the demurrer as to second cause of action.

            The Court takes notice that the parties have agreed to withdraw the attorney fee’s allegation from the complaint.

            Defendant is to give notice and provide an answer within 10 days of this hearing.
