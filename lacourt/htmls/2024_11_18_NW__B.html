DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV22415    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: B

TENTATIVE RULING

MATTHEW LAREZ, et al, Plaintiffs, vs. KAYRY GONZALEZ, Defendant. Case No.: 21STCV22415 Hearing Date: November 18, 2024

BACKGROUND

On May 7, 2024, Defendant Kayry Gonzalez (“Defendant”) initially noticed the deposition of Plaintiff’s non-retained expert witness Jon Greenfield, M.D. (“Dr. Greenfield”) for June 5, 2024. (Muench Decl. ¶ 9; Exh. B [notice of deposition].) Dr. Greenfield was served with the deposition subpoena on May 20, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 10; Exh. C [subpoena].) The deposition did not proceed as scheduled. (Ibid. at ¶ 11.) On August 7, 2024, Defendant attempted to reschedule Dr. Greenfield’s deposition. That day, Dr. Greenfield’s administrator indicated that Dr. Greenfield was “unable to participate” and had no return date as he suffered from medical issues. (Ibid. at ¶ 12; Exh. D [August 7, 2024, email correspondences].) Defendant now moves the Court to compel Dr. Greenfield’s deposition.

LEGAL STANDARD

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not

privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)

CCP § 2025.450 provides in pertinent part the following:

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040 or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.

CCP § 1987.1(a) further states, “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.” (emphasis added). “[U]pon motion reasonably made by the party, judges may rule upon motions for quashing, modifying or compelling

compliance with, subpoenas.” (Lee v. Swansboro Country Property Owners Ass'n (2007) 151 Cal.App.4th 575, 582-583.)

DISCUSSION

While the Court is sympathetic to Dr. Greenfield’s health issues, Defendant would be prejudiced without his deposition. This case arises from a vehicle collision which Plaintiff claims caused continuing neck pain, back pain, shoulder pain, and other physical ailments which Dr. Greenfield has treated. (Muench Decl. ¶¶ 2-4.) Dr. Greenfield likely made relevant findings in his reports and examinations with Plaintiff. (Ibid. at ¶ 4.) Dr. Greenfield was served with a valid subpoena on May 20, 2024. (Muench Decl. ¶ 10; Exh. C [subpoena].) Neither Plaintiff nor Dr. Greenfield raised objections to this subpoena.

Case Number: 21STCV27586    Hearing Date: November 18, 2024    Dept: B

CARLOS ROLANDO RODRIGUEZ, Plaintiff, vs. TYLER BRUCE MASON, Defendant. ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 21STCV27586 [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: CROSS-DEFENDANT JEFF LEAL’S MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

I. BACKGROUND

On July 27, 2021, Plaintiff Carlos Rolando Rodriguez (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Tyler Bruce Mason (“Mason”) and Does 1 to 50 alleging three causes of action: (1) negligence; (2) negligence per se; and (3) statutory liability. Plaintiff alleges that, on or about August 2, 2019, Mason negligently operated his vehicle, colliding with Plaintiff’s vehicle and causing injury thereof.

On January 24, 2023, Mason filed a cross-complaint for indemnity, declaratory relief, and apportionment of fault against Cross-Defendant Jeff Leal (“Cross-Defendant”).

On June 5, 2024, the action was reassigned to the Honorable Karen Moskowitz in Department B at Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On October 18, 2024, Cross-Defendant Leal filed the instant motion for terminating sanctions.

The motion remains unopposed.

II. LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 permits the Court, in its discretion, to impose the following types of terminating sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process”:

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.

(CCP § 2023.030(d).) Conduct that is a “misuse of the discovery process” includes failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (CCP § 2023.010(d) and (g).)

“Imposition of sanctions for misuse of discovery lies within the trial court's discretion,

and is reviewed only for abuse.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 991.) “The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery[.]” (Ibid. at 992.) Sanctions should be tailored to the harm created by the withheld discovery and should not be used as punishment. (Ibid.)

The court should also consider whether the discovery misconduct was “willful” and whether the requesting party was prejudiced by the other’s failure to comply. (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 37.)

Lastly, courts have held that terminating sanctions are warranted when a party repeatedly fails to propound adequate discovery responses, even after the court imposed monetary sanctions. (Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrations, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093.) For example, in Mileikowsky v. Tenet Healthsystem (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 262, the Court of Appeal held that five motions to compel created a record “replete with evidence of [appellant’s] failures to answer discovery requests.” (Ibid. at 279–280.)

III. DISCUSSION

Cross-Defendant makes this motion on the grounds that Plaintiff failed to appear for his Court-ordered deposition on September 23, 2024, and because Plaintiff has “altogether abandoned the litigation.” (Motion p. 4:11.) Due to a significant breakdown in the attorney-client relationship, Plaintiff’s former counsel was relieved as counsel for Plaintiff in March 2024. (Englet Decl. ¶ 3.) On or about March 8, 2024, Cross-Defendant properly noticed Plaintiff’s deposition for March 26, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 4.) Plaintiff did not object but still failed to appear. (Ibid.) On August 22, 2024, the Court granted Cross-Defendant's motion to compel Plaintiff’s deposition and ordered Plaintiff to appear for his deposition within 30 days. (Ibid; Exh. A

[August 22, 2024 Minute Order].) On September 11, 2024, Cross-Defendant noticed Plaintiff’s deposition for September 23, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 5.) Again, Plaintiff failed to appear, this time in violation of Court order. (Ibid.)

“A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly. But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.) Here, Plaintiff did not comply with the Court’s August 22, 2024, order. Additionally, Plaintiff refuses to participate in this litigation, indicating that less severe sanctions would fail to produce Plaintiff’s compliance. Therefore, the Court finds terminating sanctions appropriate.

IV. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, Cross-Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s operative complaint is dismissed without prejudice.

Moving party is ordered to give notice to Plaintiff Carlos Rolando Rodriguez and Defendant Tyler Bruce Mason.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiff Ernie Irawati Soebroto (“Plaintiff”), individually and as successor in interest to decedents Andrea Bullo and Marco Bullo (“Decedents”), alleges that, on November 22, 2022, Decedents died as a result of Defendants Ford Motor Company (“Ford”) and Kevin Ivan Gonzalez (“Gonzalez”)(collectively “Defendants”)’s negligence.

On September 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Ford, Gonzalez, and Does 1 to 100, alleging three causes of action: (1) strict product liability; (2) negligence; and (3) breach of express warranty.

On October 17, 2024, Clyde T. Turner, Esq. (“Counsel”) filed the instant unopposed application to be appear pro hac vice on behalf of Plaintiff.

[TENTATIVE] RULING

Clyde T. Turner’s Application to Appear Pro Hac Vice is GRANTED.

LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Rules of Court Rule 9.40 provides that an attorney in good standing in another jurisdiction may apply to appear pro hac vice in this State by way of written application upon due notice to all interested parties, as well as service on the State Bar in San Francisco with payment of a $50.00 fee, so long as that attorney is not a resident of California, does not work in California, and does not perform regular or substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State. (CRC Rule 9.40.)

The written application must provide the following information: (1) applicant attorney’s residence and office addresses; (2) the courts to which the applicant attorney has been admitted and dates of admission; (3) representation that the attorney applicant is a member in good standing in the courts of admission and is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (4) the title of each court and action in which the applicant

attorney has appeared pro hac vice in this State in the preceding two years, if any; and (5) the name, address, and telephone number of the active California State Bar member who is counsel of record in the local action. (CRC 9.40(d).)

ANALYSIS

Counsel seeks to appear on behalf of Plaintiff pro hace vice in this action. Based on the application and the attached declaration: (1) Counsel is not employed or residing within California (Turner Decl. ¶¶ 1 and 2; 7); (2) Counsel has not applied to any other California courts to appear pro hace vice within the past two years (Ibid. at ¶ 8); (3) the application has the proper admission information for other courts where the attorney is admitted (Ibid. at ¶¶ 3 and 4); (4) the attorney is in good standing (Ibid. at ¶ 6); and (5) the applicant is to be associated with local counsel, Mark S. Algorri (Ibid. at ¶ 9). Additionally, notice of hearing upon this application was served upon the State Bar of California, along with the required $50.00 fee. (Algorri Decl. ¶ 5.)

Accordingly, the application abides by CRC Rule 9.40 and is granted.

CONCLUSION

Counsel’s application to appear on behalf of Plaintiff pro hace vice is GRANTED.
