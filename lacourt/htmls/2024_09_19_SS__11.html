DEPARTMENT 11 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV11136    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: 11

23STCV11136 (Doe)

 

Tentative Ruling Re: Demurrer and Motion to Strike Re: First Amended Complaint

 

Date:                           9/19/24

Time:                          10:30 am

Moving Party:           Adobe, Inc. (“Defendant” or “Adobe”)

Opposing Party:        Jane Doe (“Plaintiff”)

________________________________________________________________________

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Demurrer

 

First cause of action (California Invasion of Privacy Act (“CIPA”)) – overruled.

 

Fifth cause of action (Unfair Competition Law (“UCL”) – sustained with leave to amend as to standing and overruled as to the unfair prong, restitution, and disgorgement.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Third cause of action (violation of the right of privacy – California Constitution) – granted as restitution and disgorgement.

 

Fourth cause of action (invasion of privacy/intrusion upon seclusion) – denied as to restitution and disgorgement.

 

Punitive damages – granted.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 18, 2024, the Court heard Adobe’s demurrer and motion to strike.  The Court sustained the demurrer in part with leave to amend and overruled it in part and granted the motion to strike with leave to amend.

 

On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed her amended complaint, which alleges:

 

1. Patients rightfully expect that their healthcare concerns and choices will be private. And the law protects the privacy of healthcare information. This case is about a serious violation of that core privacy interest.

 

2. Plaintiff, like millions of other class members, obtained healthcare from Kaiser Permanente (“Kaiser”). As for any medical provider, state and common law protect the privacy of the healthcare information held by Kaiser and exchanged between Plaintiff and Class Members, on the one hand, and Kaiser, on the other.

 

3. One place that legal protection is important is the Kaiser Website, which Kaiser Members use to access their medical records, including prescriptions and immunizations, research their medical conditions, find and communicate with doctors, and undertake other interactions related to the provision of healthcare services.

 

4. Unfortunately and unlawfully, Defendant . . . repeatedly and systematically violated that legally-protected privacy interest by extracting private health and personally identifiable information from Kaiser Members’ communications with the Kaiser Website.

 

5. Through Defendant’s code embedded on the Kaiser Website, Defendant has vacuumed up information about Kaiser Members’ medical conditions, immunizations, prescriptions, physician information, and other private data, including healthcare search terms, videos watched, and links accessed. All of that information is linked to particular patients because Defendant takes that data together with unique identifiers that allow Defendant to identify the corresponding Kaiser Member.

 

6. Plaintiff and Class Members did not consent to Defendant’s taking of this highly sensitive and legally-protected medical and other information. Defendant’s conduct is unlawful, and it must be stopped.

 

(First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), ¶¶ 1-6.)

 

Now, the Court considers Adobe’s new demurrer and new motion to strike.

 

LAW

 

Demurrer

 

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context, and “treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.)  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  It is error “to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if the plaintiff shows there is a reasonable possibility any defect identified by the defendant can be cured by amendment.”  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

 

Motion to Strike

 

“Motions to strike can be used to reach defects in or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer.”  (Edmon & Karnow, Cal. Practice Guide: Civ. Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group June 2023 Update) ¶ 7:156.)  “Complaints, cross-complaints, answers and demurrers are all subject to a motion to strike [citation].”  (Ibid.)  “Moreover, a motion to strike can be used to attack the entire pleading, or any part thereof – i.e., even single words or phrases (unlike demurrers).”  (Ibid.)

 

The motion can be used to strike “any ‘irrelevant, false or improper matter inserted in any pleading’” or “any pleading or part thereof ‘not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.’”  (Id. at ¶ 7:167, emphasis in original.) 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Incorporation

 

The Court incorporates the March 18, 2024 ruling on Defendant’s first demurrer and motion to strike.

 

Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to the first (CIPA section 631) and fifth (UCL) causes of action.

 

CIPA Section 631

 

“Section 631 has been interpreted by California courts as containing three different clauses which cover ‘three distinct and mutually independent patterns of conduct.’”  (Valenzuela v. Nationwide Mutual Ins. Co. (C.D. Cal. Aug. 14, 2023) No. 2:22-cv-06177-MEMF-SK, 2023 WL 5266033, at *3.)  “The three patterns of conduct that Section 631 prohibits are: (1) ‘intentional wiretapping;’ (2) ‘attempting to learn the contents or meaning of a communication in transit over a wire;’ and (3) ‘attempting to use or communicate information obtained as a result of engaging in either of the previous two activities.’”  (Ibid.)  “In addition to these three clauses, Section 631 contains an aiding provision which imposes liability on anyone who ‘aids, agrees with, employs, or conspires with any person or persons’ in violating the three clauses described above.”  (Ibid.)

 

Last time, the Court recognized that case law is split as to the meaning of clause (2).  Defendant asked the Court to follow a line of cases led by Graham v. Noom (N.D. Cal. 2021) 533 F.Supp.3d 823, and Plaintiff asked the Court to follow a line of cases led by Javier v. Assurance IZ, LLC (N.D. Cal. 2023) 649 F.Supp.3d 891.  The Court analyzed the statutory language and found Javier “more compatible with the plain language.”  (3/18/24 Ruling Re: Demurrer and Motion to Strike, p. 6.)

 

“Nevertheless,” the Court sustained the demurrer with leave to amend because “Plaintiff fail[ed] to highlight a paragraph . . . that allege[d] that Defendant had the capability to use Plaintiff’s personal information for another purpose.”  (Ibid.)

 

The Court turns to the FAC.  The FAC alleges:

 

. . . through its [software development kit (“SDK”)] embedded on the Kaiser Website, and while Plaintiff and Class Members are communicating with Kaiser through the Kaiser Website, Adobe unlawfully intercepts, collects, uses, and discloses to third parties Plaintiff and Class Members’ Private Data, including PHI and PII, and transmits this Private Data to Adobe’s servers. Adobe subsequently uses this Private Data on its servers to create and/or modify profiles of Kaiser Members that Kaiser uses in exchange for fees paid to Adobe. Given Adobe’s total control of the Private Data resting on its own servers—and its ability to analyze, process, and repackage Kaiser users’ data—Adobe is plainly capable of transmitting this data to third parties other than Kaiser for Adobe’s own purposes.

 

(FAC, ¶ 32, emphasis added.)  The emphasized wording clearly states that Defendant has the capability to use Plaintiff’s data for its own purposes.

 

Defendant makes two arguments.  One, Defendant reasserts that the Court should follow Graham instead of Javier.  Two, Defendant contends the emphasized wording is speculative and conclusory.  (See Demurrer, pp. 4-5.)

 

The first argument is unavailing.  The analysis supporting the Court’s decision to follow Javier stands (Defendant offers nothing new to change the Court’s mind).

 

The Court also rejects the second argument.  In addition to paragraph 32, the FAC states:

 

46. The Adobe Trackers also collect and send to Adobe identifying information about a user’s web browser, which they collectively term “User-Agent” (“User Agent Data”).

 

47. Kaiser Website users’ unique Demdex IDs are tied to the user’s activity across different websites that use the Adobe Experience Cloud suite of products. This permits Adobe to track Kaiser users’ browsing activity across unrelated websites. More importantly, as part of the services Adobe provides to its clients through the Adobe Experience Cloud, Adobe incorporates the personal data it obtains from Kaiser Website users across these websites—including the Kaiser Website—to serve its customers using a comprehensive profile of the Kaiser user (which profile includes the user’s Private Data) to enrich the customers’ marketing analytics.

 

48. Further, when a user visits the Kaiser Website, the Adobe SDK downloads a script onto the user’s browser which, when executed, sends the Kaiser Website users’ unique Demdex ID to a server operated by Everest Technologies. Once Everest Technologies receives the users’ Demdex ID, it sets an Everest Technologies cookie on the user’s browser.

 

49. Adobe uses Everest Technologies to perform real-time advertising bidding to enhance advertising display campaigns for its Adobe Experience Cloud customers. Through use of Kaiser Website users’ unique Demdex ID that is sent to Everest Technologies when a user visits the Kaiser Website, Adobe uses the personal data that Everest Technology collects from the Kaiser Website users’ activity on the Kaiser Website to provide advertising services for its Adobe Experience Cloud customers. Through this process, Adobe Private Data from Kaiser Website users is disclosed to third parties.

 

50. Additionally, the fact that Everest Technologies—which is designed to promote third party advertising campaigns—obtains Kaiser Website users’ unique Demdex ID when they visit the Kaiser website means that Adobe has the capability to use the Kaiser users’ data for its own purposes, including but not limited to enhancing advertising display campaigns for its third party customers.

 

51. Further, a Quantum Metric SDK installed on the Kaiser Website intercepts the same type of data as the Adobe Trackers do (as further detailed below). The Adobe Trackers collect a Kaiser Website user’s KPAAMID and GUID identifiers. The values of those identifiers are the same as those collected by Quantum Metric, respectively. Each of the KPAAMID and GUID is a numeric string. Per Adobe, the Adobe Experience platform contains a “Quantum Metric extension” that “facilitates codeless deployment of Quantum Metric’s data collection capabilities,” and “offers the ability to capture data elements containing useful information from the Quantum Metric API.” Pursuant to the integration between Adobe and Quantum Metric (a third party), Adobe Analytics transmits the browsing data it collects to Quantum Metric so the Quantum Metric platform can, in turn, use this data to enrich the analytics it provides to its customers. Given that Adobe collects Kaiser Website users’ KPAAMID and GUID identifiers, and these are the same identifiers collected by Quantum Metric, Adobe transmits Kaiser Website users’ browsing data to Quantum Metric pursuant to the advertised integration between the two companies. Obviously, the fact that Adobe Analytics can transmit browsing data to Quantum Metric means that Adobe has the capability to transmit browsing data from Kaiser Website users to Quantum Metric.

 

(FAC, ¶¶ 46-51, emphasis in original, footnote omitted.)  These allegations must be accepted as true at the demurrer stage.  Read together, they suffice to allege capability and to satisfy the Javier standard.[1]

 

Defendant claims the allegations only show a capability to collect information for Kaiser’s benefit, which is inadequate.  (See, e.g., Reply, pp. 2, 4 [quoting Yockey v. Salesforce, Inc. (N.D. Cal. 2023) 688 F.Supp.3d 962, 973, contending Plaintiff fails to state a claim because the allegations “do not support a reasonable inference that [Defendant] has the capability to use these communications for any purpose other than furnishing them to Kaiser”].)

 

The Court disagrees.  Paragraph 47 alleges that, “as part of the services Adobe provides to its clients through the Adobe Experience Cloud, Adobe incorporates the personal data it obtains from Kaiser Website users across these websites—including the Kaiser Website—to serve its customers using a comprehensive profile of the Kaiser user (which profile includes the user’s Private Data) to enrich the customers’ marketing analytics.”  (FAC, ¶ 47, emphasis added.)  The allegation conveys that Defendant is capable of providing – and does provide – information about Kaiser uses to Adobe’s other clients and customers.

 

The demurrer is overruled.

 

UCL

 

“[T]he UCL permits a cause of action to be brought if a practice violates some other law.  In effect, the ‘unlawful’ prong of § 17200 makes a violation of the underlying law a per se violation of § 17200.”  (Stern, supra, at ¶ 3:53.)  “Virtually any law or regulation — federal or state, statutory or common law — can serve as predicate for a § 17200 ‘unlawful’ violation.  Thus, if a ‘business practice’ violates any law — literally — it also violates § 17200 and may be redressed under that section. [Citation.] As the California Supreme Court has said, § 17200 ‘borrows’ violations of other laws and treats them as unlawful practices independently actionable under § 17200.”  (Id. at ¶ 3:56.)

 

“The second ‘wrong’ proscribed by § 17200 is ‘unfair’ business practices.  Because § 17200's definition of the five proscribed ‘wrongs’ is set forth in the disjunctive, a business practice can be ‘unfair’ — and violative of § 17200 — even if it is not ‘deceptive’ and even if it is ‘lawful.’”  (Id. at ¶ 3:112.)  “The ‘unfair’ standard is intentionally broad, allowing courts maximum discretion to prohibit new schemes to defraud.”  (Id. at ¶ 3:113.)

 

“The third type of conduct proscribed by § 17200 is ‘fraudulent’ business practices.”  (Id. at ¶ 3:153.)  “A business practice is ‘fraudulent’ within the meaning of § 17200 if ‘members of the public are likely to be deceived.’”  (Id. at ¶ 3:154.)  “An advertisement's potentially deceptive effect is measured by the audience to which it is addressed.  Under the UCL and False Advertising statute, this will usually be the ‘reasonable person’ standard.”  (Ibid.)

 

Proposition 64 limits “private standing . . . to any ‘person who has suffered injury in fact and has lost money or property’ as a result of unfair competition [citations].”  (Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310 320-321.)  “The intent of this change was to confine standing to those actually injured by a defendant's business practices and to curtail the prior practice of filing suits on behalf of clients who have not used the defendant's product or service, viewed the defendant's advertising, or had any other business dealing with the defendant. . . .”  (Id. at 321, internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

Standing

 

The Court sustained Defendant’s first demurrer with leave to amend to give Plaintiff a chance to allege standing under Moore v. Centrelake Medical Group, Inc. (2002) 83 Cal.App.5th 515.  (See 3/18/24 Ruling Re: Demurrer and Motion to Strike, pp. 9-10.)

 

Plaintiff contends the FAC alleges two standing theories.  The first is a lost-value theory (see Opposition, p. 9), and the second is a benefit-of-the-bargain theory.  (See id. at p. 10.)

 

For lost value, Plaintiff cites paragraphs 77 through 90, 104, and 185.  (See Opposition, p. 9.)  She claims the allegations show “the existence of a market for her data[,]” “that her data is property,” and that “the value of” her data” “has been diminished by Adobe’s conduct.”  (Ibid.)  

 

The Court disagrees.  Moore states:

 

Appellants properly pled only that their PII was stolen and disseminated, and that a market for it existed. They did not allege they ever attempted or intended to participate in this market, or otherwise to derive economic value from their PII. Nor did they allege that any prospective purchaser of their PII might learn that their PII had been stolen in this data breach and, as a result, refuse to enter into a transaction with them, or insist on less favorable terms. In the absence of any such allegation, appellants failed to adequately plead that they lost money or property in the form of the value of their PII.

 

(Moore, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at 538, emphasis added.)  Undisputedly, Plaintiff fails to allege that she “ever attempted or intended to participate” in the purported market for her data or that she “otherwise derive[d] economic value from [her] PII.”  (Ibid.; see also Demurrer, p. 9.)  Bottom line, the lost-value allegations do not comply with Moore.

 

Plaintiff claims “Moore misapprehends the authorities it relies on and cannot be read to impose market participation as a pleading requirement.”  (Opposition, p. 9, underling of case name added; see also id. at p. 10.)

 

The Court disagrees.  “Moore is a published Second District opinion[.]”  (3/18/24 Ruling Re: Demurrer and Motion to Strike, p. 10.)  The opinion says what it says, and the Court intends to follow it.  Since Plaintiff fails to allege each Moore component, the lost-value theory fails.[2]

 

For benefit of the bargain, Plaintiff asserts that “she expected her private data to remain private and secure, and that she would have paid less for her healthcare had she known that Adobe would intercept, collect, and use her private data.”  (Opposition, p. 10 [citing paragraphs 92, 95, 102, 139 through 140, 148, 156, 169, and 185].)

 

Defendant contends the allegations “fail[] for the simple reason that Plaintiff never alleges she entered into any bargain with Adobe (or interacted with Adobe at all).”  (Reply, p. 6.)

 

“[A] ‘benefit of the bargain’ approach to establishing UCL standing is rooted in the California Supreme Court’s recognition that a plaintiff may demonstrate economic injury from unfair competition by establishing he or she ‘surrender[ed] in a transaction more, or acquire[d] in a transaction less, than he or she otherwise would have.’”  (Moore, supra, 83 Cal.App.5th at 527.) 

 

In Moore, the plaintiffs alleged that “they relied on Centrelake’s false representations and promises concerning data security in entering contracts with Centrelake and accepting its pricing terms, paying more than they would have had they known the truth that Centrelake had not implemented and would not maintain adequate data security practices.”  (Ibid., emphasis added.) 

 

In light of these rules, the Court believes the demurrer should be sustained.  The FAC indicates that Plaintiff bargained with Kaiser, not with Adobe.  (See FAC, ¶¶ 92, 95, 102, 139-140, 148, 156, 169, 185.)  Plaintiff does not cite a case upholding benefit-of-the-bargain allegations in this context.  (See Reply, pp. 6-8.)

 

The demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.  The Court believes Plaintiff should get one more opportunity to allege the lost-value theory and/or the benefit-of-the-bargain theory.

 

Unfair Prong

 

At the hearing on the first demurrer, the Court found that Plaintiff stated an unlawful-prong claim and gave her the option of amending to “add detail” to her unfair-prong claim.  (3/18/24 Ruling Re: Demurrer and Motion to Strike, p. 11.) 

 

The demurrer is overruled.  The UCL is written in the conjunctive.  If a plaintiff pleads a claim under one of the prongs, it is unnecessary to analyze the other prongs, and it is appropriate to overrule the demurrer.  (See, e.g., Morgan v. AT&T Wireless Services, Inc. (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1235, 1255 [stating: “In this case, we need not, and do not, decide whether the conduct alleged meets any of the definitions of ‘unfair’ (or whether it meets the definition of ‘unlawful’) because we find that plaintiffs have alleged a fraudulent business practice under the UCL”]; see also, e.g., Somerville v. Stryker Orthopaedics (N.D. Cal., Sept. 4, 2009 No. C 08-02443 JSW) 2009 WL 2901591, at *3 [stating: “Therefore, regardless of the other contentions of unfair and fraudulent conduct, because the requirement is conjunctive, the Court need not reach the other elements and finds Plaintiff has stated a cause of action under Section 17200”].)  Again, Plaintiff already stated an unlawful-prong claim.  Moreover, Plaintiff did not move to strike the new unfair allegations (see Notice, p. 2), so this portion of the demurrer constitutes an improper partial demurrer.

 

Restitution and Disgorgement

 

The Court granted Plaintiff leave to amend to allege “a ‘diminution’ in the value of . . . her personal information.”  (3/18/24 Ruling Re: Demurrer and Motion to Strike, p. 11 [quoting Brown v. Google LLC (N.D. Cal., Dec. 12, 2021 No. 20-CV-03664-LHK) 2021 WL 6064009].)

 

The demurrer is overruled.  Plaintiff’s new allegations do not quantify the alleged diminution, but they do state that a diminution occurred.  (See FAC, ¶ 185.)  Admittedly, the statement is conclusive; however, quantification is, ultimately, a factual issue that necessitates discovery.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike the requests for restitution, disgorgement, and punitive damages.

 

Restitution and Disgorgement

 

The restitution and disgorgement requests pertain to the third (violation of the right of privacy – California Constitution) and fourth (invasion of privacy/intrusion upon seclusion) causes of action.

 

Defendant contends “Plaintiff cannot seek restitution in connection with the third cause of action . . . because ‘the state constitutional privacy provision . . . serves as the basis for injunctive relief,’ not restitution or disgorgement (i.e., the claim is not monetarily compensable).”  (Motion, p. 12.)

 

On one hand, Defendant’s case – Hernandez v. Hillsides (2009) 47 Cal.4th 272 – does not address restitution and disgorgement and merely states that “it is an open question whether the state constitutional privacy provision, which is otherwise self-executing and serves as the basis for injunctive relief, can also provide direct and sole support for a damages claim[.]”  (Hernandez, supra, 47 Cal.4th at 286, emphasis added.)

 

On the other hand, Plaintiff’s case – Allen v. County of Sonoma (N.D. Cal., Aug. 18, 2017 No. 17-cv-00448-YGR) 2017 WL 3593340 – seems to undermine Plaintiff’s position.  Allen states that “the ‘privacy provision affords only a right to injunctive relief[.]”  (Allen, supra, 2017 WL 3593340, at *5.)  Restitution and disgorgement are not mentioned.  (See ibid.)

 

On balance, the Court agrees with Defendant because Plaintiff fails to cite authority permitting monetary relief, and the Court is unaware of a case supporting Plaintiff’s requests.

 

Next, Defendant claims “Plaintiff cannot seek equitable relief in connection with the fourth cause of action . . . because Plaintiff’s remedies at law are adequate relief.”  (Motion, pp. 12-13.)

 

The motion is denied.  This is the pleading stage.  The Court agrees with Plaintiff that she should receive latitude to request alternative and inconsistent remedies.  (See Opposition, p. 14.)

 

Finally, Defendant contends the motion should be granted as to both causes of action because “there is no relationship between Plaintiff and Adobe[,]” and “Plaintiff fails to plead any connection between the money Adobe made by selling its services to Kaiser and Plaintiff.”  (Motion, p. 13; see also Reply, p. 10.)

 

In response, Plaintiff cites paragraphs 146 and 167, asserting that “Defendant benefitted from Plaintiff’s Private Data” by “unlawfully extract[ing] and monetize[ing] her PII and PHI through use of that data to develop and modify Kaiser member profiles and provide advertising services[.]”  (Opposition, p. 15.)

 

At this stage, the Court agrees with Plaintiff.  Paragraph 146 provides:

 

Plaintiff and Class Members seek restitution based on Defendant’s unjust enrichment because they have conferred substantial benefits on Defendant by virtue of their use of the Kaiser Website. These benefits include Defendant’s interception, collection, use and disclosure of Plaintiff’s and Class Members’ Private Data, including PHI and PII, and the revenues and profits resulting therefrom – e.g., Adobe’s use of the Private Data, including PHI and PII, on its servers to create and/or modify profiles of Kaiser Members that Kaiser uses in exchange for fees paid to Adobe; Adobe’s use and disclosure of the Private Data to develop profiles of Kaiser Members for use by other website owners that utilize the Adobe Experience Cloud suite of products; and Adobe’s use and disclosure of the Private Data in Everest Technology to provide advertising services for its Adobe Experience Cloud customers.

 

(FAC, ¶ 146, emphasis added.)  Paragraph 167 is identical.  (See id. at ¶ 167.)  While it is true that Plaintiff does not allege that she paid money to Defendant, she does allege that Defendant obtained “revenues and profits” from her data.  (Id. at ¶¶ 146, 167.)  “The object of restitution is to restore the status quo by returning to the plaintiff funds in which he or she has an ownership interest.”  (Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1149.)  It is a question of fact whether Plaintiff possesses an ownership interest in those “revenues and profits” since they allegedly derived from Defendant’s allegedly unlawful use of her information. 

 

Punitive Damages

 

The motion is granted.  Plaintiff is no longer seeking punitive damages and agrees that the allegations should be stricken.  (See Opposition, p. 15.)

 

 

 

 


[1] The Court believes capability is a factual question.
