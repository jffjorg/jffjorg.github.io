DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV24719    Hearing Date: August 14, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

JOSHUA PAYTON,

 

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

20STCV24719

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

LOS ANGELES COUNTY METROPOLITAN TRANSPORTATION AUTHORITY., et al.,

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:             August 14, 2024        

 

Moving Parties:          Plaintiff, Joshua Payton

Responding Party:      Defendants Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority and Geneva Smith

HEARING: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AUGMENT EXPERT WITNESS DESIGNATION TO ADD TOXICOLOGY EXPERT

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

 

RULING

Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to Augment Expert Witness is GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

On June 30, 2020, plaintiff Joshua Payton (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against defendants Geneva Smith (“Smith”) and Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (“Metro”) (collectively, “Defendants”), alleging (1) motor vehicle and (2) general negligence. Plaintiff alleges that on December 8, 2019, Smith was driving Metro’s bus in the course of her employment with Metro. (Compl., GN-1.) While at a bus stop, Smith abruptly drove off, Plaintiff fell, and Smith ran over Plaintiff’s legs with the bus. 

 

On September 3, 2020, Defendants filed an answer. 

 

On July 12, 2022, the Court granted Metro’s ex parte application to continue the trial date from August 12, 2022 to May 5, 2023. 

 

On August 18, 2022, the Court granted Defendant’s unopposed motion to reopen discovery. 

 

On October 18, 2022, the Court denied Metro’s motion for leave to take a subsequent deposition of Plaintiff. 

 

On October 26, 2022, the parties conducted an IDC.

 

On April 28, 2023, the Court granted Metro’s ex parte application to continue the trial date from May 5, 2023 to July 19, 2023.

 

On September 1, 2023, the Court granted Metro’s ex parte application to continue the trial date from September 15, 2024 to December 14, 2023.

 

On November 27, 2023, the Court granted Metro’s ex parte application to continue the trial date from December 5, 2023 to February 20, 2024.

 

On February 13, 2024, the Court denied Metro’s ex parte application to continue the trial date.

 

On April 15, 2024, due to unavailability of trial courtrooms, the Court on its own motion continued the trial to April 29, 2024.

 

On April 29, 2024, the Court on its own motion continued the trial to April 29, 2024. At 11:13 a.m., Department 1 informed the Court that there were no assignments left. Department 1 informed Department 27 that the trial estimate does not match the witness list indicated in the trial transfer order. Department 1 noted that the operative witness list filed on 4/11 indicates 81hrs of testimony. Counsel were ordered to file any revised version of the trial binders before the next hearing to avoid trial assignment delays.

 

On May 9, 2024, the Court granted Metro’s ex parte application to continue trial without re-opening discovery. Jury trial was continued to September 25, 2024.

 

On July 12, 2024, the denied Plaintiff’s motion to re open discovery. In the tentative, the Court granted in part Plaintiff’s motion to reopen discovery for the limited purpose of taking PMQ depositions, and denied all other aspects of the motion, but the Court later modified the ruling by denying additional depositions to be held, stating: “Discovery, consistent with the tentative, remains closed.” (7/12/24 Minute Order.)

 

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion for leave to augment expert witness designation and add toxicology expert. On July 13, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition. On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed a reply.

 

On August 6, 2024, Metro filed a notice of change of address for its counsel, Ashour Yehoushua, APC.

 

            Trial is set for September 25, 2024.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2034.610(a), a party who has timely exchanged expert witness information may, with the court's permission, either:

 

1.     Add a new expert witness to their expert list and declaration.

2.     Amend the declaration to modify the general substance of testimony expected from an already designated expert.

 

According to section 2034.610(b), motion for an Order for leave to augment expert designation “shall be made at a sufficient time in advance of the time limit for the completion of discovery.” The court may allow a later filing under exceptional circumstances. The motion must be accompanied by a “meet and confer” declaration (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.610).

 

Under section 2034.620, the court will grant leave to augment or amend the expert list or declaration only if:

 

1.     The court considers how much the opposing party has relied on the original expert list.

2.     The opposing party will not be prejudiced in maintaining their case on the merits.

3.     The moving party either:

a.      Could not have reasonably determined to call the new expert or offer the new testimony earlier.

b.     Failed to do so due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, and promptly sought leave to amend or augment after realizing the need.

 

The motion’s approval is conditional upon making the expert available for deposition and may include additional terms, such as allowing the opposing party to designate additional experts or granting a trial continuance. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2034.620.)

 

The court’s decision to grant or deny such relief is discretionary and subject to appellate review only for abuse of discretion. (Bonds v. Roy (1999) 20 Cal.4th 140, 149.) Prejudice under section 2034.620(b) relates to the opposing party’s ability to respond to the new expert or testimony, not merely the adverse nature of the testimony. (Dickison v. Howen (1990) 220 Cal.App.3d 1471, 1479.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiff argues that he has conducted reasonable diligence throughout the litigation process. The complaint was filed on June 30, 2020, and the trial has been continued approximately six times. Both Plaintiff and Defendants have exchanged expert witness designations multiple times, with the most recent exchange occurring on January 2, 2024. Neither party has designated a toxicology expert at any point in the case. Plaintiff contends that he could not have anticipated the need for a toxicology expert, as Defendants had not designated one, despite including an intoxication instruction in their proposed jury instructions. Plaintiff asserts that his diligence is evidenced by the timely exchange of designations and their inability to foresee the relevance of toxicology without prior indication from Defendants. (Miles Decl. at ¶¶ 5-6, Ex. B-C.) Plaintiff also argues that the request for leave to designate a toxicology expert is justified by surprise. The incident in question involved Plaintiff attempting to board a Metro bus, and Plaintiff was not operating a motor vehicle at the time. Given this context, Plaintiff argues that any blood alcohol level or impairment should be considered irrelevant to the case. Moreover, Plaintiff’s hospital records indicate an extremely low blood alcohol level, and drug testing from an emergency room is rarely admissible in court. Plaintiff was surprised when Defendants included an intoxication instruction in the trial documents, prompting Plaintiff to file a Motion in Limine to exclude such evidence. With the trial still over two months away, Plaintiff asserts that it would be prejudicial not to grant leave to designate a toxicology expert now, as doing so would ensure a fair trial. (Miles Decl. at ¶ 7, Ex. D.)

 

Additionally, Plaintiff emphasizes that the request for leave to add a toxicology expert has been made in a timely manner. Although the trial was previously set for April 2024, it was continued to September 25, 2024, without reopening discovery. Plaintiff had previously filed a motion to reopen discovery, which was denied on the grounds that it pertained only to fact discovery, not the designation of expert witnesses. Recognizing this, Plaintiff has now brought a motion specifically to augment the expert witness designation to include a toxicology expert, arguing that this request is both timely and necessary under the circumstances. (Ca. Civ. Proc. Code § 2024.050.) Plaintiff further argues that granting leave to add one expert will not prejudice Defendants or delay the trial. The trial is scheduled for September 25, 2024, and Plaintiff has provided Defendants with the supplemental designation of the toxicology expert more than 50 days before the trial date. This timeframe provides Defendants ample opportunity to prepare for expert discovery. Finally, Plaintiff notes that they have made efforts to meet and confer with Defendants’ counsel regarding this motion. On July 15, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel reached out to Defendants’ counsel to discuss the motion and requested a stipulation to the augmented expert witness designation. However, Defendants’ counsel did not respond to this communication. (Miles Decl. at ¶ 10, Ex. F.)

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s motion is untimely and should be denied. They emphasize that discovery and expert discovery cutoffs have long passed, and Plaintiff’s previous motion to reopen discovery was denied by the court on July 12, 2024. According to Defendants, Section 2034.610 of the California Code of Civil Procedure mandates that such a motion be filed “in sufficient time in advance of the time limit for the completion of discovery,” which Plaintiff failed to do. Even though Plaintiff claims to have been surprised by Defendants’ proposed intoxication jury instruction, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has been aware of this issue for over three months, yet waited until now to file the motion. This delay renders the motion untimely under Section 2034.610(b). Defendants also contend that there are no exceptional circumstances justifying the late filing of this motion. They point out that Metro has been investigating Plaintiff’s potential intoxication throughout the litigation, as evidenced by various discovery requests concerning Plaintiff’s history of substance use, including admissions related to drug and alcohol use. These requests, made as early as October 2021 and again in May 2022, clearly indicate that the issue of intoxication was known to Plaintiff long before the current motion was filed. (Ashour Decl. ¶¶ 5-7.) Furthermore, Defendants argue that granting this motion would be highly prejudicial to them. The parties have already appeared twice for trial, on April 15 and April 29, 2024, but were delayed due to courtroom unavailability. Plaintiff’s current motion, filed three months after these trial dates, would disrupt Defendants’ trial preparation, especially with the trial only six weeks away and the Final Status Conference just four weeks away at the time of the hearing. Defendants assert that allowing the motion would force them to reassess their trial strategy, possibly requiring them to designate additional expert witnesses, which would prolong litigation in an already old case. Defendants argue that this case is nearly five years old, and Plaintiff’s repeated motions are merely attempts to delay proceedings and waste the court's time and resources. (Ashour Decl. ¶ 5.)

 In reply, Plaintiff argues that Defendants are attempting to challenge Plaintiff’s toxicology without having designated a toxicology expert themselves. Throughout the case, neither party designated a toxicology expert, and Plaintiff asserts that he conducted reasonable diligence by exchanging timely designations. Plaintiff contends that he could not have anticipated Defendants raising the issue of Plaintiff’s toxicology levels, as no such expert was designated by Defendants. To address Defendants’ proposed intoxication instruction, Plaintiff now seeks to designate a toxicology expert. Plaintiff also refutes Defendants’ claim that this motion was untimely. Plaintiff outlines the timeline of events, noting that on May 9, 2024, Defendants’ ex parte application to continue the trial was granted, and Plaintiff opposed it, requesting discovery be reopened. This request was denied on July 12, 2024, because the Court considered it under the statute pertaining to fact discovery, not expert designation. Plaintiff argues that he has been diligently attempting to address this issue since the trial was continued and have already provided Defendants with the proposed supplemental designation of the toxicology expert, giving Defendants ample time to prepare. Furthermore, Plaintiff contends that the discovery requests made by Defendants concerning Plaintiff’s substance abuse history do not prove intoxication at the time of the incident. Plaintiff argues that while Defendants sought information about substance abuse, this did not indicate an intent to question Plaintiff’s intoxication during the incident, especially since Defendants never designated a toxicology expert. Plaintiff emphasizes that he denied being under the influence at the time of the incident and that their hospital records show a low blood alcohol level, with drug testing from an emergency room being rarely admissible. Finally, Plaintiff argues that Defendants have shown no evidence of prejudice from the late designation of a toxicology expert. While Defendants mention that the trial has been delayed twice, Plaintiff notes that these delays were due to the unavailability of courtrooms, not any fault of their own. With nearly two months remaining before the trial and a supplemental designation deadline of August 26, 2024, Plaintiff asserts that there is no prejudice to Defendants. On the contrary, Plaintiff argues that he would be severely prejudiced if he is not allowed to designate a toxicology expert..

After consideration of the arguments and evidence presented, the Court finds that the motion was timely under the circumstances, that Plaintiff has demonstrated good cause for the late designation, and that granting the motion will not unduly prejudice Defendants or delay the trial. While discovery and expert discovery deadlines have passed, the Court finds that Plaintiff acted with reasonable diligence in bringing this motion. Although the Court denied the reopening of discovery under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.050, which pertains to fact discovery, Plaintiff shows that he has been attempting to address the need for a toxicology expert since learning of Defendants’ intent to include an intoxication jury instruction. The Court also finds that Plaintiff has shown sufficient justification for the late designation of a toxicology expert. The issue of intoxication was not initially central to the case, as neither party designated a toxicology expert during the discovery phase. Moreover, our Supreme Court has emphasized the importance of the general policy in favor of a trial on the merits, stating that this policy should be honored with “some showing” of excusable delay. (Salas v. Sears, Roebuck & Co. (1986) 42 Cal.3d 342, 347). In Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, the court reviewed several reasons supporting the trial court’s discretion to allow additional discovery and suggested that, in the absence of discovery being unduly burdensome or oppressive, the policy should favor allowing it. (Id. at 254). In the present case, the Court finds that there has been an adequate showing of excusable delay on the part of Plaintiff. Allowing Plaintiff to augment the expert witness list is in line with the public policy of permitting a trial on the merits and encouraging pretrial settlements based on the knowledge gained from such depositions. Furthermore, the Court is not persuaded by Defendants’ claims of prejudice. While Defendants argue that granting this motion would disrupt their trial preparation, the Court notes that the trial is still nearly two months away. The Court also acknowledges that while the parties have appeared for trial twice, these delays were due to the unavailability of courtrooms and not due to any fault of the Plaintif
Thus, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to augment expert witness designation.

BACKGROUND

 

            On May 28, 2021, plaintiff Thelidria Calhoun, in and through her successor-in-interest Dimitria Calhoun, filed a complaint against Defendants Torrance Memorial Medical Center, Transitional Care Unit, Kindred Hospital SouthBay (“Kindred”), and Memorial Hospital of Gardena.

On September 25, 2023, plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, (3) willful misconduct, (4) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights, and (5) wrongful death.

On April 9, 2024, the court sustained with 20 days leave to amend Defendant Kindred’s demurrer to the second cause of action for negligence, and sustained without leave to amend Defendant Kindred’s demurrer to the fourth cause of action for Violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights. The court also granted Defendant’s motion to strike.

On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed the second amended complaint (“SAC”) which alleges causes of action for (1) elder abuse, (2) negligence, and (3) wrongful death. The SAC alleges that while Plaintiff’s mother, Thelidria Calhoun was under the care of Defendants, she developed a Stage 4 infected pressure ulcer on her coccyx, which caused septic shock and death.

            On June 17, 2024, Defendant Kindred Hospital South Bay filed this demurrer and motion to strike the SAC. On August 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On August 8, 2024, Defendant Kindred filed a reply.

           

LEGAL AUTHORITY

 

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.) The court “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law ….” (Berkley v. Dowds (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 518, 525).) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994).) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters; therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70.) The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

DISCUSSION

I.                Untimely Opposition

 

As an initial matter, Plaintiff’s opposition is untimely.  Plaintiff’s opposition was filed and served on August 6, 2024.  Plaintiff’s Opposition was due nine court days before the August 15, 2024 hearing date, which was August 2, 2024. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) “A trial court has broad discretion to overlook late-served papers and to resolve the matter on the merits.”   (Gonzalez v. Santa Clara County Dept. of Social Services (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 162, 168 [“(E)ven if the service had been untimely, the trial court was vested with discretion to overlook the defect”]; see also Bozzi v. Nordstrom, Inc.  (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 755, 765 [“A trial court has broad discretion under rule 3.1300(d) of the Rules of Court to refuse to consider papers served and filed beyond the deadline without a prior court order finding good cause for late submission.”].) Defendant has not argued it was prejudiced by the delay and the reply addresses the merits of the opposition. Thus, the Court exercises its discretion and considers the merits of the opposition.

 

II.              Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to the entire SAC, arguing that Plaintiff failed to file the amended complaint within the time allowed by the court. Defendant also demurs to the first cause of action for Elder Abuse and second cause of action for negligence. Defendant contends the cause of action for negligence is barred by the statute of limitations and the cause of action for elder abuse fails to plead specific facts sufficient to support the claim.

Defendant’s Request for Dismissal of the SAC under Code of Civil Procedure §581(f)(2)

Code of Civil Procedure section 581 provides in relevant part that “[t]he court may dismiss the complaint as to that defendant…after a demurrer to the complaint is sustained with leave to amend, the plaintiff fails to amend it within the time allowed by the court and either party moves for dismissal.” (Id. at subd. (f)(2).) 

 

“The authority of the trial court to dismiss an action under the provisions of Code Civ. Proc., § 581… (action may be dismissed when plaintiff fails to amend complaint within time allowed by court after demurrer has been sustained or when plaintiff fails to amend complaint within time allowed by court after motion to strike has been granted), rests in the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Harding v. Collazo (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 1044, 1046.) 

Defendant points out that its demurrer was sustained on April 9, 2024 and Plaintiff was given 20 days leave to amend. In fact, Defendant argues, Plaintiff did not file the SAC until May 29, 2024 – fifty days following the hearing.

However, Defendant’s demurrer was sustained with leave to amend as to the negligence cause of action only. Defendant had not previously demurred to the entire FAC. As such, the entire SAC would not be subject to dismissal under this section, because the court never ruled that the allegations pertaining to Defendant Kindred had to be amended with respect to the other causes of action. In addition, as discussed more fully below, the demurrer to the cause of action for negligence is being sustained without leave to amend. Thus, the court declines to dismiss the entire SAC. Moreover, dismissal under this section is discretionary.

First Cause of Action for Elder Abuse

In order to state a cause of action under the Elder Abuse Act, “a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive
damages.” (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal. 4th 771, 789.) There must be a showing of recklessness, fraud, malice, or oppression. (Id. at 783.)

“[T]he facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury ‘must be pleaded with particularity,’ in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley (2011) 198 Cal. App. 4th 396, 407 (citing Covenant Care).) “Allegations of misconduct directed against one defendant . . . do not state a cause of action against another defendant against whom the allegations of misconduct are not directed.” (Id. at 410 (citation omitted).) “[W]hen a plaintiff asserts a claim against a health care provider on a legal theory other than professional negligence, courts must determine whether the claim is nonetheless based on the health care provider’s professional negligence.” (Larson v. UHS of Rancho Springs, Inc. (2014) 230 Cal. App. 4th 336, 347 (citation omitted).) Citing to the content of the definition of “neglect” in Welf. and Inst. Code §15610.57(b), the Supreme Court stated, “[w]hat they each seem to contemplate is the existence of a robust caretaking or custodial relationship—that is, a relationship where a certain party has assumed a significant measure of responsibility for attending to one or more of an elder’s basic needs that an able-bodied and fully competent adult would ordinarily be capable of managing without assistance.” (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal. 4th 148, 58.) “What seems beyond doubt is that the Legislature enacted a scheme distinguishing between – and decidedly not lumping together – claims of professional negligence and neglect.” (Id. at 159.)

“If some care is provided, that will not necessarily absolve a care facility of [elder or] dependent abuse liability. For example, if a care facility knows it must provide a certain type of care on a daily basis but provides that care sporadically, or is supposed to provide multiple types of care but only provides some of those types of care, withholding of care has occurred. In those cases, the trier of fact must determine whether there is a significant pattern of withholding portions or types of care. A significant pattern is one that involves repeated withholding of care and leads to the conclusion that the pattern was the result of choice or deliberate indifference.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 90.)

            Here, the SAC alleges the following. On January 27, 2019, Thelidria was transferred to Kindred for rehabilitation of her hip and to help wean her off her ventilator. During a cardiology consultation on February 25, 2019, it was noted that she had “a recent cervical neck fracture, multiple orthopedic fractures, malnutrition, interstitial lung disease (likely related to rheumatoid arthritis), acute on chronic respiratory failure status post tracheostomy with trach collar, decubitus wound with wound Vac” after being transferred from Torrance Memorial Medical Center to Kindred. It is clear that the medical records noted she required frequent repositioning every two hours for pressure relief on the decubitus wounds she suffered. (SAC, ¶ 27.)

At the time in 2018, Thelidria was discharged from Torrance Memorial to Kindred the emergency department noted that she had a UTI and was septic, emaciated, severely anemic, hypotensive, and hyperglycemic. Additionally, Thelidria had a large, necrotic, foul-smelling, black, purple and yellow oozing unstageable ulcer on her sacral area covered by a bandage saturated in feces and urine. (Id., ¶ 28.)

 On March 5, 2019, she was taken to Memorial Hospital of Gardena after 911 was called “because of her horrific treatment and Kindred.” Memorial Hospital of Gardena prepared a skin care protocol, including a foam mattress, frequent repositioning, and floating of her heels to prevent any more pressure ulcears from developing. These protocols had been required at Kindred but never provided. Later, she was transferred back to Kindred. (Id., ¶ 29.)

On April 1, 2019, Thelidria’s daughter Dimitria Calhoun demanded a meeting with the CEO of defendant Kindred and head of all departments at the hospital. She requested that Thelidria be transferred to Kindred Orange, but they refused to do so. Dimitria informed the CEO and other department heads that Thelidria was not being turned, that she had severe bed sores, that she looked swollen, and that her GI tube was leaking, but the treatment stayed the same. By April 30, 2019, she had developed an infection and was taken to ICU. (Id., ¶ 31.) By May 12, 2019, Kindred’s nursing staff indicated that Thelidria’s pressure ulcer had yellow slough and drainage, indicating infection. (Id., ¶ 32.) On May 15, 2019, Kindred completed a wound care consult that indicated that the two scattered Stage 4 pressure ulcers progressed to being unstageable. (Id., ¶ 35.) She died on June 1, 2019 from septic shock secondary to the large, infected, necrotic sacral decubitus ulcer. (Id., ¶ 34.)

The court finds that the allegations in the SAC are insufficient to meet the elements for a cause of action for Elder Abuse, as the SAC does not plead facts that rise to oppression, fraud, or malice or a conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (unstageable bedsore). The allegations against Kindred relate to the failure to turn or reposition Thelidria in order to prevent her bedsores. However, there are no specific factual allegations pertaining to this allegation, let alone any allegations showing a significant pattern of withholding portions or types of care. The allegations as to Defendant Kindred are made in passing or conclusory. For example, Plaintiff alleges she informed the CEO and other department heads that Thelidria was not being turned, and that she had severe bed sores.

Next, Defendant argues that Plaintiff has not pled facts amounting to authorization or ratification on the part of an officer, director or managing agent of the moving defendant, as is required to establish a cause of action for elder abuse.

 

Welfare and Institutions Code §15657(c) states, “The standards set forth in subdivision (b) of Section 3294 of the Civil Code regarding the imposition of punitive damages on an employer based upon the acts of an employee shall be satisfied before any damages or attorney’s fees permitted under this section may be imposed against an employer.” 

           

Civil Code §3294(b) provides, “An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.”

The SAC also fails to allege any specific facts that an officer, director or managing agent, authorized or ratified Defendant’s employees’ conduct. The allegations pertaining to informing the CEO are conclusory, and fail to plead with specificity as required.

As a result, the demurrer to the cause of action for Elder Abuse is sustained. As this is Kindred’s first demurrer to this cause of action, and there is a possibility of curing the defect, the court will allow 20 days leave to amend.

Second Cause of Action for Negligence

Defendant argues that the cause of action is barred by the one-year statute of limitations under CCP §340.5. Code of Civil Procedure section 340.5 provides that an action for injury against a health care provider based on a person’s alleged professional negligence must be commenced within three years after the date of injury or one year after the plaintiff discovers the injury, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc. § 340.5.) Defendant asserts that it is a healthcare provider, and that the allegations against it relate to professional negligence. Defendant contends that the complaint was not filed until May 28, 2021, almost two years after decedent died on June 1, 2019.

Here, as ruled on by the court in Defendant Kindred’s prior demurrer, the conduct that is alleged to have been provided by Kindred was that of medical care. The incidents giving rise to the negligence cause of action occurred in June 2019. Thus, the complaint is time barred on its face as the complaint was filed in May 2021.

In opposition, Plaintiff concedes that the cause of action for negligence should be dismissed. As such, the demurrer to the second cause of action for negligence is sustained without leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend as to the cause of action for Elder Abuse, and SUSTAINED without leave to amend as to the cause of action for negligence.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
