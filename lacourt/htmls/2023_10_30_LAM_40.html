DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 21STCV13920    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

RANDALL L. BORT, an individual,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

KBS HOLDCO, LLC, a California limited liability company; BRIAN KENNEDY, an individual, and DOES 1-50, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

KBS HOLDCO, LLC, a California limited liability company; BRIAN KENNEDY, an individual,

                        Cross-Complainants,

            v.

RANDALL L. BORT, an individual; and ROES 1-10, inclusive,

                        Cross-Defendants.

 Case No.:                           21STCV13920

 Hearing Date:                           10/30/23

 Trial Date:                           11/7/23

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Brian Kennedy and KBS Holdco, LLC’s Motion to Quash Four (4) Subpoenas Served on Various Third-Party Law Firms, and Request for Sanctions; and

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Brian Kennedy and KBS Holdco, LLC’s Motion to Quash Six (6) Subpoenas Served on Various Third-Party Law Firms, and Request for Sanctions.

 

Background

Pleadings

Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Randall L. Bort (hereafter, Plaintiff Bort) sues Defendants/Cross-Complainants KBS Holdco, LLC (KBS), Brian Kennedy (KBS’s owner), and Does 1-50 pursuant to claims of (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Promissory Estoppel, (3) Quantum Meruit, and (4) Account Stated.

The claims arise from allegations that KBS and Kennedy (hereafter, Defendants) entered into a written Agreement with Plaintiff Bort to employ his professional services to perform various tasks—including obtaining financing—for Defendants’ intended purchase of the remainder of a company named Regency Outdoor Advertising (Regency), with Plaintiff Bort to be paid a Retainer Fee of $50,000 or relevant Success Fee of $1,470,000 if the Regency Project was structured as an asset purchase. Plaintiff Bort alleges that his efforts helped the Defendants successfully purchase Regency on February 2, 2021 but that the Defendants breached the Agreement by only paying Plaintiff Bort a $10,000 downpayment and no other monies, causing Bort damages of $1,460,000.

In turn, Defendants KBS and Kennedy sue Plaintiff Bort and Roes 1-10 pursuant to a June 30, 2021 Cross-Complaint alleging claims of (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Rescission, (3) Unfair Competition, and (4) Declaratory Relief.

The claims in the cross-complaint arise from allegations that, among other things, Defendant Kennedy acquired Regency through use of his own funds and that Plaintiff Bort breached the Agreement by failing to secure the financing contemplated by the parties’ contract, for which reason a rescission of the Agreement is merited and Plaintiff Bort is not entitled to the $1,460,000 sought in the Complaint.

Motions Before the Court

On September 20, 2023, Defendants filed and served a motion to quash four subpoenas duces tecum served on various third-party law firms by Plaintiff Bort on September 5, 2023.

Specifically, the subpoenas were served on Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton LLP, Karish & Bjorgum PC, LTL Attorneys, LLP, and Parker, Milliken, Clark, O’Hara & Samuelian. The subpoenas contain identical categories of examination and an identical production request. The categories of examination are (1) “[d]id you provide services to Brian Kennedy and/or KBS Holdco, LLC,” (2) “[w]ere you paid in full for the services”; and (3) “[w]hat is the balance due for those services?” The production request seeks “documents sufficient to identify any amounts due from Brian Kennedy and/or KBS Holdco, such as a statement of account or a last invoice.”

On September 29, 2023, Defendants filed and served a motion to quash six additional subpoenas served on third-party law firms by Plaintiff Bort on September 8, 2023.

The subpoenas were served on Robins Kaplan LLP, Crochetiere LLP, Kinsella Weitzman Iser Kump & Aldisert LLP, Drinker Biddle & Reath LLP, Stubbs, Alderton & Markiles, LLP, and Shartsis Friese LLP. These subpoenas contain categories of examination and a production request identical to those in the September 5th subpoenas.

The motions attached separate statements and were respectively scheduled for hearing on January 12 and 22, 2024.

On October 23, 2023, the Court held a final status conference (FSC) in this action, at which time the Court, on oral stipulation, advanced and continued the hearings on the motions to quash to October 30, 2023.

On October 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed and served a combined opposition to the motions to quash. No separate statement was attached thereto.

The two motions to quash are now before the Court.

 

Motions to Quash Subpoena Duces Tecum

Legal Standard

A motion to quash a deposition subpoena or deposition notice is used to strike, modify, or impose conditions on a subpoena or notice that is procedurally or substantively defective. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1987.1, subd. (a), 2025.410, subd. (c); see, e.g., Catholic Mut. Relief Soc’y v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 358, 365 [motion based on ground that subpoenas sought information outside scope of discovery]; John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186 [motion based in part on ground that subpoenas violated right of privacy]; McClatchy Newspapers v. Superior Court (1945) 26 Cal.2d 386, 391 [motion based on ground that subpoenas were unreasonable and oppressive]; Far W. S&L Assn. v. McLaughlin (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 67, 71 [motion based on ground that subpoena was not properly served]; City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 883, 888 [procedural remedy for a defective subpoena is generally a motion to quash under § 1987.1].)

Such a motion may be made by:

(1) Any party to the action (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(1));

(2) The subpoenaed witness (e.g., a custodian of another person’s personal records) (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(2); Monarch Healthcare v. Superior Court (2000) 78 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1287-1288);

(3) A person (party or nonparty) whose consumer, governmental, or employment information or records have been subpoenaed (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1985.3, subd. (g) [party consumer], 1985.4 [state or local-agency employee or any other natural person], 1985.6, subd. (f)(1) [employee], 1987.1, subd. (b)(3) [consumer], 1987.1, subd. (b)(4) [employee]; see also Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1985.3, subd. (g), ¶ 2, 1985.6, subd. (f)(2) [nonparty consumer can make written objections instead of motion to quash]);

(4) A person whose “personally identifying information” within the meaning of Civ. Code § 1798.79.8, subd, (b), subpoenaed in connection with an action involving that person’s exercise of free-speech rights; and

(5) The Court, on its own motion, after giving the parties notice and the opportunity to be heard (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

A motion to quash may be based on various grounds, including that:

The subpoena contains error or irregularities in the deposition notice (see Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.410, subds. (a), (c));

The subpoena seeks information that is not relevant to the issues in the case (see, e.g., Catholic Mut. Relief Soc’y v. Superior Court, supra, 42 Cal.4th at p. 365 [motion to quash deposition subpoena because documents were outside the scope of discovery]; cf. Cadiz Land Co. v. Rail Cycle, L.P. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 74, 122-123 [motion to quash deposition notice because deposition would not lead to admissible evidence]);

The subpoena seeks information that is not relevant to the issues in the case (see, e.g., Slagle v. Superior Court (1989) 211 Cal.App.3d 1309, 1314-1315 [motion to quash trial subpoena because medical records were not relevant was properly overruled]);

The subpoena makes unreasonable or oppressive demands for information (see Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a); see, e.g., McClatchy Newspapers v. Superior Court, supra, 26 Cal.2d at p. 391 [motion to quash deposition subpoenas because they were unreasonable and oppressive]);

The disclosure of the records sought will violate the consumer’s constitutional right to privacy (see Cal. Const., art. I, § 1; see, e.g., Fett v. Medical Bd. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 211, 213 [petitions to quash to nonparties’ doctor because of privacy rights]; Manela v. Superior Court (2009) 117 Cal.App.4th 1139, 1150-1151 [motion to quash a subpoena to party’s doctor because of privacy rights]); and

The disclosure of records will violate a privilege of the consumer, the witness, or a person with whom the witness has a relationship that requires the witness to assert the privilege on the person’s behalf. (See, e.g., Monarch Healthcare v. Superior Court, supra, 78 Cal.App.4th at p. 1290 [objections to subpoena for business records based on trade-secret privilege]).

Order Quashing Subpoena: GRANTED.

I. Preliminary Discussion

The Court initially notes that because all ten subpoenas duces tecum are identical in their categories for examination and production request, the Court can apply its determination equally to all of them, informed by the arguments advanced by the parties.

The identical categories of examination at issue ask:

(1) “Did you provide services to Brian Kennedy and/or KBS Holdco, LLC”;

(2) “Were you paid in full for the services”; and

(3) “What is the balance due for those services?”

The identical production request seeks:

“Documents sufficient to identify any amounts due from Brian Kennedy and/or KBS Holdco, such as a statement of account or a last invoice.”

(See Mots., Ex. A.)

II. Proper Party

Defendants have standing to make this motion because they are parties to the action (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (b)(1)) and/or persons (party or nonparty) whose consumer, governmental, or employment information or records have been subpoenaed (see Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1985.3, subd. (g) [party consumer], 1987.1, subd. (b)(3) [consumer]).

III. Merits

In their motions, Defendants argue that the documents and information sought are not relevant to Plaintiff’s causes of action, that the documents and information are impermissible character evidence and are thus not admissible, that Plaintiff requests documents and information protected by the attorney-client privilege, and that the documents and information requested violate Defendant Kennedy’s personal privacy rights. (9/20/23 Mot., pp. 3-8; 9/26/23 Mot., pp. 3-10.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the documents and information sought are relevant to pattern and practice of not paying for services and do not involve character evidence, but rather, involve evidence of intent, credibility, custom, truthfulness, and lack of illegality or nonperformance by Plaintiff Bort. The opposition also argues that the billing and payment history involved in the categories for examination and production request are not privileged or confidential, though the briefing fails to discuss privacy. Last, the opposition briefly argues that there was an insufficient meet and confer prior to these motions. (Opp’n, pp. 11-15.)

The Court finds in favor of Defendants.

The Court determines that the documents and information sought in the subpoena are beyond the scope of discovery because they are irrelevant to the question of whether the specific conditions to trigger the Success Fee took place. Liability turns on that question, not on a question of intent or state of mind, which would be relevant to a fraud claim. In contrast, Plaintiff’s Complaint pleads claims sounding in contract alone. It is noteworthy that the other lawsuits principally relied upon by Plaintiff in which at least one court allowed such discovery (which ruling is, of course, not precedent) included a federal action which included a RICO and fraud claim, and a state court action that included a fraud claim.

To the extent that Plaintiff contends that this discovery will assist in refuting Defendants’ arguments as to their real “motive” for not paying Plaintiff, or show a “custom” regarding like cases pursuant to Evidence Code section 1101, the Court has several concerns. First, it is not clear whether Defendants will in fact “open the door” to impeachment in this manner, and if they do so, there appears to already be substantial evidence that is in the possession of the Plaintiff that can be used to impeach Mr. Kennedy. Under Evidence Code section 352, it is very unlikely that the Court would allow all of the evidence sought to be discovered in these subpoenas at trial, as proving up both the failure to pay, as well as the purported reason for not paying, each of these law firms will require a consumption of time that would become undue at some point.

Moreover, Defendants have a right to privacy which merits the quashing of these motions, which is a topic unaddressed by Plaintiff’s opposition.

Defendant Kennedy, as an individual, has a right to prevent disclosure of information about personal finances, including information about bank accounts, assets, and debts. (See In re Ins. Installment Fee Cases (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 1395, 1428 [information privacy in personal financial affairs].) The right to privacy extends to an individual’s confidential financial information in whatever form it takes, e.g., tax returns, checks, statements, or other account information. (Look v. Penovatz (2019) 34 Cal.App.5th 61, 73; SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 754; Overstock.Com, Inc. v. Goldman Sachs Group, Inc. (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 471, 503.) Similarly, Defendant KBS has a right to privacy, even if it is more limited than Defendant Kennedy’s. (Ameri-Med Corp. v. Worker’s Comp. Appeals Bd. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1286-1287 (Ameri-Med) [corporation has no standing to assert a constitutional right to privacy but has a general right to privacy that depends on the circumstances, including the strength of the nexus between the artificial entity and human beings and the context in which the controversy arises].)

In the face of an objection based on privacy, the party that is seeking discovery of another party’s financial information must show that this information is directly relevant to a cause of action or defense, such that the disclosure is essential to a fair resolution of the lawsuit. (Look v. Penovatz, supra, 34 Cal.App.5th at p. 73.) Here, as discussed above, Plaintiff has not met this burden.

Last, the Court notes that it is satisfied with the meet and confer efforts here. Moreover, Plaintiff presents no authority for the purpose of showing that meet and confer efforts are specifically necessary for motions to quash pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1; see also Mots., Notice [motions brought under Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, while sanctions brought separately under Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2020.010 and 2023.010].)

Defendants’ motions are therefore GRANTED.

Sanctions: DENIED.

Except as specified in subdivision (c), in making an order pursuant to motion made under subdivision (c) of Section 1987 or under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.2, subd. (a).)

The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348, subd. (a).)

The September 20, 2023 motion seeks $6,800 in sanctions against Plaintiff Bort and his counsel for discovery abuse in serving the subpoenas and for opposing this motion without substantial justification. (9/20/23 Mot., pp. 8-9; 9/20/23 Mot., Angioni Decl., ¶¶ 4-6 [seeking reduced fees of $6,800, but representing that (1) counsel Nannina Angioni spent eight hours on this motion at a rate of $687 per hour, (2) counsel Allyssa Rose spent eight hours on this motion at a rate of $400 per hour, (3) counsel Allyssa Rose expects to expend eight hours on a reply at a rate of $400 per hour, and (4) counsel Nannina Angioni expects to expend five hours on a reply and appearing at this hearing at a rate of $687 per hour, which, per the Court’s calculation, totals $15,331 in work by counsel].)

The September 29, 2023 motion seeks $8,500 in sanctions against Plaintiff Bort and his counsel for the same reasons. (9/29/23 Mot., pp. 10-11; 9/29/23 Mot., Swanson Decl., ¶¶ 5-6 [seeking reduced fees of $8,500, but representing that (1) counsel Sarah Swanson spent seven hours on this motion at a rate of $500 per hour, (2) counsel Allyssa Rose spent five hours on this motion at a rate of $400 per hour, (3) counsel Sarah Swanson expects to expend two hours on a reply at a rate of $500 per hour, (4) counsel Allyssa Rose expects to expend three hours on a reply at a rate of $400 per hour, and (5) counsel Nannina Angioni expended or expects to expend five hours preparing this motion and attending the hearing at a rate of $687 per hour, which, per the Court’s calculation, totals $11,135 in work by counsel].)

The opposition does not discuss the merit of sanctions.

The Court finds in favor of Plaintiff.

The Court finds that Plaintiff had a substantial justification for opposing these motions, because of the possibility that Defendants may seek to defend this lawsuit at trial with testimony that could be impeached with evidence of a failure to pay other professionals. However, for all the reasons stated above, the Court ultimately did not find that Plaintiff had met his heavy burden of showing that these subpoenas should not be quashed.

In addition, even if the Court were to have granted sanctions, the time expended by Defendants on each motion was excessive.

The sanctions requests are thus DENIED.

 

Conclusion

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Brian Kennedy and KBS Holdco, LLC’s Motion to Quash Four (4) Subpoenas Served on Various Third-Party Law Firms is GRANTED.

The Court QUASHES the September 5, 2023 subpoenas served on Sheppard, Mullin, Richter & Hampton LLP, Karish & Bjorgum PC, LTL Attorneys, LLP, and Parker, Milliken, Clark, O’Hara & Samuelian.

Defendants and Cross-Complainants Brian Kennedy and KBS Holdco, LLC’s Request for Sanctions is DENIED.

Defendant Lisa Nguyen’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint.

 

Background

Plaintiffs Marisela Vasquez, Anthony Ixta, Anabelle Ixta, Luna Vasquez, and Manuel Vasquez (the latter four Plaintiffs being minors) sue Defendants Pacific Urban Investors (Pacific; erroneously sued as Haven Warner Center), FPI Management, Inc. (FPI), Lisa Nguyen, Dennis Treadaway, Regina Jasinksi, and Does 1 through 20 pursuant to an October 2, 2023 First Amended Complaint alleging claims of (1) Battery, (2) Negligence, (3) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, (4) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Habitability, (5) Breach of the Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment, (6) Violation of Civil Code Section 1942.4, (7) Private Nuisance, (8) Public Nuisance, and (9) Breach of Contract.

The claims arise from allegations that during their tenancy of the premises located at 6530 Independence Ave., Canoga Park, California 91303 (the Haven Warner Center), Unit 237 (Subject Premises), Plaintiffs for months suffered from a bed bug infestation, resulting in bites, injuries, and medical expenses to Plaintiffs, with Plaintiffs informing Defendants of the infestation, which Defendants failed to remedy.

The action was originally assigned to Department 27 of the Personal Injury Court at the Spring Street Courthouse. The summons of the Complaint was not issued and filed until November 14, 2022.

On March 9, 2023, the summons and Complaint were served on Lisa Nguyen by substituted service on a leasing office representative at the Haven Warner Center.

On May 8, 2023, Department 27 determined that this action was not a personal injury case as defined in the Superior Court’s General Order re Jurisdiction of such cases, for which reason, Department 27 sent this action to Department 1 for reassignment. Department 27 also took off calendar all hearings for this action then set in that Department.

On May 9, 2023, Department 1 reassigned this action to Department 40 at the Stanley Mosk Courthouse (the Court).

On May 17, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a proof of service for Defendant Nguyen.

On August 9, 2023, Defendant Nguyen made a motion to quash the service of summons and Complaint effected on her at the Haven Warner Center on March 9, 2023.

Plaintiffs have failed to oppose Defendant Nguyen’s motion to quash, which is now before the Court.

 

Motion to Quash

Preliminary Note

The Court makes a dispositive ruling on this motion but notes that this motion is directed at a pleading (the May 28, 2021 Complaint) that has been superseded by Plaintiffs’ filing of the October 2, 2023 FAC. Moreover, any service of the FAC on Defendant Nguyen is not affected by this ruling. A new motion to quash would need to be filed.

Timeliness

A motion to quash service of summons must be filed and served “on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a).)

Here, the time “to plead” was the time to answer the Complaint, which was 30 days after service on March 9, 2023. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.40, subd. (a).) This motion was made on August 9, 2023, i.e., more than 30 days after March 9, 2023. Yet, because Defendant Nguyen argues that she was never properly served with the summons and Complaint, the Court finds good cause to allow this motion to be heard.

Legal Standard

A motion to quash can be brought on the ground that the court lacks personal jurisdiction over the defendant, e.g., because service was improper. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1); see, e.g., Tresway Aero, Inc. v. Superior Court (1971) 5 Cal.3d 431, 433 [defendant filed motion to quash on ground that service on corporation was defective because it did not comply with Code Civ. Proc., § 410, now § 412.30]; County of Riverside v. Superior Court (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 443, 446 [defendant filed motion to quash on ground that service was defective because summons did not specify date for answering and appearing].)

The defendant should attach evidence to support its motion. (See School Dist. of Okaloosa Cty. v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1126, 1131 [defendant must present some admissible evidence in form of affidavits or declarations to place issue of lack of jurisdiction before court]; see, e.g., Aquila, Inc. v. Superior Court (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 556, 563 [defendant supported motion to quash with declarations]; cf. Floveyor Int’l v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 789, 793-794 [“‘A defendant who takes the position that the service of summons as made upon him did not bring him within the jurisdiction of the court’[] may serve and file a notice of motion to quash the service,’” where “‘the effect of such a notice is to place upon the plaintiff the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction … [or] the facts requisite to a[] [showing of] effective service,’” citations omitted].)

Order Quashing Summons: GRANTED.

In her motion, Defendant Nguyen moves to quash the service of summons and Complaint effected on her on the grounds that service was made at the Haven Warner Center on March 9, 2023 under circumstances where Defendant Nguyen has not worked for FPI since March 28, 2020 and was not physically present at the Haven Warner Center on that day. Defendant Nguyen also argues that she has not been personally served or served by substituted service at an appropriate location. (Mot., pp. 3-4.)

In support, Defendant Nguyen provides a sworn declaration advancing these grounds for quashing service on her. (Mot., Nguyen Decl., ¶¶ 1-7.)

Plaintiffs have failed to oppose this motion and its arguments.

The Court finds in favor of Defendant Nguyen.

Defendant Nguyen has provided evidence to show that, on March 9, 2023, a process server effected service of the summons, Complaint, and other documents at a location—the Haven Warner Center—where Defendant Nguyen was not present and did not work.

Moreover, this motion is unopposed, lending merit to it. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1342 [“The failure of the opposing party to serve and file a written opposition may be construed by the court as an admission that the motion is meritorious, and the court may grant the motion without a hearing on the merits”]; Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410 [“The rule [inferring merit in unopposed motions] seems to apply only when a party has not filed any written opposition”].) 

Defendant Nguyen’s motion is thus GRANTED. 

Conclusion

Defendant Lisa Nguyen’s Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint is GRANTED.

The March 9, 2023 service of the summons and Complaint on Defendant Lisa Nguyen is QUASHED.
