DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV44865    Hearing Date: December 4, 2023    Dept: F49

Dept. F-49

Date: 12-4-23

Case #20STCV44865

Trial Date: 4-22-24 c/f 2-5-24

 

SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants, Saugus Union High School District, et al.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Solomon Perkins

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion for Summary Judgment/Summary Adjudication on the First Amended Complaint

·         1st Cause of Action: Negligence

·         2nd Cause of Action: Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention

·         3rd Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

·         4th Cause of Action: Violation of California Education Code sections 200, 201, 220 and 260 – Discrimination on the Basis of Disability

·         5th Cause of Action: Violation of Equal Protection Clause of the California Constitution

·         6th Cause of Action: False Imprisonment

·         7th Cause of Action: Violation of Americans with Disability Act

·         8th Cause of Action: Violation of California Government Code section 11135 – Discrimination Based on Disability

·         9th Cause of Action: Violation of 42 USC section 1983 – Violation of the Equal Protection Clause

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On February 13, 2020, Plaintiff Solomon Perkins, a student at Tesoro Del Valle Elementary School, a school campus within the territory of Defendant Saugus Union School District (Saugus Union), was an enrolled student on campus, when a fellow classmate, “Tobin,” reported to a staff member on campus that Plaintiff was carrying a gun in his backpack. According to the complaint, Plaintiff told Tobin he had a “”Nurf-Slingshot gun” with him, which led to the misrepresentation of an actual gun.

 

Plaintiff is a student with special needs and a learning disability. Plaintiff attends school with an individualized education plan (IEP), which requires the presence of a para-professional during instructional time. The para-professional was not present during the interactive period with Tobin.

 

Following the report, Plaintiff’s backpack was examined by school principal, defendant Gina Nolte. The search revealed no actual firearm or slingshot gun. Nolte subsequently contacted the Los Angeles County Sheriff, and the “mental health department.” According to the complaint, the mental health department “attempted to commit Plaintiff.”

 

Although Plaintiff’s mother, Thea Perkins, was apparently contacted and came to the school campus, prior to her arrival, the Sheriffs Department interrogated Plaintiff. No other adult acting on Plaintiff’s behalf was present. Upon Thea Perkins arriving at campus, both Plaintiff and his mother were involuntarily detained on the premises, until they were both escorted off the premises by armed deputies.

 

Following the incident, Thea Perkins demanded new and different resources be assigned to Plaintiff. The request was denied, and instead Defendant Saugus Union “disabled access on Plaintiff’s Chromebook,” which Plaintiff used for remote learning during the Covid school campus closure. Plaintiff has been cut-off from the school therapist and contact via other Google applications. On a personal level, para-professional services were also denied during remote learning, and Plaintiff was never sent any learning supplies associated with remote learning.

 

Plaintiff alleges a claim was submitted to Saugus Unified School District on May 28, 2020 and rejected. On November 23, 2020, Plaintiff filed a ten cause of action complaint for Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Violation of California Education Code sections 200, 201, 220 and 260, Violation of Equal Protection Clause of the California Constitution, False Imprisonment Violation of Americans with Disability Act, Violation of California Unruh Act, Violation of California Government Code section 1135, and Violation of 42 USC section 1983. Both Defendants are named in all causes of action.

 

On March 9, 2021, the action was transferred out of Department 28. On March 12, 2021, the action was assigned to Department 49. On July 12, 2021, the court denied the special motion to strike of Saugus Union High School District. On September 28, 2021, the court granted Plaintiff leave to file a later government claim.

Defendants Saugus Union High School District, and Gina Nolte move for summary judgment/summary adjudication on the first through ninth causes of action for Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, Violation of California Education Code sections 200, 201, 220 and 260 – Discrimination on the Basis of Disability, Violation of Equal Protection Clause of the California Constitution, False Imprisonment, Violation of Americans with Disability Act, Violation of California Government Code section 11135 – Discrimination Based on Disability, and Violation of 42 USC section 1983 – Violation of the Equal Protection Clause.

 

Defendants move on grounds of the litigation privilege, and lack of evidence demonstrating animus. Plaintiffs in opposition challenges the application of the litigation privilege, denies any applicable immunity for the actions of the school, and contends triable issues of material of fact on the course and conduct of the underlying incident in regards to discriminatory animus.

 

Defendants in reply reiterate the litigation privilege and protection from liability of the conduct articulated in the first, second and third causes of action. Defendants maintain principal Nolte was entitled to detain Solomon without potential civil liability due to the potential gun threat regardless of whether any weapon was found at the time. Defendants cite to a recent school shooting case in the state of Virginia as a form of post-event support of the state of mind of principal Nolte at the time. On the civil rights cases, Defendants again reiterate the lack of evidence of animus regarding the gun search and report to law enforcement.

 

Defendants also present supplemental deposition transcripts from Thea Perkins, an August 15, 2023, plea deal involving a person identified as Deja Taylor in the state of Virginia, and the news report regarding the six year old shooter. The court declines to consider the subject supplemental exhibits given it deprives Plaintiffs of the opportunity to respond to the argument, and it’s not clear how a shooting occurring after the subject incident in any way reflects on the merits of the subject action for purposes of ruling on the motion.

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)”  (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Ibid.) 

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.” (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

1st Cause of Action, Negligence: Continued for Supplemental Briefing

2nd Cause of Action, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention: Continued for Supplemental Briefing.

3rd Cause of Action, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress: Continued for Supplemental Briefing.

Defendants moves for summary adjudication on grounds that the conduct of Gina Nolte, principal of Tesoro Elementary School in reporting potential criminal conduct to law enforcement constitutes an action protected by litigation privilege thereby immunizing defendants from liability in the Negligence, Negligent Supervision, Training and/or Retention, and Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress causes of action.

 

“The litigation privilege in section 47 applies to ‘any communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that have some connection or logical relation to the action. [Citations.]’ [Citation.]” (n (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 28, 37.) The litigation privilege applies to any and all causes of action except malicious prosecution. (Hagberg v. California Federal Bank FSB (2004) 32 Cal.4th 350, 375.) “A plaintiff cannot establish a probability of prevailing if the litigation privilege precludes the defendant's liability on the claim.” (Digerati Holdings, LLC v. Young Money Entertainment, LLC (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 873, 888.) Whether the privilege applies is “a factual question that will require evaluation of plaintiffs’ proffered evidence to determine whether they have made a prima facie showing of their ability to negate these factors.” (Birkner v. Lam (2007) 156 Cal.App.4th 275, 286.)

 

“An absolute privilege exists to protect citizens from the threat of litigation for communications to government agencies whose function it is to investigate and remedy wrongdoing. (Citation.) The privilege is based on ‘[t]he importance of providing to citizens free and open access to governmental agencies for the reporting of suspected illegal activity.’ (Citation.)” (Wise v. Thrifty Payless, Inc. (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 1296, 1303.) “One policy underlying the absolute privilege for statements made in governmental investigations and reports of misconduct ‘is to assure utmost freedom of communication between citizens and public authorities whose responsibility is to investigate and remedy wrongdoing.’ (Citations.) … [¶] … As other courts have stated, ‘there must be an open channel of communication by which citizens can call [the investigator's] attention to suspected wrongdoing.’” (Braun v. Bureau of State Audits (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1382, 1390–1391; Kibler v. Northern Inyo County Local Hospital Dist. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 192, 203; Mann v. Quality Old Time Service, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 328, 335; ComputerXpress, Inc. v. Jackson (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 993, 1009.) “This subdivision does not make privileged any communication between a person and a law enforcement agency in which the person makes a false report that another person has committed, or is in the act of committing, a criminal act or is engaged in an activity requiring law enforcement intervention, knowing that the report is false, or with reckless disregard for the truth or falsity of the report. (Civ. Code, § 47 subd. (b)(5).)

 

Plaintiff concedes to the existence of the privilege, but maintains the standard excludes non-communicative conduct and false reports by “non-victims.” The argument depends on both the separation of the conduct following the call to the Sheriff, whereby Solomon was questioned and searched, as well as a claim of a knowingly false report to the Sheriff. Nolte purportedly searched the bag of Solomon and discovered no weapon of any sort, yet still called the Sheriff to investigate. Plaintiffs additionally contend that because Nolte was a “non-victim” or non-party to any suspected crime and the conduct of reporting to the police was otherwise not part of any mandatory duty, the statutory exception for malicious conduct applies.

 

As provided in the standard for summary judgment/summary adjudication cited above, the court considers the foundational allegations of the three challenged causes of action in order to determine the scope of the argument. Paragraph 32 specifically states that even after searching the backpack and discovering no weapon, Nolte still called law enforcement and the “mental health department,” albeit without any allegation of a false or malicious intent. The second cause of action lacks any additional or new claims, while the third cause of action both incorporates the report allegations, as well as reliance on the allegations of improper isolation/detention of Solomon, thereby causing emotional distress.

 

Nolte states in declaration that while no gun was discovered in the backpack of Solomon, Nolte was unaware of whether Solomon otherwise had potential access to a firearm at home, and therefore called the Sheriff. School safety regulations require the reporting any potential threat of extreme violence. The decision was also based on the purported admission of Solomon regarding his admission to shoot everyone in his class, except for two friends, and confirmation from another classmate. [Declaration of Gina Nolte, ¶¶ 14-21, 23.]

 

Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b)(5) of the statute was added on January 1, 2021. Because the incident occurred in February 2020, it remains unclear whether Defendants seek application of the prior version of the statute though Plaintiffs challenge any assertion of an absolute bar, as addressed in earlier cases considering the statute and/or whether Plaintiff also relies on the malicious intent exception. (Chabak v. Monroy (2007) 154 Cal.App.4th 1502, 1514.)

 

“‘[L]egislative enactments are generally presumed to operate prospectively and not retroactively unless the Legislature expresses a different intention’” (Evangelatos v. Superior Court (1988) 44 Cal.3d 1188, 1208; Myers v. Philip Morris Companies, Inc. (2002) 28 Cal.4th 828, 844 [“[A] statute may be applied retroactively only if it contains express language of retroactivity or if other sources provide a clear and unavoidable implication that the Legislature intended retroactive application”].) “[A] statute's retroactivity is, in the first instance, a policy determination for the Legislature and one to which courts defer absent “some constitutional objection” to retroactivity.” (Myers v. Philip Morris Companies, Inc., supra, 28 Cal.4th at p. 841.) “But it has long been established that a statute that interferes with antecedent rights will not operate retroactively unless such retroactivity be ‘the unequivocal and inflexible import of the terms, and the manifest intention of the legislature.’” (McClung v. Employment Development Dept. (2004) 34 Cal.4th 467, 475.) The court finds no basis for retroactivity, and therefore considers the 2020 version of the statute applicable at the time of the incident, which lacked the malicious conduct exception under subdivision (b)(5).

 

Under the prior standard, the privilege remained absolute. The court also finds the argument for third party/non-victim in no way undermines a finding of immunity for principal Nolte in the capacity of an administrator responsible for reporting safety concerns impacting individual students and staff at a school campus. (Wise v. Thrifty Payless, Inc., supra, 83 Cal.App.4th at p. 1303; Braun v. Bureau of State Audits, supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1390–1391; Kibler v. Northern Inyo County Local Hospital Dist., supra, 39 Cal.4th at p. 203; Mann v. Quality Old Time Service, Inc., supra, 139 Cal.App.4th at p. 335; ComputerXpress, Inc. v. Jackson, supra, 93 Cal.App.4th at p. 1009.) Finally, the court concludes that any argument for exception under such statutes as the Child Abuse and Neglect Reporting Act are not applicable. (Chabak v. Monroy, supra, 154 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1514-1515.)

 

Regardless, the three causes of action allege more than just conduct related to the police and mental health services report. Courts must “draw a careful distinction between a cause of action based squarely on a privileged communication … and one based upon an underlying course of conduct evidenced by the communication.” (White v. Western Title Ins. Co. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 870, 888.) “‘The distinction between communicative and noncommunicative conduct hinges on the gravamen of the action.... [T]he key in determining whether the privilege applies is whether the injury allegedly resulted from an act that was communicative in its essential nature.’ (Citations.) Notably, ‘if the gravamen of the action is communicative, the litigation privilege extends to noncommunicative acts that are necessarily related to the communicative conduct.’ (Citations.) To show that the litigation privilege does not apply, the burden is on the plaintiff to demonstrate that ‘an independent, noncommunicative, wrongful act was the gravamen of the action.’” (Mireskandari v. Gallagher (2020) 59 Cal.App.5th 346, 369 (internal quotation marks omitted).)

 

The motion lacks any address on the differences in articulated conduct underpinning the three challenged causes of action, while Plaintiffs seek to distinguish the conduct based at least on part on the damages claims arising from conduct beyond the phone call itself. As noted above, paragraph 32 of the operative complaint presents a series of claims originating from the report of the fellow second grade student. The student statement triggered a search, phone call, and placement of Solomon into a separate space for questioning by the Sheriff.

 

The intent of the statute provides protection from conduct intended to offer protection from potential reprisal and encourage the reporting of illegal, including threatening, activity. (Wise v. Thrifty Payless, Inc., supra, 83 Cal.App.4th at p. 1303; Braun v. Bureau of State Audits, supra, 67 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1390–1391.) The court interprets the protection narrowly in that while communicative acts (e.g. the reporting) clearly constitutes a protected activity, the privilege in no way shields a party from all potential claims tangentially related damages. (Kimmel v. Goland (1990) 51 Cal.3d 202, 211 [“a review of the myriad cases that have applied section 47(2) to shield defendants from liability demonstrates that, without exception, the privilege has applied only to torts arising from statements or publications”]; Mancini & Associates v. Schwetz (2019) 39 Cal.App.5th 656, 661 [“Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b) applies only to communicative acts and does not apply to tortious courses of conduct’].) In other words, the law interpreting privileged communications in no way excuses other claims of tortious conduct even if the communication initially prompts the pathway.

 

Nevertheless, as also addressed below in consideration of the false imprisonment claim, the court must distinguish between the conduct between principal Nolte and the subsequent conduct of the Sheriff in taking over the questioning. While Plaintiffs rightfully seeks to assert separate damages as a result of the entire incident, such an argument lacks factual distinction between the conduct of principal Nolte and the responding sheriffs. Other than the general claim of a violation of a duty to protect Solomon from “unnecessary law enforcement contact,” which constitutes a separate action beyond the scope of Nolte’s control in that the Sheriff assumed the questioning of Solomon, the conduct essentially constitutes allegations arising from the detention of a student pending a safety investigation and the flowing emotional distress of the incident. Under the gravamen of the complaint standard, the distinction between the search, detention and report to the Sheriff, and the subsequent assumption of the investigation by the Sheriff presents distinctive differences in the conduct between the principal and law enforcement. The court finds the distinction critical in that statutory and case law vests a school administrator with certain rights and privileges in the role of in loco parentis, especially in situations involving student well being and safety.

 

Nevertheless, the motion and reply lacks address of this specific aspect raised in the opposition and expanded upon by the court. The court declines to consider unmade arguments, but also finds the subject motion necessitates further discussion. The issue requires further consideration regarding both the potential extension of the absolute privilege standard to the moment leading to the turnover of Solomon to the Sheriff, as well as other protections arising from Nolte exercising rights as a principal of the school separate and apart from the absolutely privileged conduct of the report. The court will therefore set a supplemental briefing schedule on the issue of the allowable course and scope of conduct by principal Nolte in responding to the potential threat. Defendants may argue for an extension of the absolute privilege and/or consider other forms of potential protections to a school administrator. (Vernonia School Dist. 47J v. Acton (1995) 515 U.S. 646, 655-656; see Mullenix v. Luna (2015) 577 U.S. 7.)

 

4th Cause of Action, Violation of California Education Code sections 200, 201, 220 and 260 – Discrimination on the Basis of Disability: Denied.

Defendants generally move for summary adjudication on grounds of a lack of evidence establishing discriminatory animus on the fourth, fifth, seventh, eighth, and ninth causes of action. Unlike the report and detention claims, the subject causes of action address essentially allege disparate treatment of school resources to students with disabilities. The court addresses the individual causes of action.

 

Defendants specifically challenge the subject cause of action on grounds of insufficient support for the claim under section 220, and lack of an actual legal foundation for liability under sections 200, 201, and 260. Plaintiffs in opposition contends unequal treatment in the form of lack of accommodations for Solomon’s handicap, and an “overreaction” to the gun threat presented in this case.

 

The opposition presents new allegations not pled in the operative complaint. As for the actual allegations in the first amended complaint, in addition to the detention allegations, Plaintiffs essentially plead a failure to provide sufficient resources to Solomon, a handicapped child, and to protect him from bullying students. [First Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 27-29.]

 

The subject cause of action incorporates the prior allegations but otherwise only offers a summary citation of the relied upon sections. Sections 200 and 201 in fact only constitute policy statements regarding equal access and rights for educational opportunities within the state, with section 260 vesting primary authority for ensuring discrimination free schools with local school districts as provided in section 220. “No person shall be subjected to discrimination on the basis of disability, gender, gender identity, gender expression, nationality, race or ethnicity, religion, sexual orientation, or any other characteristic that is contained in the definition of hate crimes set forth in Section 422.55 of the Penal Code, including immigration status, in any program or activity conducted by an educational institution that receives, or benefits from, state financial assistance, or enrolls pupils who receive state student financial aid.” (Ed. Code, § 220.)

 

The court declines to parse out the separate sections and instead considers the sections for the stated public policy intention. In order “to prevail on a claim under section 220 for peer sexual orientation harassment, a plaintiff must show: (1) he or she suffered ‘severe, pervasive and offensive’ harassment, that effectively deprived plaintiff of the right of equal access to educational benefits and opportunities; (2) the school district had ‘actual knowledge’ of that harassment; and (3) the school district acted with ‘deliberate indifference’ in the face of such knowledge.” (Donovan v. Poway Unified School Dist. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 567, 579.)

 

The argument in support of the motion only addresses the allegations regarding the gun report incident, and otherwise offers no address on the claims regarding the failure to provide school resources to Solomon, a known handicapped student. The motion itself therefore fails to completely address and offer a means for complete adjudication the claims presented in the instant cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1).) The court also finds triable issues of material fact on the subject claims via the deposition of Thea Perkins who specifically states to the notice of Solomon as a special needs child, requests for accommodation, and denial. [Declaration of Christian Contreras, Ex. 2-3: Deposition of Thea Perkins, 19:14-19, 24:4-22, 90:3-91:20, 112:9-20.] The motion for summary adjudication is therefore denied.

 

5th Cause of Action, Violation of Equal Protection Clause of the California Constitution: Granted.

Defendants present summary argument regarding the failure of the cause of action due to lack of difference between special education and “regular” student enrollment, as well as a defense of the response to the gun report. Plaintiff maintains that Solomon was part of a protected group as a disabled student, and therefore entitled to equal protection.

 

“‘The first prerequisite to a meritorious claim under the equal protection clause is a showing that the state has adopted a classification that affects two or more similarly situated groups in an unequal manner.’ (Citations.) This initial inquiry is not whether persons are similarly situated for all purposes, but ‘whether they are similarly situated for purposes of the law challenged.’” (Cooley v. Superior Court (2002) 29 Cal.4th 228, 253.)

 

Defendant cites to a case directly addressing special needs students and equal protection. “By definition, ‘special’ and ‘regular’ education students are not situated similarly with respect to the applicable laws. As the complaint itself pleads, special education students require special services including specially trained teachers, more teachers and instructional aides and mental health services that regular students do not need. And, as we explained at length in an earlier case, the purpose of the federal special educational laws was to help states satisfy their responsibilities under equal protection principles to provide adequate education to disabled children.” (Grossmont Union High School Dist. v. State Dept. of Education (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 869, 892.)

 

Plaintiffs cite to Education Code section 66270. The section states in relevant part: “No person shall be subjected to discrimination on the basis of disability ... in any program or activity conducted by any postsecondary educational institution that receives, or benefits from, state financial assistance or enrolls students who receive state student financial aid.” (Ed. Code, § 66270.) The court finds the application to postsecondary institutions renders the section inapposite to the subject cause of action.

 

It remains undisputed that Solomon was an identified special needs student. Nothing in the recitation of equal protection language in any way establishes a lack of equal protection as a special needs student relative to the regular student population as a matter of law. No triable issues of material fact exist in that Plaintiffs lack any evidence or supporting legal argument of a lack of equal protection as a special needs student simply based on the alleged improper withholding of resources accorded for special needs students. The motion for summary adjudication is therefore granted.

 

6th Cause of Action, False Imprisonment: Granted.

Defendants challenge the subject cause of action on grounds that principal Nolte remains effectively legally immune from a false imprisonment claim, due to the right of a school administrator to detain and interview a student without the consent of a parent. Plaintiff counters that the detention was for an effectively unlawful purpose because Nolte was already aware that Solomon lacked a weapon in his possession upon completion of the search. Plaintiff disputes any right to detain a student by a school administrator. Plaintiff then proceeds to accuse principal Nolte of engaging in a form of racial profiling against black students like Solomon.

 

Plaintiffs’ argument somewhat misstates the actual alleged conduct by describing the detention of Solomon as taking him into “custody,” which Plaintiffs then seek to invoke the provisions of Welfare and Institutions Code section 625 regarding a law enforcement officer taking effectively arresting a juvenile. Nothing in the operative complaint even uses the word custody. The plain language of paragraph 61 only states “unlawful restraint and confinement.” Principal Nolte called Solomon into the office, which was then followed up by the Sheriff deputies response from the phone call. The court specifically decouples the course of conduct between the action of principal Nolte, and the Sheriffs presence. The conduct of the Sheriff deputy/ies is not at issue in the subject motion. Nevertheless, given the claim of improper custody and the potential invocation of Fourth Amendment argument, the court considers the argument under this claim.

 

A public school official exercises a certain amount of discretion in loco parentis without the need for obtaining a warrant or otherwise in violation of the Fourth Amendment. (Vernonia School Dist. 47J v. Acton (1995) 515 U.S. 646, 655-656.) Plaintiffs concede this point and rely on Education Code section 44807 as a means of ascribing the conduct of principal Nolte as arbitrary and unreasonable under the circumstances. “A teacher, vice principal, principal, or any other certificated employee of a school district, shall not be subject to criminal prosecution or criminal penalties for the exercise, during the performance of his duties, of the same degree of physical control over a pupil that a parent would be legally privileged to exercise but which in no event shall exceed the amount of physical control reasonably necessary to maintain order, protect property, or protect the health and safety of pupils, or to maintain proper and appropriate conditions conducive to learning. ...” (Ed. Code, § 44807.)

 

The argument under Education Code section 44807 and for improper custody dovetails into consideration of qualified immunity. “The doctrine of qualified immunity shields officials from civil liability so long as their conduct ‘does not violate clearly established statutory or constitutional rights of which a reasonable person would have known.’ (Citations.) A clearly established right is one that is ‘sufficiently clear that every reasonable official would have understood that what he is doing violates that right.’ (Citation.) ‘We do not require a case directly on point, but existing precedent must have placed the statutory or constitutional question beyond debate.’ (Citation). Put simply, qualified immunity protects “all but the plainly incompetent or those who knowingly violate the law.’ (Citation.)” (Mullenix v. Luna, supra, 577 U.S. at pp. 11–12 (internal quotation marks omitted).)

 

The court finds no basis for a determination of unreasonableness in that the statute provides plenary authority for the protection and welfare of students and staff on a school campus. The detention itself, even if no weapon was found on Solomon, in no way reasonably ended the inquiry given concern for potential access to a weapon at the home of Solomon, and school regulatory requirements requiring inquiry upon the determination of potential harm to the student or others. [Nolte Decl., ¶¶ 17-21, 31-32; see Contreras Declaration, Ex. 2: Deposition of Thea Perkins, 40:1-6.] Furthermore and again, principal Nolte only detained Solomon until the Sheriff arrived and effectively took over the duration of the questioning period. Ms. Perkins was also present during at least part of the period with the Sheriffs as well, which, again, constitutes a separate period of time in terms of the ability to exert a form of custody held by the law enforcement officer.

 

Again, to the extent the complaint depends on a claim of improper “custody,” the court finds no triable issues of material fact regarding the conduct of principal Nolte in the detention of Solomon pending the arrival of the Sheriff as part of any larger civil rights deprivation or civil liability claim. (Mullenix v. Luna, supra, 577 U.S. at pp. 11–12.) The court finds mere argument based on description of the circumstances insufficiently overcomes the standard of a reasonable person (e.g. a school administrator) adhering to a set of established guidelines for student safety and encompassed within state standards for school administration. To the extent Nolte acted on behalf of the school district, the court finds no basis of liability against Saugus Union High School District. The motion for summary adjudication is therefore granted.

 

7th Cause of Action, Violation of Americans with Disability Act: Denied.

Beginning with the fourth cause of action, the court again returns to the statutory rights based claims. Defendant solely challenges the subject cause of action on grounds that the detention of Solomon lacks any evidence of racial motivation. Plaintiffs in opposition counters that the claim arises from the failure to accommodate Solomon, a student with disabilities.

 

“A plaintiff bringing suit under section 504 or Title II of the ADA must show: (1) she is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) she was denied “a reasonable accommodation that [she] needs in order to enjoy meaningful access to the benefits of public services;” and (3) the program providing the benefit receives federal financial assistance.” (A.G. v. Paradise Valley Unified School Dist. No. 69 (9th Cir. 2016) 815 F.3d 1195, 1204.) “An organization that receives federal funds violates § 504 if it denies a qualified individual with a disability a reasonable accommodation that the individual needs in order to enjoy meaningful access to the benefits of public services.” (Mark H. v. Hamamoto (9th Cir. 2010) 620 F.3d 1090, 1097.) “[T]o prevail on a claim for damages under section 504 and Title II, ‘plaintiffs must prove a mens rea of “intentional discrimination” ... [and] that standard may be met by showing “deliberate indifference,” ... not only by showing ‘discriminatory animus.’” (A.G. v. Paradise Valley Unified School Dist. No. 69, supra, 815 F.3d at p. 1204.)

 

Paragraphs 64-73 categorically identify Solomon as a disabled person and the withholding of resources towards educational needs. The actions were done with indifference. Nothing in the subject cause of action in any way identifies the underlying search and detention incident. On this basis alone, the court finds Defendants fail to sufficiently address the facts of the subject cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1), (p)(2).) Triable issues of material fact exist regarding the withholding of benefits appearing as a form of “deliberate indifference” to the needs of Solomon given the requests of Thea Perkins. [Nolte Decl., ¶¶ 39-41; Thea Perkins Depo.] The motion for summary adjudication is denied.

 

8th Cause of Action, Violation of California Government Code section 11135 – Discrimination Based on Disability: Denied.

Defendants challenge the subject cause of action on grounds that Plaintiffs failed to exhaust administrative remedies and fail to justify the need for equitable relief. Plaintiffs counter that administrative remedies were exhausted, and Plaintiffs properly seek injunctive relief enjoining further discrimination.

 

The parties lack any dispute over the standard utilized for the ADA violations. Other than a terse summary of the law and concluding arguments, the motion itself lacks any actual factual or specific legal support regarding the failure to exhaust administrative remedies or the insufficiency of equitable remedies. The court declines to make the arguments for Defendants. The motion for summary adjudication is denied for failure to sufficiently address the standard. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1), (p)(2).)

 

9th Cause of Action, Violation of 42 USC section 1983 – Violation of the Equal Protection Clause: Denied.

Defendants challenge the argument by citation to the standard, then transition to an argument of qualified immunity for any and all actions arising from the search and detention of Solomon. Plaintiff in opposition also cites to the standard, and denies the application of qualified immunity in the underlying incident.

 

Paragraph 83 of the operative complaint itself in fact relies on the withholding of school resources, and it remains unclear how the search and detention incident is part of the vaguely pled claim. Plaintiff in fact incorporates the arguments regarding discrimination based on the wrongful withholding of resources to Solomon. Again, the court declines to consider unmade arguments, or make the case for Defendants. The court therefore denies the motion on the failure to address the gravamen of the claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (f)(1), (p)(2).)

 

As addressed above, the court invites supplemental briefing on the first, second and third causes of action regarding the extension of the litigation privilege and/or any other potentially applicable immunity. VEXATIOUS LITIGANT

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants, Jennifer Craig-Ford, et al.

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Margarita Carranza, pro per

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Designate Plaintiff Margarita Carranza a Vexatious Litigant

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Defendants Martinian & Associates, et al. represented Plaintiff Margrarita Carranza in an action against the County of Los Angeles, et al. for Negligence, Government Tort Liability, and Premises Liabiity (19STCV05567). According to Plaintiff the case settled for $50,000, which led to a (contingency recovery) fee of $25,000. Plaintiff expected a net payment of $25,000, but when presented with the final check, additional “expenses” were also deducted, thereby leading to a net payment of “20K.” The expenses included medical “liens,” which Plaintiff contends were never “authorized.”

 

On September 29, 2022, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed a complaint for Breach of Contract and General Negligence. On January 12, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed a first amended complaint for “Attorney Misconduct.” The first amended complaint contains a conformed copy of a December 8, 2022, filed “Request to Re-Open Complaint,” as well as a Breach of Contract, General Negligence, and Fraud attachments.

 

On March 15, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer of defendants to the first amended complaint for “Attorney Misconduct” with 30 days leave to amend. On May 12, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed a second amended complaint for Fraud though the caption identifies additional causes of action. On May 18, 2023, Plaintiff, in pro per, filed an “addendum” to the second amended complaint again with only the fraud cause of action attachment and additional exhibits, plus new allegations. 

 

On July 6, 2023, Plaintiff filed eight (8) requests for dismissals for the following defendants: Robert Yaspan, Brian Kuhn, Fennia Hakobyan, Sevana Vatazarian, Philip Craft, Ronald Whittaker, Jonathan Berger, and D.A. Lucas.

 

RULING: Continued

Request for Judicial Notice: Granted.

The court takes judicial notice of the existence of the court filings and court docket for purposes of establishing the existence of the purported conduct, but cannot take judicial notice of the content of any and all items for the truth of the matter asserted.

 

Defendants Martinian & Associates  and Tigran Martinian move to designate plaintiff Margrarita Carranza, in pro per, a vexatious litigant. Defendants summarize the basis for the request based on continued e-mails to defendants even following a stipulation to limit or stop said emails; “frivolous” motions to reopen a dismissed action (19STCV05567); Addition of Defendants as parties to a separate lawsuit (22STCV17802); the prosecution of two other lawsuits (22STCV06945 & 22STCV13981), whereby Plaintiff seeks to relate the subject cases to the instant action; and, a general statement of “abusive” tactics to “bully” and/or “annoy” Defendants and counsel.

 

Plaintiff in opposition requests the court “dismiss” the motion. Plaintiff reiterates the basis of “fraud” in the disbursement of the settlement proceeds. Plaintiff also addresses the disposition of two cases apparently as an effort to establish the validity of the complaints.

 

Defendants in reply first notes the unserved, late filed opposition to the motion. Defendants then reiterate the argument regarding the conflation of cases and systematic efforts to relitigate the subject action.

 

“Except as provided in subdivision (b) of Section 391.3, when a motion pursuant to Section 391.1 is filed prior to trial the litigation is stayed, and the moving defendant need not plead, until 10 days after the motion shall have been denied, or if granted, until 10 days after the required security has been furnished and the moving defendant given written notice thereof. When a motion pursuant to Section 391.1 is made at any time thereafter, the litigation shall be stayed for such period after the denial of the motion or the furnishing of the required security as the court shall determine.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 391.6.)

 

Due to impacted court calendars as a result of inventory transfer included highly contested actions such as this one, and the court resource intensive nature of the instant motion, the court continues the hearing to March 4, 2024. The court shows a number of reserved items, with only two items actually scheduled: an OSC re Failure to File Proofs of Service and a Case Management Conference for January 9, 2024. No trial date is set, and the disposition of the action may change depending on the outcome of the instant hearing. The court finds no prejudice to the parties in continuing the hearing. The court therefore vacates the two hearings pending the March 4, 2024, hearing on the instant motion.

 

The case is therefore stayed. Nothing in this order in any bars any action on any and all of the unrelated cases, or provides any form of injunctive relief against social media use, or email communications.

 

Defendants to give notice.
