DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES, COSTS, AND EXPENSES AND DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO TAX COSTS

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: January 7, 2025

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 10/29/2021

TRIAL: None set

 

 

MOVING PARTY:              Plaintiff Augusto Barco-Robledo

RESPONDING PARTY:     Defendant General Motors, LLC      

Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs, Motion to Tax Costs

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers and supporting declarations filed in connection with these motions.

Discussion

Plaintiff Augusto Barco-Robledo (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order awarding attorney’s fees, costs and expenses in his favor, to be paid by defendant General Motors, LLC (“Defendant”), in the total amount of $95,255.36, consisting of (1) $89,267.25 in attorney’s fees after applying a multiplier of 1.5 to base fees of $56,511.50, and (2) $5,988.11 in costs. 

First, the court finds that Plaintiff is entitled to recover attorney’s fees from Defendant pursuant to the Song-Beverly Act.  Civil Code section 1794, subdivision (d), provides:¿ “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.”¿¿¿ 

Plaintiff has presented evidence showing, and Defendant does not dispute, that on March 5, 2024, the parties arrived at a settlement agreement which stipulated that Plaintiff would be deemed the prevailing party so that Plaintiff could seek attorney’s fees and costs by motion. (Crandall Decl. ¶ 24.) 

Second, the court finds, as to the attorney’s fees incurred by Plaintiff in connection with the commencement and prosecution of this action and the preparation of this fee motion, that Plaintiff has established a lodestar amount of $54,769.50.

“[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted); Reck v. FCA US LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 682, 691 [“To determine a reasonable attorney fee award, the trial court applies the lodestar method”].) 

The prevailing party is entitled to “’compensation for all the hours reasonably spent” in litigating the action to a successful conclusion. (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc.¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 821.) ‘Reasonably spent’ means that time spent ‘in the form of inefficient or duplicative efforts is not subject to compensation.’ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 394.)

“[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)  However, the court may rely on his or her own experience and is given broad discretion in calculating reasonable attorney’s fees. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132 ["The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in his court...”].)

The lodestar may be augmented or diminished “by taking various relevant factors into account including (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved and the skill displayed in presenting them; (2) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys; and (3) the contingent nature of the¿¿fee award, based on the uncertainty of prevailing on the merits and of establishing eligibility for the award.” (Ibid.) The multiplier is a risk enhancement based on the probability of loss. (Robertson v. Fleetwood Travel Trailers of California, Inc.¿(2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 785, 821 (Robertson).)

Plaintiff requests that the court take judicial notice of thirteen orders in Superior Court proceedings unrelated to this action, and excerpts of the United States Consumer Law Attorney Fee Survey Report for 2017-2018.  Factual findings in prior judicial orders, opinions, or decisions are not a proper subject of judicial notice. (Kilroy v. State of Calif. (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 140, 148.)  Nor does the court take judicial notice of matters not relevant to the proceeding.  As a result, the court denies Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice in its entirety.

However, Plaintiff has submitted the declaration of attorney Sean Crandall, in which he has attested to the qualifications, skill, and experience of the attorneys that worked on this action.  (Crandall Decl., ¶¶ 27, 47-50.)  The court finds that the hourly rates requested are reasonable in light of counsel’s qualifications and experience.  (Ibid.)

Defendant opposes the motion, contending that Plaintiff’s counsel’s claimed fees are unreasonable because many of the claimed hours are unnecessary, padded, and unsupported.  Defendant also contends a multiplier is unwarranted because the case is not complex, the case did not prevent Plaintiff’s counsel from taking on other clients, and Plaintiff’s counsel’s rates already take the contingent risk of Song-Beverly cases into account. Defendant also moved to tax $1,051.55 of Plaintiff’s expenses, claiming they were unnecessary.  

The court finds that this case was a routine Song-Beverly case that did not involve novel issues, extraordinary risk, or protracted litigation, and therefore does not warrant a lodestar multiplier. Plaintiff’s reliance upon Defendant’s discovery tactics and sanctions imposed against it to justify application of a multiplier is not persuasive as those facts are not relevant to the factors set forth in Robertson, particularly where substantial monetary sanctions were already imposed against Defendant.  Plaintiff’s request for a 1.5x multiplier is denied.

Upon the court’s review of counsel’s declarations, the court finds that the following hourly rates are reasonable: $425.00 to $500.00 per hour for attorney Sean Crandall; $435 to $520 per hour for attorney Carey Wood; $175 to $200 per hour for paralegal Clarence Serrano; and $200 to 210 per hour for paralegal Alexis Valdovinos.

The court has reviewed each entry on the billing statement submitted by Plaintiff’s counsel, Defendant’s opposition papers, and its objections to specific billing entries. Based on the court’s review of the billing statement and Defendant’s challenges thereto, the court finds that:

(1) Of the total 2 hours spent by Clarence Serrano on April 12 and 13, 2022 to make calls to the court clerk to schedule IDC, 2 of those hours were secretarial and not reasonably incurred at a rate of $175, for a reduction of $350.00;

(2) Of the 1 hour spent by Alexis Valdovinos on November 29, 2023 to review emails and update the case with respect to FSC and trial dates, 0.5 of those hours were not reasonably incurred at a rate of $200, for a reduction of $100.00;

(3) Of the 2.7 hours spent by Sean Crandall on January 19 and 20, 2022 to review repair history and draft defect definitions, 1.2 of those hours were not reasonably incurred at a rate of $425, for a reduction of $510.00;

(4) Of the 1.1 total hours spent by Sean Crandall on October 12, 2023 to review the repair chronology, 0.6 of those hours were not reasonably incurred at a rate of $470, for a reduction of $282.00;

(5) Of the 6 total additional hours anticipated to be spent by Sean Crandall on the motion for attorney’s fees and motion to tax costs, 1 of those hours is not reasonably incurred at a rate of $500, for a reduction of $500.00.

The court therefore finds that after reducing the requested fees by a total of $1,742.00 as described above, Plaintiff has established, as to the attorney’s fees incurred to commence and prosecute this action and incurred in connection with this fee motion and the motion to tax costs, a lodestar amount of $54,769.50.

Plaintiff also seeks $5,988.11 in costs and expenses. For purposes of the Song-Beverly statute, expenses encompass out-of-pocket expenses beyond the costs identified in Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5.  (Smalley v. Subaru of Am., Inc. (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 450, 457, as modified.)  “In ruling upon a motion to tax costs, the trial court's first determination is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face. ‘If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show [the costs] to be unnecessary or unreasonable.’ [Citation]. Where costs are not expressly allowed by the statute, the burden is on the party claiming the costs to show that the charges were reasonable and necessary. [Citation].” (Foothill-De Anza Community College Dist. v. Emerich¿(2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 11, 29–30.)

Defendant seeks to tax $152.50 for jury fees from the memorandum of costs, arguing that the case never went to trial and that the cost of jury fees are therefore not properly compensable. However, under Code of Civil Procedure section 631, payment of a nonrefundable jury fee is required to avoid waiver of the right to a jury trial. Posting jury fees was therefore a necessity for Plaintiff to preserve his right to a jury trial prior to settlement. Defendant has therefore failed to carry its burden to show that these costs were unreasonable and unnecessary.

Defendant seeks to tax $308.95 in costs associated with Plaintiff’s motions to compel. Defendant argues that the motions were one-sided and unnecessary. However, the motions were granted, which indicates that the motions and its associated costs were reasonable and necessary. (Crandall Decl. Exs. 2-5.) Defendant argues that once a party has properly objected to costs, the burden shifts to the party presenting costs to show that the costs were reasonable and necessary. However, “[i]f the items appearing in a cost bill pursuant to Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5 (costs recoverable by prevailing party), appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that the costs were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto Ass’n (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 763.) Merely contending that costs were unreasonable and unnecessary is insufficient to shift the burden to Plaintiff. Defendant has therefore failed to carry its burden to show that these costs were unreasonable and unnecessary.

Defendant seeks to tax $108.00 in service of process costs and subpoenas related to serving non-party deposition notices from the memorandum of costs. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1033.5(a)(4), service of process by a public officer, registered process server is recoverable as costs. Defendant argues that the non-parties deposed had their own counsel and were not associated with Defendant, and that the work done did not advance Plaintiff’s case and benefited only Plaintiff’s counsel. In opposition, Plaintiff contends that “the two subpoenas that Defendant identifies as objectionable are deposition subpoenas for the Person Most Qualified of Felix Chevrolet and Technician 4909 of Felix Chevrolet. Felix Chevrolet is the dealership where Plaintiff’s vehicle was repaired and Technician 4909 was a technician who performed repairs on Plaintiff’s vehicle.” (Opp. Strike Costs, p. 5.) Plaintiff argues that the depositions were therefore reasonably incurred in connection with the commencement and prosecution of the action. The Court finds that the costs were reasonably necessary to the conduct of the litigation.

Defendant seeks to tax $482.80 for courtesy copy delivery and minute order retrieval, arguing that courtesy copies are unnecessary, unreasonable, and excessive. Plaintiff contends that these costs primarily arose from the lodging of sealed, confidential documents directly with the court, as required by the parties’ protective order. Defendant has not established that the expense of serving courtesy copies to the court is unreasonable.

Based on the foregoing, the court denies Defendant’s motion to tax costs and grants in part Plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees as follows:

The court orders that Plaintiff Augusto Barco-Robledo shall recover from Defendant General Motors, LLC a total of $60,757.61, consisting of $54,769.50 in attorney’s fees and $5,988.11 in costs and expenses, pursuant to Civil Code § 1794, subdiv. (d).  Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

1.     Background

This is a lemon law action. Plaintiff Pedro Mauricio Mendoza (“Plaintiff”) alleges in the complaint that, on January 30, 2021, Plaintiff purchased a 2020 Chevrolet Silverado 1500 with Vehicle Identification Number 3GCPWCET1LG439113 (the “Vehicle”), manufactured and/or distributed by defendant General Motors LLC (“Defendant”). The complaint alleges that the Vehicle contained or developed defects after Plaintiff took possession of the Vehicle. The parties settled the matter on April 17, 2024.

2.     Discussion

          On September 5, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion to tax costs, contending that $917.85 out of the $1,542.74 requested in costs are not properly compensable and should be struck from the memorandum of costs. Defendant argues that costs of $167.29 for jury fees, $61.65 in filing costs and $623.91 in “[o]ther…: Network Deposition Services Court Reporter” costs, and $65.00 in “Notice of Name Extension” are inappropriate.

          On December 23, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion, contending that the requested costs and expenses are reasonable and properly compensable and that Defendant failed to meet its burden to show that the requests costs and expenses were not reasonable or necessary. Plaintiff additionally filed a declaration containing invoices supporting the challenged costs and requested attorney’s fees in connection with the instant motion.

“In ruling upon a motion to tax costs, the trial court's first determination is whether the statute expressly allows the particular item and whether it appears proper on its face. ‘If so, the burden is on the objecting party to show [the costs] to be unnecessary or unreasonable.’ [Citation]. Where costs are not expressly allowed by the statute, the burden is on the party claiming the costs to show that the charges were reasonable and necessary. [Citation].” (Foothill-De Anza Community College Dist. v. Emerich¿(2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 11, 29–30.)

Defendant seeks to tax $167.29 for jury fees from the memorandum of costs, arguing that the case never went to trial and that the cost of jury fees are therefore not properly compensable. However, under Code of Civil Procedure section 631, payment of a nonrefundable jury fee is required to avoid waiver of the right to a jury trial. Posting jury fees was therefore a necessity for Plaintiff to preserve their right to a jury trial prior to settlement. Defendant has therefore failed to carry their burden to show that these costs were unreasonable and unnecessary.

 

Defendant seeks to tax $61.65 in court filing fees and $623.91 in deposition court reporter costs associated with Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant argues that the motion practice was one-sided and unnecessary and was denied in part. However, the court’s minute order filed on August 27, 2024 indicates that the motion was granted as to all but one request and granted but narrowed as to the final request, which indicates that the motion and its associated costs were reasonable and necessary. Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a)(3), transcribing necessary depositions is allowable as a cost.

Defendant argues that once a party has properly objected to costs, the burden shifts to the party presenting costs to show that the costs were reasonable and necessary. However, “[i]f the items appearing in a cost bill pursuant to Code Civ. Proc. section 1033.5 (costs recoverable by prevailing party), appear to be proper charges, the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show that the costs were not reasonable or necessary.” (Ladas v. California State Auto Ass’n (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 761, 763.) Merely contending that costs were unreasonable and unnecessary is insufficient to shift the burden to Plaintiff. Defendant has therefore failed to carry their burden to show that these costs were unreasonable and unnecessary.

Defendant seeks to tax $65 in “Notice of Name Extension” costs, arguing that these are considered business overhead costs and are unrelated to the present case, and are therefore not properly compensable. However, these are court filing fees, as indicated by the invoices that Plaintiff attached to the memorandum of costs, and under Code of Civil Procedure section 1033.5, subdivision (a)(1), court filing fees are properly compensable as costs. Plaintiff also argues that these costs were associated with Plaintiff’s previous motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant does not address this argument. Defendant has therefore failed to carry their burden to show that these costs were unreasonable and unnecessary.

Plaintiff requests attorney’s fees necessary to respond to the instant motion in the amount of $1,701, based on attorney Lara Rogers’ hourly rate of $415 and attorney Carey Wood’s hourly rate of $525. Plaintiff is the prevailing party in this Song-Beverly Act action and is therefore entitled to recover attorney’s fees reasonably incurred in connection with the commencement and prosecution of the action. (Civ. Code § 1794(d).) Plaintiff bases this request upon the 2.4 hours spent drafting the opposition to the instant motion and an expected $700 incurred to review Defendant’s reply and attend the hearing. Plaintiff provides billing records showing that 2.3 hours were spent by Ms. Rogers and 0.1 hours were spent by Mr. Wood. The court finds this amount unreasonable and reduces the hours spent by Ms. Rogers by 0.4, totaling 1.9 hours spent by Ms. Rogers and 0.1 hours spent by Mr. Wood and a subtotal of $841. It is unclear how Plaintiff arrived at the expected $700 incurred to review Defendant’s reply and attend the hearing. The court will therefore award 1 hour for these tasks at $415 per hour for a total of $1,256.00.

3.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court DENIES Defendant’s motion to tax costs. The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees in the amount of $1,256.00.

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION OF DEFENDANT

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: January 7, 2025

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT FILED: 02/17/2023

TRIAL: 04/07/2025

 

Moving Party:        Plaintiff R.B.

Opposing Party:    Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District

1.     Background

This is a childhood sexual assault revival action filed pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 340.1. Plaintiff R.B. (“Plaintiff”) alleges in the complaint that, when he was a student at Gompers Middle School within defendant Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant”), he was sexually assaulted by Robert Crumb, a teacher employed at Gompers Middle School.  The complaint alleges causes of action against to Defendant for negligence and negligent hiring, retention, and supervision.

          Plaintiff filed the instant motion to compel deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”) after Defendant failed to appear to a deposition set for October 31, 2024. Plaintiff contends that Defendant objected to the scheduled deposition date without providing alternative dates and that Defendant claimed that no PMK exists for the categories noticed in the Notice of Deposition despite the fact that Plaintiff’s counsel has previously taken depositions of Defendant’s PMK on the noticed categories in different matters.

The deposition categories noticed by Plaintiff are:

“1. Any and all complaints against PERPETRATOR while he was employed by YOU. 2. Any and all investigations into allegations PERPETRATOR committed CHILDHOOD SEXUAL ASSAULT while he was employed by YOU. 3. Any and all discipline imposed on PERPETRATOR by YOU while he was employed by YOU. 4. PERPETRATOR’S employment with YOU.”

          After objecting to Plaintiff’s first PMK deposition notice, Defendant agreed to produce a witness at a mutually agreeable time.  (Rineer Dec., ¶ 3.)  Nonetheless, Defendant subsequently objected to the second noticed deposition based again upon the unilaterally selected date, and also contending that it does not have a PMK on any of the four deposition categories because Defendant has been unable to find Robert Crumb’s employment file, outside of a few microfiched pages containing minimal information.  (Id., Ex. 4.)  Plaintiff noticed a third PMK deposition, for which Defendant failed to appear or produce a PMK.  Defendant opposes the motion on the basis that it has no PMK on the four PMK categories, because it is unable to locate Robert Crumb’s employment file.  (Id., Ex. 7.) 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.450, where a party or party-affiliated deponent fails to appear for a noticed deposition without having served a valid objection, the noticing party may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony and the production of any document or tangible thing described in the deposition notice. Such a motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450.)

The issue presented here is whether Defendant’s objection to the deposition notice is valid.  Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2025.230, an organization is obligated to present their person most qualified to testify as to noticed deposition categories. If this person lacks knowledge as to the noticed deposition categories, the organization must prepare and inform the designated PMK so that they can adequately give knowledgeable answers on behalf of the organization based on responsive documents. (Maldonado v. Superior Court (2002) 94 Cal.App.4th 1390, 1396 (Maldonado) [“[d]espite the fact that defendant’s counsel was handicapped by the loss of personnel due to defendant’s having filed for bankruptcy, the deponent’s appearance without the necessary documentation and with no further explanation for the deficiency was inexcusable.”].)

          2. Discussion

If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.  (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2025.230.)  When a request for documents is made, the witness or someone in authority is expected to make an inquiry of everyone who might be holding responsive documents or everyone who knows where such documents might be held.  (Maldonado, supra, 94 Cal.App.4th 1396 [a loss of personnel and pending bankruptcy proceedings does not justify appearing at depositions without documents that were readily available, such as a personnel file, or with no explanation as to whether other responsive documents existed or when they would be produced].)  Thus, even if the designated deponent does not have personal knowledge of the subject matter of the deposition, they have an affirmative duty to take reasonable efforts to discover the information, and should be prepared to testify as to the nature and extent of those efforts made by Defendant and the deponent themselves, and to explain any inability to comply.

The court finds that the deposition notice identifies the matters on which examination is requested are described with reasonable particularity.  As a result, upon receipt of Plaintiff’s notice of deposition, Defendant was required to designate an individual to testify on its behalf as to those matters, to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.

Defendant contends that they have no PMK as to the noticed deposition categories because it is unable to locate Robert Crumb’s employment file and therefore cannot inform and then produce a knowledgeable witness. However, Defendant’s reading of the PMK categories is artificially narrow.  Plaintiff does not seek a PMK to testify as to the contents of Robert Crumb’s employment file.  The categories are much broader than that, particularly when viewed alongside the requests for production of documents contained in the deposition notice. 

While Defendant’s difficulty in locating the entirety of Mr. Crumb’s employment file is understandable given the passage of time, it is difficult to conclude or assume that Defendant maintains no records of sexual abuse complaints, investigations into allegations of sexual abuse, discipline of an employee, or an employee’s employment other than those records maintained in the particular employee’s employment file.  Again, given the passage of time it is entirely possible that no other records exist outside of Mr. Crumbs full employment file, but even so Defendant has a duty to make a diligent, reasonable and good faith attempt to search for any such information falling within the PMK categories along with any documents requested in the deposition notice, and to produce one or more persons most knowledgeable to testify as to those efforts and any results, along with the categories included in the deposition notice. 

Moreover, Defendant is, by its own admission, in possession of part of Robert Crumb’s employment file. (Opp. Ex. D.) Although Defendant may or may not be in possession of information as to all noticed categories, and although the information and/or documents Defendant has in its possession, custody or control may not be complete, Defendant is obligated to inform and then produce a knowledgeable witness to the best of its ability, using its readily available responsive documents. (Maldonado, supra, 94 Cal.App.4th at p. 1396.) For example, payroll records referred to by Defendant very well may confirm dates of Mr. Crumb’s employment by Defendant.  As to any information beyond the portion of Mr. Crumb’s employment file that has been located, if Defendant has no additional information its designated PMK must be prepared to testify to that fact under oath, and must further be prepared to describe in detail all efforts undertaken by Defendant and the deponent to ascertain the information.  Plaintiff is entitled to know the extent to which Defendant possesses or does not possess information on the noticed categories in any and all locations under its possession, custody or control, including but not limited to the individual’s employment file, through testimony given under oath.  Assertions by Defendant’s counsel of any purported inability by Defendant to comply with its statutory obligations are simply inadequate.

Based on the foregoing, the court will grant Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s PMK.

3. Sanctions

If a motion to compel is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the noticing party and against the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).) The burden is on the party opposing sanctions to show substantial justification. (Doe v. United States Swimming, Inc. (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1424, 1436.)

With respect to Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions, Defendant contends Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts were inadequate.  A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion. (Code of Civ. Proc. §2016.040.) The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious effort at negotiation and informal resolution of each issue. This rule is designed to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order…’ “Argument is not the same as informal negotiation” (Clement v. Alegre (2009) 177 Cal.App.4th 1277, 1293-1294, citing Townsend v. Superior Court (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1431 1435, 1437.) Attempting informal resolution means more than the mere attempt by the discovery proponent “to persuade the objector of the error of his ways.” (Townsend, supra at p. 1435). A reasonable and good faith attempt at informal resolution entails something more than bickering with [opposing] counsel.... (Id.) Rather, the law requires that counsel attempt to talk the matter over, compare their views, consult, and deliberate.” (Id. at p. 1439.)

 Plaintiff’s meet and confer efforts were adequate. Plaintiff’s July 22, 2024 letter and October 16, 2024 letter each demanded that Defendant produce a PMK on the noticed categories, noting that Defendant has an obligation to educate a witness on Plaintiff’s categories of inquiry even where witnesses have no knowledge and requesting dates and times of availability from Defendant. Plaintiff’s counsel supported her position with applicable legal authorities.  (Mot. Ex. 6; Opp. Ex. C.) Plaintiff met his burden to meet and confer in good faith, as although Plaintiff was unwilling to negotiate on the existence of a PMK, there simply was no room to negotiate between Plaintiff’s and Defendant’s positions on the issue.

Conversely, Defendant repeatedly failed to provide dates of availability, even after agreeing to produce a witness, and subsequently insisted that no PMK could be produced because none exists for the noticed categories. Defendant failed to meet and confer in good faith, and failed to provide any legal authority supporting its position that it had no duty to comply with Code of Civ. Proc. § 2025.230, including in opposition to this motion. 

Plaintiff requests $3,200 in sanctions, based on attorney Allegra Rineer’s hourly rate of $450, comprised of 5.5 hours preparing the motion and reply, reviewing the opposition and attending the hearing, and $724.00 in costs.  The court finds counsel’s hourly rate reasonable, and awards monetary sanctions in the total amount of $2,700.00

4.  Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable. Defendant is ordered to produce one or more persons most knowledgeable to testify to the subject matters identified in the deposition notice, and to testify as to Defendant’s ability or inability to comply with the document requests contained in the notice, as well as to the efforts by the deponent(s) and Defendant to comply with those requests.  The deposition shall commence on or before February 7, 2025. If the parties are unable to agree by January 13, 2025 on a mutually convenient date, then not later than 5:00 p.m. on January 13, 2025 Defendant shall provide Plaintiff with three alternate deposition dates from which Plaintiff shall choose to proceed. 

The court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of amount of $2,500.00 against Defendant Los Angeles Unified School District and Rudy Ruiz, Esq.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTIONS TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, SET TWO; FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET FOUR; AND REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION, SET THREE

 

Dept. A

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT FILED: 03/12/2021

TRIAL: 01/13/2025

 

 

This is a discrimination, harassment, and retaliation action. Plaintiff Kareemah Mateen-Bradford (“Plaintiff”) alleges that defendant City of Compton (“Defendant”) placed Plaintiff on involuntary administrative leave in retaliation for a lawsuit that Plaintiff filed against Defendant on August 6, 2012.

The Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) alleges causes of action for retaliation in violation of FEHA; discrimination in violation of FEHA; failure to engage in a good faith interactive process; failure to accommodate; harassment in violation of FEHA; failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, or retaliation; and whistleblower retaliation.

Plaintiff has filed motions to compel Defendant to provide further responses to written discovery, including Requests for Production (Set Two), Requests for Admissions (Set Three) and Form Interrogatories (Set Four).  Plaintiff requested monetary sanctions in connection with each motion.  Defendant did not oppose the motions. 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2024.020, subdivision (a), any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day before the trial for the action.  Here, Plaintiff filed the instant motions on December 11, 2024, with a hearing set for January 7, 2025, the day after the scheduled Final Status Conference. Trial in this action is set for January 13, 2025. As January 7, 2025 is only six days before trial, rather than on or before the 15th day before trial, the hearing is set impermissibly close to trial in violation of § 2024.020.

Based on the foregoing, the court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production, Set Two. The court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Form Interrogatories, Set Four. The court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Admission, Set Three.
