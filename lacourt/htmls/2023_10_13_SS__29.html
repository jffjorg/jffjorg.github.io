DEPARTMENT 29 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 29 - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS IMPORTANT  (PLEASE SEND YOUR E-MAIL TO DEPT. 29 NOT DEPT. 2)

Communicating with the Court Staff re the Tentative Ruling 1. Please notify the courtroom staff by email not later than 9:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion. The email address is SSCDEPT29@lacourt.org. Please do not use any other email address. 2. You must include the other parties on the email by "cc." 3. Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the Subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear at the hearing and argue the motions. THE COURT WILL HEAR ARGUMENT UNLESS BOTH SIDES SUBMIT ON THE TENTATIVE.  4. Include the words "SUBMISSION BUT WILL APPEAR" if you submit, but one or both parties will nevertheless appear. 5. For other communications with Court Staff a. OFF-CALENDAR should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed to have a matter placed off-calendar. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) date of proceeding. b. CASE SETTLED should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have agreed that the case has settled for all purposes. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) whether notice of settlement/dismissal documents have been filed; (c) if (b) has not been done, a date one year from the date of your email which will be a date set by the court for an OSC for dismissal of the case. c. STIPULATION should appear in all caps in the Subject line where all parties have stipulated that a matter before the court can be postponed. All counsel should be cc'ed (and where appropriate parties not represented by counsel) and the body of the email should state: (a) name and case number; (b) what proceeding is agreed to be postponed e.g. Trial, FSC; (c) the agreed-upon future date; (d) whether all parties waive notice if the Court informs all counsel/parties that the agreed-upon date is satisfactory. This communication should be used only for matters that are agreed to be postponed and not for orders shortening time. 6. PLEASE MAKE SURE THAT ALL COMMUNICATIONS WITH COURT STAFF DEAL ONLY WITH SCHEDULING AND ADMINISTRATIVE MATTERS AND DO NOT DISCUSS THE MERITS OF ANY CASE. (UPDATED 6/17/2020) 
IMPORTANT:  In light of the COVID-19 emergency, the Court encourages all parties to appear remotely.  The capacity in the courtroom is extremely limited.  The Court appreciates the cooperation of counsel and the litigants. 
ALSO NOTE:  If the moving party does not contact the court to submit on the tentative and does not appear (either remotely or in person), the motion will be taken off calendar.  THE TENTATIVE RULING WILL NOT BE THE ORDER OF THE COURT.



Case Number: 19STCV23705    Hearing Date: October 13, 2023    Dept: 29

TENTATIVE

 

The three motions to compel are GRANTED. The requests for sanctions are GRANTED in part.

 

Background

 

On July 9, 2019, Plaintiffs Jose Cruz, Reina Andino and Delmis Andino filed a complaint against Defendants Moses Cardona and Robertson’s Ready Mix alleging that Defendant Cardona was allegedly negligently operating a vehicle that collided with Plaintiffs’ vehicle on November 17, 2017. 

 

Legal Standard

 

A party must respond to interrogatories within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd.(a).) If a party to whom interrogatories are directed does not provide a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories. (Id., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel initial responses, and no meet and confer efforts are required. (See id., § 2030.290; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.) Nor must a separate statement be filed. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1345(b)(1).)  In addition, a party who fails to provide a timely response generally waives all objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

When a party moves to compel initial responses to interrogatories, “the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes [the motion], unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).)

In Chapter 7 of the Civil Discovery Act, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010, subdivision (d), defines “[m]isuses of the discovery process” to include “[f]ailing to respond to or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” Where a party or attorney has engaged in misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose a monetary sanction in the amount of “the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.020, subd. (a).)

Discussion

 

Defendant Robertson’s served Plaintiffs Jose Cruz, Reina Andino and Delmis Andino each with Form Interrogatories (Set One). (Kezbar Dec., ¶ 3.) Plaintiffs did not timely serve, and still have not served, responses, despite multiple extensions and Plaintiffs’ reassurances that responses would be served. (Kezbar Dec., ¶¶ 3-14.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s three motions to compel are GRANTED.

 

Defendant Robertson’s requests sanctions in the amount of $1,650 against each Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel. Given the relatively straightforward nature of a motion to compel initial responses to discovery requests, and the economies of scale associated with preparing multiple discovery motions in the same case, the Court awards sanctions in the amount of $510 per motion, calculated as two hours of attorney time on each motion, multiplied by counsel’s billing rate of $225 per hour, plus a $60 filing fee. 

 

Conclusion

 

The Court GRANTS the three motions to compel.

 

Plaintiffs Jose Cruz, Reina Andino, and Delmis Andino are each ORDERED to serve code compliant, verified responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One) within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

Plaintiff Jose Cruz and counsel of record the Halpern Law Firm are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $510 under the Civil Discovery Act to Defendant within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

Plaintiff Reina Andino and counsel of record the Halpern Law Firm are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $510 under the Civil Discovery Act to Defendant within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

Plaintiff Delmis Andino and counsel of record the Halpern Law Firm are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay monetary sanctions in the amount of $510 under the Civil Discovery Act to Defendant within 30 days of notice of this order.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.  

 

 


Case Number: 20STCV47832    Hearing Date: October 13, 2023    Dept: 29

TENTATIVE

 

Defendants Salvador Penatovar and KS Trans Service’s motion for relief from waiver of right to jury trial is GRANTED.

 

Legal Standard

 

CCP section 631 provides that “[a]t least one party demanding a jury on each side of a civil case shall pay a nonrefundable fee of one hundred fifty dollars ($150), unless the fee has been paid by another party on the same side of the case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 631(b).) The fee must be paid before the date scheduled for the initial case management conference in the action. (Id., § 631(c).) If no case management conference is scheduled in a civil action, the fee shall be due no later than 365 calendar days after the filing of the initial complaint. (Id., § 631(c)(2).) A party waives jury trial by failing to timely pay the fee unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee. (Id., § 631(f)(5).)

 

“The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.” (Id., § 631(g).) “A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when [] relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice¿to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver.”¿ (Tesoro del Valle Master Homeowners Assn. v. Griffin¿(2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 619, 638.) Policy favors the court using its discretion to proceed with a jury trial. (Bishop v. Anderson (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 821, 823.) 

 

“Where the right to jury is threatened, the crucial focus is whether any prejudice will be suffered by any party or the court if a motion for relief from waiver is granted. [Citations.] A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when ‘…relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver. [Citations.]’” (Wharton v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 100, 104.) “The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.” (Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 808, 811 [prejudice not shown by need to prepare motions in limine, enlarged exhibits, and jury instructions unless inadequate time also shown].) Rather, the prejudice that must be shown is “prejudice from the granting of relief from waiver not prejudice from the jury trial.” (Winston v. Superior Court (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 600, 602, 603 [fact that it takes longer to try a jury case is not prejudice from granting relief from waiver].) 

 

Discussion

 

Defendants assert their waiver of jury trial was inadvertent and have submitted a declaration from their counsel, Matthew G. Evans of Hosp, Gilbert & Bergsten, to support their motion. Defense counsel attests to the following facts. Defendants’ Answer to the Complaint, filed by Defendants’ then-counsel of record, Clark Hill, LLP, on or about February 17, 2021, requested a jury trial. (Motion, Evans Decl., ¶ 5.) On January 4, 2021, Plaintiff posted jury fees, but Clark Hill, LLP did not during its defense of the case. (Evans Decl., ¶ 6.) On May 4, 2023, defense counsel’s law firm substituted in as counsel, replacing Clark Hill, LLP. (Evans Decl., ¶ 7.) Although the firm was diligent in defending the case, it did not realize that Clark Hill, LLP had not posted jury fees. (Evans Decl., ¶ 7.) On August 10, 2023, the defense counsel’s firm realized that the jury fees had not been posted and, accordingly, immediately posted the jury fees on that date and filed a Notice of Posting Jury Fees. (Evans Decl., ¶ 10.) On August 14, 2023, Plaintiff Robert Bishop, III (“Plaintiff”) served an objection to the notice. (Evans Decl., ¶ 11.) Defendants argue, therefore, that their failure to timely post their jury fees, was the result of their counsels’ mistake or inadvertence.

 

Plaintiff argues that current defense counsel’s declaration does not account for prior defense counsel’s delay and, therefore, without a competent declaration showing mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, the Court should deny the motion.

 

The Court finds good cause to grant the motion for the following reasons.

 

First, since Defendants’ prior counsel included a request for jury trial with Defendants Answer, it appears that Defendants have always intended to proceed through jury trial, lending credence to their argument that the failure to timely post jury fees was the result of inadvertence or mistake.

 

Second, current defense counsel’s declaration shows that his firm was unaware Defendants had not yet paid the jury fees and acted within a reasonable time after substituting in as counsel to post those fees.

 

Third, although a declaration from prior defense counsel would have been helpful, it was not necessary. “Where the right to jury is threatened, the crucial focus is whether any prejudice will be suffered by any party or the court if a motion for relief from waiver is granted. [Citations.] A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when ‘…relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver. [Citations.]’” (Wharton v. Superior Court (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 100, 104.) “The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.” (Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court (1993) 17 Cal.App.4th 808, 811 [prejudice not shown by need to prepare motions in limine, enlarged exhibits, and jury instructions unless inadequate time also shown].) Rather, the prejudice that must be shown is “prejudice from the granting of relief from waiver not prejudice from the jury trial.” (Winston v. Superior Court (1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 600, 602, 603 [fact that it takes longer to try a jury case is not prejudice from granting relief from waiver].) Here, Plaintiff has not shown what prejudice, if any, he will suffer from the granting of relief from waiver. As stated above, it is an abuse of discretion to deny relief from waiver of the right to jury trial where there has been no prejudice¿to the other party or to the court. The Court will not suffer prejudice.

 

Accordingly, the motion is granted.

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants Salvador Penatovar and KS Trans Service’s motion for relief from waiver of right to jury trial is GRANTED.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.  

 

 


Case Number: 22STCV26465    Hearing Date: October 13, 2023    Dept: 29

Tentative¿ 

 

Defendant’s motion is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part.

¿ 

Background¿ 

 

Plaintiff Laurie Scott (“Plaintiff”) alleges that she was injured on March 23, 2022, when she slipped and/or fell on a substance on a floor and/or walkway negligently owned, controlled, operated, and maintained by Defendant Yard House USA, Inc. (sued as The Yard House, L.P.) (“Defendant”) on Shoreline Village Drive in Long Beach, California. 

 

On August 16, 2022, Plaintiff filed her complaint against Defendant and Does 1 through 100. On September 27, 2022, Defendant filed its answer. 

 

On December 21, 2022, Defendant served Plaintiff with a Request for Statement of Damages.  Under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.11, subdivision (b), a response was due within 15 days.  (Rios Decl., Exh. 1.)  Plaintiff served a Statement of Damages in response but it did not state any amounts; instead, on the mandatory Form Civ-050, for eight categories of damages, Plaintiff repeated the same response: “According to Proof.  Discovery & Investigation is continuing.”  (Id., Exh. 2.) 

 

On February 2, 2023, Defendant filed a Motion to Compel a Further Statement of Damages from Plaintiff. The motion was assigned a hearing date of October 13, 2023.

 

On October 2, 2023, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the motion.  On the same day, she also served a revised Statement of Damages.  The Statement of Damages lists $13 million in general damages, broken down as follows: $5 million for pain; suffering, and inconvenience; an additional $5 million for emotional distress; and $3 million for “other,” identified as “according to proof under CACI 3905A” (which relates to pain, suffering, and emotional distress damages).  The Statement of Damages also lists more than $11 million in special damages, broken down as follows: medical expenses of “less than $25k”; $4 million in future medical expenses; $4 million in loss of future earning capacity; and $3 million for “other,” identified as “according to proof under CACI 3903A” (which relates to past and future medical expenses).  (Slatkin Decl., Exh. 2.)

 

On October 6, 2023, Defendant filed a reply.

 

Legal Standard 

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.11, subdivision (b), provides the procedure for requesting a statement of damages in personal injury cases.

 

When a complaint is filed in an action to recover damages for personal injury or wrongful death, the defendant may at any time request a statement setting forth the nature and amount of damages being sought. The request shall be served upon the plaintiff, who shall serve a responsive statement as to the damages within 15 days. In the event that a response is not served, the defendant, on notice to the plaintiff, may petition the court in which the action is pending to order the plaintiff to serve a responsive statement.

 

Discussion 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.11, subdivision (b), a defendant is entitled to request that the plaintiff provide a statement “setting forth the nature and amount of damages being sought.”  The statement that Plaintiff served in January 2023, which does not set forth any “amount” of damages, is plainly deficient.  (Rios Decl., Exh. 2.)

 

The updated statement, served earlier this month, does set forth amounts.  (Slatkin Decl., Exh. 2.)  But it is still deficient in that, in certain respects, it does not set forth the “nature” of the damages being sought.  On mandatory form CIV-050, line 1(e), under general damages, Plaintiff lists $3 million of “other” damages but does not “specify” the nature of these other general damages.  The reference to CACI 3905A on line 1(e) only deepens the mystery, as that jury instruction relates to pain, suffering, and emotional distress, which are specifically covered by lines 1(a) and 1(b) of the form.  This leaves Defendant to guess at what these “other” damages are and, for example, whether they are in addition to, or included within, the $5 million listed on line 1(a) and the additional $5 million listed on line 1(b).

 

Similarly, on line 2(i), under special damages, Plaintiff lists $3 million of “other” damages but does not “specify” the nature of these other special damages.  The reference to CACI 3903A only deepens the mystery, as that jury instruction relates to past and future medical expenses, which are specifically covered by lines 2(a) and 2(b) of the form.  This leaves Defendant to guess at what these “other” damages are and, for example, whether they are in addition to, or included within, the “less than $25k” listed on line 2(a) and the additional $4 million listed on line 2(b).

 

The motion is GRANTED in part.  Plaintiff is ordered to serve a new Statement of Damages, on mandatory form CIV-050, that sets forth the nature and amount of damages being sought.

 

Defendant also complains that the numbers on the Statement of Damages served earlier this month are “absurdly excessive and unsubstantiated” and “cannot be reconciled with discovery that has been exchanged to date.”  (Reply, at 2.)  A statement of damages, however, is just that: a statement of damages, functionally similar to the amount stated at the end of a complaint in a prayer for relief (when permitted).  If, as Defendant argues, the amount is excessive, then Defendant has an adequate remedy available to it: Defendant may pursue the issue of damages through robust discovery.  A further statement of damages is neither necessary nor authorized to address this issue.  (Indeed, it is unclear what relief the Court could fashion or enforce, as the Court cannot be expected to adjudicate, at this stage of the proceedings, what claimed damages are or are not excessive and unsubstantiated.)  Defendant’s request that the Court order Plaintiff to serve a new Statement of Damages that is not excessive, or one that is tethered to the discovery responses to date, is DENIED.

Finally, although there is some discussion of potential sanctions in the briefing, Defendant makes request for sanctions in the notice of motion and motion.  Accordingly, the Court makes no award of sanctions.

Conclusion 

 

Defendant’s motion to compel a further Statement of Damages is GRANTED in part and DENIED in part. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to serve a further Statement of Damages within 30 days of notice of this order.  In this further statement, Plaintiff must set forth the nature and amount of damages being sought.  On mandatory form CIV-050, if any amounts are claimed on lines lines 1(e), 1(f), 2(i), or 2(j), Plaintiff must “specify” (as the form requires) the nature of the claimed damages.

 

Moving party is ordered to give notice.
