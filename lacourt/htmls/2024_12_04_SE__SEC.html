DEPARTMENT SEC LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT SE-C LAW & MOTION PROCEDURES ARE AS FOLLOWS: APPEARANCES: The Court will hear oral arguments on all matters at the scheduled time of hearing. If all counsel intend to submit on the Tentative Order and do not want oral argument, please advise the clerk, in Department “C”, by calling (562-345-3702). If all sides submit on the Tentative Order and the clerk is so advised, the Tentative Order will become the final order of the court and the prevailing party shall give written Notice of Ruling per CRC 3.1312. If the Moving and Responding parties do not agree to submit on the Tentative Order, the motion will be called as calendared for hearing. There is no need to contact Department “C”, as the matter will remain on calendar for hearing. If the Moving party does not call Department “C” to submit on the Tentative Order and there is no appearance by any party, then the motion(s), at the Court’s discretion, may be taken off calendar without ruling on the motion(s). ORDERS: The minute order reflecting the Court’s Order will constitute the final Order. No additional orders should be submitted to the Court for signature unless required by law or by the Court. Prevailing party shall give written Notice of Ruling per CRC 3.1312. Minute orders, which constitute the final Order of the Court, will only be sent to the parties via U.S. mail  for the following: OSC re: sanctions, OSC re: contempt or matters taken under submission after oral arguments or briefing. Counsel or parties may request copies of all other minute orders/final orders either at the clerk’s office or in writing. If a request is in writing, a self-addressed stamped envelope and the appropriate fee for copies shall be submitted.

DOMINGUEZ ET AL. V. ESTES EXPRESS LINES, INC., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 21NWCV00720

HEARING: 12/04/2024

 

#1

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

I.             Defendants Estes Express Lines and G.I. Trucking Company’s motion to strike the first and second causes of action in the third amended complaint is DENIED.

 

II.            Defendants’ motion to strike “back income,” “back pay,” and “compensation” is DENIED.

 

III.          Defendants’ motion to strike “funeral costs” and “emotional distress” damages is GRANTED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Estes Express Lines and G.I. Trucking Company (Defendants) move this Court for an order striking portions of the third amended complaint (TAC) brought by Claudia Dominguez on behalf of the Estate of Gilbert Dominguez (Plaintiff).

 

Background

 

On November 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a TAC against Defendant alleging six causes of action: (1) wrongful discharge, (2) disability discrimination, (3) failure to prevent/remedy discrimination, (4) negligent supervision, (5) failure to accommodate, and (6) failure to engage in the good faith interactive process.

 

Gilbert Dominguez (Decedent) was employed by Defendants. Plaintiff alleges that on December 4, 2017, Decedent suffered life-threatening injuries while working for Defendants when a tractor operated by another employee backed into him. (TAC, ¶ 2.) Defendants and third parties had removed the tractor’s back-up safety alarm prior to this accident. (TAC, ¶ 4.) Following Decedent’s death on October 28, 2019, Defendants’ told Decedent’s family that he had been terminated and they would not pay any funeral costs. (TAC, ¶ 4.) Decedent had been unaware about the termination. (TAC, ¶ 8.) Plaintiffs allege he was wrongfully terminated based on his inability to return from leave and his disability. (TAC, ¶ 8.)

 

In the instant action, Defendants seek to strike Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action and Plaintiff’s requests for back income, back pay, compensation, funeral costs, and emotional distress.

 

Procedural History

 

On October 27, 2021, Estate of Gilbert Domingues, Claudia Dominguez, Angelica Hernandez, and Gisell Escalera filed a complaint against Estes Express Lines, Inc., Tony Talley, Ashley Hening, Life Insurance Company of North America, Inc., Cigna, Inc., Cigna Health and Life Insurance Company, Inc., Cigna Holding Company, Inc., Guardian Life Insurance Company Inc., and Does 1-200 for (1) wrongful discharge, (2) disability discrimination; (3) failure to prevent/remedy discrimination; (4) negligent supervision; (5) failure to accommodate; (6) failure to engage in the good faith interactive process; (7) declaratory relief; (8) breach of contract; (9) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; and (10) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

On March 2, 2022, Estate of Gilbert Dominguez, Claudia Dominguez, Angelica Hernandez, and Gisell Escalera amended the complaint, substituting G.I. Trucking Company dba Estes West for DOE 1.

 

On September 7, 2022, Life Insurance Company of North America, Inc. removed the case to federal court based on federal question jurisdiction due to ERISA preemption of Plaintiffs’ state law claims for recovery of certain benefits provided under an employee benefit plan.

 

On October 21, 2022, Estate of Gilbert Dominguez, Claudia Dominguez, Angelica Hernandez, and Gisell Escalera filed a first amended complaint.

 

On January 26, 2023, the Estate of Gilbert Dominguez, Claudia Dominguez, Angelica Hernandez, and Gisell Escalera filed a second amended complaint (SAC) against Estes Express Lines, Inc., Tony Talley, Ashley Hening, Life Insurance Company of North America, Inc., Cigna, Inc., Cigna Health and Life Insurance Company, Inc., Cigna Holding Company, Inc., Guardian Life Insurance Company Inc., and DOES 1 to 200, inclusive. The SAC alleges causes of action for (1) wrongful discharge, (2) disability discrimination (3) failure to prevent/remedy discrimination, (4) negligent supervision, (5) Failure to accommodate, and (6) failure to engage in the good faith interactive process.

 

On March 8, 2023, the federal court remanded the case.

 

On April 26, 2023, Estate of Gilbert Dominguez, Claudia Dominguez, Angelica Hernandez, and Gisell Escalera dismissed Claudia Dominguez individually.

 

On October 17, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant Estes Express Lines and G.I. Trucking company’s demurrer to the SAC with leave to amend in part, and overruled it, in part. The Court sustained the demurrer as to the SAC’s wrongful discharge and disability discrimination causes of action. The Court declined to rule on Defendants’ demurrer as to damages.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendants request the Court take judicial notice of (1) the original complaint for damages, (2) the first amended complaint, (3) the second amended complaint, (4) the Court’s order on Defendants’ demurrer to the second amended complaint, (5) the third amended complaint, and (6) Plaintiff Claudia Dominguez’s voluntary dismissal of her individual capacity claims.

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for judicial notice pursuant to Evidence Code section 452 subd. (d). Where a pleading contradicts, or omits facts pled in, prior complaints, judges have discretion to take judicial notice of the earlier allegations and disregard inconsistent ones, absent the pleader’s satisfactory explanation. (State of Cal. ex rel. Metz v. CCC Information Services, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 402, 412; Holland v. Morse Diesel Internat. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447; Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 946 (perceiving no inconsistency or sham pleading.).)

 

Legal Standard

 

The Court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) The Court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc. § 437.)

 

Discussion

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s wrongful discharge and physical discrimination causes of action fail as a matter of law and under the sham pleading doctrine.

 

          First Cause of Action – Wrongful Discharge

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s first cause of action fails as a matter of law because it is not tethered to a fundamental public policy “delineated in a statutory or constitutional provision.” (Green v. Ralee Eng’g Co., 19 Cal. 4th 66, 74 (1998).) The TAC alleges that Decedent was wrongfully discharged pursuant to Government Code section 12940, Labor Code section 132a, and Labor Code section 1102.5. (TAC, ¶¶ 28-29.)

 

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s first cause of action is related to the statutory allegation of disability discrimination in violation of Government Code section 12940 subd. (a). To the extent Defendants argue that the TAC does not plead sufficient facts to constitute the statutory violation, the Court cannot decide that on the instant motion to strike. A motion to strike cannot be based upon the grounds that a complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, but instead is properly based on grounds of superfluous or abusive allegations, or improprieties in form or procedure. (Ferraro v. Camarlinghi (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 528-529.) Accordingly, the Court declines to strike the TAC’s first cause of action.

 

          Second Cause of Action – Disability Discrimination

 

Plaintiff’s second cause of action alleges physical disability discrimination in violation of Government Code section 12940 subd. (a).

 

Defendants argue Plaintiff’s second cause of action fails as a matter of law and advance the same argument as in their demurrer to the SAC. Defendants argue Plaintiff has improperly amended allegations in the TAC that contradict the allegations from the complaint, FAC, and SAC. Specifically, paragraph 6 of Plaintiff’s TAC states:

 

Mr. DOMINGUEZ could not immediately return to work as a result of his life-altering, life changing injuries and remained paralyzed up through his death on October 28, 2019. Although he was paralyzed following the accident, there was an expectation that Mr. DOMINGUEZ would live and that he could return to work in some capacity in a wheelchair prior to being unlawfully terminated and prior to his death on October 28, 2019.

 

Defendants argue this contradicts Plaintiff’s allegations in the complaint, FAC, and SAC, which state:

 

Mr. DOMINGUEZ could not physically return to work as a result of his life-altering, life changing injuries and remained paralyzed and unable to work up through his death on October 28, 2019.

 

"Under the sham pleading doctrine, allegations in an original pleading that rendered it vulnerable to demurrer or other attack cannot simply be omitted without explanation…The purpose of the doctrine is to enable the courts to prevent an abuse of process…The doctrine is not intended to prevent honest complainants from correcting erroneous allegations or to prevent the correction of ambiguous facts." (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 751 (citing Deveny v. Entropin, Inc. (2006) 139 Cal.App.4th 408, 425-426).) “A trial court has authority to strike sham pleadings, or those not filed in conformity with its prior ruling.” (Ricard v. Grobstein, Goldman, Stevenson, Siegel, LeVine & Mangel (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 157, 162.)

 

Here, the Court recognizes Plaintiff’s amendment to allege there was an expectation Decedent could return to work in some capacity rather than that he remained physically unable to work for the rest of his life. The Court does not agree that the TAC contradicts the prior complaints. Rather, the TAC adds allegations about an expectation Decedent could live to work again in some capacity. The TAC does not omit the facts that Decedent was paralyzed for the remainder of his life and remained unable to work until his death. Therefore, the Court declines to strike Plaintiff’s second cause of action. The Court cannot rule on whether these facts are sufficient to sustain a cause of action based on a motion to strike.

 

          Damages

 

Lastly, Defendants move to strike Plaintiff’s requests for “back income,” “back pay,” “compensation,” “funeral costs,” and “emotional distress” damages. Defendants argue these damages are not supported by law.

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court declines to strike Plaintiff’s complaint for compensatory damages in the form of “back income,” “back pay,” and “compensation.”

 

Next, the Court finds there are no grounds for funeral costs damages. Plaintiff does not provide, and the Court does not find, case law authority supporting funeral costs as damages.

 

The Court also finds there are no grounds for emotional distress damages. Pursuant to Claudia Dominguez’s voluntary dismissal of all her individual capacity claims on May 8, 2024, the only Plaintiff is the Estate of Gilbert Dominguez. The TAC does not allege facts supporting Plaintiff’s claim for emotional distress damages.

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motion to strike “back income,” “back pay,” and “compensation.” The Court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to strike “funeral costs” and “emotional distress” damages.


Case Number: 22NWCV00332    Hearing Date: December 4, 2024    Dept: SEC

ADRIANA CORTES TECUANHUEHUE V. H-T GENERAL ENGINEERING, INC. ET AL.

CASE NO.: 22NWCV00332

HEARING: 12-04-2024

 

#7

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Plaintiff Adriana Cortes Tecanhuehue’s motion for summary adjudication is GRANTED.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Adriana Cortes Tecanhuehue (Plaintiff) moves the Court for summary adjudication as to her second cause of action for disgorgement.

 

Background

 

On August 19, 2022, Plaintiff filed a first amended complaint (FAC) against H-T General Engineering, Inc., Felix Madariaga, Jesus Blanquel, Miguel Aguirre, Daniel Molgaard, and Optima Asset Management Services, Inc. (Defendants). The FAC alleges five causes of action (1) breach of written contract, (2) disgorgement, (3) negligence, (4) fraud, and (5) declaratory relief.

 

Plaintiff owns the real property known as 13019 Cornuta Avenue, Downey, California 90242 (Property). (FAC, ¶ 1.) Plaintiff entered into a written contract with Defendant H-T Engineering to construct a single-family residence on the Property. (FAC ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges Defendants attempted to deceive Plaintiff by having Defendant Molgaard sign the contract using Defendant Optima’s Class B contractor license number. (FAC, ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges Defendants left the Property construction defective and incomplete. (FAC, ¶ 28.) Plaintiff has paid Defendants $694,578, to date. (FAC, ¶ 25.)

 

Undisputed Material Facts

 

Plaintiff entered into a written contract with Defendant H-T Engineering to construct a single-family residence on the Property. (UMF, ¶ 1.) Defendants Madriaga and H-T Engineering held class A contracting licenses. (UMF, ¶¶ 3, 4.) Defendant H-T Engineering’s Class A license expired on May 31, 2022. (UMF, ¶ 6.) Defendants Madriaga and H-T Engineering tried to pull permits for construction on the Property under their Class A licenses but the Department did not permit it. (UMF, ¶ 9.) No invoices were sent to Plaintiff by a licensed general contractor. (UMF, ¶ 18.) Plaintiff has paid Defendants H-T Engineering and Madariaga $694,578, to date. (UMF, ¶ 19.)

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of contractor license personnel details for Felix Madariaga, H-T General Engineering, Inc., Rushmore Construction Company, Inc., and Optima Asset Management Services, Inc. accessed through the California Contractor’s State License Board.

 

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s requests for judicial notice pursuant to Evidence Code section 452 subd. (c).

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

Defendants put forth the following evidentiary objections:

 

Objection no. 1. Cortes Declaration, para 3: overruled.

Objection no. 2. Cortes Declaration, para 4: sustained in part “illegally,” overruled as to the rest (opinion testimony).

Objection no. 3. Cortes Declaration, para 6: sustained in part “illegally,” overruled as to the rest (opinion testimony).

Objection no. 4. Cortes Declaration, para 7: overruled.

Objection no. 5. Cortes Declaration, para 18: overruled.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c subd. (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 381-382.)

 

The moving party’s initial burden of proof, in moving for summary judgment, does not include disproving affirmative defenses, but instead involves proving each element of a cause of action. (Oldcastle Precast, Inc. v. Lumbermens Mutual Casualty Co. (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 554, 564.)

 

Once a moving party has satisfied the initial burden of proof, the burden “shifts to the opposing party to show, by responsive separate statement and admissible evidence, that triable issues of fact exist.” (Ostayan v. Serrano Reconveyance Co. (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1411, 1418, disapproved on other grounds by Black Sky Cap., LLC v. Cobb (2019) 7 Cal.5th 156, 165. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

An issue of damages must dispose of an entire cause of action. (DeCastro West Chodorow & Burns, Inc. v. Superior Court (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 410, 422.) Summary adjudication as to punitive damages is proper only if a reasonable jury could not find clear and convincing proof of malice, fraud or oppression. (Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 762.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff contends there are no triable issues of material fact as to her second cause of action for disgorgement because Defendants were unlicensed contractors.

 

“[A] person who utilizes the services of an unlicensed contractor may bring an action in any court of competent jurisdiction in this state to recover all compensation paid to the unlicensed contractor for performance of any act or contract.” (Bus. Prof. Code § 7031 subd. (b).)  “Disgorgement is permitted even when the project owner knows that the contractor is unlicensed, the contractor is only unlicensed for part of the time it performed work requiring a license, or when the work performed by the unlicensed contractor is free of defects.” (San Francisco CDC LLC v. Webcor Construction, L.P. (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 266, 277.) General engineering contractors hold class A licenses and principally work on “fixed works requiring specialized engineering knowing and skill, including … irrigation, drainage, water power, water supply” and more. (Bus. Prof. Code § 7056.) General building contractors hold class B licenses and “may take a prime contract or a subcontract for a framing or carpentry project.” (Bus. Prof. Code § 7057 subd. (b).)

 

Plaintiff has established Defendants worked on construction on the Property without a class B license. On May 3, 2021, Plaintiff contracted with Defendant H-T General Engineering to build her home on the Property. (Cortes Decl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff includes the contract signed by Defendants Madriaga, Blanquel, and Aguirre who were listed as “Class ‘A’ License Contractors” and Defendant Molgaard listed as “Class ‘B’ License Contractor.” (Cortes Decl., Exh. 1, p. 12.) On April 29, 2021, Defendant Madariaga texted Plaintiff identifying himself as the prime contractor. (Cortes Decl., ¶ 5, Exh. 6.) The Court notes that the evidence Plaintiff includes regarding this identification is inadmissible. “Exhibits written in a foreign language must be accompanied by an English translation, certified under oath by a qualified interpreter.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110 (g).) Plaintiff does not include a certified English translation. However, this evidence is not necessary to fulfill the elements of this cause of action. Next, over the course of the construction, Defendant Madriaga sent invoices to Plaintiff on H-T General letterhead. (Cortes Decl., ¶¶ 8, 9.) Plaintiff paid a total of $694,578 to Defendant H-T General Engineering and Defendant Madriaga. (Cortes Decl., ¶ 11, Exh. 7.)

 

Thus, Plaintiff has met her burden of proving her claim of disgorgement.

 

Defendants argue a triable material fact exists as to whether Defendants improperly performed tasks outside of the scope of their license. Defendant Molgaard, who held a Class B license, is also a signatory of the contract. Defendants contend Defendant Molgaard worked on the framing portions of the construction as a subcontractor pursuant to a framing contract with Defendant H-T General Engineering. (Molgaard Decl., ¶¶ 4, 6.) Defendants provide the framing contract entered into between H-T Engineering and Optima Construction and signed by Defendants Blanquel, Aguirre, Madariaga, and Molgaard. (Compendium of Evidence, Exh. 9.) Defendants further state Defendant Madariaga and his employees did not work on framing. (Molgaard Decl., ¶ 7; Blanquel Decl., ¶ 7.)

 

In reply, Plaintiff argues that she was not party to the framing contract and that Defendant Molgaard was therefore an illegal framing sub-contractor. However, Plaintiff does not provide, and the Court does not find, authority which requires a Plaintiff to be party to a subcontract. Additionally, it is undisputed that Defendant Molgaard was a party to the original contract entered into between Defendant H-T General Engineering and Plaintiff. (Cortes Decl., Exh. 1, p. 12.)

 

Next, Plaintiff argues that both Defendant Madariaga’s and Defendant Optima’s license had expired. However, in opposition, Defendants point out Defendant Madariaga’s license expired on May 31, 2022, which is after the contract between the parties had terminated in March of 2022. (Compendium of Evidence, Exh. 7.) As for Defendant Optima, it is undisputed that its workers’ compensation insurance lapsed during the period of construction. (UMF, ¶.) However, Plaintiff’s evidence reflects the license expired on January 31, 2023, after the termination of the contract. (Plaintiff’s RJN, Exh. 5.) Plaintiff cites to Business and Professions Code § 7125 subd. (a) to argue that a lapse in workers’ compensation renders the license void. The statute is subject to exceptions and alternatives such as providing a certification of self-insurance. (Bus. Prof. Code §§ 7125 subd. (a)-(b).) Defendants do not provide evidence that the license was not rendered void and instead argue that Defendant Molgaard’s license was active when Defendant Molgaard entered the contract. However, disgorgement is permitted even when a contractor is unlicensed for a portion of the duration of a project. (See San Francisco CDC LLC v. Webcor Construction, L.P. (2021) 62 Cal.App.5th 266, 277.) Defendants, therefore, do not provide evidence sufficient to meet their burden of production.

 

Finally, in reply, Plaintiff argues that her payments directly to Defendants H-T General Engineering and Madariaga, and the lack of payments reflecting transactions between Defendants Madriaga and Molgaard establish that Defendant Molgaard did not exclusively perform the framing work. The Court agrees. Defendants’ opposition does not address the invoices or explain how Plaintiff’s payments were later distributed pursuant to the subcontract. Accordingly, Defendants do not provide substantial responsive evidence that a triable issue of material facts exists.    

 

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication as to the disgorgement cause of action.

RHODES V. CERRITOS GARDENS, LLC

CASE NO.: 22NWCV01155

HEARING: 12/04/2024

 

#3

TENTATIVE ORDER

Defendant Cerritos Gardens, LLC’s motion for terminating sanctions is GRANTED. Plaintiff Felicia Rhodes’s case is DISMISSED WITH PREJUDICE.

 

Moving party(s) to give notice.

 

Cerritos Gardens, LLC (Defendant) moves this Court for an order for terminating sanctions against pro per plaintiff Felicia Rhodes (Plaintiff).

 

Background

This is a wrongful eviction action. On October 31, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant alleging wrongful eviction and fraud.

 

Legal Standard

 

If a party fails to comply with a court order compelling discovery responses or attendance at a deposition, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions. (Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2023.010 subd. (g); 2030.290 subd. (c); 2023.030; R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.) Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules. (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.) “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.” (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.)

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 subd. (d), “The court may impose a terminating sanction by one of the following orders:

(1) An order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process.

(2) An order staying further proceedings by that party until an order for discovery is obeyed.

(3) An order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party.

(4) An order rendering a judgment by default against that party.”

 

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances: [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’” (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390, quoting Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)

 

Discussion

 

Defendant moves the Court for an order of terminating sanctions against Plaintiff. Defendant seeks dismissal of Plaintiff’s complaint with prejudice.

 

Here, ultimate discovery sanctions are justified. Defendant has provided a timeline of the discovery process in the instant matter. First, Plaintiff failed to respond to Defendant’s form interrogatories, set one, and requests for admission. (Mallon Decl., ¶ 4.) Then, Plaintiff did not appear for the motion to compel hearings on October 19, 2023. (Mallon Decl., ¶ 6.) Defendant then served an amended notice of deposition, set for November 20, 2023, to which Plaintiff failed to appear. (Mallon Decl., ¶ 8.) On April 10, 2024, the Court heard Defendant’s unopposed motion to compel the deposition of Plaintiff and ordered the deposition happen within 30 days. (Mallon Decl. ¶ 11.) Defendant properly noticed Plaintiff’s deposition to take place on May 7, 2024. (Mallon Decl. ¶ 12.) Plaintiff failed to appear. (Mallon Decl. ¶ 12.)

 

Within this timeframe, it is clear Plaintiff has engaged in multiple misuses of the discovery process. By ignoring Defendant’s notice of depositions, Plaintiff failed to respond to an authorized method of discovery. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010 subd. (d).) Plaintiff disobeyed the Court’s April 10, 2024, minute order by failing to appear for the Court-ordered deposition. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010 subd. (g).) The failure to obey the Court order further shows Plaintiff fails to comply with the discovery process under the Court’s less severe factions.  Plaintiff has additionally not engaged in any meet and confer process nor provided her availability for the past depositions. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010 subd. (i).) “If a plaintiff wishes to avail himself of the California courts, he owes a duty to comply with its rules, such as the rules of discovery. To hold otherwise, would permit a litigant to disappear and not be obliged to comply with our discovery statutes.” (Cornwall v. Santa Monica Dairy Co., 66 Cal.App.3d 250, 253.) Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the discovery process, without any appearances or justifications, constitutes a willful violation. Accordingly, terminating sanctions are warranted.

 

Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s case is DISMISSED WITH PREJUDICE.

TIRMAN V. TBC, L.P., ET AL.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV03115

HEARING: 12-04-2024

 

#4

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant The Bicycle Casino L.P.’s motion for attorney’s fees is GRANTED in the amount of $8,010.

 

Moving party to give notice.

 

Defendant The Bicycle Casino L.P. (TBC) brings this motion for attorney’s fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 subd. (c).

 

Background

 

This is a wrongful death and survival action. On September 27, 2023, Phillip Tirman, individually and as successor in interest to Vanessa Jung Tirman, (Plaintiff) filed a complaint against TBC. and Parkwest Bicycle Casino, LLC (Defendants). The complaint alleges causes of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress and abuse of process. In July of 2022, decedent Vanessa Jung Tirman (Decedent) brought a wrongful death action against Defendants regarding the death of her brother. (Complaint ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges Defendants intentionally showed Decedent a surveillance video of her brother’s death during a deposition to prompt her to dismiss the case. (Complaint ¶¶ 17-19.) On August 16, 2023, Decedent commit suicide. (Complaint ¶ 28.)

 

On November 8, 2023, Defendant Parkwest filed its special motion to strike pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 (Anti-SLAPP motion). On December 1, 2023, TBC filed its Anti-SLAPP motion. On December 20, 2023, the Court granted Defendant Parkwest’s Anti-SLAPP motion. On January 3, 2024, the Court granted TBC’s Anti-SLAPP motion. Accordingly, the Court entered a judgment in favor of Defendants on February 27, 2024.

 

TBC now moves the Court for an order requiring Plaintiff to pay $8,010 in attorney fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 subd. (c).

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 subd. (c)(1) provides, in part, “a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover that defendant’s attorney’s fees and costs.” California law makes the award of attorney’s fees to a prevailing Anti-SLAPP defendant mandatory. (See Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1131 (“[u]nder Code of Civil Procedure §425.16, subdivision (c), any SLAPP defendant who brings a successful motion to strike is entitled to mandatory attorney’s fees.”).)

 

In determining what fees are reasonable, California courts apply the “lodestar” approach. (See, e.g., Holguin v. DISH Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1332.) This inquiry “begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) From there, the “[t]he lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Ibid.) Relevant factors include: “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, [and] (4) the contingent nature of the fee award.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

 

The trial court has broad authority to determine the amount of a reasonable fee. (PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) The party bears the burden of proof as to “reasonableness” of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5, subd. (c)(5).) The party seeking fees has the burden of documenting the appropriate hours expended and hourly rates. (City of Colton v. Singletary (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 751, 784.) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.)

 

An attorney's testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records or billing statements, and there is no requirement that such records or statements be offered in evidence. (Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, 293; see also Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396 (“the verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”). Ascertaining the fee amount is left to the trial court’s sound discretion. (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.)

 

Discussion

 

TBC requests attorney’s fees in the total amount of $8,010. To justify each attorney’s reasonable rate, TBC compares each rate to the Fitzpatrick Matix and Laffey Matrix, finding each rate is at or below the rates estimated based on experience.

 

TBC was represented by Bordin Semmer LP at all times during this action. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 1.) Four attorneys at Bordin Semmer LP worked on this matter. Defendant provides each of the four attorney’s billing records on TBC matters. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., Exh. 3.)

 

Attorney Christopher Blanchard spent 13.7 hours of work on TBC’s Anti-SLAAP motion and 3.9 hours on the instant motion at a billing rate of $225 per hour. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶¶ 5, 7.) TBC’s counsel declares that this rate is reasonable as Mr. Blanchard is Bordin Semmer LP’s of counsel, has been a practicing attorney since 2005, and is experienced in complex litigation. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 6.)

 

Attorney Bryan Aghakhani spent 8.5 hours of time on TBC’s Anti-SLAAP motion including reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition, conducting research, preparing a reply, and attending the hearing. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 8.) Mr. Aghakhani’s billing rate is $225 per hour. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 8.) TBC’s counsel declares that this rate is reasonable because Mr. Aghakhani has practiced law since 2016, and this rate is lower than market rates for similar defense-side attorneys. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶¶ 9, 10.)

 

Attorney Tara Alaegha spent 6.9 hours on TBC’s Anti-SLAAP motion at a rate of $225 per hour, which includes preparing the initial draft. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 11.) TBC’s counsel declares this rate is reasonable as Ms. Alaegha is an associate who has practiced law since 2022 and has concentrated on law and motion matters. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 12.)

 

Attorney Joshua Bordin-Wosk spent 1.5 hours on TBC’s Anti-SLAAP motion and 0.6 hours on the instant motion at an hourly rate of $225 per hour. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 15.) He declares this rate is reasonable as is the managing partner of Bordin Semmer LP, has practiced law since 2005, and was principal point of contact for TBC. (Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶¶ 14, 15.)

 

Plaintiff submit a joint-opposition to both Defendant Parkwest and TBC’s motions for attorney’s fees. The Court notes Defendants’ argument in their joint reply that Plaintiff’s opposition is submitted in the form of a declaration and exceeds the page limit. “No opening or responding memorandum may exceed 15 pages.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1113(d).) Since Plaintiff’s opposition jointly responds to both Defendants’ motions for attorney’s fees, the Court will consider the merits of Plaintiff’s opposition as it relates to the instant motion for attorney’s fees. The Court need not rule on Defendants’ objections where the declaration functions as a memorandum.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ fees are redundant because the work of Defendants were the same and Defendants could have filed a joinder pleader. Plaintiff argues that Defendants were represented by the same law firm, Bordin Semmer LP. However, this argument is not correct. Defendant Parkwest states that it was represented by Umberg Zipser LLP when it brought the Anti-SLAPP Motion. (Parkwest, Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 2.) Bordin Semmer LLP, Defendant Parkwest’s current counsel, substituted as counsel after the initial Motion was filed, but before Plaintiff filed the opposition. (Parkwest, Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 2.) Each Defendant, therefore, separately filed an Anti-SLAAP motion. Attorney’s fees are mandatory for any defendant who brings a successful Anti-SLAAP motion. (See Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1131.) Here, TBC is entitled to recover under Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 subd. (c)(1) because it successfully brought an Anti-SLAAP motion. Whether Defendants may have brought a joint Anti-SLAAP motion is immaterial under the statute.

 

As to whether the fees are redundant or excessive, the Court recognizes Anti-SLAAP motions are complex motions requiring skill, time, and extensive research in an ever-evolving area of law. Here, as outlined above, TBC’s counsel provided each attorney’s hours worked, experience, and reasons for the hourly rate. The Court sees that the hours spent on TBC’s Anti-SLAAP motion and motion for attorney’s fees are generally lower than the hours spent on Defendant Parkwest’s motions. Defendants argue that this is because TBC’s counsel was able to use Parkwest’s motions as frameworks. For example, Mr. Blanchard spent 8.9 hours drafting Defendant Parkwest’s motion for attorney fees, and 3.9 hours drafting the instant motion for TBC. (See Bordin-Wosk Bicycle Decl., ¶ 5; Parkwest, Bordin-Wosk Decl., ¶ 21.) The Court finds that TBC’s evidence is more than enough to substantiate the 37.4 hours spent on the Anti-SLAAP motion and supporting papers.

 

As to the reasonableness of the rates, the Court finds such rates are reasonable based on the experience of each attorney. The attorneys’ rates suggested are at or below the rates estimated via the Laffey Matrix, adjusted for Los Angeles. The Declaration of Attorney Bordin-Wosk confirms the reasonableness of such rates for defense attorneys handling commercial litigation matters in Southern California. The Court may rely on such support in finding the rates are reasonable. (See Pasternack v. McCullough (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1050, 1059 (“The trial court was entitled to rely on [one attorney’s declaration] to determine the reasonable rates for experienced attorneys in Los Angeles County.”); see also Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 702 (holding the trial court properly relied on the Laffey Matrix as adjusted for the San Francisco-San Jose-Oakland region to determine reasonableness of attorneys’ rates).) The Court thus finds that each attorney’s rate is reasonable.

 

Plaintiff also argues that pursuit of attorney’s fees under the circumstances of this case are undignified and cruel. However, regardless of the circumstances of the case, California law makes the award of attorney’s fees to a prevailing Anti-SLAPP defendant mandatory. (See Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1131.)

 

Plaintiff lastly argues that that the fees were billed to a non-party, and thus are not recoverable. The Court finds Plaintiff cites no authority to support that contention, and thus, rejects it.

 

Accordingly, TBC’s motion for attorney’s fees pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 subd. (c)(1) is GRANTED in the amount of $8,010.

FLORES, ET AL. V. SAFECO INSURANCE, A LIBERTY MUTUAL COMPANY

CASE NO.: 23NWCV03289

HEARING: 12-04-2024

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

I. Plaintiff Tonia Flores’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatory set three, no. 17.1 is GRANTED.

II. Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the reduced amount of $1,810.

Moving party to give notice.

Tonia Flores (Plaintiff) moves the Court for an order compelling Safeco Insurance Company of America (Defendant) to serve further responses to form interrogatories, set 3, no. 17.1.

Background

This action arises out of a motor vehicle incident. On October 12, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendant alleging a personal injury cause of action. Plaintiffs Tonia Flores and her husband, Allen Flores, maintained an uninsured/underinsured motorist policy (Policy) with Defendant. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) On or about September 28, 2022, Doe defendants 1-25 collided with Plaintiff’s vehicle resulting in bodily injuries. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) Doe defendants allegedly failed to stop to identify themselves. (Complaint, ¶ 6.) Plaintiff alleges after reporting the incident to Defendant pursuant to the Policy, Defendant tendered an improperly low settlement offer. (Complaint, ¶ 10.)

On January 12, 2024, Plaintiff served Defendant with request for admissions, set two, and form interrogatories, set three. (Koontz Decl., ¶¶ 6, 8; Exhs. 5, 7.) On January 31, 2024, Defendant provided responses and objections. (Koontz Decl., ¶¶ 7, 9; Exhs. 6, 8.) Defendant served supplemental responses on March 15, 2024. (Koontz Decl., ¶ 10; Exh. 9.)

Plaintiff brings the instant motion to compel Defendant’s further responses to form interrogatories, set 3, no. 17.1 which correspond with Plaintiff’s requests for admission, set two, nos. 1, 9, 10, and 11. This motion is unopposed.

Legal Standard

Where responses to interrogatories have been served but the requesting party believes that they are deficient because the answers are evasive or incomplete, or, because an objection is without merit, that party may move for an order compelling a further response. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (a).) The motion must be made within 45 days after service of verified responses in question, or any verified supplemental responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (c).)

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response…the party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the production for work product.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 subd. (a)(1).) The court may relieve the party from this waiver if (1) the party has subsequently served code complaint responses and (2) the failure was a result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300 subd. (a).)

“The court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

Meet and Confer

A motion to compel further response to interrogatories must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.300 subd. (b).) The declaration must state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented in the motion.¿ (Code Civ. Proc. § 2016.040.)¿

Here, Defendant has evaded its meet and confer obligations. Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to meet and confer with Defendant on multiple occasions. (Koontz Decl., ¶ 1.) Defendant responded to only one meet and confer email regarding extending the deadline to bring this motion. (Koontz Decl., ¶ 1; Exh. 1.) Given Plaintiff’s attempts to engage in a meaningful meet and confer process, and Defendant’s refusal to respond after multiple attempts, the Court will rule on the merits of this motion.

Discussion

Plaintiff’s form integratory set 3, no. 17.1 requests additional information based on Plaintiff’s requests for admission, set two, served on the same day. Form interrogatory, set 3, no. 17.1 is as follows:

INTERROGATORY NO. 17.1: Is your response to each request for admission served with these interrogatories an unqualified admission? If not, for each response that is not an unqualified admission:

(a) state the number of the request;

(b) state all facts upon which you base your response;

(c) state the names, ADDRESSES, and telephone numbers of all PERSONS who have knowledge of those facts; and

(d) identify all DOCUMENTS and other tangible things that support your response and state the name, ADDRESS, and telephone number of the PERSON who has each (Flores Form Interrogatory 17.1, Request No. 1, Exhibit 4).

At issue are Defendant’s form interrogatory 17.1, set three, responses to requests for admission, set two, nos. 1, 9, 10, and 11.

Request for Admission, Set Two, No. 1

Defendant’s response was as follows:

RESPONSE TO INTERROGATORY NO. 17.1:

(a) 1;

(b) This request calls for a legal conclusion and/or expert opinions of a layperson and invades the province of a jury, which is the ultimate trier of fact. This objection is based on personal knowledge and information. See Chodos v. Superior Court (Lowe) (1963), 215 Cal.App.2d 318. Further, this request is misleading, argumentative and presumes facts not in evidence. This request for admission seeks a legal conclusion of an ultimate fact rather than a conclusion of an evidentiary fact which invades the domain of the trier of fact;

(c) Responding Party, Liberty Mutual, and defense counsel.

(d) Expert report.

Plaintiff argues Defendant’s response is incomplete and nonresponsive. As for response to form interrogatory no 17.1(b), Defendant objects to providing the facts that form the basis of its denial of request for admission, no. 1. A party may obtain discovery of “any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending … if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.) The Court finds that Defendant’s objection is without merit given Defendant’s response to the corresponding request for admission.

As for the response to form interrogatory no 17.1(d), Defendant’s response is incomplete. This response fails to identify any individuals with knowledge of the report nor explain any justification for failing to identify any individuals with knowledge of the report. Therefore, this response is not responsive to the interrogatory.

Request for Admission, Set Two, No. 9 and 10

Defendant’s responses to form interrogatory 17.1(b) for requests for admissions no. 9 and no. 10 are as follows:

RESPONSE TO INTERROGATORY NO. 17.1: 9,10;

The request is overly broad and remote and as such is not calculated to lead neither to the discovery of information relevant to the subject matter of this action nor to the discovery of admissible evidence. Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968), 263 Cal. App. 2d 12.;

This objection is the exact same response as Defendant’s response to requests for admission nos. 9 and 10. Pursuant to the Court’s ruling in the December 3, 2024, hearing on the requests for admission, the Court finds this objection meritless. Repeating the same boilerplate objection in response to an interrogatory requesting facts underpinning the request for admission objection is evasive.

Request for Admission, Set Two, No. 11

Defendant’s response to form interrogatory 17.1(b) for request for admission no. 11 is as follows:

RESPONSE TO INTERROGATORY NO. 17.1: 11

This request is vague, ambiguous, unintelligible, and unduly prejudicial. This request is also overly broad and remote and as such is not calculated to lead neither to the discovery of information relevant to the subject matter of this action nor to the discovery of admissible evidence. Columbia Broadcasting System, Inc. v. Superior Court (1968), 263 Cal. App. 2d 12.;

Defendant’s response to form interrogatory no. 17.1(b), in connection with its response to request for admission no. 11, is evasive. This response provides boilerplate objections instead of facts. This objection does not provide any facts as to why Defendant objects to request for admission, no. 11. An objection to an

interrogatory may not be “without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010 subd. (a)(3).) This objection is both too general, because it is not particular to the interrogatory, and without merit, because it does not explain how the request is vague, ambiguous, or unintelligible.

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s unopposed motion to compel further responses to form interrogatory set three, no. 17.1 is GRANTED.

Request for Sanctions

Plaintiff requests sanctions pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 subds. (e), (f), and (h). Plaintiff’s counsel requests $2,442.50 “and a like sum for its corresponding Motion being heard separately.” (Koontz Decl., p. 5.) Plaintiff’s counsel states he spent 6.75 hours on this motion and has an hourly billing rate of $350. (Koontz Decl., p. 5.) Additionally, Plaintiff’s counsel spent $60 to file this motion.

The Court agrees sanctions are warranted pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 because of Defendant’s willful refusal to meet and confer and evasive objections. Given the similarities in the instant motion with Plaintiff’s motion for requests for admission, and the fact that Plaintiff’s declaration applies to both motions, the Court reduces the hours to 5 hours. Accordingly, the Court awards Plaintiff sanctions in the reduced amount of $1,810.
