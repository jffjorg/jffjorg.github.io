DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 22STCV19770    Hearing Date: July 3, 2024    Dept: 56

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

LETICIA VASQUEZ,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 

CENTRAL BASIN MUNICIPAL WATER DISTRICT, ET AL.,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  22STCV19770

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

EX PARTE APPLICATION TO STRIKE PRIVILEGED MATERIALS

 

Date: July 3, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Central Basin Municipal Water District (“District”) and Alex Rojas (“Rojas”) (collectively, “Defendants”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Leticia Vasquez (“Plaintiff”)

 

            The Court has considered the moving and opposition papers.

 

BACKGROUND

             This action arises out of a complaint by Plaintiff, an elected public official who serves on the District’s Governing Board (the “Board”), against the District and its general manager, Rojas, among other defendants.  Plaintiff’s complaint (the “Complaint”) alleges violations of the Tom Bane Civil Rights Act (the “Bane Act”). The Complaint alleges that Defendants violated the Bane Act by preventing Plaintiff from exercising her right to freedom of speech under the United States Constitution and the California Constitution.

 

            On March 21, 2024, Defendants filed a Motion for Summary Judgment and/or Summary Adjudication (the “MSJ”) on Plaintiff’s claims.  On June 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition to the MSJ (“Opposition”), and concurrently filed her declaration in support of the Opposition (the “Vasquez Declaration”), attaching certain exhibits thereto.

 

            On June 24, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Ex Parte Application to Strike Privileged Materials (the “Motion”), seeking to strike portions of the Vasquez Declaration and certain exhibits thereto from Plaintiff’s Opposition and from the Court’s records.  On the same day, Plaintiff filed her opposition to the Application.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Plaintiff’s Objection to the Declaration of Victor Ponto (“Ponto”) (the “Ponto Decl.”), Number 1 is sustained.

 

Plaintiff’s Objection to the Ponto Decl., Numbers 2 through 5 are overruled.

             

DISCUSSION

Defendants contend that identified portions of Exhibits Nos. 3 through 5 to the Vasquez Declaration are protected by the attorney-client privilege and/or attorney work-product privilege, and should thus be stricken.

 

Attorney-Client Privilege

            Evidence Code section 954 states:

“…the client, whether or not a party, has a privilege to refuse to disclose, and to prevent another from disclosing, a confidential communication between client and lawyer if the privilege is claimed by:

(a) The holder of the privilege;

(b) A person who is authorized to claim the privilege by the holder of the privilege; or

(c) The person who was the lawyer at the time of the confidential communication, but such person may not claim the privilege if there is no holder of the privilege in existence or if he is otherwise instructed by a person authorized to permit disclosure.”

 

Attorney Work-Product Privilege

Code of Civil Procedure section 2018.030 provides for the attorney work-product privilege: “A writing that reflects an attorney’s impressions, conclusions, opinions, or legal research or theories is not discoverable under any circumstances.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030, subd. (a).) “The work product of an attorney, other than a writing described in subdivision (a), is not discoverable unless the court determines that denial of discovery will unfairly prejudice the party seeking discovery in preparing that party's claim or defense or will result in an injustice.” (Id., § 2018.030, subd. (b).)

 

Exhibit 3 to the Vasquez Declaration (the “Report”)

Ponto, a partner with Burke, Williams & Sorensen, LLP (“BWS”), general counsel for the District, states in his declaration that the Report is a confidential investigation report authored by Carr, Riggs, & Ingram, LLC (“CRI”), an agent of his office.  (Ponto Decl., ¶ 7.)  The Court notes, however, that there is no evidence to support the contention that CRI is an agent of Burke, Williams & Sorensen, LLP.  To the contrary, evidence suggests that CRI was retained by the District to conduct an investigation well before Ponto or BWS was retained as general counsel for the District.  The Board authorized the contract with CRI during its regular meeting held on November 27, 2023, whereas Ponto served as general counsel for the District only in January 2024.  (Minutes of the November 27, 2023 Board Meeting, Exhibit 1 to the Vasquez Declaration; Ponto Decl., ¶ 2.)  As such, CRI does not qualify as an “agent” of Burke, Williams & Sorensen, LLP whose work product or communications would be covered by the attorney work-product privilege or by the attorney-client privilege.  Thus, the Court finds that the Report is not privileged.

 

Exhibit 4 to the Vasquez Declaration (the “Correspondence”)

Defendants contend that the Correspondence includes confidential attorney-client communications.  (Ponto Decl., ¶ 11.)  Defendants acknowledge that the first three pages of the Correspondence are not privileged because they were sent to an outside entity. Additionally, the top part of page 4, which pertains to Plaintiff’s correspondence with Mr. Wilson, is also not privileged.  Defendants argue, however, that the rest of the Correspondence is protected by the attorney-client privilege.  (Id.)  The Court agrees that apart from the portion conceded as not privileged, the rest of the Correspondence consists of communications between Ponto, as general counsel of the Board, and Plaintiff, in her capacity as member of the Board.  As such, the portion of the Correspondence pertaining to the email exchange between Ponto and Plaintiff is covered by the attorney-client privilege and should thus be stricken.

 

Exhibit 5 to the Vasquez Declaration (the “Memorandum)

A cursory reading of the Memorandum indicates that it consists of legal opinion and guidance provided by Porto to the Board as its counsel.  (Ponto Decl., ¶¶ 12-15.)  Therefore, it is a protected attorney-work product and is also covered by the attorney-client privilege.  As such, the Memorandum is stricken in its entirety.

 

In accordance with the above discussion and the Court’s findings, portions of the Vasquez Declaration referencing privileged portions of the Correspondence and the Memorandum should be removed from the record.  Specifically, the first sentence of paragraph 4, lines 6-9, and the entirety of paragraph 5 of the Vasquez Declaration are stricken. 

 

            Accordingly, the Motion is DENIED in part, and GRANTED in part.

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

 

COCHRAN, DAVIS & ASSOCIATES, PC, et al.,

                                                                             

                        Defendants.                              

 

      CASE NO.:  23STCV14422

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

MOTIONS TO QUASH AND/OR LIMIT DEPOSITION SUBPOENA FOR PRODUCTION OF BUSINESS RECORDS

 

Date: July 3, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

 

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiffs Saeed Farkhondehpour (“Farkhondehpour”) and Illusion Holdings, LLC (“Illusion”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”)

 

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendants Cochran, Davis & Associates, PC, Jeffrey Thomas Bolson, and Joan E. Cochran (collectively, “Defendants”)

 

The Court considered the moving and opposition papers.  No reply papers are filed.

 

BACKGROUND

            This action stems from a legal malpractice claim brought by Plaintiffs against Defendants and other parties.

 

On various dates in April 2024, Defendants served deposition subpoenas for production of business records (the “Subpoenas”) on Alon, LLP (“Alon”); First Credit Bank (“FCB”); Rosenheim & Associates, Inc. (“Rosenheim”); and Jonathan Hakakha (“Hakakha”). 

 

On May 2, 2024, Plaintiffs filed four separate motions to quash and/or limit the Subpoenas (the “Motions”).

 

On June 20, 2024, Defendants filed oppositions to each of the four Motions.  Plaintiffs did not file a reply.

             

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1987.1, a party may move for orders to quash a subpoena for the production of documents, as well as orders “modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders.  In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 1987.1, subd. (a).)

 

A motion to quash a deposition subpoena or deposition notice is used to strike, modify, or impose conditions on a subpoena or notice that is procedurally or substantively defective. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 1987.1, subd. (a), 2025.410, subd. (c); see, e.g., Catholic Mut. Relief Soc’y v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 358, 365 [motion based on ground that subpoenas sought information outside scope of discovery]; John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186 [motion based in part on ground that subpoenas violated right of privacy]; McClatchy Newspapers v. Superior Court (1945) 26 Cal.2d 386, 391 [motion based on ground that subpoenas were unreasonable and oppressive]; Far W. S&L Assn. v. McLaughlin (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 67, 71 [motion based on ground that subpoena was not properly served]; City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 883, 888 [procedural remedy for a defective subpoena is generally a motion to quash under § 1987.1].)

 

A deposition subpoena may be quashed for the following:¿(1) defects in form or content of the subpoena (e.g., inadequate description of requested records); (2) defects in service of the subpoena (e.g., failure to satisfy the requirements of providing notice to consumer); (3) requesting¿production of records not within the permissible scope of discovery, i.e. privileged, privacy or attorney work product; or not “relevant to the subject matter”; (4) being unjustly burdensome or oppressive; (5) with regard to requested ESI discovery, objection to the specified form for producing the ESI (Code Civ. Pro. § 1985.8(c)) or objection to production on the ground that the ESI is from a source that is not reasonably accessible because of undue burden and expense (Code Civ. Pro. § 1985.8(e)); (6) “consumer’s” right of privacy in “personal records” (Code Civ. Pro. § 1985.3(e)); and (7) employee’s right of privacy in “employment records” (Code Civ. Pro. § 1985.6(e).)¿ (Weil & Brown,¿Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶¿8:598.) Further, a court may quash a subpoena entirely or partially, and issue an order to protect parties, witnesses, or consumers from unreasonable or oppressive demands including violations of privacy.  (Code Civ. Pro., §1987.1.)

 

            Here, Plaintiffs move to limit the Subpoenas on the ground that they are overly broad, seek information that is either irrelevant or beyond the reasonable scope of permissible discovery, and unduly invade an individual’s right of privacy.

Relevance

All unprivileged information that is relevant to the subject matter of the action is discoverable if it would itself be admissible evidence at trial or if it appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.  (Code Civ. Pro. § 2017.010; Schnabel v. Super. Ct. (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 711.)  For discovery purposes, information is relevant if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement.  (Gonzalez v. Super. Ct. (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1539, 1546.)  The burden is on the party seeking constitutionally protected information to establish direct relevance.  (Harris v. Super. Ct. (1992) 3 Cal.App.4th 661, 665.)

 

Here, Defendants assert that Alon is Farkhondehpour’s former attorney and is believed to be in custody, possession and control of relevant, unprivileged documents relating to Plaintiffs’ allegations and Defendants’ defenses in this action, specifically relating to the lot line adjustment and the division of the subject property.  Defendants also assert that FCB provided a loan to Farkhondehpour and Morad Ben Neman (“Neman”) relating to the Medallion property (the “Property”), and that the refinancing with FCB was completed at the same time the lot line adjustment was pursued by Plaintiffs and Neman.  Therefore, Defendants contend that the bank loan and any related communications are relevant to this case. 

 

Similarly, Defendants assert that Hakakha was a loan broker Plaintiffs used in connection with the Medallion refinancing in 2015, and that this refinancing was completed at the same time that the lot line adjustment was pursued by Plaintiffs and Neman.  Under these circumstances, Defendants contend that Hakaka’s testimony and file are key to their defense.  Finally, Defendants assert that Rosenheim was the expert on land use entitlements whom Farkhondehpour hired in relation to the phase 2 development of the Property, and that it produced documents pursuant to a subpoena issued by the arbitrator, including documents relating to phase 2 of the development of the Property.  Thus, communications with Rosenheim and documents relating to Illusion in the possession, custody and control of Rosenheim would also be relevant. 

 

Based on Defendants’ assertions, the Court finds that the information sought in the Subpoenas is relevant or appear reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and are appropriate for discovery through the Subpoenas.

 

Privacy

When the information sought to be discovered impacts a person’s constitutional right to privacy, limited protections come into play for that person.  (Shaffer v. Super. Ct. (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 993, 999.)  The protections cover both a person’s personal and financial matters.  (Id.)  The court must balance competing rights — the right of a litigant to discover relevant facts and the right of an individual to maintain reasonable privacy — in determining whether the information is discoverable.  (Id.)

 

The party asserting a privacy interest must establish the extent and the seriousness of the prospective invasion, and against that showing the court must weigh the countervailing interests the opposing party identifies.  (Williams v. Super. Ct. (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 557.)  What suffices to justify an invasion will vary according to the context. (Id.)  Only obvious invasions of interests fundamental to personal autonomy must be supported by a compelling interest.  (Hill v. Nat’l Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1,34-35.)

 

Here, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not established the extent and seriousness of the prospective invasion of privacy.  Plaintiffs assert a right to privacy, but state nothing more in support of their assertion.  Moreover, the right to privacy under the California Constitution is only applicable to natural persons and does not apply to corporations.  (Roberts v. Gulf Oil Corporation (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 770, 794-795.)  Here, the Subpoenas request records for Illusion, an entity which has no constitutional right to privacy.  Furthermore, the Court notes that the Subpoenas contained an instruction to redact any consumer’s private information.  (Motions, Exhibit A, p. 3.)  Plaintiffs’ claim of invasion of the right to privacy thus lacks merit.

 

Overbreadth

To the extent Plaintiffs assert that the requests are overbroad, Defendants claim that their defense counsel offered to narrow the scope of the subpoenas and for the parties to sign a stipulated protective order.  Defendants claim that Plaintiffs’ counsel appeared initially receptive, but that no response was received from Plaintiffs’ counsel after subsequent follow-ups.  (Declaration of Maryam Karson in Support of Opposition to the Motions, ¶¶ 3-7.)  The Court notes that Plaintiffs did not refute Defendants’ assertion regarding Plaintiffs’ counsel’s lack of communication with Defendants’ counsel to potentially resolve objections regarding the scope of the subpoenas before filing the Motions.  The Court finds that the Subpoenas are not overbroad and declines to limit their scope.

 

Monetary Sanctions

The court may, in its discretion, order the losing party to pay the prevailing party's expenses, including reasonable attorneys’ fees, incurred on the motion to quash, if it finds that the motion was “made or opposed in¿bad faith¿or¿without substantial justification¿or that one or more of the requirements of the subpoena was oppressive.”  (Code Civ. Pro., § 1987.2(a).)¿

 

Here, the Court does not find that the Motions could have been avoided by Plaintiffs’ fully participating in the meet and confer efforts that were begun on the Subpoenas.  Therefore, the Court finds that sanctions are appropriate because the Motions were made in bad faith or without substantial justification.  Thus, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ request for sanctions, but not in the full amount requested of over $8,625, representing over 20 hours of work.  The Court instead exercises its discretion and awards Defendants sanctions in the reasonable amount of $1,000, payable by Plaintiffs and their counsel, jointly and severally, within twenty days of the date of this order. 

 

RULING

Based on all the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motions are DENIED.
