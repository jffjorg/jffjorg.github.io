DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV26623    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: B

 

Hearing:             01/22/25

Re:                         19STCV26623 Veronica Ramsey, et al. vs New Era Investments, LLC, et al.

 

 

Plaintiffs Veronica Ramsey, Anthony Munoz, Alexis Lopez, Angel Lopez, Damia Munoz, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Veronica Ramsey, and Santino Munoz, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Veronica Ramsey’s Request for Default Judgment

 

Defendants: New Era Investments, LLC and Andrew Hsieh

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiffs Veronica Ramsey, Anthony Munoz, Alexis Lopez, Angel Lopez, Damia Munoz, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Veronica Ramsey, and Santino Munoz, a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Veronica Ramsey’s Requests for Default Judgment against Defendant New Era Investments, LLC and Andrew Hsieh are DENIED.

           

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from an alleged bed bug infestation. On July 30, 2019, Plaintiffs Veronica Ramsey (“Ramsey”), Anthony Munoz (“Anthony”), Alexis Lopez (“Alexis”), Angel Lopez (“Angel”), Damia Munoz (“Damia”), a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Veronica Ramsey, and Santino Munoz (“Santino”), a minor by and through their guardian ad litem Veronica Ramsey (collectively “Plaintiffs”), filed a complaint against Defendants New Era Investments, LLC (“New Era”), Louise Hsieh (“Louise”), Andrew Hsieh (“Andrew”), and Does 1 through 20 (collectively “Defendants”), alleging eight causes of action: (1) battery; (2) negligence; (3) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (4) breach of implied warranty of habitability; (5) breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment; (6) violation of Civil Code § 1942.4; (7) private nuisance; and (8) public nuisance.

 

On August 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant request for default judgment.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (CCP § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1800.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Plaintiffs seek damages in the total amount of $150,861.72, representing $60,000.00 in special damages, $30,000.00 in general damages, $60,000.00 in attorney’s fees, and $861.72 in costs.

 

Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment against Defendants is denied for the following deficiencies:

 

1.       Plaintiffs failed to dismiss Louise Hsieh (CRC 3.1800(a)(7).)

2.      Plaintiffs seek damages jointly and severally against Defendants New Era Investments, LLC and Andrew Hsieh, but fail to file one JUD-100 identifying both defendants accordingly. Instead, Plaintiffs improperly filed one JUD-100 per defendant. (CRC 3.1800(a)(7).)

3.      Plaintiffs’ JUD-100s seek attorney’s fees in the amount of $60,000.00. However, Plaintiffs failed to provide a retainer agreement or attorney declaration in support of the amount sought. (Cal. Civ. Code § 1717.5(a); CRC 3.1800(a)(9).) Additionally, the request for attorney’s fees is inconsistent among Plaintiffs’ filings, with the JUD-100s requesting attorney’s fees while the separately submitted proposed orders do not. Finally, on their face the attorney fees appear too high and need to be further justified.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiffs’ requests for default judgment against Defendants New Era Investments, LLC and Andrew Hsieh are DENIED. This represents Plaintiffs’ second default judgment denial for deficiencies, some deficiencies being identical to previous denials. (See November 20, 2024.) Therefore, pursuant to CCP § 128.7 and CRC Rule 2.30, the Court will set an Order to Show Cause Re: Sanctions for Failure to Obtain Default Judgment.

 


Case Number: 21STCV44536    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: B

Hearing: 01/22/2025

Re: JADE CARRASCO BY AND THROUGH HER GUARDIAN AD LITEM JESSE RONALD CARRASCO, et al. vs POSITIVE INVESTMENTS, INC., et al. (21STCV44536)

Petitioner Jazelle Dimas’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability

Respondent: NO OPPOSITION

TENTATIVE RULING

Petitioner Jazelle Dimas’s Petition for Approval of Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a December 17, 2019, incident in which Claimant Jade Carrasco (“Claimant”) alleges the pitbull dog of Defendants Positive Investments, Inc. (“Positive Investments”), PI Properties LLC (“PI Properties”), Becky Radgowski (“Radgowski”), and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”) attacked Claimant, causing injuries and damages.

On December 7, 2021, Claimant and Petitioner Jazelle Dimas (“Petitioner”) filed a complaint against Defendants alleging four causes of action: (1) strict liability for dog bite (Cal. Civ. Code § 3342); (2) premises liability; (3) general negligence; and (4) negligent infliction of emotional distress.

On October 16, 2024, Claimant filed a notice of settlement.

On December 31, 2024, Petitioner filed the instant Petition to Confirm Minor’s Compromise of Claim or Action or Disposition of Proceeds of Judgment for Minor or a Person with a Disability on behalf of Claimant.

ANALYSIS

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $50,000.00 from Positive Investments and PI Properties.

Legal Standard

An enforceable settlement of a minor’s or incompetent’s claim can only be consummated with court approval. (Cal. Prob. Code §§ 2504, 3500, 3600 et seq.; Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 372.) For this purpose, a petition for approval must be presented to the court and, until it is granted,

there is no final settlement. (Scruton v. Korean Air Lines Co. (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 1596, 1603-1606.) Any settlement agreement therefore is voidable by the minor’s guardian ad litem. (Ibid. at 1606.)

Prob. Code § 3600, et seq., governs how the settlement proceeds are to be paid. Pursuant to § 3601, the order shall approve payment of reasonable expenses from the settlement as follows:

“The court making the order or giving the judgment referred to in Section 3600, as a part thereof, shall make a further order authorizing and directing that such reasonable expenses, medical or otherwise and including reimbursement to a parent, guardian, or conservator, costs, and attorney’s fees, as the court shall approve and allow therein, shall be paid from the money or other property to be paid or delivered for the benefit of the minor or person with a disability.” (Prob. Code § 3601(a).)

In cases pursuant to § 3601, “unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney’s fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.” (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 7.955(a)(1).) In doing so, “[t]he court must give consideration to the terms of any representation agreement made between the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made, except where the attorney and the representative of the minor or person with a disability contemplated that the attorney's fee would be affected by later events.” (CRC Rule 7.955(a)(2).)

Discussion

Petitioner seeks court approval for a settlement under which Claimant would receive $50,000.00. Of the $50,000.00 settlement, $590.87 is to be deducted for medical expenses, $12,500.00 for attorney’s fees, and $18,634.46 for costs. (See MC-350 Item 16.) Therefore, Claimant’s proposed net settlement amount is $18,274.57.

As a preliminary matter, the court notes that Petitioner is not the authorized guardian ad litem (“GAL”) for Claimant. Claimant’s GAL in the instant action is Jesse Ronald Carrasco (“Jesse”), whose GAL application was approved on December 16, 2021. No other GAL application has been filed since.

Only an appointed GAL has the authority to enforce settlement of a minor’s claim. (See CCP § 372.) Therefore, the Petition is denied as Petitioner lacks authority to enforce Claimant’s proposed settlement.

Nonetheless, for Petitioner and Claimant’s benefit, the Court now discusses the Petition’s conformity with CRC Rule 7.950.

The Petition sets forth information that bears upon the reasonableness of the compromise

pursuant to CRC Rule 7.950. (MC-350 Attachment 8 [medical records].) Petitioner will receive $10,000.00 from Defendants as she also suffered injuries during the dog attack. (MC-350 Item 11b.) The reason for this apportionment is provided as Attachment 11b(3). Costs, while high, are reasonable given the extent of litigation. A memorandum of costs is provided as Attachment 13b. Additionally, attorney’s fees represent 25% of the total settlement agreement. (MC-350 Attachment 13a.) Claimant’s counsel provides their retainer agreement as Attachment 17a. Finally, the settlement is to be deposited in an annuity, subject to terms properly provided in Attachment 18b(3).

The Court notes that, in the future, any refiled petitions should include a proposed order on form MC-351.

CONCLUSION

Petitioner lacks authority to enforce settlement of Claimant’s claim. Accordingly, the Petition filed on behalf of minor Claimant Jade Carrasco is DENIED.

Petitioner to give notice.

Case Number: 22STCV16258    Hearing Date: January 22, 2025    Dept: B

Plaintiff Arpine Alaverdyan’s Request for Default Judgment

Defendants: Frank Binder

TENTATIVE RULING

Plaintiff Arpine Alaverdyan’s Requests for Default Judgment against Defendant Frank Binder is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

This action arises from a February 26, 2022, incident in which Plaintiff Arpine Alaverdyan (“Plaintiff”) alleges to have sustained serious injuries from Defendant Frank Binder (“Defendant”)’s negligently entrustment of his motor vehicle to Diego Sanchez Rosado.

On May 17, 2022, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Defendants Diego Sanchez Rosado (“Rosado”), Swell Property Managment, LLC (“Swell Property”), MUVR Enterprise for Car Rental, LLC (“MUVR”), and Does 1 to 100, alleging two causes of action: (1) motor vehicle negligence; and (2) negligent entrustment.

On August 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to their complaint identifying Frank Binder as Doe 1.

On December 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant request for default judgment against Binder.

LEGAL STANDARD

Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 585 permits entry of a default judgment after a party has failed to timely respond or appear. (CCP § 585.) A party seeking judgment on the default by the court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) declaration of nonmilitary status; (6) a proposed form of judgment; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorney’s fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1800.)

ANALYSIS

Plaintiffs seek damages in the total amount of $2,438,116.24, representing $715,000.00 in special damages, $1,500,000.00 in general damages, $221,500.00 in interest, and $1,616.24 in costs. (See CIV-100 Item 2.)

Plaintiffs’ request for default judgment against Defendants is denied for the following deficiencies:

1. Plaintiff failed to dismiss Does 3-100. (CRC Rule 3.1800(a)(7).)

2. Plaintiff failed to provide any evidence in support of damages sought. (CRC Rule 3.1800(a)(2); see Johnson v. Stanhiser (1999) 72 Cal.App.4th 357, 361.)

3. Plaintiff requests interest but failed to provide an interest calculation and/or authority for obtaining pre-judgment interest on a default. (CRC Rule 3.1800(a)(3).)

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment against Defendant Frank Binder is DENIED. Plaintiff is put on notice that future deficient filings risk sanctions pursuant to CCP § 128.7 and CRC Rule 2.30 and/or dismissal for failure to obtain default judgment within 45 days of default and to timely prosecute. See, CRC 3.110 (h); CCP 583.410.
