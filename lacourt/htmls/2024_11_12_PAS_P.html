DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV00916    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING MOTION BY DEFENDANT TO GRANT RELIEF FROM ADMISSIONS DEEMED ADMITTED

I.         INTRODUCTION

            This action arises from a defamation case filed by Plaintiff Ivy Zihan Geng (“Plaintiff”) against Defendant Ella Tsai (erroneously sued as Wansong Hou) (“Defendant”). Plaintiff served Requests for Admission (RFAs) on Defendant on August 30, 2023. Defendant failed to respond to the RFAs by the deadline, prompting Plaintiff to move to have the RFAs deemed admitted. The Court granted the motion on December 12, 2023, deeming all RFAs admitted and imposing sanctions on Defendant. 

Defendant has since obtained counsel and now moves to set aside the December 12 order, asserting that her failure to respond resulted from mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

Defendant filed this motion on October 15, 2024. On October 29, 2024, Plaintiff opposed the motion, and on November 1, 2024, Defendant submitted a reply.

 

Moving arguments

Defendant argues that her failure to respond to the RFAs resulted from a misunderstanding of U.S. procedural requirements, given her lack of legal background, limited English proficiency, and absence of prior experience with the U.S. court system. She asserts that, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.330, she is entitled to relief based on mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

 

Opposing arguments

Plaintiff opposes the motion, contending that Defendant’s failure to respond was due not to excusable neglect but to inexcusable ignorance of the law. Plaintiff further argues that granting relief from the admissions would cause significant prejudice, as Plaintiff has relied on these admissions in preparing for the upcoming summary judgment hearing.

 

Reply arguments 

Defendant reiterates the same arguments in reply.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A party may withdraw or amend an admission made in response to a request for admission only on leave of court granted after notice to all parties. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.300, subd. (a).) The court may permit the withdrawal only if it determines the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party’s action or defense on the merits.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.300, subd. (b).) The “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect” under CCP § 2033.300 have similar meanings as those words used in CCP § 473(b). (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1419.) 

“Because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 2033.300 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief. Accordingly, the court's discretion to deny a motion under the statute is limited to circumstances where it is clear that the mistake, inadvertence, or¿neglect was inexcusable, or where it is clear that the withdrawal or amendment would not substantially prejudice the party who obtained the admission in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.” (New Albertsons, Inc., supra, 168 Cal.App.4th at 1420–21.)

 

            III.      DISCUSSION

Defendant moves under CCP § 2033.300, asserting that her failure to respond to RFAs was due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. She argues that her pro per status, lack of legal training, limited English proficiency, and unfamiliarity with U.S. court procedures led to a misunderstanding of her obligations in responding to discovery. Specifically, Defendant claims she did not understand the consequences of failing to respond and did not believe she could be held responsible, stating she “blah blah” (Motion, p. 9). Defendant further contends that she was unable to retain counsel earlier due to financial constraints, only deciding to hire an attorney after understanding the implications of Plaintiff’s pending MSJ (Motion, p. 6). Defendant also argues that Plaintiff will not suffer substantial prejudice if relief is granted, as there is still time before the March 2025 discovery cutoff to develop the case on the merits (Motion, pp. 9-10).

Plaintiff counters that Defendant’s motion for relief is untimely, coming nearly a year after the RFAs were deemed admitted and only one month before the summary judgment hearing. Plaintiff contends that ignorance of procedural requirements is not a valid excuse under California law, as pro per litigants are held to the same standards as represented parties (Opposition, p. 2).  (See Monastero v. Los Angeles Transit Co. (1955) 131 Cal. App. 2d 156, 160 ("Under the law one may be his (or her) own attorney if he (or she) wants to be. A layman with resources who insists upon exercising the privilege of representing himself must expect and receive the same treatment as if represented by an attorney -- no different, no better, no worse."))

Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s continuous disregard for discovery requests, meet-and-confer efforts, motions to compel, and court-ordered sanctions demonstrates inexcusable neglect rather than mistake or inadvertence. Plaintiff asserts that Defendant knowingly chose to represent herself despite warnings from the Court and court orders, resulting in the deemed admissions, not through “mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect” (Id.).

Finally, Plaintiff argues that granting Defendant’s motion would be prejudicial, as Plaintiff has based her motion for summary judgment on the deemed admissions since December 2023. With the hearing less than a month away, setting aside these admissions would impair Plaintiff’s case preparation and strategy (Opposition, p. 3).

The Court agrees with Plaintiff and finds that Defendant has not demonstrated mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect that justifies relief. Defendant’s claimed lack of legal knowledge does not constitute excusable neglect; rather, her prolonged inaction in response to clear court orders and numerous discovery requests constitutes inexcusable neglect.

While an honest mistake of law may, in certain complex and debatable matters, constitute grounds for relief from judgment, courts have held that relief may properly be denied where the record reflects only ignorance of the law coupled with negligence in ascertaining it (See Hopkins & Carley v. Gens (2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 1401, 135 Cal.Rptr.3d 1). Here, Defendant’s failure to act does not reflect a mere mistake in a complex matter but rather an inexcusable lack of diligence.

The Court previously issued orders compelling Defendant’s compliance on December 11, 2023; December 14, 2023; and hearings were held on November 29, 2023; March 7, 2024; June 12, 2024; and July 19, 2024. These dates provided Defendant with multiple opportunities to engage with the process or, at a minimum, to seek assistance or clarification regarding the proceedings. Defendant’s prolonged inaction, despite frequent court appearances and orders, renders her neglect inexcusable.

Moreover, although the hearing on the motion for summary judgment was rescheduled to February 2025, the Court finds that granting relief would prejudice Plaintiff, who has reasonably relied on the admissions obtained in December 2023 for nearly eleven months in preparing the case.

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s motion.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court DENIES Defendant’s motion granting relief from admissions deemed admitted.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV02981    Hearing Date: November 12, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT SALVATORE DI BENEDETTO’S MOTION TO SEVER

I.        INTRODUCTION

          On December 26, 2023, Plaintiff E1011 Labs LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Salvatore Di Benedetto, Kevin Wang, Daniel Hwang, Shenzhen Vizi Technology Co., Ltd and Does 1 through 50. The complaint alleges (1) breach of contract, (2) conversion, (3) violation of comprehensive computer data access and fraud act, (5) violation of business & professions code section 17200, and (6) intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. 

This action involves a breach of contract along with tort and statutory claims arising from Defendants alleged misappropriation of Plaintiff’s property and business opportunities.

On October 7, 2024, Defendant Salvatore Di Benedetto (“Di Benedetto”), appearing in pro per, filed a motion to sever. Plaintiff opposed the motion on October 29, 2024, and Di Benedetto filed a reply on October 30, 2024.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

          Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1048, subdivision (b): “The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues, preserving the right of trial by jury required by the Constitution or a statute of this state or of the United States.”   

          “Whether there shall be a severance and separate trials on issues in a single action is a matter within the discretion of the trial court, whose ruling will not be disturbed on appeal absent a manifest abuse of discretion.” (Downey Savings & Loan Assn. v. Ohio Casualty Ins. Co. (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 1072, 1086.) 

          III.     ANALYSIS

Procedural issues    

 

          The Court notes that Defendant Di Benedetto did not comply with procedural requirements for service. According to the Proof of Service, Di Benedetto personally served the motion and utilized electronic service. Under California law, parties are not permitted to serve documents directly on other parties (see Caldwell v. Coppola (1990) 219 Cal.App.3d 859, 865 [holding that parties may not directly effect service of process on other parties]). Nonetheless, as Plaintiff has opposed the motion, the Court will proceed to address the merits.

 

Severance

Defendant Di Benedetto seeks to sever his case, arguing that his alleged business interactions with Plaintiff differ substantially from the employment-related claims against Defendants Kevin Wang and Daniel Hwang. Di Benedetto contends that his actions were unrelated to the core breach of contract claims involving Wang and Hwang and that trying the claims jointly would cause prejudice and confusion (Motion p. 3, Response p.5)¿

Di Benedetto further argues that he was not involved in the projects central to the claims against Wang and Hwang, namely the "Vape Joint Venture" and "Screen Project."  (Motion p.3) He asserts a lack of communication and contractual obligations between himself and E1011 Labs (Id.).

In opposition, Plaintiff opposes severance, asserting that Di Benedetto’s conduct cannot be cleanly separated from Wang’s and Hwang’s actions, as they allegedly acted in concert to misuse Plaintiff’s proprietary information and resources. (Opposition p.4) Severing claims, Plaintiff argues, would necessitate two separate trials involving the same evidence which would undermine judicial economy. (Opposition pp. 3-4)¿

The Court, exercising its discretion under Code of Civil Procedure section 1048, subdivision (b), finds that the overlapping evidence and Plaintiff’s claims of joint conduct by Defendants could result in two near-identical trials if severance were granted, thereby undermining judicial efficiency. The Court therefore concludes that severance is not appropriate. 

For these reasons, the Court DENIES Defendant Di Benedetto’s motion to sever.

 

IV.     ORDER

          Based on the foregoing, Di Benedetto’s motion to sever is DENIED.

Plaintiff is to give notice.
