DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22TRCV01631    Hearing Date: June 24, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

ENRIQUE SALAS,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22TRCV01631

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

NISSAN NORTH AMERICA, INC., et al.,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          June 24, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Enrique Salas

Responding Party:                  Defendant Nissan North America, Inc.

Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant’s Person Most Knowledgeable and Production of Documents

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

The motion is GRANTED as to Categories Nos. 17, 18, and 30-34, and DENIED as to No. 29, and MOOT as to the balance.

Defendant is ordered to produce for deposition its Person(s) Most Knowledgeable within twenty days.

The request for sanctions is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            On December 29, 2022, plaintiff Enrique Salas filed a complaint against Nissan North American, Inc. for violations of statutory obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act and fraudulent concealment.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            CCP §2025.450(a) provides:  “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”

CCP §2025.450(b) provides, “A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1)        The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2)        The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

CCP §2025.230 states:  “If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Enrique Salas requests that the Court compel the deposition of defendant Nissan North America, Inc.’s Person Most Knowledgeable (“PMK”), Nos. 1 through 34 along with Request for Production, Nos. 1 through 24.

The complaint alleges that on March 17, 2020, plaintiff leased a 2020 Nissan Sentra.  The subject vehicle was delivered to plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities to warranty, such as suspension, steering, electrical, and structural system defects, including those listed in the repair history.  Plaintiff’s repair history includes presenting to defendant’s authorized repair facility on May 31, 2020 (116 miles), April 27, 2020 (1037 miles), May 20, 2020 (1519 miles), January 11, 2021 (10,130 miles), November 16, 2021 (20,193 miles), May 5, 2022 (23,881 miles), October 19, 2022 (29,967 miles) for complaints regarding inoperative remote start function on key remote, improper engagement of Automatic Emergency Braking, faulty fuel door latch, stuck driver’s side buckle, abnormal clicking noise upon turning left, infotainment system changing color, abnormal clicking noise upon turning he steering wheel.  None of the repair attempts successfully repaired the subject vehicle.

Plaintiff argues defendant’s knowledge of the defects plaguing the subject vehicle is through its internal investigation and analysis of customer complaints, warranty data, field service reports, recalls and product evaluations.

Plaintiff explains that on November 10, 2023, plaintiff served a notice of deposition and document demand for Nissan’s PMK, for November 30, 2023.  On November 21, 2023, defendant served its objections.  On March 14, 2024, plaintiff served an amended notice of deposition of defendant’s PMK, for May 28, 2024, along with a detailed meet and confer letter, asking for alternative dates for before June 11, 2024.  On May 24, 2024, defendant served its objections.

Plaintiff argues that the scope of discovery is broad, and that defendant’s objections are without merit.  Plaintiff also argues that the categories and requests are directly relevant to plaintiff’s claims as to whether defendant complied with the Song-Beverly Act.  Plaintiff further contends that they are also relevant to defendant’s monitoring, discussions, internal investigations, and analysis of the various defects contained in the vehicle and to establishing defendant previously knew of these defects but yet refused to replace or repurchase it.  Plaintiff asserts that as evidenced by its own Technical Service Bulletins, and like records, the testimony and documents will establish that defendant is and has been aware of the defects, that the defects are ongoing, and that defendant is unable to repair the defects, and that despite all of this information, defendant acted in bad faith by willfully violating the Song Beverly Act by refusing to accept the return of plaintiff’s vehicle.

In opposition, defendant contends that plaintiff failed to adequately meet and confer as to Nissan’s objections to the categories of examination or documents served on May 24, 2024.  Rather, defendant asserts, plaintiff “merely sought a date upon which the PMK witness would be produced,” and Nissan complied by providing plaintiff’s counsel with a date for the deposition to take place on July 29, 2024.

In reply, plaintiff contends that Nissan produced a PMK on June 14, 2024 but that the deponent was not produced for matters of examination Nos. 17, 18, and 29-34.  Thus, those categories remain at issue.  

The Court rules as follows:  As stated in the reply, Nissan produced its PMK on June 14, 2024.  According to plaintiff, the deponent failed to testify as to categories Nos. 17, 18, and 29-34.  It appears that the motion is moot as to the requests for production of documents at the deposition.  The Court addresses the categories that remain at issue and considers defendant’s objections.  The Court finds that defendant’s objections lack merit as to Nos. 17, 18, and 30-34.  As to No. 29, the category is overly broad as to time and plaintiff has not shown good cause.

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED as to Categories Nos. 17, 18, and 30-34, and DENIED as to Category No. 29.

Sanctions

If a motion to compel deposition is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent or the party with whom the deponent is affiliated, unless the court finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. CCP §2025.450(g)(1).

Under CCP §2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” Under CCP §2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

The Court DENIES plaintiff’s request for sanctions as the notice is deficient under CCP §2023.040 as the notice does not “identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.”

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED as to Categories Nos. 17, 18, and 30-34, and DENIED as to No. 29, and MOOT as to the balance.

Defendant is ordered to produce for deposition its Person(s) Most Knowledgeable within twenty days.

The request for sanctions is DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

TORRANCE CARE CENTER WEST,

Hearing Date:                          June 24, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Torrance Care Center West, Inc.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion for an Order Compelling Plaintiffs to Provide Responses to First Set of Form and Special Interrogatories

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

 RULING

The motion is GRANTED.

Plaintiffs Lonnie Lee Gipson and Betty Gipson are ordered to respond without objections to defendant Torrance Care Center West’s Form Interrogatories, Set One and Special Interrogatories, Set One within twenty days.

The Court orders that plaintiffs pay a monetary sanction to defendant in the amount of $490 within 30 days.

BACKGROUND

On June 13, 2023, Lonnie Lee Gipson and Betty Gipson, ind. and as guardian ad litem of Lonnie Lee Gipson, filed a complaint against Torrance Care Center West, Inc. for (1) statutory elder abuse/neglect, (2) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights and Health and Safety Codes, (3) negligence, and (4) unfair business practices.

On March 18, 2024, the court sustained defendant’s demurrer to the first, second, and fourth causes of action with lave to amend.  To date, plaintiffs have not filed a FAC.  The court granted plaintiffs’ counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Torrance Care Center West, Inc. requests that the Court compel plaintiffs Lonnie Lee Gipson and Betty Gipson to respond to Form Interrogatories, Set One and Special Interrogatories, Set One.

            Defendant asserts that on December 11, 2023, defendant served plaintiffs with written discovery.  Responses were due by January 15, 2024.  Subsequent thereto, defendant extended the time for plaintiffs to provide responses on three separate occasions, with the last extension granted on January 30, 2024, with a due date of April 15, 2024.  On May 14, 2024, defense counsel attempted to contact Betty Gipson by phone and sent a meet and confer letter by email and registered letter, offering to provide an extension to May 22, 2024.  After receiving no response, defense counsel attempted to meet and confer and left a voice mail message.  To date, defense counsel has not received responses.

            There is no opposition.

            The Court finds that defendant properly served written discovery and plaintiffs failed to timely serve responses, and thus have waived objections.

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

            Defendant requests $682.50 in sanctions against plaintiffs.  The Court finds that $490 ($215/hr. x 2 hrs., filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against plaintiffs.

 

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.

Plaintiffs Lonnie Lee Gipson and Betty Gipson are ordered to respond without objections to defendant Torrance Care Center West’s Form Interrogatories, Set One and Special Interrogatories, Set One within twenty days.

The Court orders that plaintiffs pay a monetary sanction to defendant in the amount of $490 within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

VINCE VALDEZ,

Hearing Date:                          June 24, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Vince Valdez

Responding Party:                  Plaintiffs Glen Hamel and Cynthia Hamel

Motion to Strike Portions of FAC

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion to strike is DENIED.  Defendant is ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

            On June 29, 2023, plaintiffs Glen Hamel and Cynthia Hamel filed a complaint against Vince Valdez for (1) breach of contract – personal guarantee and (2) common counts – goods and services rendered.

            On April 22, 2024, the court granted plaintiffs’ motion for leave to amend to file a FAC.

            On April 26, 2024, plaintiffs filed a FAC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

“The court may, upon a motion . . ., or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper:  (a) Strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  CCP §436(b).

            CCP §431.10 states:  “(a) A material allegation in a pleading is one essential to the claim or defense and which could not be stricken from the pleading without leaving it insufficient as to that claim or defense. 

(b) An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:  (1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.  (2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.  (3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint. 

(c) An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.”

The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  CCP §437.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant requests that the Court strike all requests for interest and principal derived from compounded interest from the FAC at paras. 19, 22, and 27 and prayer for relief as to 1 (general damages of $2,808,623.15) and 3 (interest at an agreed upon rate of 10% from September 30, 2022 to date of entry of judgment).

            The FAC alleges that on February 14, 2020, plaintiffs and Health Care Building Investment, LLC dba Welbrook Investments, LLC entered into a written loan agreement, entitled February 2020 Promissory Note.  Defendant Vince Valdez personally guaranteed the loan.  FAC, ¶¶10, 12.  On May 10, 2020, plaintiffs and Welbrook Investments entered into another loan agreement, entitled May 2020 Promissory Note.  Defendant personally guaranteed the loan.  Id., ¶¶11, 13.  Plaintiffs stopped receiving payments from defendants under the Promissory Notes.  Id., ¶15.   In September 2022, plaintiffs obtained a default judgment in the State of Arizona in a civil action in case no. CV2022-006299 against Welbrook Investments in the sum of $2.24 million.  Id., ¶18.  Pursuant to the Arizona Judgment, plaintiffs are entitled to recovery of $2.24 million, prejudgment interest of $562,147.95, costs of $417.70, and attorney’s fees of $6,057.50, plus interest at the agreed upon rate of 10% until the Arizona Judgment is paid in full.  Id., ¶19.  By reason of the foregoing and of the failure and refusal of defendants to perform pursuant to the Guaranty Agreements and the breach of the same, plaintiffs have been damaged in the amount of no less than $2,808,623.15 ($2.24 million in principal, $562,147.95 in interest, $417.70 in costs, and $6057.50 in attorney’s fees), plus interest at the agreed upon rate of 10%.  Plaintiffs are also entitled to attorney’s fees and costs incurred in connection with the enforcement of the Guaranty Agreements.  Id., ¶22.

            Under the second cause of action for common counts, plaintiffs allege that the Guaranty Agreements set forth the reasonable value of the funds that were provided to defendant.  The unpaid sum due to plaintiffs under the Guaranty Agreements is $2,808,623.15, plus prejudgment interest at the rate of 10% per year from September 30, 2022.  Plaintiffs are also entitled to attorney’s fees under the Guaranty Agreements.  Id., ¶27.

            Defendant argues that plaintiffs are improperly seeking to recover interest as well as principal, which is derived from compounded usurious interest, and that the Court may strike requests for relief that are invalid as a matter of law.  Defendant contends that plaintiffs are not entitled to interest because the loans made by plaintiffs and guaranteed by defendant had an interest rate of 12%; thus, the loans and guaranties are usurious as a matter of law.  Defendant asserts that the remedy under California law is the complete voiding of the interest obligation—the interest rate on a usurious loan is retroactively set to zero.  Defendant contends that the lender is not permitted to recover any interest and all payments to date are attributed to principal regardless of whether the payments were initially classified as interest or principal payments.  Defendant asserts that plaintiffs have admitted in multiple forums and pleadings that they intended to charge 12% interest. 

            Defendant further argues that a lender cannot avoid the remedy for usury by purporting to reduce the interest rate after the fact, including reliance on a “usuary savings clause” that purports to reduce the interest rate on a loan below its facial interest rate if it is deemed usurious.  Such a clause is given effect only if the lender had a bona fide intent not to exceed the maximum legal rate of interest.  Moreover, defendant contends, to the extent leave to amend is granted, plaintiffs should only be allowed to seek principal, less all sums paid to them to date.

            In opposition, plaintiffs argue that the loans are not usurious because the lenders did not have the specific intent to enter into a usurious transaction.  Plaintiffs cite to the usury savings clause in the 2020 Promissory Notes, which plaintiffs argue, although not conclusive by themselves, establish plaintiffs’ intent to not charge defendants any interest that would violate California’s usury law.  Plaintiffs also contend that they have never actually collected usurious interest or any payments at all under the 2020 Promissory Notes.  Plaintiffs further argue that defendant should be estopped from asserting a usury defense because it was defendant and the original obligor on the subject loans who insisted on the 12% rate “for the purpose of defeating an action for interest on the note.”

            In reply, defendant reiterates his arguments that the 2020 Notes are “facially usurious.”

            The Court rules as follows:  “The essential elements of usury are:  (1) The transaction must be a loan or forbearance; (2) the interest to be paid must exceed the statutory maximum; (3) the loan and interest must be absolutely repayable by the borrower; and (4) the lender must have a willful intent to enter into a usurious tarnation.”  Ghirardo v. Antonioli (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 791, 798 (citation omitted).  “A transaction is rebuttably presumed not to be usurious.”  Id. (citation omitted).  “The borrower bears the burden of proving the essential elements of a usurious transaction.”  Id. at 799 (citation omitted).  One of the elements is “the lender must have a willful intent to enter into a usurious transaction.”  Id. at 798 (citation omitted).  “The element of intent is narrow.”  Id.  “The existence of the requisite intent is always a question of fact.”  Boerner v. Colwell Co. (1978) 21 Cal. 3d 37, 44 (emphasis added).  As the Court had previously stated in its ruling on the motion for leave to amend, defendant is not barred from asserting an affirmative defense based on usury.  As the existence of the requisite intent is a question of fact, the Court declines to make a finding at the pleading stage as to usury and whether plaintiffs are entitled to 10% interest or no interest.

The motion is thus DENIED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED.

            Defendant is ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

            Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

BACKGROUND

            On November 20, 2023, plaintiff Rodolfo Nicdao filed a complaint against American Honda Motor Company, Inc. for violations of statutory obligations under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act based on the purchase of a 2021 Honda Accord.

            On March 15, 2024, plaintiff filed a FAC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “(a) If a party filing a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling under Sections 2031.210, 2031.220, 2031.230, 2031.240, and 2031.280 thereafter fails to permit the inspection, copying, testing, or sampling in accordance with that party's statement of compliance, the demanding party may move for an order compelling compliance.

(b) Except as provided in subdivision (d), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel compliance with a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §2031.320.

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff requests an order compelling defendant American Honda’s compliance with its own response to plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, pursuant to CCP §2031.320.

            Plaintiff explains that on January 19, 2024, he served his Request for Production of Documents, Set One on defendant.  On March 21, 2024, defendant served its responses.  As to Nos. 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 86, 89, 90, 91, 96, 98, 100, 103, 115, 116, and 134, defendant responded that subject to and without waiving certain objections, “the requested production will be allowed in whole and all documents specific to the subject vehicle maintained in AHM’s database that are in the possession, custody, or control of AHM and to which no objections are being made will be included in the production” and specified certain documents or that it would produce documents “pursuant to the entry of an appropriate protective order.”  On April 15, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel sent an email to defense counsel requesting the documents.  On April 18, defense counsel responded that they would “look into these productions.”  On April 22, 2024, plaintiff’s counsel sent a follow-up request for defendant’s production of responsive documents.  Plaintiff asserts that, as of the filing date of the motion (on May 15, 2024), defendant had not served any responsive documents.

On May 30, 2024, the Joint Stipulation and Protective Order, Confidential Designation Only was entered. 

In opposition, defendant argues that there was no unreasonable delay.  It asserts that on May 16, 2024, even before the protective order was entered, it produced 1124 pages of documents.

In reply, plaintiff requests monetary sanctions because defendant “stalled more than two months to produce its promised documents.”

The Court finds that the motion is MOOT in light of defendant’s production of documents.  As for sanctions, the motion is DENIED as it appears that defendant produced documents once the stipulation was submitted to the court on May 16, 2024, thus acting with “substantial justification.”

 

ORDER

            The motion is DENIED as MOOT.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.

The defaults entered against defendants on March 29, 2024 are VACATED.

Defendants are ordered to file responsive pleadings forthwith.

BACKGROUND

            On January 17, 2024, plaintiffs Sun D. Lee and Kwan Lee (self-represented) filed a complaint against Chun Kil Chong, Chun Kil Chong, trustee of Chong Kil Chong Trust, Min Ju Bark, Richard Chong, and Jon Kim for (1) fraudulent misrepresentation and concealment, (2) breach of oral contract, (3) constructive trust, (4) quasi contract, (5) quiet and clear title, (6) financial elder abuse, and (7) IIED.

            On February 6, 2024, John Kim (self-represented) filed a general denial.

            On February 20, 2024, Chun Kil Chong (self-represented) filed an answer.

            On March 29, 2024, defaults were entered against Chung Kil Chong, trustee of Chun Kil Chong Trust, Min Ju Bark, and Richard Chong.  A dismissal was entered as to John Kim.

            On June 13, 2024, Chun Kil Chong filed a substitution of attorney.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

CCP §473(b) states, in relevant part:  “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . .”

“[T]he policy of the law is to have every litigated case tried upon its merits, and it looks with disfavor upon a party, who, regardless of the merits of the case, attempts to take advantage of the mistake, surprise, inadvertence, or neglect of his adversary.”  Fasuyi v. Permatex, Inc. (2008) 167 Cal. App. 4th 681, 697 (citations and internal quotations omitted).

DISCUSSION

            Under CCP §473(b), defendants Chung Kil Chong, as trustee, and Min Ju Bark, and Richard Chong request that the court set aside and vacate defaults entered against them. 

            The court notes that two motions were filed on defendants’ behalf, one on May 13, 2024 and one on May 28, 2024, which appear to be the same.

            Defendants explain that after they learned about the lawsuit, they made an appointment with Howard Lee of the International Pacific School of Law.  They found him through an advertisement promoting his experience in commercial law and met with him the second week of February 2024.  They asked his advice on how they needed to respond.  He informed them that they needed to file an answer, and he helped them prepare one.  Defendants assert that although they intended that the answer be filed on behalf of Chun Kil Chong, individually and as trustee, his wife (Min Ju Park), and his son (Richard Chong), it was deemed filed on behalf of Chun Kil Chong, as an individual only, as he was the only one to have signed it.  They assert that on April 16, 2024, they appeared at a CMC and first learned that their defaults had been entered.  Defendants then met with an attorney and filed the motion to vacate.  See Chun Kil Chong, Min Ju Bark, and Richard Chong declarations.  Defendants further argue that there will be no prejudice to plaintiffs.

            There is no opposition.

            The Court finds that defendants have met their burden of showing that the defaults were entered based on their mistake, inadvertence, and/or neglect.

            The motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

The defaults entered against defendants on March 29, 2024 are VACATED.

Defendants are ordered to file a responsive pleading forthwith.

            Defendants are ordered to give notice.
