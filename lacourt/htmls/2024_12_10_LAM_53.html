[tentative] Order RE:

 

(1)   plaintiff’s motion to compel further response to special interrogatory, set four, no. 20

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Tammy Davis

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation

(1)   Motion to Compel Further Response to Special Interrogatory, Set Four, No. 20

(2)   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Four

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with each motion.[1]

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSE TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORY, SET FOUR, NO. 20

Plaintiff Tammy Davis (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order (1) compelling defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation (“Defendant”) to serve a further response to Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatory, Set Four, No. 20, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $9,450.

The court finds that Plaintiff has not submitted a meet and confer declaration that satisfies the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040 and therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b)(1).)

“A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)  The meet and confer requirement “is designed to encourage the parties to work out their differences informally so as to avoid the necessity for a formal order . . . .  [Citation.]  This, in turn, will lessen the burden on the court and reduce the unnecessary expenditure of resources by litigants through promotion of informal, extrajudicial resolution of discovery disputes.”  (In re Marriage of Moore (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 1275, 1293 [internal quotation marks and citations omitted].)  

Plaintiff contends that she satisfied the meet and confer requirement because counsel for Plaintiff sent an email to Defendant’s counsel regarding Defendant’s response to this interrogatory on October 1, 2024, in which counsel stated (1) that the email was “the single meet and confer concerning” Defendant’s discovery responses; (2) that counsel for Plaintiff was “used to [Defendant] ignoring [counsel’s] attempts to be reasonable so this is the single email [counsel] w[ould] be sending on this topic as it is futile to attempt to reach informal resolutions[;]” (3) why counsel believed that Defendant’s objections were without merit; and (4) that Defendant had “until Friday to supplement the responses[,]” “[o]therwise [Plaintiff’s counsel] w[ould] just file yet another motion, seek sanctions, and ultimately seek evidentiary sanctions and an CACI jury instruction that [Defendant] has hidden (more) documents.”  (Harings Decl., ¶ 8; Harings Decl., Ex. 4, pp. 1-2.)  Counsel for Plaintiff did not respond to Defendant’s email.  (Stern Decl., ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff thereafter filed the pending motion on October 11, 2024.

The court finds that the October 1, 2024 email to Defendant’s counsel does not show “a reasonable and good faith attempt” by Plaintiff to informally resolve the issues presented by this motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)  Instead, counsel for Plaintiff expressly communicated that she would not discuss the issues presented by this motion further, stating that the October 1, 2024 email would be “the single email” sent to Defendant’s counsel.  (Harings Decl., Ex. 4, pp. 1-2.)  Moreover, counsel for Plaintiff did not “attempt to talk the matter over [with Defendant’s counsel], compare their views, consult, and deliberate[,]” instead asserting that the objection was without merit and demanding supplemental responses.  (In re Marriage of Moore, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th at p. 1293 [internal quotation marks and citation omitted].) 

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that she made a reasonable attempt to informally resolve each issue presented by this motion as required.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300, subd. (b)(1).)

The court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  (Opp., p. 12:8-9 [seeking $1,000 in sanctions]; Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)  The court finds that $850 ((2.0 hours x $425 per hour)) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Plaintiff in connection with this motion.  (Stern Decl., ¶ 11.)

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET FOUR

Plaintiff moves the court for an order (1) compelling Defendant to serve further responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set Four, Nos. 50-52, and (2) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the amount of $13,300.

The court finds that Plaintiff has not submitted a meet and confer declaration that satisfies the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 2016.040 and therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

First, Plaintiff did not submit a meet and confer declaration in connection with this motion because the supporting declaration of Peggy Farrell did not describe the meet and confer process and therefore did not comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(2) [“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040”] [emphasis added]; Farrell Decl.) 

Second, the court notes that, in opposition, Defendant has submitted the same October 1, 2024 email discussed in connection with Plaintiff’s motion to compel Defendant’s further response to Special Interrogatory, Set Four, in which Plaintiff referred to Defendant’s responses to the discovery that is the subject of this motion.  (Stern Decl., Ex. 6, pp. 1-2 [stating that counsel was sending “the single meet and confer” concerning Defendant’s responses to “Special Interrogatory Set 4 and Requests for Documents”] [emphasis added].)  The court finds that the October 1, 2024 email does not show that Plaintiff made “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to informally resolve the issues presented by this motion for the same reasons as set forth above.

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that she made a reasonable attempt to informally resolve each issue presented by this motion as required.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

The court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  (Opp., p. 9:4-6 [seeking $1,000 in sanctions]; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (h).)  The court finds that $850 ((2.0 hours x $425 per hour)) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Plaintiff in connection with this motion.  (Stern Decl., ¶ 11.)

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff Tammy Davis’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatory, set four, no. 20.

            The court denies plaintiff Tammy Davis’s motion to compel further responses to requests for production, set four.

            The court orders plaintiff Tammy Davis to pay monetary sanctions to defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation in the total amount of $1,700 within 30 days of the date of service of this order.

 

 

            The court orders defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation to give notice of this ruling.

defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff John Doe, A.G.

Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

The court denies defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s requests for judicial notice as to Exhibits 1-3 because Superior Court decisions are neither binding on this court nor can be considered as persuasive authority and are therefore irrelevant.  (Harrott v. County of Kings (2001) 25 Cal.4th 1138, 1148 [“Trial court decisions are not precedents binding on other courts under the principle of stare decisis”]; Sargent Fletcher, Inc. v. Able Corp. (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1658, 1668 [“‘[a] written trial court ruling has no precedential value’”].)

The court grants plaintiff John Doe, A.G.’s request for judicial notice.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

The court grants defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s requests for judicial notice as to Exhibits 4-5 and 9.  (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

The court denies defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s requests for judicial notice as to Exhibits 6-7 because those documents are not relevant to the court’s ruling on this motion.  (Malek Media Group LLC v. AXWG Corp. (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 817, 825 [“Any matter to be judicially noticed must be relevant to a material issue”].)

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff John Doe, A.G. (“Plaintiff”) filed this action for personal injuries and damages from childhood sexual abuse pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 on July 11, 2022, against Doe defendants 1 through 60.

On September 29, 2022, Plaintiff filed an “Amendment to Complaint,” identifying the true name of fictitious Doe defendant 1 to be Los Angeles Unified School District (“Defendant”).

Defendant now moves the court for an order granting its motion for judgment on the pleadings on Plaintiff’s Complaint on the ground that “the Legislature’s Assembly Bill 218 (‘AB 218’), which further amended Code of Civil Procedure section 340.1 in 2019, is unconstitutional because it retroactively strips governmental immunity from public entities in violation of Article XVI, section 6 of the California Constitution, which prohibits gifts of public funds.”  (Notice of Mot., p. 2:8-12.)

The court denies Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings because the Court of Appeal recently issued its decision in West Contra Costa Unified School District v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 1243, which is binding on this court, concluding that the “waiver of the claim presentation requirement did not constitute an expenditure of public funds that may be considered a ‘gift’ because AB 218 did not create new ‘substantive liability’ [citation] for the underlying alleged wrongful conduct.”  (West Contra Costa Unified School District, supra, 103 Cla.App.5th at p. 1257 [internal citation omitted]; Id. at pp. 1264 [again concluding that no gift occurred when the Legislature waived the claim presentation requirement], 1252 [rejecting the District-defendant’s contentions that reviving a claim that was formerly barred for failure to satisfy the claim presentation requirement would constitute an unconstitutional gift of public funds]; People v. Martin (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 825, 832 [“‘Decisions of every division of the District Courts of Appeal are binding upon all the . . . superior courts of this state . . . .’”].)

The court notes, as Defendant argues in its reply, that Roe #2, a Public Elementary School District v. Superior Court of Santa Barbara County, Case No. B334707, which concerns the constitutionality of AB 218, is currently pending before the Court of Appeal, Second Appellate District.  (Def. RJN Ex. 4, p. 13 [asserting the claimed error is based on the trial court’s denying its motion for judgment on the pleadings, which argued that AB 218’s retroactive removal of the claim presentation requirement was an unconstitutional gift of public funds].)  However, the Court of Appeal has not issued a decision in that case.  The court further notes that the California Supreme Court denied the petition for review of West Contra Costa Unified School District on October 30, 2024.

ORDER

            The court denies defendant Los Angeles Unified School District’s motion for judgment on the pleadings.

            The court orders plaintiff John Doe, A.G. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this motion.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Defendants California Landmark Group, Inc., R3 Lofts LLC, and CLG Management LLC (“Defendants”) request that the court issue a protective order that Janney and Janney Attorney Service Inc. (“Janney”) is not required to comply with the Deposition Subpoena for the Production of Business Records served on it by plaintiff Martin Ruben Ramirez (“Plaintiff”).

Defendants filed the pending motion on September 20, 2024, for a hearing date of May 27, 2025.  On September 26, 2024, the court granted the ex parte application to advance the hearing date on this motion, ordering that (1) the hearing on this motion was advanced from May 27, 2025 to December 10, 2024, and (2) deponent Janney’s obligation to comply with the Deposition Subpoena for the Production of Business Records is stayed until the court issues a ruling on the motion.  (Sep. 26, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)  Defendants gave Plaintiff notice of the court’s ruling on October 2, 2024.  (Def. Oct. 2, 2024 Notice of Ruling, pp. 2:13-16, 7-9 [proof of service].) 

On November 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed a document entitled “Plaintiff’s Omnibus Opposition to Defendant InterSolutions, LLC’s Notice of Motion and Motion for Protective Order Against Plaintiff Martin Ramirez from Taking the Deposition of InterSolutions, LLC’s CEO Brian Kares and General Counsel Stuart Skylar and Request for Monetary Sanctions Against Plaintiff in the Amount of $1,460,” in which Plaintiff appears to oppose the motion for a protective order filed by defendant InterSolutions, LLC on September 23, 2024.  (Nov. 25, 2024 Opp., pp. 1:16-17 [citing Sep. 23, 2024 Mot. for Protective Order], 6:24-26 and Exs. A-B [Notices of Deposition served on Brian Kares and Stuart Sklar].)  Plaintiff’s opposition identifies the hearing date on the aforementioned motion to be December 10, 2024.  (Nov. 25, 2024 Opp., p. 1.)  However, defendant InterSolutions, LLC’s motion for a protective order is not set on December 10, 2024; rather, Defendants’ motion for a protective order as to the Deposition Subpoena for the Production of Business Records served on Janney is set for that date pursuant to the court’s September 26, 2024 order.[1]  (Sep. 26, 2024 Minute Order, p. 1.)

            In order to give Plaintiff an opportunity to file an opposition to the correct motion, the court finds that it is appropriate, and therefore exercises its discretion, to continue the hearing on Defendants’ motion for a protective order.[2]

Special Motion to Strike First Amended Complaint

Defendant Kelli Bielema (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order striking the First Amended Complaint filed against her by plaintiffs Amanda Swulius, Bunny’s Buddies, and The Organic Bunny, Inc. (“Plaintiffs”) pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, also known as the anti-SLAPP (“strategic lawsuit against public participation”) statute.

The court finds that Defendant and Plaintiffs have not complied with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113.

“Except in a summary judgment or summary adjudication motion, no opening or responding memorandum may exceed 15 pages.”   (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1113, subd. (d).)  That “page limit does not include the caption page, the notice of motion and motion, exhibits, declarations, attachments, the table of contents, the table of authorities, or the proof of service.”  (Ibid.)  

Defendant’s opening memorandum, excluding the caption page, table of contents, table of authorities, and signature page, amounts to a total of 25 pages, thereby exceeding the page limit by 10 pages.  (Def. Memo., pp. 11-35.)  Plaintiffs’ responding memorandum, excluding the table of contents and table of authorities, amounts to a total of 17.5 pages, thereby exceeding the page limit by 2.5 pages.  (Pl. Memo., pp. 1-18.)

The court acknowledges that, on October 9, 2024, Plaintiffs, Defendant, and nonmoving defendants Gianna Massei, Christine Cassy, Phillip Blair, and Kristen Marie Nicholas lodged a joint stipulation and proposed order to permit them to file memoranda up to 25 pages in length.  (Oct. 9, 2024 Stip., p. 2:5-9.)  However, the parties did not file an ex parte application (1) requesting that the court enter an order on their stipulation before filing their special motions to strike with the court, or (2) requesting permission from the court to file longer memorandums supported by reasons why the arguments cannot be made within the stated limit.  (Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 3.1113, subd. (e).)  Further, on November 4, 2024, the court denied the parties’ stipulation.  (Oct. 9, 2024 Stip., p. 1:1-3 [“The court denies the parties’ request to increase the page limits on the demurrers and special motions to strike”].)  The parties did not thereafter file an ex parte application requesting leave to amend their memoranda to comply with California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113.

Thus, the court finds that it is appropriate, and therefore exercises its discretion, to continue the hearing on Defendant’s special motion to strike in order to give the parties an opportunity to file opening and responding memoranda that do not exceed 15 pages in length pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.1113.

The court further notes that the opening memoranda filed in connection with the special motions to strike filed by nonmoving defendants Kristen Marie Nicholas, Phillip Blair, Christine Cassy, and Gabrielle Green, Lindsay Paluba, and Kaitlyn Davis a/k/a Kaitlyn Owens[1] also exceed 15 pages in length.  (Def. Nicholas Memo., pp. 11-32 [totaling 22 pages]; Def. Blair Memo., pp. 11-35 [totaling 25 pages]; Def. Cassy Memo., pp. 12-36 [totaling 25 pages]; Defs. Green, Paluba, and Davis’s Memo., pp. 4-20 [totaling 17 pages].)  Further, Plaintiffs’ responding memorandum filed in connection with defendant Kristen Marie Nicholas’s special motion to strike amounts to a little over 17 pages.  (Dec. 4, 2024 Pl. Opp., pp. 1-18.)

The court finds that it is appropriate, and therefore exercises its discretion, to continue the hearings on the special motions to strike filed by nonmoving defendants Kristen Marie Nicholas, Phillip Blair, and Christine Cassy in order to give the parties an opportunity to file opening and responding memoranda that do not exceed 15 pages in length.  Because the hearing on defendants Gabrielle Green, Lindsay Paluba, and Kaitlyn Davis a/k/a Kaitlyn Owens’s special motion to strike is set for February 13, 2025, the court will not continue that hearing, but will order Gabrielle Green, Lindsay Paluba, and Kaitlyn Davis a/k/a Kaitlyn Owens to file a revised memorandum no later than January 13, 2025.
