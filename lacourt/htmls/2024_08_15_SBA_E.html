DEPARTMENT E LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV04038    Hearing Date: August 15, 2024    Dept: E

Los Angeles Superior Court

825 MAPLE AVENUE l TORRANCE l CALIFORNIA l 90503

 

HONORABLE ALAN B. HONEYCUTT

DEPARTMENT E – TENTATIVE RULING

 

v Case Name:

MBS Media Campus, LLC
v. MK Business Centers, LLC et al.

v Case Number:         

23TRCV04038

v Hearing Date:       

August 14, 2024

v Trial Date:

None Set [MTC Arbitration set 9/26/24]

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Motion(s):

Motion to Compel Initial Responses to Discovery

Movant(s):

Plaintiff MBS Media Campus, LLC

Opposing Party(ies):

Defendants MK Business Centers, LLC and Emily Karp

 

 

 

The motions are denied in their entirety. No sanctions.

 

Background

 

This is a commercial unlawful detainer action based on nonpayment of rent.

 

Plaintiff MBS Media Campus, LLC sued defendants MK Business Centers, LLC (“MKBC”) and Emily Karp (“Karp”) on December 1, 2023, to recover possession of commercial space within the property located at 1600 Rosencrans. Ave., Bldg. 7, Manhattan Beach 90266 (“the Property”). Plaintiff alleges Defendants agreed to rent the Property for 7.5 years at a rate of approximately $21,000.00 per month. (Compl., ¶ 6.) Defendants eventually stopped paying, and Plaintiff served them with a pay or quit notice on November 27, 2023. (Id., ¶ 10.) At the time Plaintiff served the notice, Defendants owed approximately $2.5 million in rent arrears. (Id., ¶ 12.) Defendants failed to pay off their arrears before the notice expired, and Plaintiff sued.

 

On July 17 and July 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed six (6) motions, three as to each defendant, seeking orders compelling Defendants to respond to Plaintiff’s Requests for Production (“RPDs”) and Interrogatories, and deeming admitted all matters referred to in Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission (“RFAs”).

 

On August 2, 2024, Defendants filed a consolidated opposition to Plaintiff’s six (6) motions.

 

On August 7, 2024, Plaintiff filed its reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2030.290, “[i]f a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [t]he party to whom the interrogatories are directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010.  . . .   [and] The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)-(b).)  

 

Similarly, under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300, “[i]f a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it . . . [t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010.  . . .   [and] The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.  (Id., § 2031.300(a)-(b).)  

 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (a), “[i]f a party to whom requests or admission are directed fails to serve a timely response…[t]he party to whom the requests for admission are directed waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product[.]”  (Id., § 2033.280(a).)  Where a party fails to respond to requests for admissions, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction.  (Id., § 2033.280 (b).)  The court “shall” grant a motion to deem admitted the matters specified in the requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Id., § 2033.280(c).)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff should have filed a motion to compel further discovery responses.

 

Plaintiff contends the Court should disregard Defendants’ boilerplate, all-objection responses. Plaintiff’s point is well-taken that all-objection responses generally do not indicate a good-faith participation in the discovery process. Nonetheless, objections are one form of response expressly permitted by the Code. (See Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.200(a)(3) [interrogatories], 2031.210(a)(3) [RPDs], 2033.210(b) [RFAs].)

 

Plaintiff’s argument that Defendants did not verify their responses is similarly unavailing. Objections are not factual statements, so objection-only responses do not require verification. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.250(a) [the party “shall sign the response under oath unless the response contains only objections”].)

 

Plaintiff’s motion for initial responses cannot succeed because initial responses were already provided. The motion is denied.

 

Sanctions

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under [section 2023.010 et seq.] against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel [initial responses] ... unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c) [interrogatories], 2031.300(c) [requests for production].)

 

Here, Plaintiff filed a motion that was substantively meritorious but procedurally misplaced. It would be unjust for the Court to sanction Plaintiff for a purely procedural lapse, when the insufficiency of Defendants’ responses is readily apparent.

 

The Court imposes no sanctions on Plaintiff for its unsuccessful motion.

 

Conclusion

 

The motions are denied in their entirety. No sanctions.

The motion is granted with leave to amend within twenty (20) days.

 

Background

 

This is a dispute between neighbors over damage to trees in Plaintiffs’ yard, allegedly caused when Defendants sprayed the now-dying trees with phytotoxin. Plaintiffs sued on February 6, 2024 for negligence, trespass, trespass to chattels, conversion, and violations of Code of Civil Procedure sections 733 and 3346.

 

On June 24, 2024, Defendants moved to strike Plaintiffs’ claims for punitive damages from the complaint. Plaintiffs opposed on August 5, 2024. Defendants filed no reply.

 

Legal Standard

 

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer, such as words, phrases, and prayers for damages. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436, and 437.) The proper procedure to attack false allegations in a pleading is a motion to strike. (Id. § 436 (a).)

 

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435 [notice of motion to strike whole or part of complaint], or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Id. § 436 (a).) Irrelevant matters include immaterial allegations that are not essential to the claim or those not pertinent to or supported by an otherwise sufficient claim. (Id., § 431.10.) The court may also “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id. § 436 (b).)

 

A motion to strike is also the proper procedure to attack a purportedly improper remedy such as unjustified punitive damages. (Venice Town Council, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1547, 1561-1562.) A complaint including a request for punitive damages must also include allegations showing that the plaintiff is entitled to such an award. (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)

 

Meeting and Conference

 

The statutes permitting a motion to strike contain a meet and confer requirement equivalent to the requirement for demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.) Counsel submitted a declaration that satisfies section 435.5. (King Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

Discussion

 

A plaintiff may seek punitive damages for “oppression, fraud or malice” by the defendant. (Civ. Code, § 3294(a).) “Malice” means conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct that is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(1).)  “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights. (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(2).) “Fraud” means intentional misrepresentation, deceit or concealment of a material fact with the intention of depriving a person of property or legal rights, or otherwise causing injury. (Civ. Code, § 3294(c)(3).)

 

A claim for punitive damages cannot be pleaded generally and allegations that a defendant acted “with oppression, fraud and malice” toward plaintiff are insufficient legal conclusions to show that the plaintiff is entitled to an award of punitive damages. (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) Specific factual allegations are required to support a claim for punitive damages. (Ibid.)

 

The complaint contains no facts that suggest fraud. Malice and oppression each require Plaintiff to plead a particular state of mind. Plaintiffs allege Defendants “intentionally sprayed harmful chemicals on [their] trees” (Compl., ¶ 18), but no more facts regarding their knowledge or mental state. Plaintiffs have pled the basis for punitive damages only by conclusion.

 

The motion is granted with leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court grants Defendants’ motion to strike with twenty (20) days’ leave to amend.
