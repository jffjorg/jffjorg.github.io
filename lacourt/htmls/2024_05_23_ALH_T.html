DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV05327    Hearing Date: May 23, 2024    Dept: T

Motion:                                   Motion for Relief from Waiver of Jury Trial

 Moving Parties:                      Plaintiffs Aaron Berkman and Sara Griffin

 Responding Party:                  None

The court considered the moving papers. No opposition was filed.

BACKGROUND

On April 24, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a notice of posting jury fees. On April 25, 2024, Defendant filed an objection to the notice of posting jury fees. 

On April 29, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this motion for relief from waiver of jury trial. Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition.

LEGAL STANDARD

CCP section 631 provides that “[a]t least one party demanding a jury on each side of a civil case shall pay a nonrefundable fee of one hundred fifty dollars ($150), unless the fee has been paid by another party on the same side of the case.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 631(b).)  The fee must be paid before the date scheduled for the initial case management conference in the action.  (Id., § 631(c).)  If no case management conference is scheduled in a civil action, the fee shall be due no later than 365 calendar days after the filing of the initial complaint.  (Id., § 631(c)(2).)  A party waives jury trial by failing to timely pay the fee, unless another party on the same side of the case has paid that fee.  (Id., § 631(f)(5).) 

 

“The court may, in its discretion upon just terms, allow a trial by jury although there may have been a waiver of a trial by jury.”  (Id., § 631(g).)  “A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when [] relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice¿to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver.”¿ (Tesoro del Valle Master Homeowners Assn. v. Griffin¿(2011) 200 Cal.App.4th 619, 638.)  Policy favors the court using its discretion to proceed with a jury trial.¿ (Bishop v. Anderson¿(1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 821, 823.)¿ 

 

“Where the right to jury is threatened, the crucial focus is whether any prejudice will be suffered by any party or the court if¿a motion for relief from waiver is granted. [Citations.]¿¿A trial court abuses its discretion as a matter of law when ‘…relief has been denied where there has been no prejudice to the other party or to the court from an inadvertent waiver. [Citations.]’”  (Wharton v. Superior Court¿(1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 100, 104.)¿¿“The mere fact that trial will be by jury is not prejudice per se.”¿¿(Johnson-Stovall v. Superior Court¿(1993) 17 Cal.App.4th¿808, 811 [prejudice not shown by need to prepare motions¿in limine, enlarged exhibits, and jury instructions unless inadequate time also shown].)¿¿Rather, the prejudice that must be shown is “prejudice from the granting of relief from waiver not prejudice from the jury trial.”¿¿(Winston v. Superior Court¿(1987) 196 Cal.App.3d 600, 602, 603 [fact that it takes longer to try a jury case is not prejudice from granting relief from waiver].)   

DISCUSSION

Here, Plaintiff failed to timely pay jury fees and thus waived trial by jury pursuant to CCP section 631(f). 

Plaintiffs assert the waiver of jury trial was inadvertent and has submitted a declaration from their counsel to support the assertion.  Plaintiff’s counsel states her office inadvertently filed the jury fees untimely.  (Avanesov Decl., ¶ 4.) 

The Court finds there is good cause to permit relief from waiver of jury trial in this case because of counsel’s inadvertent failure to timely post jury fees.  There will be no prejudice to Defendant as Defendant filed a notice of non-opposition. 

CONCLUSION 

Plaintiffs’ motion for order granting relief from waiver of jury trial is GRANTED.  The non-jury trial scheduled for August 5, 2024 is now a jury trial.

 

Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of this ruling.

 I.          INTRODUCTION

On April 22, 2024, Defendant filed this motion to compel Plaintiff’s responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One. Defendant also requests sanctions. As of May 21, 2024, no opposition has been filed.  

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Standard for Compelling Interrogatories – Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290 provides that a party may bring a motion to compel responses to interrogatories, where the responding party failed to serve a timely response. Unless otherwise agreed, the responding party is required to serve response within 30 days after service of the interrogatories. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.260.) “The party propounding interrogatories and the responding party may agree to extend the time for service of a response to a set of interrogatories, or to particular interrogatories in a set, to a date beyond that provided in Section 2030.260.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.270(a).) While this agreement may be informal, it must be confirmed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.270(b).)  If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, the party “any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290(a).)  

Standard for Compelling Responses¿to Requests for¿Production¿(“RFP”)¿–¿Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 provides that a party may bring a motion to compel responses to a request for production of documents, where the responding party failed to serve a timely response. Unless otherwise agreed, the responding party is required to serve response within 30 days after service of the discovery demand for production documents. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).) If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, the party “waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).) The requesting party need not demonstrate “good cause to compel responses, nor that it satisfied the “meet and confer” requirement. (Sinaiko¿Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants¿(2007) 143 Cal.App.4th¿390, 404.) 

 III.      DISCUSSION

Defendant served Form Interrogatories, Set One (“FROGs”), Special Interrogatories, Set One (“SROGs”) (collectively, the “ROGs”) and Demand for Production of Documents, Set One (“RFPs”) on Plaintiff on January 15, 2024. (Declarations of Edna Wenning in support of the motions (“Wenning Decls.”), Ex. A.)  Plaintiff requested an extension of the deadline to respond. (Id., Ex. D.) Defendant never received Plaintiff’s responses. (Id., ¶ 10.)

Defendant requests sanctions in the total amount of $1,684.95 against Plaintiff and her counsel. The Court may impose sanctions for misuse of the discovery process under CCP § 2023.030. Plaintiff’s conduct constitutes misuse of the discovery process under CCP § 2023.010, subd. (d) (“failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery”). Defendant requests $561.65 per motion, which corresponds to two hours to prepare the at $250/hour, plus a $61.65 filing fee. The requested amount is reasonable. The Court grants Defendant’s request for sanctions in the total amount of $1,684.95.

IV.       CONCLUSION

            Defendant’s motions are GRANTED. Plaintiff to serve code compliant responses to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Demand for Production of Documents, Set One within 20 days of Defendant serving notice of this Order.  Defendant’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $1,684.95, payable jointly and severally by Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel within 30 days of Defendant serving notice of this Order.   

Defendant to give notice.
