DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV07064    Hearing Date: August 6, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce G. Iwasaki    

Department 58

Hearing Date:             August 6, 2024

Case Name:                Nataly Consuelo v. Lyneer Staffing Solutions, LLC et al.

Case No.:                    22STCV07064

Matter:                        Motion to Quash Service of Summons

Moving Party:             Specially Appearing Defendants Franco Vago S.p.A. and Nippon Express Italia S.p.A.

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Nataly Consuelo

Tentative Ruling:        The motion to quash service of summons is granted.

 

 

            This is a wrongful termination action alleging fourteen causes of action. On February 25, 2022, Plaintiff Nataly Consuelo filed the complaint against Defendants Lyneer Staffing Solutions, LLC.; Employers HR LLC; Fashion Logistics, Inc.; Franco Vago L.A. INC.; Franco Vago SpA, an Italian Joint Stock Company; Nippon Express Italia SpA, an Italian Joint Stock Company; Joseph Lopez; Jose Arciega; and DOES 1-2000.  

 

On June 6, 2024, Franco Vago SpA and Nippon Express Italia SpA (Italian Defendants) specially appeared and moved to quash service of summons based on lack of personal jurisdiction.

 

On July 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On July 30, 2024, Specially Appearing Defendants filed a Reply.

 

Plaintiff relies on inapplicable law and fails to present any evidence that the Italian Defendants are subject to personal jurisdiction in this Court. The motion to quash service of summons is granted.

 

Evidentiary Objections

 

The Court sustains all of Specially Appearing Defendants Franco Vago SpA and Nipon Express Italia SpA’s evidentiary objections to the Declaration of John C. McCarty.  His declaration – a rambling, disorganized, 53-page screed – fails to establish any personal knowledge of the facts asserted. And to the extent it relies on Plaintiff’s own discovery responses, those responses similarly lacks foundation and are inadmissible hearsay.

 

Discussion

 

Legal Principles of Personal Jurisdiction

 

            “The Due Process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment constrains a State’s authority to bind a nonresident defendant to a judgment of its courts. [Citation.] [A] nonresident generally must have ‘certain minimum contacts . . . such that the maintenance of the suit does not offend “traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” ’ ” (Walden v. Fiore (2014) 571 U.S. 277, 283 (Walden).)  Personal jurisdiction may be either general or specific.  For general jurisdiction, a defendant’s forum contacts must be “so ‘continuous and systematic’ as to render [the defendant] essentially at home in the forum State.”  (Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, 139.)  The paradigm of general jurisdiction for a corporation is its state of incorporation or principal place of business. (Id. at p. 137.)

 

            Specific or case-linked jurisdiction “ ‘focuses on “the relationship among the defendant, the forum, and the litigation.” ’ ”  (Walden, supra, 571 U.S. at pp. 283-284.) In particular, “the defendant’s suit-related conduct must create a substantial connection with the forum State.”  (Id. at p. 284.)  There are three requirements for a court to exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant: “First, the defendant must have purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits or purposefully directed activities at forum residents.  Second, the controversy must relate to or arise out of the defendant’s forum-related activities.  Third, the exercise of jurisdiction must comport with traditional notions of fair play and substantial justice.” (David L. v. Superior Court (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 359, 366.)  The plaintiff has the initial burden to establish the first two requirements.  Only after doing so does the burden shift to the defendant to show that exercising jurisdiction would be unreasonable.  (Id. at p. 367.)  The plaintiff must do more than merely allege jurisdictional facts.  “ ‘It must present evidence sufficient to justify a finding that California may properly exercise jurisdiction over the defendant.’ ”  (Zehia v. Superior Court (2020) 45 Cal.App.5th 543, 552.)

 

“ ‘[P]urposeful availment occurs where a nonresident defendant “ ‘purposefully direct[s]’ [its] activities at residents of the forum” [citation], “ ‘purposefully derive[s] benefit’ from” its activities in the forum [citation], “create[s] a ‘substantial connection’ with the forum” [citation], “ ‘deliberately’ has engaged in significant activities within” the forum [citation], or “has created ‘continuing obligations’ between [itself] and residents of the forum” [citation]. By limiting the scope of a forum’s jurisdiction in this manner, the “ ‘purposeful availment’ requirement ensures that a defendant will not be haled into a jurisdiction solely as a result of ‘random,’ ‘fortuitous,’ or ‘attenuated’ contacts … .” [Citation.] Instead, the defendant will be subject to personal jurisdiction only if “ ‘it has clear notice that it is subject to suit there, and can act to alleviate the risk of burdensome litigation by procuring insurance, passing the expected costs on to customers, or, if the risks are too great, severing its connection with the state.’ ” [Citations.]’ ”  (HealthMarkets, Inc. v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1160, 1168 (HealthMarkets).)

 

            In Walden, the U.S. Supreme Court emphasized two important principles underlying the jurisdictional inquiry: “First, the relationship must arise out of contacts that the ‘defendant himself’ creates with the forum State. [Citation.]  Due process limits on the State’s adjudicative authority principally protect the liberty of the nonresident defendant – not the convenience of plaintiffs or third parties.”  (Walden, supra, 571 U.S. at p. 284.)  “Second, our ‘minimum contacts’ analysis looks to the defendant’s contacts with the forum State itself, not the defendant’s contacts with persons who reside there.” (Id. at p. 285.)  “[T]he plaintiff cannot be the only link between the defendant and the forum.  Rather, it is the defendant’s conduct that must form the necessary connection with the forum State that is the basis for its jurisdiction over him.”  (Ibid.)  Therefore, “mere injury to a forum resident is not a sufficient connection to the forum.”  (Id. at p. 290.)  “The proper question is not where the plaintiff experienced a particular injury or effect but whether the defendant’s conduct connects him to the forum in a meaningful way.”  (Ibid.)

 

The Court lacks personal jurisdiction over Defendants

 

Plaintiff alleges she was subjected to sexual, racial, disability and other types of harassment, discrimination, retaliation, failure to protect from such, and intentional misconduct in violation of the FEHA by Defendants including Franco Vago SpA and Nippon Express Italia SpA. (Compl., ¶ 1.) Italian Defendants argue that there is no general or specific jurisdiction over them because they are two Italian entities based entirely in Italy without any minimum contacts with California and have not purposefully availed themselves to California. (Mot. at pp. 4-5.) In response, Plaintiff further argues that general jurisdiction can be established by the “Representative Services” Doctrine, “Alter Ego” Doctrine, and the “Single Enterprise” Doctrine. (Opp. at p.2.)

 

Plaintiff seeks a four-month continuance to engage in jurisdictional discovery. She does not explain why this discovery was not done during the two and one-half years the case has been pending, and more important, Plaintiff fails to make any showing that discovery would elicit facts to support her allegations regarding personal jurisdiction. The discovery request is denied.

 

a. Representative Services Doctrine

 

Plaintiff contends that the representative services doctrine supports imposition of general personal jurisdiction over the Italian Defendants.  The representative services doctrine was an approach for subjecting a foreign parent corporation to general jurisdiction when its local agent exists solely to further the business of the parent. The theory rested on the idea that were it not for the local agent’s existence, the parent would be performing those functions in the forum itself.

 

Plaintiff fails even to cite the United States Supreme Court’s decision in Daimler AG v. Bauman (2014) 571 U.S. 117, which all but eliminated this theory of agency as a basis for imposing general jurisdiction over a foreign corporation. The Supreme Court unanimously reversed the Ninth Circuit’s use of this doctrine when it imposed general jurisdiction over a foreign corporation because its local subsidiary “performs services that are sufficiently important to the foreign corporation that if it did not have a representative to perform them, the corporation’s own official would undertake” them.  (Id. at p. 134.)

 

The high court rejected the assumption underlying the representative services doctrine. Even if one assumed that the local subsidiary was subject to general jurisdiction, and further that the subsidiary’s California contacts were imputable to the foreign corporation, “there would still be no basis to subject [the foreign corporation] to general jurisdiction in California, for [its] slim contacts with the State hardly render it at home there.” (Daimler, supra, 571 U.S. at p. 136.) 

 

After the U.S. Supreme Court decision in Daimler, our Court of Appeal recognized that Daimler effectively eliminated the agency theory underlying the representative services doctrine.  In Young v. Daimler AG (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 855, 866-867, the Court concluded that in Daimler, the Supreme Court held that no theory of agency, including the provision of services as a representative of a foreign principal, was a proper basis for general jurisdiction. 

 

Plaintiff’s reliance on the representative services theory fails to consider applicable precedent.  Accordingly, this basis for imposing jurisdiction over the Italian Defendants fails.

 

b. Alter Ego Doctrine

 

“The alter ego doctrine arises when a plaintiff comes into court claiming that an opposing party is using the corporate form unjustly and in derogation of the plaintiff's interests. [Citation.] In certain circumstances the court will disregard the corporate entity and will hold the individual shareholders liable for the actions of the corporation.” (Ming-Hsiang Kao v. Holiday (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 199, 205.) Whether to invoke alter ego liability depends on both: (1) “such unity of interest and ownership that the separate personalities of the corporation and the individual no longer exist,” and (2) whether “adherence to the fiction of separate existence would, under the circumstances, promote fraud or injustice. On the second score it is sufficient that it appear that recognition of the acts as those of a corporation only will produce inequitable results. [Citations.]” (Id.)

 

The court may consider factors including but not limited to: “Commingling of funds and other assets, failure to segregate funds of the separate entities, and the unauthorized diversion of corporate funds or assets to other than corporate uses; the treatment by an individual of the assets of the corporation as his own; the failure to obtain authority to issue stock or to subscribe to or issue the same; the holding out by an individual that he is personally liable for the debts of the corporation; the failure to maintain minutes or adequate corporate records, and the confusion of the records of the separate entities….” (Associated Vendors, Inc. v. Oakland Meat Co. (1962) 210 Cal.App.2d 825, 838.)

 

Here, Plaintiff fails to provide any evidence that her alter ego theory applies. As noted, Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration offers his beliefs and opinions, but no facts based on personal knowledge. McCarty’s declaration merely echoes the Complaint’s conclusory averments that Defendants are a “single enterprise.” Because she offers no specific evidence, Plaintiff’s alter ego theory fails.

 

c. Single Enterprise Doctrine

 

For the same reasons, Plaintiff’s third theory, asserting that the Defendants are all part of a “single enterprise,” cannot be sustained.  “Two conditions are generally required for the application of the [single enterprise] doctrine to two related corporations: (1) such a unity of interest and ownership that the separate corporate personalities are merged, so that one corporation is a mere adjunct of another or the two companies form a single enterprise; and (2) an inequitable result if the acts in question are treated as those of the corporation alone.”  (Tran v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1202, 1219.) 

 

Again, because Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration was stricken, Plaintiff has no evidence and thus cannot meet her burden. In contrast, Italian Defendants submit the Declaration of Davide Gori. Gori states that Nippon Express Italia S.p.A. is an Italian corporation with a principal place of business at Via 8 Marzo, 6 - 50018 Badia a Settimo, Scandicci, Firenze, Italy. (Declaration of Davide Gori [“Gori Decl.”], ¶ 2.) Franco Vago S.p.A is the same entity as Nippon Express Italia S.p.A. and is not a separate legal entity. (Gori Decl., ¶ 6.) Nippon Express Italia S.p.A. is in the business of international shipping, logistics, and distribution. (Id. at ¶ 3.) Neither Franco Vago S.p.A. nor Nippon Express Italia S.p.A. do any business in the State of California, nor have they ever applied for a California business license. (Id. at ¶ 7.)  Neither Franco Vago S.p.A. nor Nippon Express Italia S.p.A. have a bank account in the United States of America or had employees in the State of California. (Id. at ¶ 8-9.) Moreover, neither specially appearing defendant has or ever operated any offices in the State of California or own real estate in the State of California. (Id. at ¶¶ 10-11.) Neither Franco Vago S.p.A. nor Nippon Express Italia S.p.A. jointly conduct business with Defendants Lyneer Staffing Solutions, LLC, Employers HR LLC, Fashion Logistics, Inc., or Franco Vago L.A. Inc. (Id. at ¶ 13.)  The only evidence before the Court demonstrates that Plaintiff’s single enterprise theory also fails.

 

Conclusion

  Italian Defendants’ motion to quash for lack of personal jurisdiction is granted.  They are dismissed from this case.

BACKGROUND: 

 

This action involves sexual harassment and other employment law related claims.

 

On July 12, 2024, Attorney Neil Pedersen (Pedersen) of Pedersen Law, specially appearing for Plaintiff’s counsel John McCarty (McCarty), filed an ex parte application to continue the trial and other hearing dates due to McCarty’s health condition.

 

On July 15, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the ex parte application. The Court set a motion to continue trial hearing date for August 6, 2024, with the ex parte application serving as the moving papers. The Court ordered oppositions filed by July 23, 2024, and replies by July 30, 2024.

 

On July 23, 2024, Defendants Fashion Logistics, Inc. and Franco Vago L.A. Inc. (collectively, Defendants) filed a partial opposition to the motion.

 

On July 30, 2024, Pedersen, specially appearing for McCarty, filed a reply.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

 “Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits. The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (c).) The Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted: (1) proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance of trial due to any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; and (6) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subds. (d)(1)-(11).) Additionally, factors for the Court to consider include: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; and any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subds. (c), (d).) 

 

McCarty filed a declaration in support of the motion, attesting to the following facts:  “I am presently dealing with incapacitating physical and emotional issues that require my focused attention.” (Ex Parte Application, McCarty Decl., ¶ 3.) “I have a history of cancer and have presently been diagnosed with four confirmed melanomas that require surgeries and other treatment. My doctor has told me that he is worried that one or more of my melanomas have metastasized or I have new cancer issues. Testing is needed but I have delayed those tests because of the press of client needs.” (McCarty Decl., 5.) “I also have a history of heart problems including a heart attack, multiple stents placed because of great occlusion in my coronary arteries, Coronary Slow Flow Phenomenon, Afibrillation, a small Ventricular Septal Defect – a hole in the Ventricular wall along with a history of bacterial endocarditis – which can be deadly or cause a stroke - such that I need protection from it happening again. I also have had a stroke, presently have out-of-control diabetes, asthma, orthopedic injuries, Gird, nerve damage and many more maladies/injuries. Multiple doctors have been saying for a long time that if I do not stop working these long, stressful hours and/or if I do not follow up on all of these medical matters, I will die. In fact, one doctor ‘fired me’ for not following up on these ‘life and death’ matters (as it has been called) saying he did not want me to ‘die on his watch.’ For too long, I have had the need – and still do – to follow up on multiple additional cancer surgeries, nerve damage surgery, orthopedic surgery and other procedures, but could not get to them with my long hours and not getting the help I needed from my present and prior law firms. If that was not enough, I am assisting in the care of a deaf elderly loved one.” (McCarty Decl., ¶ 6.) “Emotionally, I was recently hospitalized because of my emotional condition.” (McCarty Decl., ¶ 10.)

 

McCarty further testifies that he has sent messages to other employment lawyers and four top employment lawyers have responded and agreed to take over one or more of his cases. (McCarty Decl., ¶ 10.) McCarty will be leaving his current firm but will work with those lawyers to the extent that he can. (McCarty Decl., ¶ 11.) Pedersen (an employment law specialist and professor with decades of experience) will be making special appearances in many of McCarty’s cases to help assist with the transition process. (McCarty Decl., ¶ 11.)

 

Defendants do not dispute that there is good cause to continue the trial date given McCarty’s current health condition.

 

However, they ask that the Court only continue the discovery cut-off date for some (not all) forms of discovery.

 

They also ask the Court to set a further status conference in early September regarding whether McCarty has been able to find an attorney to replace him in this matter.

 

 

The Court finds good cause to continue the trial date given McCarty’s health records.

 

The Court also finds it will be more efficient and less confusing for the parties to continue all trial-related dates instead of carving out deadline exceptions for some discovery.  Therefore, Defendants’ request to continue the discovery cut-off dates for some discovery but not others is denied.

 

However, while the Court grants the unopposed request to continue the trial, the Court sets new discovery cut-off dates:  Discovery must be completed no later than the 60th day before the new trial date.  The last date for hearing a discovery motion will be the 30th day before the new trial date. 

 

The Court also sets a status conference on September 30, 2024 at 8:30 a.m. in Dept. 58.

 

The Court therefore grants Plaintiff’s motion to continue trial and all related dates. The date for Trial is advanced and continued to _______________, 2025, at 9:00 a.m., Dept. 58, Stanley Mosk Courthouse.  The Final Status Conference is advanced and continued to ____.

 

The date for the hearing on Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is not changed: it remains on October 10, 2024.
