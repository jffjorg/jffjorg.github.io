DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV17040    Hearing Date: March 7, 2024    Dept: 61

Plaintiff Ultimate Action, LLC’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED in part. Monetary sanctions are awarded against Defendant Patricia Schwartz and is ordered to appear for deposition.   

 

Plaintiff Ultimate Action, LLC’s Motions to Compel Deposition Answers from Esteban Felix and Lin S. Yit are GRANTED as to all questions at issue save those related to the amount of Insignia’s profits, Patricia Schwartz’s pericarditis, and the subject matter of deponents’ communications with their counsel. Sanctions are awarded in favor of Plaintiff.

 

Defendant Patricia Schwartz’s Motion to Quash Deposition Subpoenas to Eisenhower Desert Cardiology Center and Samy M. Sharobeem, M.D. is GRANTED. Sanctions are DENIED.

 

Plaintiff to give notice.

 

 

I.                   MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

The court may impose terminating sanctions, include an order striking pleadings, and order dismissing an action, or an order rendering judgment by default against a party, for conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030.) This conduct include “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery,” and “[d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010.)

Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.  (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.)  Dismissal is a drastic measure, and terminating sanctions should only be ordered when there has been previous noncompliance with a rule or order and it appears a less severe sanction would not be effective.  (Link v. Cater (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1326.)  “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.”  (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.)

Plaintiff Ultimate Action, LLC (Plaintiff) moves for terminating sanctions against apparently all Defendants, based on Defendant Patricia Schwartz’s failure to appear for deposition as ordered on January 18, 2024.

Plaintiff presents the following facts. This court’s ruling of November 27, 2023, directed that the depositions of Defendants Scott and Patricia Schwartz were to be scheduled between January 11 and 18, 2024. (See 11/27/23 Ruling.) Scott and Patricia Schwartz’s deposition dates were accordingly scheduled, as of December 1, 2023, for January 17 and 18, 2024. (White Decl. ¶ 19.) When Plaintiff on January 16, 2024, sent emails seeking to confirm that depositions would go forward as scheduled, Defendants’ counsel responded that Scott Schwartz’s deposition would go forward, but that “Patricia was recently hospitalized and will be unable to attend her deposition on Thursday.” (White Decl. Exh. 8.) Defendant sent a note dated January 12, 2024, from Dr. Ali Naderi, of the Eisenhower Desert Cardiology Center, stating that “Ms. Schwartz is a patient of our practice. She’s currently being actively treated and needs to have regular follow-up with our office. It is of high importance that she is not under physical or emotional stress during this time.” (White Decl. Exh. 9.) Defendant’s counsel offered to continue the trial date and associated deadlines “to allow a later deposition date of Patricia.” (White Decl. Exh. 10.)

Scott Schwartz’s deposition went forward on January 17, 2024, but Patricia Schwartz’s did not. (White Decl. ¶ 25.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that no deposition dates were offered for Patricia’s deposition as of the filing of the motion, but that she has since offered to appear at mediation on February 8, 2024, and was present for the mid-December deposition of Ted Fox, which lasted all day. (White Decl. ¶ 26.) On January 22, 2024, Defendant mailed Plaintiff another letter, this time from Patricia Schwartz’s primary care physician, Dr. Samy Sharobeem, stating that Patricia Schwartz suffered from “acute pericarditis” and “is medically advised to avoid both physical and emotion stressors that may exacerbate her condition.” (Opposition Exh. 6.)Plaintiff on January 25, 2024, served subpoenas on Eisenhower Desert Cardiology Center and Sharobeem, seeking documents related to Schwartz’s pericarditis. (Opposition Exh. 7, 8.)

On February 21, 2024, after the present motion was filed, this court granted Plaintiff’s ex parte application to shorten the time for hearing and briefing on the present motion, Defendant’s motion to quash the subpoenas, and Plaintiff’s motions to compel deposition answers, with opposition materials due on March 4 and replies due on March 6, 2024. (See 2/21/24 Order.)

On February 26, 2024, Schwartz’s counsel informed Plaintiff that Patricia Schwartz could appear remotely for deposition on March 7 and 8, the day of or the day after the hearing date scheduled for these motions. (Tirtasaputra Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. 10.) Plaintiff’s counsel responded that the dates were unavailable, as March 7 was the date of hearing on these motions, and March 8 was already occupied for another deposition. (Ibid.) Plaintiff also stated that Schwartz “does not have a right to have her deposition taken remotely,” and did not agree to any remote deposition. (Ibid.) When Defendant responded that a remote deposition was a reasonable accommodation for her medical condition, and further stated that March 8 was “her only available date,” Plaintiff responded that they did not agree to a remote deposition, and asked why March 8 was her only available date. (Ibid.) Defendant responded that Patricia Schwartz could be available on March 6, 7, or 8. (Ibid.) When Plaintiff protested that these dates were already occupied by motion practice or another deposition, Defendant responded that the hearing date on March 7 was only in the morning, and “In any case, Patricia can be deposed in person on the 8th if you’re willing to move Mr. McBride’s deposition.” (Ibid.) Plaintiff declined the offer, stating that Patricia Schwartz’s changing availability and need for remote deposition belied her claim to be unavailable, and accused Plaintiff at length of using illness as a pretext to frustrate Plaintiff’s right to discovery. (Ibid.)

Patricia Schwartz in opposition presents her declaration, in which she states:

At the time my deposition was scheduled, I felt prepared to sit for it. However, I was rushed to the emergency room on two occasions due to my sudden onset of acute pericarditis symptoms of sharp chest pain, shortness of breath, cough, and pains in my back and left shoulder. Following my hospital trips, I was advised that it would take a couple months until my acute systems cleared and that, in the meantime, I must avoid stressful activities as such activities would likely worsen my symptoms and counteract the positive effects of my treatment. Accordingly it was concluded that it was best for my health to cancel and reschedule my deposition to a date after my treatment is complete.

(Schwartz Decl. ¶ 4.) Schwartz states, “In order to not prolong this matter anymore, and against the advice of my doctors, I agreed to be deposed . . . in person.” (Schwartz Decl. ¶ 8.)

Plaintiff’s showing is persuasive in part. Patricia Schwartz is an under a court order to appear for deposition by a certain date, and Plaintiff has shown that she has failed to appear as ordered. Just prior to her scheduled deposition, her counsel represented that she was “recently hospitalized” (White Decl. Exh. 8), but Schwartz in her declaration provides no indication as to when her trips to the emergency room occurred. Indeed, Scott Schwartz testified at his January 17, 2024 deposition that he had known of Patricia’s condition for six to twelve months prior.(White Decl. Exh. 5 at pp. 22–23.) The condition was not brought to the court’s attention when it ordered the deposition to take place.

It is unclear why these matters are framed so evasively in Defendant’s opposition. Patricia’s declaration in support of her motion to quash, filed February 2, 2024, frames the facts in a more straightforward manner: Patricia Schwartz suffers from pericarditis, which causes acute chest pain, and for which she is currently undergoing treatment. (2/2/2024 Schwartz Decl. ¶¶  2–3.) Although she believed she was ready for deposition, “in the days leading up to the deposition and in anticipation of the same, I experienced higher than usual stress, which, in turn, exacerbated my heart condition and caused worsening symptoms.” (Id. at ¶ 6.) She initially withheld information concerning the precise nature of her condition because she considered it private information. (Id. at ¶¶ 9–10.)

This reticence was error. Patricia Schwartz was under a court order to appear for deposition. If she required accommodations in the conduct of that deposition, she ought to have disclosed the same and, if necessary, sought a protective order from the court. She instead unilaterally cancelled the deposition with no indication as to when or how it could safely resume, only to propose one accommodation (that the deposition be taken remotely) after Plaintiff had moved for sanctions, an accommodation that Schwartz now appears ready to abandon. (Schwartz Decl. ¶ 8.)

Sanctions are therefore appropriate, though not in the drastic form that Plaintiff seeks. The threat of sanctions evidently caused Schwartz to offer limited dates of deposition after the present motion was filed. More limited sanctions may therefore curb any further discovery abuses. Moreover, any prejudice that Plaintiff suffers by a belated deposition so close to the trial date may be ameliorated by continuing trial. Though such an order is not desirable, it is preferable to the loss of an opportunity to try the case on the merits. (See Weitz v. Yankosky (1966) 63 Cal.2d 849, 854 [“It is the policy of the law to favor, wherever possible, a hearing on the merits”].)

Plaintiff seeks $35,160.00 in sanctions, representing 23 hours ($15,525) of attorney work preparing and defending the motion, five hours to bring an ex parte application to shorten time for hearing ($3,375), four hours preparing for the now-missed deposition ($2,700), and 20 hours ($13,500) preparing for trial, which Plaintiff anticipates may be made irrelevant depending on the timing and resolution of this motion, at an hourly rate of $675 per hour. (White Decl. ¶ 29.)

The motion for sanctions is GRANTED in part. Monetary sanctions are awarded against Defendant Patricia Schwartz in the amount of $1,500. Patricia Schwartz is directed to appear for deposition before 3/18/24.

II.                MOTION TO QUASH SUBPOENAS

“If a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, electronically stored information, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court's own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1, subd. (a).) A party may bring a motion under this section. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1987.1, subd. (b)(1).)

 

Defendant Patricia Schwartz moves to quash two deposition subpoenas issued by Plaintiff against Defendant’s physicians Eisenhower Medical Center and Samy Sharobeem on January 25, 2024. These subpoenas seek all documents and communications concerning Patricia Schwartz’s pericarditis from January 1, 2023 to the present. (Motion Exhs. 1, 2.) Defendant argues that her pericarditis is relevant only to why she could not attend the deposition previously ordered by the court, not to any underlying issue in the case. (Motion at pp. 6–9.)

 

The California Constitution protects an individual’s right to privacy. (Davis v. Superior Court (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1008, 1013.) The right to privacy extends to medical records. (John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1198.) Communications between patients and their physicians or psychotherapists are also protected by statutory privileges. (Evid. Code §§ 994 [physician-patient], 1014 [psychotherapist-patient].)

None of these protections or privileges is absolute. Physician-patient privilege does not exist if the communication sought is “relevant to an issue concerning the condition of the patient if such issue has been tendered by . . . the patient.” (Evid. Code § 996, subd. (a).) In the constitutional privacy context, “[t]he party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious. The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.)

Defendant’s argument is persuasive. Her health is relevant to no underlying issue in the case, but only to her ability to attend the scheduled deposition. Given the obvious gravity of the privacy interest involved, and the ancillary nature of the issue for which Defendant’s condition has only arguably been tendered, the discovery sought is not worth the substantial invasion of privacy contemplated.

The motion to quash is GRANTED.

Defendant seeks $11,864.50 in sanctions, representing 20 hours of attorney work at $555 per hour, plus 1.1 hours at $695 per hour. (Tirtasaputra Decl. ¶¶ 13–15.) Such sanctions are awardable in the court’s discretion under Code of Civil Procedure § 1987.2, subd. (a). Sanctions are DENIED.

III.             MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION ANSWERS

“If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.”  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.480, subd. (a).) The party moving for an order compelling third-party compliance with a deposition subpoena must make a fact-specific showing of good cause for the request. (See Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 223–24.)

 

Plaintiff moves to compel answers to deposition questions from third parties Esteban Felix and Lin S. Yit, co-owners of Defendant Insignia PMG. Plaintiff contends that Insignia’s counsel interposed meritless objections during the depositions of these two witnesses, and instructed them not to answer relevant questions concerning Plaintiff’s allegations that Insignia is an alter ego of the Schwartz defendants and the intermediary for impermissible payments to themselves in the form of management fees. (SAC ¶¶ 14, 28.)

 

Deponents should generally “not be prevented by counsel from answering a question unless it pertains to privileged matters or deposing counsel's conduct has reached a stage where suspension is warranted.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1015.) “It is generally improper . . . for counsel to instruct a witness not to answer on grounds other than privilege, privacy, trade secrets or other matters statutorily or constitutionally exempt from discovery.” (Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial (The Rutter Group 2023) Ch. 8E-12 [8:695].)

Certain of Insignia’s objections to the questions offered to Felix and Yit were appropriate, and no further response is warranted. Plaintiff at one point asked Lin Yit if she had gone over depositions preparations with her attorney, which concerned straightforwardly privileged matters, i.e. the subject of confidential communications between attorney and client. (Evid. Code § 952.) The motion is DENIED as to this question.

 

Moreover, Civil Code § 3295 states that “[n]o pretrial discovery by the plaintiff shall be permitted with respect to” evidence of a defendant’s profits gained by virtue of wrongful conduct or the financial condition of the defendant unless the court grants a motion made by the plaintiff showing a substantial probability that they will prevail on their punitive damages claim. (Civ. Code § 3295, subd. (c).) Plaintiff’s questions concerning Insignia’s “profits . . . year-over-year” and whether Insignia has “made a healthy profit” go directly to its financial condition under Civil Code § 3295. (Separate Statement at pp. 16–17.)

 

Plaintiff argues that these questions relate to its alter ego allegations against Insignia. This is partly correct. “[I]nformation as to the financial condition of a corporate defendant is admissible and discoverable before trial in an action involving an alter ego issue.” (Doak v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1968) 257 Cal.App.2d 825, 835, fn. 8.) Because such evidence goes to underlying liability rather than to the case for punitive damages, Civil Code § 3295 does not forbid discovery into it. (See Rawnsley v. Superior Court (1986) 183 Cal.App.3d 86, 91 [holding that trial court’s denial of discovery for financial information relevant to the underlying case was abuse of discretion because Civil Code § 3295 did not apply].) However, the justification for inquiries directly concerning the amount of Insignia’s profits have little to do with the factors underlying alter ego liability, and rather relate, as Plaintiff acknowledges, “to the general financial performance of insignia.” (Separate Statement at p. 18.) Although Plaintiff argues that this discovery also relates to how Insignia “allocates and distributes” its profits, these questions did not ask concerning how profits were allocated.

 

Certain other objections are proper based on privacy, without any countervailing good cause to justify a further response. “In determining whether disclosure is required [against a privacy objection], the court must indulge in a ‘careful balancing’ of the right of a civil litigant to discover relevant facts, on the one hand, and the right of the third parties to maintain reasonable privacy regarding their sensitive personal affairs, on the other. The court must consider the purpose of the information sought, the effect that disclosure will have on the affected persons and parties, the nature of the objections urged by the party resisting disclosure and availability of alternative, less intrusive means for obtaining the requested information. Based on an application of these factors, the more sensitive the nature of the personal information that is sought to be discovered, the more substantial the showing of the need for the discovery that will be required before disclosure will be permitted.” (Hooser v. Superior Court (2000) 84 Cal.App.4th 997, 1004, internal citations omitted.)

 

These proper privacy objections include questions related to the deponents’ knowledge of Patricia Schwartz’s pericarditis, and questions concerning whether income from Insignia was the only source of income for the deponents. Parties possess a limited right to privacy in their financial affairs. (Valley Bank of Nevada v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 652, 656.) The deponents here are not parties to this dispute, and their sources of income beyond those derived from the party-defendant (Insignia) is of scant relevance. Questions related to Schwartz’s pericarditis, as discussed in the motion to quash, implicate concerns for medical privacy and are of little relevance to the underlying case.

 

The remaining objections — indeed, the vast majority — furnished no basis to instruct the deponents not to answer, and further responses are properly compelled. Insignia argues that Plaintiff questioned Felix concerning his “private romantic life.” (Opposition at p. 4.) Plaintiff actually asked Felix — who is married to Yit, and who founded Insignia with her — whether he and Yit were together when they founded Insignia. (Separate Statement at pp. 5–6.) Plaintiff asked no questions concerning their “sexual relations.” (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 835.)

Other objections based on attorney-client privilege were unsupported. These objections were not addressed to confidential communications, but the date on which communications occurred. “The privilege does not protect independent facts related to a communication; that a communication took place, and the time, date and participants in the communication.” (2,022 Ranch v. Superior Court (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 1377, 1388, internal quotation marks omitted.)

The other privacy objections were also of little merit. Insignia interposed privacy objections to questions ranging from the address of the deponent, their capital contributions to Insignia, their share of ownership in the company, and other questions related to its corporate management. It is, however, Insignia’s burden to establish both a reasonable expectation of privacy and the prospect of a serious intrusion into that interest before a privacy objection may be entertained. (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) Defendant establishes no such interest or intrusion as to these remaining matters, noting only that the questions concern finances in some way, and citing to authority concerning much more sensitive materials, such as corporate trade secrets or consumer bank records. (See Hofmann Corp. v. Superior Court (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 357, 363 [concerning secret customer lists]; Valley Bank, supra, 15 Cal.3d 652 [bank records].) The questions here, however, relate to the amounts Insignia’s owners invested in the company, their ownership shares obtained thereby, and the manner by which profits were allocated among them. Given the minimal showing here, any privacy interest or intrusion resulting from these questions, whether belonging to Insignia or its owners, is de minimis, and in any event overborne by Plaintiff’s interest in obtaining discovery concerning the facts of this case and Insignia’s potential alter ego liability. The same is true of questions related to the number of Insignia’s clients or properties.

Insignia argues that Plaintiff’s alter ego theory is infirm. (Opposition at p. 4.) But Insignia remains a party to this case, and has neither sought nor obtained any dispositive relief by demurrer or motion for summary judgment. Insignia’s objection to its inclusion in this lawsuit furnishes no basis for instructing the deponents not to answer relevant questions at deposition.

Insignia argues that any matters ordered disclosed pursuant to this motion ought to be designated “for attorney’s eyes only” pursuant to the protective order entered in this matter. (Opposition at p. 8.) Insignia and the deponents have made no showing that would justify such an order.

Insignia finally argues that Plaintiff failed to adequately meet and confer. (Opposition at pp. 9–10.) But Insignia acknowledges that that the parties held a 90 minute conference on these very issues before the motion was filed. (Opposition at pp. 9–10.) The objection is not based on any failure to confer, but rather upon Plaintiff’s cutting off further conference efforts when a tentative settlement had been reached. (Ibid.) Given the opposition here, however, no such resolution to this dispute has been reached.

The motions are therefore GRANTED as to all questions at issue save those related to the amount of Insignia’s profits, Patricia Schwartz’s pericarditis, and the subject matter of deponents’ communications with their counsel.

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel an answer or production, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (j).)

Plaintiff seeks $12,680.25 in sanctions connected with the Felix motion, representing 14 hours preparing and defending the motion at $675 per hour, $3,845.25 for the deposition, and a $60 filing fee. (White Decl. ¶ 12.) Plaintiff seeks $12,193.40 in connection with the Lin Yit motion, representing another 14 hours to prepare and defend the motion $3,358 for the deposition, and a $60 filing fee. (White Decl. ¶ 10.) These sanctions are to be imposed against the third party deponents and/or their counsel. (White Decl. ¶ 10.)

Sanctions are awarded against deponents and their counsel in the amount of $3,000.


Case Number: 24STCP00048    Hearing Date: March 7, 2024    Dept: 61

 

Peachtree Settlement Funding, LLC’s Petition for Approval of Payment Rights from K.L. is GRANTED.

 

I.      TRANSFER PETITION

Insurance Code section 10136 states:

(a) No direct or indirect transfer of structured settlement payment rights by a payee to which this article applies shall be effective, and no structured settlement obligor or annuity issuer shall be required to make any payment directly or indirectly to a transferee, unless all of the provisions of this section are satisfied.

(b) Ten or more days before the payee executes a transfer agreement, the transferee shall provide the payee with a separate written disclosure statement, accurately completed with the information that applies to the transfer agreement, in substantially the following form . . . .

In order for a court to approve such transfer, certain statutory provisions as set forth in Section 10139.5 must be met. These include findings of the following:

(1) The transfer is in the best interest of the payee, taking into account the welfare and support of the payee's dependents.

(2) The payee has been advised in writing by the transferee to seek independent professional advice regarding the transfer and has either received that advice or knowingly waived, in writing, the opportunity to receive the advice.

(3) The transferee has complied with the notification requirements pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (f), the transferee has provided the payee with a disclosure form that complies with Section 10136, and the transfer agreement complies with Sections 10136 and 10138.

(4) The transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority.

(5) The payee understands the terms of the transfer agreement, including the terms set forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136.

(6) The payee understands and does not wish to exercise the payee's right to cancel the transfer agreement.

A court must also find that the transfer is “fair and reasonable” in light of the payee’s best interest and their dependents. (Ins. Code § 10137.)

Payee Kadie Lobo (Payee) submits a declaration in support of this petition to transfer payment rights to Petitioner Peachtree Settlement Funding, LLC (Petitioner). Payee is 28 years old, married, with on minor child, age three. (Amended Lobo Decl. ¶ 8.) By the proposed transfer of rights to four payments of $10,000 each, due to be paid on the third of June for the years 2027–2030, Payee has agreed to receive a lump sum of $15,419.09. (Lobo Decl. ¶ 5.) Payee states that the payments were not intended to pay for future medical care or necessary living expenses. (Lobo Decl. ¶¶ 6–7.) Payee intends to use the lump sum payment to pay current living expenses while transitioning back into the workforce. (Lobo Decl. ¶ 11.) Payee previously obtained court approval for a transfer of rights to three annual payments of $12,500.00 to take place on June 3, 2024–2026. (Lobo Decl. ¶ 9.)

Payee has received and signed a disclosure form in compliance with Insurance Code § 10136 and states that they have read and understood the transfer agreement and do not wish to cancel it. (Lobo Decl. ¶ 13; Petition Exhs. A, B.) The disclosure form states that the discounted present value of the payment rights is $31,221.43, and if Payee instead obtained a loan in the amount of the lump sum and paid back the loan with the installments to be transferred, the interest on the loan would be 21.99% per year. (Petition Exh. B.)

Based on the above, the transfer is fair and reasonable in light of Payee’s best interest. The Payee has been advised to seek independent counsel, the required disclosure form has been provided, the transfer does not contravene any statute or court order, and the Payee understands the terms of the transfer. Payee does not wish to cancel the transfer.

The petition is GRANTED.
