Case Number: 22VECV00579    Hearing Date: October 30, 2023    Dept: T

BALOUCH V GENERAL MOTORS 22VECV00579

[TENTATIVE] ORDER:  Defendant General Motors LLC’s Demurrer to the First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

Defendant General Motors LLC’s Motion to Strike Punitive Damages is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first through fourth causes of action; and DENIED as to the fifth cause of action.

Defendant is ordered to file an Answer to the First Amended Complaint within 20 days.

Introduction

Defendant General Motors LLC (Defendant) demurred to Plaintiff Amir Balouch’s (Plaintiff) First Amended Complaint (FAC).  Defendant demurred to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action (COA) for fraudulent inducement-concealment.  Defendant moved to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages.

Discussion 

Defendant argued that there are insufficient facts alleging Defendant’s knowledge of the defects in Plaintiff’s vehicle at the time of purchase and Defendant’s intent to defraud.  This pleading defect was previously reviewed at the prior demurrer to the Complaint’s fraud COA.  Plaintiff cured the defective pleading to expressly allege that Defendant knew of the battery defect since 2016 that the model year 2017 or newer Chevrolet Bolt EV, including Plaintiff’s vehicle, contained the battery defect and that Defendant knew of the defect in Plaintiff’s vehicle based upon “pre-production and post-production testing data, early consumer complaints about the Battery Defect made directly to GM and its network of dealers, aggregate warranty data compiled from GM’s network of dealers, testing conducted by GM in response to these complaints, as well as warranty repair and part replacements data received by GM from GM’s network of dealers, amongst other sources of internal information.”  Plaintiff further alleged that the battery in 2017-2022 Chevrolet Bolt vehicles, including Plaintiff’s vehicle, shared common battery system components and lithium-ion battery which included the battery defect.  Plaintiff also alleged that Defendant made communications to the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) relating to the battery components in the Chevrolet Bolt EV.  (FAC pars. 24-25, 27, 29-32.)  These new allegations plead sufficiently specific facts as to Defendant’s knowledge of the defect and that the knowledge of the defect was inclusive of Plaintiff’s vehicle.  Specifically as to the element of intent to deceive, Plaintiff’s FAC alleged that Defendant concealed the defect from Plaintiff prior to and at the time of entering the lease based upon the allegation that Defendant informed consumers that the vehicles were functioning properly or conducted repairs that only masked the defect.  Further, Plaintiff alleged that Defendant failed to inform consumers of the defect in sales materials, advertisements, publications, online marketing, television, radio, and other marketing campaigns” and concealed the defect with ineffective repair procedures published to its dealerships.  (FAC pars. 34-36.)  These additional fact allegations are sufficient to plead supportive facts as to the intent to defraud element.  Plaintiff’s FAC cured the pleading defects raised in the prior demurrer to the Complaint and the argument made against the instant FAC is not persuasive.

Defendant argued that there is insufficient fact pleading to allege that Defendant had a duty to disclose.  Defendant asserted that Plaintiff failed to allege any transaction involving “direct dealings.”  (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 311-312.)  This argument was reviewed at the prior demurrer to the original Complaint.  The Court agreed with the argument raised previously.  However, Plaintiff presented case law both then and in opposition to the instant demurrer to show that the duty to disclose, as to the manufacturer of a vehicle, is imposed if there is (1) an affirmative misrepresentation, or, (2) if plaintiff alleges “physical injury or . . . safety concerns posed by the defect” at issue.  (Daugherty v. Am. Honda Motor Co. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 824, 836.)  As reviewed previously, the Complaint, as well as the FAC, sufficiently pled facts to allege “safety concerns posed by the defect”  (i.e., loss of propulsion, catastrophic fire, no crank, reduced range, thermal runaway, and/or spontaneous combustion.  (See FAC pars. 24-26, 39-40, and 92.))  As reviewed previously and reviewed now, Plaintiff sufficiently pled facts to allege a duty to disclose within the FAC.

In response to arguments in the Opposition, Defendant’s Reply added that the FAC failed to allege sufficient facts as to the pleading element of a concealment and failed to plead facts to allege the element of damages.  Despite Defendant’s argument otherwise, Plaintiff sufficiently pled facts to allege that Defendant concealed the battery defect.  (FAC pars. 24-27, 90-92.)  Factual pleading as to the element of damages is also sufficiently alleged because Plaintiff pled that he would not have entered into the lease had he known of the defect.  (FAC pars. 8-9, 37, 94, and 102.)  The arguments as to the elements of concealment and damages are not persuasive.

The Demurrer to the FAC is OVERRULED.

Defendant moved to strike the request for punitive damages made in Prayer, par. g.  Defendant argued that punitive damages are not recoverable under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.  This issue was resolved at the prior demurrer to the original Complaint, wherein the Court reviewed the case law authority and arguments presented by both parties.  The parties again asserted the same legal authority and argument previously raised.  Re-asserting the same argument and legal authority previously raised did not change the Court’s position on the issue.  Further, Plaintiff expressly represented to the Court that punitive damages are not being requested under the first four COAs.  Despite Plaintiff’s representation to the Court previously, Plaintiff’s instant Opposition showed that Plaintiff is seeking punitive damages under the first four COAs.  As such, the Court reiterates its analysis from the prior demurrer hearing.

Defendant argued that punitive damages is not proper under the first four COAs for violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civ. Code, sec. 1790 et seq.; The Act) because civil penalties are “akin to punitive damages.”  (Kwan v. Mercedes-Benz of North America, Inc. (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 174, 184-185; Troensegaard v. Silver crest Industries, Inc. (1985) 175 Cal.App.3d 218, 228 (Troensegaard.))  Damages under The Act included a refund of the purchase price or replacement of the vehicle and, under certain circumstances, a civil penalty not to exceed two times Plaintiff’s actual damages.  (Civ. Code, sec. 1794.)  Civil penalties are awarded if the trier of fact finds Defendant willfully violated its obligations.  (Civ. Code, sec. 1794(c).)  Plaintiff cited to several federal cases to dispute Troensegaard.  (See Brilliant v. Tiffin Motor Homes, Inc. (N.D. Cal. July 7, 2010) 2010 WL 2721531; Clark v. LG Elecs. U.S.A., Inc. (S.D. Cal. Oct. 29, 2013) 2013 WL 5816410;  Romo v. FFG Ins. Co. (C.D. Cal. 2005) 397 F.Supp. 2d 1237, 1240.)  Although the federal cases may have opined otherwise, the Court exercised its discretion in finding the federal cases to be unpersuasive.  Because our California Courts of Appeal opined that civil penalties under The Act are “akin to punitive damages” and there is no California case law opining that punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294 can be pled or awarded under The Act, the Court finds Defendant’s argument persuasive. 

Plaintiff asserted that punitive damages and civil penalties may be sought simultaneously at the pleading stage.  (Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1256-1257 (Clauson.))  However, Clauson is distinguishable from the allegations in the instant action.  The Court opined that James Clauson could plead punitive damages under the common law tort claim of invasion of privacy and simultaneously plead civil penalties under the statutory claim for unlawful wiretapping pursuant to Penal Code section 637.2, subdivision (a).  The Court of Appeal did not opine that punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294 could be pled under the statutory claim for unlawful wiretapping.  Plaintiff is attempting to argue that Civil Code section 3294 punitive damages can be pled under The Act.  However, Plaintiff’s argument is without any supportive legal authority. 

The motion to strike punitive damages is GRANTED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the first through fourth COAs. 

As to the request for punitive damages under the fifth COA for fraud, the Court finds that the fifth COA is sufficiently pled to survive the general demurrer, as reviewed above, and thus the claim is sufficient to support the fraud element required for punitive damages.  (Code Civ. Proc., sec. 3294.)  The Court does not find Defendant’s argument against punitive damages to be persuasive.

The motion to strike punitive damages is DENIED as to the fifth COA.

23VECV03352 MILLER V NOMAIR

[TENTATIVE] ORDER:  Defendant Baz Nomair’s Motion to Set Aside the Default and Default Judgment is DENIED.

The court does need to know, however, if there is a pending bankruptcy of BAZ NOMAIR.

Introduction

Defendant Baz Nomair (Defendant) moved to set aside the entry of default and the possession only default judgment entered in favor of Plaintiff Paul Miller, as Trustee of the Paul Miller Separate Property Living Trust Dated 8/31/99 (Plaintiff).

Discussion 

Defendant moved to set aside the default and default judgment based upon his mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.  (Code Civ. Proc., sec. 473(b).)  Defendant asserted that he never received notice of the September 14, 2023 ex parte hearing that advanced Defendant’s demurrer hearing date from October 3, 2023 to September 14, 2023.  However, he does not seek to set aside that order.  Also, Defendant does not deny that the number called to advise him of the ex parte was his number.

Defendant admits that he received the Court’s Minute Order showing that the demurrer was heard on September 14, 2023 and overruled, and that he was to answer the complaint within 5 days.  (Defendant’s Decl. pg. 5:20-26.) 

Defendant gives no explanation, much less excusable neglect, to show why he did not answer the complaint, did not call the court, did not talk to defense counsel, did not seek legal advice, or otherwise act reasonably after he received the notice that the answer was due.  He does not deny receiving the overnight delivery package so he could tell what happened at the ex parte application.  He does not deny that the phone number called was not his.  He has no explanation why he did not put his phone number, which is mandatory, on his papers. Indeed, he did not put a phone number on any of the papers he filed with the court, nor an email address either.  One cannot sit on his rights, especially when he has been advised by the court that there is a deadline to answer.

The motion to set aside the default and default judgment is DENIED.
