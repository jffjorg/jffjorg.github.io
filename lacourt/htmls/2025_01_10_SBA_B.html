DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24TRCV03280    Hearing Date: January 10, 2025    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

DAVE MARTIN,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.: 24TRCV03280

 

 

 

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

ROBERT SHEIPF, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          January 10, 2025

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants Robert Sheipe and Patricia Shiepe  

Responding Party:                  None

Demurrer to Complaint and Motion to Strike Portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint

            The court considered the moving papers. No opposition papers were filed.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the Fourth Cause of Action for fraud and Sixth Cause of Action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

            The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

BACKGROUND

On September 30, 2024, plaintiff Dave Martin filed a complaint against defendants Robert Sheipf, Patricia Sheipf, and Does 1-100, alleging causes of action for (1) violation of Civ. Code § 1940.2, (2) breach of implied warranty of habitability, (3) negligence, (4) fraud, (5) negligent infliction of emotional distress, and (6) intentional infliction of emotional distress.

Defendants Robert Shiepe (erroneously sued as Robert Sheipf) and Patricia Shiepe (erroneously sued as Patricia Sheipf) demur to the Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action in the complaint. Defendants also move to strike punitive damages allegations from the complaint, as well as move to strike plaintiff’s claim for attorney’s fees.

As of January 7, 2025, both the demurrer and motion to strike are unopposed. Any opposition was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. CCP § 1005(b). The court informs plaintiff that “[a] failure to oppose a motion may be deemed a consent to the granting of the motion. Cal. R. Ct., rule 8.54(c).

LEGAL AUTHORITY

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” CCP§ 430.10(e). A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078. “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905. “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747. On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43. Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349. The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. Ibid.

“Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” CCP § 435 (b)(1). A court may “[s]trike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” CCP§ 436(a). A court may “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” CCP § 436(b).   

DISCUSSION

Defendants demur to the Fourth and Sixth Causes of Action in the complaint on the grounds that such causes of action fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. Defendants request that their demurrer be sustained without leave to amend. Defendants also seek an order striking punitive damages allegations, as well as plaintiff’s claim for attorneys’ fees, from the complaint.

The Meet and Confer Requirement

Before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the moving party must meet and confer in person, by video conference, or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading to attempt to reach an agreement that would resolve the objections to the pleading. CCP §§ 430.41(a), 435.5. “Any determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer” or to grant or deny a motion to strike. CCP §§ 430.41(a)(4), 435.5.

The meet and confer requirement has not been satisfied as counsel for defendants only attests to sending a meet and confer e-mail and a meet and confer letter to plaintiff’s counsel. Block Decls., ¶ 2. The court, however, will still consider the demurrer and motion to strike on the merits. The court reminds the parties of the need to comply with the requirements of the CCP.

Pertinent Allegations of the Complaint

Plaintiff alleges that he was a tenant at a property located at 7832 Veragua Drive, Playa Del Rey, California (the “Subject Property”). Compl., ¶¶ 1, 9. Plaintiff was assigned a spare guest room by defendants. Compl., ¶ 9. Defendants charged plaintiff rent consisting of a fixed amount which varied from time to time and hired plaintiff to work on the Subject Property which related to various tasks including upkeep and repair of the Subject Property. Compl., ¶ 10. The complaint alleges that defendants, despite knowledge of plaintiff’s outside work schedule, kept increasing plaintiff’s workload to the point that it was physically impossible for plaintiff to work to sustain his modest lifestyle and do the myriad of ever increasing time consuming tasks assigned to him by defendants. Compl., ¶ 13.

Plaintiff alleges that defendants also assigned tasks that were often dangerous and to which plaintiff lacked the skills, expertise, and in some cases, the license, required to complete the task. Compl., ¶ 14. Plaintiff allegedly suffered a number of serious injuries trying to perform the tasks assigned to him by defendants including falling off a ladder. Compl., ¶ 14. Due to the combination of mold damage and unreasonable dangerous work demands made on plaintiff by defendants, plaintiff was forced to vacate the Subject Property and incur moving expenses. Compl., ¶ 14. Defendants badgered, threatened, and humiliated plaintiff when plaintiff did not complete the tasks or did not complete such tasks to liking of defendants. Compl., ¶ 15. Plaintiff alleges that he “suffered severe emotional distress during the term of the subject tenancy and with near nightly nightmares based on [d]efendants’ unreasonable work demands and their constant and incessant harassment of [p]laintiff.” Compl., ¶ 16.

Plaintiff’s Fourth Cause of Action for Fraud is Insufficiently Alleged

“The elements of fraud are (a) a misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) scienter or knowledge of its falsity; (c) intent to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.” Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Ctr. (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294. The facts constituting the alleged fraud must be alleged factually and specifically as to every element of fraud, as the policy of “liberal construction” of the pleadings will not ordinarily be invoked. Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.

The court finds that plaintiff’s Fourth Cause of Action for fraud is insufficiently alleged. Compl., ¶¶ 40-46. Plaintiff has specifically failed to allege that defendants misrepresented that the Subject Property was a safe and habitable unit. Compl., ¶ 41. Moreover, the court finds that the Fourth Cause of Action for fraud is much too conclusory. The complaint does not allege how, when, or by what means the purported misrepresentations of defendants were tendered. Compl., ¶¶ 40-46.

The court therefore SUSTAINS the demurrer of defendants to the Fourth Cause of Action for fraud with leave to amend as the court does not find that there exists no reasonable possibility of successful amendment.

Plaintiff’s Sixth Cause of Action for IIED is Insufficiently Alleged

“The elements of a prima facie case for the tort of intentional infliction of emotional distress are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant with the intention of causing, or reckless disregard of the probability of causing, emotional distress; (2) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (3) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct. Conduct to be outrageous must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community.” Wilson v. Hynek (2012) 207 Cal.App.4th 999, 1009, citation and ellipses omitted.

The court finds that the allegations of the Sixth Cause of Action for IIED are insufficient. Compl., ¶¶ 55-61. The court does not find that the failure to remedy issues and alleged unspecified verbal abuse constitutes extreme and outrageous conduct. Compl., ¶ 57. Moreover, the purported demands of plaintiff that he perform unreasonable and dangerous work is not extreme and outrageous conduct. Compl., ¶ 58. Critically, plaintiff has failed to allege the elements of actual and proximate causation to support a cause of action for IIED. Compl., ¶¶ 55-61.

The court SUSTAINS the demurrer of defendants to the Sixth Cause of Action for intentional infliction of emotional distress with leave to amend as the court does not find that there exists no reasonable possibility of successful amendment.

Motion to Strike

Applicable Law

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.” Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255. Civ. Code § 3294 authorizes punitive damages upon a showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. Malice is defined as either “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff,” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” Civ. Code § 3294 (c)(1). “Despicable conduct is conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” Mock v. Michigan Millers Mutual Ins. Co. (1992) 4 Cal. App. 4th 306, 331. Fraud under Civ. Code § 3294(c)(3) “means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.” Civ. Code § 3294(c)(2) defines oppression as “despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” Specific facts must be alleged in support of punitive damages. Hillard v. A.H. Robins Co. (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 391-392. Facts must be pled to show that a defendant “act[ed] with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff’s rights.” Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462. Conduct that is merely negligent will not support a claim for punitive damages. Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1288.

As to attorneys’ fees, such fees are only authorized by contract, statute, or law. CCP § 1033.5(a)(10).

Analysis

The court references its recital of the allegations of the complaint from above. The court finds that the complaint does not set forth sufficient facts to show entitlement to punitive damages. Plaintiff has not alleged specific facts showing fraud, oppression, or malice.

As to attorneys’ fees, the complaint fails to articulate a basis in statute, contract, or law for the recovery of attorneys’ fees.

The court therefore GRANTS the motion to strike with leave to amend.

ORDER

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 4th cause of action for fraud and 6th cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

The motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. 

Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.
