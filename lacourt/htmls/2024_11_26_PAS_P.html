DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22AHCV01269    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT EMMAUS’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO CONDUCT MENTAL EXAMINATIONS OF PLAINTIFFS

I.        INTRODUCTION

This action arises from claims of (1) sexual assault, (2) negligence, and (3) negligent failure to educate, train, or warn, brought by Plaintiffs JANE M.A.T. DOE and JANE M.I.G. DOE (“Plaintiffs”) against Defendants DEFENDANT DOE 1, DEFENDANT DOE 2, and DOES 3 through 100, inclusive. Plaintiffs initiated this action on December 29, 2022, alleging harm stemming from sexual abuse they endured as minors.

On October 30, 2024, Defendant Doe 1 (“Emmaus”) filed a motion for leave to conduct mental examinations of Plaintiffs. On November 11, 2024, Plaintiffs opposed the motion, and on November 19, 2024, Emmaus filed its reply.

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

 Pursuant to CCP § 2024.020, "except as otherwise provided in this section, any party shall be entitled as a matter of right … to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day before the date initially set for the trial of the action. A continuance or postponement of the trial date does not operate to reopen discovery proceedings." When the hearing for a discovery motion is held after the discovery motion hearing cut-off date, the Court cannot grant a motion to compel without first deciding on a motion to reopen discovery (Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1588.)

“The purpose of imposing a time limit on discovery is to expedite and facilitate trial preparation and to prevent delay.¿ Without a cutoff date, the parties could tie up each other and the trial court in discovery and discovery disputes right up to the eve of trial or beyond. Furthermore, . . . to be effective the cutoff date must be firm or some litigants will manipulate the proceedings to avoid the cut-off date.”¿ (Beverly Hosp. v. Superior Court¿(1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 1289, 1295.)  The reopening of discovery is a matter that is committed to the trial court’s sound discretion.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050(a), (b).)¿ In exercising that discretion, the trial court considers “any matter relevant to the leave requested,” including:¿ 

          (1) The necessity and the reasons for the discovery.¿ 

 (2) The diligence or lack of diligence of the party seeking the discovery or the hearing of a discovery motion, and the reasons that the discovery was not completed or that the discovery motion was not heard earlier.¿ 

 

(3) Any likelihood that permitting the discovery or hearing the discovery motion will prevent the case from going to trial on the date set, or otherwise interfere with the trial calendar, or result in prejudice to any other party.¿ 

(4) The length of time that has elapsed between any date previously set, and the date presently set, for the trial of the action.¿ 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050(b).)¿ 

          A motion to reopen discovery pursuant to CCP section 2024.050 must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating a good faith effort at informal resolution.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.050(a).) 

III.     ANALYSIS

          Emmaus files the instant motion for leave to conduct mental examinations of Plaintiffs JANE M.A.T DOE and JANE M.I.G DOE pursuant to CCP §§ 2032.310 and 2032.320. Emmaus asserts that the mental conditions of each plaintiff are in controversy in this action, and as a result, Emmaus must be allowed to conduct appropriate discovery into these claims by obtaining mental examinations conducted by their retained psychologist, Dr. Veronica Thomas. Emmaus maintains they have good cause for the examinations to proceed because the allegations of emotional distress place Plaintiffs mental health directly at issue.

Plaintiffs oppose the motion, raising two main arguments: (1) the motion is untimely, and (2) Emmaus is not entitled to inquire into Plaintiffs’ sexual histories beyond what pertains to the alleged perpetrator in this case. Upon review, the Court finds that the issue of timeliness is dispositive and will address this argument.

Plaintiffs argue that the motion is untimely because it was filed after the non-expert discovery cut-off, which occurred 30 days before the original trial date of November 12, 2024, under CCP § 2024.020(a). They contend that mental health examinations are governed by CCP § 2032 and constitute non-expert discovery. Plaintiffs highlight that trial continuances do not automatically extend discovery deadlines under CCP § 2024.020(b) and note that Emmaus failed to file a motion to reopen discovery as required by CCP § 2024.050 (Opposition at pp. 2–4).

In response, Emmaus asserts that the mental health examinations sought fall under expert testimony. Alternatively, Emmaus argues that the Court has discretion under CCP § 2024.050 to allow the examinations to proceed despite their timing. Emmaus also claims to have made good-faith efforts to stipulate to the examinations (Reply, pp.3-4)

The Court finds that the applicable cut-off date for mental health examinations is 30 days before the originally set trial date, as outlined in CCP § 2024.020(a). Accordingly, the non-expert discovery cut-off in this case was October 13, 2024, based on the original trial date of November 12, 2024. The mental health examinations sought by Emmaus fall under CCP § 2032, and thus the discovery deadline for these examinations has passed. Even if the 15-day expert discovery cut-off under CCP § 2024.030 were applicable, the motion would still be untimely as it was filed on October 30, 2024, two days after that deadline. The mental health examinations sought by Emmaus fall under CCP § 2032, and thus the discovery deadline for these examinations has passed.

Pursuant to CCP § 2024.050, Emmaus must file a motion to reopen discovery if additional discovery is sought after the statutory cut-off. Without such a motion, the Court lacks a statutory basis to evaluate whether good cause exists to allow the examinations after the cut-off (Pelton-Shepherd Industries, Inc. v. Delta Packaging Products, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1568, 1578). Emmaus has not complied with the procedural requirements of CCP § 2024.050 by failing to file a motion to reopen discovery. 

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Emmaus motion for leave to conduct mental examinations of Plaintiffs without prejudice. Should Emmaus wish to pursue the examinations, Emmaus must submit a proper motion to reopen discovery which would allow the Court to address whether good cause exists to permit the examinations at this stage of proceedings.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          The Court DENIES Emmaus motion for leave to conduct mental examinations of Plaintiffs.

Plaintiffs are to give notice.

Case Number: 23AHCV02451    Hearing Date: November 26, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS - SET ONE and MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.         INTRODUCTION

            This is a personal injury action arising from a motor vehicle collision involving plaintiffs Robert James Ruiz, Michael Meza, a minor by and through his guardian ad litem, Alicia Aragon, Aniela Ruiz, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Robert Ruiz Jr., Sarina Ruiz, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Robert Ruiz Jr. and Amara Ruiz, a minor by and through her guardian ad litem, Robert Ruiz Jr. (collectively, “Plaintiffs”), against defendants Rafael Heraldez; Pactrack Inc., Amazon.com Inc., Amazon.com Services LLC,  Amazon Logistics, Inc., and Does 1 to 100, inclusive.

Plaintiffs allege that on October 25, 2021, defendant Rafael Heraldez, while acting within the course and scope of his employment with defendant Pactrack Inc., failed to stop at a stop sign and collided with Plaintiffs vehicle, causing severe and lasting injuries. Plaintiffs bring this action to recover damages related to those injuries.

On October 10, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion to compel Defendant Pactrack Inc. (“Defendant”) to produce documents responsive to Plaintiffs Request for Production of Documents - Set One. Defendant filed a late Opposition on November 22, 2024.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

            A party must respond to interrogatories and requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories or requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories or production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2024.020, subd. (a), 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.)

 

III.      ANALYSIS

            Plaintiffs move for an order compelling Defendant Pactrack Inc. to produce documents responsive to Plaintiffs’ Requests for Production of Documents - Set One, specifically Request Nos. 9, 19, 26, 28, 31, and 32. These requests seek critical documents, including insurance coverage information (Request No. 9), photographs and videos of individuals involved (Request No. 19), evidence of Defendant Heraldez’s employment status and scope at the time of the incident (Requests Nos. 26 and 31), Heraldez’s driving history (Request No. 28), and the relationship between Defendant Pactrack and Heraldez (Request No. 32).

Defendant has failed to produce documents responsive to Request for Production of Documents Set One, served on April 23, 2024. (Malek Decl. ¶ 4.) While Defendant provided verified responses on June 18, 2024, these responses included commitments to produce responsive documents, yet no documents were provided. (Malek Decl. ¶¶ 7, 10.)  On July 12, 2024, Plaintiffs sent detailed meet and confer letters identifying deficiencies in Defendant’s responses and requesting a reply by July 19, 2024. (Malek Decl., ¶ 8.) Plaintiffs subsequently granted Defendant several extensions to respond, but Defendant failed to remedy its deficient production. (Malek Decl., ¶¶ 9–15.)

On August 28, 2024, the parties met and conferred on the phone, but Defendant’s counsel was unprepared to address the deficiencies. Defendant served second supplemental responses on September 26, 2024, but these were unverified and failed to resolve the identified issues. (Malek Decl., ¶¶ 14–17.) Under California law, failure to verify responses is equivalent to providing no response. (Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3d 632, 636.)

Despite multiple meet and confer efforts and numerous extensions, Defendant has failed to comply with its discovery obligations. To date, Defendant has not produced any documents responsive to Plaintiffs Request for Production Nos. 9, 19, 26, 28, 31, and 32. (Malek Decl., ¶ 18.)

Defendant has filed an opposition, while late, on November 22, 2024, and has conceded to the motion to compel. Defendant argues that the failure to provide timely responses and documents was due to mistake, inadvertence, and/or excusable neglect under Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b). Defendant’s counsel contends that errors, including the failure of staff to properly calendar the motion and upload documents to the case file, resulted in the oversight. (Natoli Decl., ¶¶ 4–7.)

Defendant further asserts that it has now remedied these issues by providing verifications for prior responses and producing all responsive documents within its custody or control by November 22, 2024. (Natoli Decl., ¶¶ 9–14.) While the Court acknowledges these remedial efforts, they are not yet completed and occurred only after Plaintiffs were compelled to file this motion.

Therefore, the Court grants the motion to compel.

 

Sanctions

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a), authorizes the Court to impose a monetary sanction for misuse of the discovery process, requiring the offending party, or their attorney, to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by the opposing party, including attorney’s fees. If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the Court must impose that sanction unless it finds that the offending party acted with substantial justification or that the imposition of the sanction would be unjust. However, Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b) permits the court to relieve a party or its counsel from the consequences of procedural missteps due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, provided the errors are promptly addressed.

Here, Plaintiffs request monetary sanctions in the amount of $6,213.51, encompassing fees and costs related to the preparation of three motions, multiple meet and confer efforts, and anticipated responses to opposition briefs. Plaintiffs seek sanctions jointly and severally against Defendants Pactrack Inc., Rafael Heraldez, Defendant’s counsel of record, and Defendant’s law firm of record, Kahana & Feld LLP, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030(a).

Plaintiffs’ requested amount includes reviewing initial discovery responses, drafting four meet and confer letters, conducting telephonic conferences, reviewing supplemental responses, preparing the instant declaration, and preparing motions and supporting documents. Plaintiffs’ counsel’s hourly rate is $450/hour, and Plaintiffs also claim $206.73 in filing costs, with additional anticipated costs for oppositions and reply briefs.

Defendant argues that the failure to provide timely verifications and documents was the result of excusable neglect rather than intentional misconduct. Defense counsel admits that internal errors, including the mishandling of deadlines by former staff members, led to the oversight. (Declaration of Noelle M. Natoli, ¶¶ 4–5.) Specifically, defense counsel states that the motion to compel was not properly calendared or uploaded to the file, and the attorney responsible for managing these obligations, Mr. Yen, was no longer employed at the firm. (Natoli Decl., ¶¶ 3–5.)

Upon discovering the deficiencies on November 20, 2024, defense counsel took immediate steps to rectify the situation by serving verifications, meeting with the client to locate additional documents, and filing a late opposition to the motion. (Natoli Decl., ¶¶ 6–7.) Defense counsel has also apologized to the court and opposing counsel, emphasizing that these errors were unintentional and have since been addressed.

While the Court acknowledges defense counsel’s efforts to remedy the deficiencies, and the explanation of excusable neglect under section 473(b), the court finds that Plaintiffs were nonetheless forced to file this motion due to Defendant’s initial noncompliance. Plaintiffs incurred unnecessary costs in preparing and filing the motion, as well as engaging in meet and confer efforts that could have been avoided had Defendant acted in a timely manner. 

Given the above, the Court finds that Defendant’s conduct constitutes a misuse of the discovery process but mitigates the severity of sanctions in light of defense counsel’s explanation and remedial efforts. 

For the instant motion, the court finds that $450/hour is a reasonable hourly rate but reduces the time allocated to reflect the scope of this motion. The court allocates one hour for preparation of this motion, one hour for meet and confer efforts related to this motion, and a filing fee of $68.91.

Based on the foregoing, the Court awards monetary sanctions in the amount of $968.91, consisting of $900 for attorneys’ fees (2 hours at $450/hour) and $68.91 for the filing fee. 

These sanctions are imposed against Defendant Pactrack Inc.’s counsel of record and their law firm, Kahana & Feld LLP, jointly and severally,

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiffs motion to compel and orders monetary sanctions in the sum of $968.91.

Defendant is ordered to provide documents and pay sanctions within 20 days of this order.
