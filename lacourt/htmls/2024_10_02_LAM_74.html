DEPARTMENT 74 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 



 




Case Number: 19STCV30239    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: 74

The Wonderful Company LLC v. Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company, et al.

Plaintiff The Wonderful Company LLC’s Motion for Leave to File Third Amended Complaint

 

BACKGROUND 

            On August 22, 2019, plaintiff The Wonderful Company LLC (Plaintiff) filed a complaint on behalf of itself and others similarly situated, against defendants Anthem Blue Cross Life and Health Insurance Company (Anthem) and Lucile Packard Children’s Hospital Standard (LPCH) and Does 1-100 (collectively Defendants).  The complaint alleged six causes of action: (1) Breach of the Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (2) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, (3) Violation of the Cartwright Act (Bus. & Prof Code, § 16720, et seq.), (4) Unreasonable Restraint of Trade (Bus. & Prof Code, § 16720, et seq.), (5) Combination to Monopolize in Violation of the Cartwright Act (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 16720, et seq.), and (6) Violation of Cal. Bus. & Prof. Code Section 17200, et. seq.¿¿ 

            On May 5, 2023, Plaintiff was granted leave to amend and filed a second amended.

            On June 28, 2024, Anthem filed a Motion for Summary Judgement, and on January 5, 2024, Defendant LPCH filed a Motion for Summary Judgement.

            On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff informed the parties of its intent to file a Motion for Leave to Amend.

            On July 25, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Motion for Leave to Amend and file the Third Amended Complaint (TAC).

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

¿¿            Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.”  Amendment may be allowed at any time before or after commencement of trial.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.)  “[T]he court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.  The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”  (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428 (internal citations omitted).)  “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend….”  (Morgan v. Sup. Ct. (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) 

Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.”  (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)  “When a plaintiff seeks leave to amend his or her complaint only after the defendant has mounted a summary judgement motion directed at the allegations of the unamended complaint, even though the plaintiff has been aware of the facts upon which the amended is based, ‘[i]t would be patently unfair to allow plaintiffs to defeat [the] summary judgement motion by allowing them to present a ‘moving target’ unbounded by the pleadings.’ [citations.]”  (Falcon v. Long Beach Genetics, Inc. (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1281.) 

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must include a supporting declaration that must, among other things, specify when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered and the reason the request for amendment was not made earlier.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.134(b).) 

 

DISCUSSION 

            The Court finds that Plaintiff has not shown that it did not delay in filing this motion.  Plaintiff does not identify the additional information it discovered that it only “recently learned” from the declarations, written discovery, and depositions.  (Mot., pg. 3.)  In the supporting declaration accompanying Plaintiff’s motion, Plaintiff’s counsel does not state when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered nor the reason the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Rule 3.134(b)).  Plaintiff points generally towards “depositions and written discovery” about the same factual allegations against the same parties arising out of the same core circumstances, but does not identify any additional facts, when the depositions and written discovery were supplied to Plaintiffs, nor what the new information contained that gave rise to the new theory of liability.  (Selesnick Decl., ¶ 5.) 

            The Court also finds that Defendants would be prejudice by allowing a Third Amended Complaint.  Both Anthem and LPCH have moved for summary judgement on the Second Amended Complaint.  (Jesus Decl., ¶¶ 7,8; Sorensen Decl., ¶ 8.)  Allowing Plaintiff to amend the complaint would require that Defendants file new Motions for Summary Judgement in response to the new cause of action.  (Jesus Decl., Exhibit “F”, “G”.)  In Falcon, the Court denied leave to amend when the Plaintiff sought leave to amend only after the defendant had mounted a summary judgement motion when the Plaintiff had been aware of the facts upon which the amendment was based.  (Falcon, supra, 224 Cal.App.4th at 1280.)  Here, Plaintiff was aware of substantially similar issues.  (TAC, pg. 7 (“[Plaintiff] does not seek to add any “substantially different issues”).)   Because Plaintiff does not submit any new facts upon which the additional cause of action is based, it would substantially prejudice Defendants to grant leave to amend. 

             

            Given that leave to amend should be denied for delay and prejudice, the Court does not address Defendants’ futility arguments.

CONCLUSION 

The court denies Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a Third Amended Complaint. 

Plaintiff shall give notice.


Case Number: 21STCV09151    Hearing Date: October 2, 2024    Dept: 74

Yan Shalomov v. Grace Cho, et al.

Defendant Airbnb’s Demurrer

 

BACKGROUND 

            On March 9, 2021, Plaintiff Yan Shalomov (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants Grace Cho (“Cho”) and Does 1-10 alleging causes of action for (1) Negligence and (2) Premises Liability. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that he sustained catastrophic injuries after falling from an elevated hot tub at a Vacation Rental Property (“Property”), owned by Cho and rented through Airbnb. (Compl. ¶ 6-9.)

            On September 13, 2023, Plaintiff filed an amendment to his complaint, listing the true name for Doe 6 as “Airbnb, Inc.” (“Defendant” or “Airbnb”).

            On November 1, 2023, Airbnb filed its demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint. Airbnb argues that Plaintiff has failed to allege sufficient facts to establish a cause of action for either negligence or premises liability for the following reasons: (1) Plaintiff has not pleaded sufficient facts to demonstrate that Airbnb owed a duty of care for the condition of the Property because no special relationship between Airbnb and Plaintiff exists, and Airbnb does not own, possess, or control the Property; (2) Airbnb did not have notice of the dangerous condition on the Property it did not own, possess, or control; and (3) Airbnb did not reasonably foresee Plaintiff’s injuries at a Property which it did not own, possess, or control.

            On September 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed its opposition. Plaintiff argues that its complaint pleads facts sufficient to establish a cause of action for negligence and premises liability because the express allegations demonstrate the following: (1) Airbnb owned, managed, controlled, and/or operated the Property, and thereby owed Plaintiff a duty to maintain the Property in a safe and habitable condition; (2) Airbnb breached its duty, and allowed for a significant safety hazard on the Property; and (3) Airbnb’s breach caused Plaintiff’s serious injuries.

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

            The court denies Airbnb’s request for judicial notice of Airbnb’s Terms of Service.

DISCUSSION 

            To survive demurrer, Plaintiff must state sufficient allegations of material fact to establish each element of the causes of action it asserts. “The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.”  (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) “Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, cleaned up.)

 

1.                  Existence of a Duty

            Airbnb’s control over the factors that led to plaintiff’s injury is central to establishing a duty of care for either negligence or premises liability. (See Nally v. Grace Cmty. Church (1988) 47 Cal.3d 278, 293 (“one is ordinarily not liable for the actions of another and is under no duty to protect another from harm, in the absence of a special relationship of custody or control”) (internal citations omitted); Annocki v. Peterson Enters., LLC (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 23, 27 (“where there is no control over the premises, there is no duty to exercise reasonable care to prevent injury”).) Plaintiff contends that, taken together, the express allegations in his complaint establish valid causes of action against Airbnb for both negligence and premises liability. First, the complaint states that “[e]ach reference to ‘Defendants’ shall collectively encompass any and all co-defendants and Does 1 through 10 inclusive[,]” which includes Airbnb after Plaintiff’s September 13, 2023 amendment. Further, Plaintiff alleges the following series of facts that it contends establishes his causes of action against Airbnb: (1) “[a]t all relevant times alleged herein, Defendants owned, managed, controlled and/or operated the [Property] … located at 64380 Sullivan Road, Joshua Tree, CA 92252.” (Compl., ¶ 6.) “As the owners, managers, controllers and/or operators of the [Property] … Defendants owe a duty to its vacationers/invitees to comply with all laws and to maintain the [Property] … in a safe and habitable condition for their vacationers/invitees.” (Id. at ¶ 7.) “Defendants failed their duties by constructing an outdoor hot tub on the [Property] … that violates the law and creates a significant safety hazard for vacationers/invitees.” (Id. at ¶ 8.) “On or about June 26, 2020, Plaintiff was an invitee of the [Property] … and was sitting on the ledge of the hot tub when he slipped and fell six (6) feet onto the hard ground. Plaintiff suffered catastrophic injuries and was rushed to the emergency room where he was diagnosed with a broken hip and immediately sent into surgery.” (Id. at ¶ 9.)

 

A.    Airbnb’s Alleged Ownership, Management, Control, and/or Operation of the Property are, in this particular case, Legal Conclusions

“In reviewing the sufficiency of a complaint against a general demurrer, … ‘[w]e treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. [Citation.] We also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.’ [Citation.] Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context. [Citation.]” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311.)

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges that “[a]t all relevant times alleged herein, Defendants [including Airbnb] owned, managed, controlled and/or operated the vacation rental property located at 64380 Sullivan Road, Joshua Tree, CA 92252 …” (Compl. ¶ 6.) The Court takes judicial notice of its own orders and the parties’ previous filings in this matter, finding that the parties do not dispute that Defendant Cho owns the Property and uses Airbnb’s platform to rent it. (See, e.g., Sep. 25, 2024 Minute Order.) But – and this is key in sustaining Airbnb’s demurrer – Plaintiff’s remaining contention that Airbnb somehow manages, controls, or operates the Property, without providing any supporting allegations how Airbnb manages, controls or operates the Property is – when one reads the complaint as whole – a legal conclusion the Court need not accept as true. (Youngman, supra, 70 Cal.2d at 245 (a plaintiff “is required … to set forth the essential facts of his case with reasonable precision and with particularity sufficient to acquaint a defendant with the nature, source and extent of his cause of action”); Berstein v. Piller (1950) 98 Cal.2d 441, 443-44 (mere “recitals” or legal conclusions that leave the material facts to “surmise” are insufficient and subject to demurrer).) Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff did not plead sufficient facts to establish that Airbnb owns, manages, controls, or operates the subject Property.

 

B.     Airbnb Did Not Have a Special Relationship with Defendant that would Give Rise to a Duty of Care

A plaintiff may still establish a defendant’s duty of care in the context of third-party acts, but California courts distinguish between a third party’s “misfeasance” and “nonfeasance.” (Melton v. Boustred (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 521, 531.) Misfeasance is when a defendant makes the plaintiff’s position worse, while nonfeasance is when a defendant does not help a plaintiff. (Lugtu v. Cal. Highway Patrol (2001) 26 Cal.4th 703, 716.) The law imposes a duty of care on a defendant who has committed a misfeasance because they have “‘created a risk’ of harm to the plaintiff ….” (Brown v. Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204, 214 (internal citations omitted).) However, for a nonfeasance, a defendant is under no duty of care, unless plaintiff can also demonstrate a special relationship between the defendant and plaintiff. (See Nally, supra, 47 Cal.3d at 293.)

First, the Court notes that Plaintiff has failed to present sufficient evidence to establish that Defendant created Plaintiff’s risk of harm. Absent anything to substantiate that Airbnb owned, managed, controlled, and/or operated the Property, Plaintiff’s assertion that Airbnb constructed the dangerous hot tub, without more, need not be accepted as true. (See Compl. ¶ 8; Youngman, supra, 70 Cal.2d at 245.)

Further, absent any ownership or duty to control the Property, Plaintiff also fails to allege sufficient facts to demonstrate a special relationship between Airbnb and Plaintiff that would give rise to a duty for the condition of the Property. The existence of a special relationship typically requires a reasonable expectation of dependency or reliance and has been found in the contexts of “(i) common carriers and passengers, (ii) innkeepers and their guests, and (iii) mental health professionals and their patients.” (Melton, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 535; cf. Nally, supra, 47 Cal.3d at 293-94 (holding that pastoral, non-therapist counselors had no special relationship with potentially suicidal person; although counselors may have known of potential for suicide, counselors had no control over the patient’s environment).) Here, Airbnb’s relationship with Plaintiff is not one that gives rise to a reasonable expectation of dependency, reliance, or protection, such that any special relationship exists. Although Plaintiff used Airbnb’s website to facilitate a transaction that ultimately resulted in serious harm to Plaintiff, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts demonstrating that Airbnb increased Plaintiff’s risk of harm. Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff did not plead sufficient facts to establish a special relationship giving rise to a duty between Plaintiff and Airbnb.

 

A.    Airbnb Did Not Have Notice of the Property’s Dangerous Condition, and Could Not Reasonably Foresee Plaintiff’s Injuries

“Where there is a legal basis for imposing a duty—as in cases of misfeasance or when a special relationship exists—the court considers the foreseeability of risk from the third party conduct.” (Melton, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 532.) “Foreseeability is balanced ‘against the burden of the duty to be imposed. Where the burden is great, a high degree of foreseeability is usually required[] ….’” (Ibid (internal citations omitted).) When addressing the foreseeability of harm the Court must address whether an objectively reasonable person, who was aware or should have been aware of the same facts as defendant, would have foreseen plaintiff’s harm. (Ibid; Dillon v. Legg (1968) 68 Cal.2d 728, 739; see also Joshi v. Fitness International, LLC (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 814, 832 (“[a] plaintiff asserting a claim for premises liability must show that the owner knew or reasonably should have known about the claimed dangerous condition on the property”) (citing Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206 (“where the plaintiff relies on the failure to correct a dangerous condition to prove the owner’s negligence, the plaintiff has the burden of showing that the owner had notice of the defect in sufficient time to correct it”) (internal citations omitted)).)

Here, Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to demonstrate the reasonable foreseeability of his injuries. As set out above, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts to support the finding that Airbnb owned, managed, controlled, and/or operated the Property. Plaintiff also fails to allege in his Complaint any facts that Airbnb knew or should have known about the allegedly dangerous condition in the absence of ownership or control. Accordingly, the Court finds that Plaintiff did not plead sufficient facts to establish that Airbnb had notice of the Property’s dangerous condition and should have foreseen Plaintiff’s injuries.

            Based on the foregoing, the court sustains Airbnb’s Demurrer to Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

LEAVE TO AMEND

Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245; see also Virgina G v. ABC Unified School Dist. (1993) 15 Cal.App.4th 1848, 1852 (“[w]here a demurrer is sustained … denial of leave to amend constitutes an abuse of discretion if the pleading does not show on its face that it is incapable of amendment.”))

            Here, Plaintiff has successfully carried his burden to establish that his Complaint can be successfully amended. The original complaint, as pleaded, does not offer sufficient allegations of Airbnb’s management, control, and/or operation of the Property to support a finding that Airbnb owed a duty for the condition of the Property, or that it could reasonably foresee Plaintiff’s injuries. However, Plaintiff contends that it can amend its pleadings with “further details about how Airbnb … controlled whether the Subject Property could be rented.” (Opp., at p. 4:20-21.) In addition, Plaintiff contends that such amendment could contain information about the reasonable foreseeability of Plaintiff’s injuries because “Airbnb knew or should have known that the Subject Property did not have a rental permit because the Property was not constructed per the Building Code, thereby making the Subject Property unsafe for occupancy.” (Id. at p. 4:22-24.) Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for leave to amend is granted.

 

CONCLUSION

The court sustains Defendant’s demurrer with leave to amend. The court orders Plaintiff to file and serve an amended complaint, if any, within 20 days of the date of notice of this order.

Defendant shall give notice.
