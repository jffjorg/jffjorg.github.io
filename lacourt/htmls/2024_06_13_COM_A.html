DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

Case Number: 20STCV02062    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: A

20STCV02062 Edwin Nolasco, by and through his Guardian ad Litem, Ana Lloyd v. Los Angeles Unified School District; Doe 1

Thursday, June 13, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO SEAL RECORDS

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING THE HEARING ON PETITION FOR COMPROMISE OF A DISPUTED CLAIM OF A PERSON WITH A DISABILITY                                                                                                                                                           

 

I.            BACKGROUND

       Claimant, Edwin Nolasco, by and through his sister and Guardian ad Litem, Ana Lloyd, settled his claims arising from multiple incidents of sexual assault perpetrated by Defendant, Doe 1. Claimant was 21 years old at the time and attending classes provided by Los Angeles Unified School District (“LAUSD”) at Banneker Career and Transition Center (“Banneker”). (FAC, ¶ 2-3. 

II.            MOTION TO SEAL

Petitioner proposes to file the petition and supporting documents under seal to protect the minor claimant’s financial privacy, identity, and ongoing psychological and emotional injuries reflected in the records. Court records are presumed to be open. (Cal. Rules of Court, 2.550 subd. (c).) The burden is on the moving party to show compelling reasons for sealing records. (Mary R. v. B. & R. Corp. (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 308, 317.) To support an order to seal records, the Court must find that “(1) there is an overriding interest supporting sealing records; (2) there is a substantial probability that the interest will be prejudiced absent sealing; (3) the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored to serve the overriding interest; and (4) there is no less restrictive means of achieving the overriding interest." (McNair v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (2015) 234 Cal.App.4th 25, 29; Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.550 subd. (d).)

An agreement not to disclose can constitute an “overriding interest” where there is specific showing of serious injury. (McNair at 35–36 [“specificity is essential. [Citation.] Broad allegations of harm, bereft of specific examples or articulated reasoning, are insufficient. [Citation.] We have been unable to find any appellate court decision which construes Publicker to permit sealing of court documents merely upon the agreement of the parties without a specific showing of serious injury.”].) Once the moving party identifies an overriding interest, the moving party must show “a substantial probability that it will be prejudiced absent closure or sealing." (Universal City Studios, Inc. v. Superior Court (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1273, 1283.)

Petitioner has identified a compelling interest to support redacting the petition and supporting documents to protect Plaintiff’s private financial and health information and identity. (City of Carmel-By-The-Sea v. Young (1970) 2 Cal.3d 259, 268 ["In any event we are satisfied that the protection of one's personal financial affairs and those of his (or her) spouse and children against compulsory public disclosure is an aspect of the zone of privacy which is protected by the Fourth Amendment and which also falls within that penumbra of constitutional rights into which the government may not intrude absent a showing of compelling need and that the intrusion is not overly broad."].) Publication of a person’s assets "can be expected to bring unwanted solicitation from a variety of salesmen and others, could well encourage harassment lawsuits or demands of like nature, and could expose the public officer and family to various criminal elements in our society." (City of Carmel-By-The-Sea at p. 270.)

Additionally, courts have recognized an overriding interest in protecting minor victims of sex crimes from further trauma and embarrassment (NBC Subsidiary (KNBC-TV) v. Superior Court, (1999) 20 Cal. 4th 1178, 1223.)

Petitioner has shown a substantial probability that Plaintiff’s privacy interests will be prejudiced absent sealing the records. The proposed order is narrowly tailored to serve Plaintiff’s privacy interests, and there is no less restrictive means of protecting that interest.

III.            CONCLUSION

Accordingly, Petitioner’s motion to seal the petition and supporting exhibits are GRANTED.

Case Number: 23CMCV00621    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: A

23CMCV00621 Atlas VI JCP Carson, LLC v. Khaled I. Azzam, et al.

Thursday, June 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER CONTINUING THE HEARING ON MOTION BY DEFENDANT, METRO, INC., FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES AND REQUIRING ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE

 

I.        BACKGROUND

       The complaint alleges that Plaintiff’s predecessor-in-interest leased commercial real property to Defendant, Khaled I. Azzam dba Metro Fusion (“Tenant” or “Azzam”).  Plaintiff alleges that at some point during the tenancy, the tenant transferred his interest in the lease to Metro Fusion, Inc. (“Metro Inc.” or “Defendant”). Defendants allegedly defaulted on the lease agreement by failing to pay rent. Plaintiff alleges one cause of action for breach of contract for overdue rent and other expenses totaling $267,648.89.

       On April 18, 2024, the Court granted Defendant, Metro Inc.’s, Motion for Summary Judgment as Plaintiff did not meet its burden of establishing that Metro Inc. was liable for the breach of lease agreement between Plaintiff and Tenant Azzam.

II.      ARGUMENTS

       Defendant Metro Inc. requests an award of $99,209.25 in attorney’s fees and expenses on grounds it is entitled to such an award as it prevailed on its Motion for Summary Judgment against Plaintiff. The lease agreement between Plaintiff and Tenant Azzam contained an attorney fee provision. Despite the fact that Metro Inc. was not a signatory to the lease, Metro Inc. argues it is entitled to an attorney fee award since the facts alleged against Metro Inc. arise from the same set of facts alleged against Tenant Azzam.

       In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that Metro Inc. is not entitled to rely on a lease agreement between Plaintiff and Tenant Azzam as a nonsignatory. If the court awards attorney’s fees, the court should apportion the award for causes of action on which Metro Inc. prevailed. The prevailing party’s fee request should consider the prevailing party’s success in relation to the litigation as a whole. Plaintiff argues the fee request is improperly inflated and the bills are vague, duplicative, and contain blocked billing, which the court should disregard.

       In reply, Defendant argues that Metro Inc. is the equivalent of a targeted party as alleged in the complaint and is entitled to recover fees under the lease agreement, although it is not a signatory.

III.    DISCUSSION

A.      Metro Inc. has demonstrated that it is entitled to recover attorney’s fees under the lease agreement between Plaintiff’s predecessor and Azzam, the tenant.

       A party to a contract with an attorney fee provision may recover such fees “where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs." (Civ. Code, § 1717 subd. (a).)

       The fee provision at issue states as follows:

where “either Landlord of Tenant institutes any action or proceeding against the other relating to the provisions of this Lease or any default hereunder, the non-prevailing party in such action or proceeding shall reimburse the prevailing party for the reasonable expenses of attorney’s fees and all costs and disbursements incurred therein by the prevailing party, including, without limitation, any such fees, costs or disbursements Incurred on any appeal from such action proceeding.” (Azzam decl. p. 39, ¶ 27.22.)

       The lease agreement at issue was made between Plaintiff’s predecessor as landlord on the one hand and “Khaled L. Azzam doing business as Metro Fusion” as tenant on the other hand. (Azzam Decl., Ex. 1, pdf p. 5.) The lease identifies “Metro Fusion” as a trade name for the tenant. (Id. pdf, p. 4.) The Motion for Summary Judgment was made by Metro Inc., not a party to the contract.  (Id. pdf page 5.) Metro Inc. prevailed.

       An action to avoid enforcement of a contract is an “action on contract” within the meaning of Section 1717 which is construed liberally to include not only a traditional action for damages for breach of contract, “but also any other action that ‘involves a contract under which one of the parties would be entitled to recover attorney’s fees if it prevails in the action.” (Eden Township Healthcare Dist. v. Eden Medical Center (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 418, 427.) In its Motion for Summary Judgment, Metro Inc. argued successfully that the undisputed facts did not establish that Metro Inc., a non-signatory, had been assigned or otherwise assumed the tenant Kazzam’s lease obligations as Plaintiff alleged. (Complaint, ¶ 31.)

       Section 1717 was “enacted to establish mutuality of remedy where [a] contractual provision makes recovery of attorney fees available for only one party ….” (Reynolds Metals Co. v. Alperson (1979) 25 Cal.3d 124, 128.)  Reynolds interpreted the statute “to further provide a reciprocal remedy for a nonsignatory defendant, sued on a contract as if he were a party to it, when a plaintiff would clearly be entitled to attorney fees should he prevail in enforcing the contractual obligation against defendant.” (Id.)  Accordingly, the court held that “… since defendants would have been liable for attorney fees pursuant to the fee provision in the promissory note had plaintiff prevailed, defendants could recover attorney fees pursuant to Civ. Code, § 1717, now that they had prevailed." (Id.)

       Similarly, had Plaintiff landlord here prevailed in an action on contract against non-signatory Defendant (Metro Inc.), Plaintiff would have been entitled to recover attorney’s fees against Metro Inc. as an alleged assignee of the tenant’s obligations. The mutuality of remedy provided by section 1717, by its express provisions, entitles the non-signatory party to recover attorney’s fees where, as here, Metro Inc. prevailed on its Motion for Summary Judgment

       Plaintiff argues that the contract should be construed strictly to apply only to actions between landlord and tenant. However, Eden instructs that section 1717 is construed liberally to permit mutuality of remedy where a non-signatory prevails on the action on contract. (Eden at 427.) Accordingly, Metro Inc. has established it is entitled to recover attorney’s fees, although a non-signatory, under the fee provision of the lease agreement.

B.      Metro Inc. is entitled to recover attorney’s fees incurred in defense of this litigation, but not including fees and costs incurred by tenant Azzam alone as the alleged bases for liability against each defendant arose from different alleged facts.

       Apportionment of attorney’s fees incurred by Metro Inc. and Azzam is not required “when incurred for representation on an issue common to both a cause of action in which fees are proper and one in which they are not allowed." (Reynolds  at 129–130.) The alleged bases for liability against Azzam and Metro Inc. were not based on common issues of fact; Plaintiff alleged Azzam was the tenant and signatory under the lease agreement and alleged Metro Inc. was liable based on an assignment of the tenant’s lease obligations.

       Metro Inc. does not request attorney’s fees incurred to pursue Azzam’s motion for relief from waiver of the writ of execution against his wages or to defend against Plaintiff’s request for attachment against Azzam only. (Motion, 4:17-25.) Instead, Metro Inc. asks the court to “focus solely on all other fees and costs for which Metro Inc. had involvement” which also includes Plaintiff’s first writ of attachment against both defendants. (Mot. 4:21-22.)

       Metro Inc. argues that as to the first writ of attachment, the court need not apportion fees since " the claims for relief are so intertwined that it would be impracticable, if not impossible, to separate the attorney's time into compensable and non-compensable units.” (Bell v. Vista Unified School Dist. (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 672, 687; Plaintiff’s App. For Writ filed 8/31/23.)

However, Defendants’ joint opposition argued two separate theories: on behalf of Metro Inc., Defendant argued that Plaintiff cannot recover since it is not a tenant obligated under the lease, and that the alleged assignment of the lease was a “knowing deception” and “recognition that Metro Inc. has no liability.” (Opp. filed 9/18/23, 4:6-7; 6:8-10.)

       On the other hand, Azzam argued in his defense that Plaintiff could not show that Azzam’s liability arose out of his “trade, business, or profession.” (Id. at 7:1-4). The assertion that Plaintiff did not establish a fixed or readily ascertainable amount for the writ was a claim common to both defendants. (Id. 7:25-28.)

       Trial courts have discretion not only to set the amount of a fee award but also in allocating the award among various defendants based on their “relative culpability.” (Walker v. Ticor Title Co. of California. (2012) 204 Cal.App.4th 363, 374–375.) It is an abuse of discretion “not to allocate fees when defendants are of very different relative culpability." (Id. at 375.) The court may also apply principles of equity to justify its decision to apportion an award of attorney’s fees among multiple parties in appropriate circumstances. (Id.)

       Plaintiff persuasively argues that Metro Inc. demonstrated it was not liable for rent as an occupant transferee or sublessee, which is “entirely distinct from Azzam’s sweeping dispute of the lease, including the amounts owned, validity of notices, Covid-19 defenses, among other theories. (Opp. 4:2126.) Defendants’ opposition to the Motion for Summary Judgment, while jointly filed, separately addressed each defendant’s relative culpability based on an entirely different set of facts. Accordingly, the court will apportion fees incurred to oppose the 9/18/23 writ on grounds of equity.

C.      Standards for determining a reasonable fee.

       To determine whether fees are reasonable, the court begins with the lodestar, which is the number of hours reasonably spent multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) The court considers a number of factors including "the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required in its handling, the skill employed, the attention given, the success or failure, and other circumstances in the case.’” (PLCM Group at 1096.) The lodestar figure can be adjusted based on the factors specific to the case. (Id.)

       To determine a reasonable market rate, "the courts will look to equally difficult or complex types of litigation.” (Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691, 700.) The “market rate” is generally based on the rates prevalent in the community where the court is located. (Id.) The trial court is in the best position to value the services rendered by the attorneys in his or her courtroom for the type of litigation at issue. The prevailing party is entitled to “’compensation for all the hours ‘reasonably spent‘ in litigating the action to a successful conclusion.” (Ibid., italics in original.) ‘Reasonably spent’ means that time spent in the form of inefficient or duplicative efforts is not subject to compensation. (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 394.)

       The court has considered the declaration of counsel, Shelly Jay Shafron, in support of its motion for a fee award. Shafron asserts that 321.10 hours were spent defending Metro Inc., which includes fees for filing the present motion. The evidence does not provide sufficient evidence for the court to determine whether costs incurred in defense of Metro Inc. were reasonable, not duplicative, excessive, or inefficient. Defense counsel excluded fees incurred on behalf Azzam, subtracted that subtotal from total fees incurred, to arrive at a fee award on Metro Inc.’s behalf of $99,209.25, apparently by default. (Shafron decl., Ex. 1, 2.)

       It is not clear how defense counsel was able to parse the fees as between Azzam and Metro Inc. when the itemized billing statements do not differentiate between the two defendants, but were block billed. Block billing is not objectionable per se unless it "exacerbate[s] the vagueness of counsel's fee request, a risky choice since the burden of proving entitlement to fees rests on the moving party.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325.)  The court has discretion to reduce fee awards or deny them altogether where it is difficult to determine the reasonableness of time spent, particularly in this case, in defense of Metro Inc. Defense counsel must endeavor to “prune the fee request to comply with the law” and “may not transfer that responsibility to the court.” (Id. at 1329; Guillory v. Hill (2019) 36 Cal.App.5th 802, 814–815 ["overinclusive, unculled, redundant, redacted, and padded billing  entries …  destroy the credibility of the submission and ... justifies a severe reduction."].)

       In submitting a fee request an attorney's “chief asset” is his or her credibility. (Christian at 1325.) Vague, block billed time entries inflated with non-compensable hours destroy that credibility with the trial court.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325–1326.)

VI.  CONCLUSION

       The billing statements are not helpful to the court as they seek reward for preparing vague emails, “rent checks,” “telephone calls” and letters which do not provide a basis for the court to consider whether such tasks had anything to do with Metro Inc.’s defense. Therefore, Metro Inc. is ordered to submit a supplemental declaration and spreadsheet identifying the date, tasks (specific to each Defendant where possible), who performed the work, the hours expended and at what hourly rate. The chart should reflect the total hours each counsel spent on the case.

       Where certain tasks are common and involved a joint filing, such as preparing Defendants’ joint answer, defense counsel is to so indicate so as to permit the court to apportion such tasks equitably between the two Defendants.

       Accordingly, the court continues the hearing to July 30, 2024,  at 8:30 a.m. in Department A of the Compton Courthouse. Metro Inc. is ordered to provide the above supplemental evidence and spreadsheet to identify the work specific to Metro Inc., which shall be filed 10 days before the hearing.

24CMCP00073 In re Petition of: Palm Gate Agency, LLC

Thursday, June 13, 2024, at 8:30 a.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PETITION TO APPROVE TRANSFER OF STRUCTURED SETTLEMENT PAYMENT RIGHTS

 

       Real party in interest, Breniyah Burley (“Burley”), settled a prior civil action in Case No. TC027341 Brejanea Burley, et al. v. County of Los Angeles for the alleged wrongful death of a parent. The Hon. Mark C. Kim approved the settlement of $600,000 on January 13, 2022, which funds were used to settle an annuity providing for structured payments to Burley over time.

       Burley has entered into an agreement to sell a portion of future scheduled payments totaling $179,457.24. In exchange, Burley agreed to accept $45,548.00, which is the discounted present value of the transferred payments.

       California Insurance Code section 10139.5 provides that a transfer of structured settlement payment rights is not effective unless the transfer has been approved in advance in a final court order based on the following express findings by the court:

(1)  The transfer is in the best interest of the payee, considering the welfare and support of the payee’s dependents.

(2)  The payee has been advised in writing by the transferee to seek independent professional advice regarding the transfer and has either received that advice or knowingly waived that advice in writing.

(3)  The transferee has provided the payee with a disclosure form that complies with Section 10136 and the transfer agreement complies with Sections 10136 and 10138.

(4)  The transfer does not contravene any applicable statute or the order of any court or other government authority.

(5)  The payee reasonably understands the terms of the transfer agreement, including the terms set forth in the disclosure statement required by Section 10136.

(6)  The payee understands and does not wish to exercise the payee’s right to cancel the transfer agreement. (Ins. Code, § 10139.5(a).)      

       In determining whether the proposed transfer of structured settlement rights is reasonable and in the best interest of the transferor, the court considers the totality of circumstances “including, but not limited to: (1) the reasonable preference and desire of the payee to complete the proposed transaction, taking into account the payee’s age, mental capacity, legal knowledge, and apparent maturity level; (2) the stated purpose of the transfer; (3) the payee’s financial and economic situation; (4) the terms of the transaction, including whether the payee is transferring monthly or lump sum payments or all or a portion of his or her future payments; (5) whether the future periodic payments were intended to pay for future medical care of the payee related to the incident that was the subject of the settlement; (6) whether the payee has other means of income or support sufficient to meet the payee’s future financial obligations for support of payee’s dependents, such as child support; (7) whether there were previous transactions involving payee’s structured settlement payments; and (8) whether the payee and his or her dependents are facing a hardship situation.” Ins. Code, § 10139.5(b).

       Petitioner provided the required disclosures to the transferor including the terms of the transfer and advising transferor to seek legal advice, among other information required by statute. (Pet Ex. D, E.).

       Burley is now 19 years old, is not married, and does not have dependents. (Burley Decl, Ex. F.) Burley will use the funds to pay for college tuition as she is currently in three different schools. (Burley Decl., Ex. H.) She requires the funds for a rental she has obtained. She does not wish to incur student loan obligations. She will not use the funds for future living expenses.

       Based on the foregoing, the Court approves the Petition for Transfer of Structured Settlement Payments.
