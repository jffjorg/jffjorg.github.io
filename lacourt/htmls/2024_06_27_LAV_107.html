DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22VECV01184    Hearing Date: June 27, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

GOLDEN STATE MEDICAL SERVICES, LLC,

 

         Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

 

TERRY MCELROY,

        

          Defendant.

   Case Number             Department

   22VECV01184       107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

 

[THE FOLLOWING IS A TENTATIVE RULING IN THE ABOVE CASE]:       

 

Cross-defendants Golden State Medical Services, LLC, Vikram Singh, Nova Surgical Institute, and California Back and Pain Specialists move for summary judgment on defendant and cross-complainant Terry McElroy’s cross-complaint. This court grants the cross-defendants’ motion in part and denies the motion in part.

 

I. BACKGROUND

On February 4, 2022, plaintiff Golden State Medical Services, LLC (“Plaintiff”) and defendant Terry McElroy (“Defendant”) entered into an agreement through which Defendant assigned his rights in certain liens on patients to Plaintiff. (Complaint p. 2.) On March 7, 2022, Defendant told Plaintiff that the agreement was not valid. (Ibid.) 

 

On August 17, 2022, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint alleging a cause of action for breach of contract. 

 

On October 28, 2022, Defendant filed a cross-complaint (the “Cross-Complaint”) against Plaintiff, Vikram Singh (“Singh”), Nova Surgical Institute (“Nova”), and California Back and Pain Specialists (“Specialists” and, together with Plaintiff, Singh, and Nova, “Moving Parties”) alleging causes of action for (1) financial elder abuse, (2) fraudulent deceit under Civil Code Sec. 1709, (3) breach of fiduciary duty, and (4) identity theft. 

 

On March 4, 2024, Moving Parties filed a motion for summary judgment (the “Motion”).

 

II. DISCUSSION

 

“A party may move for summary judgment in¿an¿action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿437c, subd. (a)(1).) The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure §¿437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)¿“A triable issue of material fact exists where ‘the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.’” (California Medical Assn. v. Aetna Health of California Inc. (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 660, 665, quoting Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co., supra at 850.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the moving party must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) A cause of action “cannot be established” if the undisputed facts presented by defendant prove the contrary of plaintiff's allegations¿as a matter of law. (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1597.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) Once the moving party has met that burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. (Code Civ. Proc, § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)¿¿ 

 

When a party “attempt[s] to show that plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of action by showing that plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, the needed evidence,” “[s]uch a showing may be made by presenting evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence because she has been provided with extensive discovery and has discovered nothing.” (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 176.)

 

A.    The First Cause of Action for Financial Elder Abuse

 

“[F]inancial abuse of an elder occurs when any person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, or retains real or personal property of an elder adult to a wrongful use or with an intent to defraud, or both. A wrongful use is defined as taking, secreting, appropriating, or retaining property in bad faith. Bad faith occurs where the person or entity knew or should have known that the elder had the right to have the property transferred or made readily available to the elder or to his or her representative.” (Teselle v. McLoughlin, supra, 173 Cal.App.4th 156 at 174.)

 

In Teselle v. McLoughlin, supra, at 176, the defendants failed to meet their burden of showing evidence that the plaintiff did not possess, and could reasonably obtain, needed evidence to establish its claim of financial elder abuse because “one of plaintiff's discovery responses state[d] that defendants ha[d] the partnership books and records, and that plaintiff ha[d] requested, but not been given access to them” and the defendants had not “affirmatively shown that they ha[d] provided plaintiff with the partnership books and records.”

 

Moving Parties do not bring evidence to conclusively negate an element of Defendant’s cause of action but instead argue that Defendant does not possess, and cannot reasonably attain, needed evidence to establish the cause of action. However, similarly to the case in Teselle v. McLoughlin, supra, at 176, Defendant responded to discovery that evidence of damages for this claim is “in the custody and control of [Moving Parties] and third parties” and Moving Parties have not affirmatively shown that they have provided Defendant with the necessary documents and records for him to possess evidence of his cause of action.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion as to the first cause of action for financial elder abuse.

 

B.     The Second Cause of Action for Fraudulent Deceit

 

“The well-established common law elements of fraud which give rise to the tort action for deceit are: (1) misrepresentation of a material fact (consisting of false representation, concealment or nondisclosure); (2) knowledge of falsity (scienter); (3) intent to deceive and induce reliance; (4) justifiable reliance on the misrepresentation; and (5) resulting damage.” (People v. Lucero (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 370, 424.)

 

Defendant bases his claim for fraudulent deceit on Moving Parties’ “failure to disclose and concealment” of certain alleged facts. (Cross-Complaint pp. 6–7.)

 

“There are four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.” (Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1186.) “Other than the first instance, in which there must be a fiduciary relationship between the parties, the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presuppose the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. Where material facts are known to one party and not to the other, failure to disclose them is not actionable fraud unless there is some relationship between the parties which gives rise to a duty to disclose such known facts. A relationship between the parties is present if there is some sort of transaction between the parties. Thus, a duty to disclose may arise from the relationship between seller and buyer, employer and prospective employee, doctor and patient, or parties entering into any kind of contractual agreement.” (Id. at 1187, emphasis original, internal citations omitted.)

 

Moving Parties contend that Defendant cannot show the existence of a relationship between himself and them, as is necessary to support Defendant’s fraudulent deceit cause of action predicated on concealment. (Motion pp. 11–12.) Moving Parties bring evidence that they did not have a direct relationship with Defendant but rather that Defendant’s corporation was an independent contractor of theirs. (Cross-Complaint ¶ 10; Singh Decl. ¶ 11, Exhs. A–G; Ivanova Decl. ¶ 5.) Thus, Moving Parties bring evidence that negates an essential element of Defendant’s claim, and the burden shifts to Defendant to show a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to this cause of action. Because Defendant does not oppose the Motion and submits no evidence, he fails to meet this burden.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to the second cause of action for fraudulent deceit.

 

C.    The Third Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty

 

“The elements of a claim for breach of fiduciary duty are (1) the existence of a fiduciary relationship, (2) its breach, and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach.” (O'Neal v. Stanislaus County Employees' Retirement Assn. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1184, 1215.)

 

Moving Parties contend that Defendant cannot show the existence of a fiduciary duty between him and themselves. (Motion p. 14.) Moving Parties bring evidence that they did not have a direct relationship with Defendant but rather that Defendant’s corporation was an independent contractor of theirs. (Cross-Complaint ¶ 10; Singh Decl. ¶ 11, Exhs. A–G; Ivanova Decl. ¶ 5.) Thus, Moving Parties bring evidence that negates an essential element of Defendant’s claim, and the burden shifts to Defendant to show a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to this cause of action. Because Defendant does not oppose the Motion and submits no evidence, he fails to meet this burden.

 

Accordingly, this court GRANTS the Motion as to the third cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.

 

D.    The Fourth Cause of Action for Identity Theft

 

To bring a cause of action for identity theft, a party must show that they have been a victim of identity theft as defined in Civil Code section 1798.93. (CTC Real Estate Services v. Lepe (2006) 140 Cal.App.4th 856, 860.) “‘Victim of identity theft’ means a person who had their personal identifying information used without authorization by another to obtain credit, goods, services, money, or property, and did not use or possess the credit, goods, services, money, or property obtained by the identity theft, and has submitted a Federal Trade Commission identity theft report. In the alternative, the person may have filed a police report in this regard pursuant to Section 530.5 of the Penal Code.” (Civil Code. § 1798.93, subd. (d).)

 

Defendant alleges in the Cross-Complaint that Moving Parties obtained a post office box in his name without his knowledge or authorization, directed various law firms to mail settlement checks to this post office box, “signed [Defendant’s] name to checks received to this fraudulent post office box and negotiated those checks, retaining those funds,” “failed to advise [Defendant] at any time as to the existence of this [p]ost office box, the receipt of any checks or of the fact that they were retaining the funds from those checks for themselves,” and “intentionally misrepresented to third parties that they were [Defendant] in an attempt to obtain funds that rightfully belonged to [Defendant].” (Cross-Complaint p. 9.)

 

Moving Parties argue that Defendant does not have standing to bring this cause of action and bring evidence that Defendant’s corporation authorized the collection of liens by Moving Parties on its behalf. However, this evidence only partially contradicts the allegations made by Defendant in the Cross-Complaint and therefore fails to negate an essential element of the cause of action. Moving Parties do not argue and raise no evidence to contradict Defendant’s allegation that they opened a post office box in his name, signed his name on checks, or misrepresented to third parties that they were Defendant. For example, they raise no evidence that the post office box was opened in the name of Defendant’s corporation, not in Defendant’s name as an individual, and do not submit affidavits by each of Moving Parties averring that each never signed any checks in Defendant’s name.

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion as to the fourth cause of action for identity theft.

I. BACKGROUND

On June 27, 2022, plaintiff Walter Morales (“Plaintiff”) purchased a certain new 2022 Honda Accord (the “Vehicle”), which was subject to a warranty by defendant American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Defendant”). (Complaint ¶ 8.)

 

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff initiated this lawsuit by filing a complaint (the “Complaint”) alleging causes of action for (1) violation of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act—breach of express warranty and (2) fraudulent inducement—concealment.

 

On May 28, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer (the “Demurrer”) and a motion to strike (the “Motion”). On June 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed oppositions (the “Oppositions”) to the Demurrer and the Motion, and on June 20, 2024, Defendant filed replies (the “Replies”) to the Oppositions.

 

II. PROCEDURAL MATTERS

 

  1. Timeliness of Filings

 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.40, subdivision (a), a demurrer to a complaint should be filed within 30 days after service of the complaint. Similarly, per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿435, subdivision (b)(1), a motion to strike should be filed “within the time allowed to respond to a pleading,” which is 30 days. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿412.20, subd. (a)(3).)¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Per California Code of Civil Procedure §¿1005, subdivision (b), moving papers should be filed at least 16 court days before they are set for hearing, oppositions thereto should be filed at least nine court days before the set hearing, and replies to any oppositions should be filed at least five court days before the set hearing. Judges have discretion as to whether to consider or strike an untimely demurrer or pleading. (McAllister v. County of Monterey (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 253, 281; Tuck v. Thuesen (1970) 10 Cal.App.3d 193, 196, disapproved on other grounds by Neel v. Magana, Olney, Levy, Cathcart & Gelfand (1971) 6 Cal.3d 176.)¿¿ 

 

Plaintiff filed a proof of service indicating that Defendant was personally served on April 11, 2024. Defendant attaches to the Demurrer proof that parties agreed in writing to extend the deadline to respond to the Complaint to May 28, 2024. We are satisfied that all filings made in connection with the Demurrer and the Motion are timely.

 

  1. The Meet and Confer Requirement

 

California Code of Civil Procedure §¿430.41, subdivision (a) and §¿435.5, subdivision (a) requires parties to “meet and confer” in good faith before the filing of a demurrer or a motion to strike, respectively, to attempt to informally resolve the objections raised in the demurrer or the motion to strike. Each section requires that such meet and confer be done “in person or by telephone.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§¿430.1, subd. (a), 435.5, subd. (a).)¿¿ 

 

Defendant submits with the Demurrer and the Motion a declaration by its counsel, which confirms that parties spoke about the issues raised in the Demurrer and the Motion but were unable to resolve their dispute before their filings. We are therefore satisfied that this requirement has been met.

 

III. LEGAL STANDARD

 

  1. The Demurrer

 

Defendant demurs to the second cause of action for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute the cause of action, for being vague and uncertain, and for “includ[ing] allegations that clearly disclose some defense or bar to recovery.” (Demurrer p. 6.)

 

1.      Federal Preemption by the NHTSA

 

“Preemption occurs three ways: (1) where federal law states expressly that state law is preempted; (2) where federal law is so comprehensive that it leaves no room in the covered field for supplementary state regulation; and (3) where there is an actual conflict between state and federal law. The issue is one of Congressional intent.” (Capital Research & Management Co. v. Brown (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 58, 65.) There is a strong presumption that federal statutes do not preempt state law, especially as to matters traditionally governed by states such as health and safety. (Frastaci v. Vapor Corp.  (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 1389, 1395.) “Thus, pre-emption will not lie unless it is ‘the clear and manifest purpose of Congress.’” (CSX Transp., Inc. v. Easterwood (1993) 507 U.S. 658, 664.) “Courts are reluctant to infer preemption, and it is the burden of the party claiming Congress intended to preempt state law to prove it.” (McKenney v. Purepac Pharmaceutical Co. (2008) 167 Cal.App.4th 72, 80.)

 

Defendant contends that “Plaintiff’s concealment claim is preempted by federal law” because while California law does not “regulate the disclosure of the information sought by Plaintiff,” the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (“NHTSA”) specifies “when and how a manufacturer provides notice to owners of noncompliant motor vehicles.” (Demurrer pp. 7–10.) Defendant does not contend that the NHTSA expressly states that its regulations preempt state law and Defendant’s argument about the absence of a California law mandating the disclosure of the information at issue points to no direct conflict between California and federal law. So, Defendant seems to argue that the NHTSA’s regulation of motor vehicle manufacturer disclosures to motor vehicle owners “is so comprehensive that it leaves no room in the covered field for supplementary state regulation.”

 

Defendant, as the party who is claiming that Congress intended for the NHTSA’s regulations to preempt state law, bears the burden of proving that preemption is Congress’ “clear and manifest purpose.” Defendant must overcome a strong presumption that federal law does not preempt state law that exists in this case because disclosure of motor vehicle sensor defects relates to health and safety. We find that Defendant fails to meet this burden. Defendant argues that “NHTSA may not impose undue burdens upon manufacturers” and that “NHTSA also may not disclose information that it is prohibited by law to release to the public such as personal identifying information or confidential business information.” (Demurrer p. 10.) Defendant explains the “[f]actors considered by NHTSA in its development of this rule” (ibid.), which implies a goal of the NHTSA to prevent unfair use of the data by competitors or consumers, but which does not speak to NHTSA’s or Congress’ intentions regarding how these regulations should affect state law.

 

Plaintiff argues that it is inappropriate to consider Defendant’s argument about federal preemption in a demurrer because “Defendant is requesting in their demurrer is to ask the Court to consider extrinsic evidence such as the duties and responsibilities of the [NHTSA] and the intent of Congress,” which is premature before a motion for summary adjudication. (Opposition p. 2.) We disagree in this case. Where “issues are tendered on undisputed facts and are purely legal in nature,” the “issue of preemption is properly handled by demurrer.” (Washington Mutual Bank v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 606, 612; accord Credit Managers Ass'n of California v. Countrywide Home Loans, Inc. (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 590, 593.)

 

Because we find that Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing Congress’ intent that NHTSA’s regulations should preempt state law, we consequently decline to find that state law and Plaintiff’s cause of action for fraudulent concealment are preempted by federal law.

 

2.      Cause of Action for Fraudulent Concealment

 

“Concealment is a species of fraud, and fraud must be pleaded with specificity.” (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 878.) “[T]he requirement that ‘fraud must be pleaded with specificity’ applies equally to a cause of action for fraud and deceit based on concealment.” (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1472.) This requirement that fraud be pled with specificity “necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom and by what means the representations were tendered.” (Gutierrez v. Carmax Auto Superstores California (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 1234, 1260.)

 

Defendant contends that “[a] plaintiff's burden in asserting a fraud claim against a corporate employer is even greater.” (Demurrer p. 12.) While this is true, it is not applicable to the instant action. Plaintiff does not allege that he was ever employed by Defendant. Thus, the pleading standard that Plaintiff must meet through the allegations of the Complaint is not heightened by this rule of law in the instant case.

 

“The required elements for fraudulent concealment are (1) concealment or suppression of a material fact; (2) by a defendant with a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff; (3) the defendant intended to defraud the plaintiff by intentionally concealing or suppressing the fact; (4) the plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he or she did if he or she had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) plaintiff sustained damage as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact.” (Hambrick v. Healthcare Partners Medical Group, Inc. (2015) 238 Cal.App.4th 124, 162.)

 

“Although, typically, a duty to disclose arises when a defendant owes a fiduciary duty to a plaintiff, a duty to disclose may also arise when a defendant possesses or exerts control over material facts not readily available to the plaintiff.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1199.) “There are four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.” (Hoffman v. 162 North Wolfe LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 1178, 1186.) “Other than the first instance, in which there must be a fiduciary relationship between the parties, the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presuppose the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. Where material facts are known to one party and not to the other, failure to disclose them is not actionable fraud unless there is some relationship between the parties which gives rise to a duty to disclose such known facts. A relationship between the parties is present if there is some sort of transaction between the parties. Thus, a duty to disclose may arise from the relationship between seller and buyer, employer and prospective employee, doctor and patient, or parties entering into any kind of contractual agreement.” (Id. at 1187, emphasis original, internal citations omitted.) “Suppression of a material fact is actionable when there is a duty of disclosure, which may arise from a relationship between the parties, such as a buyer-seller relationship.” (Dhital v. Nissan North America, Inc. (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 828, 843 (hereafter, Dhital).) However, “[s]uch a transaction must necessarily arise from direct dealings between the plaintiff and the defendant; it cannot arise between the defendant and the public at large.” (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 312 [finding that while “a manufacturer has a duty to warn consumers of a product's hazards and faults” applied to strict products liability claims, the same duty does not apply to fraud causes of action].)

 

In Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th 828 at 833, the plaintiffs brought a cause of action for fraudulent inducement by concealment against the manufacturer of a vehicle they had purchased. The court in Dhital, supra, at 844 found that plaintiffs adequately pled the existence of a relationship between themselves and the vehicle’s manufacturer because they alleged that (1) they bought the vehicle from the manufacturer’s dealership, (2) the manufacturer “backed the car with an express warranty,” (3) the manufacturer’s “authorized dealerships [were] its agents for purposes of the sale of [the manufacturer’s] vehicles to consumers.”

 

Defendant contends that the Complaint does not allege each of the elements of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment and specifically that Defendant owed any duty to disclose the Vehicle’s alleged issues to Defendant before the sale of the Vehicle. (Demurrer pp. 13–15.) We disagree.

 

The Complaint alleges that (1) Defendant “manufactured and/or distributed vehicles throughout the United States, including the [Vehicle], equipped with defective computerized driver-assistance safety systems”; (2) “[t]hese defective computerized driver-assisting safety systems were installed in the [Vehicle]”; (3) “[t]he [Vehicle] was purchased by Plaintiff(s) on June 27, 2022 from an authorized [Defendant] dealership in Los Angeles”; (4) “[t]he [Vehicle] was equipped with a defective computerized driver-assistance safety system and a defective collision mitigation braking system”; (5) Defendant backed the sale of the Vehicle with an express written warranty; (6) Defendant was aware of the defect in the Vehicle and similar vehicles but “[Defendant] and its agents actively concealed the existence and nature of the [defect] from Plaintiff(s) at the time of purchase, repair, and thereafter”; (7) Plaintiff dealt with “authorized agents” of Defendant in the purchase of the Vehicle; (8) “[Defendant] and its agents intentionally concealed and failed to disclose facts relating to the [defect]”; (9) “[Defendant] was the only party with knowledge of the [defect]. Information regarding the defect was never made publicly available, nor did Defendant publicly or privately disclose any of the information to Plaintiff. [Defendant] had exclusive knowledge of the defect”; (10) “[Defendant’s] authorized dealerships are its agents for purposes of the sale of [Defendant’s] vehicles to consumers such as Plaintiff(s)”; (11) “[Defendant] produces and distributes marketing materials, including brochures and manuals, to its authorized dealerships for the purpose of providing them to consumers such as Plaintiff(s). These marketing materials are meant to provide certain information regarding [Defendant’s] vehicles to the consumer”; (12) “[w]ith these marketing materials and its dealership personnel, [Defendant] is able to communicate indirectly to consumers such as Plaintiff(s) regarding its vehicles”; (13) “[Defendant] actively concealed information from the public, preventing Plaintiff(s) from discovering any of the concealed facts as described in detail above”; (14) “[Defendant] fraudulently induced Plaintiff(s) to acquire the [Vehicle] [he] would not have purchased but for [Defendant’s] concealment of the [defect]”; (15) “[p]rior to the date of sale and on the date of sale Defendant had an opportunity to disclose the [defect] to Plaintiff(s), but instead concealed from and failed to disclose to Plaintiff(s), any of the known irreparable issues with the [Vehicle]”; (16) “Plaintiff(s) has been exposed numerous times to potential personal injury, physical harm, and related liability from motor vehicle accidents caused by the [defect] and problems presented by the [Vehicle] as a result of [Defendant’s] fraudulent concealment in failing to disclose the [defect] to Plaintiff(s) at the time they purchased the [Vehicle] and each time they presented the [Vehicle] for repair”; (17) “Plaintiff(s) was harmed by purchasing the [Vehicle] that Plaintiff(s) would not have purchased had [Defendant] and/or its agents disclosed the true facts about the [defect] at or prior to the acquisition. Plaintiff(s) also suffered diminution in the value of [the Vehicle], out-of-pocket expenses, damages in the amount of the difference between the value of the [V]ehicle equipped with a defective computerized driver-assistance safety system and the value of the [V]ehicle if it had been equipped as warranted, and reliance damages. Plaintiff(s) also incurred expenses relating to registration, insurance, and maintenance of the [Vehicle]”; (18) “Plaintiff(s) was harmed by Defendant’s concealment of the [defect] because Plaintiff(s) was induced to acquire a vehicle that Plaintiff(s) would not have otherwise purchased”; (19) “Defendant’s concealment of the defect was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiff’s harm”; and (20) “[Defendant] intended to deceive Plaintiff(s) by concealing the known issues with the [defect], in an effort to sell the [Vehicle].” (Complaint pp. 19–23, 25–26.)

 

The Complaint thus alleges that Defendant and its agents “actively concealed” the existence of the defect from Plaintiff and the public before and after the sale of the Vehicle despite being aware of the defect and possessing exclusive knowledge of the defect, which adequately pleads the first element of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment. The Complaint also alleges that Defendant had a duty to disclose the defect. Similar to the pleading in Dhital, supra, 84 Cal.App.5th 828 at 844, the Complaint alleges that (1) Plaintiff bought the vehicle from an authorized dealership and “authorized agents” of Defendant, (2) Defendant backed the car with an express warranty, (3) and that the dealership and its agents were authorized agents of Defendant. Therefore, like in Dhital, supra, at 844, this adequately pleads the second element of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment by alleging a buyer-seller relationship or transaction between Plaintiff and Defendant that arose from direct dealings between them. The Complaint also alleges that “[Defendant] intended to deceive Plaintiff(s) by concealing the known issues with the [defect], in an effort to sell the [Vehicle],” which adequately pleads the third element of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiff had no knowledge of the defect and would not have purchased the Vehicle had he known of the defect, which adequately pleads the fourth element of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment. Finally, the Complaint alleges that the Vehicle contained a defect and that Plaintiff was damages by its purchase, which adequately pleads the fifth element of a cause of action for fraudulent concealment. The Complaint thus alleges each of the elements for a cause of action for fraudulent concealment. We find that these allegations are not so vague or uncertain as to prevent Defendant from understanding the nature of the claims being made against it. We further find that these allegations are pled with the requisite specificity for fraud allegations.

 

Accordingly, this court OVERRULES the Demurrer.

 

  1. The Motion to Strike

 

Defendant moves to strike the Complaint’s prayer for punitive damages. (Motion pp. 1–2.)

 

A motion to strike is appropriate to attack a claim for punitive damages. (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 164.) “In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff.¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.” (Clausen v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255 (internal citations omitted).) “Further, even though certain language pleads ultimate facts or conclusions of law, such language when read in context with the facts alleged as to defendants' conduct may adequately plead the evil motive requisite to recovery of¿punitive¿damages.” (Monge v. Superior Court (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 503, 510.)¿ 

¿ 

“In order to state a prima facie claim for¿punitive¿damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general¿punitive¿damage¿statute,¿Civil Code section 3294.” (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California, Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) California Civil Code section 3294 authorizes the recovery of punitive damages “[i]n an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice….”¿ (Civ. Code § 3294(a).) “In order to justify an award of punitive¿damages, the defendant must be guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. He must act with the intent to vex, injure or annoy, or with a conscious disregard of the plaintiff's rights.” (Silberg v. California Life Ins. Co. (1974) 11 Cal.3d 452, 462 (internal citations omitted).) “A claim for exemplary damage may be supported by pleading that the wrong was committed willfully or with a design to injure.” (Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1055.) “[M]alice in fact is required rather than implied malice.¿However, malice does not require actual intent to harm.¿Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences.” (Angie M. v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1217, 1228 (internal citations omitted).) “In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his¿conduct, and that he wilfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.” (Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895–96.)¿¿ 

¿ 

“Punitive¿damages¿may not be¿pleaded¿generally.” (Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of¿punitive¿damages.” (Grieves v. Superior Court, supra, 157 Cal.App.3d 159 at 166.) And, “conclusory characterization of defendant's conduct as intentional, wilful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of “oppression, fraud, or malice, express or implied,” within the meaning of¿section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) “Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves, supra, at 166; Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority, supra, at 1193 [“In addition to the requirement that the operative complaint set forth the elements as stated in¿section 3294, it must include¿specific¿factual allegations showing that defendant's conduct was oppressive, fraudulent, or malicious to support a claim for¿punitive¿damages.”].)¿ 

 

California Civil Code section 3294 provides that an employer shall be liable for punitive damages only if it “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice,” and further, “[w]ith respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Cal. Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (b).) “In order to demonstrate that an employee is a true managing agent under¿section 3294, subdivision (b), a plaintiff seeking punitive damages would have to show that the employee exercised substantial discretionary authority over significant aspects of a corporation's business.” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 577.) “The scope of a corporate employee's discretion and authority under our managing¿agent test is therefore a¿question of fact¿for decision on a case-by-case basis.” (Davis v. Kiewit Pacific Co. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 358, 366, emphasis original.) Moreover, “liability under¿Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (b)¿requires actual knowledge by an¿employer.” (Weeks v. Baker & McKenzie (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1158.) 

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiff fails to plead that it acted with “oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Motion p. 7.) However, the Complaint alleges that Defendant intentionally marketed and sold to Plaintiff a vehicle with a sensor safety defect of which Defendant possessed exclusive knowledge and which Defendant intentionally concealed from Plaintiff despite its knowledge that the defect posed a safety risk to the drivers of vehicles with such defect. A reasonable person could find that these alleged actions constitute oppression, fraud, or malice, or with design to injure, or at least with reckless disregard for Plaintiff’s safety.

 

Defendant further argues that the Complaint’s punitive damages claim must fail because “as set forth in [Defendant’s] concurrently filed Demurrer, which [Defendant] incorporates by reference herein, Plaintiffs' fraud claim is preempted by federal and is insufficiently plead.” (Motion p. 7.) However, as explained above, we disagree with this conclusion and therefore decline to strike the punitive damages claim on these grounds. Defendant also argues that the punitive damages claim is not pled with sufficient specificity (ibid.), but as explained above, we disagree and therefore also decline to strike the punitive damages claim on these grounds.

 

Defendant finally argues that the punitive damages claim “must plead and prove that the oppression, fraud, or malice was on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation” because it, as a corporate entity, only acts through its agents and employees. (Motion pp. 7–8.) However, the Complaint alleges that “[a]ll acts of corporate employees as alleged were authorized or ratified by an officer, director or managing agent of the corporate employer.” (Complaint p. 2.)

 

Accordingly, this court DENIES the Motion.
