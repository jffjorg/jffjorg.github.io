DEPARTMENT M LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23SMCV02923    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Adamyan v. O’Gara Coach Company LLC, et al.

CASE NO.:                23SMCV02923

MOTION:                  Motion to Compel Initial Discovery Responses

HEARING DATE:   12/10/2024

 

COMPEL Legal Standard

 

 If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction. (CCP § 2030.290(b).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906.) 

 

Where there has been no timely response to a CCP section 2031.010 inspection demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response. (CCP § 2031.300.) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product. Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion. Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required. 

 

Pursuant to CCP section 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions. Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.” (CCP, § 2033.280(b).) The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (CCP, § 2033.280(c).) 

 

COMPEL ANALYSIS 

 

Defendant O’Gara Coach Company, LLC moves to compel initial discovery responses from Plaintiff Adamyan Akop as to its first set of Special Interrogatories, Form Interrogatories, and Inspection Demands, as well as to deem matters admitted as to its first set of Requests for Admissions. For each motion, Defendant requests monetary sanctions in the amount of $465.00 against Plaintiff Adamyan Akop (“Plaintiff”) and/or his counsel of record, Mgdesyan Law Firm.

 

On August 20, 2024, O’Gara concurrently served Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Admission and Document Requests on Plaintiff. (Kim Decl., ¶ 2, Ex. A.) The last day to serve responses was September 23, 2024. (Kim Decl. ¶ 3.) Plaintiff never requested an extension to respond before the due date and failed to serve timely responses to the outstanding discovery. (Id.) On October 8, 2024, counsel for O’Gara sent correspondence to counsel for Plaintiff requesting a response, but to no avail. (Kim Decl. ¶ 4, Ex. B.) On October 10, 2024, at the hearing on Defendant Automobili Lamborghini America, LLC’s motions to compel, Plaintiff’s counsel informed the Court that he had lost all contact with his client and therefore, he would be withdrawing as counsel in this matter. (Kim Decl. ¶ 5.) Plaintiff has not responded to the subject discovery. (Id. at ¶ 7.)

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

 

Sanctions are mandatory. The Court must sanction any party that unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response, “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust” (See, e.g., CCP, § 2030.290(c) and without exception as to the RFAs. The court finds the noticed sanctions reasonable in amount. Therefore, sanctions are imposed as noticed in the total amount of $1,860.00, inclusive of costs, against Plaintiff and his counsel of record, Mgdesyan Law Firm, jointly and severally, payable within 30 days.

 

Further responses are due within 20 days without objection.  The RFAs are deemed admitted.

 

RELIEVED Legal Standard

 

The court may order that an attorney be changed or substituted at any time before or after judgment or final determination upon request by either client or attorney and after notice from one to the other. (CCP § 284(2).) The attorney seeking to withdraw must take “reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the rights of the client, including giving due notice to the client, allowing time for employment of other counsel.” (Cal. Rules of Prof. Conduct, 3-700(A)(2); see, e.g., Vann v. Shilleh (1975) [holding withdrawal prejudicial where attorney withdraw from the representation of defendant on the Friday before trial began the following Monday].) “The determination whether to grant or deny a motion to withdraw as counsel lies within the sound discretion of the trial court.” (Manfredi & Levine v. Superior Court (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 1128, 1133.) 

 

An application to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Counsel Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (CRC 3.1362(a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (CRC, 3.1362(d).) When a client is served by mail, the attorney’s declaration must indicate that the client’s address was confirmed within the last 30 days and how it was confirmed. (Id.) If the attorney is unable to confirm the client’s current address, the declaration must state the reasonable efforts made within the last 30 days to obtain the client’s current address. (Id.)  Additionally, the declaration “must state in general terms and without compromising the confidentiality of the attorney client relationship why” a motion is brought instead of filing a substitution of attorney. (CRC, 3.1362(c).)

 

RELIEVED ANALYSIS

 

Counsel Karapet Galajian of Mgdesyan Law Firm moves to be relieved as counsel of record for Plaintiff. Counsel submits all the mandatory forms. Counsel states that an irreparable breakdown of the attorney-client relationship has occurred, as counsel no longer has contact with Plaintiff. Counsel served his client by mail at his last known address, which was confirmed within the past 30 days by online searches and mail. The declaration notes the hearings that are on calendar, including the upcoming FSC and trial dates.

 

The Court notes that trial commences in less than a month, Withdrawal may create a risk of prejudice to Plaintiff. That said, the declarations demonstrate that counsel has taken reasonable steps to avoid reasonably foreseeable prejudice to the client. Plaintiff has a reasonable opportunity to obtain new counsel and may seek any appropriate relief if necessary.

 

Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED. The order is effective upon the filing of the proof of service of the order on the client. Until then, counsel remains counsel of record.


Case Number: 24SMCV00651    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: M

CASE NAME:           Shen, et al., v. Li, et al.

CASE NO.:                24SMCV00651

MOTION:                  Special Motion to Strike

HEARING DATE:   12/12/2024

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16 permits the Court to strike causes of action arising from an act in furtherance of the defendant's right of free speech or petition, unless the plaintiff establishes that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim. “The anti-SLAPP procedures are designed to shield a defendant’s constitutionally protected conduct from the undue burden of frivolous litigation.” (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 393.) “The anti-SLAPP statute does not insulate defendants from any liability for claims arising from the protected rights of petition or speech. It only provides a procedure for weeding out, at an early stage, meritless claims arising from protected activity.” (Id. at 384.) 

 

“Resolution of an anti-SLAPP motion involves two steps. First, the defendant must establish that the challenged claim arises from activity protected by section 425.16. If the defendant makes the required showing, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate the merit of the claim by establishing a probability of success.” (Baral, supra, 1 Cal.5th at 384, citation omitted.) The California Supreme Court has “described this second step as a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure.’ The court does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims. Its inquiry is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment. It accepts the plaintiff’s evidence as true, and evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law. ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’” (Id. at 384-385 [citations omitted].) 

 

A moving party must move to strike under this section within 60 days of service of the complaint. (CCP §425.16(f).)

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

            Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice is GRANTED.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Defendant Ziyi Yang moves to strike the Ninth (Fraudulent Transfer), Tenth (Fraudulent Transfer), Eleventh (Constructive Trust), Twelfth (Accounting), and Thirteenth (Declaratory Relief) causes of action in the First Amended Complaint (FAC). Yang argues that the gravamen of these causes arise from her activities in furtherance of her constitutionally protected rights of petition and speech, specifically, her alleged formation of two business entities activities through governmental bodies, including the California Secretary of State (SOS) and the Internal Revenue Service (IRS).

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16(e) defines protected acts as the following: 1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; 2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; 3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest; or 4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest. 

 

“To prevail on an anti-SLAPP motion, the movant must first make ‘a threshold showing the challenged cause of action’ arises from an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue.” (Varian Medical Systems, Inc. v. Delfino (2005) 35 Cal.4th 180, 192.) “The phrase ‘arising from’ means the defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.” (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 67.)  

 

“A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim.”  (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1062.)  “‘[D]efendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.’” (Id. at 1063.) “‘[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’”  (Id.)  “Instead, the focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’”  (Id.) “‘The only means specified in section 425.16 by which a moving defendant can satisfy that [‘arising from’] requirement is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct by which plaintiff claims to have been injured falls within one of the four categories described in subdivision (e) . .  .’”  (Id.) “In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.”  (Id.) 

 

The FAC alleges the following pertinent facts against Yang. Yang is a secretary and director of defendant Apple Blossom Foundation. (FAC, ¶¶ 14, 62.) Along with the other defendants, Yang secretly executed a grant deed for an entirely different property (a warehouse) with insufficient consideration. (¶ 172.) Yang and the other defendants sold the warehouse for “no consideration or insufficient consideration, in an attempt to pocket the proceeds, avoid creditors and to place such asset/property out of the reach of Plaintiffs’ collection efforts . . ..” (¶ 175.) Yang also set up Apple Blossom Foundation “intentionally…not to be a non-profit foundation…and instead, they set up the purported ‘foundation’ as a registered corporation.” (¶ 225.) Yang did not seek recognition of the entity by the IRS as a non-profit. (¶ 226.) Yang “attempted to cover [her] tracks and register a new entity as a true non-profit, using a similar name of Apple Blossom US Foundation.” (FAC, ¶ 228.) Yang filed documents with the California Secretary of State which reflected Yang as a manager/director/officer, but not Plaintiff. (¶ 230.) Additionally, Defendants Li and Steve purchased the Scientific Way Building on September 29, 2021. (¶ 231.) Defendants Li and Steve’s alleged purchase was “an attempt” by Defendants (including Yang) “to avoid creditors and to place such asset/property out of reach of Plaintiffs’ collection efforts.” (¶¶ 231-232.)

 

The Ninth and Tenth causes of action for Fraudulent Transfer alleges that Yang is liable for the warehouse transfers (FAC, ¶ 391), the Scientific Way Building transfer (FAC, ¶ 393), and the Claremont House transfer (FAC, ¶ 396). (See FAC ¶¶ 405, 407, 441, 414.) The Twelfth and Thirteenth causes of action generally pertain to these same activities.

 

Yang argues that the gravamen of the allegations against her show that liability is premised on protected conduct: writings made before agencies. She highlights that she allegedly set up Apple Blossom Foundation “intentionally…not to be a non-profit foundation…and instead, they set up the purported ‘foundation’ as a registered corporation.” (FAC ¶ 225.) The FAC further alleged that Yang did not seek recognition of the entity by the IRS, and as a result the entity was not recognized or listed by the IRS as a nonprofit. (FAC, ¶ 226.)  Yang attempted to cover up her misdeeds by registering a different entity as a the non-profit (FAC, ¶ 228).

 

Yang cites Li v. Jin (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 481 for the proposition that such conduct is protected by Code of Civil Procedure section 425.15(e)(1)-(2). However, Li is distinct from these alleged facts.  In Li, liability arose from protected statements (forms submitted to IRS) as opposed to a failure to register a corporation as non-profit. (See Id. at 491-494.) More specifically, Li concerned three alumni of a university and the governance of an alumni association. Fuzu Li served as vice-president, Jigang Jin served as executive vice-president, and Yaning Li served as president of the association. (Id. at 487.) The parties sought to incorporate as a non-profit organization. (Id. at 488.) Jin listed himself as secretary and chief financial officer, and Yaning Li as chief executive officer, and listed the wrong address for the organization on filings with the SOS. (Id.) Jin also filed several forms with the IRS for the association's application for tax-exempt status, listing Fuzu Li as a director, with no notice or consent given to Fuzu Li. (Id.) Jin also filed another form with the Franchise Tax Board (FTB), again without any board of directors meeting or consent from Fuzu Li. (Id.) When Fuzu Li first learned that he had been listed as a director on the application to the IRS, he was upset that his personal information had been used without his consent and that he wasn’t told he was on the board of directors. (Id.) Fuzu Li posted a message in the Association WeChat group, telling alumni that Jigang Jin registered a nonprofit corporation using Fuzu Li's personal information without telling him, that he felt “fooled,” and that he was concerned that Jin provided Fuzu Li's Social Security number and driver's license number in the application. (Id.)

 

Four days later, Jin filed a defamation complaint against Fuzu Li. In turn, Fuzu Li cross-claimed stating causes of action for breach of charitable trust, fraud, misappropriation of likeness, and negligent infliction of emotional distress. (Id. at 489.) Fuzu Li claimed Jin and Yaning wrongfully seized control of the association through Jin's filing of (1) the IRS application, (2) articles of incorporation and corporate statement of information with the California Secretary of State, and (3) a FTB application for state tax-exempt status. (Id. at 487, 489.) Jin and Yaning Li filed special motions to strike the cross-complaint under section 425.16, arguing that the crossclaims all arose from protected communications made with the SOS, FTB, and IRS. (Id.) The trial court denied the motions, finding that the statements to the agencies were not protected. (Id.) The appellate court reversed finding that the trial court should have reached the second prong of the analysis as to statements made to the IRS, but not as to the statements made to the SOS or FTB. (Id. at 491.)

 

Since it was undisputed that Fuzu Li’s claims rested on the filing of the statements, the Li court focused on whether such statements were before an “official proceeding” as defined by section 425.15(e)(1)-(2). (See Id., at 492-494.) The appellate court noted that the IRS’s determination of tax-exempt status is an administrative proceeding involving a comprehensive federal scheme, where the IRS makes discretionary, non-ministerial determinations interpreting the law and facts regarding a particular organization. (Ibid.) Statements made during the application process would be made before or in connection with an official proceeding, therefore giving the statements anti-SLAPP protection. On the other hand, the court found the other writings (the articles of incorporation and information filed with the SOS, and the submission exemption request to the FTB) were not made in connection with an official proceeding or with an issue of public interest. (Id. at 494-496.) Unlike the IRS application, these submissions were ministerial in nature and did not involve any agency discretion. (Id. at 495.) Further, section 425.15(e)(3)-(4) offered no protection because there was no ongoing controversy, dispute or discussion at the time Jin filed the documents with the SOS and FTB. (Id. at 496.)

 

Applying these principles to the case at hand, the claims in this matter do not arise from any statements made before or in connection with Yang’s statements to the IRS. As Yang observes, it was the opposite—she allegedly did not attempt to obtain tax-exempt charitable status for the Apple Foundation entities from the IRS. (FAC, ¶ 225.) Thus, the filings with the SOS and IRS are not the complained-of wrong. Yang notes the allegation that she attempted to “cover up” her misdeeds by registering a different entity as a non-profit. (FAC, ¶ 228.) While such allegation involves statements made during the IRS’s non-profit determination, this allegation is incidental to liability. It merely provides necessary context for the claims. Plaintiff alleges that he first learned of the transgressions after discovering that Defendants attempted to cover their tracks by registering a new entity as a true non-profit, using a similar name of Apple Blossom US Foundation. (Id.) Yang does not cite any cause of action which attempts to hold her liable for registering Apple Blossom US Foundation (the non-profit), as opposed to failing to register the Apple Blossom Foundation (a for-profit corporation) as the parties allegedly agreed. For the same reasons, the claims do not arise from any statements to the SOS. Even if the statements to the SOS did give rise to liability, such statements are not entitled to protection.

 

Here, the gravamen of the FAC as to Yang is that Yang aided and abetted the other defendants in the fraud perpetrated upon Plaintiff.  The IRS and SOS filings are not the alleged wrongs, but steps Yang allegedly took to conceal the fraud. Therefore, Yang fails to meet her burden on the first prong of the anti-SLAPP analysis and the motion to strike is DENIED.
