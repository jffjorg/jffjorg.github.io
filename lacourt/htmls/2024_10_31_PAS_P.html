DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23AHCV01187    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: P

TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS and ORDER DENYING FORMER COUNSEL’S REQUEST FOR MONETARY SANCTIONS

I.         INTRODUCTION

This is an action involving claims for: (1) breach of statutory obligations under Labor Code § 1194 et seq. for minimum wage violations; (2) breach of statutory obligations under Labor Code § 226 for paycheck stubs; (3) breach of statutory obligations under Wage Order 5-2001 regarding maximum rent; (4) common count for money paid, laid out, and expended; (5) misclassification as an independent contractor; and (6) unfair business practices under Business and Professions Code § 17200 et seq. Plaintiff Diana Cuamea (“Plaintiff”) filed this suit on May 25, 2023, against Defendants BP RE Management LLC, Zhimin Li (“collectively Defendants”). 

Defendants were initially represented by Farhad Novian of Novian & Novian LLP (“Former Counsel”), who was later substituted with David M. Daftary as counsel of record.

On September 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion seeking monetary sanctions against Defendants, current counsel, and former counsel, alleging discovery misuse and non-compliance with court orders. Defendants and former counsel each filed oppositions to Plaintiff’s motion on October 18, 2024, disputing the validity of Plaintiff’s claims and procedural basis for sanctions. Plaintiff subsequently filed a reply on October 22, 2024, addressing the arguments raised by both Defendants and former ounsel.

 

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 permits courts to impose sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that constitutes a misuse of the discovery process. Section 2023.010 provides a non-exhaustive list of conduct that is considered misuse of the discovery process, including as relevant here, failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery and disobeying a court order to provide discovery. (Cal. Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d), (g).)  

Further, Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 subdivision (d) gives courts the authority to issue a terminating sanction by one of four following orders: (1) an order striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings of any party engaging in misuse of the discovery process; (2) an order staying further proceedings until an order for discovery is obeyed; (3) an order dismissing the action, or any part of the action, of that party; or (4) an order rendering a default judgment against that party.  

In determining whether to impose terminating sanctions, trial courts must consider the totality of the circumstances, including whether the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of informal and formal attempts to obtain the discovery. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App,4th 1225, 1246.) 

The trial court has broad discretion in selecting discovery sanctions, subject to reversal only for abuse. (Reedy v. Bussell (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1272, 1293; Miranda v. 21st Century Ins. Co. (2004) 117 Cal.App.4th 913, 928–929.)  The trial court should consider both the conduct being sanctioned and its effect on the party seeking discovery and, in choosing a sanction, should “attempt to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.” (Do It Urself Moving & Storage, Inc. v. Brown, Leifer, Slatkin & Berns (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 27, 36.) 

The discovery statutes encourage an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.  “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Laguna Auto Body v. Farmers Ins. Exchange, 231 Cal.App.3d at p. 487.)  If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted; continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.  (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal. App. 4th 967, 992.) 

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Plaintiff argues that current counsel and former counsel engaged in discovery misuse by withholding relevant information about properties owned or managed by Defendants, concealing identities of additional resident managers associated with those properties, providing incomplete response to specific interrogatories, providing evasive responses to document demands, and failing to cooperate in good faith during meet and confer discussions. Specifically, Plaintiff contends that Defendants’ counsel failed to comply with the court’s February 27, 2024, Order, which required full, complete responses under oath to Interrogatories 1 and 2, without objection, within 20 days. (Motion pg. 4) Plaintiff argues that Defendants did not disclose all resident managers at Defendants’ properties in the court-ordered responses, forcing Plaintiff to take two depositions to obtain this information. (Id.) Plaintiff also claims Defendants misrepresented that all properties owned or managed by Defendants had been listed in response to Special Interrogatories (Set One), which required Plaintiff to physically visit certain properties located independently and to take further depositions to confirm which properties had resident managers pertinent to Plaintiff’s PAGA claims. (Id.)  Plaintiff further alleges that Defendants failed to properly search for and produce documents in response to document demands, necessitating additional depositions to clarify the completeness of Defendants' production. Lastly, Plaintiff asserts that Defendants canceled a scheduled deposition at the last minute, resulting in reporter fees. (Motion pg. 5) Plaintiff argues that this collective behavior constitutes discovery misuse under CCP § 2023.010 and seeks sanctions on the basis that the misuse stems from Defendants' counsel's actions.

In response, Defendants and former counsel argue that Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions under CCP § 2023.030 is procedurally flawed. Former counsel asserts that they did not violate the February 27, 2024, Order, as they were substituted out of the case on December 4, 2023, well before the order was issued (Novian Opposition, pg. 4). They also argue that they did not fail to respond to special interrogatories as claimed; during meet-and-confer correspondence, they clarified that “Defendants will not agree to describe all buildings owned by them,” as Plaintiff’s interrogatories “grossly exceed[ed] the scope of permissible discovery” (Novian Opposition, pg. 4). Former counsel further contends that Plaintiff’s accusations regarding hidden documents are unsupported and lack specific evidence showing how or why the document production was inadequate. Additionally, they emphasize that the cancellation of the April 17, 2024, deposition occurred months after their substitution and thus cannot be attributed to them (Novian Opposition, pg. 7-8).

Additionally, former counsel argues that Plaintiff’s assertion that Defendants failed to disclose all resident managers in Court-ordered responses is unsubstantiated. Former counsel maintains that they were entitled to rely on Defendants verified discovery responses to Special Interrogatories Set One, which identified only one additional resident manager besides Plaintiff. Former counsel further contends that current defense counsel had the opportunity to identify any additional individuals in the supplemental responses. Consequently, former counsel asserts they should not be held accountable for any oversights attributed to current counsel.

Defendants assert that Plaintiff cannot bring a motion for sanctions under CCP § 2023.030 without an underlying discovery motion or court order. Defendants emphasize that Plaintiff’s failure to file any motion to compel further responses undermines her request for sanctions, as CCP § 2023.030 sanctions are generally intended to be granted in conjunction with method-specific discovery violations, such as failing to attend depositions or respond to interrogatories, where conduct is directly tied to a discovery motion (Defendants’ Opposition, pg. 2-3). Additionally, Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s allegations reflect routine discovery disputes that could have been resolved through further meet-and-confer efforts or by filing a motion to compel, rather than escalating to sanctions.

Under the general sanctions provision in CCP §§ 2023.010 and 2023.030, the California Supreme Court in City of Los Angeles v. PricewaterhouseCoopers, LLP held that a court may impose sanctions for systemic or extensive discovery abuse, beyond method-specific violations, when there is a demonstrated pattern of discovery misuse. (15 Cal.5th 794.) In that case, the City engaged in repeated, egregious discovery misconduct, including improper privilege claims, defiance of court orders, and withholding critical documents over years of litigation, which warranted independent sanctions under the Civil Discovery Act. 

Here, Plaintiff’s alleged conduct does not constitute the extensive and egregious conduct displayed in the City of Los Angeles. In that case, the abuse involved ongoing and intentional violations of the discovery process and court orders, while here, Plaintiff’s claims are more general allegations. Without support from prior court orders or subsequent motions to compel on these issues, the Court finds that this does not rise to the level of egregious conduct required to warrant sanctions against former counsel or current counsel for Defendants.

Additionally, the Court finds that both former counsel and current counsel acted in good faith by relying on the provided verifications of discovery responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, at the time. Given the foregoing, the Court does not find it appropriate to impose sanctions on either former counsel or current counsel on this basis.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions.

           

Former Counsel Sanction Requests

Former counsel seeks sanctions against Plaintiff under California Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a), citing a lack of civility by Plaintiff’s counsel throughout this process. Specifically, former counsel points to the lack of response from Plaintiff’s counsel to their letter requesting withdrawal of the Motion, asserting that Plaintiff’s counsel ignored their plea for civility and professionalism (Novian Opposition pg. 8-9; see Phipps Dec. at ¶10-11, Exh. G). Additionally, during the meet-and-confer process, former counsel suggested a civil discussion to address competing interests, but Plaintiff’s counsel responded dismissively, stating that “the ship has sailed” on mediation (Id.)

Due to the necessity of opposing what they consider a meritless motion, former counsel incurred $8,533.50 in attorneys’ fees, plus an additional $1,980 for hearing preparation and attendance, totaling $10,513.50. These fees represent time spent by Novian & Novian LLP attorneys, with rates consistent with prevailing market rates in Los Angeles, which they argue are reasonable under the circumstances (Novian Opposition pg. 9) 

The Court finds that there is no explicit showing of malice or intent by Plaintiff’s counsel in filing the motion. Therefore, the Court DENIES former counsel’s request for monetary sanctions.

 

IV.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for Sanctions.

The Court DENIES former counsel’s request for sanctions.

Defendants are to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING IN PART PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO FORM INTERROGATORIES SET ONE

I.        INTRODUCTION

          On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff Chuan Tang (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action against Defendants FCA US LLC (“FCA”) and Puente Hills Chrysler Dodge Jeep Ram (“Puente Hills”), asserting violations of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act. The complaint arises from alleged defects in a 2022 Dodge RAM 3500, which Plaintiff claims persisted despite numerous repair attempts under warranty. Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that Defendants failed to adequately resolve issues with the vehicle, including metal-to-metal noises and transmission problems.

On September 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Compel Further Discovery Responses from FCA. On October 18, 2024, FCA filed its Opposition, contending that its responses were code-compliant and complete based on the current stage of discovery. Plaintiff replied to FCA’s Opposition on October 24, 2024, maintaining that FCA’s responses remain insufficient and do not meet discovery requirements.

 

II.      LEGAL STANDARD

         

Code Civ. Proc. §2030.300 governs motions to compel further responses to interrogatories. It provides as follows:

“(a) On receipt of a response to interrogatories, the propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that any of the following apply:

(1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete.

(2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate.

(3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.”

Motions must be made within 45 days of any response or supplemental response (Subdivision (c)) and must be accompanied by a declaration demonstrating the parties met and conferred to attempt informal resolution; they must also be accompanied by a separate statement or concise outline. (Subdivision (b).)

Subdivision (d) provides for the issuance of monetary sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further responses, unless the court finds the party who would be subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make sanctions unjust.

 

On the 45-day limit: Golf & Pro Shop, Inc. v. Superior Court (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 127

           

III.     ANALYSIS

Meet and Confer

A motion to compel further discovery responses must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration stating, “facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.310, subd. (b)(2); 2016.040.)¿

          Here, on April 29, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel served FCA with the first set of Form Interrogatories with responses due by May 31, 2024. Defendant responded to Plaintiff’s FIs on May 29, 2024, but without verifications. Verifications were subsequently served on July 31, 2024. (Aivazov Decl. ¶¶ 5-8)

On July 18, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendant a meet and confer letter, requesting supplemental responses within ten days. (Aivazov Decl. ¶ 9) On July 31, 2024, Defendant responded with its own meet and confer letter but did not provide the requested supplemental responses or any substantive explanation addressing Plaintiff’s concerns. (Aivazov Decl. ¶ 10)

On September 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent a follow-up email to Defendant regarding the outstanding FI responses, but no supplemental responses were received. (Aivazov Decl. ¶ 11)

The Court notes that, as of October 24, 2024, the Parties have resolved the dispute regarding Form Interrogatory 1.1. Defendant’s supplemental response is now complete and code-compliant, and Plaintiff has withdrawn this item from the motion. However, Form Interrogatories 12.1, 15.1, and 17.1 remain unresolved, necessitating the Court’s intervention.

Therefore, the Court notes the meet and confer requirement has been met. 

 

Legal Analysis

Plaintiff moves to compel FCA to further responses to Form Interrogatory No. 12.1, 15.1, and 17.1.

 

Interrogatory No. 12.1

Plaintiff argues for compelling further responses to 12.1 because FCA has the necessary information to identify individuals who performed repairs on Plaintiff’s vehicle. Specifically, Plaintiff argues that FCA has extensive training and certification of mechanics at authorized repair facilities and likely keeps records indicating technicians and their associated dealerships. 

Plaintiff also challenges FCA’s claim that “no principal agency: exists, highlighting that it is a franchisor-franchisee relationship.” Plaintiff argues this supports the inference that FCA would retain technician details, including those performing warranty repairs.

Plaintiff also asserts that a similar interrogatory was served on the repairing dealership (Puente Hills), but the responses were evasive. Plaintiff contends that FCA can provide this information now. This would avoid the need for an additional motion to compel against the Puente Hills. 

FCA argues that they have a lack of direct knowledge of the identities of the individuals who worked on Plaintiff’s vehicle beyond what is listed in repair orders. FCA also objects as vague, ambiguous, compound and overly broad and unintelligible specifically to the definition of “incident” arguing these phrases are unclear. FCA also denies having a principal-agent relationship with the independent dealership. FCA also argues that it has provided complete code-compliant responses and should not be compelled further.

In this case, the Court finds that FCA’s responses sufficiently comply with Code requirements. FCA has reasonably provided the available information from the repair orders, identifying individuals who worked on the vehicle who are current and/or former employees. Additionally, Defendant asserts that it has no knowledge beyond what is contained in the repair orders. Accordingly, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request to compel further responses to Interrogatory No. 12.1.

 

Interrogatory No. 15.1

Interrogatory No. 15.1 requests that FCA provide detailed information for each denial and affirmative defense, including specific facts supporting each position. FCA must also identify all individuals with knowledge of these facts, along with their contact information, and list all documents or tangible evidence that supports each denial or defense, specifying the custodian of each document. This interrogatory aims to ensure each defense is factually substantiated and transparent about evidence and knowledgeable persons.

Plaintiff argues that FCA’s response is inadequate and requests further responses because Defendant failed to provide specific facts supporting each affirmative defense. Plaintiff contends that FCA relies on general statements and legal conclusions. Additionally, Plaintiff challenges FCA’s reliance on privilege claims, arguing that the lack of detailed factual information prevents proper judicial evaluation of these claims.

FCA argues that no further responses are necessary, as they have provided complete responses based on the information available at this stage of discovery. FCA maintains that affirmative defenses were pled as a general procedural measure and will be sustained later through continued discovery. FCA also asserts that privilege objections are appropriate due to the ongoing nature of the investigation.

The Court finds that FCA’s responses are insufficient, as affirmative defenses require factual support. While FCA’s answer may comply with Code requirements as to the general denials, FCA should provide a Code-compliant response specifically addressing the affirmative defenses with factual support. Consequently, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Interrogatory No. 15.1.

 

Interrogatory 17.1

This interrogatory asks FCA to confirm if each response to Plaintiff’s requests for admission is an unqualified admission. If not, for each denial or qualification, FCA must (a) state the request number, (b) provide all supporting facts, (c) list names and contact information of individuals knowledgeable about those facts, and (d) identify documents and their custodians that support the response.

Plaintiff argues FCA’s responses are inadequate and improperly formatted as Defendant grouped responses together without separating each request or providing specific facts for each denial. Plaintiff contends that FCA’s explanations lack factual support.

FCA argues that further responses are unnecessary because they have provided Code-complaint answer. FCA objects that the interrogatory is overly broad, burdensome and seeks information equally available to Plaintiff while also asserting privileges over certain materials.

The Court finds that FCA’s responses are insufficient and should provide more detailed information for each denial. Defendants must separately address each request for admission. Accordingly, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to No. 17.1.

 

IV.     CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Accordingly, the Court GRANTS in part Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Form Interrogatory Set One - Interrogatory No. 15.1 and No. 17.1.

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Interrogatory No.12.1.

Plaintiff is to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANT DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF’S COMPLAINT and GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO STRIKE

I.         INTRODUCTION

Plaintiff Dennis Quoc-Binh Pham-Lu (“Plaintiff”) commenced this action against Defendant McWong Holding Company (“Defendant”) alleging multiple causes of action arising from alleged construction defects in the property located at 414 E. Lemon Avenue, Arcadia, CA (“the Property”). Plaintiff alleges four causes of action: (1) Breach of Implied Covenant to Perform Work in a Good and Competent Manner, (2) Breach of Contract, (3) Breach of Implied Duty to Perform with Reasonable Care, and (4) Violations of Standards Enumerated Under Civil Code § 896 et seq. (the California Right to Repair Act).

On June 21, 2024, Defendant filed a demurrer with a motion to strike the first three causes of action, arguing that Plaintiff failed to state sufficient facts and that these claims are barred by the California Right to Repair Act. Defendant also contends that Plaintiff cannot maintain a breach of contract claim because there is no direct contract between Plaintiff and Defendant.

On October 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Opposition to Defendant’s demurrer, arguing that the California Right to Repair Act does not preclude the claims and that sufficient facts have been pled to establish third-party beneficiary status for the breach of contract claim. On the same day, Plaintiff filed a notice of non-opposition to Defendant’s motion to strike.

 

Meet and Confer

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41, subdivision (a), the demurring party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading at least five days before the responsive pleading is due. This must occur in person, telephone or by video conference with the aim of resolving any objections raised in the demurrer. Additionally, the demurring party must file and serve a declaration detailing the meet and confer efforts. It should be noted, however, that the failure to meet and confer is not itself grounds for sustaining or overruling a demurrer or granting or denying a motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(4), 435.5, subd. (a)(4).)

Here, Defense counsel sent a meet-and-confer letter via email on May 1, 2024. (Demurrer, pg. 5). Emails were exchanged between counsel, and the parties held a telephone conference on June 11, 2024. (Demurrer, pg. 5). During these discussions, Plaintiff’s counsel declined to dismiss the first, second, and third causes of action, but both parties agreed to extend Defendant’s deadline to file its Demurrer and Motion to Strike to June 21, 2024. (Demurrer, pg. 5; Bubion Decl. ¶ 2). Accordingly, the Court finds that Defendant has satisfied the meet-and-confer requirement under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of a complaint. Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994. A plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for purposes of demurrer, no matter how “improbable” they are. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 (Del E. Webb).) Allegations need not be accepted as true if they are contradicted by judicially noticeable facts. (Cansino v. Bank of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1462, 1474.)

Pleadings are to be broadly construed (Code Civ. Proc., § 452) and demurrers are to be overruled where the facts are sufficient to state any cause of action. (Quelimane Co. v. Stewart Title Guaranty Co. (1998) 19 Cal.4th 26, 38.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) provides for a demurrer where a pleading is uncertain. Demurrers for uncertainty are disfavored and are only sustained where a pleading is so incomprehensible a defendant cannot reasonably respond. (A.J. Fistes v. GDL Best Contractors, Inc. (2019) 38 Cal.App.5th 677, 695; Khoury v. Maly’s of California (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

An action for injury to real property is subject to a three-year statute of limitations under Code of Civil Procedure section 338(b). In tort cases, the statute of limitations generally begins to run upon the occurrence of the last essential event, even if the plaintiff is unaware of the cause of action. (Wilshire Westwood Associates v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1993) 20 Cal.App.4th 732, 739). In the case of injury to property, the injury is considered to be to the property itself, not the property owner (Ibid.)

The discovery rule postpones the accrual of a cause of action until the plaintiff discovers, or has reason to discover, the cause of action (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc. (2005) 35 Cal.4th 797, 807). Under the rule, a plaintiff has reason to discover a cause of action when they have reason to suspect a factual basis for its elements. Suspicion of wrongdoing, coupled with knowledge of any remaining elements, triggers the statute of limitations period (Ibid.). The discovery rule only delays accrual until the plaintiff has, or should have, inquiry notice of the cause of action (Ibid.).

However, Plaintiff must specifically plead facts showing (1) the time and manner of discovery and (2) the inability to have made earlier discovery despite reasonable diligence. The burden is on the plaintiff to show diligence, and conclusory allegations will not withstand demurrer (Fox v. Ethicon Endo-Surgery, Inc., supra, 35 Cal.4th at p. 808).

 

III.      ANALYSIS

Defendant demurs to the first three causes of action in Plaintiff's Complaint (1) Breach of Implied Covenant to Perform Work in a Good and Competent Manner, (2) Breach of Contract, and (3) Breach of Implied Duty to Perform with Reasonable Care—arguing that Plaintiff has failed to state sufficient facts to support these claims. Defendant does not demur to the fourth cause of action for violations of Civil Code § 896 et seq. (the California Right to Repair Act).

 

First cause of action: Breach of Implied Covenant to Perform Work in a Good and Competent Manner.

The elements of breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing are: 

(1) existence of a contractual relationship; (2) implied duty; (3) breach; and (4) causation of damages. Smith v. San Francisco, 225 Cal.App.3d 38, 49 (1990).  “Every contract imposes on each party an implied duty of good faith and fair dealing.”  Egan v. Mutual of Omaha Ins. Co., 24 Cal.3d 809, 818 (1979).  Breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing is a form of breach of contract action. The same elements apply, but the breach is not of an express term of the contract but rather an implied term necessary to protect the reasonable expectations and purposes of the contracting parties as to the express terms of the contract.  Carma Developers, Inc. v. Marathon Development California, Inc., 2 Cal.4th 342, 373 (1992).

Here, Plaintiff alleges that Defendant, as the builder of the Property “failed to build the Property free from Defects” (Complaint ¶ 26). And that Plaintiff suffered harm because of Defendant’s conduct (Complaint P 27). Plaintiff further lists defects in Paragraph 17 of the complaint to include 23 defects. Further, Plaintiff alleges that “Plaintiff believes around May 2021, Defendant had a contract with the owner of the Property to build a brand-new home at the property specifically for sale to a third-party beneficiary such as Plaintiff.” (Complaint ¶ 13.) This allegation demonstrates that Plaintiff has alleged the existence of the contract between Defendant and the owner, and that he is the third-party beneficiary of that contract. 

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to provide sufficient details regarding the alleged contract between McWong and the prior owner. Specifically, Defendant points out that Paragraph 13 of the Complaint vaguely states that McWong had a contract with the property owner, but it lacks crucial details such as the date, scope, and terms of the contract. Defendant contends that Plaintiff is asserting contract-based claims without adequate knowledge of the contract’s existence or terms. 

The Court finds that the allegations in the Complaint are sufficient to state a cause of action for Breach of the Implied Covenant to Perform Work in a Good and Competent Manner. Plaintiff has adequately alleged the existence of a contractual relationship (albeit as a third-party beneficiary), an implied duty to perform competently, and the breach of that duty through the various construction defects listed in the Complaint, such as faulty installation of windows and water damage.

While Defendant argues that the first and third causes of action are duplicative, the Court finds that the claims are sufficiently distinct to survive demurrer. The first cause of action focuses on the breach of an implied contractual duty, while the third cause of action deals with negligence and the breach of a duty of care inherent in construction work.

The Court also finds that the lack of specific details about the contract does not warrant sustaining the demurrer. Plaintiff has alleged sufficient facts at this stage to indicate the existence of a contract. Plaintiff is not required to allege every specific term of the contract in the complaint. (“In an action based on a written contract, a plaintiff may plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal. 4th 189,198-199.)

The Court OVERRULES Defendant’s demurrer as to the first cause of action. 

Second cause of action: Breach of Contract.

The elements for a cause of action for breach of contract are: (1) existence of contract; (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance; (3) defendant’s breach (or anticipatory breach); and (4) resulting damage. (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. N.Y. Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) 

In order for a plaintiff to allege breach of contract as a third-party beneficiary, the plaintiff must plead a contract that was made expressly for his benefit. (See Martin v. Bridgeport Community Association, Inc. (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 1024, 1034.) A third party need not be named or identified individually to be an express beneficiary and a third party may enforce a contract where he shows that he is a member of a class of persons for whose benefit it was made. (See Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1023.)  

As discussed above, Plaintiff has alleged in the complaint that Defendant McWong entered into a contract with the developer-owner of the Property to construct a new home, which was to be sold to third-party beneficiaries like Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶ 13). Plaintiff further alleges that as a third-party beneficiary under the builder-developer/owner contract, he entered into an agreement with Defendant for the construction of the Property. (Complaint, ¶ 29). Additionally, Plaintiff claims that Defendant failed to build the Property free from defects, as required under its contract with the owner, and that this failure caused Plaintiff to suffer damages. (Complaint, ¶¶ 32-33).

Defendant argues that Plaintiff has failed to allege sufficient facts to support the existence of a contract between Defendant and the prior owner. Defendant asserts that Paragraph 13 of the Complaint merely provides a vague and conclusory statement regarding the existence of a contract, without including key details such as the date, scope, or specific terms of the agreement. Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s lack of specific knowledge about the contract’s terms should preclude him from asserting contract-based claims, as Plaintiff has not demonstrated a factual basis to establish the contract’s existence or its applicability to him.

As discussed above, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s allegations are sufficient to support the existence of a contract for the purposes of pleading. The Complaint alleges that Defendant entered into a contract with the developer-owner to construct a new home intended for sale to third-party beneficiaries like Plaintiff. Under Civil Code § 1559, a third-party beneficiary may enforce a contract if the contract was expressly made for their benefit. Plaintiff’s allegations that he was a member of the intended class of beneficiaries, and that Defendant breached its obligations by failing to address construction defects, are sufficient to survive demurrer at this stage.

While Defendant argues that Plaintiff lacks specific knowledge about the terms of the contract, Plaintiff is not required to plead every term with specificity at the pleading stage. A general allegation of the contract’s existence, especially when based on information and belief, is sufficient to support a cause of action for breach of contract, as further details can be explored during discovery. The allegations here reasonably support the inference that Plaintiff, as the purchaser of the Property, was an intended third-party beneficiary of the construction contract between Defendant and the developer-owner.

For the foregoing reasons, Defendant’s demurrer to the second cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Third cause of action: Breach of Implied Duty to Perform with Reasonable Care 

“Accompanying every contract is a common-law duty to perform with care, skill, reasonable expedience, and faithfulness the thing agreed to be done, and a negligent failure to observe any of these conditions is a tort, as well as a breach of the contract.” The rule which imposes this duty is of universal application as to all persons who by contract undertake professional or other business engagements requiring the exercise of care, skill and knowledge; the obligation is implied by law and need not be stated in the agreement [citation].’ [Citation.]” (Holguin v. Dish Network LLC (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1310, 1324.

Plaintiff alleges that the contract between Defendant McWong and the developer-owner for the construction of the Property implied that the work would be performed competently and with reasonable care. (Complaint, ¶ 35) Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant failed to use reasonable care in building the Property to be free from defects, and that Defendant breached this implied condition. (Complaint, ¶¶ 35, 39) Plaintiff further claims that he performed all conditions required under the contract or was excused from doing so, and that McWong’s failure to use reasonable care caused harm to Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶¶ 38-40).

Defendant argues that the First and Third Causes of Action are duplicative, as both allege that there is an implied covenant to provide materials and workmanship in a competent manner. Defendant contends that there is no substantive difference between the two causes of action, as both involve the same alleged failure to construct the Property free from defects.

Moreover, Defendant argues that Plaintiff’s allegations regarding the contract are vague and conclusory. Paragraph 13 of the Complaint only briefly mentions that Defendant had a contract with the owner to build the Property, without providing specifics such as the date, scope, or terms of the contract. Defendant asserts that Plaintiff lacks sufficient knowledge of the contract’s terms to support a breach of contract or implied duty claim.

Plaintiff contends that while both the First and Third Causes of Action relate to the Construction Contract, they are distinct in nature. The First Cause of Action focuses on a breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, which is part of every contract, while the Third Cause of Action specifically addresses Defendant’s negligent performance of its duties under the contract. Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s failure to perform with reasonable care, leading to various defects, is a separate claim that should not be dismissed as duplicative. Plaintiff also asserts that pursuing both claims will not prejudice Defendant or negatively impact judicial economy, as they involve overlapping witnesses and evidence, and both relate to the same contractual obligations.

The Court finds that Plaintiff has sufficiently alleged a cause of action for Breach of Implied Duty to Perform with Reasonable Care. While Defendant argues that the First and Third Causes of Action are duplicative, the Court concludes that they are distinct. The First Cause of Action concerns the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, while the Third Cause of Action pertains specifically to the negligent performance of Defendant’s duties under the contract.

The duty to perform with reasonable care is implied in all contracts for services, including construction contracts. (North American Chemical Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 764, 774). Plaintiff has alleged specific defects and damages resulting from Defendant’s failure to perform competently, which is sufficient to state a claim for breach of the implied duty of care. Additionally, Plaintiff is not required to plead every specific detail of the contract at this stage, and the allegations provide a reasonable inference that Plaintiff, as the third-party beneficiary, was harmed by Defendant’s failure to perform its obligations with reasonable care.

Accordingly, Defendant’s demurrer to the third cause of action is OVERRULED. 

 

Motion to strike

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false, or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)

Defendant moves to strike portions of Plaintiff’s Complaint. Defendant argues that Plaintiff failed to allege sufficient facts to support the claim for “any and all costs or fees recoverable by contract or statute” because no contract or statute is cited in the Complaint. As a result, Defendant seeks to strike Prayer for Relief No. 7 as to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action, found on Page 12 of the Complaint. Additionally, Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s request for “attorney’s fees where allowed” must also be stricken, as Plaintiff has not identified any contract or statute supporting the claim for attorney’s fees. Therefore, Defendant seeks to strike Prayer for Relief No. 2 as to all causes of action, also located on Page 12 of the Complaint.

In response, Plaintiff filed a Notice of Non-Opposition to the motion to strike, agreeing to remove the contested prayers for relief. Plaintiff specifically agrees to strike Prayer for Relief No. 7 as to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action, as well as Prayer for Relief No. 2 as to all causes of action. A copy of the Complaint, reflecting these agreed changes, was attached as Exhibit A to Plaintiff’s Notice of Non-Opposition.

Given Plaintiff’s non-opposition, the Court grants Defendant’s motion to strike the following: Prayer for Relief No. 7 as to the first, second, third, and fourth causes of action, and Prayer for Relief No. 2 as to all causes of action. Accordingly, these portions are ordered stricken from the Complaint.
