Case Number: 23CHCV00871    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 11-13-24

Case #23CHCV00871, Jennifer Bailey, et al. vs. Dometic Corporation, et al.

Trial Date: 6-2-25

 

MOTION TO BE ADMITTED PRO HAC VICE

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Dometic Corporation

RESPONDING PARTY: No response has been filed.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Defendant has requested that an attorney be admitted pro hac vice.

 

RULING: Motion is granted.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION AND ANALYSIS

Defendant Dometic Corporation’s California counsel, Erica Rutner, has filed an application for pro hac vice status for Florida attorney Maia Dombey. The declaration of this attorney filed in support of her application for pro hac vice status complies with the requirements of Cal. Rules of Court Rule 9.40.  

 

Rule 9.40 states that an applicant’s application “must” state (1) the applicant’s residence and office address; (2) the courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission; (3) that the applicant is a licensee in good standing in those courts; (4) that the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (5) the title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and (6) the name, address, and telephone number of the active licensees of the State Bar of California who are the attorneys of record for the case. (See Cal. Rules of Court Rule 9.40(d).)

 

Ms. Dombey’s declaration contains all of the information required by Rule 9.40(d). The Court grants her application.

 

The attorney’s application to be admitted pro hac vice is granted.

 

Moving party to give notice.


Case Number: 23CHCV02890    Hearing Date: November 13, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 11-13-24

Case # 23CHCV02890, Christopher Anderson vs. Ashot Mkhitaryan

Trial Date: N/A

 

MOTION TO STRIKE

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant Ashot Mkhitaryan

RESPONDING PARTY: No response has been filed

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion to Strike

·         Claim for Punitive Damages [Comp., ¶¶ 11 and 12; and Paragraph 8 of the prayer]

 

RULING: Motion is granted with leave to amend.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff Christopher Anderson (Plaintiff) filed this case on September 27, 2023. Plaintiff is a FedEx delivery driver and has alleged that a dog attacked him on premises owned by Defendant Ashot Mkhitaryan (Defendant). Plaintiff alleges that he suffered injuries and bites to his right leg and continues to suffer from residual pain and has been through depression, anxiety, fear, and embarrassment from the injuries.

 

Plaintiff further alleges that Defendant knowingly permitted a dangerous dog to be present on the premises and that Defendant was aware of the dog’s dangerous and aggressive tendencies. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant acting willfully and maliciously by permitting the dog on the premises and that Defendant’s conduct was willful and malicious by allowed the dog to remain on the premises after the incident occurred and after receiving notice of its dangerous and aggressive tendencies. (Comp., ¶¶ 11-12.) Because of Defendant’s alleged willful and malicious conduct, Plaintiff has requested punitive damages.

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges causes of action related to negligence and strict liability.

 

Defendant’s motion to strike portions of Plaintiff’s complaint was deemed filed after the Court granted Defendant’s motion to set aside default. Defendant has moved to strike Plaintiffs’ request for punitive damages. No opposition has been filed as of November 6, 2024.

 

ANALYSIS

This Court may strike from the complaint any irrelevant, false, or improper matter. Under CCP § 435, “[a]ny party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” Under CCP § 436(a), “[t]he court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper . . . [s]trike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.”

 

            Punitive Damages

Punitive damages are governed by Civ. Code § 3294: “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.” (Civ. Code § 3294(a).)

 

To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in Civ. Code § 3294. (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) In this case, Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant acted with malice by knowingly having a dog with dangerous and aggressive tendencies on his premises. “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Id. at 725.)

 

Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1004 explained that “punitive damages sometimes may be assessed in unintentional tort actions.” Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 894-895 noted that “something more than the mere commission of a tort is always required for punitive damages. There must be circumstances of aggravation or outrage, such as spite or malice, or a fraudulent or evil motive on the part of the defendant, or such a conscious and deliberate disregard of the interests of others that his conduct may be called willful or wanton.” Indeed, “punitive damages are proper only when the tortious conduct rises to levels of extreme indifference to the plaintiff’s rights, a level which decent citizens should not have to tolerate.” (Lackner v. North (2006) 135 Cal.App.4th 1188, 1210 (internal quotation omitted).) 

 

The “conclusory characterization of defendant’s conduct as intentional, willful and fraudulent is a patently insufficient statement of oppression, fraud or malice…within the meaning of section 3294.” (Brousseau v. Jarrett (1977) 73 Cal.App.3d 864, 872.) 

 

“It is not sufficient to allege merely that defendant ‘acted with oppression, fraud or malice.’ Rather, plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that defendant’s conduct was oppressive, fraudulent or malicious (e.g., that defendant acted with the intent to inflict great bodily harm on plaintiff or to destroy plaintiff's property or reputation).” (Croskey, et al., Cal. Prac. Guide: Insurance Litigation Ch. 13-C (Thomson Reuters, 2016) ¶ 13:197.2; and see, Anschutz Entertainment Group, Inc. v. Snepp (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 598, 643 [allegations that defendant’s conduct was “intentional, and done willfully, maliciously, with ill will towards Plaintiffs, and with conscious disregard for Plaintiff's rights” did not satisfy specific pleading requirements].) 

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges that Defendant’s “conduct was willful and malicious by knowingly permitting the DOG on the PREMISES initially and/or in permitting the DOG to remain on the PREMISES despite its dangerous and aggressive tendencies.” (Comp., ¶ 11.) Plaintiff’s complaint also alleges that Defendant’s “conduct was willful and malicious by allowing the DOG to remain on the PREMISES after the incident occurred and after receiving notice of its dangerous and aggressive tendencies.”

 

As defined above, malice is “conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” Plaintiff has not alleged that Defendant acted intentionally to cause injury to Plaintiff. Therefore, Plaintiff’s allegations must demonstrate that Defendant acted with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others.

 

Malice must be specifically pleaded. The allegations cannot be conclusory. Here, Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant knew of the dog’s dangerous and aggressive tendencies and that this constituted willful and malicious conduct. Plaintiff also alleges that Defendant’s conduct was malicious by allowing the dog to remain on the premises after the incident, but this may not be actionable since there is no indication from Plaintiff’s complaint that Plaintiff had any further contact with the dog after the subject incident.

 

Taken as a whole, the allegations in Plaintiff’s complaint do not appear to rise above the level of ordinary negligence to malicious conduct such that Plaintiff would be able to recover punitive damages. Plaintiff has not alleged that Defendant acted with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights of safety of others. As for Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendant knew of the dog’s dangerous and aggressive tendencies, Plaintiff has not alleged any facts to support this assertion. Plaintiff’s allegations are merely conclusory.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to demonstrate that Defendant acted with malice. The requests for punitive damages is stricken from the complaint. Plaintiff is given leave to amend.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s motion to strike is granted.

 

Plaintiff is given 30 days leave to amend their complaint.
