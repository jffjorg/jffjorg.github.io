Case Number: 22TRCV01573    Hearing Date: December 10, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

Plaintiff,

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

DEVANAND SHARMA,

Hearing Date:                          December 10, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Devanand Sharma aka Frank Martini

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Cynthia A. Blanks

Motion for Summary Judgment

            The Court considered the moving and opposition papers and defendant’s motion to amend the date of the summary judgment motion.

RULING

The motion for summary judgment is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

The motion to amend the date of the summary judgment motion is advanced and deemed MOOT and taken off calendar for February 13, 2025.

BACKGROUND

On December 22, 2022, plaintiff Cynthia A. Blanks, as trustee of The Cynthia A. Blanks Revocable Trust #2 and as trustee of The Seanjay Ramanand Sharma Revocable Trust filed a complaint against Devanand Sharma aka Frank Martini (and nominal defendants 4200 West Century Associates, 4229 West 101st Street Associates, and 4223 Associates) for (1) declaratory relief and (2) accounting.

On March 1, 2023, defendant (self-represented) filed a cross-complaint against Cynthia A. Blanks, Seanjay R. Sharma, Kamelia Yagadari, and Satanand Sharma for (1) breach of fiduciary duty, (2) breach of implied covenant of good faith and dealing, (3) fraud, (4) conversion, (5) constructive trust, (6) accounting, (7) quiet title action, (8) methodone clinics purchased with defendant’s property, and (9) declaratory relief.

On September 19, 2023, cross-complaint (represented by counsel Walton Law Group) filed a FACC for (1) declaratory relief and (2) accounting.

On March 28, 2024, the Court granted defendant’s motions to compel further responses against the nominal defendants.

On April 15, 2024, the Court denied without prejudice plaintiff’s motion for an order to deposit all future rent payments to nominal defendants.

On June 21, 2024, defendant filed a substitution of attorney replacing Walton Law Group with himself.

On November 7, 2024, defendant filed a motion for summary judgment, setting the motion for hearing on December 10, 2024.

On November 21, 2024, defendant filed a motion to amend the date of the summary judgment motion, re-setting if for hearing on February 13, 2025.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.”  Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal. 4th 826, 843.  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal. App. 4th 1110, 1119.

“On a motion for summary judgment, the initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”  Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal. App. 4th 1510, 1519.  A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”  CCP § 437c(p)(2).  “Once the defendant . . . has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” CCP § 437c(p)(2). 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inferences that may be drawn from that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467; CCP §437c(c).

Importantly, the court does not decide disputed issues of fact in a summary judgment motion.  As an example, if one person said the light was green and 100 other people said it was red, the court still could not grant the motion, since there is a factual dispute shown by the evidence.  The court is NOT empowered in a summary judgment motion to decide which of two versions of the facts the court finds most credible.  The court must deny a motion if there is a material factual dispute, even if the court believes one version is more credible than the other.

Finally, Code of Civil Procedure § 437c(a)(2) states that,

“[n]otice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing.”

 

DISCUSSION

Defendant Devanand Sharma (self-represented) requests summary judgment “pursuant to Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 56.”  (This is not a federal court and it does not follow the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.)  The motion was filed on November 7, 2024, and noticed for hearing on December 10, 2024.

In the opposition, plaintiff contends that the motion failed to provide at least 75 days’ notice before the hearing as required by CCP § 437c(a)(2).  Plaintiff requests that the Court take the motion off calendar or set a new hearing date at least 75 days after the date of the Court’s order.

The Court notes that defendant filed a “motion” on November 21, 2024 to “amend the date of the summary judgment motion,” which is set for hearing on February 13, 2025.  Defendant acknowledges that he failed provide the required notice.

The Court finds that the motion has failed to provide the statutory time period for notice.  CCP §437c(a)(2) states that,

“[n]otice of the motion and supporting papers shall be served on all other parties to the action at least 75 days before the time appointed for hearing.”

If the notice is served by mail to an address within the State of California, the 75-day period is increased by five days.  Defendant provided only thirty-three days’ notice.  The Court lacks power to change the notice and filing periods of section 437c.  See McMahon v. Superior Court (2003) 106 Cal. App. 4th 112, 117 (“In sum, we hold that, in light of the express statutory language, trial courts do not have authority to shorten the minimum notice period for summary judgment hearings.”).

The Court also finds that the motion is improperly based on a federal rule, as opposed to the applicable California statute for motions for summary judgment.

The motion is thus DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.  Moving party may start over and comply with the statutory requirements of Civil Code § 437c.  All of the statutory requirements.  See California Rule of Court 3.1350.

ORDER

The motion for summary judgment is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

The motion to amend the date of the summary judgment motion is advanced and deemed MOOT and taken off calendar for February 13, 2025.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Special Motion to Strike Complaint as Strategic Lawsuit Against Public Participation

 

            The Court considered the moving papers and plaintiff’s request for dismissal submitted on October 16, 2024.  The Court continued the hearing from October 24, 2024 to December 10, 2024 to allow supplemental briefing.  No supplemental briefing was filed.

RULING

The motion is MOOT. 

The Court awards defendant her attorney’s fees and costs against plaintiff in the amount of $8,810, pursuant to CCP §425.16(c).

BACKGROUND

            On June 24, 2024, plaintiff Albert Morales (self-represented) filed a complaint against Ramesh Johnston for intentional tort.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Under CCP §425.16(b), “(1) A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.  (2) In making its determination, the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.”

“The anti-SLAPP procedures are designed to shield a defendant’s constitutionally protected conduct from the undue burden of frivolous litigation.”  Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal. 5th 376, 393.  “The anti-SLAPP statute does not insulate defendants from any liability for claims arising from the protected rights of petition or speech.  It only provides a procedure for weeding out, at an early stage, meritless claims arising from protected activity.”  Id. at 384.  “Resolution of an anti-SLAPP motion involves two steps.  First, the defendant must establish that the challenged claim arises from activity protected by section 425.16.  If the defendant makes the required showing, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate merit of the claim by establishing a probability of success.”  Id. (citation omitted).  The California Supreme Court has “described this second step as a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure.’  The court does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims.  Its inquiry is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment.  It accepts the plaintiff’s evidence as true, and evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law.  ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’”  Id. at 384-85 (citations omitted). 

“At the first step, the moving defendant bears the burden of identifying all allegations of protected activity, and the claims for relief supported by them.  When relief is sought based on allegations of both protected and unprotected activity, the unprotected activity is disregarded at this stage.  If the court determines that relief is sought based on allegations arising from activity protected by the statute, the second step is reached.  There, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate that each challenged claim based on protected activity is legally sufficient and factually substantiated.”  Id. at 396.

DISCUSSION

            Pursuant to §425.16, defendant Ramesh Johnston requests that the Court strike the complaint.

In ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, the trial court engages in a two-step process.  “First, the court decides whether the defendant has made a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one arising from protected activity.  The moving defendant’s burden is to demonstrate that the act or acts of which the plaintiff complains were taken ‘in furtherance of the [defendant]’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue,’ as defined in the statute.  If the court finds such a showing has been made, it then determines whether the plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim. . . . Only when a defendant shows that a cause of action is based on protected conduct and the plaintiff fails to show a likelihood of success on that claim is it subject to dismissal.”  Wong v. Jing (2010) 189 Cal. App. 4th 1354, 1360 (citation omitted).

The complaint alleges that defendant filed a false sexual assault Title IX complaint to her employers, Hawthorne School District, to get plaintiff fired and portray him as a sexual predator at work in 2022.  In the Title IX complaint, defendant stated that plaintiff sexually assaulted her to cover up the affair between defendant and plaintiff.  Plaintiff’s husband found out about the affair that had been going on for months after he found texts in his wife’s phone.  Both Tad Johnston and defendant threatened plaintiff via text stating that he needed to leave the school district or there would be consequences.  When plaintiff ignored the threats, defendant filed a false Title IX complaint in retaliation.  Plaintiff was immediately put on administrative leave and that is when defendant told co-workers about her false allegations to influence the co-workers’ testimonies when it would be their turn to be interviewed during the investigation.  Defendant’s initial allegations were dropped when the investigators cited lack of evidence and cooperation from plaintiff to provide proof of her allegations.

The complaint further alleges that in January 2024, defendant was called in for a deposition by a third party hired by the district to continue the investigation and under oath stated that she was raped by plaintiff multiple times to get defendant fired.  After multiple interviews of witnesses to the consensual relationship, and looking at concrete evidence between defendant and plaintiff, in January 2024, it was determined that defendant’s allegations were unsubstantiated.  For two years, plaintiff suffered emotional damage and depression while defendant spread lies to co-workers that plaintiff was a sexual predator who sexually assaulted her.  Plaintiff resigned from the workplace because defendant had ruined his reputation at work portraying him as a sexual predator. 

Step One:  Conduct in furtherance of right of petition or free speech

            CCP §425.16(e) states, in relevant part:  “As used in this section, ‘act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue’ includes:  (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.”

“The phrase ‘arising from’ . . . has been interpreted to mean that ‘the act underlying the plaintiff’s cause’ or ‘the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff’s cause of action’ must have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.’”  Schwarzburd v. Kensington Police Protection & Community Services Dist. Bd.  (2014) 225 Cal. App. 4th 1345, 1350 (citation omitted).  “What nexus must a defendant show between a challenged claim and the defendant’s protected activity for the claim to be struck?  . . . [A] claim is not subject to a motion to strike simply because it contests an action or decision that was arrived at following speech or petitioning activity, or that was thereafter communicated by means of speech or petitioning activity.  Rather, a claim may be struck only if the speech or petitioning activity itself is the wrong complained of, and not just evidence of liability or a step leading to some different act for which liability is asserted.”  Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal. 5th 1057, 1060 (considering the relationship a defendant must show between a plaintiff’s claim and the sorts of speech on public matters the Legislature intended to protect).  “’[T]he mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.’  ( . . . see City of Cotati [v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal. 4th 69], at p. 78 [suit may be in “response to, or in retaliation for,’ protected activity without necessarily arising from it].)  Instead, the focus is on determining what ‘the defendant’s activity [is] that gives rise to his or her asserted liability—and whether that activity constitutes protected speech or petitioning.’  ‘The only means specified in section 425.16 by which a moving defendant can satisfy the [arising from] requirement is to demonstrate that the defendant’s conduct by which plaintiff claims to have been injured falls within one of the four categories described in subdivision (e) . . . .’  In short, in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.”  Id.

            Defendant argues that her filing of a Title IX complaint arises from protected activity under CCP §425.16(e)(1) because it is an oral and written statement made before a “judicial proceeding.”  Defendant also asserts that communications and petitioning activity to educational institutions seeking to prompt disciplinary action, including termination, against an employee are part of an official proceeding authorized by law.  See, e.g., Lee v. Fick (2005) 135 Cal. App. 4th 89, 96 (letter written by a parent to school officials that contained allegedly libelous statements about coach was written to prompt official action and was privileged).

            The Court finds that as argued by defendant, the filing of a Title IX complaint is protected activity within the meaning of section 425.16.  See, e.g., Laker v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2019) 32 Cal. App. 5th 745, 784-65 (internal investigations have been found to be an official proceeding authorized by law and internal investigations by schools into claims of discrimination qualify as official proceedings authorized by law that receive the protections of the anti-SLAPP statute).  Accordingly, defendant has met her burden under the first prong that plaintiff’s cause of action for “intentional tort” falls under CCP §425.16(e).

 

            Step Two:  Plaintiff’s Responding Burden

            “To establish a probability of prevailing, the plaintiff must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.  For purposes of this inquiry, the trial court considers the pleadings and evidentiary submissions of both the plaintiff and the defendant (§ 425.16, subd. (b)(2)); though the court does not weigh the credibility or comparative probative strength of competing evidence, it should grant the motion if, as a matter of law, the defendant's evidence supporting the motion defeats the plaintiff's attempt to establish evidentiary support for the claim.”  Hawran v. Hixson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 256, 273-74.  However, the court must accept as true the evidence favorable to plaintiff.  Soukup v. Law Offices of Herbert Hafif (2006) 39 Cal.4th 260, 291.  Plaintiff need only establish that the claim has minimal merit.  Id.  

            Defendant argues that plaintiff cannot prevail because the cause of action for defamation is barred by the litigation privilege under Civil Code §47, which states in part, “A privileged publication . . . is one made: . . . (b) In any . . . (2) judicial proceeding, . . . or (4) in the initiation or course of any other proceeding authorized by law and reviewable pursuant to Chapter 2 . . . .”  Further, plaintiff cites to Civil Code §47.1, which states that a communication regarding an “incident of sexual assault, harassment or discrimination is privileged under section 47.” 

            On October 16, 2024, plaintiff filed a request for dismissal before the hearing on the motion.

            As argued by defendant, plaintiff cannot show that he has a probability of prevailing as the litigation privilege under Civil Code §47 bars the cause of action as it is based on the filing of the Title IX complaint.  “[C]ommunications made in connection with litigation do not necessarily fall outside the privilege simply because they are, or are alleged to be, fraudulent, perjurious, unethical, or even illegal.”  Kashian v. Harriman (2002) 98 Cal. App. 4th 892, 920.

            As noted, plaintiff submitted a request for dismissal and, thus, the motion is MOOT.

 

            Attorney’s Fees

Under CCP §425.16(c), “In any action subject to subdivision (b), a prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover his or her attorney’s fees and costs.”

Although plaintiff filed a voluntary dismissal prior to the hearing, the Court has limited jurisdiction to decide whether to award to award attorney fees and costs to defendant.  See Law Offices of Andrew L. Ellis v. Yang (2009) 178 Cal. App. 4th 869, 878-79.  “In short, the dismissal is given effect, but the defendant does not lose its anti-SLAPP right to recover fees if its motion would have been successful.”  Dickinson v. Cosby (2017) 17 Cal. App. 5th 655, 679 (citing Yang, supra). 

“[T]he trial court’s adjudication of the merits of a defendant’s motion to strike is an essential predicate to ruling on the defendant’s request for an award of fees and costs.  An award of these expenses under section 425.16 is only justified when a defendant demonstrates that plaintiff’s action falls within the provisions of subdivision (b) and the plaintiff is unable to establish a reasonable probability of success.”  Moore v. Liu (1999) 69 Cal. App. 4th 745, 752-53.

As stated above, the motion would have been successful as defendant has demonstrated that plaintiff’s action falls within the provisions of CCP §425.16 and that plaintiff is unable to establish a reasonable probability of success. 

            On October 24, 2024, the Court called the matter for hearing.  There was no appearance by plaintiff.  The Court noted that the clerk had taken the matter off calendar when the dismissal was entered, but that defendant contends that she is entitled to recover her attorney’s fees for the motion notwithstanding the dismissal of the complaint by plaintiff prior the hearing.  The Court ordered that the motion be restored to the Court’s calendar for hearing on December 10, 2024.  The Court also granted the parties the right to file a supplemental brief addressing the motion, including the merits of the motion, if deemed necessary, and a discussion on the issue of whether the Court has continued jurisdiction to award attorney’s fees on the motion notwithstanding plaintiff’s dismissal, citing to Law Offices of Andrew L. Ellis v. Yang, supra and Dickinson v. Cosby, supra. 

            Neither party filed supplemental briefing.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that it has limited jurisdiction to determine whether defendant is entitled to her attorney’s fees and costs.  Defendant demonstrated that plaintiff’s action falls within the provisions of CCP §425.16 and that plaintiff is unable to establish a reasonable probability of success.  Thus, the Court finds that defendant is the prevailing party and entitled to her attorney’s fees.

Defendant requests $9,720 ($350/hr. x 27.6 hrs.) for attorney’s fees and costs.  The requested amount includes anticipated hours to file a reply and appear at the hearing.  The Court finds that $8,810 ($350/hr. x 25 hrs.; $60 filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against plaintiff.

 

ORDER

            The motion is MOOT, except for the request for attorneys fees pursuant to statute.

The Court awards defendant her attorney’s fees and costs against plaintiff in the amount of $8,810, pursuant to CCP §425.16(c).

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of the ruling.
