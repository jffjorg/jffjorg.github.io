DEPARTMENT 32 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV09649    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 32

 

MARCUS LAUN,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

JAMES PAKULIS, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  20STCV09649

  Hearing Date:  May 6, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant james pakulis’ motion to order clerk to file proposed settled statement

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On March 9, 2020, Plaintiff Marcus Laun filed the present action against James Pakulis, Arnie Johansson, and TransCanna Holdings, Inc. The complaint asserts causes of action for breach of contract, breach of fiduciary duty, fraud, constructive trust, declaratory relief, interference with contract, and unfair competition.

            The complaint alleges that Plaintiff and Defendant Pakulis formed a partnership called MarJa for the distribution of marijuana products. Plaintiff and Pakulis purchased Geopulse Exploration, Inc. through which to operate their cannabis production and distribution business. Thereafter, Pakulis allegedly betrayed Plaintiff by stealing Plaintiff’s proprietary information for use in a competing venture. Pakulis allegedly formed TransCanna Holdings with Defendant Johansson. 

            On March 23, 2021, TransCanna filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Geopulse, and MarJa for declaratory relief. On March 29, 2021, Pakulis filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff, Geopulse, and Growth Circle, Inc., asserting fraud, unjust enrichment, and declaratory relief.    

In a statement of decision following a bench trial, the Court made the following findings. Plaintiff and Pakulis formed a partnership. Pakulis breached the partnership agreement and his fiduciary duties to Plaintiff, and committed constructive fraud. Plaintiff and Pakulis were entitled to share equally in TransCanna, but Plaintiff was not entitled to any equitable remedy against TransCanna. The Court awarded Plaintiff $1.23 million against Pakulis. Pakulis failed to establish fraud but was entitled to restitution from Geopulse and Growth in the amount of $25,000. Judgment was entered accordingly on September 25, 2023. Pakulis has since appealed the judgment.

            On March 30, April 3, and April 5, 2024, Defendant Pakulis attempted to file his Proposed Settled Statement, which was rejected each time. On April 3 and April 15, 2024, Defendant filed the instant two motions for an order directing the clerk to file his Proposed Settled Statement nunc pro tunc.   

DISCUSSION

            “If a document is presented to the clerk's office for filing in a form that complies with the rules of court, the clerk's office has a ministerial duty to file it.” (Voit v. Superior Court (2011) 201 Cal.App.4th 1285, 1287, emphasis added.) Here, the clerk’s notices of rejection provided clear and specific reasons why Defendant’s filings were not compliant. (See Shatford Decl., Ex. A-D.) Defendant’s continued submission of defective documents does not require the clerk to file them, nor does Voit support such a requirement. It is unclear why Defendant chose to file this motion rather than simply cure the defects.

            The most recent notice of rejection states that the filing was missing a proof of service. Defendant must properly serve the documents and attach a corresponding proof of service before the documents can be filed. The clerk is not otherwise required to file a defective document.

            Proof of service is obviously important.  Within in 10 days after filing of the proposed statement, the respondent has may serve and file proposed amendments to the statement.  Respondent cannot serve proposed amendments unless it is served with the initial statement. Within 10 days after the respondent has filed proposed amendments, any party may request a hearing.  (See. California Rule of Court, Rule 8.837.)

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Pakulis’ motions to order the clerk to file Proposed Settled Statement are DENIED.   Defendant is ordered to properly serve the proposed settled statement.

  Case No.:  22STCV34203

  Hearing Date:  May 3, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES (CRS# 8631, 0353, 7732)

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 24, 2022, Plaintiff Jane Doe filed this action against Defendants Casa de la Familia (CDLF), Nogales Psychological Counseling, Inc. (Nogales), and Oscar Aquino (Aquino). The complaint asserts causes of action for: (1) sexual harassment in violation of Civil Code section 51.9; (2) sexual battery in violation of Civil Code section 1708.5; and (3) negligence. Nogales has been dismissed from the lawsuit.

            According to the complaint, Aquino was employed by CDLF as a marriage and family therapist from April 2019 to May 2020. (Compl. ¶ 15.) Plaintiff alleges that on October 5, 2019, she attended a therapy appointment with Aquino wherein Aquino sexually assaulted her. (Id., ¶¶ 16-17.)

            On April 5 and 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant three motions to compel further responses from Defendant Aquino as to Requests for Admission, Requests for Production, and Special Interrogatories. All three motions are advanced and heard on this date (May 3, 2024). 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

Upon receiving responses to its discovery requests, the propounding party may move for an order compelling further responses if the responses are incomplete or evasive, or objections are without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(a), 2031.310(a), 2033.290(a).)

MEET AND CONFER

A motion to compel further must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration demonstrating an attempt to resolve the matter informally. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.300(b)(1), 2031.310(b), 2033.290(b).) The Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the meet and confer requirement. (See Seber Decl.)

DISCUSSION

I. Requests for Admission and Special Interrogatories

The RFAs and SROGs at issue seek to establish that Defendant Aquino exhibited inappropriate behavior or sexually harassed/assaulted certain patients and that Defendant was reported or disciplined for inappropriate behavior. Defendant objected to the RFAs and SROGs by invoking his Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination and the patients’ right of privacy.  

a. Fifth Amendment

                        1. General Principles

“It is a bedrock principle of American (and California) law, embedded in various state and federal constitutional and statutory provisions, that witnesses may not be compelled to incriminate themselves.” (People v. Trujeque (2015) 61 Cal.4th 227, 267.) “To sustain the privilege, it need only be evident from the implications of the question, in the setting in which it is asked, that a responsive answer to the question or an explanation of why it cannot be answered might be dangerous because injurious disclosure could result.” (Ibid.) “In that regard, a witness's answers need not in themselves support a conviction under a criminal statute, but may ‘furnish a link in the chain of evidence’ needed to prosecute the witness for a crime.” (Ibid.) “Ultimately, a trial court may reject an assertion of the privilege only when it appears to the court ‘perfectly clear, from a careful consideration of all the circumstances in the case, that the witness is mistaken, and that the answers cannot possibly have such tendency’ to incriminate.” (Ibid., emphasis in original.)

            A party’s say-so does not establish the privilege; it is up to the trial court to determine whether the privilege applies. (Trujeque, supra, 61 Cal.4th at pp. 267-68.) “[A] trial court must make ‘a particularized inquiry, deciding, in connection with each specific area that the questioning party wishes to explore, whether or not the privilege is well-founded.’ Although the witness may have a valid claim to the privilege with respect to some questions, the scope of that privilege may not extend to all relevant questions. The witness may be totally excused only if the court finds that he could ‘legitimately refuse to answer essentially all relevant questions.’” (Id. at p. 268.) “This has long been the rule in California in both criminal and civil proceedings.” (Ibid.) 

                        2. Application

            Here, Defendant’s answers regarding whether he sexually harassed or assaulted other individuals, or was disciplined for such, fall within the “chain of evidence” that could ultimately result in criminal liability. Plaintiff argues that the actions implicated by the RFAs and SROGs, while inappropriate, do not amount to criminal behavior. However, some of the requests clearly describe sexual battery, which is a crime. (See, e.g., RFA Nos. 2, 3, 21; SROG Nos. 113, 114.) Moreover, the law is clear that “a witness's answers need not in themselves support a conviction under a criminal statute.” (Trujeque, supra, 61 Cal.4th at p. 267.) “The test is whether the statement might tend to incriminate, not whether it might tend to lead to an actual prosecution or, stated slightly differently, whether the statement could, not would, be used against the witness.” (People v. Seijas (2005) 36 Cal.4th 291, 305, emphasis in original.)

            Given the nature of the case and the information sought, “inappropriate behavior” in this context clearly involves sexual assault or sexual battery, which are crimes. Even if some of the questions do not directly implicate criminal conduct, Defendant’s answers regarding whether he was reported or disciplined for sexual misconduct, or made sexually charged comments, may “tend to incriminate” him as part of the “chain of evidence.” Therefore, it does not “clearly appear” to the Court that Defendant’s answers “cannot possibly” have a tendency to incriminate Defendant. (See Seijas, supra, 36 Cal.4th at p. 305.) The Court finds that Defendant properly asserted his privilege against self-incrimination. 

            In reply, Plaintiff cites to Maldonado v. Superior Court (2012) 53 Cal.4th 1112, 1127-28 for the proposition that a Fifth Amendment violation does not occur until incriminating information is used against a defendant in a criminal case. However, Maldonado does not preclude a trial court in a civil case from restricting discovery based on concerns over self-incrimination. A party may invoke the Fifth Amendment to refuse to answer questions in a noncriminal proceeding so long as the privilege is “specifically claimed on a particular question and the matter [is] submitted to the court for its determination as to the validity of the claim.” (Warford v. Medeiros (1984) 160 Cal.App.3d 1035, 1045.) A “trial court has broad discretion in ruling on discovery matters.” (Perkins v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 761, 765.) For the reasons articulated above, the Court finds that Defendant properly declined to answer the subject RFAs and SROGs based on the Fifth Amendment.

b. Privacy

            The information sought is not otherwise precluded from disclosure by privacy. Defendant cites no authority supporting the proposition that information about his inappropriate conduct constitutes “protected medical information” under HIPAA or CMIA. Nor does the information implicate the psychotherapist-patient privilege, as the alleged sexually harassing comments are not “confidential communications.” (See Evid. Code, § 1014.) The information is directly relevant, and Plaintiff’s need for the information would outweigh the privacy interests. (See Williams v. Sup. Ct. (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) Nonetheless, as discussed above, Defendant’s refusal to answer is justified by the privilege against self-incrimination.  

II. Requests for Production

“A representation of inability to comply . . . shall affirm that a diligent search and a reasonable inquiry has been made in an effort to comply with that demand. This statement shall also specify whether the inability to comply is because the particular item or category has never existed, has been destroyed, has been lost, misplaced, or stolen, or has never been, or is no longer, in the possession, custody, or control of the responding party. The statement shall set forth the name and address of any natural person or organization known or believed by that party to have possession, custody, or control of that item or category of item.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)

Defendant responded to each of the subject RFPs by repeating the following statement: “Responding is not in possession, custody, or control of any DOCUMENTS responsive to this request.” This response is not compliant because it merely asserts that Defendant is not in possession of responsive documents without providing any of the information required by Section 2031.230. While Defendant cannot be compelled to produce documents that he does not have, his written responses must still comply with the Code. Defendant’s subsequent explanations during meet and confer and in his opposition do not negate the fact that his responses are defective as currently written. Therefore, amendment is necessary.

CONCLUSION

            Plaintiff’s motions to compel further responses to Requests for Admission and Special Interrogatories are DENIED. Plaintiff’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED. Defendant shall produce further responses to the subject RFPs within 15 days of this order.

Case Number: 23STCV01067    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 32

 

MARIO ROSALES,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

RICHARD ROE, M.D., et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  23STCV01067

  Hearing Date:  May 6, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendants’ motion for summary judgment

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On January 17, 2023, Plaintiff Mario Rosales filed this action for medical negligence against Defendants Richard Roe, M.D. (Dr. Roe) and Retina-Vitreous Associates Medical Group (RVAMG). Plaintiff alleges that Dr. Roe, who works at RVAMG, negligently diagnosed him and failed to render proper treatment.

            On February 16, 2024, Defendants filed the instant motion for summary judgment. Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

DISCUSSION

            “The elements of a cause of action for medical malpractice are: (1) a duty to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of the profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of the duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the injury; and (4) resulting loss or damage.” (Lattimore v. Dickey (2015) 239 Cal.App.4th 959, 968.) “The first element, standard of care, is the key issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by expert testimony, unless the circumstances are such that the required conduct is within the layperson's common knowledge.” (Ibid.)

            Here, Defendants’ uncontested expert testimony establishes that Dr. Roe’s actions complied with the applicable standard of care. (See Smith Decl.) This satisfies Defendants’ initial burden. Plaintiff does not oppose the motion and presents no contrary evidence. Therefore, Plaintiff fails to raise a triable issue. RVAMG cannot be vicariously liable without any underlying liability. (See Lathrop v. HealthCare Partners Medical Group (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1412, 1423 [“The employer cannot be held vicariously liable unless the employee is found responsible”].)

CONCLUSION

            Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

 

 


Case Number: 23STCV31794    Hearing Date: May 6, 2024    Dept: 32

 

ANGELA HSU,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

 

TSASU, LLC, et al.,

                        Defendants.

 

  Case No.:  20STCV41059

  Hearing Date:  May 6, 2024

 

     [TENTATIVE] order RE:

defendant pichi lin’s motion to sever

 

 

BACKGROUND

            On October 26, 2020, Plaintiff Angela Hsu initiated this action against various Defendants for (1) quiet title, (2) cancellation of instruments, and (3) usury. Plaintiff alleges that she and her husband (Decedent) were joint tenants of real property located in Oceanside, California as of September 1977 (the Property). In May 2015 and October 2017, unbeknownst to Plaintiff, Decedent borrowed $100,000 and $50,000 from Defendants, secured by the Property. Decedent passed away in August 2020, and Plaintiff alleges that as a result, she wholly owns the Property without encumbrance. Defendants have refused to remove the subject liens from the Property, prompting this lawsuit.

            On March 4, 2021, Defendants Tsasu LLC and Hestter Investment, LLC (collectively, the creditors) filed a cross-complaint against Plaintiff for breach of contract and declaratory relief, alleging that Plaintiff is responsible for the loans made to Decedent.

            On August 1, 2022, Plaintiff filed the First Amended Complaint, which added causes of action for unjust enrichment and equitable indemnification against Pichi Lin (Lin). Plaintiff alleges that Decedent and Lin made the loans together without Plaintiff’s knowledge and that Lin benefitted from the money. Plaintiff alleges that if she is held liable to the creditors, then in fairness Lin should be liable to her, since Lin was the true beneficiary of the loans.

On February 17, 2023, the Court sustained Lin’s demurrer to the FAC because the facts did not support how Lin was unjustly enriched or at fault for Plaintiff’s liability, since it is undisputed that Decedent alone made the loans. However, the Court granted Plaintiff leave to amend to allege a cause of action based on Family Code section 1100(b), which allows a spouse to void an improper transfer of community property. The Court also granted leave to assert causes of action based on improper rent collection and forgery.

On March 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative Second Amended Complaint incorporating the Family Code allegations and adding causes of action for conversion and fraud by forgery. On May 8, 2023, the Court sustained Lin’s demurrer without leave to amend as to the unjust enrichment and equitable indemnity claims.

On April 10, 2024, Defendant Lin filed the instant motion to sever the causes of action against her from the causes of action against the other defendants. Plaintiff filed her opposition on April 23, 2024. Defendant filed her reply on April 29, 2024.       

LEGAL STANDARD

“The court may, when the convenience of witnesses, the ends of justice, or the economy and efficiency of handling the litigation would be promoted thereby, on motion of a party, after notice and hearing, make an order . . . that the trial of any issue or any part thereof shall precede the trial of any other issue or any part thereof in the case . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 598.) “The court, in furtherance of convenience or to avoid prejudice, or when separate trials will be conducive to expedition and economy, may order a separate trial of any cause of action, including a cause of action asserted in a cross-complaint, or of any separate issue or of any number of causes of action or issues . . . .” (Id., § 1048, subd. (b).) “It is within the discretion of the court to bifurcate issues or order separate trials of actions . . . and to determine the order in which those issues are to be decided.” (Royal Surplus Lines Ins. Co. v. Ranger Ins. Co. (2002) 100 Cal.App.4th 193, 205.)

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff has maintained throughout this litigation that the subject property was not community property and that therefore the creditors have no right to pursue Plaintiff for the loans made by Decedent. This is the premise of Plaintiff’s claims against the creditors. By contrast, Plaintiff’s primary theory against Lin is premised on the subject property being community property, thereby entitling Plaintiff to void up to half of the associated loan proceeds to the extent Decedent transferred them to Lin. (See Fam. Code, § 1100(b).) In other words, the claim against Lin is pled in the alternative in the event Plaintiff’s theory against the creditors fails. If Plaintiff succeeds against the creditors, i.e., establishes that the property was not community property, then there would be no claim against Lin under Family Code section 1100. Logically then, the claims against the creditors should be tried before the claims against Lin. It would be wasteful to litigate Plaintiff’s entitlement to void a community property transfer under Section 1100 if it turns out the subject property was not community property at all.

            In her opposition, Plaintiff argues that Lin is intertwined in all of the claims because the entire impetus for this dispute is Decedent and Lin taking the loans in the first place, thereby encumbering the subject property and causing the creditors to pursue Plaintiff for repayment. While the claims naturally involve some common facts and witnesses, that does not negate the reality that Plaintiff’s community property theory against Lin necessarily depends on whether Plaintiff succeeds against the creditors. Depending on the outcome of the claims against the creditors, the community property claim against Lin may be moot. Therefore, they should not be tried together. Furthermore, the fraud and conversion claims against Lin for stealing rent proceeds are distinct from the quiet title claims against the creditors. Lastly, equitable claims such as quiet title are ordinarily tried by the Court, whereas statutory and tort claims may be tried by a jury. (See Estate of Phelps (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 332, 340 [“Generally, there is no right to a jury trial in a quiet title action which is fundamentally equitable in nature”].)

            In sum, the Court finds that severance in this case is “in furtherance of convenience,” necessary “to avoid prejudice,” and “conducive to expedition and economy.” (See Code Civ. Proc., § 1048(b).)

CONCLUSION

            Defendant Pichi Lin’s motion to sever is GRANTED. Plaintiff’s claims against Tsasu LLC, Hestter Investment, LLC, and Leonard Hsu shall be tried before the claims against Lin.
