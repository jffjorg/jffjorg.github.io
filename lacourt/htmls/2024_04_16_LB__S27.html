DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 20LBCV00117    Hearing Date: April 16, 2024    Dept: S27

Cross-Defendant, Fortia Enterprises, Inc.’s attorney of record, Emahn Counts, seeks to be relieved as counsel, contending Fortia is a suspended corporation and can no longer participate in this litigation.    

 

The motion is denied for several reasons. 

 

As an initial note, the Notice of Continuance, filed on 2/29/24, references “Fortia Enterprises, LLC,” but all other papers refer to Fortia as Fortia Enterprises, Inc.  The Court is not rejecting the motion on this ground, but notes the inconsistency. 

 

More importantly, there is no proof of service of the original motion, declaration, and/or proposed order on Fortia or any other party who has appeared in the action.  The Court will not grant a motion without proof of service.  The notice of continuance indicates a “courtesy copy” was served on the client, but the client is absolutely entitled to notice of the motion and service of all moving papers.

 

Additionally, the declaration in support of the motion is unclear and conflicts with the proposed order.  The declaration, at ¶3(b), must state how Counsel confirmed the client’s address is confirmed.  Counsel checked the box for “by conversation,” but then also wrote in “via secretary of state,” but did not check the box for “by other means.”  Notably, an address cannot be confirmed by the secretary of state, as records on file with the secretary of state are often not current.  On the proposed order, ¶6, Counsel checked the box for “last known address” instead of “current address.”  The order form makes clear that, if the address is “last known” instead of “current,” then service must be made per CCP §1011(b) and CRC 3.252.  §1011(b)(3) makes clear that, if a party’s address is not known, service must be made on the clerk of the court.  CRC 3.252 also provides for service of papers on the clerk of the court if a party’s address is not known. 

 

If Counsel re-files the motion, Counsel must file proof of service of the moving papers on Client and all parties who have appeared in the action, and must make clear whether Client’s address is confirmed or not, and if so, by what means. 

 

Counsel is ordered to give notice.

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.  


Case Number: 23LBCV01654    Hearing Date: April 16, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Beach Front Properties, LLC filed this action against Defendants, Waterford Property Company, LLC, Sean Rawson, and John Crachman for breach of oral contract and related claims.  The crux of Plaintiff’s complaint is that Plaintiff entered into an oral agreement with the entity defendant, by and through the individual defendants, pursuant to which they would jointly invest in a California Community Housing Agency initiative to provide affordable housing to middle income residents. 

 

The parties cooperated between June of 2000 and October of 2001 regarding the venture, communicating regularly, identifying properties to include in the program, working through municipal requirements, identifying bond financing, and setting up a $5 million line of credit.  Defendants continued to make representations to Plaintiff concerning the ongoing efforts to secure properties into October of 2001, including assuring Plaintiff that the joint venture was very close to securing the first property on behalf of the joint venture.  However, Plaintiff later learned that, at the time these representations were being made, Defendants had actually acquired several properties that were supposed to be part of the joint venture for themselves solely and exclusively, cutting Plaintiff out of the project completely.

 

Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that, in October of 2021, Plaintiff learned, through an acquaintance, of a news article making clear that Defendant had acquired a property in Long Beach that was supposed to be a joint venture property.  Plaintiff immediately confronted Rawson, who acknowledged the acquisition and indicated the parties could work it out.  The parties had a meeting on 10/18/21, at which time Defendants admitted to having lied to Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff filed the complaint on 8/21/23.  The complaint includes causes of action for breach of oral joint venture agreement, breach of oral partnership agreement, breach of fiduciary duty, breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, promissory estoppel or detrimental reliance, fraudulent concealment in business dealings, constructive fraud, accounting, unjust enrichment and constructive trust, and declaratory relief. 

 

2.     Demurrer

Defendants demur to the entire complaint, contending it is barred by the statute of limitations.  Specifically, Defendants contend the fraud-based claims fail because judicially noticeable facts show media outlets published at least 22 articles between February and August of 2021 about the investment activities that violated the parties’ agreement, and the remaining claims fail because they are barred by the statute of limitations. 

 

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Request for Judicial Notice

Defendants seek judicial notice of 22 newspaper articles, published between 2/12/21 and 8/23/21, in publications including the Multi-Housing News, Bisnow, Northmarq, the Registry So Cal, the Long Beach Post News, the Long Beach Press-Telegram, the Commercial, the Real Deal, Real Estate News & Media, the Commercial Observer, RE Business LA, Traded LA, Orange County Business Journal, Los Angeles Business Journal, KCRW, and ultimately, on 8/23/21, the LA Times.

 

The Court takes judicial notice of the fact that these newspaper articles were published, are what they purport to be, and say what they purport to say.  The Court does not take judicial notice of the truth of anything asserted in the articles. 

 

c.     Meet and Confer

The Waggoner declaration, submitted with the moving papers, is sufficient to meet this requirement.    

 

d.     Statute of Limitations

Defendants contend all of the non-fraud-based claims are barred by the statute of limitations.  They contend this is so because the judicially noticeable documents, detailed above, put Plaintiff on notice as of February of 2021 that they were in breach of the alleged oral agreement, and Plaintiff did not file suit until more than two years later.

 

Defendants rely on several cases to support their position that newspaper articles place potential plaintiffs on inquiry notice such that the statute of limitations on their claims commences running.  These cases include Shively v. Boanich (2003) 31 Cal.4th 1230, 1248, McKelvey v. Boeing North America, Inc. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 151, 160-161, and 32 Am.Jur. Proof of Facts 3d 129 (1995). 

 

In Shively, the Court noted:

When the basis for a claim has been published in the public record or has been the subject of publicity, several cases have declined to apply the discovery rule, commenting that the plaintiff may be expected to be sufficiently diligent to discover the basis for his or her claim within the statutory period. (See  Utility Cost Management v. Indian Wells Valley Water Dist. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1185, 1197, 114 Cal.Rptr.2d 459, 36 P.3d 2 [rejecting application of the discovery rule to a claim for refund of an assertedly excessive charge, when the basis for the plaintiff's claim could have been discovered by means of the public reporting requirements that were imposed by statute on the defendant];  Norgart v. Upjohn Co., supra, 21 Cal.4th at pp. 408–409, fn. 7, 87 Cal.Rptr.2d 453, 981 P.2d 79 [relying upon press coverage in rejecting the plaintiffs' claim that, because they had no actual or constructive suspicion concerning a defendant's culpability, they could not have ***589 amended their complaint to name the defendant within the period of limitations];  McKelvey v. Boeing North American, Inc. (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 151, 160–161 & fn. 11, 86 Cal.Rptr.2d 645 [referring to publicity as a basis for rejecting tolling under a statutory discovery rule]; 3 Witkin, Cal. Procedure (4th ed. 1996) Actions, § 602, p. 773; but see  Prudential Home Mortgage Co. v. Superior Court, supra, 66 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1247–1248, 78 Cal.Rptr.2d 566 [observing that public records may impart “presumptive notice” of the basis for a claim, but declining to hold that they always do so as a matter of law].)

 

In Utility Cost, the Court noted that utility companies are required to make information concerning capital facilities fees publicly available.  Those were the very fees that formed the basis of the lawsuit.  Because the fees were publicly available, the Court held that delayed discovery did not apply.  In Norgart, the plaintiffs were suing for the wrongful death of their child who died by suicide.  The parents admitted that they suspected someone had done something wrong and caused the death; that suspicion, coupled with the fact that the defendant drug manufacturer’s own package inserts indicated depression and suicidal ideation could be caused by the drug.  Once the parents suspected something caused the suicide, they were on inquiry notice and needed to look for information, including the package inserts, that would support their suspicions and explain who it was who had done the wrong.  In Boeing, the plaintiffs pled that there were numerous articles published about Boeing’s wrongdoing, and that they were aware of the articles; they contended, however, that they were not aware of the actual and potential harm caused by the wrongdoing.  The Court held this was insufficient. 

 

Finally, in Prudential, the Court held that public records impart presumptive notice under some circumstances, but their mere existence is not conclusive as a matter of law. 

 

The Court finds none of the cited cases supports a conclusion that Plaintiff knew or should have known about the articles that are the subject of the RJN.  The Court notes that the vast majority of the articles were in publications that are virtually unknown.  While Defendants may, at trial, introduce evidence that, in certain circles, these publications are widely read and Plaintiff was expected to have known about and read them, these would be evidentiary issues. 

 

The demurrer to these causes of action is overruled.

 

e.     Fraud

Defendants contend the fraud-based causes of action fail because Plaintiff cannot plead concealment with an intent to defraud.  They contend the fact that Defendants were being quoted in newspaper articles specifically stating that they were acting in a way that contravenes Plaintiff’s interests and is in contravention of the parties’ alleged oral agreement conclusively negates this element of the claim.

 

The fraud, however, alleged commenced in or around June of 2020, when Defendants indicated an agreement to work with Plaintiff as a joint venture.  Articles that commenced publication in February of 2021 cannot be used to show lack of intent to defraud at the time the statements were made.  The issue becomes more muddied with respect to representations made after the first article was published, but the Court cannot sustain a demurrer to a part of a conversation.  Additionally, it seems that the issue of whether ongoing misrepresentations constitute fraud while the defendant is making contradictory statements in the public media would be a question for the trier of fact, who would need to weigh what the defendant suspected the plaintiff was reading in the media, etc., while making those representations. 

 

The demurrer is overruled. 

f.      Conclusion

The demurrer is overruled in its entirety.  Defendants are ordered to file an answer to the complaint within ten days.
