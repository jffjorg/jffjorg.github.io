Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 18STCV06837    Hearing Date: May 13, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

andres manzo , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

hyundai motor america , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

18STCV06837

 

 

Hearing Date:

May 13, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiffs’ motion to strike or, in the alternative, tax costs

 

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Plaintiffs Andres Manzo and Eva Mora                    

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Defendant Hyundai Motor America

Motion to Strike or, in the Alternative, Tax Costs

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs Andres Manzo and Eva Mora (“Plaintiffs”) move the court for an order (1) striking, in its entirety, the memorandum of costs filed by defendant Hyundai Motor America (“Defendant”), or (2) alternatively, taxing $20,001.18 in costs claimed by Defendant.  

The court finds that Defendant is not entitled to recover costs from Plaintiffs and therefore grants Plaintiffs’ motion to strike Defendant’s memorandum of costs.

“If an offer made by a defendant is not accepted and the plaintiff fails to obtain a more favorable judgment or award, the plaintiff shall not recover his or her postoffer costs and shall pay the defendant’s costs from the time of the offer.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (c)(1).)  “On a motion to strike or tax costs, ‘[t]he burden is on the offering party to demonstrate that the offer is valid under section 998.’  [Citation.]”  (Covert v. FCA USA, LLC (2022) 73 Cal.App.5th 821, 832.)  “Once the offeror shows the section 998 offer is valid, the burden shifts to the offeree to show the offer was not made in good faith.”  (Id. at p. 833.)

The court finds that (1) Defendant has met its burden to show that its January 10, 2019 Code of Civil Procedure section 998 Offer to Compromise (the “998 Offer”) was sufficiently specific so as not to render it invalid for lack of specificity, and (2) Plaintiffs have met their burden to show that the 998 Offer was not made in good faith and therefore is invalid.  (Covert, supra, 73 Cal.App.5th at pp. 832, 833.)

First, the court agrees with Defendant that (1) although the 998 Offer did not expressly state that it would allow judgment to be entered in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiffs, section 998 provides that, upon acceptance of the offer and the filing of the proof of acceptance, “the clerk or the judge shall enter judgment accordingly[,]” thus providing Plaintiffs with judgment in their favor, and (2) the absence of a provision identifying a specific date of payment of the settlement funds does not render the 998 Offer impermissibly uncertain and invalid, particularly because the 998 Offer states that payment would be made within five business days of the surrender of the subject vehicle with clear title and no liens to Defendant.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 998, subd. (b)(1); Cutler Decl., Ex. C, 998 Offer, ¶¶ 3 [provision requiring dismissal of the complaint with prejudice], 1 [provision stating that Defendant shall pay $34,000 to Plaintiffs on an unspecified date but within five business days of date of surrender]; Covert, supra, 73 Cal.App.5th at p. 840 [noting that “the Courts of Appeal have repeatedly upheld the validity of section 998 offers without a payment date” and finding that the plaintiff “cite[d] no authority for his contention that an offer to pay money in exchange for a dismissal without a payment date renders the offer invalid”].)

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has met its burden to show that its 998 Offer was not invalid for the reasons asserted by Plaintiffs in their moving papers.  (Mot., pp. 4:24-5:12; Covert, supra, 73 Cal.App.5th at p. 832.)

Second, the court finds that Plaintiffs have shown that the otherwise valid 998 Offer was not made in good faith because it was premature.  (Covert, supra, 73 Cal.App.5th at p. 833; Licudine v. Cedars-Sinai Medical Center (2019) 30 Cal.App.5th 918, 926 [“it is the 998 offeree who bears the burden of showing that an otherwise valid 998 offer was not made in good faith”].)

“A 998 offer is made in good faith only if the offer is ‘ “realistically reasonable under the circumstances of the particular case” ’ [citation]—that is, if the offer ‘carr[ies] with it some reasonable prospect of acceptance’ [citation].”  (Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at p. 924 [internal citations omitted].)  “Whether a section 998 offer has a reasonable prospect of acceptance is a function of two considerations, both to be evaluated in light of the circumstances ‘at the time of the offer’ and ‘not by virtue of hindsight.’”  (Ibid. [internal citations omitted].)  The first consideration concerns whether the offer was within the range of reasonably possible results at trial, considering all the information that the offeror knew or reasonably should have known.  (Id. at pp. 924-925.)  The second consideration concerns whether the offeror know that the offeree had sufficient information to assess whether the offer was a reasonable one, such that the offeree had a fair opportunity to intelligently evaluate the offer.  (Id. at p. 925.)  As to the second consideration, courts assess three circumstances: (1) how far into the litigation the offer was made; (2) the information bearing on the reasonableness of the offer that was available to the offeree prior to the offer’s expiration; and (3) whether the offeree alerted the offeror that it lacked sufficient information to evaluate the offer and, if so, how the offeror responded.  (Id. at pp. 925, 926.)

The court acknowledges that the 998 Offer was “within the ‘range of reasonably possible results’ at trial” because (1) the 998 Offers a repurchase price of the subject vehicle in the amount of $34,000, and (2) on August 14, 2023, judgment was entered in favor of Plaintiffs and against Defendant in the amount of $23,276.13.  (Cutler Decl., Ex. C, 998 Offer, ¶ 1; Aug. 14, 2023 Judgment, JUD-100, ¶ 6.)  However, the court finds that, under the circumstances presented here, Defendant’s 998 Offer was not made in good faith because Defendant knew that Plaintiffs did not have sufficient information to “assess whether the offer ‘[was] a reasonable one,’ such that the [Plaintiffs] [did not have] a ‘fair opportunity to intelligently evaluate the offer’[.]”  (Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at p. 925.)

Plaintiffs filed this action on December 3, 2018.  (Pl. Compl.)  Defendant served its 998 Offer on Plaintiffs a little over one month later, on January 10, 2019.  (Cutler Decl., Ex. C, 998 Offer.)  “Although section 998 fixes no ‘minimum period that must elapse following commencement of suit for service of a valid 998 offer [citation], a litigant receiving a 998 offer at the time a lawsuit is filed or soon thereafter is, as a general matter, less likely to have sufficient information upon which to evaluate that offer.”  (Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at p. 925 [emphasis in original] [internal citation and footnote omitted].)  Here, because Defendant served the 998 Offer on Plaintiffs only a month after they filed this action, Plaintiffs were less likely to have sufficient information upon which to evaluate Defendant’s offer.  Moreover, Plaintiffs have presented evidence establishing that, at the time that the offer was made, Plaintiffs had not obtained pertinent information regarding their claims through post-complaint discovery.  (Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at p. 926 [noting that information may be obtained through post-complaint discovery].)  Specifically, Plaintiffs’ counsel has stated that Defendant “did not provide responses and document production to Plaintiff’s discovery requests until June 12, 2019 and June 19, 2019[,]” i.e., six months after the date that the 998 Offer was served.  (Cutler Decl., ¶ 6; Cutler Decl., Ex. C, 998 Offer.)  Defendant has not disputed this in its opposition.  Further, while Plaintiffs did not address whether they alerted Defendant that they lacked sufficient information to evaluate the 998 Offer, it appears that Plaintiffs may have done so by requesting discovery since Plaintiffs have shown that they received discovery responses from Defendant in June 2019.  (Cutler Decl., ¶ 6; Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at p. 926 [“An offeree may alert the offeror by (1) requesting discovery, either formally or informally . . . .”].)

Thus, the court finds that Defendant’s 998 Offer was unreasonable, was not made in good faith, and therefore is invalid and cannot support Defendant’s request for costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 998, subdivision (c)(1).  (Licudine, supra, 30 Cal.App.5th at p. 924 [“A 998 offer is valid only if it is made in ‘good faith’”].)

 

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiffs Andres Manzo and Eva Mora’s motion to strike costs.

            The court orders that the Memorandum of Costs filed by defendant Hyundai Motor America on August 29, 2023, is stricken.

            The court orders plaintiffs Andres Manzo and Eva Mora to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
