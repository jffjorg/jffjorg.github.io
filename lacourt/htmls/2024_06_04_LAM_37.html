DEPARTMENT 37 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV00208    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: 37

HEARING DATE:                 Tuesday, June 04, 2024

CASE NUMBER:                   21STCV00208

CASE NAME:                        Virginia Pech-Torres v. Windsor Cheviot Hills, LLC, et al.

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Virginia Pech-Torres

OPPOSING PARTY:             Defendants Modern HR, Inc.; Today’s HR, Inc.; Kevin Jones; Nancy Rosas; and Jeff Huang

TRIAL DATE:                        Not Set

PROOF OF SERVICE:           OK

                                                                                                                                                           

PROCEEDING:                      Motion for Reconsideration of the February 27, 2024, Order

OPPOSITION:                        16 April 2024

REPLY:                                  22 April 2024

 

TENTATIVE:                         Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration of the February 27, 2024, Order is denied. Plaintiff to give notice.

                                                                                                                                                           

 

Background

 

This action arises out of the employment of Virginia Pech-Torres’ (“Plaintiff”) with Defendants CPE HR, Inc. (“CPE HR”) and Windsor Cheviot Hills, LLC. (“Windsor”).  Plaintiff alleges that on August 30, 2018, she injured her wrist when she was assisting a patient. Plaintiff allegedly informed both Defendant Kevin Jones (“Jones”) and Director of Nursing Eva Cameron about her wrist injury but they ignored her.  The Complaint also alleges that in early September 2019, Plaintiff had to visit the emergency room due to pregnancy complications and subsequently informed Defendants, Nancy Doe (“Doe”) and Jeff Huang (“Huang”) about these complications. Plaintiff requested light duty because of her pregnancy complications, but Defendants Doe and Huang allegedly intentionally refused to accommodate her.

 

The Complaint alleges twenty causes of action: (1) actual/perceived disability harassment in employment in violation of the Fair Housing Employment Act (“FEHA”), (2) actual/perceived disability discrimination in employment in violation of FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR, (3) actual/perceived disability retaliation in employment in violation of FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR, (4) failure to engage in the good-faith interactive process in violation of FEHA, (5) failure to provide reasonable accommodations in violation of the FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR, (6) violation of the California Family Rights Act, (7) violation of the California Pregnancy Disability Leave Law (“PDLL”), (8) sex/gender harassment in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor, CPE HR and Jones , (9) sex/gender discrimination in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR, (10) sex/gender retaliation in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR, (11) race/national origin harassment in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor, Jones and CPE HR, (12) race/national origin discrimination in employment against Windsor and CPE HR, (13) race/national origin retaliation in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor, CPE HR, (14) harassment, discrimination, retaliation and/or expulsion for reporting patient abuse in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR, (15) whistleblower retaliation against Windsor and CPE HR, (16) failure to provide meal and rest periods against Windsor and CPE HR, (17) failure to pay overtime wages against Windsor and CPE HR, (18) violation of Business and Professions Code §§ 17200 et seq. against Windsor and CPE HR, (19) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (20) injunctive relief- physical disability discrimination in employment in violation of the FEHA against Windsor and CPE HR.

 

On September 2, 2021, the court granted Windsor and CPE HR’s Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay Proceedings.

 

On August 7, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Motion to Lift the Stay and Proceed against Defendants Huang and Jones (hereinafter “Individual Defendants”). Defendants Windsor and CPE HR (collectively “Entity Defendants”) filed opposing papers on August 16, 2023. Plaintiff filed a reply on August 22, 2023.

 

The court continued the hearing after receiving Windsor Cheviot Hills, LLC’s filed a notice of stay of proceedings due to bankruptcy. On December 30, 2023, the court stayed the action as to Defendants Windsor Cheviot Hills, LLC and CPE HR, Inc. but lifted the stay as to individual Defendants Huang and Jones.

 

On December 22, 2023, Defendants Modern, Inc., Today’s HR, Inc., Kevin Jones, Nancy Rosas, and Jeff Huang (collectively “Defendants”) moved for an order compelling Plaintiff to arbitrate her claims against them. On February 27, 2024, the court granted Defendants’ Motion.

 

Plaintiff now moves for reconsideration of the February 27, 2024, Order. Defendants oppose the Motion. The matter is now before the court.

II.        Discussion

 

Plaintiff requests reconsideration of this court’s February 27, 2024, Order which granted Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration and found that Jones and Huang had not waived the right to compel arbitration of Plaintiff’s claims. The primary basis for which this court granted Defendants’ February 2024 Motion to Compel arbitration was that the agency exception applied to the dispute. (See Order 2/27/2024.)

 

Previously, the court found a valid agreement to arbitrate exists between Plaintiff and the Entity Defendants, CPE HR, Inc. (“CPE HR”) and Windsor Cheviot Hills, LLC. (“Windsor”). (See Order 9/2/21.)  The court found that although Plaintiff disputed being provided with a copy of the Arbitration Agreement policy terms, she did not dispute that she signed the Arbitration Agreement. (Ibid.) The court noted that the Alternative Dispute Resolution Policy (the “ADR Policy”) applied to all disputes between Plaintiff and Windsor and/or its outsourced Human Resources provider CPE HR or any of their respective employees or officers. (Ibid.)

 

On February 27, 2024, the court granted the Motion to Compel Arbitration and Stay the Action brought by Defendants Modern HR, Inc., Today’s HR, Inc., Kevin Jones, Nancy Rosas, and Jeff Huang.  Plaintiff specifically requested that the court find that Individual Defendants Jones and Huang waived their right to compel arbitration due to Defendants’ prior refusal to submit to arbitration. (See Order 2/27/2024.) The court denied Plaintiff’s request, finding that Jones and Huang had not waived the right to compel arbitration. (See Order 02/27/2024.)

 

The court now finds that Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration is deficient. One of the explicit requirements for reconsideration under CCP § 1008(a) is that “[t]he party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.”  The Declaration of Plaintiff’s counsel, Zachary Wagner, relitigated points as to why the court should find that Defendants Jone and Huang waived the right to compel arbitration but fails to lay out new or different facts, circumstances, or law would warrant reconsideration. (See e.g. Wagner Decl.)

 

The court also finds that Desert Regional Medical Center, Inc. v. Miller (2022) 87 Cal.App.5th 295 (Desert) is not “new law”, as the decision was published in 2022, and was not “new” at the time Plaintiff filed her opposition on February 13, 2022, or at the time the original hearing was held. Desert stands for the proposition that when California law applies “to the determination of whether the court or arbitrator has jurisdiction over the issue of waiver,” “[t]he usual federal rule of waiver [that] does not include a prejudice requirement” does not apply. (Id. at pp. 314, 322.) The Desert, “case is distinguishable from Morgan in that the instant case concerns state statutory rights and law, rather than rights asserted under federal law.” (Id. at p. 322.) Accordingly, the argument that the court should have considered the prejudice to Plaintiff if the motion to compel was granted is an argument Plaintiff should have raised in her opposition or at the hearing. Moreover, the fact that the prejudice factor weighs in favor of Plaintiff does not negate this court’s finding that the other five St. Agnes/Peterson factors favored Jones and Huang. (See Order 2/27/2024 at p. 4.)

 

On reply, Plaintiff’s counsel presents evidence that Defendant CPE HR has admitted that Defendant Jones and Huang are not its agents. (Wagner Supp. Decl. ¶ 3, Ex. A (CPE HR, Inc.’s responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Set One, Nos. 1 and 2).)  “‘[P]oints raised for the first time in a reply brief will ordinarily not be considered, because such consideration would deprive respondent of an opportunity to counter the argument.’ ” (Jay v. Mahaffey (2013) 128 Cal.App.4th 1522, 1538 citing American Drug Stores v. Stroh (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1446, 1453.) Even if the court were to consider CPE HR, Inc.’s responses to Plaintiff’s Requests for Admission, Plaintiff has failed to show that Individual Defendants Jones and Huang were not agents of employer Windsor. Thus, Jones and Huang remain entitled to compel arbitration under the agency exception. Should Plaintiff present evidence that Individual Defendants Jones and Huang are not agents of Windsor, and the agency exception does not apply, Plaintiff may refile a motion for reconsideration under CCP § 1008(e).

 

For the reasons set forth above, the court denies Plaintiff’s Motion.

 

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Reconsideration of the February 27, 2024, Order is denied. Plaintiff to give notice.
