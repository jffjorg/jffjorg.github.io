Case Number: 22AVCV00272    Hearing Date: March 12, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a motor vehicle – personal injury action. Plaintiffs Jessica Joseph (“Jessica”)[1] and Jay Joseph (“Jay” and collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege that on November 18, 2020, on or about 10th Street West at or near the 14 Freeway Southbound exit ramp in the City of Palmdale, County of Los Angeles, State of California, Defendant Jerry Wood, Jr. (“Wood, Jr.”) failed to exercise due care in the management, operation, and use of their vehicle and, as a result, struck a singular unspecified plaintiff’s vehicle, causing damages to the unspecified plaintiff. Defendant Fairmount Contractors LLC (“Fairmount” and with Wood, Jr., “Defendants”) is alleged to be the owner of the vehicle and negligently entrusted, allowed, and permitted Wood, Jr., to use its vehicle when it knew or should have known that Wood, Jr., (1) did not possess the requisite skill, care, control, training, knowledge and management to operate a motor vehicle; (2) did not possess the requisite skill, care, control, training, knowledge and management to operate a motor vehicle on the public streets of the County of Los Angeles; and (3) would and could endanger the health and well-being of persons on the street and in vehicles in the area where he operated their vehicle.

 

On April 20 2022, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint alleging three causes of action for: (1) Motor Vehicle by Plaintiffs against Defendants; (2) General Negligence by Plaintiffs against Defendant; and (3) Loss of Consortium by Jay against Defendants.

 

On June 06, 2022, Fairmount filed its Answer.

 

On April 03, 2023, Wood, Jr., filed his Answer.

 

On February 13, 2024, Defendants filed this Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Answer.

On February 22, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition.

 

On March 05, 2024, Defendants filed their Reply.

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Leave to Amend Answer – “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading or proceeding by adding or striking out the name of any party, or by correcting a mistake in the name of a party, or a mistake in any other respect; and may, upon like terms, enlarge the time for answer or demurrer. The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(a)(1).) 

 

California law holds that leave to amend is to be granted liberally, to accomplish substantial justice for both parties. (Hirsa v. Superior Court (1981) 118 Cal.App.3d 488-89.) “Assuming proper notice, the trial court has wide discretion in determining whether to allow the amendment, but the appropriate exercise of that discretion requires the trial court to consider a number of factors: ‘including the conduct of the moving party and the belated presentation of the amendment. The law is well settled that a long deferred presentation of the proposed amendment without a showing of excuse for the delay is itself a significant factor to uphold the trial court’s denial of the amendment.’” (Leader v. Health Ind. of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603, 613. Absent prejudice, trial delay alone is not grounds to deny a motion for leave to amend. (See Higgins v. Del Faro (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 558, 563-65.) In particular, liberality is displayed in allowing amendments to answers, for a defendant denied leave to amend is permanently deprived of a defense. (Hulsey v. Koehler (1990) 218 Cal.App.3d 1150, 115.)

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – Defendants present that there was an arbitration proceeding between the insurance companies for Defendants and Plaintiffs on June 10, 2021 and it was decided that Plaintiff was 60% liable and Defendants were 40% liable as to property damage only. (See Motion 2:27-3:2, 6:9-12.)

 

Defendants argue that Plaintiffs will not be prejudiced by the amendment as (1) no new discovery or very little additional discovery would be needed; (2) there would be no delay in trial as Plaintiffs’ counsel is aware of the arbitration decision and no depositions have occurred; (3) there was no bad faith or intentional delay in bringing this motion; and (4) “the trial is scheduled for May 24, 2024, meaning the discovery cut-off is June 24, 2024” (Motion 5:9-10; Decl. Ching ¶ 9). Defendants argue that the amendments are necessary as res judicata and collateral estoppel are proper affirmative defenses and, whether liability was decided as to the parties by their insurance carriers should be determined upon a motion for summary judgment. Defendants present that they have adhered to Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324 requirements.

 

Defendants have presented that the discovery cutoff will occur 30 days after the trial. The Court believes Defendants mean April 24, 2024 as the discovery cutoff is 30 days before trial. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2024.020.)  

 

The Court notes that counsel for Defendants, David Ching, has provided a declaration that states “there was a previously arbitration that decided liability as it relates to these facts and circumstances,” but does not attest to the date of the arbitration proceeding and the determination as stated in the Memorandum of Points and Authorities. No attachment has been provided with the moving papers that evidences an arbitration proceeding has occurred. 

 

As there is no evidence provided by Defendants as to the arbitration proceeding and its determination, Defendants’ presentation is purely conclusory.

 

The Court notes that Plaintiffs’ Opposition provides more information regarding the arbitration proceeding; however, the burden is on Defendants to meet their burden in establishing a factual and legal basis for leave to file an amended answer. For reasons, ante, Defendants have failed to do so.

 

Had the Court found that Defendants had met their burden, it appears that the arbitration award was unconfirmed. (See Decl. Jason G. Eisenberg, Exh. 4.) Each party has provided cases that they believe supports their respective arguments as to whether an unconfirmed award between two insurers is binding on the parties. “An arbitration award may only be enforced as written and, when confirmed, the award is reduced to a final and enforceable judgment.” (Mossman v. City of Oakdale (2009) 170 Cal.App.4th 83, 89.) An unconfirmed arbitration award “has the same force and effect as a contract in writing between the parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1287.6.) Defendants are asking the Court to allow them to amend their Answer to include res judicata and collateral estoppel. The unconfirmed arbitration award is not a final and enforceable judgment and, as such, res judicata is not a proper affirmative defense.

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES the Motion for Leave to Amend without prejudice.

 

-----

 

Conclusion

 

Defendants Jerry Wood, Jr., and Fairmount Contractors, LLC’s Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Answer is DENIED without prejudice.

Case Number: 23AVCV00066    Hearing Date: March 12, 2024    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is an action stemming from the lease of real property located at 46645 60th St. West, Lancaster, CA 93536 (“the Property”). Plaintiff Leilani L. Antoine (“Plaintiff”) alleges that Defendants Charles E. Mosman (“Charles”[1]); Sandra Mosman (“Sandra”); Mosman’s Country Steakhouse & Bar, Zubie Enterprises, Inc. (“Zubie Enterprises”); and Charles E. Mosman DBA Mosman’s Town & Country Carpet & Construction (“Mosman’s Town & Country Carpet & Construction” and collectively “Defendants”) are former commercial tenants of hers. Plaintiff further alleges damages stemming from their time as her tenants.

 

On January 18, 2023, Plaintiff filed her Complaint alleging once cause of action for General Negligence and four causes of action labelled “Intentional Tort.”

 

On February 24, 2023, Defendants filed their Demurrer with Motion to Strike to the Complaint, subsequently sustained with leave to amend.

 

On April 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed her First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging causes of action for General Negligence and Intentional Tort (four cases of action with this label). Plaintiff also wrote in, but not did not provide an attachment, for a cause of action under Cal. Pen. Code § 496(c).

 

On May 30, 2023, Defendants filed their Demurrer with Motion to Strike to the FAC, subsequently sustained with 30 days’ leave to amend.

 

On August 10, 2023, Plaintiff filed an Ex Parte Application for Leave to File a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). The Ex Parte Application for Leave to File a SAC requested leave to include a sixth cause of action for battery against Charles and a seventh cause of action against all defendants for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage. The matter was continued to August 14, 2023 as Department A-14 was dark on August 11, 2023. On August 14, 2023, the Court denied the Ex Parte Application for Leave to File a SAC as no exigent circumstances existed to hear it on an ex parte basis. The matter was scheduled for regular hearing on September 19, 2023.

 

On August 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed a substitution of attorney, substituting William Garrett Cothran as counsel.

 

On September 19, 2023, the following were held by the Court: (1) an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”), (2) the hearing on the Leave to File a Second Amended Complaint, (3) a Case Management Conference (“CMC”), and (4) an Order to Show Cause (“OSC”) RE: Dismissal. Plaintiff was represented by her counsel of record, William Garrett Cothran (“Cothran”); and Defendants were represented by their counsel of record, Karen M. Stephanyan for Patrick J. Foley (“Stephanyan”). The Court denied Defendant’s oral motion to dismiss without prejudice and advised that a noticed motion may be filed. The OSC RE: Dismissal and hearing on the Leave to File a Second Amended Complaint were taken off calendar. The CMC was continued to January 12, 2024.

 

On October 04, 2023, Defendants filed their Motion to Dismiss.

 

On October 24, 2023, Defendants filed a Notice of Non-Opposition regarding their Motion to Dismiss.

 

On October 30, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint, set for hearing on December 12, 2023.

 

On October 31, 2023, the Court held a hearing on the Motion to Dismiss. No opposing papers were filed; however, Cothran appeared on behalf of Plaintiff. Stephanyan appeared for Defendants. Oral argument was presented by each counsel for their respective client. The Court granted the motion.

 

On December 06, 2023, the Court held a non-appearance case review. The Court noted that the action was dismissed on October 31, 2023; the Court ordered the FAC, filed on April 27, 2023, dismissed with prejudice; and the Court vacated the December 12, 2023 Hearing on Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint and the January 12, 2024 CMC.

 

On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff filed this Motion to Set Aside Dismissal.

 

On February 28, 2024, Defendants filed their Opposition.

 

No Reply has been filed. “. . .[A]ll reply papers [hall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party] at least five court days before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1005(b).) “Section 1013, which extends the time within which a right may be exercised or an act may be done, does not apply to a notice of motion, papers opposing a motion, or reply papers governed by this section.” (Ibid.) Here, the motion is set for March 12, 2024. Accordingly, a Reply was due by March 05, 2024. Should a Reply be filed, it is now untimely.

 

 

-----

 

Legal Standard

 

Standard to Set Aside – ¿“ ‘ A motion for relief under section 473 is addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court and an appellate court will not interfere unless there is a clear showing of an abuse.’ ” (Burnete¿v. La Casa Dana Apartments¿(2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1262, 1266.)¿ “Although a trial court has discretion to vacate the entry of a default or subsequent judgment, this discretion may be exercised only after the party seeking relief has shown that there is a proper ground for relief, and that the party has raised that ground in a procedurally proper manner, within any applicable time limits.” (Cruz v.¿Fagor¿America, Inc.¿(2007) 146 Cal.App.4th 488, 495.)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Additionally, the California Supreme Court has held the following:¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Section 473 is often applied liberally where the party in default moves promptly to seek relief, and the party opposing the motion will not suffer prejudice if relief is granted. [Citations.] In such situations “very slight evidence will be required to justify a court in setting aside the default.” [Citations.] [¶] Moreover, because the law strongly favors trial and disposition on the merits, any doubts in applying section 473 must be resolved in favor of the party seeking relief from default. [Citations.] Therefore, a trial court order denying relief is scrutinized more carefully than an order permitting trial on the merits. [Citations.]¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

(Elston¿v. City of Turlock¿(1985) 38 Cal.3d 227, 233-34.)¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) permits the Court to “relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect.”¿(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b).) “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.” (Id.)¿¿¿ 

 

-----

 

Discussion

 

Application – Plaintiff moves to set aside the dismissal entered on October 31, 2024. (See Mtn. to Set Aside Dismissal 1:19-23.)[2] Plaintiff presents the following timeline:

 

·       Defendants filed a motion to dismiss on October 04, 2023, the motion was served to the Law Office of Loa and Cothran (the firm of Cothran), the attorney’s assistant filed the notice in the client’s file but did not inform either attorney of the motion;

·       Neither attorney reviewed the case online and, as a result, the time to respond was not calendared;

·       Cothran discovered a “motion of no response” on October 24, 2023 on October 25, 2023;

·       Cothran believed that filing a motion for leave to amend would satisfy the need to respond and filed a Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint on October 30, 2023;

·       The Court granted the dismissal on October 31, 2023.

 

However, Cothran’s declaration supports only the following:

 

·       Richard Loa (“Loa”), Cothran’s partner was supposed to originally handle this matter during the month of September 2023 and into the month of October 2023, but Loa’s ill health limited his time in the office and Cothran’s ability to contact Loa which led to Cothran being unaware that the amended complaint had not been prepared for filing;

·       From October 06, 2023 to October 18, 2023, Cothran was out of office due recovery from an emergency dental surgery;

·       Cothran prepared the amended complaint only upon receiving the Notice of Non-Opposition on October 25, 2023; and

·       Cothran believed he needed to prepare a motion for leave to amend the complaint due to how drastically the amended complaint was compared to the previous complaints.

 

(See Decl. Cothran ¶¶ 4-6.)

 

Cothran’s declaration regarding his knowledge is contradicted by the Court’s records. The Court takes judicial notice of its records pursuant to Cal. Evid. Code § 452(d). The Court provides this tentative to incorporate the respective documents, discussed infra, as part of the record for the purpose of this motion and to afford each party reasonable opportunity to meet such information before judicial notice of the matter may be taken. (See Cal. Evid. Code § 455.) The Court’s records show:

 

·       The hearing on the Demurrer to the FAC was sustained with 30 days’ leave to amend on July 11, 2023 (see 07/11/2023 Minute Order), making the date the amended pleading needed to be filed by Thursday, August 10, 2023;

·       On August 15, 2023, Cothran substituted in (see Substitution of Attorney filed 08/16/2023);

·       On September 19, 2023, the the following were held by the Court: (1) an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”), (2) the hearing on the Leave to File a Second Amended Complaint, (3) a Case Management Conference (“CMC”), and (4) an Order to Show Cause (“OSC”) RE: Dismissal. Cothran was present for these hearings. At the hearings, with Cothran present, the following relevant events occurred: (1) Defendants made an oral motion to dismiss; (2) the Court denied the oral motion without prejudice; and (3) the Court advised that a noticed motion to dismiss may be filed (see 09/19/2023 Minute Order).

 

Therefore, Cothran’s presentation to the Court is inaccurate. Cothran was aware that a Motion to Dismiss would be filed as early as September 19, 2023. Cothran’s declaration does not attest to a calendaring error. Thus, the presentation that the attorney’s assistant filed the notice in the client’s file but did not inform either attorney of the motion is unsupported. Likewise, the presentation that neither attorney reviewed the case online and, as a result, the time to respond was not calendared is unsupported. While Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 does not provide a statutory requirement that there be an affidavit of the merits to support a motion under subdivision (b), where the burden is on moving party to place before court such material as will show that ends of justice will be served by granting relief under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473, an affidavit may be only efficacious method of bringing facts before court. (See Plummer v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1963) 212 Cal.App.2d 841, 843-44 [discussing requirement of affidavit under the 1963 version of Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 in the leave to amend context].)

 

Here, only the following is supported:

 

·       Cothran substituted in after the amended pleading was due;

·       Cothran is required to perform legal services with competency (see Cal. State Bar Rules of Professional Conduct, Rule 1.1. Competence) and with reasonable diligence (see id. at Rule 1.3 Diligence), thus Cothran should have been aware that the action had no underlying pleading and action needed to be taken;

·       Cothran was present for September 19, 2023 hearings and, as such, was aware of the status of the case and that a motion to dismiss was to be filed due to the failure to file a Second Amended Complaint by the Court Order; and

·       Cothran’s filed a Motion for Leave to Amend the Complaint on October 30, 2023, set for a hearing on a different date from the Motion to Dismiss.

 

No action was taken by Cothran until October 30, 2023, over two months after Cothran was retained.

 

Thus, based on the presentation by counsel and the court’s records, it does not appear that the dismissal stemmed from mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect as the issue with the pleadings had been present since Cothran substituted into the case and no action was taken until October 2023.

 

Plaintiff also argues that relief is mandatory under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b).

 

Regarding Plaintiff’s citations to Yeap v. Leake (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 591 (“Yeap”), Yeap is distinguished as it is based on calendaring errors. (See Yeap, supra, 60 Cal.App.4th 591 [generally].) However, Cothran’s actions that are supported evidence inaction rather than error. Surprisingly, Cothran does not cite the most relevant case on this matter – Daley v. County of Butte (1964) 227 Cal.App.2d 380 (“Daley”) which created the Daley exception. Daley and its progeny created an exception to the general rule that an attorney’s negligence is imputed on a client. The exception occurs where an attorney abandons the client through a total failure to represent the client. (See Daley, supra, 227 Cal.App.2d 591 [generally]; see also Fleming v. Gallegos (1994) 23 Cal.App.4th 68 [summarizing the Daley exception and cases where it applied].) However, such an exception requires that the client be relatively free from negligence. (See Daley, supra, 227 Cal.App.2d 395.) Here, Plaintiff herself is not relatively free from negligence. That is, unlike in Daley where it was held that the ignorance of Plaintiff was due to secretarial error (i.e., failure to inspect a file until several weeks after its arrival) and counsel’s ongoing misconduct, including retention of the client file for five months without executing the substitution of attorney files, Plaintiff in this action was aware an amended pleading needed to be filed, retained counsel only after the amended pleading was due, and no action was taken once counsel was retained despite Plaintiff’s awareness of the matters within her case.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) does state: “Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect.” First, as discussed ante, Cothran’s errors alone are not the sole reason for the dismissal as Plaintiff represented herself in pro per, retained Cothran only after an amended pleading was due, and was aware of the matters within her case both before and after Cothran was retained. Next, Leader v. Health Industries of America, Inc. (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 603 (“Leader”) has analyzed this same section in a similar context and found that Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473 did not apply. Specifically, the Leader court explained that “the ‘day in court’ envisioned by [prior case law] is not a guaranteed trial on the merits, but merely the opportunity to appear and present evidence and argument in opposition” to a motion. (See Leader, supra, 89 Cal.App.4th at 621.) While the motions in Leader’s cited case discussed motions to dismiss, Leader discussed how their plaintiffs had a day in court in opposition to the demurrers, availed themselves of the opportunity, had an opportunity to amend, and belatedly attempted to do so. (See ibid.) That is, the Leader court held that a trial court acted within its discretion under Code of Civil Procedure section 58(f)(2), in granting the defendant's motion to dismiss for failure to file the amendment in time. This is directly applicable to the case at hand as Plaintiff opposed the Demurrer with Motion to Strike on its merits and does not challenge the ruling sustaining the Demurrer. Further, while Cothran elected to file a separate motion instead of an Opposition to the Motion to Dismiss, Cothran appeared on behalf of Plaintiff and presented oral argument, thus, availing Plaintiff of the opportunity to appear and present evidence and argument in opposition to the Motion to Dismiss. (See 10/31/2023 Minute Order.)

 

The Court notes that had it been able to relieve Plaintiff under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b), there is no proposed pleading to accompany this motion. The statute states clearly, in relevant part: “Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b) [emphasis added].)

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is DENIED.

Conclusion

 

Plaintiff Leilani L. Antoine’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is DENIED.
