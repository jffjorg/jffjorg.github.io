DEPARTMENT 57 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Inform the clerk if you submit on the tentative ruling. If moving and opposing parties submit, no appearance is necessary.

Case Number: 20STCV22026    Hearing Date: July 11, 2024    Dept: 57

 

 

 

 

Michael Mann (“Mann”) and Marilyn Craig (“Craig”) (collectively, “the Plaintiffs”) sued Farmers Insurance Exchange; Fire Insurance Exchange; Truck Insurance Exchange; Mid-Century Insurance Company; Farmers New World Life Insurance Company (collectively, “Farmers”).  The Plaintiffs also sued Arnoldo Jaquez (“Jaquez”).  The Plaintiffs assert claims against Farmers and Jaquez for age discrimination in violation of California’s Fair Employment and Housing Act and claims for violation of wage and hour laws in California’s Labor Code.  As alleged in the Plaintiffs’ complaint, Mann was an insurance agent for Farmers; Craig worked for and assisted Mann in his insurance business pursuant to a Customer Service Representative Agreement.  Jaquez is a District Manager and independent contractor for Farmers. 

 

Pending before the Court are eight motions that Jaquez filed arising from multiple discovery disputes with the Plaintiffs. 

 

Four of the motions initially were scheduled to be heard on June 26, 2024, but were continued to today.  Those four motions are as follows:  Compel Craig to Provide Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set 2; Compel Mann to Provide Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set 2; Compel Craig to Provide Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set 2; and Compel Mann to Provide Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents Set 2.  The Court’s detailed tentative rulings on those four motions are set forth below.  The tentative rulings are to grant all four motions. 

 

The other four motions on calendar today are as follows:  Compel Craig to Provide Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set 2; Compel Mann to Provide Further Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set 2; Compel Craig to Provide Further Responses to Requests for Admissions; and Compel Mann to Provide Further Responses to Requests for Admissions.   The Court’s tentative rulings are to deny all four of those motions.  Unfortunately, the Court bit of more than it could chew in scheduling all eight motions for today, and thus the Court has been unable to provide in advance of today’s hearing a detailed explanation of the basis of its rulings denying the other four motions.  The Court is therefore continuing those four motions to July 18, 2024 at 8:30.  Detailed tentative rulings on those four motions will be provided prior to the July 18, 2024 hearing.

 

MOTION TO COMPEL CRAIG TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET TWO

 

At issue in this motion are Special Interrogatories, Set Two, Numbers 76-94 (“SROGs”) directed at Craig.   The Court is granting the motion.

 

Jaquez served the SROGs on Craig on November 30, 2023.  On January 16, 2024, Craig served Jaquez with her responses to the SROGs. Craig’s responses consisted entirely of objections to the SROGs.  The objections posited, inter alia, that the SROGs sought irrelevant information, were unduly burdensome particularly as to their temporal scope, and were vague and ambiguous. 

  Counsel for Jaquez, Amanda Garica (“Garcia”), and counsel for Plaintiffs, Nancy Anfanger (“Anfanger”), met and conferred about the SROGS via telephone on January 26, 2024.  During this meeting, Anfanger explained the basis of Craig’s objections and suggested that Garcia revise and redraft the SROGs. Garcia agreed to take Craig’s objections under consideration but did not agree to redraft the SROGs.   On February 6, 2024, Garcia sent Anfanger a written meet and confer letter (“the February 6 Letter”) that addressed some of Craig’s objections and provided clarification in response to certain objections.  In particular, in the February 6 Letter, Garcia narrowed the time frame of the SROGs to begin on May 1, 2016 and provided further definitions of certain terms that were capitalized in the SROGS.  On March 4, 2024, Craig provided supplemental responses to the SROGs.  Notwithstanding the clarifications set forth in the February 6 Letter, Craig’s supplemental responses maintained the objections that the temporal scope of the SROGs were unduly burdensome and the use of certain capitalized terms was vague and ambiguous.   Craig also maintained her objections on relevance grounds.  Jacquez filed the instant motion the following day.  [To counsel -- there is no rhyme or reason to the blue font here -- it just came out this way when the tentative was posted.]


“Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)  A motion to compel further responses to interrogatories may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.300(c).)

If a timely motion to compel further responses has been filed, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully answer. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220–221; see also Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.)  To the extent there is any doubt on whether information should be discoverable, California’s liberal approach to discovery provides that doubt should be resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Pacific Tel. & Tel. Co. v. Superior Court (1970) 2 Cal.3d 161, 173.)

 

In the Court’s view, the SROGs seek information from Craig that is relevant to her claims against Jaquez.  Craig’s relevance objections is unmerited.  So, too, are her objections that the SROGS are burdensome due to their temporal scope and that they are vague and ambiguous with respect to the use of capitalized terms.  The February 6 Letter addressed those objections.  Yet, Craig maintained those objections in her supplemental responses.

 

Contrary to Craig’s contention in her opposition, Jaquez did not ambush her through the motion.  From Jaquez’s perspective, the supplemental responses Craig provided on March 4, 2024 revealed that the meet and confer process, including the fruits of that process that were reflected in the February 6 Letter, had run its course.

 

Also unavailing are the charges in Craig’s opposition that Garcia had no authority to meet and confer on Jaquez’s behalf.   To be sure, Garcia did not respond to Anfanger’s email to confirm that she (Garcia) had authority to make binding decisions regarding the meet and confer process, there is nothing in the record to suggest that Garcia lacked that authority.  Indeed, Garcia has appeared at multiple hearings in this action and argued  on behalf of Jaquez.  

 

Craig gets no traction either from her accusation that Jaquez filed the motion for financial reasons -- that is, to obtain sanctions against Craig and Anfanger.  The motion is well-taken.  That Jaquez also seeks sanctions against them through the motion is hardly nefarious.  Sanctions come with the discovery dispute territory if a party’s objections to discovery lack substantial justification.  Here, Craig’s objections, and adherence to the objections in her opposition, lack substantial justification.  Thus, sanctions against Craig and Anfanger are warranted,  

 

As to the amount of the sanctions, Jaquez is seeking an award of the attorney’s fees he incurred in connection with the motion.  That figure is $6,470.  In the Court’s view, the declaration submitted in support of the sanctions request demonstrates that the hourly billing rates of Jaquez’s attorneys who worked on the motion are reasonable and the number of hours the attorneys worked on the motion  are reasonable as well. 

 

Craig must provide further responses to the SROGs by August 5, 2024. The sanctions must be paid by October 31, 2024.

 

MOTION TO COMPEL MANN TO PROVIDE FURTHER RESPONSES TO SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET TWO

 

The SROGs as to Mann are Numbers 76-98.  Mann’s objections tracked Craig’s.  The meet and confer process as to the SROGs Jaquez propounded on Mann tracked the meet and confer process as to the SROGS Jaquez propounded on Craig, including the back and forth that led to the February 6, 2024.    The motion as to Mann was filed the same day as the motion as to Craig.  One difference is that Mann’s supplemental responses were  served on Jaquez the day after the motion was filed, whereas Craig’s supplemental responses were served the day before the filing.  All told, the Court is granting the substance of the motion as to Mann for the same reasons the Court granted the substance of the motion as to Craig.  

 

As for sanctions, however, the result for the Mann motion diverges from the result for the Craig motion.  Jaquez seeks the exact same amount of sanctions with respect to the Mann motion as he did with respect to the Craig motion.  In the Court’s view, the two motions are virtually identical and thus there necessarily were redundancies in the attorney’s work on the motions.  Accordingly, the Court is reducing the amount of sanctions Jaquez requested with respect to the Mann motion by ½, which comes out to $3,235.

 

Mann must provide further responses to the SROGs by August 5, 2024. The sanctions must be paid by October 31, 2024.

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL CRAIG AND MANN TO PROVIDE FURTHE RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET TWO

 

The Court will address the two motions to compel further responses to requests for production of documents (“RFPs”) together as they are virtually identical.  The Court is granting both motions. 

 

The Meet and Confer Process

 

The meet and confer process for the two motions was as follows.

 

On November 30, 2023, Jaquez served the RFPs on Craig and Mann.  Jaquez granted Craig and Mann a two-week extension to respond to the RFPs.  The responses were served on Jaquez on January 16, 2024.  On February 7, 2024, Garcia emailed Anfanger to schedule a call to discuss the responses to the RFPs. Counsel agreed telephonically to meet and confer regarding the RFPs on Tuesday, February 13, 2024 at 3:00 PM.   On the morning of February 13 at 9:51 AM, Anfanger emailed Garcia and cancelled the scheduled telephonic meet and confer due to a disagreement between counsel regarding the terms of their oral agreements concerning their discovery disputes. Anfanger, stated, “I will no longer meet and confer with you telephonically, including today. Please provide a written response regarding each of Plaintiff’s objections.”

 

 On February 14, 2024, Garcia emailed Anfanger a written meet and confer letter (“February 14 Letter”), which set forth Jaquez’s position with respect to the RFPs and provided further clarification regarding Plaintiff’s objections.  The clarifications included, inter alia, narrowing the temporal scope of the RFPs. The February 14 Letter also requested that Mann and Craig provide substantive and code-compliant responses to the RFPs because their responses and objections to date were deficient.  Garcia further requested in the February 14 Letter that Mann and Craig provide confirmation of their intent to supplement the responses by the close of business on Friday, February 16, 2024.   Lastly, Garcia stated in the February 14 Letter that, on behalf of Jaquez, she would be forced to move for an order compelling further responses, as well as sanctions, in the event Plaintiffs did not respond to the letter. Plaintiffs did not subsequently respond to the February 14 Letter.  On March 5, 2024, Jaquez filed the two motions to compel further responses to the RFPs.  

 

On June 4, 2024, Anfanger emailed Garcia, requesting that the motions be taken off calendar.  Garcia responded that Jaquez would welcome the receipt of supplemental responses, but given the discovery history, the motions would not be taken of calendar. Garcia also advised Anfanger to review the requests and responses and any deficiencies therein, so any subsequent meet and confer efforts could be expedited.  In response, Anfanger stated she would be supplementing the requests for production, but did not provide a date for that.

 

On June 10, 2024, Mann and Craig provided their supplemental responses to the RFPs. In an email that night, Anfanger asked Garcia what time would work for Garcia to meet and confer telephonically the next day.  On June 11, 2024, at 11:30 AM, Anfanger emailed Garcia again, stating “As I have not heard back from you and it’s almost lunch time I am relying on your refusal to meet and confer today.” Garcia responded to Anfanger by 12:00 PM that same day, stating that she was in receipt of the supplemental responses, as well as the request to meet and confer that day and would be available to meet and confer at 2:00 PM that same day.  Garcia also stated she was available later in the week if 2:00 PM did not work. At 12:41 PM, Anfanger responded and said, “I will meet and confer today at 2 PM, as you said you would prior to today. As the oppositions are due tomorrow, later in the week won’t work.”  Then, at 12:44 PM, Anfanger emailed Garcia, asking if Garcia had the authority to make binding decisions during the meet and confer call. At 1:14 PM, Anfanger emailed Garcia again, (without having yet received a response), stating, “Please respond.” At 1:44 PM, Anfanger emailed Garcia yet again, stating “Your refusal to respond is duly noted.”  

 

At 2:30 PM on June 11, 2024, after counsel for Plaintiff and Defendant telephonically met and conferred, Anfanger sent Garcia an email, declaring that Garcia had no authority to make binding decisions regarding the discovery requests and corresponding motions.   Additionally, Anfanger stated that Garcia’s motivation to file the instant motions was financial, and that Garcia never answered Anfanger’s question over the phone, requesting that Garcia explain the time frame chosen in the February 14 Letter.

 

At 2:53 PM, Garcia emailed Anfanger back and informed her that because Anfanger served the supplemental responses to the RFPs “late yesterday evening,” insufficient time was left to review and meet and confer prior to Plaintiffs’ deadline to file their oppositions to the motions. Garcia emphasized that, as mentioned during their telephone call, Plaintiffs’ supplemental responses failed to take into account the February 14 Letter written, which provided definitions and revised the temporal scope in response to the Plaintiffs’ objections and initial responses.  At 5:41 PM, Anfanger emailed Garcia back, again accusing Garcia of not having the authority to make decisions about discovery issues and that “[t]he entire undertaking was a complete fraud . . . we have no duty to accept your definitions or terms. You don’t’ compromise substantively or make them position neutral as I tried to get you to do.”  

 

On June 12, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their oppositions to the motions.  On June 18, 2024, Jaquez filed a reply brief, along with a reply to the Plaintiffs separate statements and a  supplemental declaration from Garcia. Plaintiff’s Separate Statement, and the Supplemental Declaration of Amanda Garcia (“Garcia Supp. Decl.”).   On June 21, 2024, Plaintiffs filed “Objection[s] to Defendant Jaquez’s Reply and Supporting Documents,” objecting to “new matter, supplemental Declaration, and exhibits, and supplemental Separate Statement”, requesting the Court not consider them.  In the Objections, Plaintiffs contend that Jaquez had ample notice that Plaintiffs would be providing further responses prior to filing the instant motions, but did not include such facts in the motions, and that therefore the motions reflected an ambush by Jaquez.  

 

Analysis

 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).)  Such a motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1).) In Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224, the Court of Appeal defined “good cause” as a showing that there “a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”   If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)

 

 

 

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party’s subjective intent to create burden, or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to actually respond. (See West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.)  However, where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face, no such evidence is necessary. (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.) 

 

In the Court’s view, Jaquez demonstrated good cause for the RFPs.   The Plaintiffs’ objection on relevance grounds are unmerited.  Their objections that the RFPs are unduly burdensome because the temporal scope is too wide and vague and ambiguous because of the definitions of certain terms are unmerited as well in light of the clarifications Jaquez made in the February 14 Letter to address those concerns.  The supplemental responses maintain the same objections, notwithstanding the February 14 Letter.  

 

The Court finds that it was appropriate for Jaquez to submit a supplemental declaration and reply to Plaintiffs’ separate statement.  That submission presented Jaquez’s argument as to why the supplemental responses, filed after the motions were filed, were deficient and failed to account for the February 14 Letter.   The Court also rejects the idea that the motions reflected an ambush of the Plaintiffs.  Anfanger informed Garcia in mid-February that supplemental responses would be forthcoming.  But the supplemental responses were not filed until June.  Jaquez did not jump the gun by filing the motions on March 5.

 

The notion that the Plaintiffs did not have to accept the clarifications in the February 14 Letter due to Garcia’s asserted lack of authority to bind Jaquez is misplaced.  Again, Garcia has represented Jaquez at multiple hearings. There is nothing in the record to suggest that Garcia could not bind Jaquez to agreements Garcia made in the meet and confer process.

 

The Court also notes inconsistency in Anfanger’s approach to meeting and conferring telephonically.  On February 13, 2024, Anfanger told Garcia that she (Anfanger) would refuse to meet with Garcia telephonically – everything had to be writing.  Four months later, however, on June 10, 2024, Anfanger demanded that Garcia meet with her telephonically the next day.

Plaintiffs must provide further responses to the RFPs by August 5, 2024.

  Sanctions

 

Jaquez seeks the same amount of sanctions, $6,470, in both motions, for a total of nearly $13,000.  The Court notes that $6,470 is the amount that, as noted above, Jaquez sought for both motions seeking to compel further responses to the SROGs.  Sanctions are warranted here because the Plaintiffs’ position as to the RFPs lacked substantial justification.  But the Court finds that the amount of sanctions that Jaquez is seeking as to both motions is a bit excessive.  The Court is awarding $3,225 apiece for both motions.   The sanctions must be paid by October 31, 2024.


Case Number: 22STCV13054    Hearing Date: July 11, 2024    Dept: 57

 

 

Plaintiff Mayra Sandoval dba Ilusiones (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant LA Celebrations, LLC (“Defendant”) over an alleged agreement through which Plaintiff would rent a party hall from Defendant to use on several days in 2022.  In her complaint, Plaintiff asserts claims against Defendant for (1) breach of contract; (2) intentional interference with prospective economic relations; (3) negligent interference with prospective economic relations; and (4) declaratory relief.   Pending before the Court is Defendant’s motion for judgment on the pleadings pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 438 as to all of the claims in Plaintiff’s complaint (“the Motion”).  The Court is granting the Motion with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is directed to file and serve an amended complaint by August 8, 2024.  The Court will assess the state of the pleadings at the further status conference, which is scheduled for October 24, 2024.

 

TIMELINESS OF THE MOTION

 

Plaintiff’s contention that the Motion is untimely is mistaken  The time limitation for filing a statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings under Section 438 is as follows: the motion cannot be “made” after entry of a “pretrial conference order pursuant to Section 575, or within 30 days of the date the action is initially set for trial, whichever is later, unless the court otherwise permits.”  (Section 438(e); see Tung v. Chicago Title Co. (2021) 63 Cal.App.5th 734, 757.)   The pretrial conference order mentioned in Section 438(e) refers to the case management order.  The case management order in this action was entered on January 12, 2023, following a case management conference that day.  At the case management conference, the Court initially set the action for trial on September 9, 2024.  (The trial date subsequently was moved to February 24, 2025.)  Thirty days prior to September 9, 2024 is August 9, 2024.  The Motion was filed on June 10, 2024, which is not within 30 days of the initial trial date of September 9, 2024.  Accordingly, the Motion was timely.

 

SUBSTANCE OF THE MOTION

 

1.    Breach of Contract

 

“To establish a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must plead and prove (1) the existence of the contract, (2) the plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant's breach, and (4) resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Maxwell v. Dolezal (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 93, 97-88.)   To establish the existence of a contract, a party must attach the contract to the complaint, set forth the terms of the contract in the complaint, or plead the operative effect of the contract within the complaint.  (Miles v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2015) 236 Cal.App.4th 394, 401-402.)  Here, Plaintiff did none of the above.  The complaint simply asserts there was an agreement for the use of a party hall, and that is it.  This is insufficient.  Accordingly, the Court is granting the Motion as to the breach of contract claim with leave to amend.

 

2.    Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Relations and Negligent Interference with Prospective Economic Relations

 

A common element of the complaint’s second claim for intentional interference with prospective economic relations and third claim for negligent interference with prospective economic relations is an economic relationship between the plaintiff and a third party.  (Gaab & Reese, Cal. Practice Guide: Civil Procedure Before Trial Claims and Defenses (The Rutter Group, 2023) ¶ 3:152.)  Here, for both claims, the complaint alleges that “Plaintiff had enjoyed continued success with the location and goodwill” and that Defendant “knew of these continued relationships with customers.” (Compl., ¶¶ 13-14, 19-20.) Because the complaint does not identify a specific third party with whom Plaintiff had an economic relationship, both the second and third claims fail.  Plaintiff’s opposition to the Motion mounts no defense on this point.  Accordingly, the Court is granting the Motion as to the second and third claims with leave to amend.

 

3.    Declaratory Relief

 

For the declaratory relief claim, “Plaintiff requests that this court issue a declaration mandating defendant to comply with the contracts for the events to take place at their respective dates and issue an order accordingly.” (Compl., ¶ 24.) The fourth claim fails for the reason the first claim fails: the complaint does not sufficiently plead “the contracts” on which both claims are based.  The Court thus grants the Motion as to the fourth claim with leave to amend.
