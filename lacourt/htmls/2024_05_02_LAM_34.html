Case Number: 19STCV32597    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Demurrer to Cross-Complaint of James Harden

 

Moving Party: North Beverly Park Homeowners’ Association

Resp. Party:    James Harden

 

 

North Beverly Park Homeowners’ Association’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED in part. The Demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the second cause of action for “contract liability of indemnity,” without leave to amend the Harden FACC. The Demurrer is OVERRULED as to all else.

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On September 12, 2019, George Santopietro filed his Complaint against James Harden, Jeff Wiseman, Patrick Michael, Lia Vasdekis, LA Estate Rentals LLC, and Solimar Management LLC. The causes of action arise from damage that allegedly occurred after these individuals and corporate entities rented George Santopietro’s real property.

 

On February 20, 2020, Jeff Wiseman and Solimar Management LLC filed their Cross-Complaint against George Santopietro.

 

On April 13, 2020, LA Real Estate Rentals LLC filed: (1) Answer to the Complaint; and (2) Joinder to the Cross-Complaint.

 

On July 20, 2020, the Court granted summary adjudication in favor of Solimar Management LLC and against George Santopietro as to the second cause of action (breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing) in the Complaint.

 

On April 13, 2021, Jeff Wiseman and Solimar Management LLC filed their First Amended Cross-Complaint (“Wiseman and Solimar FACC”).

 

On May 5, 2021, George Santopietro filed his Answer to the First Amended Cross-Complaint.

 

On June 30, 2023, by request of George Santopietro, the Clerk’s Office dismissed without prejudice Lia Vasdekis from the Complaint.

 

On January 19, 2024, James Harden filed: (1) Answer to the Complaint; and (2) Cross-Complaint against “Beverly Park North Homeowners Association” and “Vmw Trust.”

 

On January 22, 2024, James Harden filed his First Amended Cross-Complaint (“Harden FACC”) against “North Beverly Park Homeowners Association” and “V.M. Rosich.”

 

        On January 26, 2024, George Santopietro filed his First Amended Complaint (“Santopietro FAC”).

 

        On February 5, 2024, Patrick Michael filed his First Amended Answer to the Santopietro FAC.

 

        On February 5, 2024, Capital Investment Realty Group, LLC filed its First Amended Answer to the Santopietro FAC.

 

        On February 6, 2024, James Harden filed his First Amended Answer to the Santopietro FAC.

 

        On February 6, 2024, Jeff Wiseman and Solimar Management LLC filed their First Amended Answer to the Santopietro FAC.

 

        On February 20, 2024, the Court found related cases 19STCV32597 and 23STCV19315, and designated 19STCV32597 as the lead case.

 

        On March 25, 2024, North Beverly Park Homeowners’ Association (“the HOA”) filed its Demurrer to the Harden FACC.

 

        On March 29, 2024, James Harden filed his Opposition to the HOA’s Demurrer.

 

        On April 5, 2024, the Court sustained V.M. Rosich’s Demurrer to the Harden FACC and dismissed with prejudice V.M. Rosich from the Harden FACC.

 

        On April 25, 2024, the HOA filed its Reply in support of its Demurrer.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Legal Standard

 

“The party against whom a complaint or cross-complaint has been filed may object, by demurrer or answer as provided in Section 430.30, to the pleading on any one or more of” various grounds listed in statute. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10.)

 

“When any ground for objection to a complaint, cross-complaint, or answer appears on the face thereof, or from any matter of which the court is required to or may take judicial notice, the objection on that ground may be taken by a demurrer to the pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30, subd. (a).)

 

“A demurrer to a complaint or cross-complaint may be taken to the whole complaint or cross-complaint or to any of the causes of action stated therein.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.50, subd. (a).)

 

“In reviewing the sufficiency of a complaint against a general demurrer, we are guided by long-settled rules. We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We also consider matters which may be judicially noticed. Further, we give the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.” (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318, citations and internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

II.       Discussion

 

A.      The HOA’s Arguments on Demurrer

 

The Harden FACC contains causes of action against the HOA for negligence and contract liability or indemnity. (Harden FACC, pp. 5:24–25, 6:21–22.)

 

The HOA demurs to both of these causes of action. (Demurrer, p. 9:131–16.)

 

The HOA argues: (1) that the negligence cause of action is barred by the statute of limitations; (2) that James Harden does not state sufficient facts to constitute a negligence cause of action against the HOA; (3) that James Harden lacks standing to sue the HOA; (4) that the contract liability cause of action is barred by the statute of limitations; and (5) that James Harden does not state sufficient facts to constitute a breach of contract cause of action against the HOA. (Demurrer, pp. 5:17, 6:5–6, 7:22–23, 8:17–18.)

 

B.      Standing

 

1.      Legal Standard

 

“A party against whom a cause of action has been asserted in a complaint or cross-complaint may file a cross-complaint setting forth either or both of the following:

 

“(a) Any cause of action he has against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him. Nothing in this subdivision authorizes the filing of a cross-complaint against the plaintiff in an action commenced under Title 7 (commencing with Section 1230.010) of Part 3.

 

“(b) Any cause of action he has against a person alleged to be liable thereon, whether or not such person is already a party to the action, if the cause of action asserted in his cross-complaint (1) arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause brought against him or (2) asserts a claim, right, or interest in the property or controversy which is the subject of the cause brought against him.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10.)

 

2.      Discussion

 

Here, James Harden has alleged that the HOA acted or failed to act regarding the same occurrences that brought George Santopietro’s suit against James Harden. (Harden FACC, ¶ 41.)  James Harden has standing to file a cross-complaint against the HOA. (Code Civ. Proc., § 428.10, subd. (b).)

 

C.      Statutes of Limitations

 

“Civil actions, without exception, can only be commenced within the periods prescribed in this title, after the cause of action shall have accrued, unless where, in special cases, a different limitation is prescribed by statute.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 312.)

 

        However, the Harden Cross-Complaint is in a special category.

 

“Although ordinarily the statute of limitations will bar a cross-complaint in the same fashion as if the defendant had brought an independent action, the rule is different when the original complaint was filed before the statute of limitations on the cross-complaint had elapsed. Such a cross-complaint need only be subject-matter related to the plaintiff's complaint – i.e., arise out of the same occurrence (see §§ 426.10, 428.10) – to relate back to the date of filing the complaint for statute of limitation purposes.” (Sidney v. Super. Ct. (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 710, 714, cleaned up.)

 

Here, the Harden Cross-Complaint is subject-matter related to the occurrence in George Santopietro’s Complaint and his FAC — the parties that allegedly occurred in August 2019 on George Santopietro’s real property. Therefore, George Santopietro appears to have timely filed his pleadings, and there does not appear to be any statute of limitations issue with James Harden’s pleadings.

 

D.      Negligence Cause of Action

 

1.      Legal Standard

 

In order to state a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must allege the elements of (1) “the existence of a legal duty of care,” (2) “breach of that duty,” and (3) “proximate cause resulting in an injury.” (McIntyre v. Colonies-Pacific, LLC (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 664, 671.)

 

2.      Discussion

 

Here, James Harden alleges: (1) that the HOA owed a duty to James Harden, who was subject to the HOA’s rules; (2) that the HOA breached its rules, and thus its duty, in a variety of ways, including by not telling James Harden about its entry rules; and (3) that James Harden was directly harmed by this breach. (Harden FACC, ¶¶ 38–41.)

 

For the purposes of a demurrer, the Court must assume the truth of the pleading. These allegations are sufficient to withstand demur.

 

The Court OVERRULES the Demurrer to the first cause of action for negligence.

 

E.      Breach of Contract and/or Contractual Indemnity

 

1.      Legal Standard

 

a.       Breach of Contract

 

To state a cause of action for breach of contract, a plaintiff must be able to establish “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (Oasis W. Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)

 

If a breach of contract claim “is based on alleged breach of a written contract, the terms must be set out verbatim in the body of the complaint or a copy of the written agreement must be attached and incorporated by reference.” (Harris v. Rudin, Richman & Appel (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 299, 307.)

 

“In an action based on a written contract, a plaintiff may plead the legal effect of the contract rather than its precise language.” (Constr. Protection Servs., Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198–199.)

 

b.       Contractual Indemnity

 

The current case law regarding indemnity is best explained by the Supreme Court in Prince v. Pacific Gas & Electric Co. (2009) 45 Cal.4th 1151, 1157–58.

 

“In general, indemnity refers to ‘the obligation resting on one party to make good a loss or damage another party has incurred.’ (Rossmoor Sanitation, Inc. v. Pylon, Inc. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 622, 628.) Historically, the obligation of indemnity took three forms: (1) indemnity expressly provided for by contract (express indemnity); (2) indemnity implied from a contract not specifically mentioning indemnity (implied contractual indemnity); and (3) indemnity arising from the equities of particular circumstances (traditional equitable indemnity). (Ibid.; see PPG Industries, Inc. v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 310, 318.)

 

“Although the foregoing categories of indemnity were once regarded as distinct, we now recognize there are only two basic types of indemnity: express indemnity and equitable indemnity. (See Bay Development, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1990) 50 Cal.3d 1012, 1029–1030 & fn. 1 (Bay Development).) Though not extinguished, implied contractual indemnity is now viewed simply as ‘a form of equitable indemnity.’ (Id. at p. 1029; see E. L. White, Inc. v. City of Huntington Beach (1978) 21 Cal.3d 497, 506–507 (E. L. White).)

 

[¶]

 

“Express indemnity refers to an obligation that arises ‘by virtue of express contractual language establishing a duty in one party to save another harmless upon the occurrence of specified circumstances.’ (Bay Development, supra, 50 Cal.3d at p. 1029.) Express indemnity generally is not subject to equitable considerations or a joint legal obligation to the injured party; rather, it is enforced in accordance with the terms of the contracting parties' agreement. (Markley v. Beagle (1967) 66 Cal.2d 951, 961.)

 

[¶]

 

“Unlike express indemnity, traditional equitable indemnity requires no contractual relationship between an indemnitor and an indemnitee. Such indemnity ‘is premised on a joint legal obligation to another for damages,’ but it ‘does not invariably follow fault.’ (Western Steamship Lines, Inc. v. San Pedro Peninsula Hospital (1994) 8 Cal.4th 100, 114 (Western Steamship).) Although traditional equitable indemnity once operated to shift the entire loss upon the one bound to indemnify, the doctrine is now subject to allocation of fault principles and comparative equitable apportionment of loss. (Bay Development, supra, 50 Cal.3d at pp. 1029–1030, fn. 10; American Motorcycle Assn. v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 578, 591–598 (American Motorcycle).)”

 

2.      Discussion

 

Here, the Harden FACC pleads a cause of action against the HOA for “contract liability or indemnity.” It is unclear whether this is actually a cause of action for breach of contract or a cause of action for contractual indemnity (i.e., express indemnity). James Harden does not argue that implied indemnity (i.e., equitable indemnity) is at issue here.

 

        Just as with the Court’s April 5, 2024 ruling regarding Defendant V.M. Rosich’s Demurrer, the Court considers the contract at issue, which is titled “Vacation Rental Agreement.”

 

First, the HOA is not a signatory to the agreement.

 

Second, paragraph 17 of the Vacation Rental Agreement deals with homeowners’ associations and others planned unit developments. However, despite a box that was left to be checked if this section applied and a line that left to be written on with the name of any relevant homeowners’ association, the box was left unchecked, and no name was written on the line. (See Complaint, Exh. A, ¶ 17.) Thus, the HOA was not expressly a part of this contract.

 

Finally, as with V.M. Rosich’s Demurrer, the Court again notes that the only provision in the Vacation Rental Agreement involving indemnity is paragraph 24.C., which states:

 

“Indemnity and Hold Harmless: Occupant agrees to indemnify, defend and hold harmless Owner and Owner’s Representative from all claims, disputes, litigation, judgments, costs and attorney fees resulting from loss, damage or injury to Occupant, Occupant’s guests or licensees or their personal property.”

 

The Court agrees with the HOA that James Harden cannot sustain a cause of action for breach of contract and/or express indemnity against the HOA on this contract. The Court has not been presented with any evidence or allegation that the HOA is a signatory to and/or a beneficiary of the contract. Indeed, intentionally leaving out the HOA when paragraph 17 envisions its inclusion strongly indicates that the HOA was not intended to be included in the contract in any manner. Furthermore, the “Occupant” — whoever that was/were — is/are the ones who is/are the party/parties that could be required to indemnify and hold harmless under the indemnity clause, not the “Owner.”

 

        The Court SUSTAINS the HOA’s Demurrer to the second cause of action for “contract liability or indemnity,” without leave to amend the Harden FACC. 

 

III.     Conclusion

 

North Beverly Park Homeowners’ Association’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED in part. The Demurrer is SUSTAINED as to the second cause of action for “contract liability of indemnity,” without leave to amend the Harden FACC. The Demurrer is OVERRULED as to all else.

 


Case Number: 21STCV22304    Hearing Date: May 2, 2024    Dept: 34

SUBJECT:        Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment

 

Moving Party: Vazgen Hakopian

Resp. Party:    Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau

 

 

The Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND:

 

On June 15, 2021, Ari Chazanas filed his Complaint against Vazgen Hakopian (also known as Vazgen Hakobian) on causes of action for breach of contract, fraud, negligence, restitution pursuant to Business and Professions Code section 7031, and money had and received.

 

On October 29, 2021, Vazgen Hakopian filed his Answer to the Complaint.

 

On November 12, 2021, Vazgen Hakopian filed his Verified Cross-Complaint against Ari Chazanas, Lotus West Properties, Inc., Demetra Chazanas, and Demetra Design Corp.

 

On June 24, 2022, the Court ordered Vazgen Hakopian to provide initial discovery responses to certain form interrogatories, special interrogatories, and requests for admission.

 

On September 12, 2022, the Court stayed this matter in its entirety.

 

On July 13, 2023, the Court lifted the stay on the case in its entirety.

 

On July 17, 2023, the Court granted the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel filed by Edgar Martirosyan, who was at the time Counsel for Vazgen Hakopian.

 

On September 11, 2023, Vazgen Hakopian did not appear at the Status Conference on September 11, 2023 and he did not file a Status Conference Report for that hearing.

 

On September 29, 2023, the Court issued a Temporary Protective Order against in favor of Ari Chazanas and against Vazgen Hakopian.

 

On November 9, 2023, the Court issued a Right to Attach Order against Vazgen Hakopian. As noted by the Court, Vazgen Hakopian did not file an opposition.

 

On November 9, 2023, the Court granted Ari Chazanas’s Motion for Terminating and/or Issue, Evidence, and Monetary Sanctions for Failure to Comply with the Court’s June 24, 2022 Order (“Motion for Terminating Sanctions”). The Court struck Vazgen Hakopian’s Answer to the Complaint and dismissed with prejudice his Verified Cross-Complaint. As noted by the Court, Vazgen Hakopian did not file an opposition.

 

On December 14, 2023, by request of Ari Chazanas, the Clerk’s Office entered default on Vazgen Hakopian. On that same day, Ari Chazanas filed various documents in support of default judgment.

 

On December 15, 2023, by request of Ari Chazanas, the Clerk’s Office dismissed the doe defendants from the Complaint.

 

On December 21, 2023, the Court granted in part Ari Chazanas’s Request for Default Judgment. The Court awarded default judgment in favor of Ari Chazanas and against Vazgen Hakopian in the total amount of $201,733.11.

 

On December 22, 2023, the Court entered Judgment. On that same day, the Clerk’s Office mailed Notice of Entry of Judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure sections 664.5 and 1013a.

 

On January 4, 2023, Ari Chazanas filed Assignment of Judgment, assigning his interest in the Judgment to Modern Adjustment Bureau.

 

On March 27, 2024, Vazgen Hakopian filed his Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment (“Motion”). Vazgen Hakopian concurrently filed his Proof of Service.

 

On April 19, 2024, Ari Chazanas filed his Opposition to the Motion (“Chazanas Opposition”). In support of the Chazanas Opposition, Ari Chazanas concurrently filed: (1) Appendix of Evidence; (2) Declaration of James Kawahito; (3) Declaration of Sean Smith; (4) Declaration of Ari Chazanas; (5) Request for Judicial Notice; (6) Evidentiary Objection to Declaration of Vazgen Hakopian; (7) Proposed Order; and (8) Proof of Service.

 

On April 19, 2024, Modern Adjustment Bureau filed its Opposition to the Motion (“MAB Opposition”). In support of the MAB Opposition, Modern Adjustment Bureau concurrently filed: (1) Appendix of Evidence; (2) Declaration of James Kawahito; (3) Declaration of Sean Smith; (4) Declaration of Ari Chazanas; (5) Request for Judicial Notice; (6) Evidentiary Objection to Declaration of Vazgen Hakopian; (7) Proposed Order; and (8) Proof of Service.

 

Vazgen Hakopian did not file a reply or other response in support of his Motion.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.          Preliminary Note

 

On January 4, 2023, Ari Chazanas filed Assignment of Judgment, assigning his interest in the Judgment to Modern Adjustment Bureau.

 

        Here, Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau have filed two sets of documents in opposition to the Motion. The two sets of documents are substantively identical.

 

        It is not immediately clear to the Court why they have done this. However, as Vazgen Hakopian has not raised any objections to both Chazanas and MAB filing oppositions, the Court simply cites to both the Chazanas Opposition and the MAB Opposition were appropriate.

 

 

II.       Evidentiary Objections

 

Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau object to Vazgen Hakopian’s evidence. Specifically, they object on the bases of hearsay and lack of authentication to paragraphs 1 through 7 of the Declaration of Vazgen Hakopian and Exhibit 1.

 

The Court OVERRULES these objections.

 

 

III.     Request for Judicial Notice

 

Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau request that the Court take judicial notice of fifteen different items, some of which are items filed in this matter and others which are items filed in other matters.

 

The Court DENIES as superfluous judicial notice of items filed in this matter, including prior Orders issued by the Court in this matter. Any party that wishes to draw the Court’s attention to an item filed in this action may simply cite directly to the document by execution and filing date. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1110(d).)

 

The Court DENIES as irrelevant judicial notice to the rest of the items, which were filed in other matters. None of these items have any relevance to the issues in this matter. “Although a court may judicially notice a variety of matters (Evid. Code, § 450 et seq.), only relevant material may be noticed.” (Am. Cemwood Corp. v. Am. Home Assurance Co. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 431, 441, fn. 7, quotation omitted, italics in original.)

 

IV.      Legal Standard

 

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken. . . . No affidavit or declaration of merits shall be required of the moving party. Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney’s sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

 

 

V.         Discussion

 

A.      The Parties’ Arguments

 

Vazgen Hakopian moves the Court for relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). (Motion, pp. 1:17–22, 2:5–20.) Specifically, Vazgen Hakopian moves the Court to set aside the default and default judgment entered against him “due to extrinsic mistake, inadvertence or excusable neglect.” (Id. at p. 5:10–12.)

 

Vazgen Hakopian argues: (1) that he did not have legal counsel after June 17, 2023, which led to him not receive various items, such as notice of the hearing on the Motion for Terminating Sanctions; (2) that he was involved in a major vehicle accident on June 18, 2023, which (a) incapacitated him due to internal injuries, (b) required him to undergo surgery on September 26, 2023, and (c) required him to stay at his parents’ resident in Burbank until the end of 2023; (3) that he was unable to comply with the Court’s discovery order or show up at the November 9, 2023 hearing on the Motion for Terminating Sanctions because of his accident; and (4) that dismissal of a criminal case occurred because the City Attorney did not have sufficient evidence. (Motion, pp. 2:22–3:23.)

 

Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau disagree, arguing: (1) that Vazgen Hakopian has not presented a valid basis for setting aside a default judgment under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b); (2) that he was properly served on October 17, 2023 when he instructed Sean Smith to leave the Motion for Terminating Sanctions on his porch; (3) that he had notice of the Motion for Terminating Sanctions and could have appeared at the hearing; (4) that he has offered no medical evidence that he was incapacitated; and (5) that if the Court sets aside the default judgment, the Court should impose conditions. (Chazanas Opposition, pp. 9:13–14, 10:3–5, 10:21–22, 11:21–22, 13:17–18; MAB Opposition, pp. 9:13–14, 10:3–5, 10:21–22, 11:21–22, 13:17–18.)

 

B.      Discussion

 

Vazgen Hakopian declares that he “never received plaintiff’s motion for terminating sanctions which was heard on November 9, 2023.” (Motion, Decl. Hakopian, ¶ 6.)

 

In direct contradiction, Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau tender the Declaration of Sean Smith, a registered process server. Among other things, Sean Smith declares: (1) that on October 17, 2023, he personally served the Motion for Terminating Sanctions and concurrently-filed documents on Vazgen Hakopian at 3402 Genoa Place, Palmdale, California 93551; (2) that when he arrived at 3402 Genoa Place, he rang the ringer and received a response through a camera/intercom system; (3) that he asked to speak with “Hakobian”; (4) that the speaker identified himself as Hakobian; (5) that Sean Smith informed Hakobian that Sean Smith was attempting to serve Hakobian with legal papers; (6) that Hakobian told Sean Smith to leave the papers on the porch; (7) that Sean Smith left the envelope with the papers on the porch; and (8) that Sean Smith took pictures of the home, the intercom system, and the envelope that was left at the home. (Decl. Smith, ¶¶ 4–6.) The related Proofs of Service and pictures are also provided to the Court. (Appendix of Evidence, Exhs. D–F.)

 

There are three primary issues with Vazgen Hakopian’s arguments and evidence. The Court also notes an ancillary issue.

 

1.      Logical Issues with Hakopian’s Arguments and Evidence

 

As a preliminary issue, many of the dates he cites are incorrect. For example, his counsel was relieved of duty on July 17, 2023, not June 17, 2023. Other dates also appear to be incorrect.

 

More importantly, even if the Court were to believe Vazgen Hakopian’s claims that he was incapacitated and did not receive notice for roughly the entire second half of 2023, that still would not explain why he completely stopped participating in the litigation of this matter. After all, he knew he was being sued, and he also had his Verified Cross-Complaint against multiple cross-defendants. Given that he has not alleged he was comatose, the least he could have done was either: (1) contacted Department 34 to notify the Court of what had occurred; or (2) send a family member (such as those he was allegedly staying with) to check his home for mail. Those are logical things that one would do if they were actually unable to return home but still had obligations to care for.

 

These logical issues greatly diminish Vazgen Hakopian’s credibility.

 

2.      The Evidence Does Not Support His Arguments

 

Second, the evidence Vazgen Hakopian presents do not support his arguments.

 

Upon review of the documents Vazgen Hakopian presents, it does not appear that he was so incapacitated as to be unable to participate in this litigation. For example, the document dated September 12, 2023, notes that Vazgen Hakopian was able to be evaluated in the orthopedic office — indicating that Vazgen Hakopian was not so incapacitated that he was unable to travel to his doctor’s office. (Motion, Exh. 1, p. 1.) Further, the document states that Vazgen Hakopian was able to provide the orthopedic surgeon with information by stating that information through an English/Armenian interpreter — indicating that Vazgen Hakopian was not mentally incapacitated. (Ibid.) The document goes on to note that he “apparently is pending surgery in [omitted] abdominal region”, but it also notes that “patient was advised to perform a home exercise program with stretching” and that he “take ibuprofen and Tylenol for control of pain” — mild prescriptions that do not indicate Vazgen Hakopian was so incapacitated at this time that he could not participate in this litigation.

 

The evidence presented by Vazgen Hakopian thus cuts against his arguments, further diminishes his credibility, and actually supports the arguments made by Ari Chazanas and Modern Adjustment Bureau.

 

3.          There is No Mistake, Inadvertence, Surprise, or Excusable Neglect Here

 

Finally, the Court notes that it only has discretion to grant the relief requested pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b) when there has been “mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.”

 

None of those appear to have occurred here.

 

The Court finds Sean Smith to be credible, and it appears to the Court that the evidence taken as a whole is consistent with a finding that Vazgen Hakopian was personally served with notice of the Motion for Terminating Sanctions.

 

Furthermore, given the arguments and evidence presented, it would seem that Vazgen Hakopian did not take reasonable steps to participate in this litigation, despite his ability to do so. Rather, the evidence indicates that he took steps to avoid this litigation and intentionally stopped participating in it.

 

It must be remembered that Vazgen Hakopian had terminating sanctions issued against him because he failed to obey a June 24, 2022 Court order to comply with discovery. In other words, Vazgen Hakopian had terminating sanctions imposed because he ignored a Court Order and his obligations to participate in discovery for more than a year.

 

The Court has not been presented with any credible evidence to show Vazgen Hakopian had default judgment taken against him due to his mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. On the contrary, the Court has been provided with ample reason to believe that Vazgen Hakopian had default judgment taken against him due to his intentional avoidance of this litigation.

 

Thus, the Court lacks the discretion here to grant the relief requested.

 

4.      The Criminal Matter is Irrelevant

 

The Court briefly notes that the dismissed criminal matter is completely irrelevant to the Court’s assessment of the Motion.

 

VI.      Conclusion

 

The Motion to Set Aside Default and Default Judgment is DENIED.
