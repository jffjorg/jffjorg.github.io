DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00381    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: S27

A1 moves to compel the deposition of Cargomatic’s Chairman and CEO, Richard Gerstein.  The parties met and conferred and ultimately agreed to hold Gerstein’s deposition on 1/11/24.  A1 filed the motion “out of an abundance of caution” on 12/18/23, setting it for hearing on 1/16/24.

 

Any opposition to the motion was due on or before 1/03/24.  Cargomatic filed untimely opposition on 1/08/24.  In the opposition, Cargomatic indicates 1/11/24 is no longer an available date, and it has provided alternative dates.  Specifically, Exhibit 2 to the opposition shows Cargomatic told A1 that 1/24-26, the week of 1/29, and the week of 2/05 are all available. 

 

A1 filed a reply on 1/10/24.  A1 indicates it noticed Gerstein’s deposition for 1/24/24, but now Gerstein is going out of the country and unavailable to attend.  A1 therefore contends an order compelling him to do so is necessary.

 

CCP §2025.450(a) permits a party to move to compel a deposition if a deponent fails to appear for his noticed deposition.  In this case, A1 filed the motion to compel BEFORE Gerstein failed to appear.  The motion was therefore not ripe.  The Court understands A1’s frustration and reasons for filing the motion, but the Court cannot grant a prematurely filed motion.

 

The motion is denied.  The Court notes that trial is scheduled for 3/25/24.  The Court also notes that A1 DID serve a notice of deposition for 1/11/24 and also for 1/24/24, and it appears Gerstein failed to appear on both dates.  It is therefore possible (the Court is not ruling on this issue) that a motion to compel is now ripe.  If A1 wishes to file a motion to compel the deposition, the Court will specially set a hearing date on shortened time, in light of the approaching 3/25/24 hearing date.    

 

A1 is ordered to give notice. 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative.  If the parties do not submit on the tentative, they should arrange to appear remotely.


Case Number: 22LBCV00714    Hearing Date: January 16, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Eric Gallegos filed this action against Defendants, County of Los Angeles, SSA Terminals, LLC, and Francisco Hinojosa for damages arising out of a motor vehicle accident that allegedly caused Plaintiff to sustain whiplash injuries. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Compliance; for OSC re: Contempt; and for Monetary Sanctions

  1. History of Discovery Dispute

Plaintiff, in response to FROGs, indicated he sustained injuries to his neck and back in a prior accident in 2016, and was represented by the same attorneys in connection with that accident as he is in connection with the instant litigation. 

 

In July of 2023, Defendant asked informally for more information, and Plaintiff’s attorney indicated it would be provided.  It was not.  Defendant continued to ask for the information, which was not provided.  On 9/22/23, Defendant served a deposition subpoena for production of non-privileged records on Plaintiff’s attorney.  The subpoena sought production of non-privileged documents relating to the 2016 accident.  On 10/11/23, Plaintiff’s attorney served objections to the subpoena.  Defendant attempted to meet and confer, but the issue was not resolved. 

 

  1. Parties’ Positions

On 11/20/23, Defendant filed this motion, seeking to compel production of all non-privileged documents responsive to the subpoena.  Alternatively, Defendant seeks an order issuing and OSC re: contempt against Plaintiff’s attorney for failure to comply with the subpoena.  Additionally, Defendant seeks imposition of monetary sanctions. 

 

Plaintiff, in opposition to the motion, indicates there was a change in handling attorney in December of 2023, and the new handling attorney reviewed the file and has produced all relevant, non-privileged documents. 

 

Defendant, in reply, indicates it was never seeking privileged documents, and therefore the issue is a red herring.  Defendant argues Plaintiff should not have waited until 1/02/24, the day the opposition was due, to serve the responsive documents, and should be sanctioned for doing so.  Defendant argues the motion should be granted in full. 

 

  1. Analysis

It appears the motion is substantively moot.  Defendant argues, in reply, that the motion should be granted and an OSC re: sanctions should be issued, but Defendant does not articulate any documents that remain responsive, non-privileged, and outstanding.  The Court is therefore not inclined to grant the motion substantively at this time, as the order would be a nullity.  The Court will certainly not issue an OSC re: contempt where there is no ongoing failure to obey a subpoena.

 

It appears the real issue is whether sanctions should be imposed.  As an initial note, it appears Plaintiff’s attorney was required to file a motion to quash the subpoena and was not permitted to simply file objections.  CCP §1987.1 permits a witness (subpoenaed person) to file a motion to quash the subpoena.  §§1985.3 and 1985.6 permits a consumer or employee to simply object, without filing a motion to quash, but there is no such parallel provision for a non-consumer, non-employee witness. 

 

CCP §1987.2(a) permits the Court, in its discretion, to impose sanctions if the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification.  The Court finds Defendant has been attempting to obtain the subject information since July of 2023, and if Plaintiff was going to simply provide the information, Plaintiff should have done so without requiring lengthy meet and confer communications, the propounding of a formal subpoena, and the ultimate filing of a motion.  The Court therefore finds sanctions are justified. 

 

Defendant seeks sanctions in the amount of $2610, which is comprised of five hours to prepare the motion, three hours to prepare a reply, and one hour to attend the hearing, all at the rate of $290/hour.  The Court finds the amounts reasonable in light of the complexity of the issues presented.  Sanctions are sought and imposed against Plaintiff’s attorney, Mendez & Sanchez, and must be paid within twenty days.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Magnolia Lopez, et al. filed this action against Defendants, Long Beach Healthcare Center, LLC, et al. for elder abuse, professional negligence, wrongful death, NIED, and violation of patients’ rights arising out of Defendant’s care and treatment of Decedent, Jose Perez.

 

2.     Demurrer to Complaint

  1. Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel admits he failed to meet and confer prior to filing the pleading challenge.  The Court finds it odd that Plaintiffs did not oppose the demurrer or motion to strike, as the demurrer seeks dismissal of the entire complaint against Moving Defendant. 

 

The Court is inclined to continue the hearing on the demurrer to require Counsel to meaningfully meet and confer in connection with each issue presented by way of the demurrer and motion to strike. 

 

The Court will discuss a continued hearing date and briefing schedule for the demurrer with Counsel at the CMC, which is on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the demurrer and motion to strike.
