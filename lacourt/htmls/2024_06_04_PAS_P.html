DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV07619    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO STRIKE OR TAX COSTS


I.        INTRODUCTION

          This is a personal injury case involving a car accident. The matter was tried before a jury, which rendered a verdict in Plaintiff’s favor on December 6, 2023.  Plaintiff’s counsel filed his memorandum of costs on January 31, 2024. Defendant filed a motion to strike or tax costs on February 15, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on April 17, 2024. Defendant filed a reply on April 19, 2024. The motion is denied.

  

II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of various distances between locations, as provided by Google Map printouts. As the distance between two distinct defined locations is not reasonably subject to dispute, the Court takes judicial notice of the distances but not the veracity of the Google Maps printouts, as those can be altered at any time by anybody.

III.     LEGAL STANDARD  

          CCP Section 1032 provides that a prevailing party may recover their costs. (CCP § 1032.) CCP Section 1033.5(a) itemizes allowable costs; CCP Section 1033.5(b) itemizes costs that are not allowed. Subsection (c) provides that costs shall be necessary to the litigation and reasonable in amount, and that items of cost not mentioned in the statute are recoverable in the court’s discretion.    

IV.     ANALYSIS

          Defendant argues that the majority of the costs sought by the memorandum of costs are excessive and unreasonable. Specifically, Defendant objects to the filing fees, process server fees, service fees, copy fees, witness fees, fees for production of a poster, parking fees, mediation fees and interest fees.

          Defendant argues that the ordinary witness fees ($80.40 in total) are excessive and that mediation costs ($2,500 in total) are excessive and not recoverable under CCP Section 1033.5. However, Section 1033.5 provides for recovery of costs “required to be awarded to the prevailing party pursuant to statute as an incident to prevailing in the action.” (CCP § 1033.5(a)(16).) While section 1033.5 does not explicitly permit mediation costs, it also does not forbid them.

The Court declines to strike these costs, as they were either explicitly authorized by statute or reasonably necessary to this litigation. As to the mediation costs, the Court relies on Gibson v. Bobroff (1996) 49 Cal. App. 4th 1202, in which the Court of Appeal upheld the trial court’s ruling denying defendant’s motion to tax costs and allowing plaintiff to recover the cost of mediation. While the mediation in that case was court ordered, Gibson’s reasoning applies here.  As noted by the Gibson court, “mediation is fundamental to the conduct of litigation as it encourages the parties to settle their disputes before trial and exposes parties who fail to agree to a reasonable settlement proposal to the risk of a discretionary court determination that they should pay their opponent's share of the failed mediation.” Id. at 1209. 

          Defendant also objects to several other categories of costs on the memorandum of costs but no substantive argument is advanced in the motion. The only mention of the filing fees, process server fees, service fees, copy fees, expert witness fees, production fees, interest fees and parking fees is in the notice of motion. Nevertheless, the Court considers the argument and concludes that all the named fees are recoverable under CCP Section 1033.5. (CCP § 1033.5(a)(1), (3), (4), (8), (13), (14),(16).) The Court declines to strike these costs. Plaintiff has provided additional supporting documentation in her opposition.

          In opposition, Plaintiff argues that the relevant standard is “‘[i]f items on their face appear to be proper charges, the verified memorandum of costs is prima facie evidence of their propriety, and the burden is on the party seeking to tax costs to show they were not reasonable or necessary.’” (See Jones v. Dumrichob, (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1266.) Indeed, “Defendant's mere statements in the points and authorities accompanying its notice of motion to strike cost bill and the declaration of its counsel are insufficient to rebut the prima facie showing.” (See Rappenecker v. Sea-Land Service Inc. (1979) 93 Cal.App.3d 256, 266.) Here, there is a verified memorandum of costs, so the burden of showing that the costs sought were not reasonable or necessary was on Defendant. Defendant only presented a memorandum of points and authorities. Despite this, the Court assumes arguendo the arguments advanced by Defendant.

          In sum, the Court taxes $0. Costs are granted in the amount of $19,972.41.

V.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          The Court denies Defendant’s motion to tax costs. Costs are granted in the amount of $19,972.41.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES


I.        INTRODUCTION  

This is a personal injury case involving a car accident. The case was tried before a jury, which rendered a verdict in Plaintiff’s favor on December 6, 2023.  Plaintiff now moves for an order fixing her attorney’s fees award. The motion is granted and fees are awarded in the amount requested.

          This motion was filed April 5, 2024. Defendant filed an opposition on April 15, 2024. Plaintiffs filed a reply on April 22, 2024.

  II. EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

Plaintiff’s Evidentiary Objections to Araujo Declaration (10):

The following objections are sustained: 1 (lack of personal knowledge), 2 (lack of personal knowledge), 3 (lack of personal knowledge),

The following objections are overruled: 4 (has foundation), 5 (has foundation), 6 (has foundation), 7 (has foundation), 8 (has foundation), 9 (has foundation), 10 (has foundation)

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

          Code Civ. Proc. §1033.5(a)(1) provides for attorney fees as costs when provided for by contract, statute, or law.

CCP 2033.420 allows attorney fees to be recovered in the following circumstances:

“(a) If a party fails to admit the genuineness of any document or the truth of any matter when requested to do so under this chapter, and if the party requesting that admission thereafter proves the genuineness of that document or the truth of that matter, the party requesting the admission may move the court for an order requiring the party to whom the request was directed to pay the reasonable expenses incurred in making that proof, including reasonable attorney’s fees.

(b) The court shall make this order unless it finds any of the following:

(1) An objection to the request was sustained or a response to it was waived under Section 2033.290.

(2) The admission sought was of no substantial importance.

(3) The party failing to make the admission had reasonable ground[s] to believe that that party would prevail on the matter.

(4) There was other good reason for the failure to admit.” (CCP § 2033.420.)

 “[T]he party seeking to benefit from an exception to a general statute bears the burden to establish the exception.” (Samsky v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Ins. Co. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 517, 523, internal citations omitted.)

           Civ. Code §1717 (a) provides in part that “reasonable attorney’s fees shall be fixed by the court and shall be an element of the costs of suit.”

          PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084 contains the Supreme Court’s explanation of how fees are to be determined. It states that Civ. Code §1717, “reflects the legislative purpose ‘to establish uniform treatment of fee recoveries in actions on contracts containing attorney fee provisions.’ (Citation.) Consistent with that purpose, the trial court has broad authority to determine the amount of a reasonable fee.” (Id. at 1095.) PLCM teaches that fees are to be determined by calculating the “‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.” (Id.)  

IV.     ANALYSIS

A. The Fees Were Incurred due to the Denial of Requests for Admissions

Plaintiff argues that Defendant failed to admit the genuineness of Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions. The Court agrees. (See Harshman Decl. Exh. D.) Plaintiff then prevailed at trial, with the jury finding that Defendant caused Plaintiff’s injuries. (See 1/16/24 Minute Order.)

In opposition, Defendant argues that the motion is untimely. The Court disagrees. (See Reply p. 2: 14-24.) Defendant also argues that Plaintiffs failed to meet their burden of proof by not pointing to specific evidence. However, this is not required under CCP Section 2033.420 nor does Defendant cite any other authority containing this requirement.

Defendant also argues that the admissions sought were not of substantial importance. (CCP § 2033.420(b)(2).) The Court disagrees, as the RFAs request that Defendant admit that Plaintiffs were injured in the accident and that Defendant caused the accident and Plaintiff’s subsequent injuries. This is the essence of this case. The Court disagrees with Defendant that these admissions are not of substantial importance.

Defendant argues that because its responses to the RFAs were unverified, they amount to no response at all, citing Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal.App.3rd 632, 635-636. This may well be true; however, no response is the functional equivalent to denial. Whether Defendant did not respond or denied the admission still functions as a failure to admit the genuineness of the admissions. (CCP § 2033.420.)

Lastly, Defendant argues that it reasonably believed it would prevail at trial. (CCP § 2033.420(b)(3).) In support, Defendant cites Universal Home Improvement, Inc. v. Robertson (2020) 51 Cal.App.5th 116, 130-132 for the proposition that a reasonable belief may be formed if supported by expert witnesses and evidence. Defendant then cites that at trial, it put on expert witnesses and presented evidence of the damage done to the vehicles. However, Defendant fails to meet its burden demonstrating that it relied on such evidence when denying or failing to respond to the Requests for Admissions. The evidence Defendant put on at trial has no clear bearing on this motion, as this exception pertains to whether Defendant had a reasonable belief it would prevail at trial at the time it failed to admit the genuineness of the admissions. (See Reply p. 5: 9-16.)

In support of her position, Plaintiff cites Grace v. Mansourian (2015) 240 Cal. App. 4th 523, which bears some similarity to the present case.  In Grace, an automobile accident case, the Court of Appeal found that the trial court erred in denying Plaintiff’s request for attorney’s fees after Plaintiff prevailed at trial.  The Court of Appeal found that Defendant’s failure to admit certain requests for admission prior to trial justified the imposition of an attorney’s fees award.  The Court finds Grace’s reasoning persuasive.

B. Reasonableness of Fees

The analysis of reasonableness of attorney fee awards considers “the nature of the litigation, its difficulty, the amount involved, the skill required and skill employed in handling the litigation, the attention given, the success of the attorney’s efforts, his learning, his age, and his experience in the particular type of work demanded…” (Berry v. Chaplin (1946) 74 Cal.App.2d 669, 678-679, citations omitted.) There is no requirement that billing records be provided to a trial court in connection with a motion for fees pursuant to contract. The declaration of counsel as to the hours spent on this litigation and the hourly rates charged for such time is all that is required. “[T]here is no legal requirement that an attorney supply billing statements to support a claim for attorney fees.” (Mardirossian & Associates, Inc. v. Ersoff (2007) 135 Cal.App.4th 257, 269.) “[T]here is no legal requirement that such statements be offered in evidence. An attorney’s testimony as to the number of hours worked is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records.” (Steiny & Co., Inc. v. California Electric Supply Co. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 285, 293.)

The declarations of Plaintiff’s counsels, David M. Shaby and R. Christopher Harshman, provide a sufficient basis for the fee request. Shaby and Harshman provide a table of the time spent on this case after the serving of the Requests for Admissions. (Shaby Decl. ¶ 3, Harshman Decl. ¶ 6.)

          The Court has examined the evidence presented by Plaintiffs and makes the following findings: the rates requested are reasonable and 86.5 hours is a reasonable amount of time spent, given the length of this case (5 years). As such, the Court GRANTS the motion for attorney fees in the amount of $44,674.00 ($31,625 +$13,050).

           The Court awards $44,674.00 in attorney fees.

V.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

          Plaintiff’s motion for attorney fees is granted. The Court awards attorney’s fees in the amount of $44,674.00.

Plaintiffs’ counsel to give notice of this order.

Case Number: 23AHCV02644    Hearing Date: June 4, 2024    Dept: P

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

I.        INTRODUCTION

           This case involves a breach of contract action arising from a real estate transaction. Plaintiffs Robert and Vergine Martirossian allege that Defendants Unikex LLC, Xiaolin Li, Nartin Inc., Universal Realty Inc., Xiao Zhang and Hudson Insurance Co. breached the real estate purchase agreement between them by fraudulently representing that the property had been recently repaired, was in good condition and that the systems in the housing (plumbing, HVAC etc.) functioned properly. The complaint contains eleven causes of action: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Violation of Civil Code, (3) Fraudulent Concealment, (4) Negligent Misrepresentation, (5) Negligence, (6) Breach of Implied Warranty, (7) Strict Liability, (8) Recovery of Contractor’s State License Bond, (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, (10) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress, and (11) Unfair Competition.

Defendant Unikex filed this motion on January 18, 2024. Plaintiff filed an opposition on May 21, 2024.

The motion is granted.

 

           II. REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

           Plaintiffs request judicial notice of all documents filed in this case thus far and certain acts of the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office.

Though it is not necessary to take judicial notice of papers already in the court file, the request is granted.  (See Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).) (Evid. Code § 453; Scott v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 743, 754-755.) To the extent that these documents are official acts of the Los Angeles County Recorder’s Office, judicial notice is granted. (EC § 452(c).)

           The Court takes judicial notice of Exhibits 1-3.

 

 

 

III.     LEGAL STANDARD

Code Civ. Proc. §1281 provides: “A written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter existing is valid, enforceable, and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.” (CCP § 1281.)

Paraphrased, Code Civ. Proc. §1281.2 provides that upon petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate and a party’s refusal to submit to arbitration, the court shall order the parties to arbitrate the controversy if it determines (1) that an agreement exists, (2) unless it determines that the right to arbitrate has been waived, that grounds exist for revocation, or that a party to the agreement is also party to a pending litigation arising out of the same facts and there exists a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of fact or law. (CCP § 1281.2.)

 

IV.     ANALYSIS

           A Written Agreement to Arbitrate Exists

When a petition to compel arbitration is “filed and accompanied by prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy, the court itself determines whether the agreement exists and, if any defense to its enforcement is raised, whether it is enforceable.” (Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394, 413 (Rosenthal).)  Defendant bears the burden of establishing the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate.

In this case, Defendant Unikex offers as evidence the declaration of Aren Debrasreghian, its attorney, who attaches as Exhibit 1 copies of letters sent between the parties which allegedly reference the arbitration agreement. These documents reference scheduling mediation. It is unclear to the Court whether parties voluntarily agreed between themselves to schedule mediation or whether it was pursuant to an agreement. These letters are not prima facie evidence of an arbitration agreement.

However, the motion also incorporates language of the Residential Purchase Agreement. (Exh. A to Complaint.) Plaintiffs have also requested the Court take judicial notice of this document.

The agreement consists of ten pages. At the bottom of the eighth page, in bolded words appears, “ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES. The Parties agree that any dispute or claim in Law or equity arising between them out of this Agreement or any resulting transaction, which is not settled through mediation, shall be decided by neutral, binding arbitration.” Underneath these words are separate signature lines signed by both Plaintiff Robert and Defendant Xiaolin Li. Xiaolin is the owner of Defendant Unikex LLC.

Based on this record, the Court concludes that Plaintiffs agreed to the arbitration agreement.

The language of the arbitration provision is broad. It covers, “any dispute or claim in Law or equity arising between them out of this Agreement or any resulting transaction”. After that, there is a notice in bold and capitalized font “NOTICE: BY INITIALING IN THE SPACE BELOW YOU ARE AGREEING TO HAVE ANY DISPUTE ARISING OUT OF THE MATTERS INCLUDED IN THE ARBITRATION OF DISPUTES' PROVISION DECIDED BY NEUTRAL ARBITRATION AS PROVIDED BY CALIFORNIA LAW AND YOU ARE GIVING UP ANY RIGHTS YOU MIGHT POSSESS TO HAVE THE DISPUTE LITIGATED IN A COURT OR JURY TRIAL.”

This language is broad enough to cover the claims alleged in this lawsuit, as the claims arise out of the Purchase Agreement.   

In opposition, Plaintiffs urge that there is no arbitration provision between it and Defendant Unikex. However, Defendant Xiaolin Li is the sole owner of Defendant Unikex and signed the arbitration agreement. The Court does not agree that Li did so in his individual capacity, as the Agreement provides that the sale is between Robert and Unikex. (Complaint Exh. A p. 1.) Li signed on behalf of his company.

 

V.       CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant has presented evidence establishing the existence of a signed agreement to arbitrate. That agreement encompasses the claims alleged by Plaintiffs. The agreement was signed, and the claims are not precluded from going to arbitration. Consequently, the motion is granted, and this action is stayed. A post-arbitration status conference is set for December 4, 2024 at 8:30 a.m.  The parties are ordered to file a joint Status Report at least five (5) days before the  status conference.

Defendant Unikex LLC is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
