DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV03737    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPT:

39

HEARING DATE:

October 10, 2024

CASE NUMBER:

21STCV03737, related to 21STCV03623 and 21STCV02841

MOTION: 

Motion for Leave to File Cross-Complaint

MOVING PARTY:

Defendants Julian Ledesma, Sweatheory LLC, Sweatheory Wellness, LLC, and the Sweatheory Group, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY:

None

 

MOTION

Plaintiffs Gabriel Capra and Kristin Wright (“Plaintiffs”) sued Defendants Julian Ledesma, Sweatheory LLC, Sweatheory Wellness, LLC, and the Sweatheory Group, LLC (“Defendants”) because Plaintiffs contend Defendants leased a unit in which Plaintiffs were residing and converted it to a business location.  Along with third-party JPL Ventures, LLC, Defendants seek leave to file a cross-complaint against Paragon Resources, Inc. and Royfield, Inc. (“Cross-Defendants”).  Cross-Defendants are parties to this action, and have already cross-complained Christopher Kantrowitz and Media Gobbler Inc. 

DISCUSSION

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 428.10, a party against whom a cause of action is brought may file a cross-complaint to assert “[a]ny cause of action he has against a person alleged to be liable thereon, whether or not such person is already a party to the action, if the cause of action asserted in his cross-complaint (1) arises out of the same transaction, occurrence, or series of transactions or occurrences as the cause brought against him or (2) asserts a claim, right, or interest in the property or controversy which is the subject of the cause brought against him.”  (Code Civ. Proc., §428.10, subd. (b).)  A party must obtain leave of court to file a cross-complaint if the party does not file the cross-complaint at the same time as the party files an answer.  The Court may grant leave to file a cross-complaint in the interests of justice at any time during the course of the action.  (Code Civ. Proc., §428.10, subd. (c).) 

Defendants seek leave to assert a cross-complaint against Cross-Defendants for breach of contract, indemnity, and contribution.  Defendants contend Defendants leased the premises at issue from Cross-Defendants, without knowledge of Plaintiffs’ presence on the premises.  Defendants did not file this cross-complaint in connection with their initial appearance.  However, the Court considers it in the interest of justice to resolve all claims regarding the dispute over Plaintiffs’ right to the premises in this action.  Accordingly, the Court grants the motion. 

Defendants are ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of same.

 

Case Number: 22STCV07354    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: 39

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

October 10, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22STCV07354

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment, or, in the Alternative, Summary Adjudication

MOVING PARTY

Defendant State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Ricky Montes

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Rick Montes (“Plaintiff”) sued Defendant State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Company (“Defendant”) for disability discrimination, contending Defendant did not accommodate his disability.  Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s operative first amended complaint, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of the causes of action therein.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

In its order of February 20, 2024, the Court granted Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the second and third causes of action and the sixth cause of action to the extent it relied on a theory of harassment, and the Court granted Plaintiff’s request to dismiss the eighth cause of action.  The Court continued the motion with respect to the remaining causes of action and ordered the parties to file supplemental briefing, which the Court now considers.  (See February 20, 2024 Minute Order.)

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

 

EVIDENCE

            Defendant’s objections to Plaintiff’s evidence are overruled.  The objections go to the weight, not the admissibility, of Plaintiff’s evidence.

DISCUSSION

First, Fourth, and Fifth Causes of Action for Disability Discrimination      

Plaintiff asserts causes of action for discrimination under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA).  To state a claim for discrimination under FEHA, the employee must demonstrate “(1) the employee's membership in a classification protected by the statute; (2) discriminatory animus on the part of the employer toward members of that classification; (3) an action by the employer adverse to the employee's interests; (4) a causal link between the discriminatory animus and the adverse action; (5) damage to the employee; and (6) a causal link between the adverse action and the damage.”  (Mamou v. Trendwest Resorts, Inc. (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 686, 713.)  “A defending employer seeking summary judgment in a discrimination case may meet its burden by showing that one or more of these prima facie elements is lacking, or that the adverse employment action was based on legitimate nondiscriminatory factors.  [¶]  If the employer has met its burden by showing a legitimate reason for its conduct, the employee must demonstrate a triable issue by producing substantial evidence that the employer's stated reasons were untrue or pretextual, or that the employer acted with a discriminatory animus, such that a reasonable trier of fact could conclude that the employer engaged in intentional discrimination or other unlawful action.”  (Cucuzza v. City of Santa Clara (2002) 104 Cal.App.4th 1031, 1038, internal citation omitted.)  An employer is likewise not liable for discrimination “if the employer proves it would have fired the employee anyway for lawful reasons.”  (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 234.)

Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action is for failure to provide reasonable accommodations. As Plaintiff’s employer, Defendant had to reasonably accommodate any of Plaintiff’s known disabilities to enable Plaintiff to perform the essential functions of Plaintiff’s job, unless doing so would produce undue hardship to Defendant’s operations. (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (m); 2 Cal. Code Regs., § 11068, subd. (a).)

Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is for failure to engage in a good faith interactive process. An employer must engage in a “timely, good faith interactive process . . . in response to a request for reasonable accommodation by an employee or applicant with a known physical or mental disability or known medical condition.” (Gov. Code, § 12940, subd. (n); see 2 Cal. Code Regs, tit. 2, § 11069, subd. (a).)

Defendant asserts Plaintiff indicated at his deposition he never informed Defendant when he could return to work.  (Declaration of Jennifer A. Goldberg, Exhibit A, pp. 50, 139.)  Plaintiff also testified his supervisor, Nicole Barker (“Barker”), told him on the day Plaintiff first sought treatment for his injury that his work was unacceptable.  (Goldberg Decl., Exhibit A, p. 261.) 

Defendant also advances Barker’s deposition testimony, wherein she testified when Plaintiff initially requested time off, Barker denied the request because she did not know Plaintiff was experiencing medical issues (Goldberg Decl., Exhibit B, pp. 15-17); after Plaintiff went on leave for his medical issues, Plaintiff never requested any accommodations that could enable him to return to work (id., Exhibit B, p. 73);  if Plaintiff could not sit, stand, or drive for more than four to six hours per day, he could not meet the essential functions of his job (id., Exhibit B, p. 168); and Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment because Plaintiff would not provide Defendant with any accommodation request beyond an indefinite request for leave (id., Exhibit B, p. 186.) 

Defendant also supports its contentions with deposition testimony from Barker’s supervisor, John Hubert (“Hubert”), who testified Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment because Plaintiff was not meeting job expectations, he did not return to work, and he did not indicate when he could return to work.  (Goldberg Decl., Exhibit C, pp. 71-72.)  Hubert further testified Plaintiff could not perform the essential functions of his job with the limitations he had.  (Id., Exhibit C, p. 109.) 

Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing Defendant did not discriminate against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s disability and that Defendant terminated Plaintiff’s employment for a legitimate reason.  Because Defendant was aware of Plaintiff’s disability, Defendant had the obligation to propose accommodations for it, even if Plaintiff did not request any accommodations.  (See Prilliman v. United Air Lines, Inc. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 935, 949-950.)  Defendant fails to advance any evidence to show it proposed accommodations to Plaintiff prior to terminating his employment.  While Defendant argues it was Defendant’s policy to receive proposed accommodations from its employees, Defendant cites no authority for the proposition that its policies altered its obligations under law. 

Furthermore, in opposition, Plaintiff advances evidence that, as of June 23, 2021, an orthopedic surgeon evaluated Plaintiff and stated he could return to work with restrictions only on heavy lifting, pushing, and pulling, as well as forward bending, stooping, twisting, squatting, or turning.  (Plaintiff’s Compendium of Evidence (Compendium), Exhibit 9, Exhibit 96, pp. 48-49, Exhibit 104, p. 49.)  While Defendant argues in reply Defendant was unaware of this evaluation when it terminated Plaintiff’s employment on that date, the evaluation is, nonetheless, relevant.  Had Defendant proposed accommodations to Plaintiff to facilitate Plaintiff’s return to work, Defendant may have been aware of this evaluation prior to Plaintiff’s termination. 

Plaintiff also provided evidence Defendant encouraged employees to work remotely during the COVID-19 pandemic and, therefore, remote work could have been an accommodation afforded to Plaintiff, given his disability.  (Compendium, Exhibits 11-13.)  Plaintiff also offers communications which reflect Defendant’s position Plaintiff faked his injury because of a poor performance review.  (Compendium, Exhibits 15-16.)  Even if Defendant had satisfied its burden of showing Plaintiff could not prevail on his claims for disability discrimination, Defendant’s failure to provide Plaintiff with reasonable accommodations for his disability and failure to engage in a good faith interactive process have raised triable issues of material fact requiring this Court to deny Defendant’s motion for summary judgment.

Sixth Cause of Action: Failure to Prevent Discrimination

Plaintiff’s claim for failure to prevent discrimination is derivative of his general claims for discrimination. Where triable issues of material fact exist as to discrimination that preclude summary adjudication, those issues likewise preclude summary adjudication on claims for failure to prevent discrimination.  (Nazir v. United Airlines, Inc. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 243, 288.)  As set forth above, triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Defendant discriminated against Plaintiff.

Seventh Cause of Action: Retaliation

To prevail on a claim for retaliation, Plaintiff must establish “by a preponderance of the evidence, that retaliation for an employee's protected activities was a contributing factor in a contested employment action. . . . Once the plaintiff has made the required showing, the burden shifts to the employer to demonstrate, by clear and convincing evidence, that it would have taken the action in question for legitimate, independent reasons even had the plaintiff not engaged in protected activity.” (Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022) 12 Cal.5th 703, 718.)

As set forth above, Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing Defendant terminated Plaintiff for a legitimate reason and did not terminate Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s disability.  As such, triable issues of material fact exist as to whether Defendant retaliated against Plaintiff because of Plaintiff’s disability. 

Ninth Cause of Action: Wrongful Termination in Violation of Public Policy

California law provides “a tort remedy when an employer's discharge of an employee contravenes the dictates of public policy.” (Tameny v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (1980) 27 Cal.3d 167, 177.)  As set forth above, Defendant fails to meet its burden of showing it terminated Plaintiff for a legitimate reason and not because of his disability.  As such, Defendant fails to satisfy its burden of demonstrating Plaintiff cannot prevail on his claim for wrongful termination in violation of public policy.

Punitive Damages

Defendant also moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s claim for punitive damages.  As Plaintiff advances evidence Defendant “concoct[ed] a reason for termination” after terminating Plaintiff based on his medical condition, Plaintiff has raised triable issues as to her claim for punitive damages.  (Colucci v. T-Mobile USA, Inc. (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 442, 455.) 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s motion for summary adjudication of the remaining issues is denied.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the Court’s order and to file proof of service of same.

MOTION

            Defendant Lithia Motors Support Services, Inc. (“Defendant”) moves for a protective order that Defendant need not respond to inspection demands Plaintiff Kenneth Oleesky (“Plaintiff”) served on Defendant, which require Defendant to produce its electronic devices for inspection.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

ANALYSIS

Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.020 permits the court to restrict a party’s right to limit the scope of discovery if it determines the method of discovery is unduly burdensome, expensive, or intrusive.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020.)  Plaintiff served inspection demands on Defendant in which Plaintiff seeks to inspect all computers or other electronic devices with electronically stored information about Plaintiff or any other issues from this litigation that Defendant’s employees Gina Morse, Diana Rusnick, Tiani Langston, Angela Taber, and Mike Murray used.

Defendant argues the discovery is unduly burdensome.  To demonstrate that responding to discovery requests would be so burdensome as to be oppressive, the responding party must advance evidence of “the quantum of work required . . . ,” along with evidence to show “that the ultimate effect of the burden is incommensurate with the result sought.”  Such evidence would include evidence of “the total man hours required to accomplish the task.” (West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) 

Defendant advances the declaration of its counsel, Matthew Noel (“Noel”).  Noel states the inspection demands would require him to travel to Medford, Oregon to witness the inspection.  (Declaration of Matthew Noel, ¶ 15.)  The Court is not convinced the inspection demands require Defendant’s counsel, who has not indicated he is an expert on computer forensics, to personally witness the inspection.  As such, Defendant has not presented evidence to show the burden of the inspection demands is unwarranted.  Further, Plaintiff has offered to stipulate to another location for the inspection to minimize travel expenses.  (Opposition to Motion for Protective Order, pp. 9-10.)  Defendant has not suggested an alternative location.  Thus, Defendant’s contention in this regard fails as unsupported.

Defendant also argues the discovery is cumulative, because Plaintiff has already taken depositions.  Plaintiff may pursue discovery, even if the discovery seeks information that is duplicative of other information already obtained.  (Tbg Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)  Plaintiff’s right to use inspection demands “is in addition to and exclusive of the right to take the deposition[s], and either procedure may be resorted to before or after the other.”  (Greyhound Corp. v. Sup. Ct. In and For Merced County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 373.)  Defendant has not shown Plaintiff has already inspected the electronic devices at issue.  As such, Plaintiff’s use of other discovery methods does not render the inspection demands unduly cumulative.

Likewise, Defendant’s argument the inspection demands are overbroad and harassing is unfounded.  “When discovery requests are grossly overbroad on their face, and hence do not appear reasonably related to a legitimate discovery need, a reasonable inference can be drawn of an intent to harass and improperly burden.”  (Obregon v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)  The inspection demands at issue are reasonably related to this litigation, as they are limited to percipient witnesses involved with this matter and facts related to it.  The inspection demands are not overbroad or harassing.

Defendant also speculates Plaintiff seeks this discovery to find spoliation of evidence.  The Court cannot rule on potential future issues in this litigation.  Further, actionable spoliation occurs only in limited circumstances.  “Spoliation is the destruction or significant alteration of evidence, or the failure to preserve property for another's use as evidence, in pending or future litigation.”  (Hernandez v. Garcetti (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 675, 680.)  Under California law, a party cannot destroy evidence “in response to a discovery request after litigation has commenced . . . ,” or “in anticipation of a discovery request.”  (Cedars-Sinai Medical Center v. Superior Court (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, 12.)  Unless Plaintiff discovers Defendant destroyed evidence in response to Plaintiff’s discovery requests or in anticipation of discovery requests, Plaintiff will be unable to raise spoliation as an issue in this case.  Accordingly, the motion is denied.

Defendant is to give notice of this order and to file proof of service of same.
