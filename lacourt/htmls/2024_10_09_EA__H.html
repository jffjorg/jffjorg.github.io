DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV02121    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: H

Alvarez v. Chavarria, et al., Case No. 23PSCV02121

 ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO COMPEL

Defendant Denis Heriberto Chavarria’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to provide further, Code-compliant responses within 20 calendar days from the date of the notice of ruling. Sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $935.00 and are payable within 30 calendar days from the date of the notice of ruling.

Background

Plaintiff Lucio Alvarez (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he sustained injuries and damages in a March 2, 2023 motor vehicle accident.

On December 1, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Denis Heriberto Chavarria (“Chavarria”), One Stop Pool Pros #1, Inc. (erroneously sued as One Stop Pool Pros, Inc.) (“One Stop”), and Does 1-50 for (1) motor vehicle negligence, (2) general negligence, (3) negligent entrustment, (4) respondeat superior, and (5) negligent hiring/representation.

Pending before the Court is Chavarria’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted.

Legal Standard

“[T]he propounding party may move for an order compelling a further response if the propounding party deems that . . . (1) An answer to a particular interrogatory is evasive or incomplete[,] (2) An exercise of the option to produce documents under Section 2030.230 is unwarranted or the required specification of those documents is inadequate[, and/or that] (3) An objection to an interrogatory is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (a).)

The motion must include a meet and confer declaration which “state[s] facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2030.300, subd. (b)(1).) “In lieu of a separate statement required under the California Rules of Court, the court may allow the moving party to submit a concise outline of the discovery request and each response in dispute.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (b)(2).)

Notice of the motion must be provided “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (c).) The responding party has the burden of justifying the objections to the requests. (Coy v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220-221.)

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction. . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a further response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.300, subd. (d).)

Discussion

Chavarria moves the court for an order compelling Plaintiff’s further, verified responses to Chavarria’s Special Interrogatories, Set No. One (i.e., Nos. 34, 37, 38 and 47-49). Chavarria also seeks monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel of record in the amount of $3,660.00.

Chavarria’s counsel Rodrigo Bozoghlian (“Bozoghlian”) represents as follows:

On May 3, 2024, Chavarria served the subject discovery. (Bozoghlian Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) May 31, 2024, Plaintiff served his responses to the subject discovery. (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. 2.) On June 2, 2024, Bozoghlian sent a meet and confer letter to Plaintiff’s counsel, requesting further responses by June 13, 2024. (Id., ¶ 5, Exh. 3.) On June 12, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel declined to provide further responses. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. 4.) Chavarria requested, and obtained, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) date, which proceeded on July 29, 2024; however, Plaintiff’s counsel left the IDC before the Court could reach the issues raised herein. (Id., ¶¶ 7-9, Exhs. 5 and 6.) The Court advised Chavarria to proceed with filing the instant motion. (Id., ¶ 9.)

The interrogatories at issue are Nos. 34, 37, 38, and 47-49. These interrogatories seek the following information:

No. 34 asks Plaintiff to identify each and every facility where Plaintiff sought treatment in the ten years before the subject accident to the same body parts Plaintiff claims he injured in the subject accident.

No. 37 asks Plaintiff to identify all facts for any and all motor vehicle accidents he was involved in during the ten years preceding the subject accident. No. 38 asks Plaintiff to identify all documents relating to this/these accident(s).

No. 47 asks Plaintiff to identify by date each and every occasion where he has been away from his residence on vacation since the subject accident. No. 48 asks Plaintiff to identify every place he visited for each occasion set forth in No. 47. No. 49 asks Plaintiff to identify every person that accompanied him to every place he visited identified in No. 48.

Plaintiff interposed the same response to each of the aforesaid interrogatories, as follows: “Objection: Vague, ambiguous, overbroad, invasion of privacy, irrelevant and not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery [of] admissible evidence.”

Plaintiff’s opposition focuses on his privacy and overbreadth objections. The Court determines that neither objection is well-taken.

No. 34 is limited temporally to ten years and by subject matter to the same body parts Plaintiff claimed he injured in the subject accident. Further, while plaintiffs are not obligated to sacrifice all privacy to seek redress for a specific physical, mental, or emotional injury, they “may not withhold information which relates to any physical or mental condition which they have put in issue by bringing this lawsuit.” (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 864.) A further response is warranted.

Nos. 37 and 38 are limited temporally to ten years, while Nos. 47-49 are limited temporally to the time subsequent to the subject accident. “The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.) “The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and important countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy. A court must then balance these competing considerations.” (Id.) The Court need not move on to a balancing of interests because the threshold requirements are simply absent here.

The Court grants the motion in full. Plaintiff is ordered to provide further, Code-compliant responses within 20 calendar days from the date of the notice of ruling.

Turning to Chavarria’s request for sanctions, Chavarria seeks monetary sanctions against Plaintiff and his counsel of record in the amount of $3,660.00 [calculated as follows: 5 hours preparing motion, plus 3 hours reviewing opposition and preparing reply, plus 1 hour preparing for and attending hearing at $400.00/hour, plus $60.00 filing fee].

Utilizing a Lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the pending motion is $935.00 (i.e., 2.5 hours attorney time at $350.00/hour, plus $60.00 filing fee). Sanctions are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling. 

Case Number: 24PSCV00128    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: H

Pierson v. MJB Partners LLC, et al., Case No. 24PSCV00128

ORDER ON DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRERS AND MOTION TO STRIKE

1.       Defendant Gary G. Sandhu, M.D.’s (erroneously sued and served herein as “Gary Sandu, M.D.”) Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

2.       Defendant Gary G. Sandhu, M.D.’s (erroneously sued and served herein as “Gary Sandu, M.D.”) Motion to Strike Portions of the First Amended Complaint is GRANTED. 

3.       Defendant MJB Partners, LLC dba Pomona Vista Care Center’s Demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED. The Court will hear from Plaintiffs’ counsel as to whether leave to amend is requested, and as to which cause(s) of action, and if so, will require an offer of proof.


I.        Background 

Plaintiffs Doris Pierson, Joseph E. Pierson, III, and Ronald Pierson, individually and as successors-in-interest to decedent, Joseph E. Pierson, II (“Plaintiffs”) allege that on May 1, 2023, a couple months after being admitted to Pomona Valley Vista Care Center, Joseph E. Pierson, II (“Pierson”) Pierson “suddenly stopped breathing” during his lunch and suffered preventable death. 

On June 26, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting causes of action against MJB Partners, LLC dba Pomona Vista Care Center (“MJB Partners”), Kyle Selvig, Nancy Esperanza, Gary G. Sandhu, M.D. (erroneously sued and served herein as “Gary Sandu, M.D.”) (“Sandhu”), and Does 1-100 for (1) elder abuse and neglect, (2) negligence based on medical malpractice, (3) violation of patient rights, and (4) wrongful death. 

Pending before the Court are: (1) Sandhu’s demurrer to the Operative Complaint, (2) Sandhu’s motion to strike portions of the Operative Complaint, and (3) MJB Partners’ demurrer to the Operative Complaint. 

II.      Legal Standard

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) 

“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436, “the court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” The grounds for a motion to strike must “appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437.) 

“An immaterial allegation in a pleading is any of the following:

(1) An allegation that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense.

(2) An allegation that is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense.

(3) A demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint or cross-complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subd. (b).)

“An ‘immaterial allegation’ means ‘irrelevant matter’ as that term is used in Section 436.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 431.10, subd. (c).)

 

III.     Discussion

A.       Sandhu’s Demurrer

Sandhu demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e), to the second cause of action (i.e., for Negligence Based Upon Medical Malpractice) in the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. 

“[I]n any medical malpractice action, the plaintiff must establish: (1) the duty of the professional to use such skill, prudence, and diligence as other members of his profession commonly possess and exercise; (2) a breach of that duty; (3) a proximate causal connection between the negligent conduct and the resulting injury; and (4) actual loss or damage resulting from the professional's negligence.” (Gami v. Mulliken Medical Center (1993) 18 Cal.App.4th 870, 877 [quotations and citation omitted].) “A physician’s duty of care to a patient does not arise until a physician-patient relationship is established.” (Alexander v. Scripps Memorial Hospital La Jolla (2018) 23 Cal.App.5th 206, 235.) 

Sandhu argues that Plaintiffs cannot establish that Sandhu owed them a duty of care. Sandhu, however, misconstrues this cause of action as having been brought by Plaintiffs individually, rather than as a survival cause of action as decedent’s successors-in-interest. This is incorrect. In Paragraph 1 of the FAC, the term “Plaintiffs” refers to “Plaintiffs, DORIS PIERSON, JOSEPH E. PIERSON, III and RONAL PIERSON, individually and as Successors In Interest to Decedent, JOSEPH E. PIERSON, II.” (Emphasis added). The second cause of action is asserted by “Plaintiffs.” “Plaintiffs” assert therein that “Defendants, and each of them, owed a duty to Decedent. . .” (FAC, ¶ 67 [emphasis added]; see also, ¶¶ 62 [At all times herein, Defendant[]. . . SANDHU [had] . . . an ongoing duty to provide timely medical care, surgery and  daily custodial care as necessary to prevent patients from suffering injury and death as hereinafter alleged, so patients such as JOSEPH E. PIERSON, II would not suffer needless injury and death. . .”] and 66 [Defendant[ ] . . . SANDHU breached [his] aforesaid duties to Decedent. . .” [emphasis added].) They are not contending that Sandhu owed a duty to them directly. 

Sandhu’s demurrer is overruled.

 

B.       Sandhu’s Motion to Strike 

Sandhu moves the Court, per Code of Civil Procedure sections 435-437, for an order striking out the following portions of the Operative Complaint:

1.       “. . . custodial services . . .” (¶ 61);

2.       “. . . custodial . . . care . . . daily custodial care . . .” (¶ 62); and

3.       “. . . care needs. . .” (¶ 69). 

Sandhu requests that the foregoing phrases be stricken, on the basis that Plaintiffs have not asserted an elder abuse cause of action against him and that the elements for a medical malpractice cause of action, which is asserted against him, does not require allegations of “custodial services,” “daily custodial care” and/or “care needs.” 

Sandhu’s motion is granted. The elements of a medical malpractice cause of action, as set forth in the Court’s analysis on Sandhu’s demurrer, do not require allegations of “custodial services,” “daily custodial care,” and/or “care needs.” Plaintiffs argue that “[r]ead in context, the words ‘custodial services’ apply to Pomona Vista, not Dr. Sandhu[,] because Plaintiffs have not alleged a cause of action for elder abuse against him.” (Opp. 3:17-18.) These phrases are more appropriately contained in the first cause of action for elder abuse and neglect, which is asserted against other defendants.

 

C.       MJB Partners’ Demurrer

MJB Partners demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e), to the first and fourth causes of action (i.e., for Elder Abuse and Neglect and Wrongful Death, respectively) in the Operative Complaint, on the basis that they both fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action.

(1)      First Cause of Action (Elder Abuse and Neglect)

The elements of a cause of action for dependent abuse and neglect are determined by the Elder Abuse and Dependent Adult Civil Protection Act (the “Act”). (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15600 et seq.) The Act affords heightened remedies to elders and dependent adults who can establish “by clear and convincing evidence that a defendant is liable for . . . neglect as defined in Section 15610.57, and that the defendant has been guilty of recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of this abuse.” (Welf. & Inst. Code § 15657, subd. (a).)

“Neglect” is defined in Section 15610.57 as “[t]he negligent failure of any person having the care or custody of an elder or a dependent adult to exercise that degree of care that a reasonable person in a like position would exercise” and includes, inter alia, the “(1) Failure to assist in personal hygiene, or in the provision or food, clothing, or shelter. (2) Failure to provide medical care for physical and mental health needs . . . (3) Failure to protect from health and safety hazards. (4) Failure to prevent malnutrition or dehydration.”

The Act excludes liability for acts of professional negligence; it does not apply to simple or gross negligence by health care providers. (Delaney v. Baker (1999) 20 Cal.4th 23, 32). Neglect under the Act “refers not to the substandard performance of medical services but, rather, to the failure of those responsible for attending to the basic needs and comforts of elderly or dependent adults, regardless of their professional standing, to carry out their custodial obligations. Thus, the statutory definition of ‘neglect’ speaks not of the undertaking of medical services, but of the failure to provide medical care.” (Sababin v. Superior Court (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 81, 89 [quotation marks and citation omitted].)

Further, a plaintiff must show defendant was guilty of “‘recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice in the commission of [physical, neglectful, or financial elder abuse.]’” (Benun v. Superior Court (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 113, 119 [citation omitted].) Recklessness “refers to a subjective state of culpability greater than simple negligence, which has been described as a ‘deliberate disregard’ of the ‘high degree of probability’ that an injury will occur.” (Delaney, supra, 20 Cal.4th at 31.) Oppression, fraud, and malice involve intentional or conscious wrongdoing of a despicable or injurious nature. (Id.) A plaintiff must also show that an employer “had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice” and, “[w]ith respect to a corporate employer, [that the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (b); Welf & Inst. Code, 15657, subd. (c).)

Several factors, then, “must be present for conduct to constitute neglect within the meaning of the Elder Abuse Act and thereby trigger the enhanced remedies available under the Act. The plaintiff must allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) facts establishing that the defendant: (1) had responsibility for meeting the basic needs of the elder or dependent adult, such as nutrition, hydration, hygiene or medical care; (2) knew of conditions that made the elder or dependent adult unable to provide for his or her own basic needs; and (3) denied or withheld goods or services necessary to meet the elder or dependent adult's basic needs, either with knowledge that injury was substantially certain to befall the elder or dependent adult (if the plaintiff alleges oppression, fraud or malice) or with conscious disregard of the high probability of such injury (if the plaintiff alleges recklessness). The plaintiff must also allege (and ultimately prove by clear and convincing evidence) that the neglect caused the elder or dependent adult to suffer physical harm, pain or mental suffering. Finally, the facts constituting the neglect and establishing the causal link between the neglect and the injury must be pleaded with particularity, in accordance with the pleading rules governing statutory claims.” (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 406-407 [quotation marks and citations omitted].)

The Court determines that Plaintiffs have failed to adequately plead a cause of action for elder abuse and neglect. Plaintiffs have alleged that on May 1, 2023, no custodial supervision was provided while the decedent ate lunch, that 30 minutes afterwards (at about 1:00 p.m.), a certified nurse aid reported to the supervising charge nurse that decedent “looked different” while “acting slower than usual,” that the supervising charge nurse reported to the room where the decedent was noted as “obtunded,” “non-verbal with shallow respirations” with “declining vital signs” and that the decedent’s physician was notified as to his deteriorating status, that at 1:10 p.m. decedent’s vital signs further declined and the physician was again notified, that at 1:15 p.m., the supervising charge nurse contacted 911 for paramedic assistance, that at 1:20 p.m. paramedics arrived to assist with CPR efforts, and that at 1:46 p.m. the decedent was pronounced dead due to cardiac arrest. (FAC, ¶¶ 23-27.) The majority of the aforesaid facts sounds in negligence in the undertaking of medical services. Additionally, Plaintiffs have failed to plead causation between the purported neglect and injury.

MJB Partners’ demurrer to the first cause of action is sustained.

(2)      Fourth Cause of Action (Wrongful Death)

“The elements of a wrongful death cause of action are (1) a wrongful act or neglect on the part of one or more persons that (2) causes the death of another person.” (Arista v. County of Riverside (2018) 29 Cal.App.5th 1051, 1060 [quotations and citation omitted].) MJB Partners demurs to Plaintiffs’ fourth cause of action on the basis that the element of causation has not been pled. The Court agrees.

MJB Partners’ demurrer to the fourth cause of action is sustained. 

Case Number: 24PSCV00906    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: H

Li v. Huang, et al., Case No. 24PSCV00906

ORDER ON DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER

Defendant Guotao Huang’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint is OVERRULED.

Background 

Plaintiff Shuangshuang Li (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

In March 2022, Plaintiff posted on Chinese social media that she was seeking a contractor to remodel her Diamond Bar home (the “property”). Guotao Huang (“Huang”) responded to Plaintiff’s post on behalf of Vanke Home Remodel, Inc. (“Vanke”) and met with Plaintiff at Plaintiff’s home in April 2022. Huang and Vanke (together, “Defendants”) agreed to do the requested work for $110,000.00. Plaintiff paid $65,000.00 up front, with the remainder to be paid upon completion of the work. The work performed was substandard. In May 2022, before the work was completed, a city inspector discovered the project and informed Plaintiff that a permit had not been obtained and was required. Plaintiff told Defendants to restore the property and to apply for a permit. Huang told Plaintiff they would not complete work and demanded full payment. In June 2022, Huang filed a mechanic’s lien on the property. 

On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (the “Operative Complaint” or “FAC”), asserting a breach of contract cause of action against Huang, Vanke, and Does 1-10. 

Pending before the Court is Huang’s demurrer to the Operative Complaint.  

Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

Discussion

Huang demurs, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10, subdivisions (e) and (f), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and is uncertain.

The Court first addresses Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice. A court may take judicial notice of, among other things, “official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of a state and of facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.” (People v. Terry, 38 Cal. App. 3d 432, 439, 113 Cal. Rptr. 233, 237 (Ct. App. 1974) [citing Evid. Code § 452].) With this standard in mind, the Court grants Plaintiff’s request to take judicial notice of the mechanic’s lien.

Turning to the merits of the demurrer, Huang argues that the Operative Complaint violates the sham pleading doctrine because Plaintiff deleted alter ego allegations and the phrase “on behalf of Vanke” that was present in Paragraph 9 of her original complaint. The Court disagrees. “Under the sham pleading doctrine, allegations in an original pleading that rendered it vulnerable to demurrer or other attack cannot simply be omitted without explanation.” (Hahn v, Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 751.) “The doctrine is not intended to prevent honest complainants from correcting erroneous allegations or to prevent the correction of ambiguous facts.” (Id.)

The Operative Complaint now contains an allegation that “HUANG has recorded a Mechanic’s Lien against Plaintiff’s home, in which he declared, under penalty of perjury, that in doing the work on Plaintiff’s home, he was using ‘VANKE HOME REMODL’ as a dba.” (FAC, ¶ 6.) The Court believes the foregoing allegation constitutes the “explanation” needed for purposes of withstanding demurrer.

The demurrer is overruled.

Gulf Harbour Investments Corporation v. Wright, et al., Case No. 24PSCV01136 

ORDER ON DEMURRER

Defendants’ demurrer is sustained, with 20 days’ leave to amend. The First Amended Complaint filed October 1, 2024 is hereby stricken.


Background 

Plaintiff Gulf Harbour Investments Corporation (“Plaintiff”) alleges as follows:

On April 7, 2005, a grant deed was recorded wherein Campbell Wright and Danielle Avila-Wright (the “Borrowers”) were granted an interest in the real property located at 2370 North San Benito Court in Claremont (“subject property”). On December 4, 2006, Borrowers executed and delivered a note and deed of trust to Indymac Bank, F.S.B. (“Indymac”); the latter document was recorded on December 12, 2006. Plaintiff thereafter purchased the loan and all interest in both the note and deed of trust. Indymac, however, failed to record an Assignment of Deed of Trust to Plaintiff.  

On April 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against the Borrowers, Indymac, and Does 1-50 for (1) equitable assignment of lien, and (2) declaratory relief. 

On August 14, 2024, Indymac’s default was entered. 

Pending before the Court is the Borrowers’ demurrer to the complaint.

Legal Standard 

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).) 

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal.App.3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].)

Discussion

Borrowers demur to the first and second causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint on the basis that they both fail to state facts sufficient to constitute causes of action and are uncertain.

The Court is in receipt of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint filed October 1, 2024. Although the aforementioned complaint was not filed in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 472, subdivision (a) (i.e., “[a] party may amend its pleading once without leave of the court at any time before the answer, demurrer, or motion to strike is filed, or after a demurrer or motion to strike is filed but before the demurrer or motion to strike is heard if the amended pleading is filed and served no later than the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike. A party may amend the pleading after the date for filing an opposition to the demurrer or motion to strike, upon stipulation by the parties. . .”), the Court construes the filing, coupled with Plaintiff’s lack of opposition to the instant demurrer, as a concession by Plaintiff that the instant demurrer is meritorious. 

Based on the foregoing circumstances, the Court will order the First Amended Complaint filed October 1, 2024 stricken and sustain the instant demurrer with 20 days’ leave to amend. 

Case Number: 24PSCV01758    Hearing Date: October 9, 2024    Dept: H

Yao, et al. v. Yang, Case No. 24PSCV01758

ORDER ON MOTION TO SET ASIDE DEFAULT 

Defendant Qihui Yang’s Motion to Set Aside Default is DENIED. 

Background 

Plaintiffs Mingwen Yao (“Yao”) and Worldyao Group (“Group”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

Yao is the majority shareholder of Group. On August 10, 2020, Yao and Qihui Yang (“Yang”) entered into a written agreement, in which Yang agreed to invest $30,000 in Group for a four-month period in exchange for $15,000.00 in investment profits. After Yao failed to produce any profits or return Yang’s original investment, Yang sued Yao and Group. On February 23, 2022, Yang and Yao entered into a confidential settlement agreement, which contained a non-disparagement clause. The settlement agreement is usurious. Yang brought a motion to enforce the settlement agreement after Yao defaulted in making payments due thereunder. Yang disparaged Plaintiffs in the community. Yang then sued Plaintiffs in Case No. 22PSCV01615. Yang was able to obtain a default judgment against Plaintiffs in that case because Plaintiffs were defrauded by James Lee (“Lee”) and his associate, Sandra DeLeon (“DeLeon”), who falsely represented to Plaintiffs that they were attorneys and would defend them in the lawsuit. 

On June 28, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint, asserting causes of action against Yang, DeLeon, Lee, and Does 1-50 for (1) breach of contract, (2) usury, (3) equitable relief to set aside default, and (4) fraud. 

On September 5, 2024, Yang’s default was entered. 

Pending before the Court is Yang’s motion to set aside default. 

Legal Standard

“The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).)

Discussion

Yang moves the Court for an order setting aside the default entered against her on September 5, 2024, “on the grounds of improper service and lack of personal jurisdiction.” (Motion, 2:4-5.)

Defendant cites to Code of Civil Procedure § 473, subdivision (b) as the basis of her motion (See Id., 2:5 and 2:22); however, it appears from the foregoing language that Defendant is arguing improper service, which is a subdivision (d) argument (i.e., “[t]he court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, so as to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order”). More specifically, Yang is contending that Plaintiffs improperly electronically served the summons and complaint upon Peter Deng on July 3, 2024, when he was not yet her counsel of record (Id. at 2:12-26 [“It is the Defendant’s position that the purported service by email to Attorney Peter S. Deng (“Deng”) on July 3, 2024, failed to confer personal jurisdiction over Ms. Yang, as Mr. Deng had not yet been authorized to represent her, nor was email a sanctioned method of service under the governing statutes”]; see also, Deng Decl., 2:6-14.)

Plaintiffs’ counsel Heidi Cheng (“Cheng”), in turn, attests that she spoke telephonically with Deng on July 16, 2024 at length about this case, during which time Deng confirmed he represented Yang and agreed to accept service of the First Amended Complaint via a “Notice and Acknowledgment” (“Notice”) through email (Cheng Decl., ¶ 2); that she followed up their phone call with a confirming email (Id., ¶ 2, Exh. 1); that Deng signed and returned the Notice via email to her on July 17, 2024 (Id., ¶ 3, Exh. 2)[FN1]; that on August 16, 2024, she emailed Deng to remind him that his responsive pleading was due that day (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. 3), and that instead Deng sent a meet and confer letter after hours that day (Id., ¶ 4). The instant motion was filed on September 12, 2024. 

The motion is denied.  

[FN1] Exhibit 2 is also comprised of a “Meet and Confer” letter dated July 17, 2024 from Deng to Cheng regarding the Operative Complaint.
