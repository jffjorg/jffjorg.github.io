DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV24054    Hearing Date: September 28, 2023    Dept: 61

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Cedric L. Price’s Motion for Appointment of Receiver is DENIED.

 

I.                   RECEIVER

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Cedric Price (Price) seeks an appointment of a receiver, Danielle Brinkman, to manage two properites that are the subject of this litigation, located on 5096 Llano Drive in Woodland Hills and 1836 Crenshaw Boulevard in Los Angles. Price relies on the following provisions of Code of Civil Procedure § 564 to support his request for an appointment:

(1)   In an action by a vendor to vacate a fraudulent purchase of property

. . .

 

(9) In all other cases where necessary to preserve the property or rights of any party.

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 564, subd. (b)(1), (3), (9).).

“Because the appointment of a receiver transfers property . . . out of the hands of its owners and into the hands of a receiver, the appointment of a receiver is a very drastic, harsh, and costly remedy that is to be exercised sparingly and with caution. Due to the extraordinary nature of this remedy and the special costs it imposes, courts are strongly discouraged—although not strictly prohibited—from appointing a receiver unless the more intrusive oversight of a receiver is a necessity because other, less intrusive remedies are either inadequate or unavailable.

(Medipro Medical Staffing LLC v. Certified Nursing Registry, Inc. (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 622, 628, internal quotation marks and citations omitted.)

 

Price submits the following justification for a receiver. The Llano property was purchased in May 2019, ostensibly in Price’s name, but actually through forgeries perpetrated by Defendant Jarrell Davis (Davis). (Motion at p. 1.) Shortly after the May purchase, Davis transferred the property from Price to a trust he controlled, V2 Properties Trust. (Motion at p. 1.) In January 2023, Davis was charged with 59 counts of forgery and is still in custody. (Mulkerin Decl. ¶ 3.) Price seeks an appointment of a receiver to manage the Llano property to collect rents and potentially sell the property, with the proceeds to be held in escrow or interpleaded pending the resolution of this litigation. (Motion at p. 1.)

 

Defendants Coldwell Banker and Shayan Ghalari offer a conditional opposition to the motion, seeking conditions that any receiver appointed not be permitted to hire an attorney, that the receiver be solely paid by Price, and that Coldwell Banker and Ghalari be permitted to raise Plaintiff’s failure to mitigate damages by delaying the bringing of this receivership motion. (Opposition at pp. 2–3.) Precision Dental has filed a response, indicating that it neither opposes nor supports the appointment of the receiver, and arguing that the expense of any such appointment must be borne by Price alone. (Response at pp. 3–4.)

 

Price has made no showing of entitlement to receiver. Price presents no evidence that the property at issue is subject to waste or deterioration. Price does not know whether the property currently has any tenants, or if it is currently generating income, but merely asserts that it is “potentially” wasting. (Motion at p. 1.) Nor does Plaintiff articulate the harm that is to be suffered from the waste of the property at issue, save the claim that avoiding waste “would partially offset the damages claimed by Plaintiff in this action.” (Motion at pp. 3–4.) Plaintiff’s request for the drastic, harsh, and expensive relief of a receiver is ill-supported and founded on speculation.

 

The motion is therefore DENIED.


Case Number: 23STCV08414    Hearing Date: September 28, 2023    Dept: 61

Defendants SSA Terminals, LLC, SSA Terminals (Pier A), Pacific Maritime Services, LLC, and Frank Hall’s Motion to Strike the Complaint is DENIED.

 

I.                   MOTION TO STRIKE

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435(b)(1)). The notice of motion to strike a portion of a pleading shall quote in full the portions sought to be stricken except where the motion is to strike an entire paragraph, cause of action, count or defense. (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1322.)

The grounds for a motion to strike shall appear on the face of the challenged pleading or form any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437(a)). The court then may strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading and strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436.) When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend. (Perlman v. Municipal Court (1979) 99 Cal.App.3d 568, 575.)

Defendants SSA Terminals, LLC, SSA Terminals (Pier A), Pacific Maritime Services, LLC, and Frank Hall (Defendants) move to strike the complaint of Plaintiff Plentiude Transportation, Inc. (Plaintiff) on the grounds that it was filed without the notice of related case required of California Rules of Court (CRC) Rule 3.300, and that it was further filed in violation of the order entered on March 1, 2023, in the related case LASC Case No. 22STCV37306, directing that no further pleadings be filed prior to adjudication of Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration.

Defendants note that this case rests upon an identical factual foundation as Case No. 22STCV37306, filed by Plaintiff against the same parties: namely, Plaintiff’s allegations that Defendants falsely accused Plaintiff’s employee of stealing from Defendants’ terminal, and thereafter blocked Plaintiff’s access from the same.

CRC Rule 3.300 states: “Whenever a party in a civil action knows or learns that the action or proceeding is related to another action or proceeding pending, dismissed, or disposed of by judgment in any state or federal court in California, the party must serve and file a Notice of Related Case.” (CRC Rule 3.300, subd. (b).) The determination of relatedness is made by “the judge who has the earlier filed case.” (CRC Rule 3.300, subd. (h)(1)(A).) Defendants also cite LASC Local Court Rule 3.3, subd. (d), which states: “A party must not dismiss and then refile a case for the purpose of obtaining a different judge.”

 

The underlying procedural facts are these. Plaintiff first filed the complaint against Defendants alleging similar claims on November 28, 2022, in LASC Case No. 22STCV37036. Defendants filed a motion to compel arbitration and a motion to disqualify counsel on December 30, 2022, and on March 1, 2023, in granting Plaintiff’s ex parte application to continue hearing on Defendants’ motions, the court ordered “no further pleadings allowed prior to 4/17/2023 and no further briefing on the motion to compel arbitration.” But on April 3, 2023, Plaintiff dismissed the action without prejudice. Plaintiff filed the present action with new counsel on April 14, 2023, alleging the same claims, plus claims under the Bain Act and Unfair Competition Law.

 

Plaintiff did not file a notice of related case when it filed its new complaint, and in doing so violated CRC Rule 3.300. Instead, Plaintiff filed a “notice of prior dismissed case and opposition to relating the dismissed case with this case” on May 25, 2023. Plaintiff filed no notice in the original, dismissed case, and Defendants’ motion to deem the matters related was granted on July 24, 2023.

 

Plaintiff in opposition contends that Defendants failed to meet and confer prior to filing the present motion to strike, and further argues that the motion is moot, as the cases have been deemed related. (Opposition at pp. 11–13.) Plaintiff further argues that the new action was not filed in violation of this court’s March 1, 2023 order against new pleadings, as the reason for filing the new action was the discovery of Plaintiff’s conflicted counsel, identified in Defendants’ prior motion to disqualify. (Opposition at pp. 14–15.)

 

Whether or not to grant a motion to strike is a matter of discretion for the trial court. (See Doe v. United States Youth Soccer Assn., Inc. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1118, 1123, fn. 2.) Although Defendants are correct that Plaintiff did not file a notice of related case, the granting of a motion to strike would accomplish little when the matters at issue have already been deemed related. Moreover, it is unlikely that Plaintiff dismissed the prior action and filed the present one with the hope of obtaining a new judge or evading the court’s March 1, 2023 order. Given the same factual allegations of both cases and Defendants’ obvious knowledge of same, the relation of the matters was a foregone conclusion. The most plausible explanation for the tactics is that advanced by Plaintiff: that they sought to file a new pleading with new attorneys who possessed no conflicts, however clumsily. Plaintiffs could have hoped for little else from such maneuvers except delay in the prosecution of their own claims. Accordingly, striking the pleading here is not warranted.

 

The motion is DENIED.


Case Number: 23STCV14890    Hearing Date: September 28, 2023    Dept: 61

Plaintiff Stewart Lucas Murrey’s Motion to Authorize Third Party Discovery to Identify Defendants is DENIED.

 

I.                   MOTION TO PERMIT DISCOVERY

“In any action predicated on anonymous speech, regardless of legal theory, the plaintiff should not be able to discover the speaker's identity without first making a prima facie showing that the speech in question is actionable.” (Glassdoor, Inc. v. Superior Court (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 623, 634–635.) “[I]f the defendant has not received notice of the attempt to lift the shield of anonymity, the plaintiff must make reasonable efforts to provide such notice.” (Glassdoor, Inc. v. Superior Court (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 623, 634.)

Plaintiff Stewart Lucas Murrey (Plaintiff) seeks an order authorizing him to conduct “limited third-party discovery to identify defendants in this action.” (Motion at p. 1.) Plaintiff’s case arises from myriad statements made in Facebook groups entitled “Are We Dating the Same Guy,” in which several defendants made statements to the effect that Plaintiff was involved in the murder of his ex-wife, that he was a vexatious and frequent litigant against women, and that he engaged in unpleasant and unwelcome conduct toward women he was interested in dating.

The present motion is defective in a number of ways. First, Plaintiff does not present this court with the “limited discovery” he seeks leave to undertake. In Glassdoor, the court was presented with a subpoena directing the relevant website to produce a copy of an offending review and to produce information identifying its author. (Glassdoor, Inc., supra, 9 Cal.App.5th at p. 627 .) Plaintiff here has neither served nor presented the court with the subpoena or other discovery — presumably to be leveled against Facebook — that he seeks leave to undertake here.

Indeed, it is unclear which persons Plaintiff seeks to identify. The Complaint lists ten individual defendants in the caption, as well as 50 potential Doe defendants. His Complaint also contends that Facebook itself, and potentially its “administrators/moderators,” are defendants as well. (Complaint ¶ 33.) Plaintiff further confuses matters in his declaration by identifying scores of other women whom he claims participated in the wrongful conduct at issue. (Murrey Decl. ¶¶ 49–51, 52, 55, 57), as well as a large number of Facebook moderators whom he claims “operate behind the scenes and with no responsibility to the law.” (Murrey Decl. ¶ 59.) Plaintiff does not indicate the individuals for whom discovery is sought, and the potential scope is incredibly broad.

What’s more, Plaintiff has not shown that discovery is necessary to identify several of the individuals at issue. Plaintiff himself claims to have met or texted with several of the Defendants at issue, and he identifies one named defendant (Michal Ofek) as a licensed attorney, whose contact information is published on the California State Bar’s website. (Murrey Decl. ¶¶ 14, 22, 42.)

Finally, Plaintiff has not attempted to notify Defendants of his attempts to secure their information, and thus provide an opportunity for their objections, if any, to be heard. A plaintiff seeking discovery of an anonymous defendant’s identity must make “reasonable efforts” to provide notice of the discovery sought to the defendant. (Glassdoor, supra, 9 Cal.App.5th at p. 634.) Here, no such notice has been attempted, or indeed could have been on the facts presented here, since Defendant does not identify the discovery sought or who is to be the subject of his inquiries.

The motion is therefore DENIED.
