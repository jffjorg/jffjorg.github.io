However, American Honda is granted a one week opportunity for the dept b clerk to manually clear opening a hearing date for such a motion to be heard in Dept B on minimum timely statutory notice. Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

ABSOLUTE RESOLUTIONS INVESTMENTS, LLC,

 

 

 

Plaintiffs,

 

Case No.:

 

 

22TRCV00011

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING RE: MOTION TO TRANSFER VENUE

 

 

HARJINDER KAUR,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          March 7, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Absolute Resolutions Investments, LLC (“Absolute”

Responding Party:                  None as of March 6, 2024

Motion to Transfer Venue

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Plaintiff Absolute’s Motion to Change Venue is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiff is a debt buyer and sues as assignee of First National Bank of Omaha (“Omaha”).  Omaha provided Defendant with a credit account and Defendant defaulted on the account, leaving an outstanding balance of $29,063.12.  On January 6, 2022, Plaintiff Absolute Resolutions Investments, LLC filed a complaint alleging (1) account stated; and (2) open book account. 

            When the action was filed, Plaintiff believed that Defendant resided in Inglewood, California.  However, it has learned that Defendant resides in Kern County.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “Except as otherwise provided by law and subject to the power of the court to transfer actions or proceedings as provided in this title, the superior court in the county where the defendants or some of them reside at the commencement of the action is the proper court for the trial of the action… Subject to subdivision (b), if a defendant has contracted to perform an obligation in a particular county, the superior court in the county where the obligation is to be performed, where the contract in fact was entered into, or where the defendant or any defendant resides at the commencement of the action is a proper court for the trial of an action founded on that obligation, and the county where the obligation is incurred is the county where it is to be performed, unless there is a special contract in writing to the contrary.”  (CCP §395(a).)

            “Subject to the power of the court to transfer actions or proceedings as provided in this title, in an action arising from an offer or provision of goods, services, loans or extensions of credit intended primarily for personal, family or household use… the superior court in the county where the buyer or lessee in fact signed the contract, where the buyer or lessee resided at the time the contract was entered into, or where the buyer or lessee resides at the commencement of the action is the proper court for the trial of the action….”  (CCP §395(b).) 

            “The general rule is that venue is proper only in the county of the defendant's residence. It is well established that a defendant is entitled to have an action tried in the county of his or her residence unless the action falls within some exception to the general venue rule.”  (Williams v. Superior Court for County of Contra Costa (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 101, 108 (quoting Brown v. Sup.Ct. (C.C. Myers, Inc.) (1984) 37 Cal.3d 477, 488)(plaintiff’s allegation of physical ailment as a result of harassment claim based on emails did not qualify as “injury to person” under CCP §395(a); defendant therefore entitled to transfer of the action to her county of residence).) 

            “Thus, the right of a plaintiff to have an action tried in a county other than that of the defendant's residence is exceptional. If the plaintiff would claim such right he must bring himself within the exception.  Moreover, when the plaintiff contends that the case fits within an exception to the general rule that venue is proper in the county of defendant's residence, any ambiguities in the complaint must be construed against the plaintiff towards the end that the defendant will not be deprived of the right to a trial in the county of his or her residence.”  (Id. at 109.)

            “The court may, on motion, change the place of trial in the following cases:

(a) When the court designated in the complaint is not the proper court.”  (CCP §397(a).) 

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff moves to change venue based on its mistaken belief when this action was filed that Defendant resided in Torrance, Los Angeles County.  Plaintiff’s counsel testifies that he now believes Defendant resides in Bakersfield, Kern County.  Plaintiff argues this action should be venued in Kern County and asks that the Court transfer venue to Metropolitan Division Justice Building, County of Kern, 1215 Truxtun Ave., Bakersfield, CA 93301.  (Wang Dec., ¶¶3-4.) 

            Defendant has not yet appeared and proof of service of the complaint and summons shows that Plaintiff served Defendant at an Inglewood, CA address.  However, Plaintiff indicated that upon granting of this change of venue motion it shall re-serve Defendant.  The instant motion was served on Defendant by mail at a Bakersfield address.  Defendant has not appeared, nor did he file an opposition.  (CCP §1014 (“Where a defendant has not appeared, service of notice or papers need not be made upon the defendant.”)

            For these reasons, Plaintiff’s motion for change of venue is GRANTED. 

ORDER

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Change of Venue is GRANTED.

            Plaintiff shall pay the fee associated with the transfer of a case to a different county.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.

[Tentative] RULING RE: MOTION TO STRIKE

 

 

LRW INVESTMENTS, LLC,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          March 7, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Jose Ramo Amador

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Sarah Solomon

Motion to Strike Punitive Damages from Complaint

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendant Amador’s Motion to Strike Punitive Damages is denied. 

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiff was injured while stopped at a light in the left-turn lane, facing east, on W. Century Blvd., when her vehicle was rear-ended and side swiped by Defendant Jose Ramo Amador.  (This is not the claimed basis for seeking punitive damages.)  Defendant Amador fled the scene after the incident. (This conduct of fleeing the scene of the accident is alleged to be the basis for seeking punitive damages ONLY from the driver of the vehicle, Jose Ramos Amador.)  Plaintiff alleges Defendant Amador was operating a vehicle owned by Defendants LRW Investments LLC and LR San Diego WallyPark, LLC.  Defendant Amador was allegedly acting in the course and scope of his employment with Defendants LRW Investments LLC and LR San Diego WallyPark, LLC. 

            On July 17, 2023, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendants.  The operative complaint is the First Amended Complaint filed on August 1, 2023 alleging (1) motor vehicle; (2) general negligence; (3) intentional tort; and (4) punitive damages.  (By stipulation the claim for punitive damages was dismissed as to the entity defendants.)

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  (CCP §436.) 

            Pursuant to CCP §436, the Court may strike out “any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading” and/or “all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”  (CCP §436.)  In ruling on a motion to strike, the allegations in the complaint are considered in context and presumed to be true: “[J]udges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.”  (Clauson v. Sup.Ct. (Pedus Services, Inc.) (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)  

            “In an action for the breach of an obligation not arising from contract, where it is proven by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice, the plaintiff, in addition to the actual damages, may recover damages for the sake of example and by way of punishing the defendant.”  (CC §3294(a).)

            “‘Malice’ means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.”  (CC §3294(c)(1).)  “‘Oppression’ means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person's rights.”  (CC §3294(c)(2).)

            “An employer shall not be liable for damages pursuant to subdivision (a), based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct for which the damages are awarded or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (CC §3294(b).)

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Amador moves to strike Plaintiff’s request for punitive damages from the complaint on grounds that the only conduct alleged is negligence.  Defendant argues Plaintiff’s only allegations in support of punitive damages are based on his leaving the scene of the accident.  Defendant argues this alone does not constitute malice, fraud or oppression, particularly given that the underlying motor vehicle accident was due to alleged negligence.  Defendant argues leave to amend should be denied.  Defendant argues allegations that he was inattentive while driving and driving at an unsafe speed is insufficient to support a punitive damages claim.  (But this is not the conduct that Plaintiff is basing the claim for punitive damages upon.)

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues Defendant’s decision to leave to scene of the accident, where Plaintiff was clearly seriously injured, qualifies as malice, because it was despicable conduct undertaken in conscious disregard of her rights.  Plaintiff argues Defendant’s violated the Penal Code when he fled the scene.  Plaintiff argues Defendant was also an airport shuttle driver and should have been well informed of the importance of remaining at the scene.  Plaintiff argues she was not allowed to allege an amount of punitive damages, but she has complied with CCP §425.10. 

            In reply, Defendant argues the punitive damages claim is based on conclusory allegations.  Defendant argues the Penal Code is irrelevant to the standard applicable for punitive damages here.  Defendant argues courts have not allowed punitive damages in cases involving DUI.  Defendant argues leaving the scene of an accident alone is insufficient to state a claim for punitive damages.  Defendant argues his status as an airport shuttle driver carries no higher duty of care.  Defendant denies ever having made Plaintiff’s failure to allege an amount of punitive damages an issue. 

            Plaintiff alleges a cause of action for intentional tort.  Defendant did not demur to this cause of action.  Plaintiff’s complaint allegations are sufficient to support a claim for IIED at the very least.  Defendant knowingly and intentionally left Plaintiff at the scene of the accident that he caused despite Plaintiff’s apparent serious injury.  (FAC, Third Cause of Action, Attachment IT-1, IT-2-8.)  For at least two hours, Plaintiff suffered serious emotional distress due to Plaintiff’s abandonment of the scene of the accident he caused.  (FAC, Third Cause of Action, Attachment IT-1, IT-2-8.)  A trier of fact could determine that Defendant’s knowing and intentional abandonment of the scene of an accident that he caused qualified as conduct undertaken in reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress.  (Smith, supra, 64 Cal.App.5th at 147.)  Based on these same allegations, Plaintiff’s punitive damages claim is sufficiently stated based on the “despicable conduct” prong of malice, “despicable conduct undertaken in conscious disregard of the rights and safety of others.”  (CC §3294(c)(1).)

            Defendant’s Motion to Strike is DENIED. 

ORDER

            Defendant Amador’s motion to strike is DENIED.

            Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.

23TRCV02714

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

BRIAN TAYLOR, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          March 7, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Brian Taylor

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff 2522 The Strand LLC

Demurrer to Complaint

            The court considered the moving and opposition papers. No reply was filed as of March 6, 2024.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

BACKGROUND

            On August 17, 2023, plaintiff 2522 The Strand LLC filed a complaint against defendants Brian Taylor for unlawful detainer.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

The Allegations in the Complaint

            This is an unlawful detainer lawsuit.  Plaintiff alleges that on or about July 25, 2019, Premises, one unit of a 2-unit condominium with the intent to remodel the premises and sell the unit upon completion.  (Complaint ¶ 7.)  It is alleged that:

“At the time the Premises was acquired, Plaintiff had two managers, Taylor and Sackley. Sackley provided the funding for the development and had all decision-making powers. Taylor was responsible for designing and managing the development of the project. Upon completion and sale of the Premises, the profits would be split.”  (Complaint ¶ 8.)

Construction was completed in 2021 and the property was ready to be marketed and sold.  (Complaint ¶ 9.)  In early December 2022, Taylor, without the knowledge, consent or permission of Sackley, moved into the premises and continues to occupy the premises without plaintiff’s permission or consent.  (Complaint ¶ 10.)  It is alleged in the Complaint that Defendant contends that that he had the right as a manager to occupy the Premises.  (Complaint ¶ 15.)

“However, even while a manager, Taylor had no right to occupy the Premises, and if he did, any such right no longer exists because Taylor's relationship as manager has been lawfully terminated, and any such tenancy rights Taylor may have asserted as an employee, servant, licensee, principal or agent of Plaintiff have also terminated pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 1161(1).”  (Complaint ¶ 15.)

 

            Defendant demurs to the complaint on the basis that plaintiff does not allege a landlord-tenant relationship. In opposition, plaintiff argues that it alleges an employer-employee relationship which supports an unlawful detainer action.

           

            Plaintiff contends that its relationship to defendant falls under CCP § 1161(1).  That statute states:

“1. When the tenant continues in possession, … of the property … after the expiration of the term …; including the case where the person to be removed became the occupant of the premises as a servant, employee, agent, or licensee and the relation of master and servant, or employer and employee, or principal and agent, or licensor and licensee, has been lawfully terminated or the time fixed for occupancy by the agreement between the parties has expired; but nothing in this subdivision shall be construed as preventing the removal of the occupant in any other lawful manner; but in case of a tenancy at will, it shall first be terminated by notice, as prescribed in the Civil Code.”  (Bold added.)

Here, plaintiff alleges that it defendant moved into the premises and continues to occupy it without plaintiff’s permission and that Defendant ceased to be a manager of the LLC.

Plaintiff contends that defendant asserts that he had the right as manager to occupy the premises. However, even while a manager, defendant had no right to occupy the premises, and if he did, any such right no longer exists because defendant’s relationship as manager has been lawfully terminated, and any such tenancy rights defendant may have asserted as an employee, servant, licensee, principal or agent of plaintiff have also terminated pursuant to CCP § 1161(1). (Complaint, ¶¶7-15.)

“[B]ecause of the summary nature of unlawful detainer, it has been held that the person seeking to avail himself of the unlawful detainer statute must bring himself clearly within the terms of the relationships described in said statute.” Marvell v. Marina Pizzeria (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d Supp. 1, 7. “The statutory situations in which the remedy of unlawful detainer is available are exclusive, and the statutory procedure must be strictly followed.” Greene v. Municipal Court (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 446, 450 (internal quotation marks omitted). Thus, the question here is whether plaintiff has pled an appropriate relationship between itself and defendant such that the unlawful detainer statute applies.

The court is currently unaware of any legal authority holding that co-managers of an LLC fall within CCP § 1161(1). Under CCP § 1161, the unlawful detainer “‘remedy is available in only three situations: to a lessor against a lessee for unlawfully holding over or for breach of a lease; to an owner against an employee, agent, or licensee whose relationship has terminated; and to a purchaser at an execution sale, a sale by foreclosure, or a sale under a power of sale in a mortgage or deed of trust against the former owner and possessor.’ [Citation.]” Taylor v. Nu Digital Marketing, Inc. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 283, 288-289, quoting Greene, supra, 51 Cal.App.3d at 450. Thus, for Plaintiff to plead a claim in unlawful detainer, it must allege a landlord-tenant relationship exists between the partis. Francis v. West Virginia Oil Co. (1917) 174 Cal. 168, 170.

Based on the allegations, it does not appear that a landlord-tenant relationship exists. The relationship alleges does not appear to be that of “master and servant, or employer and employee, or principal and agent, or licensor and licensee…”

As stated above, plaintiff must bring itself clearly within the statute. Plaintiff asserts that defendant was an employee of it, however it alleges that he is a co-manager of the LLC, which is arguably akin to a co-owner, who would have the right to occupy the premises as a tenant in common. Hacienda Ranch Homes, Inc. v. Superior Court (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1122, 1128. One case that found co-owners within the landlord-tenant relationship is Rossetto v. Barross (2001) 90 Cal.App.4th Supp. 1. But in that case there was a landlord tenant relationship that was created separate from the co-owner status of the parties.  In Rossetto, the court looked to the intent of the parties in creating that relationship through the written agreement of the parties. Rossetto, supra, 90 Cal.App.4th Supp. at 4-5.

Here, plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to show the intent of the parties or an agreement to create a landlord-tenant relationship. Further, plaintiff actually alleges that defendant never had the right to occupy the premises even while he was a co-manager. (Complaint, ¶10.) Accordingly, plaintiff has failed to allege a relationship that brings the parties within the unlawful detainer statute. CCP § 1161(1), which plaintiff argues this action falls under, applies “[w]hen the tenant continues in possession . . . of the property, or any part thereof, after the expiration of the term for which it is let to the tenant.” Based on plaintiff’s allegations, there was never any term for which the premises was let to the defendant.

Accordingly, the demurrer to the complaint is SUSTAINED. However, the court allows 20 days leave to amend as there may be a claim against defendant such as for forcible detainer.

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days leave to amend.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

[Tentative] RULING RE: DEMURRER AND MOTION TO STRIKE

 

 

GREEN HILLS MEMORIAL PARK,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          March 7, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Victoria Hutto

Demurrer to Complaint with Motion to Strike

            The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.  Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

BACKGROUND

            Plaintiff alleges she was confronted by Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park’s employees regarding her presence at Green Hills Memorial Park, where Plaintiff’s daughter is buried.  Plaintiff alleges she was visiting her daughter and was entitled to visit her.  Plaintiff alleges Defendant’s employees confronted her on October 30, 2021 and October 31, 2021.  On October 30, 2021, Plaintiff alleges a security guard falsely accused her of bathing in the bathroom at the memorial park.  On October 31, 2021, Plaintiff alleges two men also questioned her regarding her vehicle, which had out-of-state plates and a trailer attached, and informed her that there had been complaints about her vehicle.  Plaintiff alleges these men were Los Angeles Police Department officers. 

            On October 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint against Green Hills Memorial Park and DOES 1 to Infinity alleging (1) intentional tort and (2) general negligence.  Plaintiff’s seeks damages in the amount of $575,000 and punitive damages.  Plaintiff is self-represented.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirada (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.30, 430.70; Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him.  (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.)  The court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.)  A “demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction of instruments pleaded, or facts impossible in law.”  (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732.)  

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park demurs to the complaint on grounds of failure to state a cause of action.  Defendant argues a claim for IIED must allege extreme and outrageous conduct.  Defendant argues a security guard or police officer questioning Plaintiff regarding her use of the restroom on one day and her vehicle and presence at the park another day does not qualify as extreme and outrageous conduct.  Defendant argues it also had no control over the two LAPD officers.

            Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park also argues there are no facts alleged to support a negligence claim.  Defendant argues the fact that security personnel may have spoken to her about her use of the restroom does not allege breach of a legal duty of care, nor does it allege any injuries. 

            Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park argues there are no facts alleged to support a claim of punitive damages and moves to strike any claim for punitive damages.  Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park argues at most, Plaintiff alleges she experienced poor customer service from a  security guard who asked her a few questions. 

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues a trier of fact must determine the issues raised by the demurrer and motion to strike.  Plaintiff argues the complaint is detailed and complete. 

            In reply, Defendant argues Plaintiff’s opposition fails to address the substantive arguments in the demurrer or motion to strike.  Defendant argues the complaint fails to allege any egregious conduct that would support an intentional tort claim, nor are there any facts alleged that would establish breach of a legal duty.  Defendant argues the punitive damages claim fails because the intentional tort claim fails.

 

            Plaintiff’s complaint fails to state a cause of action for intentional tort or general negligence (or any other tort).  Plaintiff’s complaint fails to identify what intentional tort she is attempting to allege.  Even assuming Plaintiff is alleging intentional infliction of emotional distress, Plaintiff fails to state facts sufficient to constitute any cause of action.  Plaintiff alleges that on October 30, she was confronted by a security guard regarding her use of the bathroom and the security guard accused her of bathing in there and leaving the bathroom a mess.  (Complaint, First Cause of action, Item IT-1.)  Plaintiff alleges that on October 31, two LAPD officers questioned her regarding her presence at the park and her car and informed her that they received complaints about her use of the park’s bathroom and her vehicle.  (Id.)

            Without more these allegations fail to plead “extreme and outrageous conduct” for purposes of IIED.  Nor do they state a claim for any other intentional tort.  Whether conduct is outrageous is usually a question of fact but can be determined as an issue of law.”  (Smith v. BP Lubricants USA Inc. (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 138, 147 (plaintiff alleged outrageous conduct based on three racially offensive statements made by defendant’s employee before 50 of plaintiff’s coworkers and 3 of his supervisors)147.)  “Mere insulting language, without more, ordinarily would not constitute extreme outrage unless it is combined with aggravated circumstances.  But behavior may be considered outrageous if a defendant (1) abuses a relation or position which gives him power to damage the plaintiff's interest; (2) knows the plaintiff is susceptible to injuries through mental distress; or (3) acts intentionally or unreasonably with the recognition that the acts are likely to result in illness through mental distress.”  (Id. at 147.) 

            A security guard accusing Plaintiff using the bathroom to bath and leaving it a mess, without more, does not alone qualify as outrageous conduct not to be tolerated in a civilized society.  (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035, 1051 (defendant’s inappropriate comments fell “far short” of conduct so outrageous that it exceeds all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized community).)  Likewise, the police questioning Plaintiff about her presence and her vehicle based on complaints made to them does not qualify as extreme and outrageous conduct.  Moreover, Plaintiff admits that LAPD questioned her on the second day, not Defendant’s employees. 

            For these same reasons, Plaintiff’s general negligence claim fails.  Based on the allegations, Plaintiff fails to allege Defendant’s breach of any cognizable duty as a result of being confronted about her bathroom use at the park or being questioned by LAPD. 

            Demurrer is therefore sustained with 20 days leave to amend.  In light of the ruling on demurrer, the motion to strike punitive damages is moot. 

ORDER

1. Defendant Green Hills Memorial Park’s Demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

2. Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

3. As to any amended complaint and all other documents filed by Plaintiff, Plaintiff is to either (1) type the document in conformity with Rule of Court 2.101-2.112, or (2) handwrite it as described herein.  Plaintiff shall space the lines similar to double spaced typing so that there is a gap between the lines at least as large as the size of the print.  Plaintiff shall not write in margins which shall be least one-half inch on the left and right side margins.  Plaintiff shall create a space between words that is at lease as large as two letters, so that each word can easily be distinguished from the word that precedes it and that comes after it.

4. Moving party is ordered to give notice of ruling.
