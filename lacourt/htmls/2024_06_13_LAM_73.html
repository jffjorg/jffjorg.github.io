DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV29842    Hearing Date: June 13, 2024    Dept: 73

6/13/2024 

Dept. 73 

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding 

 

WESLEY MICHAEL GROVES v. COURTNEY YOUNG, et al. (22STCV29842)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/opposing party:  Dakar Diourbel (Diourbel & Associates), Franklin Ferguson (Law Office of Franklin Ferguson, Jr.)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party:  Shawn Holley (Kinsella Holley Iser Kump Steinsapir LLP)

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS FEES (filed 4/19/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Defendant’s motion for attorney fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Defendant $25,158.35 in attorney fees and costs. 

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On September 13, 2022, Plaintiff Wesley Michael Groves (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Courtney Young (“Courtney”) and Maurice Young (“Maurice”).

On November 9, 2022, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint alleging the following causes of action: (1) Abuse of Process; (2) Malicious Prosecution; (3) Trespass to Chattel; (4) Assault; and (5) Battery.

The FAC alleges the following. Plaintiff and Courtney are siblings and following their mother’s passing, had joint ownership of the residence located 4255 Enoro Drive (the “Enoro Property”). (FAC, ¶ 9.) On August 23, 2020 Courtney, her husband Maurice, and their two children moved into the Enoro Property where Plaintiff was also residing. (Id., ¶ 13.) Plaintiff and Courtney began to have disagreements over several issues related to the property. (Ibid.) On December 21, 2020, a dispute occurred between Courtney and Plaintiff that led to Courtney calling law enforcement to the Enoro Property and filing a false police report accusing Plaintiff of assault. (Id., ¶ 16.) Courtney also filed a Petition for a Temporary Restraining Order (“TRO”) based on the December 21, 2020 incident. (Id., ¶ 19.) Plaintiff alleges Courtney exercised bad faith in filing her TRO petition, simply to gain an unfair advantage relative to the ultimate determination of possession and ownership of the Enoro Property. (Id., ¶ 20.)

On March 21, 2024, this Court entered an order granting Defendant’s Special Motion to Strike (“anti-SLAPP”) motion.

On April 19, 2024, Defendant Courtney Young filed a Motion for Award of Attorneys’ fees and costs, arguing:

·       Defendant moves for an order pursuant to CCP § 425.16 compelling Plaintiff to reimburse Defendant for $22,542.50 in attorneys’ fees and $315.85 in costs incurred in connection with Defendant’s successful Special Motion to Strike, for $2,300 in attorneys’ fees incurred in connection with preparing this fees Motion and the supporting evidence, and for $2,975 in attorneys’ fees that Defendant anticipates she will incur in connection with the Reply brief and hearing on this fees Motion. (See Kossoff Decl. ¶¶ 3-19.)

·       Defendant moves on the grounds that the Court granted Defendant’s anti-SLAPP motion, Defendant is entitled to attorneys’ fees and costs pursuant to CCP § 425.16(c), and the fees and costs incurred are reasonable. 

 

Plaintiff did not file an opposition.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

California Municipal Code section 151. 10 provides: “Any person who demands, accepts or retains any payment of rent in excess of the maximum rent or maximum adjusted rent in violation of the provisions of this chapter. or any regulations or orders promulgated hereunder, shall be liable in a civil action to the person from whom such payment is demanded, accepted or retained for damages of three times the amount by which the payment or payments demanded, accepted or retained exceed the maximum rent or maximum adjusted rent which could be lawfully demanded, accepted or retained together with reasonable attorneys' fees and costs as determined by the court."

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

B.     Discussion

 

a.     Hourly Rate

 

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.”  (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.) 

 

Defendant seeks to recover fees for two attorneys that billed time in connection with Defendants’ anti-SLAPP and the instant motion. In this matter, Defendants’ counsel charged rates discounted from their standard hourly rates. Their discounted hourly rates are as follows: 

 

·        Alan Kossoff (Attorney): $625/hour 

·        Amber Melius (Attorney): $575/hour 

 

The Court finds that the rates requested by Defendant is reasonable and not higher than rates charged by attorneys with comparable skill and expertise. Kossoff attests to his experience and the reasonableness of the rates. (Kossoff Decl., ¶¶ 11-17.) Based on the evidence before it, the Court is not inclined to reduce the hourly rates requested.

 

b.     Reasonable Hours Incurred

 

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.”  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.) 

 

While not per se objectionable, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney fee request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable.  (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.) 

 

Defendant moves for attorney fees and costs in the total amount of $25,158.35 incurred in connection with her successful anti-SLAPP motion and the instant motion for attorney fees and costs. Defendant asserts the total time spent on the anti-SLAPP motion took 23.4 hours, the reply to Plaintiff’s Opposition to the anti-SLAPP motion took 11.6 hours, to prepare and attend the hearing took 2.5 hours and the instant motion took 4 hours. (Kossoff Decl., ¶¶ 7-16.)

 

Having analyzed the instant motion, the anti-SLAPP motion, and pleadings, the Court finds that the fees sought by Defendants are reasonable and do not include excessive time billed or duplicative entries. “Parties who litigate with no holds barred in cases such as this, in which the prevailing party is entitled to a fee award, assume the risk they will have to reimburse the excessive expenses they force upon their adversaries.” (Stokus v. Marsh (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 647, 653-654.) Here, the Court does not find that the time billed by defense counsel was excessive.

 

Since Defendant’s motion for attorney fees is unopposed, the Court does not grant the request for $2,975 in attorneys’ fees that Defendant anticipated she will incur in connection with the Reply brief and hearing on this fees Motion.

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion for Award of Attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in connection with her successful anti-SLAPP motion and the instant motion is GRANTED.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Defendant’s motion for attorney fees is GRANTED. The Court awards Defendant $25,158.35 in attorney fees and costs.

PUIG v. GOODPEOPLE ROASTING LLC, et al. (23STCV27674)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Scott Ernest Wheeler (Law Office of Scott Ernest Wheeler)

Counsel for Defendants/ opposing party: N/A

 

(1)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FORM INTERROGATORIES – GENERAL, SET ONE (filed on 05/08/2024)

(2)   PLAINTIFF’S MOTION TO COMPEL FORM INTERROGATORIES – EMPLOYMENT LAW, SET ONE (filed on 05/08/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Demands for Form Interrogatories – General, Set One and Form Interrogatories – Employment, Set One, are GRANTED. Defendants shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendants and their attorney of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $1,750. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s order and file a proof of service of such.  

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On November 13, 2023, Plaintiff Eric Puig brought this action individually and on behalf of all other aggrieved employees against Defendants Goodpeople Roasting LLC and GP Group LLC.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Defendants own and operate one or more coffee bars, that also operate as restaurants, bars, and Marijuana dispensaries, in Southern California. (Compl., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff, along with other non-exempt employees of Defendants, worked and performed duties and tasks at these coffee bars and additionally, received wages from Defendants. (Ibid.) Defendants implemented common policies and practices within their coffee bars that have resulted in systematic violations of California’s wage and hour laws. (Compl., ¶ 2.) Plaintiff brings this representative action to recover civil penalties relating to systematic violations of California wage and hour laws by Defendants.

 

On May 8, 2024, Plaintiff filed Motions to Compel Demands for Form Interrogatories – General, Set One and Form Interrogatories – Employment, Set One. Plaintiff seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290 (b).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product, unless “[t]he party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance” and “[t]he party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290 (a)(1), (a)(2).) The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served and no meet and confer is required when a party does not respond to discovery requests. All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served. (Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)  

 

If a motion to compel responses is filed, the Court shall impose a monetary sanction against the losing party “unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290 (c).) Further, “[t]he court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1348(a).) 

 

B.     Discussion

 

Here, Plaintiff served for Form Interrogatories – General, Set One and Form Interrogatories – Employment, Set One on Defendants on December 22, 2023. (Wheeler Decl. ¶¶ 2, Exh. A.) Responses were due on or before January 26, 2024. (Id. ¶ 3.) On February 9, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel sent Defendants a correspondence regarding its lack of responses. (Id. ¶ 6, Exh. B.) Since then, no responses have been served. (Id. ¶¶ 7-8.)  Therefore, because responses have not been served, the motions to compel are granted.  

 

Plaintiff also requests $2,625.00 in monetary sanctions for each of the motions, against Defendants and their counsel of record, representing 3 hours spent on each of the motions at an hourly rate of $875. (Id. ¶ 9.) Thus, Plaintiff requests a total of $5,250.00 in monetary sanctions.

 

The Court finds sanctions are warranted because Defendants have failed to respond. However, the amount requested is excessive due to the type of motion at issue, the fact no opposition was filed, and the fact counsel can appear at the hearing remotely. Therefore, the Court awards sanctions in the amount of $1,750 (2 hours of attorney time to file both motions and appear at the hearing.)

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel  Form Interrogatories – General, Set One and Form Interrogatories – Employment, Set One, are GRANTED. Defendants shall provide verified responses within 30 days. 

 

The Court further GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendants and their attorney of record, jointly and severally, in the reduced amount of $1,750. Said monetary sanctions are to be paid to counsel for Plaintiff within 30 days of the date of this order. 

 

Plaintiff shall provide notice of the Court’s order and file a proof of service of such.

R2 WESTLAKE DEVELOPMENT, LLC v. ANDY GUMAER (24STCV01221) 

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Mark Rosenbaum (Wolf, Rifkin, Shapiro, Schulman & Rabkin, LLP) 

Counsel for Defendant:  None

 

REQUEST FOR ENTRY OF DEFAULT JUDGMENT (filed 5/31/2024) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On January 14, 2024, Plaintiff R2 Westlake Development, LLC filed the instant action against Defendant Andy Gumaer.

 

The Complaint alleges the following. Plaintiff alleges it is the owner of the residential real property which is located at 1698 Miller Ranch Drive, Thousand Oaks, California 91362 (the "Subject Property"). On and about December 7,2 023, Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a written purchase agreement (the "Agreement") whereby Defendant agreed to purchase from Plaintiff for the sum of $5,950,000.00 the Subject Property, with the closing of escrow to occur fourteen (14) days from acceptance of the offer. After entering into the Agreement, Defendant failed to pay the deposit due and owing despite being notified by Plaintiff that the payment had to be made and demand being made for said deposit. On or about December 14 and 15,2023, as required by the parties' Agreement, Plaintiff served Defendant with a Notice to Buyer to Perform No. 1 that required Defendant to deposit the initial deposit ($178,500.00) within two (2) days after delivery of the notice. Defendant failed to cure or perform under the Notice to Buyer to Perform No. 1. Plaintiff sold the Subject Property for less than Defendant was to pay. As a direct and proximate result of the breach of the Agreement by Defendant, Plaintiff has been damaged in the amount of $178,500.00. The Complaint alleges a single cause of action for breach of contract.

 

On June 6, 2024, default was entered against Defendant.  

 

On May 31, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Request for Default Judgment. Plaintiff seeks judgment against Defendant in the total amount of $184,759.20, consisting of the amount demanded in the complaint of $178,500.00, and $6,259.50 in interest. 

 

II.DISCUSSION

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP § 585 permits entry of a judgment after a Defendant has failed to timely answer after being properly served.  A party seeking judgment on the default by the Court must file a Request for Court Judgment, and: (1) a brief summary of the case; (2) declarations or other admissible evidence in support of the judgment requested; (3) interest computations as necessary; (4) a memorandum of costs and disbursements; (5) a proposed form of judgment; (6) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought; (7) a dismissal of all parties against whom judgment is not sought or an application for separate judgment under CCP § 579, supported by a showing of grounds for each judgment; (8) exhibits as necessary; and (9) a request for attorneys’ fees if allowed by statute or by the agreement of the parties.  (CRC Rule 3.1800.) 

 

B.     Application

 

SUBMITTED: CRC 3.1800 

 

1.     Use of JC Form CIV-100                                                                      Yes 

2.     Dismissal or judgment of non-parties to the judgment                    Yes

Problems: Plaintiff has not stated that it knows Defendant is not in the U.S. military service. Note that Plaintiff also did not schedule an OSC re default hearing, Plaintiff scheduled a CMC hearing for 06/13/24.

 

JC Form CIV-100 must be used to request court judgment. On May 31, 2024, Plaintiff submitted its JC Form CIV-100. However, Plaintiff failed to sufficiently fill out item number eight of the form pertaining to the declaration of non-military status.

 

A declaration of non-military status is required to obtain a judgment. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1800(a)(5); Interinsurance Exchange v. Collins (1994) 30 Cal.App.4th 1445, 1447.) The Soldiers' and Sailors' Civil Relief Act of 1940 (50 U.S.C. Appen. §§ 501–591) requires: “In any action or proceeding commenced in any court, if there shall be a default of any appearance by the defendant, the plaintiff, before entering judgment shall file in the court an affidavit setting forth facts showing that the defendant is not in military service.... If an affidavit is not filed showing that the defendant is not in the military service, no judgment shall be entered without first securing an order of court directing such entry.... Whenever ... facts may be ... established ... by ... declaration, ... the filing of such ... declaration ... shall satisfy the requirement ... that facts be established by affidavit.” (50 U.S.C. Appen. § 520(1).)

 

Here, Plaintiff checked subsection (f) of item number eight of the form, stating “I do not have the date of birth or social security number of the defendant so I cannot access http://scra.dmdc.osd.mil/.” (Plaintiff’s Request for Entry of Default, p. 3.) Thus, Plaintiff has not sufficiently filled out item number eight because Plaintiff does not state that Defendant is not in the U.S. military service. Since Defendant’s military status is unknown, the Court finds default judgment cannot be entered.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is DENIED without prejudice.

SPAGNOLA, et al. v. FORD MOTOR COMPANY, et al. (24STCV07572)

 

Counsel for Plaintiffs/opposing party:  Tionna Carvalho (Strategic Legal Practices)

Counsel for Defendants/moving party: Judd Gilefsky (Lewis Brisbois Bisgaard & Smith LLP)

 

Demurrer AND MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF tHE complaint (filed 04/26/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

The Demurrer and Motion to Strike is CONTINUED to July 12, 2024.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On March 26, 2024, Plaintiffs Chelsea Spagnola and Michael Spagnola filed this action against Defendants Ford Motor Company and Autonation Ford Valencia.

 

This consumer warranty case arises in connection with a 2002 Ford Explorer (the “Subject Vehicle”) that Plaintiff allegedly purchased from Defendant in July 2022. (Compl., ¶ 7.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant concealed a known engine and transmission defects that caused various problems, including delayed acceleration, harsh shifting, and jerking. (Compl., ¶ 24.) Plaintiff presented the Subject Vehicle to Defendants for a reasonable number of repair attempts to no avail. (Compl., ¶ 86.) Under the Song-Beverly Act, Defendants had an affirmative duty to promptly offer to repurchase or replace the Subject Vehicle at the time it failed to conform the Subject Vehicle to the terms of the express warranty after a reasonable number of repair attempts. (Compl., ¶ 15.) Defendants have failed to either promptly replace the Subject Vehicle or to promptly make restitution in accordance with the Song-Beverly Act. (Compl., ¶ 16.)

 

The Complaint alleges six causes of action for: (1) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(D); (2) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(B); (3) Violation of Civil Code Section 1793.2(A)(3); (4) Breach of the Implied Warranty of Merchantability; (5) Negligent Repair; and (6) Fraudulent Inducement Concealment.

 

On 4/26/2024, Defendants filed the instant demurrer to the sixth cause of action for fraudulent inducement – concealment and motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages.  

 

On 5/31/2024, Plaintiffs filed oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike.  

 

On 06/06/2024, Defendants filed a notice of Non-Opposition in support of Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike, stating that Plaintiffs failed to file an opposition.

 

 

ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard for Demurrer

 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context—any defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Id.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) 

 

B.     Meet and Confer Requirement

 

“Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).)  

 

Plaintiffs argue that Defendants motions should be overruled because Defendants failed to properly meet and confer.

 

Here, counsel for Defendants sent an email to Plaintiffs on April 18, 2024, discussing the basis of Defendants’ demurrer and motion to strike, and requesting a telephonic conference. (Elias Decl., Ex. 1.) It does not appear that any discussions took place between the parties and no further meet and confer efforts were made before Defendants filed the instant motions on April 26, 2024. The Court finds Defendants’ efforts fall short of the good-faith effort requirements set forth in Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41(a). However, failure to meet and confer is not a proper ground for overruling a demurrer. (CCP § 430.41(a)(4).) Thus, the Court continues the hearing to allow the parties to meet and confer in good faith to determine whether the issues raised by the demurrer may be resolved. 

 

Moving party did not file a reply to Plaintiffs’ opposition. Instead, Defendants filed a notice of non-opposition stating that Plaintiffs did not file an opposition. Therefore, the Court is unsure if Defendants received Plaintiffs’ opposition. The proofs of service on Plaintiffs’ opposition indicates they were sent to Defendants’ counsel via email. If there is to be a reply it is to be filed within 5 days.

 

DISPOSITION

 

The Demurrer and Motion to Strike is CONTINUED to July 12, 2024.
