DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24SMCV00271    Hearing Date: August 7, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

August 7, 2024

CASE NUMBER

24SMCV00271

MOTION

Demurrer to Complaint

MOVING PARTIES

Defendants Habibi Café, Jan Fathi, and Saad Fathi

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff City of Los Angeles

 

MOTION

 

On January 19, 2024, Plaintiff City of Los Angeles (“City”) filed suit alleging one cause of action for Common Count – Money Due on Unpaid Balance, against Defendants 921-929 Broxton Ave, LLC (“Broxton”); Habibi Café (“Habibi”); Jan Fathi; and Saad Fathi (“Defendants”). 

 

Defendants Habibi; Jan Fathi; and Saad Fathi (“Moving Defendants”) demur to the Complaint on the grounds that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e).[1]  City opposes the demurrer. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.     DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In testing the sufficiency of a cause of action, a court accepts “[a]s true all material facts properly pled and matters which may be judicially noticed but disregard contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  [A court also gives] the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (290 Division (EAT), LLC v. City & County of San Francisco (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 439, 450 [cleaned up]; Hacker v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 270, 280 [“in considering the merits of a demurrer, however, “the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

 

Further, in ruling on a demurrer, a court must “liberally construe” the allegations of the complaint “with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  

 

In summary, “[d]etermining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the rule is that if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated, or may be intermingled with a statement of other facts irrelevant to the cause of action shown, or although the plaintiff may demand relief to which he is not entitled under the facts alleged.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

2.     FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

“A common count is not a specific cause of action . . . rather, it is a simplified form of pleading normally used to aver the existence of various forms of monetary indebtedness, including that arising from an alleged duty to make restitution under an assumpsit theory.”  (Avidor v. Sutter's Place, Inc. (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1439, 1454.)

 

To prevail on a claim for money had and received, the plaintiff must prove (1) defendant received money that was intended to be used for the benefit of plaintiff; (2) the money was not used for the benefit of plaintiff; and (3) the defendant has not given the money to plaintiff.  (CACI No. 370.)

 

City seeks $66,022.59 in costs incurred in connection with administrative nuisance abatement proceedings (Case No. DIR-2021-1463-RV) and its appeal (Council File No. 21-1282) to mitigate the public nuisance caused by Defendant Habibi, the tenant at Broxton’s property. 

 

City contends the amount is reimbursable pursuant to Los Angeles Municipal Code section 12.27.1(C)(2), which requires “the property owner, business operator or person in control” to pay the City’s costs, should a nuisance be declared, and pursuant to Los Angeles Administrative Code section 5.181(c), which states “[p]ersons indebted to the City of Los Angeles for delinquent accounts or obligations owing to the City . . . shall be liable for the underlying obligation and all costs incurred by the City to effectuate collection of those items . . . .”  (Complaint ¶ 8.)  City further alleges the balance remains unpaid.  (Complaint ¶¶ 8, 19.)

 

Moving Defendants contend that City fails to state a cause of action because (1) the Complaint lacks specific facts about the costs incurred; (2) some of the costs were likely incurred in connection with prior nuisance investigations, for which the statute of limitations has passed; and (3) the Complaint does not adequately allege liability as to the two individual defendants.

 

As for the first argument, as outlined above, the City has sufficiently alleged “ultimate facts” as to all requisite elements for a common count for money had and received.[2]  There is no heightened pleading requirement to allege a common count.

 

Moving Defendants’ second argument—that some of the costs may predate the three-year statute of limitations—is premised upon the Declaration of Martin S. Wolf and exhibits attached thereto.  But the Court may not consider extrinsic evidence when ruling on a demurrer.  (See, e.g., Childs v. State of California (1983) 144 Cal.App.3d 155, 163 [“It is an elementary rule that the sole function of a demurrer is to test the sufficiency of the challenged pleading.  It cannot, properly, be addressed to or based upon evidence or other extrinsic matters”].)   Nothing on the face of the Complaint itself suggests that any portion of the costs were incurred as part of prior nuisance investigations that precede the applicable statute of limitations.  Thus, the second argument raises a factual dispute to be resolved at later stages of the litigation.

 

Regarding the third argument, Moving Defendants contend that the Complaint does not adequately allege personal involvement of the individual defendants to hold them personally liable for Habibi’s debt.  As City points out in Opposition, City alleges the individual defendants are liable for the City’s costs as “the property owner, business operator or person in control” of the nuisance pursuant to Los Angeles Municipal Code section 12.27.1(C)(2).  Specifically, the Complaint alleges each individual defendant is a “business operator” or “person in control” as follows:

 

4. Operator Defendant JAN FATHI is and at all times mentioned was an individual residing in Carlsbad, California. She is and at all times mentioned was the Chief Executive Officer and Secretary of HABIBI CAFE. She has also identified herself to the CITY as one of the owners and operators of Habibi Cafe. As such, at all times mentioned, she operated and controlled Habibi Cafe at 923 South Broxton Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90024.

 

5. Operator Defendant SAAD FATHI is and at all times mentioned was an individual residing in Carlsbad, California. He is and at all times mentioned was Habibi Cafe’s owner and operator, having represented to the CITY that he is its “owner,” “President,” “CEO,” and “GM” (or general manager). Habibi Cafe employees also identified him as its owner to the Los Angeles Police Department (“LAPD”). He had access to the business’s surveillance video footage of the nuisance activities occurring there. Moreover, LAPD has cited him for the business’s nuisance activities. He further advertised for Habibi Cafe, often posting pictures of himself with other celebrities at Habibi Cafe on its Instagram account/feed. As such, at all times mentioned, he operated and controlled Habibi Cafe at 923 South Broxton Avenue, Los Angeles, CA 90024.

 

(Complaint ¶¶ 4-5.)

 

            Therefore, City has adequately alleged the individual defendants are liable for the City’s costs as business operators and/or persons in control of Habibi.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Moving Defendants’ Demurrer to the Complaint in its entirety. 

 

Moving Defendants shall file and serve an Answer to the Complaint on or before August 28, 2024.
