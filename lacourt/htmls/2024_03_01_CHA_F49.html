DEPARTMENT F49 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assistant in North Valley Department F49, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2249.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org.
All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies). 


Case Number: 23CHCV02223    Hearing Date: March 1, 2024    Dept: F49

Dept. F49

Date: 3/1/24

Case Name: Joy Rhoda Abad, et al. v. Canyon Country Mobile Home Estates, LLC; J&H Asset Property MGT., Inc.; and Does 1 to 50,

Case # 23CHCV02223

 

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT

NORTH VALLEY DISTRICT

DEPARTMENT F49

 

MARCH 1, 2024

 

MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23CHCV02223

 

Motion filed: 10/4/23

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Canyon Country Mobile Home Estates, LLC and J&H Asset Property MGT., Inc. Claudia Aceves (collectively,“Defendants”) 

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiffs Joy Rhoda Abad, et al. (“Plaintiffs”)

NOTICE: ok 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order from this Court (1) compelling the signatory Plaintiffs to submit to binding arbitration, and (2) staying proceedings for the remaining Plaintiffs pending the resolution of the arbitration.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is DENIED.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 26, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint, alleging against Defendants the following causes of action: (1) nuisance; (2) breach of contract; (3) breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (4) negligence; (5) breach of statutes; (6) breach of warranty of habitability; (7) breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment; (8) breach of unfair competition law; and (9) declaratory and injunctive relief. The Complaint alleges that Plaintiffs, who are current and former residents and/or owners of mobilehomes located in Canyon County Mobile Home Estates, a mobile home park situated at 16274 Vasquez Canyon Rd. Canyon Country, CA 91351 (the “Park”), have suffered damages due to Defendants’ alleged failure to properly manage and maintain the Park.

 

On October 4, 2023, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration (the “Motion”).

 

On November 9, 2023, Plaintiffs opposed the Motion. Subsequently, on February 26, Defendants filed their Reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy ... the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists,” unless the court finds that the right to compel arbitration has been “waived by the petitioner,” or that “grounds exist for rescission” of the arbitration agreement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.2, subds. (a) & (b).)

 

“The petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence, and a party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense.” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 972.)

A.    Applicability of Federal Arbitration Act (FAA)

The FAA applies to agreements to arbitrate disputes arising from a contract involving interstate commerce and it preempts all state laws and rules that conflict with its provisions or its objective of enforcing arbitration agreements.” (Acquire II, supra, 213 Cal.App.4th at p. 968; see 9 U.S.C. § 2 [FAA applies to a “contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce”].)

 

The United States Supreme Court has interpreted “involving commerce” as the functional equivalent of “affecting commerce,” even where the parties did not contemplate an interstate commerce connection. (Allied-Bruce Terminix Companies, Inc. v. Dobson (1995) 513 U.S. 265, 281.) The commerce clause power could be exercised to preempt state law without showing any effect upon interstate commerce if, in the aggregate, the economic activity represents “‘a general practice ... subject to federal control.’” (Citizens Bank v. Alafabco, Inc. (2003) 539 U.S. 52, 56-57.) “Only that general practice need bear on interstate commerce in a substantial way.” (Ibid.)

 

The party asserting FAA preemption has the burden of demonstrating that the subject matter of the agreement involves interstate commerce. (Lane v. Francis Capital Management LLC (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 676, 687.)

Here, the arbitration clause in question explicitly provides that it is governed by the California Arbitration Act, Civ. Code, §§ 1280, et seq. (Mot., at 11, [“ARBITRATION SHALL BE CONDUCTED PURSUANT TO CALIFORNIA CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 638, ET SEQ., AND CODE OF CIVIL PROCEDURE SECTION 1280, ET SEQ....”])

 

Despite this, Defendants in their Motion argue that FAA should govern instead, asserting that the Park’s agreements with its residents affected interstate commerce since “mobile homes are often manufactured, shipped, financed and leased in interstate commerce.” (Mot., at 13.) Additionally, Defendants argue in their Reply that “each lease constitutes, in part, a transaction involving services provided, directly or indirectly, by a public/private utility provider ... These vendors include Southern California Edison ... governed by the Public Utilities Commission and its services touch and concern interstate commerce.” (Reply, at 11-12.) Defendants cite Kassan v. Stout (1973) 9Cal.3d 39,43 (Kassan) to support their argument that “the leases forming the subject of this Motion constitute transactions that reach well beyond a transfer of a real estate leasehold.” (Id., at 11.)

 

Nevertheless, the Kassan ruling, which considers an issue of leasehold interest negotiations not constituting an abandonment of the property (Kassan, supra, 9 Cal.3d 39 at 43), is deemed by the Court as not instructive for determining the residential lease’s implication in interstate commerce.

 

Furthermore, the “Long Term Lease Agreement” presented by Defendants has nothing to do with the manufacture, sale, or finance of mobilehomes or involves transactions with utility providers. Thus, it cannot be said that the lease agreement’s general practice is subject to federal control. (See Citizens Bank v. Alafabco, Inc., supra, 539 U.S. at 56-57; SI V, LLC v. FMC Corp. (N.D. Cal. 2002) 223 F.Supp.2d 1059 [FAA inapplicable to the purchase of real property even though the seller was an out-of-state entity]; Garrison v. Palmas del Mar Homeowners Assn., Inc. (D.P.R. 2008) 538 F. Supp. 2d 468, 473.[“t”he FAA generally does not apply to residential real estate transactions that have no substantial or direct connection to interstate commerce, regardless of whether said transactions involve out-of-state purchasers.”])

 

Given the subject matter and the specific nature of the agreement, pertaining to the leasing of immovable mobilehome spaces, a matter of real property, in the Park, the Court finds the argument against the FAA’s applicability compelling. Defendants have not adequately demonstrated that the agreement involves interstate commerce.

 

Consequently, the Court concludes that the FAA does not apply in this context, as Defendants have failed to satisfy their burden of demonstrating that the “Long Term Lease Agreement” in question involves interstate commerce.

 

B.     Enforceability of Arbitration Agreements Under California Law

 

Defendants further contend that even if the FAA does not apply, the Court must enforce a valid arbitration agreement, arguing that the arbitration clauses fulfill the minimum requirements established in Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83 (Armendariz), including (1) a neutral arbitrator, (2) the provision of adequate discovery, (3) it allows the claimant to recover as much as would be available in a civil trial, (4) it calls for a written decision sufficient to permit a limited form of judicial review, and (5) it limits the share of arbitration costs borne by the claimant. (Mot. at 14.)

 

The Court acknowledges that the Armendariz court specifically addresses arbitration provisions in employment application forms and separate employment arbitration agreements (Armendariz, supra, 24 Cal.4th at 83), rather than those found in residential lease agreements or specifically in mobilehome lease agreements. In conjunction with the Mobilehome Residency Law (“MRL”), codified in Civil Code sections 798-799.11, the Court agrees with multiple authorities that a stricter standard is applicable. (See Civ. Code, § 798.19; Jaramillo v. JH Real Estate Partners, Inc. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 394(Jaramillo); Henrioulle v. Marin Ventures, Inc. (1978) 20 Cal.3d 512, 518.)

 

Plaintiffs, in their Opposition, argue that Defendants fail to demonstrate the existence of binding arbitration agreements for nearly 25% of Plaintiffs. They contend that the arbitration agreements are unenforceable on several grounds: (1) The signatory Plaintiffs could not have validly consented to arbitrate within a residential lease agreement, as it is against public policies under Code of Civil Procedure section 1953, subdivision (a)(4), Civil Code sections 798.19, 798.77, and further supported in Galland v. City of Clovis (2001) 24 Ca1.4th 1003, 1009-10; (2) The arbitration provisions are both procedurally and substantively unconscionable; and (3) Arbitrating only a subset of Plaintiffs’ claims could lead to conflicting rulings on common legal or factual issues. (Opp’n., at 6.) The Court addresses Plaintiffs’ arguments in turn.

 

1.      Arbitration agreement violates state public policy

 

Civil Code section 798.19 provides that “No rental agreement for a mobilehome shall contain a provision by which the homeowner waives his or her rights under the provisions of Articles 1 to 8, inclusive, of this chapter. Any such waiver shall be deemed contrary to public policy and void.” (Underlines added.)

 

            Similarly, Civil Code section 798.77 states, “No rental or sale agreement shall contain a provision by which the purchaser or homeowner waives his or her rights under this chapter. Any such waiver shall be deemed contrary to public policy and shall be void and unenforceable.” (Underlines added.)

           

            Here, Plaintiffs argue that the MRL explicitly states that a park resident has a right to pursue a civil action against a “substantial failure of the management to provide and maintain physical improvements in the common facilities in good working order and condition.” (Civ. Code, §§ 798.87(a),(c).) The Court agrees with Plaintiff’s argument, determining that an arbitration clause is invalid as it effectively waives the resident’s right to maintain a “civil action” under Civil Code section 798.87 subdivision (c).

 

Moreover, Plaintiffs contend that the arbitration agreement violates another public policy aimed at safeguarding residential tenants’ rights, as set forth in Civil Code section 1953 subdivision (a). Plaintiffs maintain that this protection should equally apply to mobilehome park tenants, citing Rich v. Schwab (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 803, 808 (Rich).

 

Civil Code section 1953 subdivision (a) provides, in part, that “[a]ny provision of a lease or rental agreement of a dwelling by which the lessee agrees to modify or waive any of the following rights shall be void as contrary to public policy: ... (4) His procedural rights in litigation in any action involving his rights and obligations as a tenant.”

           

            Despite Defendants’ failure to address the relevance of Civil Code section 1953 in their Reply, the Court notes that jurisprudence has broadly interpreted the chapter containing section 1953 to safeguard mobilehome tenants and owners’ rights (See, e.g., Rich, supra, 63 Cal.App.4th at 814 [as the vulnerability of mobilehome owners entitled them to “more, rather than less, protection than other lessees.”] However, this statutory framework does not impose as strict a standard as the MRL with regard to arbitration. Still, Civil Code section 1942.1 underscores that any agreement waiving or modifying his rights under section 1941 or 1942 concerning a lease of a dwelling is void against public policy.

 

            Given that the contested arbitration provision encompasses “ANY DISPUTE BETWEEN RESIDENT AND OWNER RELATING, IN ANY WAY, TO THIS AGREEMENT, RESIDENCY DOCUMENTS, THE INTERPRETATION OR ENFORCEMENT THEREOF, THE LEASEHOLD ...” (Mot., at 10), it exceeds the bounds of mere “tenantability” dispute under Civil Code section 1942.1.

 

            Therefore, the arbitration provision is deemed void as it contravenes state public policies outlined in both the MRL (Civ. Code, §§ 798.19, 798.77) and Civil Code section 1953 subdivision (a)(4).

 

2.      Unconscionability

For a contract to be unenforceable due to unconscionability:

 

Both procedural and substantive unconscionability must be shown for the defense to be established, but “they need not be present in the same degree.” [Citation.] Instead, they are evaluated on “‘a sliding scale.’” [Citation.] “[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to” conclude that the term is unenforceable. [Citation.] Conversely, the more deceptive or coercive the bargaining tactics employed, the less substantive unfairness is required. (OTO, L.L.C. v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111, 125–126, quoting Armendariz sliding scale, an arbitration agreement might not be unconscionable, even if it contains one procedurally unconscionable provision, or if it completely “fails the substantive unconscionability test.” (Mission Viejo Emergency Medical Associates v. Beta Healthcare Group (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1146, 1159.) It follows that, despite the case law holding that certain factors may indicate procedural or substantive unconscionability, the court must evaluate the agreement as a whole, rather than each factor in isolation.

            Here, in declarations from approximately 50 Plaintiffs, a consistent narrative emerges that the Park manager or employee presented them with a new lease agreement and an accompanying arbitration addendum, instructing them to sign or initial at various places without proper explanation. Specifically, Plaintiffs assert that (1) they were not informed that they had 30 days to review the lease agreement and arbitration addendum prior to signing these documents, nor were they told they could consult an attorney; and (2) they did not understand that by signing, they were giving up their rights to a jury trial, indicating a lack of comprehension of the arbitration provisions’ subject matter. (Taylor Decl., Ex. 2-51.)

 

            Here, the arbitration agreement at issue was drafted by Defendants, who held superior bargaining power, and was offered to Plaintiffs on essentially a “take it or leave it” basis, with no real opportunity for negotiation, as the mobilehomes were already on the spaces when the signatory Plaintiffs signed the agreement. Consequently, the Court finds the agreement to exhibit a mild degree of procedural unconscionability.

 

            Moreover, the Court considers the agreement substantively unconscionable, particularly given its previous determination that the agreement violates state public policy by stripping signatory Plaintiffs of statutory rights. The conclusion is furthermore supported by the provision, paragraph 1.4 A capping arbitration costs at $500.00 only “IF THE RESIDENT IS THE PARTY REQUESTING ARBITRATION.” (Mot., at 11.) By showing a lack of mutuality, this provision contributes to the agreements’ substantive unconscionability when viewed in its entirety.

 

            Accordingly, the arbitration provision is unenforceable on grounds of unconscionability.

 

3.      Potential for Inconsistent Rulings

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2 provides, in part, that “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party to the agreement refuses to arbitrate that controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: ... (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.” (Underlines added.)

 

In Abaya v. Spanish Ranch I, L.P. (2010) 189 Cal.App.4th 1490 (Abaya), approximately 120 mobilehome park residents sued the park's owners for failing to maintain the park's common facilities. The complexity arose from the fact that many of the plaintiffs’ leases included an arbitration provision, but several did not. Drawing on Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c), the trial court in Abaya denied the owners’ motion to compel the plaintiffs with arbitration agreements to arbitrate their claims. (Id., at 1493-1496.) The Court of Appeal affirmed the trial court’s ruling, declaring that “[t]he existence of this possibility of conflicting ruling on a common issue of facts is sufficient grounds to deny a motion to compel pursuant to section 1281.2, subdivision (c). (Id., at 1499, citing Henry v. Alcover Investment, Inc., (1991) 223 Cal.App.3d 94, 101.)

 

Here, Defendants admit that “[t]he dispute arises from a series of related transactions and there are common issues of law and facts as the Complaint generally pleads claims and damages common to all Plaintiffs.” (Mot., at 16.) Despite this, they argue that all 54 signatory Plaintiffs must submit to arbitration whereas the remaining five Plaintiff’s proceedings must be stayed until the arbitration has been held. (Ibid.) However, Plaintiffs have identified at least 15 individuals, representing approximately 25% of the total number of Plaintiffs, for whom the Defendants have not established the existence of enforceable arbitration agreements. (Opp’n., at 7.) It is worth noting, and uncontested by either party, that five Plaintiffs did not sign any arbitration agreement. (Ibid.) This acknowledgment plays a key role in the deliberations concerning the enforceability of arbitration provisions for the group of Plaintiffs as a whole.

 

Given the potential for inconsistent outcomes when addressing these common questions of law and fact in separate forums, the Court, guided by the precedent established in Abaya, exercises its discretion to deny the Motion to Compel Arbitration.

 

In sum, the Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED on multiple grounds as discussed above.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Defendants’ Motion to Compel Arbitration is DENIED.

 

Moving party is ordered to provide notice of this order.
