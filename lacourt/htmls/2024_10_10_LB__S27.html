DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV00130    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: S27

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - SOUTH DISTRICT

ALEYDA PENALOZA, Plaintiff(s), vs. GENERAL MOTORS, LLC, ET AL., Defendant(s). ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) ) CASE NO: 22LBCV00130 ORDER RE: MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES; MOTION TO TAX COSTS Dept. S27 8:30 a.m. October 10, 2024

MOTION FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES

Moving Party: Plaintiff, Aleyda Penaloza

Opposing Party: Defendant, General Motors, LLC

Notice: OK

MOTION TO TAX COSTS

Moving Party: Defendant, General Motors, LLC

Opposing Party: Plaintiff, Aleyda Penaloza

Notice: OK

1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Aleyda Penaloze filed this action against Defendant, General Motors, LLC for violations of the Song-Beverly Act. The parties entered into a settlement, pursuant to which Plaintiff is entitled to attorneys’ fees and costs.

2. Initial Note

Plaintiff indicates in its motion for fees and also in opposition to the motion to strike or tax costs that Defendant has not complied with the terms of the settlement. The Court declines any request to award fees or costs associated with enforcement of the settlement in connection with this motion. The Court notes that Plaintiff filed a motion to enforce the settlement and set it for hearing on 3/04/25. The Court asks Defendant to promptly comply with all requirements under the parties’ settlement, and to negotiate with Plaintiff in good faith concerning payment of fees and costs to avoid the necessity of a hearing on the issue in March.

3. Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

a. Fees Sought

Plaintiff seeks to recover $136,706 in attorneys’ fees; this amount includes a lodestar multiplier of 2. Plaintiff contends the case could have been simple, but was made complicated by Defendant’s refusal to participate in discovery, refusal to comply with court orders re: discovery, refusing to respond to requests for a draft of the parties’ settlement agreement, and failing to appear in court for hearings re: settlement, all of which ultimately led to the need for Plaintiff to prepare for and appear at trial.

b. Opposition

Defendant opposes the motion, arguing a multiplier is not appropriate, and many of the fees should be reduced because the word is “canned.”

c. “Canned” Work

The majority of Defendant’s argument in opposition to the motion is that Plaintiff is billing an excessive number of hours for what is essentially “canned” work – work that is repeated in connection with every lemon law case litigated. The Court tends to agree. Defendant has shown, at pages 7-10 of

the opposition brief, that the work done in this case is substantially the same as the work Plaintiff’s attorney does in every lemon law case. The Court will not repeat the argument or showing in this ruling, but agrees that the billing is excessive. The Court is inclined to reduce the billing by 1/3 in light of its finding that the billing is padded and redundant with other cases.

d. Multiplier

The Court agrees with Defendant and finds a multiplier is not appropriate in this case. Most of the fees incurred in this case were billed by Plaintiff’s attorney Nancy Zhang, who bills at the rate of $500/hour. Pursuant to Ketchem v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1139, the Court should only award a multiplier based on exceptional representation if it finds the quality of representation far exceeds the quality of representation that would have been provided by an attorney of comparable skill and experience billing at the hourly rate used in the lodestar calculation. The Court noted that, otherwise, the fee award would result in unfair double counting and would be unreasonable.

The Court finds $500/hour is a high billing rate. Defendant does not contest the billing rate, and the Court will allow it to stand. It is a rate, however, that does not justify a multiplier.

e. Conclusion

Plaintiff’s motion is granted in part. The Court awards 2/3 of $68,353, or $45,796.51 in attorneys’ fees.

4. Motion to Strike or Tax Costs

a. Memorandum of Costs

Plaintiff filed a memorandum of costs on 7/29/24, seeking to recover $3188 in costs. The memorandum is accompanied by the judicial council form worksheet, which contains a detailed description of the costs incurred.

b. Parties’ Positions

Defendant moves to strike the entire memorandum, contending Plaintiff failed to provide receipts with the memorandum and therefore cannot recover costs. Alternatively, Defendant moves to strike various items in the memorandum, contending they were not reasonable or necessary to the litigation.

c. Strike Entire Memorandum

Defendant urges the Court to strike the entire memorandum, contending Plaintiff failed to provide receipts to support the claimed costs, such that the entire memorandum is defective. Defendant cites no authority for this position. This position is, indeed, contrary to the law.

The memorandum of costs itself will suffice to establish the reasonable necessity of the costs claimed. There is no requirement that copies of bills, invoices, statements, or any other such documents be attached to the memorandum. Supporting documentation must be submitted only if costs have been put in issue by a motion to tax costs. Jones v. Dumrichob (1998) 63 CA4th 1258, 1267.

The motion to strike the entire costs bill is denied. The Court asks Defendant not to advance this argument in the future, as it is not legally sound.

d. Jury Fees

Plaintiff seeks to recover jury fees. Defendant argues the case never went to trial, and therefore jury fees should not be recoverable. Paying jury fees was entirely reasonable. Indeed, the case settled ON the trial date, after motions in limine were filed and after the Final Status Conference.

e. Change of Address

Defendant does not complete its argument re: change of address costs, and therefore the argument is rejected.

f. Motions in Limine

Defendant argues the motions in limine were optional and one-sided. The fact that something is optional does not make it unreasonable. Litigation is typically one-sided, and the Court is not clear on the nature of the contention; should the motions have represented Defendant’s viewpoint on the evidence? This is nonsensical.

g. Motions to Compel

Defendant argues it should not have to pay costs association with motions to compel. The motions, however, were granted, and Defendant repeatedly failed to comply with the rulings. Defendant therefore failed to show the motions were unreasonable.

h. Travel to and from FSC

Defendant argues this expense was unnecessary “but for Counsel’s inaction.” Counsel’s inaction is not detailed. The Court is unclear on the nature of the inaction that rendered the FSC necessary. Clearly, attorneys must appear at all hearings that are scheduled.

i. Courtesy Copies

Plaintiff seeks to recover $676.85 for courtesy copies of motions and other documents filed with the Court. The Court uses entirely online document imaging to review cases. Courtesy copies are not necessary. The Court asks Plaintiff to cease providing courtesy copies in the future. This cost will be taxed.

j. Costs Associated with Today’s Motions

Plaintiff seeks to recover $561.65 in anticipated costs for filing fees and court reporter fees in connection with today’s motions. Defendant argues principles of equity preclude recovery of costs that have not yet been incurred. The costs will have been incurred by the time the Court rules on this motion. The Court will ask Plaintiff to confirm the costs were actually incurred, and will allow any cost actually incurred to stand.

k. Conclusion

The motion to strike costs is denied. The motion to tax the costs associated with courtesy copies is granted. The Court will ask for confirmation of costs incurred in connection with today’s motions. The motion to tax costs is otherwise denied.

The relevant pleading in connection with this motion is Kern’s First Amended Cross-Complaint, which includes causes of action for equitable indemnity, contribution and apportionment, and declaratory relief.

2. Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

3. Burdens on Summary Judgment

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).) Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.” (Id. at §437c(o)(2).) A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim. (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.) Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.” (Ibid.)

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.) A

defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action. (Id. at §437c(p).) A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66,

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.) Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.) In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.)

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.) Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103.

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action. (Id. at §437c(p)(2).) On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion. (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.)

b. Initial Notes

All papers in opposition to the motion were due on or before 9/26/24. Kern filed opposition papers on 9/30/24 and 10/02/24. Due to the harsh nature of summary judgment, and because Diversified did not object, the Court has considered all opposition papers. The Court asks Kern to ensure timely filing and service of all papers in the future in connection with this action.

Additionally, Kern makes reference to its original cross-complaint against Diversified in its opposition papers. As Diversified correctly notes in reply, the operative cross-complaint is the FACC, and the Court has considered only the FACC in drafting this ruling.

c. Labor Code §3864

Diversified contends Kern’s cross-complaint, and each cause of action asserted therein, is barred by Labor Code §3864. §3864 provides:

If an action as provided in this chapter prosecuted by the employee, the employer, or both jointly against the third person results in judgment against such third person, or settlement by such third person, the employer shall have no liability to reimburse or hold such third person harmless on such judgment or settlement in absence of a written agreement so to do executed prior to the injury.

d. Opposition Arguments

Kern opposes the motion, arguing (a) the parties’ course of conduct must be considered, (b) the parties had drafts of an agreement requiring Diversified to indemnify Kern, (c) Diversified waived its right to the protections of §3864 by commencing work without a signed agreement, and (d) §3864 does not bar Kern from seeking equitable indemnification, contribution, and/or apportionment.

e. Course of Conduct

Kern’s first and second arguments are lumped together in the opposition papers. Kern argues the parties’ course of conduct, both in connection with other projects and also in connection with the instant project, must be considered when determining whether or not Diversified must indemnify Kern. Kern does not cite any authority for the position that course of conduct can be used to overcome the §3864 statutory requirement of a signed written agreement pre-dating the subject injury. Absent such authority, the Court finds the parties’ course of conduct is not relevant.

f. Waiver

Kern next argues Diversified waived its right to invoke the protections of §3864 because Diversified commenced work on the project without a signed agreement in place. Kern notes that the parties had been negotiating a draft of the agreement, and the draft contained indemnification language. Again, however, Kern cites no authority holding that statutory rights under §3864 can be waived. The statute’s clear and plain language, which has been upheld in numerous cases cited in the moving papers, makes clear that a signed agreement is a mandatory prerequisite to pursuing the claims set forth in the cross-complaint.

g. §3864 Not a Bar

Finally, Kern argues §3864 is not a bar to Kern’s right to pursue its claims against Diversified, but Diversified’s right to pursue those claims is capped at the amount Diversified paid out to Plaintiff in workers’ compensation benefits.

Kern cites numerous cases holding that an employee’s damage judgment against third parties must be reduced by an amount attributable to the employer’s proportionate share of the fault, up to the amount of workers’ compensation benefits paid. See, for example, DaFonte v. Up-Right, Inc. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 593, 599. However, as Diversified correctly notes in reply, the collective import of these cases is to clarify that the damages judgment must be reduced. Nothing about the cases permits a direct cross-complaint against the employer in circumvention of §3864.

c. Conclusion

Diversified established that the clear language of §3864 bars Kern’s claims against Diversified. Kern cited nothing to the contrary in opposition, and therefore the motion for summary judgment is granted.
