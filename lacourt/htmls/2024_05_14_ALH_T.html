DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV30782    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: T

MOTION TO COMPEL EXAMINATION OF PLAINTIFF BY A NEUROSURGEON

MOVING PARTY:           Defendant Guy F. Atkinson Construction, LLC

RESPONDING PARTY:     Plaintiff Kamal McHantaf

Tentative Ruling:  Denied
 
I.          INTRODUCTION

On August 13, 2020, Plaintiffs Kamal McHantaf and Dani McHantaf filed a complaint against Defendant State of California.

On January 14, 2021, Plaintiffs filed the operative second amended complaint (SAC), against Defendant Guy F. Atkinson Construction, LLC (Atkinson), and State of California Department of Transportation (Caltrans), alleging causes of action for negligence (against Atkinson), statutory liability/dangerous condition of public property (against Caltrans), and loss of consortium.

The SAC alleges that on February 11, 2020, Plaintiff Kamal, a 58-year-old man was sitting in his parked work truck, on the West SR-60 Freeway, in the number four lane when a vehicle traveled across the number 2, 3, 4, and 5 lanes after a collision and hit Plaintiff’s truck. Plaintiff was working for a contractor hired by Atkinson and Caltrans.

On March 22, 2024, Defendant Atkinson filed this motion to compel Plaintiff to attend a medical examination by neurosurgeon Paul Kaloostian, MD. On April 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. Atkinson replied on May 7, 2024.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Mental examinations may be obtained of a party whose mental condition is placed in controversy. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.020(a).)

A demand for a mental examination shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the physician who will perform the examination. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220(c).)

Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.230 provides that a plaintiff to whom a demand for a physical examination is directed shall respond within 20 days after service with a written statement that she will comply with the demand as stated, comply with the demand as specifically modified by her, or refuse, for reasons specified in the response, to submit to the demanded examination. The response must be served on the defendant making the demand and all other parties who have appeared in the action.

Code of Civil Procedure section 2032.240 further provides:

(a) If a plaintiff to whom a demand for a physical examination under this article is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, that plaintiff waives any objection to the demand. The court, on motion, may relieve that plaintiff from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) The plaintiff has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Section 2032.230.

(2) The plaintiff’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.

(b) The defendant may move for an order compelling response and compliance with a demand for a physical examination.

(c) The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel response and compliance with a demand for a physical examination, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

A motion for a mental examination should only be granted for good cause shown. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.320(a).) An order granting a mental examination shall specify the person or persons who may perform the examination, as well as the time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.320(d).)

III.      REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

            Defendant Atkinson requests judicial notice pursuant to Evidence Code §§ 451, 452 and 453, of the following documents:

1.      Exhibit “A” – 2022/02 Central New York Brain & Spine Neurosurgery Internet article entitled “What Can A Neurosurgeon Do vs An Orthopedic Surgeon [https://cnybrainandspine.com]

2.      Exhibit “B” – In the Matter of Chem Carriers Towing, LLC, 2022 WL 1702507

3.      Exhibit “C” – Orlando Orthopedic Center Internet article entitled “What Is the Difference Between a Referral from an Orthopedic Spine Surgeon Versus a Neurosurgeon for Spine Surgery?” [https://www.orlandoortho.com]

Evidence Code § 452 provides that records of any court in the United States is judicially noticeable. Accordingly, the court takes judicial notice of the court order in In the Matter of Chem Carriers Towing. There is no basis in the Evidence Code for the judicial notice of internet articles published by private actors. The court denies judicial notice of the documents attached as Exhibits A and C in the request.

IV.       DISCUSSION

Atkinson argues that good cause exists to have Plaintiff submit to a defense medical examination before a neurosurgeon because Plaintiff placed his spinal injury and neurosurgical condition in controversy. Atkinson contends that the examination is necessary for discovery purposes.

Notably, Plaintiff has already undergone three medical examinations. On February 11, 2022, Plaintiff submitted to a physical examination by neurologist Barry Ludwig, MD. (Scott Decl., ¶ 4.) Plaintiff submitted to a mental examination by Roger Light, PhD. (Id.) Plaintiff submitted to a physical examination by orthopedic surgeon Ronald Kvitne, MD on March 18, 2022. (Id., ¶ 3.)

Plaintiff opposes this motion on the ground that Dr. Kvitne has already evaluated Plaintiff’s spine and recorded his observations and opinions in a 28-page report. (Scott Decl., Exh. 2.) Plaintiff draws special attention to Dr. Kvitne’s opinion that Plaintiff suffered from “soft tissues sprain/strain injuries to his cervical, thoracic and lumbosacral spine”. (Id. at p. 27.) Plaintiff contends that Atkinson fails to address how an examination by Atkinson’s retained neurosurgeon, Dr. Kaloostian, would be different from Dr. Ludwig’s examination as a neurologist or Dr. Kvitne’s examination as an orthopedic surgeon.

In reply, Atkinson argues that, while Dr. Kvitne’s orthopedic examination of Plaintiff’s spine focused on evaluating the musculoskeletal aspects of Plaintiff’s claimed injuries, it did not address the effects that the alleged injuries may have had on Plaintiff’s spinal cord and what treatment (if any) is needed to address those effects. To address the spinal cord aspects of the alleged spinal injury, an examination by a neurosurgeon is warranted. Whereas an orthopedic surgeon focuses on musculoskeletal conditions, a neurosurgeon focuses on the brain, nerves and spinal cord. Conspicuously missing from Atkinson’s argument is how an examination by a neurosurgeon would be different from an examination by a neurologist. As discussed above, Plaintiff has examined by Dr. Ludwig, a neurologist, a fact that Atkinson does not deny.

Atkinson has not shown good cause to compel Plaintiff to submit to a third physical medical examination.

IV.       CONCLUSION

            Defendant Atkinsons’ motion to compel the mental examination of Plaintiff Kamal McHantaf is DENIED.

II.        LEGAL STANDARD

Standard for Compelling Interrogatories – Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290 provides that a party may bring a motion to compel responses to interrogatories, where the responding party failed to serve a timely response. Unless otherwise agreed, the responding party is required to serve response within 30 days after service of the interrogatories. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.260.) “The party propounding interrogatories and the responding party may agree to extend the time for service of a response to a set of interrogatories, or to particular interrogatories in a set, to a date beyond that provided in Section 2030.260.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.270(a).) While this agreement may be informal, it must be confirmed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.270(b).)  If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, the party “any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2030.290(a).)  

Standard for Compelling Responses¿to Requests for¿Production¿(“RFP”)¿–¿Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300 provides that a party may bring a motion to compel responses to a request for production of documents, where the responding party failed to serve a timely response. Unless otherwise agreed, the responding party is required to serve response within 30 days after service of the discovery demand for production documents. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.260(a).) If the responding party fails to serve a timely response, the party “waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product . . .” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(a).) The requesting party need not demonstrate “good cause to compel responses, nor that it satisfied the “meet and confer” requirement. (Sinaiko¿Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants¿(2007) 143 Cal.App.4th¿390, 404.)

(Id., ¶ 7.e)  Defendant requests sanctions in the total amount of $1,980 against Plaintiff and his attorney. The Court may impose sanctions for misuse of the discovery process under CCP § 2023.030. Applicable Law

            A court may appoint a referee “[w]hen the court in any pending action determines that it is necessary for the court to appoint a referee to hear and determine any and all discovery motions and disputes relevant to discovery in the action and to report findings and make a recommendation thereon.”¿¿(Code Civ. ¿           Absent agreement of all parties,¿courts may not make blanket referrals, except “in the unusual case where a majority of factors” favor reference, including: “(1) there are multiple issues to be resolved; (2) there are multiple motions to be heard simultaneously; (3) the present motion is only one in a continuum of many; (4) the number of documents to be reviewed (especially in issues based on assertions of privilege) make the inquiry inordinately time-consuming.”¿(Taggeres v. Applicable Law

            “[A]ny party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.010.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.)

“The Court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence. The Court may make this determination pursuant to a motion for protective order by a party or other affected person.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2017.020, subd. (a).) “Before, during, or after a deposition, any party, any deponent, or any other affected natural person or organization may promptly move for a protective order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (a).) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires that protects any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subd. (b).) Such protective order may include a direction that the deposition be taken only on certain specified terms and conditions or that certain matters not be inquired into. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.420, subds. (b)(5) and (b)(9).)

“[A] party objecting to particular discovery requests, e.g., as unduly burdensome or oppressive, may seek a protective order.” (Fairmont Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2000) 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.) “[T]he burden is on the party seeking the protective order to show good cause for whatever order is sought.” (Ibid.) “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.) The trial court has discretion to grant or deny a motion for protective order. (Greyhound Corp. v. Superior Court In and For Merced County (1961) 56 Cal.2d 355, 378-79.)

            “When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (a).) “The court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party . . . from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b).) A protective order issued under Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.060(a) “may include, but is not limited to, one or more of the following directions: (1) That all or some of the items or categories of items in the demand need not be produced or made available at all.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.060, subd. (b)(1).)

(Shaver Decl. in Opposition to Motion for Protective Order at ¶ 18.)

The declaration of Metro’s counsel in support of the motion for protective order does not indicate that the categories of testimony in Ms. Dorgalli’s notice of deposition are unduly burdensome, oppressive, or would subject Metro to annoyance or embarrassment. In fact, the declaration of Metro’s counsel does not state any reason why a protective order should issue. Metro has not met its burden under Fairmont Ins. Co., supra, 22 Cal.4th 245, 255.

Case Number: 21STCV26675    Hearing Date: May 14, 2024    Dept: T

Motion to Compel Further Responses and Documents

 

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff Nicole Cunningham    

Responding Party:                   Defendant CLG Construction

 

Tentative Ruling:                    Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses and Documents is DENIED.

 BACKGROUND

            On July 20, 2021, Plaintiff Nicole Cunningham (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants CLG Construction, Inc. (“CLG”), Hy-Max Building Corp. (collectively “Defendants”), and DOES 1 through 100, inclusive, alleging causes of action for: (1) Premises Liability; and (2) Negligence.

            Plaintiff alleges that on January 12, 2020, she suffered serious and life-altering injuries when she was involved in a trip-and-fall while at a building that was under construction being performed by Defendants. (Complaint, ¶ 1.) Plaintiff alleges that she was showing a prospective tenant a unit on the fourth floor when, while crossing a hallway, Plaintiff tripped and fell over a large, loose piece of ram board that had been negligently placed and had not been properly secured. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) Due to the unsafe condition, Plaintiff fell and suffered serious injuries to her body and health. (Complaint, ¶ 12.)  

            On October 14, 2021, Defendants filed a joint Answer to the Complaint.

            On March 19, 2024, an Informal Discovery Conference (“IDC”) was held. (03/19/24 Minute Order.) After discussion, the Court deemed that the parties’ discovery issues remained unresolves. (03/19/24 Minute Order.)

            On April 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served the instant Motion to Compel Further Responses and Documents. Plaintiff moves to compel Defendant CLG to provide further responses to request for production of documents numbers 50, 78, 79, 80, and 81 which concern “sub rosa media and social media postings” and to produce responsive documents. (Motion at p. 2:1-3.) Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions in the amount of $6,200.00 against Defendant CLG and its counsel of record, Horton, Oberrecht & Kirkpatrick.

            On April 26, 2024, this case was reassigned to the Honorable Ian C. Fusselman sitting in Department T at Alhambra Courthouse effective May 13, 2024.

            On April 30, 2024, Defendant CLG filed and served an opposition to the motion. On May 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served a reply brief. Although filed and served late, the Court exercises its discretion and will consider the reply brief. (Cal. Rules of Court, 3.1300, subd. (d).)          

DISCUSSION

Applicable Law

            In California, discovery statutes “must be construed liberally in favor of disclosure unless the request is clearly improper.” (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 541.) “Under the discovery statutes, information is discoverable if it is unprivileged and is either relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to reveal admissible evidence.” (John B. v. Superior Court (2006) 38 Cal.4th 1177, 1186.) “Discovery may relate to the claim or defense of the party seeking discovery or of any other party to the action.” (Ibid.)

            In responding to a request for production of documents, a party must directly respond by any of the following: (1) a statement that the party will comply with the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling; (2) a representation that the party lacks the ability to comply with the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of a particular item or category of item; or (3) an objection to the particular demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210, subd. (a)(1)-(3).)

            On receipt of a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that any of the following apply: (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) an objection to the response is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(a)(1)-(3).) A motion brought pursuant to CCP § 2031.310 “shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b)(1).) 

            “To establish good cause, a discovery proponent must identify a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and explain how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.” (Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.) “In law and motion practice, factual evidence is supplied to the court by way of declarations.” (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.)

            Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(d) provides that a misuse of the discovery process is failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.  Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.010(h) states that a misuse of the discovery process includes making or opposing, unsuccessfully and without substantial justification, a motion to compel or limit discovery. A court may impose a monetary sanction against a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process or any attorney advising such conduct under Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a). A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771.)  

Analysis

A.    The Meet and Confer Requirement

“The Discovery Act requires that, prior to the initiation of a motion to compel, the moving party declare that he or she has made a serious attempt to obtain an informal resolution of each issue.” (Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1016.) “A meet and confer declaration in support of a motion shall state facts showing a reasonable and good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2016.040.)

            Initially, the Court will address the contention of Defendant CLG that Plaintiff has failed to make a good faith attempt to resolve the issues presented in the motion. The Court rejects such contention as Plaintiff’s counsel, Josh Dowell (“Dowell”), provides a declaration stating that the parties had a telephonic meet and confer but were unable to resolve the issues regarding the requests for production. (Dowell Decl., ¶ 2.) Thus, the Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has been met.

B.     The Discovery at Issue

            Plaintiff’s counsel, Ryan Davidson (“Davidson”), declares the following: on September 8, 2023, Plaintiff propounded Requests for Production Nos. 50, 78, 79, 80, and 81. (Davidson Decl., ¶ 4; Exh. 1.) Request Nos. 50 and 80 concern sub rosa videos in Defendant CLG’s possession. (Id.) Request Nos. 78, 79, and 81 concern Plaintiff’s social media postings. (Id.) On November 7, 2023, Defendant CLG responded to the requests with string objections and provided no responses at all. (Id., ¶ 5; Exh. 2.) Specifically, an identical response to all requests of “Objection. This responding party objects to this request on the grounds that it is vague, ambiguous, and overly broad; it seeks information in violation of the attorney work product privilege and attorney/client privilege; it assumes facts; and it lacks foundation.” (Id.) On April 3, 2024, Defendant CLG served further responses to Request Nos. 50, 78, 79, and 81. (Id., ¶ 6; Exh. 4.) In further responses to RFP No. 50, Defendant CLG stated that “Sub Rosa was conducted in April 2022, December 2022, and January 2023, and April 2023.” (Id.) In further responses to RFP Nos. 78, 79, and 81, Defendant CLG stated that “[a] social media search was conducted in September 2023 by this responding party’s retained cyber investigator. This responding party did not designate its cyber investigator as an expert and any work and/or reports completed by the cyber investigator is attorney work-product and remains privileged. Please see attached privilege log.” (Id.) The privilege log reveals that Defendant CLG is withholding four videos of “Plaintiff’s residence,” and “downloads” from Plaintiff’s social media accounts including Facebook, Backstage, and LinkedIn. (Id., ¶ 7; Exh. 5.)

            Counsel for Defendant CLG, Cheyenne J. Page (“Page”), relevantly declares that she instructed the investigator to obtain footage, and specified the time of day, the types of activities filmed, the frequency and length of the surveillance, the location of the filming, and the subject of the surveillance. (Page Decl., ¶ 16.)

C.    Appropriateness of Compelling Further Responses

RFP No. 50 requests “All Sub Rosa films of Plaintiff taken as a result of the INCIDENT.”

RFP No. 78 requests a complete download of any social media postings made by Plaintiff that were downloaded by Defendant CLG as a result of the incident from the date of the incident through the present. RFP No. 79 requests a complete download of any social media postings by Plaintiff that were downloaded by Defendant CLG as a result of the incident from the date of the incident through the present. RFP No. 80 requests a color copy of all photographs taken of Plaintiff by Defendant CLG as a result of the incident from the date of the incident through the present. RFP No. 81 requests a copy of all screen shots of Plaintiff taken from any social media postings taken by Defendant CLG as a result of the incident.

            “[T]he work product doctrine protects the mental processes of the attorney, providing a privileged area within which he can analyze and prepare his client’s case.” (Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (2011) 196 Cal.App.4th 1263, 1281, internal quotations omitted.) “[U]n written opinion work is protected only by a qualified privilege.” (Ibid.) “A writing that reflects an attorney’s impressions, conclusions, or legal research or theories is not discoverable under any circumstances.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030, subd. (a).) “The work product of an attorney, other than a writing described in subdivision (a), is not discoverable unless the court determines that the denial of discovery will unfairly prejudice the party seeking discovery in preparing that party’s claim or defense or will result in an injustice.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.030, subd. (b).) It is the policy of this state to: (1) preserve the rights of attorneys to prepare cases for trial with that degree of privacy necessary to encourage them to prepare their cases thoroughly and to investigate not only the favorable but the unfavorable aspects of those cases; and (2) prevent attorneys from taking undue advantage of their adversary’s industry and efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2018.020, subs. (a)-(b).) Photos or films reflecting counsel’s strategies and tactics in preparation for trial should be treated as qualified work product. (California Practice Guide, Civil Procedure Before Trial (Rutter Group June 2023), Chapter 8C-4, Attorney Work Product Protection at § 8:243.) The work product of an investigator for an attorney is treated as attorney work product. (Rodriguez v. McDonnell Douglas Corp. (1978) 87 Cal.App.3d 626, 647-48.)

            The Court finds that Plaintiff’s reliance on Suezaki v. Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 166 is inapposite as such case relies on an interpretation of a repealed section of the Code of Civil Procedure. The Court also finds that Defendant CLG’s reliance on Coito v. Superior Court (2012) 54 Cal.4th 480 is inapposite as such case involved the discovery of witness statements. Here, witness statements are not at issue in the instant motion.

Unlike the petitioners in Suezaki, Plaintiff has not supported her motion by a proper affidavit. The declarations of Plaintiff’s counsel in support of the motion do not make a showing of good cause. Neither the declaration of Dowell nor Davidson explains “how the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove [a] fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove [a] fact” or identifies a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action. (Digital Music News LLC v. Superior Court, supra, 226 Cal.App.4th 216, 224.) Plaintiff has not met her burden in showing good cause to compel further responses. (Calcor Space Facility, Inc. v. Superior Court, supra, 53 Cal.App.4th 216, 223-24.)

            Additionally, the Court finds that even if Plaintiff had shown good cause, Defendant CLG would have made a showing that the sub rosa films—which were obtained by its retained investigator—are attorney work-product. Also, the social media postings (RFP Nos. 78-79) and screen shots of Plaintiff taken from any social media postings (RFP No. 81) were taken by Defendant CLG’s retained cyber investigator. RFP No. 80 seeks a color copy of all photographs taken of Plaintiff by Defendant CLG as a result of the incident. The Court finds it unfair for Plaintiff to take advantage of the efforts of counsel for Defendant CLG. (CCP § 2018.020(b).)

            The Court therefore DENIES Plaintiff’s request to compel further responses to RFP Nos. 50, 78, 79, 80, and 81 WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

D.    Monetary Sanctions

Given that Plaintiff has not met her burden in showing good cause, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions against Defendant CLG and its counsel of record.

Defendant CLG requests monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. Defendant CLG requests sanctions in the amount of $1,420.00 against Plaintiff for Plaintiff’s failure to make a reasonable and good faith attempt to informally resolve the issues raised in the motion. (Opp’n at p. 11:21-27.) The Court does not find that Plaintiff failed to informally meet and confer. As such, the Court DENIES Defendant CLG’s request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. 
CONCLUSION

Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses and Documents is DENIED.

Plaintiff to give notice.
