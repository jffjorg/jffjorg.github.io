I.            INTRODUCTION

This action was originally filed by plaintiff Universal Shopping Plaza (“Plaintiff”) on November 30, 2022, as an unlawful detainer regarding the commercial real property located at 140 West Valley Blvd. #222, San Gabriel, California 91776 (“Premises”) against defendant Vege Paradise Restaurant Inc. (“Defendant”).

Defendant vacated the Premises, which converted the action to a civil case for damages. Therefore, on March 16, 2023, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) which added Scott Huang (“Huang”) (Vege and Scott Huang collectively referred to as “Defendants”) as a guarantor and plead two causes of action against Defendants: 1) Breach of Lease; 2) Breach of Guaranty.

On February 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed this unopposed motion for summary judgment.

II.          LEGAL STANDARD

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) Summary judgment is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (CCP § 437c(c); Villa v.¿McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741).¿¿

III.        DISCUSSION

Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is premised on requests for admission which were deemed admitted by the Court on September 19, 2023. “[A] deemed admitted order establishes, by judicial fiat, that a nonresponding party has responded to the requests by admitting the truth of all matters contained therein.” (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979.) Requests for admissions “differs fundamentally from the other five discovery tools (depositions, interrogatories, inspection demands, medical examinations, and expert witness exchanges) . . . [because] admission requests seek to eliminate the need for proof: ‘The purpose of the admissions procedure . . . is to limit the triable issues and spare the parties the burden and expense of litigating undisputed issues.’ Sometimes, the admissions obtained will even leave the party making them vulnerable to summary judgment.”  [Citations.]  Matters that are admitted or deemed admitted through RFA discovery devices are conclusively established in the litigation and are not subject to being contested through contrary evidence. [Citation.]” (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 775.)

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) Based on the deemed admissions, Plaintiff has shown that there are no triable issues of material fact.

It is undisputed that Plaintiff and Defendant entered into a lease for the Premises (“Lease”). (UMF No. 2.) Plaintiff fulfilled all of its obligations under the Lease. (UMF No. 17.) Defendant breached its contractual obligations under the Lease by failing to timely pay rent due under said contract. (UMF No. 11.) Further, Defendant failed to repair and decommission the Premises in accordance to the Lease when it moved out, thus requiring Plaintiff to incur additional expenses billed to Defendant pursuant to the Lease. (UMF No. 10.) These repairs were charged to Defendant in accordance with Civil Code Section 1950.7. (UMF No. 10.) Therefore, as a direct and proximate result of Defendant’s breach, Plaintiff has suffered damages totaling $177,643.99 and prejudgment interest of $29,196.00 for a total judgment of $206,839.99. (UMF No. 15.) Defendant has admitted it owes at least $205,000.00 to Plaintiff. (UMF No. 13.) Defendant has also admitted it has no valid defenses to the present case. (UMF No. 19.)

With respect to Huang, it is undisputed that Huang executed a guaranty in connection with the Lease whereby he personally guaranteed all financial obligations of Defendant under the Lease (“Guaranty”). (UMF No. 3.) The terms of the Guaranty set forth Huang’s obligation to pay any sums due under the Lease in the event Defendant failed to do so. (UMF No. 3.) Plaintiff fulfilled all of its obligations under the Guaranty, but Huang breached the Guaranty by failing to timely pay all rent and other charges owed by Defendant pursuant to the Lease. (UMF Nos. 12, 17.) Huang owes at least $205,000.00 pursuant to the Guaranty because as a direct and proximate result of Defendant’s breach, Plaintiff has suffered damages totaling $177,643.99 and prejudgment interest of $29,196.00 for a total judgment of $206,839.99. (UMF Nos. 14,15.) Huang has also admitted he has no valid defenses to the present case. (UMF No. 20.)

VI.    CONCLUSION

          In light of the foregoing, the Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.
