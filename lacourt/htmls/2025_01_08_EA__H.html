DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCV03689    Hearing Date: January 8, 2025    Dept: H

Morales v. Cuevas, et al., Case No. 23PSCV03689


ORDER ON MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE AMENDED ANSWER

Defendants Roberto Cuevas and FBD Vanguard’s unopposed Motion for Leave to File Amended Answer is GRANTED.

 

Background 

Plaintiff Andrew Morales (“Plaintiff”) alleges that he sustained injuries and damages in a December 16, 2021 motor vehicle collision on the 60 freeway. 

On November 29, 2023, Plaintiff filed a complaint, asserting causes of action against Roberto Cuevas (“Cuevas”), FBD Vanguard Construction, Inc. (“FBD”), and Does 1-25 for (1) motor vehicle and (2) general negligence. 

Pending before the Court is Defendants’ motion for leave to file an amended answer.

 

Legal Standard

“The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1); and see § 576 [“Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order”].)

“[T]he trial court has wide discretion in allowing the amendment of any pleading.” (Bedolla v. Logan & Frazer (1975) 52 Cal.App.3d 118, 135.) “[I]t is irrelevant that new legal theories are introduced as long as the proposed amendments relate to the same general set of facts.” (Kittredge Sports Co. v. Superior Court (1989) 213 Cal.App.3d 1045, 1048 [quotation marks and citation omitted].) “[E]ven if the proposed legal theory is a novel one, the preferable practice would be to permit the amendment and allow the parties to test its legal sufficiency by demurrer, motion for judgment on the pleadings or other appropriate proceedings.” (Id. [quotation marks and citation omitted].) With that said, “the failure of a proposed amendment to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action or defense may support an order denying a motion to amend.” (California Casualty Gen. Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 274, 280, disapproved of on other grounds in Kransco v. American Empire Surplus Lines Ins. Co. (2000) 23 Cal.4th 390.)

Courts must apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to pleadings at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial, when no prejudice is shown to the adverse party. (Atkinson v. Elk Corp. (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 739, 761.) However, “even if a good amendment is proposed in proper form, unwarranted delay in presenting it may—of itself—be a valid reason for denial. . . denial may rest upon the element of lack of diligence in offering the amendment after knowledge of the facts, or the effect of the delay on the adverse party.” (Roemer v. Retail Credit Co. (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 926, 940.)

Also, “[a] motion to amend a pleading before trial must: (1) Include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments; (2) State what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and (3) State what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)

Additionally, “[a] separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify: (1) The effect of the amendment; (2) Why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)

 

Discussion

Cuevas and FBD move the Court for leave to file an amended answer to include Proposition 213.

Elizabeth McKinney (“McKinney”), counsel for Cuevas and FBD, represents as follows:

          Plaintiff’s complaint filed November 29, 2023 seeks past and future general and special damages. (McKinney Decl., ¶ 4.) Cuevas and FBD filed their answer on May 24, 2024. (Id., ¶ 5, Exh. 2.) On November 4, 2024, Cuevas and FBD became aware, through Plaintiff’s discovery responses, that Plaintiff did not have car insurance at the time of the accident. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. 3.) On November 7, 2024, counsel met and conferred about Cuevas and FBD amending their answer to include Proposition 213 as an affirmative defense. (Id., ¶ 7, Exh. 4.) Case Number: 23PSCV03888    Hearing Date: January 8, 2025    Dept: H

 Lu v. Wang, et al., Case No. 23PSCV03888


ORDER ON DEMURRER

 Defendants’ Demurrer to the Second Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, without leave to amend.

 

I.        Background

Plaintiffs Juliana Lu (“Juliana”), Alice Lu (“Alice”), and Luke Lu (“Luke”) (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege as follows:

Alice and Luke are married; Juliana is their daughter. Plaintiffs live in the same residential community. Luke served on the Community board from approximately late 2006 to summer 2017. In 2006, it was arranged for STC Management Company (“STC”) to oversee and monitor the community’s finances. Cheng Ming Wang (“C. Wang”), Chien Chyun Lee (“Lee”), Liou-Jy Wang (“L. Wang”), and David H. Cheng (“Cheng”) falsely accused Luke of fiscal mismanagement of community funds and/or have denied ever receiving community’s bank accounts and access to financial data from STC. Defendants have invaded Plaintiffs’ privacy by using community cameras to monitor them and have otherwise harassed and bullied Plaintiffs. 

On May 14, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a 193-page First Amended Complaint against C. Wang, Cheng, Lee, L. Wang, Walnut Grove Senior Housing, Inc., Walnut Grove Senior Housing Owner Association, Walnut Grove Senior Housing HOA, Walnut Grove Village HOA, Inc., Walnut Grove Senior Housing Homeowner Association, Walnut Grove Senior Housing Community, and Does 1-10 (together, “Defendants”.)

On August 28, 2024, on the Court’s own motion, the Court struck the First Amended Complaint pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 436 and gave Plaintiffs 40 days leave to amend; based on this ruling, the Court overruled as moot Defendants’ demurrer to the First Amended Complaint. 

On October 1, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the operative 201-page Second Amended Complaint (“SAC” or the “Operative Complaint”) against Defendants. 

Pending before the Court is Defendants’ Demurrer to the Operative Complaint. For the reasons stated herein, the Demurrer is sustained, without leave to amend. 

II.      Legal Standard

A demurrer may be made on the grounds that the pleading, inter alia, does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subds. (e) and (f).)

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice. (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905 [citations omitted].) At the pleading stage, a plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts sufficient to apprise the defendant of the factual basis for the claim against him. (Semole v. Sansoucie (1972) 28 Cal. App. 3d 714, 721.) “[A] demurrer does not, however, admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the pleading, or the construction placed on an instrument pleaded therein, or facts impossible in law, or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial knowledge.” (S. Shore Land Co. v. Petersen (1964) 226 Cal.App.2d 725, 732 [citations omitted].) 

When a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the plaintiff to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.; Lewis v. YouTube, LLC (2015) 244 Cal.App.4th 118, 226.)

  

III.     Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the moving party is required to meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer or motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41; 435.5.)  “As part of the meet and confer process, the demurring party shall identify all of the specific causes of action that it believes are subject to demurrer and identify with legal support the basis of the deficiencies.” (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, subd. (a)(1).)  

Here, counsel for Defendants attests that on October 10, 2024, he spoke on the telephone with Plaintiff Juliana and advised her that Defendants would be filing a demurrer to the Operative Complaint; during this call, counsel for Defendants also proposed mediation. (Kim Decl., ¶ 4.) On the same day, Juliana sent an email to Defendants’ counsel regarding the potential mediation, to which counsel responded. (Id. ¶ 5.) On October 23, 2024 and October 24, 2024, Defendants’ counsel called Julianna to discuss potential mediation, but according to counsel, Juliana did not pick up the phone. (Id.) Defendants’ counsel sent a follow up email regarding his attempt to call Juliana. (Id., Ex. 3.) 

Even assuming that the phone call on October 10, 2024 does not satisfy the meet-and-confer requirement, a “determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a)(4).) Accordingly, the Court proceeds to the merits.

 

IV.     Discussion 

Defendants demur, per Code of Civil Procedure § 430.10(e) and (f), to the Operative Complaint, on the basis that it fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and is uncertain.

As an initial matter, the Court observes that following the Court’s August 28, 2024 order, Plaintiffs have filed a Second Amended Complaint, which (1) has been executed by each Plaintiff separately, (2) includes certified translations of the attached exhibits pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 3.110(g), and (3) identifies specific causes of action – Libel, Invasion of Privacy, Stalking/Harassment, Trespass, Public Nuisance, False Impersonation, and Negligence – pursuant to California Rules of Court, rule 2.112. 

The Court addresses each cause of action in turn.[FN1]

A.       Libel 

Plaintiffs allege libel against Defendants and “libel per se” against Lee and C. Wang, based on Exhibits 1 through 10 attached to the Operative Complaint. (SAC ¶ 48(I).)

Libel is “a false and unprivileged publication by writing . . . which exposes any person to hatred, contempt, ridicule, or obloquy, or which causes him to be shunned or avoided, or which has a tendency to injure him in his occupation.” (Civ. Code, § 45.)

The statute of limitations for a libel action is one year. (Code Civ. Proc., § 340.) “As a general rule a cause of action for libel accrues when the defamatory matter is published.” (Schneider v. United Airlines, Inc. (1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 71, 76.) However, “if a party could not reasonably have discovered the facts giving rise to the cause of action for libel (i.e., defamatory matter contained in a confidential file and communicated to persons of which plaintiff is unaware), the statute of limitation starts to run upon discovery of the publication of the defamatory matter.” (Id. at p. 77.)

Here, the exhibits and allegations underlying the libel cause of action demonstrate that the cause of action is time-barred. Exhibit 1, a Community Residential bulletin board posting, is dated August 18, 2017. (SAC, Ex. 1.) Exhibit 2 is purportedly a Community Bulletin Board from August 2015, within which “libels” were allegedly “posted.” (SAC, ¶ 24, Ex. 2.) Exhibits 3 and 4 are purportedly Defendant Cheng video-taping Juliana in front of her garage, an event Plaintiffs allege occurred in August 2017. (Id., ¶ 6, Exs. 3, 4.) These exhibits, on their face, depict incidents that are all well outside the one-year statute of limitations period. 

Plaintiffs’ remaining exhibits similarly do not support a libel cause of action. Exhibit 5 is a “Report and Victims’ Bill of Rights,” which is illegible. Exhibit 6 is a “Long Scratch on [Juliana’s] Auto Windshield Glass.” (SAC, Exhibit 6.) Exhibits 7 and 8 purport to be messages involving unidentified individuals regarding inspections and cameras. (Id., ¶ 26, Exs. 7, 8.) Exhibits 9 and 10 purport to be messages regarding potential legal action taken against Alice, involving unidentified individuals using the “Community’s HOA account.” (Id. ¶ 27, Exs. 9, 10.)  These exhibits and the accompanying allegations do not make out the elements of a libel claim and are more akin to “generalized grievances,” which the Court previously advised Plaintiffs were insufficient. (Aug. 28, 2024 Order.)   

Accordingly, the Court sustains the demurrer as to the libel cause of action.

 

Invasion of Privacy 

Plaintiffs allege invasion of privacy against Defendants Cheng, Lee, and C. Wang, based on Exhibits 1, 3, 4, 7, 8, 28, 39, and 41 attached to the Operative Complaint. (SAC ¶ 48(II).) 

The elements of a cause of action for violation of invasion of privacy are “(1) a legally protected privacy interest; (2) a reasonable expectation of privacy in the circumstances; and (3) conduct by defendant constituting a serious invasion of privacy.” (Hill v. National Collegiate Athletic Assn. (1994) 7 Cal. 4th 1, 39-40). “Whether a legally recognized privacy interest is present in a given case is a question of law to be decided by the court. . . . If the undisputed material facts show no reasonable expectation of privacy or an insubstantial impact on privacy interests, the question of invasion may be adjudicated as a matter of law.” (Id. at p. 40.) 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 340, subdivision (3) provides a one-year statute of limitations for invasion of privacy claims.” (Maheu v. CBS, Inc. (1998) 201 Cal.App.3d 662, 676.) Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1 provides a two-year statute of limitations for “an action for assault, battery, or injury to . . . an individual caused by the wrongful act or neglect of another.” Code of Civil Procedure section 343 provides a “catch-all” four-year statute of limitations of actions other than for the recovery of real property “not hereinbefore provided.”

Here, the allegations and exhibits underlying the invasion of privacy cause of action demonstrate that most of the complained-of conduct falls outside the statute of limitations. Exhibit 1, a Community Residential bulletin board posting, is dated August 18, 2017. (SAC, Ex. 1.) Exhibit 2 is purportedly a Community Bulletin Board from August 2015, within which “libels” were “posted.” (SAC, ¶ 24, Ex. 2.) Exhibits 3 and 4 are purportedly Defendant Cheng video-taping Juliana in front of her garage, which Plaintiffs allege occurred in August 2017. (Id., ¶ 6, Exs. 3, 4.) These exhibits, on their face, depict incidents that fall outside the statute of limitations.

While other exhibits purportedly represent more recent conduct, they still do not support an invasion of privacy cause of action, either because they fail to allege a legally recognized privacy interest or because they amount to generalized grievances that do not align with the elements of the cause of action. Exhibits 7 and 8 purport to be messages involving unidentified individuals regarding inspections and cameras, which Plaintiffs allege occurred “around November of 2023.” (SAC, ¶ 12, Exs. 7, 8.) However, Exhibit 7 references the fact that the cameras at issue were in “the public area,” and Paragraph 44 of the Operative Complaint confirms that these are community areas. (SAC, ¶ 44.)[FN2] Accordingly, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged a legally recognized privacy interest that would support a cause of action for invasion of privacy.

Exhibits 39 and 41 and the accompanying allegations similarly do not support the invasion of privacy cause of action. Exhibit 39 purportedly references a message about a parking violation warning: “Friendly Reminder left and right, front and back two cares in parking violation. WARNING first time.” (Id., Ex. 39.) Exhibit 41 purportedly references a posting to the community, which summarily states “scam calls and emails are very common.” (Id., Ex. 41.) These exhibits and accompanying allegations do not make out the elements for invasion of privacy. 

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained as to the invasion of privacy cause of action.

 

Stalking/Harassment 

Plaintiffs allege stalking/harassment against Defendants Cheng and Lee, based on Exhibits 1, 3, and 4, attached to the Operative Complaint. (SAC ¶ 48(III).) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 335.1 provides a two-year statute of limitations for “an action for assault, battery, or injury to, or for the death of, an individual caused by the wrongful act or neglect of another.” Code of Civil Procedure section 343 provides a “catch-all” four-year statute of limitations of actions other than for the recovery of real property “not hereinbefore provided.” 

Here, the exhibits underlying the cause of action demonstrate the cause of action is time-barred. Exhibit 1, a Community Residential bulletin board posting, is dated August 18, 2017. (SAC, Ex. 1.) Exhibits 3 and 4 are purportedly Defendant Cheng video-taping Juliana in front of her garage, which Plaintiffs allege occurred in August 2017. (Id., ¶ 6, Exs. 3, 4.) These exhibits, on their face, depict incidents that all fall outside the statute of limitations periods delineated above.

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained as to the stalking/harassment cause of action. 

 

Trespass 

Plaintiffs assert a trespass action against Defendant Lee, specifically alleging that Lee committed trespass “with a locksmith into the exclusive area belong [to] the Plaintiffs and their immediate neighbor.” (SAC ¶ 48(IV).) Plaintiffs allege that this incident occurred “during the end of 2017.” (Id. ¶ 8(d).) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 338 subdivision (b) provides a three-year statute of limitations for “[a]n action for trespass upon or injury to real property.” 

As alleged by Plaintiffs, the purported trespass occurred in 2017, which falls outside the three-year statute of limitations.

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained as to the trespass cause of action.

  

Public Nuisance 

Plaintiffs allege public nuisance against Lee and L. Wang, based on Exhibit 1. (SAC, ¶ 48(V).) 

The statute of limitations for a public nuisance is three years. (Code Civ. Proc., § 338(b).) 

Exhibit 1, a Community Residential bulletin board posting, is dated August 18, 2017. (SAC, Exhibit 1.) The cause of action for public nuisance is thus barred by the three-year statute of limitations. 

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained as to the public nuisance cause of action. 

 

False Impersonation 

Plaintiffs allege false impersonation against Defendant C. Wang, based on Exhibits 24 and 27 attached to the Operative Complaint, (SAC ¶ 48(VI).) Specifically, Plaintiffs allege that C. Wang committed “False Impersonation of Walnut City, which can cause Walnut City to be civilly liable for the defendants’ actions, or to incur any other penalty or charge.” (Id.) 

Plaintiffs’ exhibits and accompanying allegations in support of the false impersonation claim do not sufficiently allege that C. Wang falsely impersonated Walnut City or that the alleged impersonation caused Plaintiffs to suffer damages. (SAC ¶ 48(VI).) Exhibit 24 purports to show Defendant C. Wang “fak[ing] to be Join[t]-Managing the Community with Walnut City and pretending to schedule [an] appointment for Walnut City to discuss with the Community Senior Residents.” (SAC, ¶ 45(o), Ex. 24.) While Plaintiffs claim that Exhibit 27 demonstrates C. Wang “fak[ing] to be representing or speaking on behalf of the Walnut City” (SAC ¶ 45(o), Ex. 27), Exhibit 27 appears to be a communication exchange where Plaintiffs are complaining to an unidentified party about Defendants. Simply put, these exhibits and the accompanying allegations do not state a cause of action for false impersonation.

  

Negligence 

Plaintiffs appear to first assert a negligence claim against Defendant L. Wang, based on Exhibit 38, attached to the Operative Complaint. (SAC ¶ 48(VII).) Specifically, Plaintiffs allege that L. Wang’s omission of Lee’s murder conviction was a breach of the duty of care that gave rise to injury or harm to the Community bank account belonging to all the Community senior residents, including Plaintiffs. (Id.) 

Plaintiffs also appear to assert a separate negligence claim against all Defendants, based on the allegations that “Defendants since approximately 2019 had been withdrawing and endorsing the same Community bank account fund with many different name endorsements, different bank accounts from different banks or often with no bank endorsement at all.” (Id., ¶ 48(VII).)[FN3]

To state a cause of action for negligence, a plaintiff must allege (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a duty of care, (2) the defendant breached that duty, and (3) the breach proximately caused the plaintiff’s damages or injuries. (Lueras v. BAC Home Loans Servicing, LP (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 49, 62.) “[T]he plaintiff must show the defendant’s act or omission (breach of duty) was a cause of the plaintiff’s injury.” (Vasquez v. Residential Investments, Inc. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 269, 288). To prove causation, the plaintiff must show: (1) that the defendant’s breach of duty was a cause in fact of his or her injury; and (2) that the defendant's breach was the proximate, or legal, cause of the injury. (Id.; see also Sandoval v. Bank of America (2002) 94 Cal.App.4th 1378, 1385 [“’In California, the causation element of negligence is satisfied when the plaintiff establishes (1) that the defendant's breach of duty (his negligent act or omission) was a substantial factor in bringing about the plaintiff's harm and (2) that there is no rule of law relieving the defendant of liability.’”].) 

The exhibits and accompanying allegations do not state a cause of action for negligence. As to the claim against L. Wang, Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged that L. Wang’s alleged failure to disclose Lee’s murder conviction resulted in harm to Plaintiffs. As to the check endorsement claim, Exhibit 38 appears to show various checks that were deposited into the community bank account (as demonstrated by markings on the rear side of the checks). Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged how “different name endorsements, different bank accounts from different banks or [the lack of] bank endorsement” amount to a breach of a duty of care, let alone that a breach that proximately caused damages or injury to Plaintiffs. 

Accordingly, the demurrer is sustained as to the negligence causes of action.

  

Conclusion 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ demurrer is sustained in its entirety, without leave to amend. 

Where “legal theories [are] soundly rejected” and the plaintiff has “not identified any additional facts that [it] can allege to overcome these legal barriers,” no leave to amend should be granted. (Nelson v. Tucker Ellis, LLP, 48 Cal. App. 5th 827, 848–49 (2020).) Here, even after two rounds of amendment, the Operative Complaint fails to assert a legally viable cause of action, and Plaintiffs fail to proffer additional facts that could overcome the defects. Accordingly, the Court finds that any further amendment would be futile as Plaintiffs have not shown “a reasonable possibility that the defect[s] can be cured by amendment.” (Heshejin v. Rostami (2020) 54 Cal.App.5th 984, 992.)
