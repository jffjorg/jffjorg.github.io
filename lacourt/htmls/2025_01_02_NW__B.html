DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 21STCV29426    Hearing Date: January 2, 2025    Dept: B

Hearing Date: January 2, 2025

Re: 21STCV29426 Stephon Taylor, an Individual vs Julio Cesar Maldonado, an Individual, et al.

 

 

 

BACKGROUND

 

Defendants Julio Cesar Maldonado (“Maldonado”) and Valencia Building Materials, Inc. (“Valencia”)(collectively “Defendants”) move to compel nonparty Radiance Surgery Center (“Radiance Surgery”) to comply with a deposition subpoena for the production of business records. Defendants also seek monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

A party seeking discovery from a person who is not a party to the action may obtain discovery by oral deposition, written deposition, or deposition subpoena for production of business records. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2020.010.) A deposition subpoena may command either: (1) only the attendance and testimony of the deponent; (2) only the production of business records for copying; or (3) the attendance and testimony of the deponent, as well as the production of business records, other documents, electronically stored information, and tangible things. (CCP § 2020.020.)   

 

A service of a deposition subpoena shall be affected a sufficient time in advance of the deposition to provide the deponent a reasonable opportunity to locate and produce any designated documents and, where personal attendance is commanded, a reasonable time to travel to the place of deposition. (CCP § 2020.220(a).) Personal service of any deposition subpoena is effective to require a deponent who is a resident of California to: personally appear and testify, if the subpoena so specifies; to produce any specified documents; and to appear at a court session if the subpoena so specifies. (CCP § 2020.220(c).) A deponent who disobeys a deposition subpoena may be punished for contempt without the necessity of a prior order of the court directing compliance by the witness. (CCP § 2020.240.) A motion to compel compliance with a deposition subpoena must be made within 60 days after completion of the deposition record, the date objections are served, or the date specified for production, and be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration. (CCP §2025.480(b); Board of Registered Nursing v. Sup.Ct. (Johnson & Johnson) (2021) 59 Cal.App.5th 1011, 1032-1033.) 

 

CCP § 1987.1(a) states: “[i]f a subpoena requires the attendance of a witness or the production of books, documents, or other things before a court, or at the trial of an issue therein, or at the taking of a deposition, the court, upon motion reasonably made by any person described in subdivision (b), or upon the court’s own motion after giving counsel notice and an opportunity to be heard, may make an order quashing the subpoena entirely, modifying it, or directing compliance with it upon those terms or conditions as the court shall declare, including protective orders. In addition, the court may make any other order as may be appropriate to protect the person from unreasonable or oppressive demands, including unreasonable violations of the right of privacy of the person.” 

  

CCP §1987.2(a) states, in relevant part, “. . . in making an order pursuant to motion made . . . under Section 1987.1, the court may in its discretion award the amount of the reasonable expenses incurred in making or opposing the motion, including reasonable attorney’s fees, if the court finds the motion was made or opposed in bad faith or without substantial justification . . .” 

 

MEET AND CONFER

 

Defendants served the subpoenas upon Radiance Surgery on March 12, 2024, requesting the appearance and document production of Radiance Surgery’s Custodian of Record (“COR”) on April 17, 2024. (Perkins Decl. ¶¶ 7 and 8; Exh. A [subpoena]; Exh. B [proof of service].) Radiance Surgery did not object. (Ibid. at ¶ 9.) Radiance Surgery did not appear for the noticed April 17, 2024, deposition. (Ibid. at ¶ 12; Exh. C [certificate of non-appearance].) On August 23, 2024, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter to Radiance Surgery regarding this issue. (Ibid. at ¶ 13; Exh. D [meet and confer].) Accordingly, defense counsel satisfies the meet and confer requirement.

 

DISCUSSION

 

This case involves a two-vehicle accident on May 29, 2020. (Perkins Decl. ¶ 2.) Plaintiff Stephon Taylor (“Plaintiff”) alleges injuries as a result. (Ibid. at ¶ 4.) On March 12, 2024, Defendants issued a deposition subpoena for the appearance and document production of Radiance Surgery’s Custodian of Record. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 7 and 8.) Plaintiff received significant treatments for his claimed injuries from Radiance Surgery, including cervical surgeries. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 5 and 6.) Therefore, Defendants show good cause for compelling Radiance Surgery’s COR’s deposition.

 

Defendants served an effective subpoena upon Radiance Surgery on March 12, 2024. (Perkins Decl. ¶¶ 7 and 8.) Radiance Surgery did not object, yet failed to appear for the April 17, 2024, noticed deposition. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 9 and 12; Exh. C [certificate of non-appearance].) Therefore, Defendants’ motion to compel the appearance and document production of Radiance Surgery’s COR is granted.

 

Sanctions

 

Defense counsel requests $1,905.00 in sanctions against Radiance Surgery, comprised of a $60.00 filing fee and nine hours of work at an hourly rate of $205.00. (Perkins Decl. ¶¶ 16 and 17.) The Court awards sanctions in the amount of $675.00, reflecting a $60.00 filing fee and three hours of work at an hourly rate of $205.00, as the motion was straightforward and no opposition was filed.

 

CONCLUSION

 

The Court GRANTS Defendants’ Motion to Compel Radiance Surgery Center’s Custodian of Record to Comply with Subpoena. Radiance Surgery’s COR is ordered to appear and produce requested documents within 20 days of this hearing. Additionally, the Court awards sanctions against Radiance Surgery in the amount of $675.00, payable to defense counsel within 20 days of this hearing.

I.       BACKGROUND  

On March 10, 2022, Plaintiff Katherine Summers (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants American Honda Motor Co., Inc. (“Honda”), Gary Pietrzak (“Pietrzak”), and Does 1 to 100 (collectively “Defendants”) alleging one cause of action for negligence. The complaint arises from an April 11, 2020, motor vehicle accident from which Plaintiff allegedly suffered injuries when Defendants, due to negligent maintenance, operation, and ownership of a motor vehicle, collided with Plaintiff’s motor vehicle. (Complaint ¶¶ 5-7.)

On June 6, 2024, the matter was reassigned to the Honorable Judge Moskowitz in Department B of Van Nuys Courthouse East, effective June 24, 2024.

On October 11, 2024, Honda filed the instant motion for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.

On December 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed their opposition. 

On December 27, 2024, Honda filed their reply.

 

II.       MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

Honda moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff cannot establish Honda’s vicarious liability for Pietrzak’s alleged negligence; and (2) Plaintiff's claim is barred as a matter of law by the Graves Amendment. (49 U.S.C. § 30106)(Motion p. 6.)

 

III.       LEGAL STANDARDS

Burden on Summary Judgment or Summary Adjudication 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, the Court must apply a three-step analysis: (1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has met his or her initial burden; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue. (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) “[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.” (Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.) A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 437c(p)(2).) 

A moving defendant may satisfy the initial burden to show that one or more elements cannot be established by “presenting evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action or by relying on the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses to show that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, evidence to establish that element.” (Schmidt v. Citibank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 1109, 1119; Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 856 [it is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.]; Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.) “The defendant must present evidence, typically in the form of factually devoid discovery responses, showing that plaintiff does not have and cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.” (Ibid.) 

Unless and until a defendant meets that burden, the plaintiff has no burden to present controverting evidence. (Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468 [“There is no obligation on the opposing party . . . to establish anything by affidavit unless and until the moving party has by affidavit stated facts establishing every element . . . necessary to sustain a judgment in his favor.”].) If a defendant fails to meet that burden, summary judgment must be denied, even if the plaintiff fails to file an opposition and fails to proffer any evidence. 

On the other hand, if the defendant “has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff . . . to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP § 437c(p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials in its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.” (Ibid.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

IV.       DISCUSSION

Judicial Notice

            Honda’s October 11, 2024, request for judicial notice of relevant excerpts of American Honda Finance Corporation’s Form 10-K Annual Report filed with the United States Securities and Exchange Commission for the fiscal year ending on March 31, 2024, is granted. (Cal. Evid. Code §§ 451, 452, and 453.)

 

Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff’s December 19, 2024, evidentiary objections are overruled as to Nos. 1, 2, and 3. Pietrzak has sufficient personal knowledge of his employment terms and benefits with Honda. (Cal. Evid. Code § 702.)

 

Defendant’s Burden 

Honda meets its initial burden. 

Honda moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff cannot establish Honda’s vicarious liability for Pietrzak’s alleged negligence; and (2) Plaintiff's claim is barred as a matter of law by the Graves Amendment. (49 U.S.C. § 30106)(Motion p. 6.)

 

Vicarious Liability

Honda argues that Plaintiff’s cause of action for negligence against Honda fails as a matter of law because Plaintiff cannot prove Honda’s vicarious liability for Pietrzak’s alleged negligence. (Motion p. 11.) Plaintiff claims that Honda owed her a legal duty to use due care because Pietrzak was acting in the course and scope of his employment with Honda at the time of the collision. (Plaintiff’s Response to Separate Statement of Facts “PR-SSF” No. 17; Exh. D [Plaintiff’s responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, No. 2].)

To succeed on a claim for negligence, Plaintiff must show that: (1) defendant owed a legal duty to plaintiff to use due care; (2) defendant breached that duty; (3) defendant’s breach was the actual or proximate cause of plaintiff’s harm; and (4) plaintiff suffered damages as a result. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 292, 318; Ladd v. County of San Mateo (1996) 12 Cal.4th 913, 917.) “[V]icarious liability for torts is imposed by operation of law upon employers for acts of their employees within the course and scope of employment, or upon principals for the acts of their agents.” (Chee v. Amanda Goldt Property Management (2006) 143 Cal.App.4th 1360, 1375.) “Employers have been held liable for the wrongful and unauthorized acts of their employees where they were committed in the course of a series of acts of the agent which were authorized by the principal.” (Thorn v. City of Glendale (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 1379, 1382.) Employers may be vicariously liable for injuries caused by employees’ torts resulting or arising from pursuits of employers’ interests. (Farmers Ins. Group v. County of Santa Clara (1995) 11 Cal.4th 992, 1005.)

Honda argues that it cannot be vicariously liable for Pietrzak’s conduct because Honda did not employ Pietrzak. Instead, Pietrzak was employed by American Honda Finance Corp. (“AHFC”), a separate entity from movant American Honda Motor Co. (PR-SSF No. 4; Pietrzak Decl. ¶ 4.) Honda further argues that, during the underlying incident, Pietrzak was not acting within the course or scope of his employment with AHFC. Pietrzak was employed as a Projects Lead for AHFC, where his main responsibilities included leading, managing, and training a team of project managers for loan origination software applications. (PR-SSF No. 5; Pietrzak Decl. ¶ 4.) His job did not require the use of an automobile. (Ibid.) In fact, at the time of the incident, Pietrzak worked remotely from his home. (PR-SSF No. 6; Pietrzak Decl. ¶ 4.) Also at the time of the underlying incident, Pietrzak was driving from his home to Home Depot to shop for personal items. (PR-SSF No. 8; Pietrzak Decl. ¶ 3.) He was not working on that day and was not engaged in any activities for the benefit of his employer. (PR-SSF No. 10; Pietrzak Decl. ¶ 5.)

Therefore, Honda meets its initial burden by providing evidence that it cannot be vicariously liable for Pietrzak’s alleged negligence because Pietrzak was not acting within the course or scope of his employment, nor was he acting for Honda’s benefit.

 

The Graves Amendment

The Court also considers Honda’s argument that Plaintiff’s claim must fail pursuant to 49 USC § 30106 (The “Graves Amendment” or “Graves”). The Graves Amendment states in relevant part:

 

“An owner of a motor vehicle that rents or leases the vehicle to a person (or an affiliate of the owner) shall not be liable under the law of any State or political subdivision thereof, by reason of being the owner of the vehicle (or an affiliate of the owner), for harm to persons or property that results or arises out of the use, operation, or possession of the vehicle during the period of the rental or lease, if--

 

(1) the owner (or an affiliate of the owner) is engaged in the trade or business of renting or leasing motor vehicles; and

(2) there is no negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner (or an affiliate of the owner).” (49 USC § 30106(a) – 49 USC § 30106(a)(2).)”

 

Graves generally preempts all state statutory and common law to the extent those laws hold owners in the business of renting or leasing motor vehicles vicariously liable for the negligence of drivers, except when there is negligence or criminal wrongdoing on the part of the owner. (29 A.L.R. Fed. 2d 223.)

At the time of the underlying incident, Pietrzak was driving a 2019 Honda Ridgeline which he leased from Honda. (PR-SSF No. 12; Pietrzak Decl. ¶ 7.) Honda provides its Form 10-K for the fiscal year ending on March 31, 2024, which states that Honda “provide[s] various forms of financing in the United States and Canada to purchasers and lessees of Honda and Acura products.” (PR-SSF No. 21; Exh. E [Form 10-K].) As discussed supra, Honda did not act negligently or with criminal wrongdoing. (49 U.S.C. § 30106(a)(2).) Therefore, Honda meets their initial burden by providing evidence that Plaintiff’s claim for negligence against Honda is barred by the Graves Amendment.

 

Plaintiff’s Burden

The burden now shifts to Plaintiff to show a triable issue of material fact as to Honda’s vicarious liability or the Graves Amendment’s applicability.

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that: (1) Honda’s failure to assert a lack of an employment relationship between Pietrzak and Honda in its answer waived any such affirmative defense; and (2) Honda fails to provide admissible evidence that a lease existed between Pietrzak and Honda. (Opposition p. 2:5-14.)

 

Vicarious Liability

First, Plaintiff argues Honda failed to assert a lack of an employment relationship between Pietrzak and Honda in its initial answer and therefore waived any related affirmative defenses

“An affirmative defense must be alleged in [a party’s] answer or it is waived.” (Green v. Healthcare Services, Inc. (2021) 68 Cal.App.5th 407, 416.) “[O]bjections a defendant might have on the merits – including an objection that liability is barred by an affirmative defense – are ordinarily deemed ‘waived’ if the defendant does not raise them in its demurrer or answer to the complaint. [Citation.]” (Ibid.) For an affirmative defense to be sufficiently plead, the defendant must allege facts “averred as carefully and with as much detail as the facts which constitute the cause of action and are alleged in the complaint. [Citation.]” (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384.) However, a court may permit a defendant to raise an affirmative defense for the first time in its summary judgment motion absent a showing of prejudice to the plaintiff. (Nieto v. Blue Shield of Cal. Life & Health Ins. Co. (2010) 181 Cal.App.4th 60, 75.) The plaintiff does not suffer prejudice so long as they have adequate notice and the opportunity to respond to the affirmative defense raised in a motion for summary judgment. (Cruey v. Gannett Co. (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 356, 367.)

Honda answered Plaintiff’s complaint on April 8, 2022. In its answer, Honda did not notice the affirmative defense of vicarious liability. However, Honda did so sufficiently in its motion for summary judgment, which Plaintiff had adequate notice of and the opportunity to respond on the merits. Therefore, the Court finds that Honda properly raised vicarious liability as an affirmative defense in its motion for summary judgment.

 

Admissible Evidence of a Lease

Second, Plaintiff argues that Honda fails to provide admissible evidence that a lease existed between Pietrzak and Honda and that, as a result, Honda fails to provide sufficient evidence showing that the Graves Amendment bars Plaintiff’s claim.

Specifically, Plaintiff relies on Cal. Evid. Code § 1523(a), which states that “oral testimony is not admissible to prove the content of a writing.” (Cal. Evid. Code § 1523(a).) However, such oral testimony “is not made inadmissible by subdivision (a) if the proponent does not have possession or control of a copy if the writing and the original is lost or has been destroyed without fraudulent intent on the part of the proponent of the evidence.” (Evid. Code § 1523(b).) Here, as evidence that Honda leased the subject vehicle to Pietrzak, Honda relies on Pietrzak’s declaration that he leased the vehicle as part of his compensation package with AHFC. (See PR-SSF Nos. 11-14.) Honda fails to provide any other evidence of the lease. However, in its reply, Honda notes that it has been unable to procure the lease agreement, and that Plaintiff has been aware of this fact. (Reply p. 8:23-27; Julian Decl. ¶ 2.) To support its argument, Honda provides its responses to Plaintiff’s Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, dated February 5, 2024. (Julian Decl. ¶ 2; Exh. F.) Here, Honda advised Plaintiff that a diligent search and reasonable inquiry was made to locate the lease agreement but was ultimately unsuccessful. (Exh. F No. 36.) Therefore, the Court finds that Evid. Code § 1523(b) applies and Pietrzak’s declaration is admissible to show that Honda leased the subject vehicle to Pietrzak.

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff fails to meet their burden showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to Honda’s vicarious liability or applicability of the Graves Amendment.

 

V.       CONCLUSION

            For the foregoing reasons, Honda’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED.

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from a motor vehicle accident. Plaintiffs Pedram Akbarineku (“Pedram”), Amanda Johnson (“Johnson”), Theodore Akbarineku (“Theodore”), a minor by and through his guardian ad litem Pedram Akbarineku, and Wyatt Akbarineku (“Wyatt”), a minor by and through his guardian ad litem Pedram Akbarineku (collectively “Plaintiffs”) allege that, on October 1, 2022, Defendants Earl G. Robinson (“Earl”), Tanya Schneider Robinson (“Tanya”), and Does 1 to 20 (collectively “Defendants”) negligently entrusted and operated a motor vehicle, colliding with Plaintiffs’ motor vehicle and causing injuries thereof.

 

On December 5, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant unopposed motion for a nunc pro tunc order correcting the date of filing of their complaint pursuant to Cal. Rules of Court 5.560(f).

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Pursuant to CCP § 473(d), the court may, upon motion of the injured party, or its own motion, correct clerical mistakes in its judgment or orders as entered, to conform to the judgment or order directed, and may, on motion of either party after notice to the other party, set aside any void judgment or order. Additionally, CRC 5.560(f) permits the court, nunc pro tunc, to correct any clerical errors in judgments, orders, or other parts of the record at any time. (CRC 5.560(f).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

The record reflects that Plaintiff’s complaint was filed on October 8, 2024. On September 27, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel filed Plaintiff’s complaint via the court’s e-filing portal. (Moghtader Decl. ¶ 7; Exh. 1 [filing confirmation].) On September 30 and October 1, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel contacted the Civil Filing Call Center to ensure that the filing would meet the statute of limitations deadline of October 1, 2024. (Ibid. at ¶ 8.) On each occasion, court staff assured Plaintiff’s counsel that the complaint would be processed in time. (Ibid.) However, on October 8, 2024, Plaintiff’s counsel received notice that their e-filing had been rejected for failure to include the first Plaintiff’s name in the data field and omission of the phrase “a minor by and through his guardian ad litem” for the minor plaintiffs. (Ibid. at ¶ 11; Exh. 2 [rejection notice].) Plaintiff’s counsel believes these errors were the result of a technical glitch and attaches his records as confirmation that he correctly completed the complaint. (Ibid. at ¶ 12; Exh. 3.)

 

Plaintiff’s counsel resubmitted the filing and received notice that the filing was accepted with an official filing date of October 8, 2024. (Moghtader Decl. ¶ 14; Exh. 4 [acceptance notice].) This filing date exceeds the statute of limitations for Plaintiffs’ complaint and, if not corrected, will significantly prejudice Plaintiffs. (Ibid. at ¶¶ 16-18.)

 

In the interests of justice, and having found good cause, the Court grants Plaintiffs’ motion for a nunc pro tunc order correcting Plaintiffs’ date of filing pursuant to CCP § 473(d) and CRC 5.560(f).

 

CONCLUSION

 

Plaintiffs’ motion for a nunc pro tunc order correcting Plaintiffs’ date of filing is GRANTED. The Court will issue a nunc pro tunc order amending the filing date of Plaintiffs’ complaint to September 27, 2024.
