DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 22STCV16372    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: B

Marshall Krupp V. Kroenke sports & entertainment, llc, et al.

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

Date of Hearing:         July 31, 2024                          Trial Date:       February 10, 2025

Department:                B                                             Case No.:        22STCV16372

 

Moving Party:             Defendants Kroenke Sports & Entertainment, LLC; Stadco LA,                                          LLC; Pincay Re, LLC; and Chargers Football, LLC

Responding Party:      Plaintiff Marshall Krupp

 

BACKGROUND

           

Factual and Procedural Background

            This case stems from a trip and fall suffered by Marshall Krupp (Plaintiff) while attending a Chargers football game at SoFi Stadium. Plaintiff filed an initial Complaint on May 17, 2022 against Kroenke Sports & Entertainment, LLC (Kroenke); Stadco LA, LLC (Stadco); Chargers Football Company, LLC (Chargers); Chargers Football Company[1]; and Contemporary Services Corporation (CSC)[2] (collectively, Defendants). On September 15, 2022, an amendment to the Complaint was made to substitute Doe 2 for Pincay Re, LLC (Pincay).

 

            On November 14, 2021, at approximately 12:00 p.m., while visiting SoFi Stadium located at 1001 Stadium Drive, Inglewood, CA 90301, Plaintiff alleges that he tripped over a curb. (Complaint, ¶¶1 and 15.) Plaintiff filed suit alleging (1) premises liability and (2) negligence against all Defendants.

 

            The motion now before the Court is Defendant Kroenke, Stadco, Pincay, and Chargers’ (Moving Defendants) Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication as to Plaintiff Marshall Krupp (the Motion). Plaintiff opposes the Motion; Moving Defendants file a reply.           

 

Objections

            Concurrently filed with their opposition papers, Plaintiff files objections to the Declarations of Mike Forrester (Forrester Decl.). Mike Forrester (Forrester) is the asset manager for Defendant Stadco. There are two declarations authored by Forrester submitted with the moving papers, the first executed on August 4, 2023, the second executed on October 24, 2023. All objections as to both declarations authored by Forrester are overruled.      

 

            Concurrently filed with their reply papers, Moving Defendants submit their own objections to the Declarations of Tom G. Antunovich and Philip L. Rosecu. The Court rules as follows:

 

Sustained: 1, 2, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14  

Overruled: 3, 4, 5, 6, 7,  

 

Tentative Ruling

            Defendant Kroenke, Stadco, Pincay, and Chargers’ Motion for Summary Judgment or Adjudication as to Plaintiff Marshall Krupp is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Legal Standard

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 

 

            “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

 

            As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

Analysis

             In their moving papers, Moving Defendants make two primary arguments: (1) the trivial defect doctrine bars liability against Moving Defendants and (2) Plaintiff signed a release that relieves Moving Defendants of liability. Because of this, Moving Defendants contend that there is no triable issue of material fact, and summary judgment should be granted in their favor. In opposition, Plaintiff presents two primary counter arguments: (1) the trivial defect doctrine is not a proper ground for summary judgment and (2) Moving Defendants fail to carry their burden of producing evidence to negate an element of either of the two causes of action. As explained below, the Court disagrees and grants the Motion.

 

A.    Trivial Defect Doctrine

            In moving for summary judgment, the first contention made by Moving Defendants is that the curb Plaintiff tripped over was not defective, and any defect or nonconformity was trivial. (Moving Papers, pgs. 7-8.) In opposition, Plaintiff argues the “Trivial Defect Doctrine” is not a proper ground in which to base this Motion because it is an affirmative defense that was never asserted in Moving Defendants’ answer. The Court disagrees. The trivial defect doctrine is not an affirmative defense, but “an aspect of duty that a plaintiff must plead and prove.” (Nunez v. City of Redondo Beach (2022) 81 Cal.App. 5th 749, 757 (Nunez).)

 

            It is well within the court’s discretion to determine whether the alleged defect is insignificant or trivial. (Caloroso v. Hathaway, 122 Cal.App.4th 922, 928 […“there is no need for expert opinion. It is well within the common knowledge of lay judges and jurors just what type of a defect in a sidewalk is dangerous.”].) With this in mind, the Court considers the evidence presented by both parties.

 

            The Court notes that there are relevant facts presented in the parties’ separate statements; for example, Moving Defendants proffer that Plaintiff testified that the subject curb was approximately three-quarters of an inch in height. (SS, UMF 7.) Plaintiff objects to this being proffered as fact, and cites Reeves v. Safeway Stores, Inc. (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 95, 106 (Reeves). The Court agrees with Reeves that parties should exercise caution with what they contend is fact versus admissible evidence. We also agree with Reeves that what a deponent says in a deposition is evidence of a material fact. (Id.) Plaintiff may dispute whether the curb was three-quarters of an inch in height, however, Plaintiff may not dispute what was stated in a recorded deposition. (See Moving Defendants’ Evidence, Exh. B, pg. 99, lines 3-5.) The Court recognizes what deponents perceive is not always fact, however a plaintiff’s deposition is given significant weight, in conjunction with other evidence submitted. (Turley v. Familian Corp. (2017) 18.Cal.App.5th 969, 976.) In opposition to Moving Defendants’ evidence, Plaintiff offers the declaration of Philip L. Rosecu (Rosecu Decl.).

 

            Rosecu notes in his declaration that the incident area where the curb is located is sloped and then turns flush with the surrounding walkway. (See Rosecu Decl., ¶7 and Figures 1, 2, 3, and 4.) Rosecu states that the curb has a vertical face with a height differential of up to 2 inches. (Rosecu Decl., ¶7.) However, “Statements made in a deposition govern and prevail over contrary declarations.” (Turley, supra, at 976.) Plaintiff is clear and consistent in their deposition stating that their estimation of the curb height was three-quarters to an inch, twice (See Moving Defendants’ Evidence, Exh. B, pg. 95, lines 3-4 & pg. 99, lines 3-5.) Nothing in Plaintiff’s testimony regarding the curb height is ambiguous. Moreover, there is no dispute as to the conditions at the time, which were clear and sunny with no overcast, supporting Plaintiff’s estimation. (See UMF 9.) Here, the Court cannot dismiss Plaintiff’s deposition in favor of a declaration, Turley advises the contrary. With the three-quarter inch estimation given by Plaintiff, the Court can rule that the height differential was trivial as noted in Huckey v. City of Temecula (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 1092, 1107 [“Sidewalk elevations ranging from three-quarters of an inch to one and one-half inches have generally been held trivial as a matter of law.”].)

 

B.     “Aguilar” Burden

            The Court rules that Moving Defendants have met their burden as defined under the summary judgment standard above. Plaintiff contends that Moving Defendants have an “Aguilar” burden, and that they failed to carry it. The Court disagrees. The burden Plaintiff’s so heavily rely on is simply a restatement of the well-known summary judgment standard: the moving party must present evidence that negates an element or presents a defense that shifts the burden to the opposing party. As to the element negated, there must be no triable issue of material fact, the opposing party must show there is.

 

            “The elements of a cause of action for premises liability are the same as those for negligence: duty, breach, causation, and damages.” (Castellon v. U.S. Bancorp (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 994, 998.) “Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158, quotation marks omitted.) “While a landowner is not the insurer of a visitor's safety, a landowner must exercise ordinary care by making reasonable inspections of the premises to ascertain whether any dangerous conditions exist on the property.” (Jones v. Awad (2019) 39 Cal.App.4th 1200, 1208.)

 

            Because the causes of action for both premises liability and negligence rely on the same elements – duty, breach, causation, & damages – a negation of any one of these elements is fatal to both causes of action. Here, as the curb height has been deemed trivial, no breach of duty has occurred. Therefore, the Motion is granted.

 

C.     Plaintiff Released Moving Defendants From Liability

            Moving Defendants make the second argument that in addition to the curb height being trivial, Plaintiff released all Moving Defendants from liability. Provided in their moving papers, Moving Defendants proffer the relevant portions of section 9 of an agreement, the section is titled “Assumption of Risk; Indemnification.” In relevant part the release provides:

 

“Neither the Stadium Parties nor their respective officers, owners, directors, employees, and agents (collectively, the “Indemnitees”) will be liable to Licensee or responsible for, and Licensee, for itself and each of Licensee’s Guests, hereby assumes, all risk for any loss, damage, or injury to any person or to any property of Licensee or Licensee’s Guests in or around the Stadium (including the parking lots) arising out of, during, or related to their attendance…” (Moving Paper, 5:17-28.)

 

            In opposition to the argument Moving Defendants are released from liability, Plaintiff contends that parties may not in advance release or waive liability for gross negligence, relying on City of Santa Barbara v. Superior Court (2007) 41 Cal.4th 747 (City of Santa Barbara). However, in no way has Plaintiff alleged or argued that Moving Defendants are liable for gross negligence, merely negligence. The case Plaintiff relies on differentiates between the two, noting that gross negligence is “an extreme departure from the ordinary standard of conduct.” (City of Santa Barbara, supra, 754.)  Moving Defendants have proffered clear evidence that Plaintiff signed the release. (Moving Defendants’ Notice of Errata Concerning Evidence, Exh. G.) Aside from contending that gross negligence cannot be waived, Plaintiff’s opposition papers are silent on this argument. Accordingly, and independent of the reasons above, the Motion is granted.

 

D.    Other Arguments

 

            Plaintiff presents two other arguments in opposition: (1) that the Notice of Motion and Separate Statement are non-conforming with CRC Rule 3.1350(b), (d), and (h) precluding Plaintiff from notice and (2) that Moving Defendants fail to negate negligence based on other theories.

 

            First, there is no evidence that Plaintiff did not receive notice of the Motion and accompanying Separate Statement nor does Plaintiff argue they suffered prejudice. Moreover, California has a strong public policy in favor of deciding cases on the merits, especially if the opposing party has suffered no prejudice. (Oliveros¿v. County of Los Angeles¿(2004) 120 Cal.App.4th 1389, 1398-1399. Also see CCP § 583.130.)

 

            Second, the Complaint makes no mention of metal fences, gates, or any other barrier being the cause of Plaintiff’s harm. It is clear both from the Complaint, and the opposition papers filed with the Court with regard to the instant Motion that the subject incident concerns the curb and whether it was a dangerous condition.

 

 

CONCLUSION

            Accordingly, Defendant Kroenke, Stadco, Pincay, and Chargers’ Motion for Summary Judgment as to Plaintiff Marshall Krupp is GRANTED.

DISCUSSION



 



Legal Standard



            The function of a motion for summary
judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing
party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an
order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) CCP
Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the
evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’
and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no
triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to
judgment as a matter of law.” (Adler v.
Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.) 



 



            “The function of the pleadings in a
motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function
of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable
issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12
Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI
Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)



 



            As to each claim as framed by the
complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial
burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to
establish a defense. (CCP § 437c(p)(2); Scalf
v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts
“liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary
judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006)
39 Cal.4th 384, 389.)



 



            Once the defendant has met that
burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one
or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To
establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must
produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster
v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)
