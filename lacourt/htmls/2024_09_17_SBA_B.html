DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV03630    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

GLORIA CALDWELL, by and through her power of attorney, TERRIE BAIRD,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV03630

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

DRIFTWOOD HEALTHCARE CENTER, LLC, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          September 17, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Driftwood Healthcare Center, LLC

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Gloria Caldwell

(1)   Motion for Terminating Sanctions, or in the alternative, for Issue Sanctions, or in the alternative, for Monetary Sanctions for Failure to Comply with Court Orders Regarding Responses to Special Interrogatories

(2)   Motion for Terminating Sanctions, or in the alternative, for Issue Sanctions, or in the alternative, for Monetary Sanctions for Failure to Comply with Court Orders Regarding Responses to Requests for Production

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers. 

RULING

            The motions are DENIED.

BACKGROUND

On October 31, 2023, plaintiff Gloria Caldwell by and through her power of attorney Terrie Baird filed a complaint against Driftwood Healthcare Center, LLC dba Driftwood Healthcare Center, Citrus Wellness Center, LLC, Ensemble Healthcare, LLC, and Rechnitz Citrus, GP for (1) negligence, (2) violations of Elder Abuse Act, and (3) violations of Resident Rights.

On May 22, 2024, the Court granted plaintiff’s motion for a trial preference.

On July 3, 2024, the Court granted defendant’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories, set one, nos. 2.2, 2.12, 2.13, 4.1, 6.3-6.7, 8.4, 12.1, 14.1, 14.2, and 17.1, regarding requests for admissions, nos. 1-11 and 13.34. and to respond to no. 6.2 and to pay sanctions.

On July 10, 2024, the Court granted defendant’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories, set one, nos. 1-8, 10, and 12-30 and demand for production of documents, set one, nos. 1-5, 7-8, 10-18, 21-22, 24, 27-29, and 31-47 and to pay sanctions.

On July 18, 2024, the parties stipulated and the court entered an order continuing trial.

On September 3, 2024, the Court denied defendant Driftwood Healthcare Center, LLC’s motion for summary judgment.

Trial is set for October 21, 2024.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

If a party fails to comply with a court order compelling discovery responses or attendance at a deposition, the court may impose monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating sanctions.  CCP § 2025.450(h) (depositions); § 2030.290(c) (interrogatories); § 2031.300(c) (demands for production of documents).  CCP § 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . .  [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . . .”  CCP § 2023.010 provides that “[m]isuses of the discovery process include, but are not limited to, the following: . . . (d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery. . . . (g) Disobeying a court order to provide discovery. . . .”

“The trial court may order a terminating sanction for discovery abuse ‘after considering the totality of the circumstances:  [the] conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.’”  Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal. App. 4th 377, 390 (citation omitted).  “Generally, ‘[a] decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’”  Los Defensores, 223 Cal. App. 4th at 390 (citation omitted).

“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination.  ‘Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.’ . . . [C]ontinuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal. App. 4th 967, 992 (citation omitted).  “’A decision to order terminating sanctions should not be made lightly.  But where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’”  Id. (citation omitted).  “A trial court has broad discretion to impose discovery sanctions, but two facts are generally a prerequisite to the imposition of nonmonetary sanctions . . . :  (1) . . . there must be a failure to comply with a court order, and (2) the failure must be willful.”  Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal. App. 4th 1315, 1327 (citation omitted).  

A trial court’s order to impose terminating sanctions will be reversed only if it “was arbitrary, capricious, or whimsical.”  Creed-21 v. City of Wildomar (2017) 18 Cal. App. 5th 690, 702 (citation omitted).

DISCUSSION

Defendant Driftwood Healthcare Center, LLC dba Driftwood Healthcare Center requests an order for terminating sanctions or, in the alternative, for issue sanctions or, in the alternative, for monetary sanctions against plaintiff.

As stated above, on July 3, 2024, the Court granted defendant’s motion to compel further responses to form interrogatories, set one, nos. 2.2, 2.12, 2.13, 4.1, 6.3-6.7, 8.4, 12.1, 14.1, 14.2, and 17.1, regarding requests for admissions, nos. 1-11 and 13.34. and to respond to no. 6.2 and to pay sanctions.  On July 10, 2024, the Court granted defendant’s motion to compel further responses to special interrogatories, set one, nos. 1-8, 10, and 12-30 and demand for production of documents, set one, nos. 1-5, 7-8, 10-18, 21-22, 24, 27-29, and 31-47 and to pay sanctions.

Defendant asserts that on July 30, 2024, plaintiff served her second supplemental responses and second supplemental document production, consisting of 5614 pages.  On August 6, 2024, defense counsel sent meet and confer correspondence to plaintiff’s counsel seeking code-compliant responses by August 13, 2024.  On August 13, 2024, defense counsel granted plaintiff a one-week extension to provide supplemental responses to discovery with an extension to file motions to compel.  On August 30, 2024, plaintiff served her third supplemental responses and third supplemental document production.

Defendant argues that plaintiff has failed to comply with the Court’s orders by failing to respond “with particularity, providing incomplete and incomprehensible responses” as to special interrogatories, set one, nos. 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 13, and 18-23 and demand for production of documents, nos. 1-3, 7, 8, 10-18, and 24 and production of documents, which, defendant asserts, only partially complies with CCP §2031.280.  Defendant also contends that plaintiff has not paid the monetary sanctions.

The Court notes that a court may not issue a terminating sanction for failure to pay a monetary discovery sanction.  Newland v. Superior Court (1995) 40 Cal. App. 4th 608, 610, 615.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that sanctions are not warranted because defendant’s arguments have no merit, plaintiff’s alleged discovery “failures” have been remedied, were inadvertent, and were not prejudicial.  Plaintiff asserts that in a further effort to comply with the Court’s order, on September 4, 2024, plaintiff served her fourth supplemental responses to correct any alleged deficiency.  Plaintiff explains that the associate assigned to the case left the firm on August 23, 2024 and that the paralegal assigned to the associate “unexpectedly took leave.”  As a result, plaintiff contends, plaintiff’s current counsel and the firm was unaware of the outstanding court order and monetary sanctions.

In reply, defendant argues that plaintiff’s fourth supplemental responses are not code-compliant.

The Court rules as follows:  The Court finds that plaintiff has not engaged in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process because plaintiff did not willfully fail to comply with the Court’s July 3 and July 10 orders to serve further responses and to produce responsive documents.

The motions are DENIED.

 

ORDER

The motions are DENIED.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of the ruling.

THE PICERNE GROUP, INC., et al.,

Hearing Date:                          September 17, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendants The Picerne Group, Inc. and Wallcraft Drywall, Inc.

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Antonio Zaragoza

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action in the FAC.

            Defendants are ordered to file an answer within twenty days.

BACKGROUND

            On February 23, 2024, Antonio Zaragoza filed a complaint against The Picerne Group, Inc. and Wallcraft Drywall Inc. for (1) negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of employees and (2) negligence.

            On June 25, 2024, the Court sustained with leave to amend defendants’ demurrer to the first and second causes of action.

            On July 15, 2024, plaintiff filed a FAC for (1) negligent hiring, supervision, or retention of employees and (2) negligence.

 

            On July 31, 2024, plaintiff filed an amendment designating Mario De La Torre dba De La Torre Construction as Doe 1.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendants The Picerne Group, Inc. and Wallcraft Drywall, Inc. demur to the first and second causes of action in the FAC on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action because they are barred by the Privette doctrine.

The FAC alleges that on March 3, 2022, plaintiff was employed by Trencore Plastering, Inc. to provide stucco, plastering, and/or other work to the exterior of a building under construction located at 24000 Crenshaw Blvd., Torrance.  FAC, ¶1.  Defendant The Picerne Group, Inc. was the general contractor at the worksite.  Picerne retained control over the safety at the worksite and interactions between contractors.  Picerne held daily meetings with defendants concerning safety protocols at the worksite, instructing, directing, managing, and/or coordinating defendants’ actions at the worksite.  Id., ¶2.  Defendant Wallcraft Drywall, Inc. was a subcontractor responsible for drywalling at the worksite.  Id., ¶3.  Defendant De La Torre Construction, a subcontractor, was delegated by Wallcraft Drywll to perform scrapping work at the worksite.  However, Wallcraft Drywall retained control over the manner of performance of some part of the scrapping work entrusted to De La Torre Construction, including instructing, coordinating, delegating, supervising, and/or managing this work.  Id., ¶4. 

The FAC further alleges that in their respective capacities at the worksite, defendants were required to create, implement, execute, and/or follow safety protocols to protect workers at the worksite, including restricting interactions between workers contracted to work in the interior of the building with workers contracted to work on the exterior of the building; clearly designating a safety barricade to make workers aware of dangerous areas at the worksite, including disposal sites for scraps and waste; instructing workers to guard or spot the disposal site; and/or otherwise safely removing scraps or other waste at the worksite.  Id., ¶5.  Defendants failed to create, implement, execute, and/or follow these safety protocols, creating a dangerous environment at the worksite.  Id., ¶6. 

The FAC further alleges that on March 3, 2022, De La Torre Construction performed scrapping work at the worksite, including the removal of drywall scraps from the interior of the building.  Id., ¶7.  Defendants failed to barricade an area or otherwise warn workers of the disposal site for drywall scraps, failed to designate an employee to act as a spotter for the disposal site, and failed to follow other safety protocols to protect workers at the worksite.  Rather, defendants instructed and/or permitted De La Torre Construction to throw drywall scraps from a fifth-floor balcony to the ground without warning or a spotter, endangering workers near the exterior of the building.  Id., ¶8.

The FAC further alleges that on March 3, 2022, at 9:30 a.m., plaintiff climbed a ladder to access scaffolding on the exterior of the worksite.  There were no warnings, tape, signs, or spotters present to indicate that the area was a disposal site.  Without warning, employees of De La Torre Construction threw large scraps of drywall from the fifth floor balcony, landing on plaintiff below.  Id., ¶9.  Plaintiff was struck in the head by a sheet of drywall.  The impact threw him from the ladder to the ground.  Id., ¶10.  As a result of defendants’ negligence, plaintiff suffered severe personal injuries.  Id., ¶11.

Defendants argue that the causes of action are barred by the Privette doctrine and the exclusive remedy of worker’s compensation as set forth in Labor Code §3602.  Defendants explain that Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal. 4th 689 holds that if an employee of an independent contractor hired to do work suffers a work-related injury, the hirer is generally not liable for on-the-job injuries to the independent contractor’s workers, and that an employee injured in the course and scope of performance is already entitled to recover from their employer under the state worker’s compensation system.  Defendants note that the Privette doctrine has been extended to apply to general contractors.  Defendants contend that as alleged, Picerne was the general contractor and that under Privette it would generally not be liable for plaintiff’s injuries on the understanding that plaintiff’s employer (Trencore Plastering) is responsible for his work-related injuries through the worker’s compensation system as his employer is responsible for supervising plaintiff and had a direct duty to him to comply with the standard of care in ensuring his safety.  As to Wallcraft, defendants argue, it should also fall within the scope of Privette because plaintiff alleges that it hired De La Torre and delegated its demolition duties to De La Torre, and thus cannot be held liable for any alleged negligence by De La Torre.  Defendants also argue that none of the Privette doctrine exceptions apply, including concealed hazard and retained control.  As to retained control exception, defendants contend that the allegations do not rise to the level of “affirmative control” necessary to invoke the exception.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the Privette doctrine does not apply because plaintiff is seeking to hold defendants directly liable that they breached duties owed to plaintiff and that the breaches were a substantial factor in plaintiff’s harm.  Further, plaintiff argues, he has adequately pleaded the retained control exception under Privette.

In reply, defendants reiterate that Privette applies, and that plaintiff has not adequately pled an exception. 

The Court rules as follows:  “The Privette doctrine holds that a hirer generally delegates to an independent contractor all responsibility for workplace safety and is not liable for injuries sustained by the contractor or its workers while on the job.”  Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021) 12 Cal.5th 29, 40.  There are, however, two exceptions to the Privette doctrine.  “The first exception . . . is usually referred to as the retained control exception.  It applies if:  (1) the hirer retains control over the manner in which the contractor performs the work; (2) the hirer actually exercises its retained control by involving itself in the work such that the contractor is not entirely free to do the work in its own manner; and (2) the hirer’s exercise of retained control affirmatively contributes to the worker’s injury. . . . The second exception . . . is usually referred to as the concealed hazard exception.  If applies if the hirer is also an owner or possessor of land, and if ‘the landowner knew, or should have known, of a latent or concealed preexisting hazardous condition on its property, the contractor did not know and could not have reasonably discovered this hazardous condition, and the landowner failed to warn the contractor about this condition.’”  Miller v. Roseville Lodge No. 1293 (2022) 83 Cal. App. 5th 825, 833-34 (citations omitted).

“Affirmative contribution occurs where a general contractor is actively involved in, or asserts control over, the manner of performance of the contracted work.  Such an assertion of control occurs, for example, when the principal employer directs that the contracted work be done by use of a certain mode or otherwise interferes with the means and methods by which the work is to be accomplished.”  Millard v. Biosources, Inc. (2007) 156 Cal. App. 4th 1338, 1348 (citations omitted).  The “failure to institute particular safety measures at the jobsite is also not actionable absent some evidence that [defendants] had agreed to implement such measures.”  Ruiz v. Herman Weissker, Inc. (2005) 130 Cal. App. 4th 52, 66.  The failure to exercise retained control does not constitute an affirmative contribution to an injury.  Such affirmative contribution must be based on a “negligent exercise of control.”  Id.

The Court finds the allegations in the FAC are sufficient at the pleading stage to meet the elements that plaintiff’s claims fall within the retained control exception to the Privette doctrine.  The allegations are sufficient to show that defendants retained a sufficient degree of authority over the safety of the work and that defendants exercised that control in a manner that affirmatively contributed to plaintiff's injury.  See FAC, ¶¶2, 4, 5, 6.  Under the first cause of action for premises liability, plaintiff further alleges that defendants so negligently and carelessly constructed, inspected, maintained, contracted, subcontracted, engineered, designed, owned or controlled the subject premises and/or failed to properly train, direct, supervise or coordinated its employees and operations at the worksite.  FAC, ¶23.  Picerne Group retained control over the safety and interactions between contractors.  Picerne Group’s conduct in carrying out its duties at the worksite affirmatively contributed to plaintiff’s injuries.  Id., ¶24.  Wallcraft Drywall retained control over the scrapping work at the worksite.  Wallcraft Drywall’s conduct in carrying out its duties at the worksite affirmatively contributed to plaintiff’s injuries.  Id., ¶25.  Under the second cause of action for negligence, plaintiff alleges that defendants were required to create, implement, execute, and/or follow safety protocols to protect workers at the worksite.  Id., ¶29.  Defendants so negligently and carelessly constructed, inspected, maintained, contracted, subcontracted, engineered, designed, owned or controlled the subject premises and/or failed to properly train, direct, supervise or coordinated its employees and operations at the worksite.  Id., ¶30.  Defendants failed to create, implement, execute, and/or follow these safety protocols, creating a dangerous environment at the worksite.  Id., ¶31.  See also pars. ¶¶32, 33.

            The demurrer is thus OVERRULED.

 

ORDER

            The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first and second causes of action in the FAC.

Defendants are ordered to file answers within twenty days.

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Hyundai Motor America

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Jennifer Monzell Pryer

Motion to Compel Arbitration

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED. 

BACKGROUND

            On March 29, 2024, plaintiff Jennifer Monzell Pryer filed a complaint against defendant Hyundai Motor America for Song-Beverly Act violations and against South Bay Hyundai, LLC for negligent repair.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (c) A party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action . . . with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact. . . . (d) . . . . If the court determines that a party to the arbitration is also a party to litigation in a pending court action . . . with a third party as set forth under subdivision (c) herein, the court (1) may refuse to enforce the arbitration agreement . . . ; (2) may order intervention or joinder as to all or only certain issues; (3) may order arbitration among the parties who have agreed to arbitration and stay the pending court action . . . pending the outcome of arbitration proceeding; or (4) may stay arbitration pending the outcome of the court action or special proceeding.” 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Hyundai Motor America requests an order compelling plaintiff to binding arbitration.

            The complaint alleges that on January 28, 2023, plaintiff purchased a 2023 Hyundai Sonata.  These causes of action arise out of warranty and repair obligations of Hyundai in connection with the subject vehicle and for which Hyundai issued a written warranty.  Complaint, ¶9.  Hyundai warranted the subject vehicle and agreed to preserve and maintain the utility or performance of the vehicle or to provide compensation if there was a failure in such utility or performance.  In connection with the purchase, plaintiff received various warranties, including a five-year/60,000 mile express bumper to bumper warranty, a ten-year/100,000 mile powertrain warranty, which covers the engine and transmission.  Id., ¶10.  The subject vehicle was delivered to plaintiff with serious defects and nonconformities, including engine, electrical, and emission system defects.  Id., ¶11.  Plaintiff first presented the vehicle for repairs in March 2020 and reported the vehicle stuttering while driving.  Id., ¶12.  In September 2023, plaintiff presented the vehicle again for repairs and reported the vehicle shutting off unprompted, emitting an abnormal noise, and failing to restart, and the exhaust pipe emitting smoke.  Id., ¶13.  Plaintiff presented the vehicle for repairs in October 2023 and reported recurrent concerns.  Id., ¶14.

 

Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant asserts that when plaintiff purchased the subject vehicle on January 28, 2023, a Warranty accompanied the purchase, which included an agreement to arbitrate on pages 12-16. The section is titled, “BINDING ARBITRATION FOR CALIFORNIA VEHICLES ONLY.” 

It states in part:

“If you purchased or leased your Hyundai vehicle in the State of California, you and we, Hyundai Motor America, each agree that any claim or disputes between us (including between you and any of our affiliated companies) related to or arising out of your vehicle purchase, advertising for the vehicle, use of your vehicle, the performance of the vehicle, any service relating to the vehicle, the vehicle warranty, representations in the warranty, or the duties contemplated under the warranty, including without limitation claims related to false or misleading advertising, unfair competition, breach of contract or warranty, the failure to conform a vehicle to warranty, failure to repurchase or replace your vehicle, or claims for a refund or partial refund of your vehicle's purchase price (excluding personal injury claims), but excluding claims brought under the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act, shall be resolved by binding arbitration at either your or our election, even if the claim is initially filed in a court of law. If either you or we elect to resolve our dispute via arbitration (as opposed to in a court of law), such binding arbitration shall be administered by and through the American Arbitration Association (AAA) under its Consumer Arbitration Rules.”

 

It further states:

“IF YOU PURCHASED OR LEASED YOUR VEHICLE IN CALIFORNIA, YOUR WARRANTY IS MADE SUBJECT TO THE TERMS OF THIS BINDING ARBITRATION PROVISION. BY USING THE VEHICLE, OR REQUESTING OR ACCEPTING BENEFITS UNDER THIS WARRANTY, INCLUDING HAVING ANY REPAIRS PERFORMED UNDER WARRANTY, YOU AGREE TO BE BOUND BY THESE TERMS. IF YOU DO NOT AGREE WITH THESE TERMS, PLEASE CONTACT US AT OPT-OUT@HMAUSA.COM WITHIN THIRTY (30) DAYS OF YOUR PURCHASE OR LEASE TO OPT-OUT OF THIS ARBITRATION PROVISION.”

Defendant also contends that on January 30, 2023, plaintiff enrolled their vehicle in Hyundai’s Bluelink service through the Dealer-Assisted Enrollment Process.  In order to enroll in Bluelink services, customers must agree to the then-effective Connected Services Agreement (“CSA”).  In order for plaintiff to have enrolled in Hyundai’s Bluelink services, they would have had to click a box to acknowledge that they “read and agreed to the Blue Link Terms & Conditions” and then click the “Complete” button.  Defendant points out that the CSA provides an arbitration provision, which states:  “Hyundai and you agree to arbitrate any and all disputes and claims between us arising out of or relating to this Agreement, Connected Services, Connected Services Systems, Service Plans, the Vehicle . . . . This agreement to arbitrate is intended to be broadly interpreted and to make all disputes and claims between us subject to arbitration to the fullest extent permitted by law. . . . The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to:  claims based in contract, tort, warranty, statute, fraud, . . . .”  See Vijay Rao decl., Exhs. 1, 2; Ali Ameripour decl., Exh. 3.

Defendant also argues that it has standing to compel arbitration because it has provided the warranty directly to plaintiff in connection with their purchase of the vehicle.  Defendant contends that given that plaintiff has purchased the vehicle in California, is bringing this action pursuant to the Warranty, and has alleged that repairs have been performed under the Warranty, “it is clear that arbitration provision applies.”

In opposition, plaintiff argues that defendant failed to submit admissible evidence of such clause because Ameripour’s declaration does not authenticate the Owner’s Handbook.  Plaintiff further contends that the Federal Trade Commission has expressly decided that binding mandatory arbitration of any warranty claim is unlawful, even if the warranty claims arise under state law.

In reply, defendant argues that it has properly established the existence of the agreements to arbitrate and that plaintiff has “not even remotely attempted to prove the falsity of the purported agreements.”  See Ali Ameripour second decl.

The Court finds that arbitration agreements exist—in the 2023 Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information and under the Connected Services Agreement.  Plaintiff has not shown the falsity of the arbitration provisions.  Although plaintiff did not sign the arbitration provision in the Warranty, that is not dispositive because plaintiff accepted the benefits found within the Warranty.  See Cavalry SPV I, LLC v. Watkins (2019) 36 Cal. App. 4th 1070, 1081 (“[A] party may accept a contract by conduct, as well as by words.”).

 

Scope

Defendant argues that plaintiff’s claims fall squarely within the scope of the arbitration clauses as they arise directly out of the sale of the vehicle and the vehicle’s warranty.

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the CSA’s arbitration provision does not apply to plaintiff’s claims because the “clear language of the CSA indicates that arbitration only applies to claims related to the ‘connected services’ that are the subject of the agreement, not the statutory warranty claims brought by plaintiff.” 

The Court finds that the arbitration provision in the “Owner’s Handbook & Warranty Information,” specifically “Section 4 Hyundai Warranty Information” covers the scope of plaintiff’s claims as it covers “all disputes and claims between us relating to or arising out of . . . use or performance of your vehicle, or the vehicle warranty.”  As alleged in the complaint, Hyundai issued a written warranty and the claims asserted in the claim “arise out of warranty and repair obligations” of Hyundai.  The arbitration provision in the CSA also appears to encompass plaintiff’s claims (“The agreement to arbitrate otherwise includes, but is not limited to:  claims based in . . . warranty, statute, . . .”).

 

Enforceability

            Unconscionable?

“Courts analyze the unconscionability standard in Civil Code section 1670.5 as invoking elements of procedural and substantive unconscionability.  The procedural element of unconscionability focuses on whether the contract is one of adhesion.  Procedural unconscionability focuses on whether there is ‘oppression’ arising from an inequality of bargaining power or ‘surprise’ arising from buried terms in a complex printed form.  The substantive element addresses the existence of overly harsh or one-sided terms.  An agreement to arbitrate is unenforceable only if both the procedural and substantive elements are satisfied.  However, Armendariz held, ‘[T]he more substantively oppressive the contract term, the less evidence of procedural unconscionability is required to come to the conclusion that the term is unenforceable, and vice versa.’ (Armendariz, at 114).”  McManus v. CIBC World Markets Corp. (2003) 109 Cal. App. 4th 76, 87 (citations omitted).

In opposition, plaintiff argues that the arbitration provision in the Owner’s Handbook is procedurally unconscionable because plaintiff was never informed by Riverside Hyundai that any warranty claims against defendant would be subject to binding arbitration pursuant to an arbitration agreement that purportedly existed within the Owner’s Handbook out of which plaintiff would be required to opt.  Plaintiff asserts that she never signed any post-sale “arbitration agreement” because the subsequent “arbitration agreement” does not require a consumer’s express consent or contain an actual signature line.  Further, plaintiff argues, the “arbitration agreements” are pre-printed adhesion contracts drafted by defendant and that the provision in the Warranty’s Booklet was “buried”

Plaintiff also argues that the arbitration provisions are substantively unconscionable because they deprive plaintiff of a right to a jury trial and that she would receive “extremely limited due process” as discovery is limited.  Moreover, plaintiff contends, the Song-Beverly Act’s anti-waiver provision precludes arbitration because it would deprive plaintiff of a fundamental right to an action under Civil Code §1794.

In reply, defendant argues that the arbitration provisions are not unconscionable because plaintiff established mutual assent in their complaint to be bound by the warranty.  Defendant also asserts that plaintiff not only uses the warranty as a foundational basis for this action, but plaintiff concedes to having accepted the benefits under the warranty, and thus agreed to be bound by its terms.  Defendant further argues that plaintiff is estopped from refusing to comply with the arbitration provision that is contained in the warranty because she is attempting to enforce the warranty for plaintiff’s benefit.  Defendant also asserts that the arbitration provision is on the first page of the “Hyundai Warranty information” section and is clearly labeled in the table of contents.  Plaintiff was under no obligation to purchase the vehicle, and she had the opportunity to opt out.  Defendant also points out that AAA’s Consumer Arbitration Rules do allow for discovery and the exchange of information between the parties and that AAA’s Rule 22 allows for additional discovery and states that the arbitrator may determine whether further information is needed to provide for a fundamentally fair process.  See Ramirez v. Charter Communications, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal. 4th 478 (analyzing same AAA rules).

The Court finds that even if the Warranty’s arbitration provision is an adhesion contract, when there is no other indication of oppression other than the adhesive aspect of an agreement, the degree of procedural unconscionability is low.  Serpa v. California Surety Investigations, Inc. (2013) 215 Cal. App. 4th 695, 704.  Further, plaintiff had the ability to opt out of the arbitration clause.  Moreover, as in Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal. 4th 899, 931, “there is no reason in this case to conclude that plaintiff lacked power to bargain.  In general, nothing prevents purchasers of . . . vehicles from bargaining with dealers, even though dealers use form contracts, and nothing in the record shows that plaintiff could not bargain in this case.”  Moreover, the arbitration provision is not “buried” as the table of contents clearly identifies where the arbitration provision can be found and the opt-out provision is conspicuous (in bold and all capitals).  

As to substantive unconscionability, the Court finds little to none as the arbitration provisions do not lack mutuality.  The discovery rules apply equally to both parties, for example. 

The motion is thus GRANTED.

 

 ORDER

            The motion to compel arbitration is GRANTED.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
