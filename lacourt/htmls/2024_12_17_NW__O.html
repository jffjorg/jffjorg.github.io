I.              BACKGROUND 

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Caryl Laverna Burke (“Plaintiff”) alleges she was injured on December 24, 2020, when she slipped on a liquid as she exited a supermarket operated by Defendant Vons Companies, Inc. (“Vons”) located at 18439 Ventura Blvd., Tarzana, CA 91356. (See Complaint, ¶¶ 10-12.) Vons now moves for summary judgment as to both Plaintiff’s causes of action for general negligence and premises liability.

II.           PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On December 22, 2022, Plaintiff filed her Complaint, alleging (1) General Negligence; and (2) Premises Liability.

On January 25, 2023, Vons filed its Answer, indicating that Plaintiff erroneously named Albertsons Companies in addition to Vons.

On April 20, 2023, Vons filed a Cross-Complaint, alleging (1) Breach of Contract against Caliber Paving; (2) Implied Indemnity against Caliber Paving, Ennis-Flint, and Roes 1 to 25; (3) Contribution and Apportionment against Caliber Paving, Ennis-Flint, and Roes 1 to 25; and (4) Declaratory Relief against Caliber Paving, Ennis-Flint, and Roes 1 to 25.

On May 1, 2023, Plaintiff amended her Complaint to add Ennis-Flint as a defendant to her Complaint.

On May 4, 2023, Plaintiff amended her Complaint to correct Caliber Paving’s name from Caliber Painting, entity type unknown, to Caliber Paving Company, Inc.

On June 20, 2023, Caliber Paving filed its Answer to Plaintiff’s Complaint.

On July 21, 2023, the previously assigned judge granted Ennis-Flint’s unopposed Motion to Quash Service of Summons and Complaint for Lack of Personal Jursidiction and denied as moot Ennis-Flint’s Motion to Dismiss the Complaint on the Grounds of Inconvenient Forum.

On December 14, 2023, Vons dismissed its Cross-Complaint without prejudice.

On February 1, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Caliber Paving from her Complaint without prejudice.

On April 5, 2024, Plaintiff dismissed Ennis-Flint from the Complaint without prejudice.

On September 30, 2024, and again on October 1, 2024, Vons filed the instant Motion for Summary Judgment (the “Motion”) and supporting declarations.

On December 3, 2024, Plaintiff filed her Opposition as well as her declaration dated December 3, 2024 (“Burke Decl.”).

On December 12, 2024, Vons filed its Reply.  

III.        LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (See Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) In analyzing such motions, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.” (Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) Thus, summary judgment or summary adjudication is granted when, after the Court’s consideration of the evidence set forth in the papers and all reasonable inferences accordingly, no triable issues of fact exist, and the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) [1]; Villa v. McFarren (1995) 35 Cal.App.4th 733, 741.) 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the party moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate or establish an essential element. (See Scalf v. D.B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc.¿(2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) A motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication must be denied where the moving party’s evidence does not prove all material facts, even in the absence of any opposition or where the opposition is weak. (See Leyva v. Superior Court (1985) 164 Cal.App.3d 462, 475; Salesguevara v. Wyeth Labs., Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 379, 384 & 387.) When a plaintiff is the moving party, the plaintiff has a higher burden: it must “present evidence that would require a reasonable trier of fact to find any underlying material fact more likely than not.” (See Aguilar, 25 Cal.4th at 845 (emphasis added).) “The burden of the moving party is to persuade the court that there is no material fact for a reasonable trier of fact to find.” (PP Mortgage, Ltd. v. Bizar (2005) 126 Cal.App.4th 773, 776 (emphasis in original) (cleaned up).)

Once the moving party has met its burden, the burden shifts to the opposing party to show via specific facts that a triable issue of material fact exists to a cause of action or a defense thereto. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(o)(2).)

IV.         ANALYSIS

A.            Evidentiary Issues

The Court SUSTAINS Plaintiff’s evidentiary objection No. 1 on hearsay as to Bates numbered pages BURKE000001-000004 attached as part of Exhibit G to the Lewis Decl. However, the Court OVERRULES Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections No. 2 to the same documents attached as Exhibit H to the Lewis Decl. because that exhibit includes a declaration from the custodian of records that properly satisfies the hearsay rule in a way the portion attached as part of Exhibit G does not.

The Court OVERRULES Vons’s evidentiary objection Nos. 1 and 2 to the Burke Decl. but SUSTAINS Vons’s evidentiary objection No. 3 pursuant to D’Amico v. Board of Med. Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.

The Court OVERRULES Vons’s evidentiary objections as to the Declaration of Brad Avrit dated December 3, 2024, except to the extent it contains legal conclusions/opinions. Vons’s objections largely go to the weight, not the admissibility, of Mr. Avrit’s statements.

Finally, the Court SUSTAINS Vons’s evidentiary objection as to the Declaration of Carlos Brazao for lack of foundation.

B.            Request for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff asks the Court to take judicial notice pursuant to Evid. Code §§ 452 and 453 and Rules of Court, rule 3.113, of two items: (1) the Complaint filed in the matter of Tsoi v. The Vons Companies, et al., LASC Case No. 21STCV42289, and (2) Google maps historical images depicting the subject premises in March 2018 and March 2019. (See Plaintiff’s Request for Judicial Notice filed December 3, 2024 (“RJN”).)

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s RJN as to the Complaint in Case No. 21STCV42289, for the limited purpose of proving that a case was filed but not for the additional purpose Plaintiff wants to use it: to wit, that the specific allegations are so similar as to put Vons on notice that a dangerous condition exists because they are hearsay and outside the scope of materials contemplated by section 452.

The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s RJN as to the two Google maps historical images because there is insufficient foundation to support the conclusion that either section 452 or 453 applies.

C.            Undisputed Material Facts

Plaintiff claims that on December 24, 2020, at approximately 1:00 p.m., while exiting Vons #2066, located at 19439 Ventura Blvd., Tarzana, CA 91356, she “suddenly and unexpectedly came into contact with a slippery substance on the floor, causing her to slip and fall.” (See Plaintiff’s Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (“UMF”) No. 1.) On December 22, 2022, Plaintiff filed the instant Complaint against Vons alleging causes of action for general negligence and premises liability. (See UMF No. 2.) On December 1, 2023, Vons took the deposition of Burke whereat she admitted to wearing sandals at the time of the incident, despite the weather. (See UMF No. 4.) Plaintiff testified that she did not observe any hazard in the area before or after she fell, despite being very careful and looking where she was walking because it was starting to drizzle. (UMF No. 5.) Vons store director, Randy Figueroa and assistant store director, Victor Siam, arrived at the area of the incident shortly after it occurred. (See UMF No. 6.) Randy Figueroa instructed Victor Siam to take photographs of the subject area and Plaintiff’s shoes. (See UMF No. 7.) The photographs of the subject area do not depict any dangerous or hazardous substance on the ground. (See UMF No. 8.) Paramedics from the Los Angeles Fire Department (LAFD) responded to the scene of the alleged incident shortly after it occurred. (See UMF No. 9.)

D.           No Triable Issues of Material Fact Exist.

Plaintiff levies two claims against Vons: general negligence and premises liability. (See Complaint.) Defendant moves for summary judgment as to both.

“The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.” (Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687.) “The elements of a negligence claim and a premises liability claim are the same: a legal duty of care, breach of that duty, and proximate cause resulting in injury. Premises liability is grounded in the possession of the premises and the attendant right to control and manage the premises; accordingly, mere possession with its attendant right to control conditions on the premises is a sufficient basis for the imposition of an affirmative duty to act. But the duty arising from possession and control of property is adherence to the same standard of care that applies in negligence cases.” (Kesner v. Superior Court (2016) 1 Cal.5th 1132, 1158 (cleaned up).) 

“To comply with this duty, a person who controls property must inspect

[the premises] or take other proper means to ascertain their condition and,

if a dangerous condition exists that would have been discovered by the exercise

of reasonable care, has a duty to give adequate warning of or remedy it.” (Staats

v. Vintner’s Golf Club, LLC (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 826, 833 (cleaned up).)

“Because the owner is not the insurer of the visitor’s personal safety, the owner’s actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition is a key to

establishing its liability. Although the owner’s lack of knowledge is not a defense, [t]o impose liability for injuries suffered by an invitee due to [a] defective condition of the premises, the owner or occupier must have either actual or constructive knowledge of the dangerous condition or have been able by the exercise of ordinary care to discover the condition, which if known to him, he should realize as involving an unreasonable risk to invitees on his premises.” (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206 (cleaned up).)

“On the issue of the fact of causation, as on other issues essential to the cause of action for negligence, the plaintiff, in general, has the burden of proof. The plaintiff must introduce evidence which affords a reasonable basis for the conclusion that it is more likely than not that the conduct of the defendant was a cause in fact of the result. A mere possibility of such causation is not enough; and when the matter remains one of pure speculation or conjecture, or the probabilities are at best evenly balanced, it becomes the duty of the court to direct a verdict for the defendant… In the context of a business owner’s liability to a customer or invitee, speculation and conjecture with respect to how long a dangerous condition has existed are insufficient to satisfy a plaintiff’s burden. (Id., 26 Cal.4th at 1205-06 (cleaned up).)

         Defendant makes two arguments: (1) Plaintiff cannot establish a dangerous condition caused her fall and (2) Plaintiff cannot establish that Vons knew or should have known of a dangerous condition (See Motion.)

Key to Vons’s arguments are Plaintiff’s own sworn testimony at her deposition that establish UMF No. 5. Specifically, Plaintiff testified that she did not observe any hazard in the area before or after she fell, despite being very careful and looking where she was walking because it was starting to drizzle. Plaintiff’s attempt to create a triable issue of fact by way of Paragraph 5 of her declaration by essentially trying to blame her slip and fall on the “layers of paint on the ramp where [she] fell” for the first time runs afoul of D’Amico v. Board of Med. Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21. D’Amico holds that “when discovery has produced an admission or concession on the part of the party opposing summary judgment which demonstrates that there is no factual issue to be tried,” it is appropriate for the trial court to conclude there is no substantial evidence of a triable issue of fact despite the offer by the party opposing summary judgment of an affidavit contradicting that admission. (Id.) The same principle applies to Plaintiff’s attempt to create a triable issue of fact by declaring for the first time the painted yellow area she slipped on felt as slippery as glass when, in her deposition, she testified the ground was “barely” wet and “more dry than wet” and that “[i]t just started to drizzle.” (UMF No. 5.) Therefore, the Court will not consider it.

The Court finds Defendant has submitted sufficient evidence to meet its initial burden of proof on summary judgment as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for general negligence that a dangerous condition did not exist.

The burden thus shifts to Plaintiff to demonstrate a triable issue of material fact on this point. The Court finds Plaintiff has not done so because Plaintiff offered no admissible evidence to support her allegation she slipped on a foreign substance. As Vons notes in its Reply, Plaintiff primarily relies on the expert testimony of Brad Avrit. However, the Court concludes that whatever admissible testimony his declaration offers is too speculative to create a triable issue of fact, especially when Plaintiff’s theory of the case apparently pivots for the first time in her Opposition from slipping on a foreign substance to a design defect in the ramped area she fell. Most of what Mr. Avrit’s offered is irrelevant and therefore not material to the Court’s analysis. Glaringly absent from Mr. Avrit’s analysis is any causal connection between Plaintiff’s fall and the alleged dangerous condition. Plaintiff “cannot manufacture a triable issue of fact through the use of an expert opinion with self-serving conclusions devoid of any basis, explanation, or reasoning… The evidence must be of sufficient quality to allow the trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion for summary judgment… The plaintiff does not meet his burden of demonstrating a triable issue where his evidence merely provides a dwindling stream of probability that narrow into conjecture.” (Nardizzi v. Harbor Chrysler Plymouth Sales, Inc. (2006) 136 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1415 (cleaned up).)

As to Plaintiff’s second cause of action, if there is no evidence a foreign substance was on the ground where she fell, it follows logically that Vons could not have been aware of a condition that did not exist.

         Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Vons’s Motion.

V.            CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Vons’s Motion for Summary Judgment. Counsel for Vons shall submit a Proposed Judgment forthwith.

         IT IS SO ORDERED.

DATED:     December 17, 2024

Judge, Superior Court

ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR

LEAVE TO FILE A FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

 

 

I.               BACKGROUND 

This case stems from a physical altercation between Plaintiff Chase Cameron Gardella (“Plaintiff”) and Defendants Justin Goslee, aka “Kane Kongg” (Goslee) and Jack Doherty (“Doherty”). Plaintiff alleges that on the morning of October 29, 2023, at approximately 12:30 a.m., Plaintiff, Goslee, and Doherty were engaged in a conversation outside of the premises located at 3703 Almora Drive, Sherman Oaks, California, when Goslee punched Plaintiff at the behest of Doherty, causing injuries to Plaintiff. (See Complaint, ¶¶ 7-9.) Plaintiff alleges that at the time of the incident, Goslee was acting as an agent of Defendant Kongg, Inc. (“Kongg”) and as a security guard for Doherty, Jack Doherty, LLC (“Doherty LLC”) and Banger Official, LLC (“Banger LLC”). Id. at ¶ 6.  Plaintiff further alleges that the incident was live-streamed on Doherty’s various social media platforms, such as YouTube, X.com, and TikTok, which generated and continues generating views and income. Id. at ¶ 12-13.

Plaintiff now seeks an order for leave to file an amended complaint that adds two causes of action. 

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On February 21, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Assault; (2) Battery; (3) Negligence; (4) Negligent Hiring/Retention/Supervision/Training; (5) Aiding and Abetting; and (6) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress.

On April 12, 2024, Defendants Doherty, Doherty LLC, and Banger LLC filed their Answer.  

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Proof of Personal Service as to Goslee.

On September 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint but later withdrew it after Defendants Doherty and Banger LLC filed their Opposition.

On November 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Leave to File Amended Complaint to allege additional causes of action against all defendants (the “Motion”).

On November 25, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted/Motion to Deem Request for Admissions Admitted as to Goslee.  

On December 4, 2024, Defendants Banger Official LLC and Jack Doherty filed their Opposition to the Motion.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

Code of Civil Procedure § 473(a) provides: “The court may likewise, in its discretion, after notice to the adverse party, allow, upon any terms as may be just, an amendment to any pleading or proceeding in other particulars; and may upon like terms allow an answer to be made after the time limited by this code.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 473(a)(1); see also Code Civ. Proc. § 576.)¿¿¿¿ ¿¿ 

The court has broad discretion to permit amendments to pleadings, and “the court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings.” (Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428.)¿“The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.”¿(Id.)¿“If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend.”¿(Morgan v. Superior Court (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.” (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)¿¿ 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must comply with the procedural requirements of Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, under which the motion must include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a), (b).)¿  

IV.          ANALYSIS

After reviewing Plaintiff’s Motion and the Declaration of Greyson M. Goody dated November 1, 2024, the Court finds Plaintiff has failed to comply with Rule of Court, rule 3.1324 (b) because Mr. Goody’s declaration does not “specify … [w]hen the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.” Because Rule 3.1324(b) states that a separately filed declaration “must accompany [a] motion” to amend a pleading before trial and “must specify” these facts, it is not discretionary. These procedural requirements are essential for the Court to conduct its substantive analysis under the applicable statutory and case law, in particular related to evaluating the prejudice of the potential amendment to the opposing party.

Accordingly, the Motion is procedurally defective and the Court DENIES the Motion without addressing its substantive merits.

V.             CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

ORDER DENYING DEFENDANT HARBOR FREIGHT TOOLS USA, INC.’S MOTION TO COMPEL ARBITRATION

 

 

I.        BACKGROUND 

This is a Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”) action filed by Representative Plaintiff Sergio Gandarilla (“Plaintiff”) against Defendant Harbor Freight Tools USA, Inc. (“Defendant”). Plaintiff was employed by Defendant from July 21, 2020, until his termination on June 4, 2024. Plaintiff filed one cause of action against Defendant for violation of Labor Code § 2698 et seq.

Defendant now moves to compel Plaintiff’s claim to arbitration.  

II.            PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On August 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Complaint, alleging (1) Violation of Labor Code § 2698[1], et seq.

On October 10, 2024, Defendant filed its Answer.

On November 8, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration (“Motion”).

On December 4, 2024, Plaintiff filed his Opposition.

On December 10, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply.

III.         LEGAL STANDARD

“The party seeking to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement.” (Avila v. Southern Cal. Specialty Care, Inc. (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 835, 844.)

“California courts traditionally have maintained a strong preference for arbitration as a speedy and inexpensive method of dispute resolution… To this end, arbitration agreements should be liberally construed, … with doubts concerning the scope of arbitrable issues being resolved in favor of arbitration.” (Market Ins. Corp. v. Integrity Ins. Co. (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1095, 1098 (cleaned up).) “This strong policy has resulted in the general rule that arbitration should be upheld unless it can be said with assurance that an arbitration clause is not susceptible to an interpretation covering the asserted dispute.... It seems clear that the burden must fall upon the party opposing arbitration to demonstrate that an arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to require arbitration of the dispute.” (Bono v. David (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 1055, 1062 (cleaned up).)

California law incorporates many of the basic policy objectives contained in the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”), including a presumption in favor of arbitrability. (See Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 971-972.) The Court is empowered by Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.2 to compel parties to arbitrate disputes pursuant to an agreement to do so.¿¿¿

The party opposing the petition to compel arbitration bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense. (See Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.) Accordingly, under both the Federal Arbitration Act and California law, arbitration agreements are valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except on such grounds that exist at law or equity for voiding a contract. (See Winter v. Window Fashions Professions, Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 943, 947.)

Thus, in deciding a motion to compel arbitration, trial courts must first decide whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then determine the second gateway issue of whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. (See Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.)

IV.          ANALYSIS

A.             Evidentiary Objections and Request for Judicial Notice

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice of other Superior Court Minute Orders, but only as to the existence of those Minute Orders. The Court cannot take judicial notice of hearsay statements contained in court records. (See Johnson & Johnson v. Superior Court (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 757, 768; Williams v. Wraxall (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 120, 130, fn. 7.)

          The Court also OVERRULES Defendant’s Objection to Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice as it takes judicial notice of the existence of the Minute Orders only.

B.             Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration is Denied

1.     There is a Valid Arbitration Agreement Between the Parties

The arbitration proponent must first recite verbatim, or provide a copy of, the alleged agreement. (See Sprunk v. Prisma LLC (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 785, 793.) A movant can meet this initial burden “‘by attaching a copy of the arbitration agreement purportedly bearing the opposing party’s signature.’” (Id. (quoting Rules of Court, rule 3.1330).) “[U]nless there is a dispute over authenticity, it is sufficient for a party moving to compel arbitration to recite the terms of the governing provision.” (Id. (citing Condee v. Longwood Mgmt. Corp. (2001) 88 Cal.App.4th 215, 219).) “A voluntary acceptance of the benefit of a transaction is equivalent to a consent to all the obligations arising from it, so far as the facts are known, or ought to be known, to the person accepting.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1589.)

          Defendant has submitted evidence of a valid arbitration agreement between the parties. Defendant has attached a copy of its 2023 Dispute Resolution Policy (“2023 DRP”) signed by Plaintiff. (See Declaration of Matthew Finney filed November 8, 2024 (“Finney Decl.”), ¶¶ 13-14, Ex. 4.) By signing the 2023 DRP, Plaintiff agreed to:

“…to submit to final and binding arbitration any and all claims and disputes, whether they exist now or arise in the future, that are related in any way to my employment or the termination of my employment with Harbor Freight Tools, except as otherwise permitted by the DRP. I understand that final and binding arbitration will be the sole and exclusive remedy for any such claim or dispute against Harbor Freight Tools and any parent, subsidiary, affiliated and/or related entities, and its and their current and former directors, officers, supervisors, Associates, representatives, agents, and attorneys, and that by agreeing to use arbitration to resolve my disputes, both Harbor Freight Tools and I agree to forgo any right we each may have had to a jury trial on issues covered by the DRP, and forgo any right to bring claims on a class, collective, coordinated, consolidated, or representative basis.

 

In cases under the California Labor Code Private Attorneys General Act (“PAGA”), Harbor Freight Tools and I agree to arbitrate only the individual PAGA claims based on Labor Code violations personally sustained by me. I also agree that the arbitration will be conducted before a mutually agreed upon arbitrator (or court-appointed arbitrator if the parties cannot agree), and that the designated arbitration provider’s rules will apply to the arbitration proceedings unless they conflict with the DRP. If the arbitration provider’s rules conflict with the DRP in any way, I agree that the DRP shall govern.”

(Finney Decl., ¶ 13, Ex. 4.)

Accordingly, Defendant has proved the existence of a valid and enforceable arbitration agreement.

2.     Plaintiff’s Claims are not Covered by the Arbitration Agreement

Defendant asserts “in response to [Defendant’s] request that he submit his claims to arbitration, [Plaintiff] argued that, because he purports to bring his claims only in a ‘representative’ capacity, and not individually, the Complaint does not allege any arbitrable claims under [PAGA].” (Motion, pg. 9.) Defendant argues Plaintiff’s representative argument is “pure sophistry” because Plaintiff cannot maintain a PAGA action unless he has suffered the alleged labor code violations. (See id. at 9, 12-13.) “In fact, if [Plaintiff] had not suffered from the alleged Labor Code violations asserted in the Complaint, he would not have standing to bring this action under PAGA.” (Id. at 13.) Defendant argues the recent Balderas v. Fresh Start Harvesting, Inc. (2024) 101 Cal.App.5th 533 (“Balderas”) case, allegedly relied on by Plaintiff when the parties discussed the arbitration agreement, does not stand for the proposition that a plaintiff can maintain a representative action without asserting any individual claims. (See id. at 12.) Rather, Defendant argues, “the Court of Appeal merely held that Balderas was not required to bring a separate individual PAGA claim seeking relief only for herself in order to maintain standing.” (Id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the parties expressly agreed to arbitrate only Plaintiff’s individual PAGA claims. (See Opposition, pg. 3.) Here, Plaintiff argues, Plaintiff did not bring any individual PAGA claims and Balderas controls. (See id. at 4.) “The central question in Balderas was whether an employee must file an individual claim to maintain a representative PAGA action. The issue was significantly influenced by the U.S. Supreme Court’s decision in Viking River Cruises, Inc. v. Moriana, 142 S.Ct. 1906 (2022) [“Viking River”], which suggested that PAGA plaintiffs need an individual claim to pursue representative-only PAGA claims.” (Id.) The Court in Balderas, Plaintiff argues, declined to follow Viking River, instead finding the plaintiff there could pursue representative claims without seeking individual relief. (See id. at 5.) “Based on the Balderas ruling, Plaintiff can proceed litigating his PAGA representative claim on behalf of Aggrieved Employees and the State alone in this Action, without prosecuting individual, non-representative claims. This is exactly what Plaintiff did here.” (Id. at 7.) Plaintiff argues he is an aggrieved employee for standing purposes and thus can bring the instant PAGA action in his representative capacity only. (See id. at 10.)  

In reply, Defendant argues the recently issued Rodriguez v. Lawrence Equipment, Inc. (2024) 106 Cal.App.5th 645 (“Rodriguez”) case “precludes the very idea that plaintiffs can assert PAGA representative claims without asserting an individual claim, holding that PAGA standing and the existence of individual claims based on Labor Code violations allegedly suffered are ‘identical issues.’” (Reply, pg. 5, quoting Rodriguez, 106 Cal.App.5th at 659.)

The Court finds Plaintiff claims are not subject to arbitration because he asserts his PAGA action in his representative capacity only. To possess standing to pursue PAGA claims a plaintiff must be an “aggrieved employee”, or one who has sustained a Labor Code violation committed by their employer. (See Adolph v. Uber Technologies, Inc. (2023) 14 Cal.5th 1104, 1121.) PAGA standing only requires a plaintiff alleges “that he or she is (1) someone who was employed by the alleged violator and (2) someone against whom one or more of the alleged violations was committed.” (Adolph, 14 Cal.5th at 1120 (quotations and citations omitted); see also Balderas, 101 Cal.App.5th at 538 (“The Adolph court concluded that the Viking River requirement of having to file an individual PAGA cause of action to achieve standing to file a representative PAGA suit was incorrect.”).)

In applying Adolph’s holding, the Court of Appeal in Balderas found the plaintiff in that case had standing to bring a representative PAGA suit, although she did not file an individual PAGA claim, because she was an aggrieved employee. (See Balderas, 101 Cal.App.5th at 539.) “The statutory goal [of PAGA] is furthered by extending broad standing to aggrieved employees who do not depend on the viability or strength of a plaintiff's individual PAGA claim. The inability for an employee to pursue an individual PAGA claim does not prevent that employee from filing a representative PAGA action. (See Balderas, 101 Cal.App.5th at 537.)

Defendant’s argument can be simplified as follows: because Plaintiff must be an aggrieved employee to bring a PAGA action, it is impossible for Plaintiff to bring a PAGA action in his representative capacity without also bringing the action in his individual capacity. But that argument not only contradicts the holdings of Adolph and Balderas but also reads into those rulings a requirement that doesn’t exist. Plaintiff can bring this PAGA action in his representative capacity without also bringing the action in his individual capacity, as he has done here and as was explicitly contemplated in Balderas. Defendant’s citation of the recent Rodriguez case does not in any way alter Balderas’s holding and is not persuasive.[2]  

In short, Defendant cannot compel Plaintiff’s non-existent individual PAGA claim to arbitration. Such an action is beyond the scope of the arbitration agreement and contrary to applicable law.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion.

As a result of the Court’s foregoing analysis and conclusion, it declines to address the parties’ arguments about the DRP’s procedural or substantive unconscionability.

V.       CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the Court DENIES Defendant’s Motion to Compel Arbitration.
