DEPARTMENT 55 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 24STCV02990    Hearing Date: June 3, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Defendants’ Motion to Compel Binding Arbitration and Stay the Action.

 

BACKGROUND

GIANNI VALENCIA (“Plaintiff”) brings this case against LITHIA MOTORS, INC. and DCH TORRANCE IMPORTS INC. (“Defendants”) alleging wage and hour and FEHA claims.

Defendants move for an order compelling binding arbitration and staying this case. Plaintiff opposes the motion.

LEGAL STANDARD

A party seeking arbitration has the burden of proving by a preponderance of evidence that a valid arbitration agreement exists. Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836, 842; see also § CCP 1281.2.  A party meets its initial burden simply by reciting the terms of the governing provision, or by attaching a copy of the provisions.  Sprunk v. Prisma LLC (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 785, 793. Once the petitioner meets its burden, “the burden shifts to the party opposing the motion to compel, who may present any challenges to the enforcement of the agreement and evidence in support of those challenges.” Baker v. Italian Maple Holdings, LLC (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 1152, 1160.

ANALYSIS

Arbitration Agreement Authentication

In determining the enforceability of an arbitration agreement, the court first considers whether an enforceable arbitration agreement exists between the parties, and then whether the claims are covered within the scope of the agreement. Omar v. Ralphs Grocery Co. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 955, 961.

Defendants argue they have met their initial burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement because they provide a copy of the agreement to arbitrate. Collinet Decl., Ex. 1. Defendants submit the declaration of Christine Collinet, who states that Defendants use the Workday program for the electronic onboarding process for job applicants, and the Adobe Sign software to track the electronic signature of documents within the Workday program. Collinet Decl., ¶¶ 2, 4. Ms. Collinet states the Adobe Sign program allows applicants to review and sign employment agreements and creates an audit trail for such signatures. Id., ¶ 5. Ms. Collinet states that Plaintiff electronically signed the arbitration agreement and her declaration attaches an audit trial for Plaintiff’s signature. Id., ¶¶ 6-7, Exs. 1-2. She states that the audit trail shows that the arbitration agreement was delivered to Plaintiff at the email address venedie@yahoo.com and then returned and marked completed in the program. Id., ¶ 7.

“If the moving party meets its initial prima facie burden and the opposing party disputes the agreement, then in the second step, the opposing party bears the burden of producing evidence to challenge the authenticity of the agreement.” Gamboa v. Northeast Community Clinic (2021) 72 Cal. App. 5th 158, 165. The evidence must be sufficient to create a factual dispute to shift the burden back to the arbitration proponent who retains the ultimate burden of proving, by a preponderance of the evidence, the authenticity of the signature. Iyere v. Wise Auto Group (2023) 87 Cal.App.5th 747, 755.

 

Here, Plaintiff contends that Defendants have not carried their burden of proving an enforceable agreement because Plaintiff does not recognize the signature on the arbitration agreement and he has no recollection of signing the agreement. Valencia Decl., ¶¶ 2, 4-5. Plaintiff’s statement that he does not recognize the signature on the agreement is immaterial because it is an electronic signature. And Plaintiff’s claim he does not recall signing the agreement is not persuasive. Ms. Collinet’s declaration explains that Defendants emailed Plaintiff the arbitration agreement at his email address and then he e-signed the agreement and sent it back, as reflected in the audit trail. Collinet Decl., ¶¶ 6-7. While Plaintiff claims he does not recall signing the agreement, he notably does not dispute that the email address listed in the audit trail is his email address. This means that he has not created a factual dispute as to whether the electronic signature “by Gianni Valencia (venedie@yahoo.com)” as stated in the audit trail means what is says it means, i.e. that Plaintiff electronically signed the agreement. Ruiz, 232 Cal.App.4th at 846 (noting that if a party does not recall signing an arbitration agreement the moving party has the ultimate burden of proving the electronic signature is authentic). Plaintiff does not provide any other challenges to the validity of the arbitration agreement; therefore, Defendants have established that an agreement to arbitrate exists. Moreover, Plaintiff does not dispute that the agreement covers the claims in his Complaint.

   

Unconscionability

For an arbitration agreement to be unenforceable as unconscionable, both procedural and substantive unconscionability must be present. Armendariz v. Foundation Health Psychcare Services, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 83, 114.

1.      Procedural Unconscionability

Procedural unconscionability includes oppression arising from unequal bargaining power causing an absence of meaningful choice and real negotiating, and surprise due to hidden terms drafted by the party seeking to enforce the provisions. Gatton v. T-Mobile USA, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 571, 581.

Contract of Adhesion: Plaintiff asserts that the presentation of the employment and arbitration agreements together would lead one to feel that the agreement was take-it-or-leave-it, particularly where no opt-out provision existed. Opposition, 5:24-6:10.

“[A] compulsory predispute arbitration agreement is not rendered unenforceable just because it is required as a condition of employment or offered on a 'take it or leave it' basis.” Lagatree v. Luce, Forward, Hamilton & Scripps (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1105, 1127.

As a matter of law, Plaintiff failed to show that the agreement was a requirement of employment and that it contained substantively unconscionable provisions. Specifically, the agreement, above the signature line, boldly states that the agreement is voluntary and Plaintiff could consult with an attorney. Those words imply that Plaintiff could opt out.

Thus, the Court rejects the contract-of-adhesion ground.

Agreement Explanations: Plaintiff contends that Defendants did not explain the arbitration agreement. E.g., opposition, 5:1.

The absence of “authentic informed choice,” due to the lack of straightforward, unbiased explanations of an agreement, may constitute procedural unconscionability. Gentry v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 443, 470 (where employer indicated preference to arbitrate, it was likely that “nonexecutive” lower echelon employees felt some pressure not to opt out of the agreement), overruled on other grounds as recognized by Marenco v. DirecTV LLC (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1421. Evidence that a moving party never communicated that an arbitration agreement was nonnegotiable, in response to opposing proof to the contrary, failed to “create a meaningful conflict in the evidence,” because the moving party did not affirmatively establish that it had made opposing party aware of an ability to negotiate, or that by law opposing party such a right. Htay Htay Chin v. Advanced Fresh Concepts Franchise Corp.  (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 704, 710.

Plaintiff’s declaration arguably supports a finding of procedural unconscionability. However, if Plaintiff had encountered the agreement, then reading it would reveal some explanations, such as the bold, capital letters above the signature line, explaining that it allows consulting an attorney and is a voluntary waiving of jury trial.

Agreement Surprise: Plaintiff reasons that the agreement, over one page long, has sentences that are complex and filled with words with small font size. E.g., opposition, 5:5-8.

Surprise caused by provisions buried in a long contract, or misleading captions, indicates procedural unconscionability. Thompson v. Toll Dublin, LLC (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1360, 1373. Here, the agreement is relatively short, with limited “legalese,” and is mostly understandable by laypersons.

Incorporated Arbitration Rules: Plaintiff argues that the agreement itself fails to explain the arbitration process, and that its link to JAMS rules does not suffice. E.g., opposition, 5:20-24.

Cases that have held that the failure to provide a copy of the arbitration rules is procedurally unconscionable involved an unconscionability claim that depended upon the incorporated arbitration rules. Baltazar v. Forever 21, Inc. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 1237, 1246. “Whether a document purportedly incorporated by reference was ‘readily available’ is a question of fact.” Baker v. Osborne Development Corp. (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 884, 895.

Plaintiff’s argument is weak because there is no evidence that anything in the JAMS rules relate to unconscionability. Further, the link to the rules was readily available to Plaintiff from the contract itself.

Thus, the Court does not find procedural unconscionability with respect to incorporated JAMS rules.

2.      Substantive Unconscionability

Plaintiff’s opposition briefly references substantive unconscionability. Opposition, 4:22.

Claims brought under the Fair Employment and Housing Act (FEHA) are subject to arbitration, if there are provisions for arbitrator neutrality, discovery, written decisions, and expense limits.  O'Hare v. Municipal Resource Consultants (2003) 107 Cal.App.4th 267, 273.

Plaintiff’s opposition contains no criticism of substantive aspects, such as arbitrator neutrality, discovery, written decisions, and expense limits. Instead, all points relate to procedural unconscionability, as addressed above.

In sum, the Court rejects unconscionability as any ground to deny the motion.

CONCLUSION

The Court grants the motion to compel arbitration. The action is stayed under Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.4 pending the outcome of the parties’ arbitration.
