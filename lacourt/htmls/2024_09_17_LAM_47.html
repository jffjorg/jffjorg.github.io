DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV06963    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     September 17, 2024               JUDGMENT: July 15, 2024.

                                                          

CASE:                         Griffin M. Bracci v. Orion Housing, LLC

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV06963           

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff Griffin M. Bracci

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant Orion Housing, LLC

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         02/23/21: Complaint filed.

·         07/15/24: Judgment entered for Plaintiff and against Defendant.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant failed to provide proper notice of construction on the property which Plaintiff was leasing from Defendant, denying Plaintiff the full use of that property.

 

Plaintiff moves for attorney’s fees following the Court’s granting of summary judgment.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is DENIED. 

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves for attorney’s fees following the Court’s granting of summary judgment pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1021.5.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1021.5 states:

 

Upon motion, a court may award attorneys’ fees to a successful party against one or more opposing parties in any action which has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest if: (a) a significant benefit, whether pecuniary or nonpecuniary, has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons, (b) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement, or of enforcement by one public entity against another public entity, are such as to make the award appropriate, and (c) such fees should not in the interest of justice be paid out of the recovery, if any.

 

            A party seeking attorney’s fees under section 1021.5 need only show that a significant benefit was conferred on the public, and the extent of that benefit need not be great nor is it required that the class of persons benefited be readily ascertainable. (Doe v. Westmont College (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 753, 764.) However, the mere possibility that an action might result in a substantial public benefit does not mean that a significant benefit was conferred. (Burgess v. Coronado Unified School District (2020 59 Cal.App.5th 1, 11.)

 

            Plaintiff contends that he is entitled to an award of attorney’s fees pursuant to this code provision because this action “was effectively a code enforcement action which substantially benefits the neighbors and the community where the Subject Property is located.” (Motion p.5:15-16.) Plaintiff states, under penalty of perjury, that Defendant began construction on the subject property without adequate notice and without compliance with all applicable building safety codes, stored tools and debris in the backyard spaces, rescinded parking spots, and began construction at 7:00 AM, negatively affecting Plaintiff, neighbors, and the community. (Declaration of Griffin M. Bracci ISO Mot. ¶¶ 5-9.) These statements are some evidence of an injury to a subset of the public. Further, Defendant’s failure to comply with applicable building codes is an established admission pursuant to the Court’s July 19, 2023 ruling. (See July 19, 2023 Minute Order on Motion to Deem Requests for Admissions as Admitted; Declaration of Scott J. Kalter ISO Mot. Exh. A.) At the least, Plaintiff has put forth a colorable argument that this action resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest, notwithstanding Defendant’s characterization of this action as merely a breach of contract action. What Plaintiff has not done, however, is demonstrate a basis to conclude that a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons in the enforcement of that right.

 

            As Plaintiff recites, “the ‘significant benefit’ that will justify an attorney fee award need not represent a ‘tangible’ asset or a ‘concrete’ gain but, in some cases, may be recognized simply from the effectuation of a fundamental constitutional or statutory policy.” (RiverWatch v. County of San Diego Dept. of Environmental Health (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 768, 781.) However, neither of the cases on which Plaintiff relies suggest that the relief obtained in this action is the type of relief which may be considered a “significant benefit.” In RiverWatch, for example, the Plaintiffs sought and successfully obtained a petition for writ of mandate to require further review under CEQA of a series of administrative decisions relating to planned development of a landfill and recycling center, and injunctive relief to halt construction and related activity pending that review. (RiverWatch, supra, 175 Cal.App.4th at 772-74.) The Court of Appeal upheld the trial court’s finding that such relief constituted a significant public benefit in ensuring that the environmental impacts associated with the project were adequately mitigated. (Id. at 781.) Similarly, in Robinson v. City of Chowchilla, the plaintiff in that case conferred a significant benefit on the public by enforcing the procedural protections under the Peace Officer’s Bill of Rights Act and, critically, because the litigation resulted in a published decision regarding the meaning and application of subdivision (c) of section 3304 of the Government Code. (Robinson v. City of Chowchilla (2011) 202 Cal.App.4th 382, 399.) Here, however, Plaintiff neither sought nor obtained any relief that could be construed as a benefit for any party other than himself. Plaintiff did not seek injunctive relief to preclude Defendant from engaging in any harmful conduct or take affirmative steps to remediate any conditions on the property. Nor did Plaintiff seek any form of restitution, monetary or otherwise, for other members of the public. Plaintiff sought and obtained monetary damages for himself only. That relief does not align with the benefits exemplified in Plaintiff’s authorities.

 

            The Court therefore finds that Plaintiff has not demonstrated that he has conferred a significant benefit on the general public or a large class of persons, and therefore has not demonstrated entitlement to attorney’s fees under Code of Civil Procedure section 1021.5.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees is DENIED.
