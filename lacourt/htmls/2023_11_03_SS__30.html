DEPARTMENT 30 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

 PLEASE NOTE:    

The parties are encouraged to meet and confer concerning this tentative ruling to determine if there is an agreement to submit.  

Regardless of whether there is any such agreement, each party who wishes to submit must send an email to the Court at SSCdept30@LACourt.org indicating the party's intention to submit. 

Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the case number in the subject line of the email and in the body provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, or non-party.  

If a party submits but still intends to appear at the hearing, include the words "SUBMITS BUT WILL APPEAR" in the subject line of the email. 

If the Court does not receive emails from the parties indicating submission on this tentative ruling and there are no appearances at the hearing, the Court may, at its discretion, adopt the tentative as the final order or place the motion off calendar. 

Unless all the parties have submitted, the Court will hear argument from any party that appears at the hearing and wishes to argue. The Court may change its tentative as a result of the argument and adopt the changed tentative as the final order at the end of that hearing, even if all the parties are not present. 

Be advised that after the Court has posted/issued a tentative ruling, the Court has the inherent authority to prohibit the withdrawal of said motion and may adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the Court.     


Case Number: 20STCV03496    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 30

ARMANDO RUIZ CARLOS, AN INDIVIDUAL vs HD SUPPLY CONSTRUCTION & INDUSTRIAL WHITE CAP, et al.

Motion to Compel Plaintiff's Responses to Supplemental Interrogatories

TENTATIVE

Motion to Compel Responses is GRANTED.  Plaintiff is ORDERED to serve responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Supplemental Requests, within 20 days of the issuance of this order. Request for Sanctions is denied. Moving party is to give notice of this ruling.

DISCUSSION

On May 15, 2023, Defendant propounded on Plaintiff supplemental responses to interrogatories, both Form and Special. On August 18, 2023, Plaintiff had not provided any responses. Defendant’s counsel contacted Plaintiff’s counsel, who acknowledged that no responses had been served. However, as of the date of the motion, no responses have been provided. (Motion 4: 1-3)  A party waives objections to a discovery request when it does not serve a timely response.  CCP 20230.290(a).  The Court declines to award sanctions as the motion is unopposed.

Case Number: 20STCV44029    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 30

LEVONNA LACY vs LONG BEACH PUBLIC TRANSPORTATION

Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff Levonna Lacy

TENTATIVE

The Motion to Be Relieved as Counsel for Plaintiff Levonna Lacy is GRANTED.  The entry of this order is delayed pursuant to CRC Rule 3.1362(e). Counsel is ordered to give notice of this ORDER within five (5) days to Client and parties.  

DISCUSSION

On October 25, 2023, this Court continued the matter to allow the parties to correct the deficiencies. Additionally, the discovery motions that were scheduled for 10/27 and 12/1 were continued to 12/28. The Court did not adopt its tentative ruling denying the motion due to the hearing date being too close to the upcoming motions.

On October 30, 2023, Counsel for Plaintiff filed 3 updated documents: a new order to be relieved as counsel, notice regarding the rescheduled dates for the three discovery motions, and a declaration regarding proof of service. In these documents, counsel attached proof of service, which indicated that Plaintiff as well as Defendant have been served via mail.

Based on new documents submitted, the Court finds that good cause exists to allow counsel to withdraw. In the Declaration it indicates that there has been an “irremediable breakdown in the attorney-client relationship that stands in way of effective representation.” The proofs of services attached indicate that all parties have been served with the most updated forms.

Case Number: 21STCV04353    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 30

SIRANOUCHE MELKONIAN vs ELIT PROPERTY MANAGEMENT INC., et al.

Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Respond to Defendant's Supplemental Request for Production of Documents

TENTATIVE

Defendants’ motion to compel Plaintiff to respond to Defendants’ supplemental Request for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ORDERED to serve verified responses, without objection, to Defendants’ supplemental Request for Production of Documents, Set One within 20 days of the issuance of this order. Request for Sanctions is DENIED. Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION

Defendants are entitled to an order compelling Plaintiff to serve a complete verified response without objection to Defendants’ supplemental Request for Production of Documents, Set One. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).) On May 10, 2023, defense counsel served Defendants’ supplemental Request for Production of Documents, Set One to Plaintiff’s counsel with a due date of June 13, 2023. (Declaration of William G. Sorkin, ¶ 3; Ex. “A.”) On July 21, 2023, defense counsel sent a letter to Plaintiff’s counsel demanding responses by no later than July 31, 2023. (Sorkin Decl., ¶ 4; Ex. “B.”) Responses were served after the motion was filed.

In opposition, Plaintiff contends that Defendants’ motion is moot because Plaintiff intends to provide supplemental responses to Defendants’ request. Given that responses have been served.  The Court declines to impose sanctions.

Case Number: 23STCV16753    Hearing Date: November 3, 2023    Dept: 30

V.S. FLORES PEREZ, et al. vs PROYECTO PASTORAL, et al.

Demurrer with Motion to Strike


TENTATIVE

Demurrer is SUSTAINED, with leave to amend within 20 days of issuance of this Order.  Motion to Strike is DENIED.  Moving party to give notice.

DISCUSSION

Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress Cause of Action (Third)

Defendant argues that this cause of action fails because it only states conclusory allegations and does not provide any factual support for an IIED claim. While it states that the conduct was outrageous, it fails to specify what conduct or incident, other than placing tape on V.S.’s hands. The complaint does not state how the tape was on V.S.’s hands or who placed it. Moreover, the Complaint does not contain sufficient facts to support liability under a theory of respondeat superior.

Plaintiffs argue that the Complaint sufficiently alleges a cause of action for IIED. The Complaint alleges that the “child abuse conduct was outrageous, despicable, cruel, unlawful, and constituted inhumane corporal punishment.” (Opp. 6: 27-28.) Additionally, the Complaint alleges that Defendants acted with reckless disregard that Plaintiffs would suffer distress, as well as the remaining to elements for suffering emotional distress and proximate cause. (Opp. 7: 4-9; Comp ¶¶ 21, 37-39, 43-46.) 

Intentional infliction of emotional distress requires the Plaintiff to show “(1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant's intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff's suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant's outrageous conduct.” (Yau v. Santa Margarita Ford, Inc. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 144, 160). Generally, conduct will be found to be actionable where the recitation of the facts to an average member of the community would arouse his resentment against the actor, and lead him to exclaim, “Outrageous!” (Ess v. Eskaton Properties, Inc. (2002) 97 Cal. App. 4th 120, 130.) Although emotional distress may consist of any highly unpleasant mental reaction such as fright, grief, shame, humiliation, embarrassment, anger, chagrin, disappointment or worry [citation], to make out a claim, the plaintiff must prove that emotional distress was severe and not trivial or transient.” (Wong v. Tai Jing (2010) 189 Cal. App. 4th 1354, 1376.) Such distress must be “of such substantial quality or enduring quality that no reasonable person in civilized society should be expected to endure it.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal. 4th 965, 1004.)

After a review of the complaint, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have failed to sufficiently allege a cause of action for IIED. The conduct – taping a two-year old’s hands – as alleged would be considered outrageous. However, the complaint only contains conclusory allegations about the remaining elements for an IIED claim. There are no specific facts as to how defendant’s intended to cause distress. Additionally, as indicated in Wong and Potter, the emotional distress must be severe. Here, paragraph 40 merely states that Plaintiffs suffered damages, including physical, phycological, and emotional injuries.” (Comp. ¶ 40.) This is insufficient for a claim for IIED.

2. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress Cause of Action (Fourth)

While Defendant assumes that Plaintiff is alleging a claim for bystander theory of liability, the Complaint still fails to allege facts that would allow a bystander theory of liability. The Complaint does not indicate that Plaintiff Silvia Perez was present at the time of the event, only that she witnessed Plaintiff V.S.’s wrists in tape when she picked her up. (Demurrer 11: 7-15.) Plaintiffs argue that the fourth cause of action is sufficient for Plaintiff Perez to claim a damages for NIED. Defendant’s conduct caused injury to Plaintiff V.S. This was witnessed by Plaintiff Perez when she arrived to pick up her daughter.

A “negligent causing of emotional distress is not an independent tort but the tort of negligence....’ [Citation.] ‘The traditional elements of duty, breach of duty, causation, and damages apply.’” (Eriksson v. Nunnink (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 708, 729). “In the absence of physical injury or impact to the plaintiff himself, damages for emotional distress should be recoverable only if the plaintiff: (1) is closely related to the injury victim, (2) is present at the scene of the injury-producing event at the time it occurs and is then aware that it is causing injury to the victim and, (3) as a result suffers emotional distress beyond that which would be anticipated in a disinterested witness.” (Burgess v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.4th 1064, 1073).

After reviewing the Complaint, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have not sufficiently alleged a cause of action for NIED. First, Plaintiff Silvia Perez is V.S.’s mother. Second, Plaintiff Perez walked in and witnessed Plaintiff V.S.’s hands bound with tape. Specifically, in Ko v. Maxim Healthcare Services, Inc.,, the Court of Appeal discussed the requirement of experiencing a contemporary sensory awareness of the infliction by defendants and injuries suffered by the relative. Ko stated, “[t]he impact of personally observing the injury-producing event ... distinguishes the plaintiff's resultant emotional distress from the emotion felt when one learns of the injury or death of a loved one from another, or observes pain and suffering but not the traumatic cause of the injury.” Ko v. Maxim Healthcare Services, Inc. (2020) 58 Cal.App.5th 1144, 1157.) Here, the Complaint alleges that Plaintiff Silvia Perez walked in to see Plaintiff V.S.’s hands taped. Thus, Plaintiff Perez witnessed her daughter crying as a result of her hands being bound. Although she was not present when the initial taping began, she was able to perceive the conduct and the later emotional distress. Had Plaintiff Perez been informed of the conduct later, but failed to witness her daughter’s hands taped, a claim for NIED would not be viable.

While the first two elements have been satisfied, the Court finds that the third element – a person suffers “emotional distress beyond that which would be anticipated in a disinterred witness” – has not been satisfied. The complaint merely contains conclusory allegations that the conduct caused Plaintiffs severe emotional distress. Like above, this is insufficient for an NIED cause of action.

Uncertain Complaint

The Court also notes that Plaintiffs do not designate which cause of action is for which plaintiff. Moreover, the first three causes of action – battery, negligence, and IIED – are not suitable against Plaintiff Silvia Perez. For example, Plaintiff V.S. is alleged to have been bound with tape, not Plaintiff Silvia Perez. Thus, Plaintiff Silvia Perez would not have standing to assert a claim for battery. Thus, because the Complaint fails to delineate which Plaintiff corresponds to which specific cause of action, the Court finds that Complaint uncertain.

Motion to Strike

Given that the Court is sustaining the demurrer. It denies the motion to strike.  Punitive Damages would be available for these causes of action. 

Leave to Amend

Leave to amend should be liberally granted if there is a reasonable possibility an amendment could cure the defect. (County of Santa Clara v. Superior Court (2022) 77 Cal.App.5th 1018,1035.) The Plaintiff has the burden of demonstrating that leave to amend should be granted, and that the defects can be cured by amendment. (“Plaintiff must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.” Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The Court finds that the Plaintiffs may be able to amend the complaint to cure the defects discussed.
