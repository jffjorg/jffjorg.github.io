DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV49011    Hearing Date: May 20, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27 

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO INTERVENE

Hearing Date: 5/20/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 20STCV49011 BEHZAD SHIRALIAN vs. KASIM MOOSAI 

Moving Party: Defendant Christian Mauricio Torresarias’ insurer, Certain Underwriters at Lloyd’s of London

Responding Party: Unopposed¿ 

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO INTERVENE IS GRANTED.

 

On December 23, 2020, Plaintiff filed this auto accident case. On July 20, 2022, Defendant Christian Mauricio Torresarias was named as a defendant. Certain Underwriters at Lloyd’s of London is Torresarias’ insurance carrier in connection to the incident at issue. Lloyd’s now moves for leave to intervene in the case, contending that despite numerous attempts, the moving party has not been able to locate or make contact with Defendant Torresarias.

 

Per CCP §387(a), permissive intervention is proper if: The nonparty has a direct and immediate interest in the litigation; and the intervention will not enlarge the issues in the case; and the reasons for intervention outweigh any opposition by the existing parties.

 

A liability insurer normally cannot intervene in a tort action against its insured. The judgment in the tort action collaterally estops the insurer only on issues necessarily adjudicated therein—i.e., the insured's liability and the amount of the injured party's damages. It does not bind the insurer on coverage issues. (Western Heritage Ins. Co. v. Sup.Ct. (Parks) (2011) 199 CA4th 1196, 1212.)

 

However, because a liability insurer agrees to pay any judgment obtained against its insured (see Ins.C. §11580(b)(2)), it has the right to intervene (not merely permissive) where an insured is barred from defending itself. In such cases, intervention is necessary to protect the insurer's own interests because it may be obligated to pay any judgment rendered against its insured. (Reliance Ins. Co. v. Sup.Ct. (Wells) (2000) 84 CA4th 383, 386–387.) In this case, the Court finds Lloyd’s adequately established Defendant’s unwillingness to participate in the present litigation and a direct and immediate interest in the litigation.

 

Plaintiff has not opposed the motion.

 

Based on the foregoing, the motion is GRANTED. Moving Party is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 22STCV09109    Hearing Date: May 20, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL SECOND IME AND MENTAL EXAMINATION

Hearing Date: 5/20/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV09109 KOURTNIE HEATHER GOOD vs STATE CENTER LOCATING

Moving Party: Defendant Stake Center Locating

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL SECOND NEUROLOGICAL IME AND ARE DENIED

 

Background

 

On March 15, 2022, Plaintiff filed this car accident case alleging that a truck driven by Defendant Dondrick Eskridge, an employee of Defendant Stake Center Locating, Inc., collided with Plaintiff’s car. Plaintiff alleges traumatic brain injury and various other neurological and psychological conditions resulting from the accident. On January 22, 2024, Defendant noticed a second neurological IME and a mental examination to take place in February 2024. Plaintiff served timely objections. The parties are at an impasse because Plaintiff consents to only one examination and requires the raw data from the mental examination to be provided to Plaintiff’s counsel. Defendant now moves the Court to compel both examinations.

 

Legal Standard

 

Except for defense physical examinations in personal injury cases (in which one examination is permitted as a matter of course) and examinations arranged by stipulation, a court order is required for a physical or mental examination. Such order may be made only after notice and hearing and for “good cause shown.” (CCP §2032.320(a).) 

 

The examination will be limited to whatever condition is “in controversy” in the action. (CCP §2032.020(a).) This means the examination must be directly related to the specific injury or condition that is the subject of the litigation. Roberts v. Superior Court (1973) 9 Cal.3d 330, 337. Often, a party's pleadings put his or her mental or physical condition in controversy ... as when a plaintiff claims continuing mental or physical injury resulting from defendant's acts: “A party who chooses to allege that he has mental and emotional difficulties can hardly deny his mental state is in controversy.” See Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 837, wherein the plaintiff claimed ongoing emotional distress from sexual harassment by former employer. Discovery responses can also frame the issues regarding the injuries and damages alleged. 

 

Where the plaintiff's injuries are complex, several exams may be necessary by specialists in different fields. There is no limit on the number of physical or mental exams that may be ordered on a showing of good cause. The good cause requirement checks any potential harassment of the plaintiff. See Shapira v. Superior Court (1990) 224 Cal.App.3d 1249, 1255. 

 

The burden is on the moving party to show (by declarations or other evidence) that the examinee's condition is “in controversy” in the action. The moving party must also establish good cause for the examination(s) sought. A court order for physical or mental examination must be based on a showing of “good cause” (CCP § 2032.320(a)): (1) relevancy to the subject matter; and (2) specific facts justifying discovery: i.e., allegations showing the need for the information sought and lack of means for obtaining it elsewhere. (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.) The purpose is to protect an examinee's privacy by preventing annoying “fishing expeditions” - i.e., one party may not compel another to undergo psychiatric testing “solely on the basis of speculation that something of interest may surface.” (Id.) 

 

The notice of motion must state the time, place, identity and specialty of the examiner, and the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination.” (CCP § 2032.310(b).) The requirement for specification of the “manner, conditions, scope and nature of the examination” apparently requires disclosure of whatever diagnostic tests and procedures will be utilized (x-rays, blood and urine samples, etc.). (See CCP § 2032.220(c).) The notice of motion must be accompanied by a separate document setting forth the discovery request, the Declarations must state facts showing that “a reasonable and good faith attempt” to arrange the examination by stipulation was unsuccessful. (CCP § 2032.310(b).) 

 

Discussion

 

        Second Neurological IME

 

Both Defendant’s notice of a second Neurological IME and the Neurologist’s declaration fail to specify the diagnostic tests to be conducted as required by CCP § 2032.220(c).

 

The notice states: "The examination will also consist of a physical evaluation and assessment of Plaintiff and her medical conditions from a neurological standpoint, which may include testing and examination deemed necessary by Dr. Alexander for the purpose of diagnosing and evaluating the nature and extent of Plaintiff's medical conditions and alleged injuries at issue in the present lawsuit."

 

The Neurologist’s declaration states: "The examination will also consist of a neurological examination, which may include the following: evaluation of Plaintiff's cognitive function, balance, coordination, sensation, strength and reflexes, blood pressure, heart rate, range of motion of the cervical and lumbosacral spine and shoulder girdles, and spinal condition post-surgical fusion from a neurological perspective."

 

The motion and the supporting evidence fail to specify the diagnostic tests to be performed and to provide justification for the necessity and scope of the examination. For example, Defendant failed to show why tests on blood pressure and heart rate are necessary for a neurological examination for Plaintiff’s conditions such as headaches, dizziness, nausea, imbalance, difficulty concentrating, and memory impairment. The Court is not a medical expert and cannot assess whether the tests to be conducted are relevant or if there is good cause without an explanation.                               

Furthermore, a limited neurovascular examination was already conducted in the first IME. Without more detail, the Court cannot determine if the second IME will be duplicative of the neurological examination done by the first IME doctor.

 

That said, the Court agrees with Defendant that, based on the discovery responses provided, Plaintiff has put several conditions at issue that, on their face, seem neurological in nature, such as headaches, dizziness, nausea, imbalance, difficulty concentrating, and memory impairment. However, without specifying the tests to be conducted and providing an explanation from the Neurologist, the Court cannot assess whether the particular tests to be administered will be relevant to the conditions in controversy and not duplicative of those conducted in the previous IME. Therefore, Defendant has failed to meet its burden of demonstrating good cause, and the motion is denied without prejudice.

 

Mental Examination

 

Defendant’s motion to compel a mental examination suffers from the same defect. It lists a universe of 18 tests that may be administered without informing the Court which specific tests will be conducted. Defendant has not provided an explanation of why the tests are relevant to the conditions in controversy. Defendant argues that Plaintiff alleges headaches, depression, suicidal ideations, post-concussive syndrome, and traumatic brain injury. However, without a declaration or explanation, the Court cannot assess the relevance of the proposed tests, such as memory tests, intelligence tests, and personality tests, to the conditions at issue. Furthermore, because Defendant failed to specify the tests that the neurologist will perform on Plaintiff in the second IME, the Court cannot assess if any of the tests to be conducted in the mental examination will be duplicative. Thus, Defendant failed to meet its burden of demonstrating good cause, and the motion is DENIED without prejudice.

 

Given the allegations of TBI in the present case, the Court would usually be inclined to permit both a mental examination and a neurological IME upon a sufficient showing of good cause.
As for the raw data, the Court will usually permit raw data from the mental examination to be provided to Plaintiff’s counsel for cross-examination purposes, subject to a protective order. Given the Court’s inclination, the parties are ORDERED to meet and confer regarding the mental examination and second IME.

MOTIONS TO COMPEL SECOND IME AND MENTAL EXAMINATION; MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL

Hearing Date: 5/20/24¿ 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV17698 ANGELA T. ELAM, et al. vs FM RESTAURANTS EL TORITO 

Moving Party: Defendant Macerich Company 

Responding Party: Plaintiffs Angela T. Elam and Jennifer P. Richards

Notice: Sufficient¿ 

Ruling:

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL SECOND IME AND MENTAL EXAMINATION ARE DENIED

 

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL IS GRANTED

 

Background

On May 31, 2022, Plaintiffs Angela T. Elam and Jennifer P. Richards filed the present personal injury case alleging that they were struck by a falling tree branch and/or pole while dining outdoors at Defendant’s restaurant. Both Plaintiffs claim brain injury, anxiety, depression, and cognitive difficulties. Defendant now moves the Court to conduct a second IME and a mental examination on both Plaintiffs.

Preliminary Matters

Each motion requests both an IME and a mental examination for each Plaintiff. Each of Defendant’s motions should be filed as two separate motions. Defendant’s approach not only potentially allows Defendant to jump ahead of other litigants in the procedural queue but also deprives the Court of the full filing fees that assist in paying for the services it provides to the public.

(CCP § 2032.310(b).)¿ 

 

Neurological Examination for Plaintiff Angela T. Elam

Defendant’s neurologist will conduct a non-invasive examination of Plaintiff Angela Elam’s head and scalp. Defendant needs to specify the diagnostic tests to be performed and show how each test is related to the conditions in controversy. Without more detail on the specific tests to be conducted, the Court cannot assess whether there exists good cause for the proposed tests in the second IME and how this neurological examination differs from the previous orthopedic IME. The motion is denied without prejudice for failing to make a sufficient showing of good cause.

Orthopedic Examination for Plaintiff Jennifer P. Richards

Defendant’s Orthopedic expert will conduct an examination of the alleged orthopedic injuries related to Plaintiff Jennifer Richards’ neck, left shoulder, left elbow, and left thumb, as well as take an oral history of the alleged injuries. For the same reasons stated above, Defendant needs to provide the specific diagnostic tests to be performed, how each test is related to the conditions in controversy, and how it is different from those done in her previous neurological IME. The motion is denied without prejudice for failing to make a sufficient showing of good cause.

Mental Examination for Both Plaintiffs

Defendant needs to provide the exact diagnostic tests to be used and an explanation of why each test is necessary and relevant to the conditions in controversy, such as whether each test is intended to assess cognitive injuries or emotional distress. For example, one of the tests to be conducted is the Personality Assessment Inventory; on its face and without an explanation, the Court is unsure how this will assess Defendant’s allegations of cognitive difficulties and emotional distress. The motion is denied without prejudice for failing to make a sufficient showing of good cause.

Having said all that, based on the nature of the conditions alleged, the Court is inclined to grant a mental examination and a neurological IME, if Defendant has made a sufficient showing of good cause. As for the mental examination, the Court typically allows recording for the interview portion of the examination, and the raw data from the tests to be provided to Plaintiff’s counsel, subject to a protective order. Although the Court denied Defendant’s motions without prejudice, the Court ORDERS the parties are to meet and confer on the IMEs and mental examinations with the Court’s inclinations as guidance in seeking informal resolution.


Motion to Continue Trial

The parties entered into a joint stipulation to continue trial to March 26, 2025 and all pre-trial deadlines to follow the new trial date. In consideration of the age of the case, only one prior continuance, and the need for more time to conduct discovery on Plaintiff’s damages, Defendant's motion to continue the trial is GRANTED.

MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL  

Hearing Date: 5/20/2024 at 1:30 p.m.  

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV26975 JOSE GOMEZ, AN INDIVIDUAL vs JOHN DOE  

Moving Party: Plaintiff’s Counsel Elina Shakhbazyan

Responding Party: Unopposed  

Notice: Sufficient  

  

Ruling: MOTION TO BE RELIEVED AS COUNSEL IS GRANTED

  

Background

 

Attorney Elina Shakhbazyan represents Plaintiff. Shakhbazyan moves to be relieved as counsel, citing a breakdown in attorney-client communication. No opposition has been filed.

 

Legal Standard

 

The Court has discretion to allow an attorney to withdraw, and such a motion should be granted provided that there is no prejudice to the client and it does not disrupt the orderly process of justice. (See Ramirez v. Sturdevant (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 904, 915; People v. Prince (1968) 268 Cal.App.2d 398, 403-407.)

 

A motion to be relieved as counsel must be made on Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, subds. (a), (c), (e).) The requisite forms must be served “on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1362, subd. (d).)

¿ 

Analysis and Conclusion

 

Shakhbazyan has filed Judicial Council Form MC-051 (Notice of Motion and Motion), MC-052 (Declaration), and MC-053 (Proposed Order). Shakhbazyan seeks to be relieved as counsel for Plaintiff on the grounds that there has been a breakdown in the attorney-client communication. The Court finds this to be proper grounds for withdrawal. (See Estate of Falco (1987) 188 Cal.App.3d 1004, 1014 (a breakdown in the attorney-client relationship/communication is grounds for allowing the attorney to withdraw).)

 

The Court notes that the next hearing is an FSC set for 8/22/24, which should be sufficient time for Plaintiff to retain new counsel. Alternatively, if Plaintiff or new counsel needs additional time to prepare for trial, sufficient time exists for Plaintiff to take action to seek a continuance of the trial. Thus, the present motion to be relieved as counsel is GRANTED effective upon service on Plaintiff.

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS COMPLAINT

Hearing Date: 5/20/24

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV11491 JOSE GOMEZ GONZALEZ vs ZACHARY BORSON

Moving Party: Defendant Zachary Borson

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS COMPLAINT IS GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

A cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the initial complaint or cross-complaint against the cross-complainant must be filed before or at the same time as the answer to the initial complaint or cross-complaint, which answer must be filed within 30 days of service of the complaint or cross-complaint.¿(CCP §§ 412.20(a)(3), 428.50(a), 432.10.)¿Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a trial date.¿(CCP §428.50(b).)

 

If a party fails to file a cross-complaint within the time limits described above, he or she must obtain permission from the court to file the cross-complaint.¿(CCP §§ 426.50, 428.50(c).) The proposed cross-complaint is mandatory when it arises out of the same transaction as plaintiff’s claim. The court must grant leave to file the mandatory cross-complaint absent bad faith. (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50, Silver Organizations, Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 99.) Leave to file a permissive cross-complaint need only be granted in the interest of justice. (CCP §428.50(c).) Judicial policy generally supports the resolution of all disputed matters between parties, leave to amend or leave to cross-complain is typically granted liberally. (See Kolani v. Gluska (1998) 64 Cal.App.4th 402, 412.) The Court may, however, deny such leave if there is demonstrated prejudice to the opposing party, such as trial delays, loss of critical evidence, or increased preparation costs. (Id.)¿ “Cross complaints for comparative equitable indemnity would appear virtually always transactionally related to the main action.” (Time for Living, Inc. v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All American Develop. Co. (1991) 230 CA3d 30, 38.)

 

California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324 requires a copy of the proposed cross complaint to be attached to the motion. 

 

Discussion

On May 22, 2023, Plaintiff filed the present motor accident case against Defendants Zachary Borson and Naomi Sengal. On July 28, 2023, Defendant Borson filed his answer. Defendant Naomi Sengal has yet to be served. Defendant Borson now moves the Court to file a cross-complaint for indemnity and declaratory relief against Co-Defendant Sengal. No opposition was filed. Borson’s cross-complaint for indemnity is mandatory (Time for Living, Inc. v. Guy Hatfield Homes/All American Develop. Co. (1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 30, 38), and no opposition or any other document alleging bad faith has been filed. Defendant also attached a proposed cross-complaint to the moving papers. Thus, the present motion is GRANTED.

MOTION TO DEEM THE CONTENTS OF REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/20/24

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV14759 SAVANNAH KYLEE IBARRA, et al. vs LAZAROUS WILLIAMS, et al.

Moving Party: Defendant LAZAROUS WILLIAMS

Responding Party: Plaintiff

Notice: Sufficient

Ruling: MOTION TO DEEM THE CONTENTS OF REQUESTS FOR ADMISSION ADMITTED AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED

 

Legal Standard

 

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.”¿ CCP § 2033.010.¿ “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared ....”¿ CCP § 2033.250(a).

 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. CCP § 2033.280(a). The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted, as well as for monetary sanctions. CCP § 2033.280(b). The court shall issue this order unless the party to whom the request was made serves a response in substantial compliance prior to the hearing on the motion. CCP § 2033.280(c). Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to deem RFA admitted does not have any meet and confer requirements.

 

Discussion

On October 31, 2023, Defendant served its Requests for Admission, Set One, on Plaintiff. On January 11, 2024, Plaintiff served unverified responses. Defendant attempted to meet and confer but received no responses and now moves the Court to deem the contents of its Request for Admission, Set One, as admitted.

Although Plaintiff served responses to Defendant's Request for Admissions (RFA), Set One, these responses were unverified. Upon reviewing the responses, the Court noted that they contain substantive answers and are not solely objections. According to Appleton v. Superior Court (1988) 206 Cal. App. 3d 632, 635-636, unverified responses are “tantamount to no responses at all.” Plaintiff did not submit any opposition or other filings to indicate that verifications were served prior to the hearing date. Consequently, Defendant’s motion is GRANTED.

Furthermore, Defendant made attempts to meet and confer with Plaintiff concerning the verifications but received no response, necessitating the filing of this motion. Defendant has requested attorney’s fees in the amount of $1,010.00, which the Court finds reasonable. Therefore, Plaintiff and her counsel, jointly and severally, are ORDERED to pay sanctions in the amount of $1,010.00 to Defendant within 20 days from today.

MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL REPSONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, SET ONE, AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 5/20/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV16729 CESAR ALDANA REYES, et al. vs ALAN D. RUCK

Moving Party: Defendant Alan Ruck

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL INITIAL REPSONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION, SET ONE, AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE GRANTED.

 

Background

On December 21, 2023, Defendant Alan D. Ruck served Plaintiff Cesar Reyes with Request for Production, Set One. Plaintiff failed to produce responsive documents by the statutory deadline. Defendant gave Plaintiff several extensions. By April 19, 2024, when the present motion was filed, Defendant had not received the discovery responses at issue. No opposition was filed.

 

Legal Standard

 

A plaintiff may make a demand for production of documents and propound interrogatories without leave of court at any time 10 days after the service of the summons on, or appearance by, the party to whom the demand is directed, whichever occurs first. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.020, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.020, subd. (b).) The demand for production of documents is not limited by number, but the request must comply with the formatting requirements in Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.030. A party may propound 35 specially prepared interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action and any additional number of official form interrogatories that are relevant to the subject matter of the pending action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.030, subd. (a)(1) - (a)(2).)

¿¿ 

The party whom the request is propounded upon is required to respond within 30 days after service of a demand, but the parties are allowed to informally agree to an extension and confirm any such agreement in writing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.270, subd. (a) - (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.270, subd. (a) - (b).)

¿¿ 

If a party fails to timely respond to a request for production or interrogatories, the party to whom the request is directed waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.230, and waives any objection, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a).)¿¿The party propounding the discovery requests may move for an order compelling responses. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) Unlike a motion to compel further discovery responses, a motion to compel initial discovery responses does not have any meet and confer requirements.

¿ 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030, subdivision (a) provides, in pertinent part, that the court may impose a monetary sanction on a party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. A misuse of the discovery process includes failing to respond or submit to an authorized method of discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subd. (d).)¿¿A court has discretion to fix the amount of reasonable monetary sanctions. (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare Reit, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 791.)

¿¿¿¿ 

Analysis

 

It is undisputed that Plaintiff did not serve timely initial discovery responses. No opposition was filed, nor were any other documents showing that discovery responses had been served prior to the hearing. Thus, the present motion to compel is GRANTED, and Plaintiff is ORDERED to submit verified discovery responses without objections within 20 days from today.

Plaintiff’s non-responsiveness prompted Defendant to file the present motion and incur attorney’s fees. Defendant requests sanctions in the amount of $455.00, which the Court finds reasonable. Consequently, the Court sanctions Plaintiff in the amount of $455. Plaintiff and his counsel are ORDERED, jointly and severally, to pay sanctions of $455 to Defendant within 20 days of today’s date.
