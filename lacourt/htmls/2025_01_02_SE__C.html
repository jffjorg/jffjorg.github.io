SILVA v. SILVA

CASE NO.:  23NWCV00949

HEARING: 01/02/25

 

#2

 

Partition Referee MATTHEW L. TAYLOR’s motion for confirmation of the sale of real property located at 13024 Lefloss Avenue, Norwalk California is  GRANTED. CCP §873.730.

 

Moving Party to give notice.

 

No Oppositions filed as of December 30, 2024.

 

This action for partition of real property located at 13024 Lefloss Avenue, Norwalk, California (“Subject Property”) was filed on March 27, 2023.

 

On February 6, 2024, this Court entered an Interlocutory Judgment of Partition by Sale of Real Property and Appointment of Referee, ordering the private sale of the Subject Property and appointing Matthew L. Taylor as partition referee to sell the Subject Property.

 

Matthe L. Taylor now moves for confirmation of the sale of the Subject Property for $712,000 to buyers Gilberto Contreras, Leticia Contreras, Jennifer Contreras, and Adrian Fernandez as the highest bidders.

 

The Court finds that the proposed sale is reasonable and thus GRANTS the Petitioner Referee’s motion.

 

The Court will sign the Proposed Order filed in conjunction with the Subject Motion.

 

 

 


Case Number: 23NWCV00990    Hearing Date: January 2, 2025    Dept: C

Cardoza et al. v. City of Compton et al. (23NWCV00990)

This wrongful death action was filed on March 30, 2023. Trial is set for March 20, 2025. Plaintiffs now move ex parte for an order continuing the hearing Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication, which is currently set for January 22, 2024. Plaintiffs argue that, despite the Court granting Plaintiffs' Motions to Compel the Depositions of David Blake Jr. and Isaac Jacob Galvan on December 17, 2024, Plaintiffs have been unable to take their depositions. Plaintiffs noticed and personally served Mr. Galvan on December 23, 2024, but he failed to appear at his deposition. Plaintiffs have been unable to serve Mr. Blake but anticipates serving him on January 8, 2025, at his next criminal hearing. Plaintiffs argue these depositions are necessary for Plaintiffs' opposition to Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment.

Plaintiffs' Ex Parte Application for an Order Continuing Defendant’s Motion for Summary Judgment, or in the alternative, Summary Adjudication, is GRANTED.  The Court finds good cause to continue the Motion for Summary Judgment. The hearing is ADVANCED to this date and CONTINUED to Tuesday, February 18, 2025, at 10:30 a.m. in Department SE-C. This date is 30 days before trial, in accordance with CCP § 437c(3). Opposition and Reply briefs to be filed and served in compliance with the new hearing dates. Moving party to give notice.

CASE NO.: 23NWCV03670

HEARING:  January 2, 2024 @ 9:30 a.m.

 

#5

TENTATIVE ORDER

 

Defendant Carlos Ochoa’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED with leave to amend. Plaintiff to file amended complaint within 20 days.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

Background

 

This is a personal injury action. On November 13, 2023, Plaintiff Lourdes Beltran (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Carlos Ochoa (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 25. Emily Mercado and Richard Mercado were later named as Doe Defendants 1 and 2. The Complaint alleges that on April 27, 2022, Defendant’s dog bit and injured Plaintiff while she was lawfully on Defendant’s property to deliver food. The Complaint alleges the following causes of action: 1) Strict Liability under Civil Code Section 3342 and 2) Negligence.

 

Defendant now moves to strike portions of the Complaint pertaining to punitive damages—specifically, paragraphs 33, 34, 41, and prayer for relief paragraph 5.

 

The Motion is unopposed as of December 27, 2024.

 

Legal Standard

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 436 permits trial courts to strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in a pleading: 

 

 “The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: 

(a)¿Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. 

(b)¿Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 436.) 

 

 

Meet and Confer

 

Parties must meet and confer “in person or by telephone” before filing a motion to strike. (Code of Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).) Defense counsel attempted to meet and confer via letter to Plaintiff’s counsel to discuss the purported deficiencies in the Complaint. (Gamboa Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) Plaintiff’s counsel responded, arguing that punitive damages were supported by the allegations in the Complaint. (Gamboa Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. C.) The Court finds the meet and confer requirement has not been met because the parties did not meet and confer “in person or by telephone.” (Code of Civ. Proc. § 435.5, subd. (a).) Nevertheless, the Court will assess the merits of the Motion in interests of judicial efficiency.

 

Discussion

 

Defendant argues the allegations in the Complaint do not support a finding of oppression, malice, or fraud—rather, they are conclusory. For example, the Complaint does not allege facts to show why the dog was a “vicious dog with vicious and dangerous propensity” or how Defendant “knew that the vicious dog had a dangerous propensity to attack other people or animals.” (Compl., ¶¶ 35-36.) Defendant argues the Complaint alleges no facts to establish Defendant’s “evil motive” or “conscious disregard” of the safety of Plaintiff that would justify a punitive damages award. Plaintiff has filed no opposition.

 

“‘In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.’ [Citation.]” (Today IV’s Inc. v. Los Angeles County MTA (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1193.) Civil Code section 3294 provides that on a non-contract cause of action, a plaintiff may recover punitive damages when they prove “by clear and convincing evidence that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud, or malice.” (Civ. Code § 3294, subd. (a).) In a negligence action, plaintiffs must allege facts in the complaint demonstrating malice—conclusory allegations that simply characterize defendant's conduct as “malicious” are insufficient and subject to motion to strike. (See Smith v. Sup.Ct. (Bucher) (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1036, 1042 (finding, in a legal malpractice claim, plaintiff’s conclusory allegations that the attorneys' conduct was “intentional, knowing, malicious, fraudulent, false and deceitful” and in “conscious and knowing disregard of the interest and rights of plaintiff” were deficient as a matter of law to show entitlement to punitive damages).)

 

The Court finds the Complaint contains conclusory allegations of malice and oppression, which are insufficient to support a punitive damages award. A complaint must plead facts demonstrating malice or oppression, as opposed to characterizing allegations as malicious or oppressive. (See Smith, supra, 10 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1036, 1042.)

 

As to paragraph 34 and prayer for relief paragraph 5 of the Complaint which seek punitive damages based on Civil Code section 3340, the Court finds such section inapplicable to the instant case. Civil Code section 3340 awards exemplary damages for “wrongful injuries to animals being subjects of property.” (Civ. Code, § 3240) (Italics added). Here, Plaintiff, not an animal, was allegedly injured. The section is thus inapplicable, and the portions should be stricken.

 

Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s Motion to Strike.
