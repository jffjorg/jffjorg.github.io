DEPARTMENT 50 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV06517    Hearing Date: August 16, 2024    Dept: 50

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 50

 

DEVIN ESTIME, et al.,  

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

 

KAISER FOUNDATION HEALTH PLAN, INC., et al.,  

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

22STCV06517

Hearing Date:

August 16, 2024

Hearing Time:

10:00 a.m.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE THIRD AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Background

Plaintiffs Devin Estime, a minor, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Arti Bhimani, Michel Estime, and Arti Bhimani filed this action on February 22, 2022 against a number of defendants. On January 26, 2024, Plaintiff Devin Estime, a minor, by and through his Guardian Ad Litem, Arti Bhimani (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) against defendants Southern California Permanente Medical Group and Enrique Earle Emel, M.D. (jointly, “Defendants”). The SAC alleges causes of action for (1) negligence and (2) unfair business practices. 

Plaintiff now moves for an order granting him leave to file a third amended complaint. Defendants oppose.

Discussion

Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a)(1), “[t]he court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” Amendment may be allowed at any time before or after commencement of trial. ((Id., § 576.) “[T]he court’s discretion will usually be exercised liberally to permit amendment of the pleadings. The policy favoring amendment is so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified.” ((Howard v. County of San Diego (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1422, 1428 [internal citations omitted].) “If the motion to amend is timely made and the granting of the motion will not prejudice the opposing party, it is error to refuse permission to amend….” ((Morgan v. Superior Court of Los Angeles County (1959) 172 Cal.App.2d 527, 530.) Prejudice includes “delay in trial, loss of critical evidence, or added costs of preparation.” (Solit v. Tokai Bank, Ltd. New York Branch (1999) 68 Cal.App.4th 1435, 1448.)

A motion to amend a pleading before trial must include a copy of the proposed amendment or amended pleading, which must be serially numbered to differentiate it from previous pleadings or amendments. (¿¿Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a).)¿¿ The motion must also state what allegations are proposed to be deleted or added, by page, paragraph, and line number.  (¿Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd¿. (a).) Finally, “¿[a] separate declaration must accompany the motion and must specify: (1)¿The effect of the amendment; (2)¿Why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3)¿When the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4)¿The reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.¿” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, subd. (b), emphasis added.)

Plaintiff’s counsel attaches as Exhibit A to her declaration a copy of Plaintiff’s proposed Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”). (Ganesan Decl., ¶ 3, Ex. A.) In addition, Plaintiff’s counsel provides a copy of the redlined version of Plaintiff’s proposed TAC, showing the changes made to the SAC. (Ganesan Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. B.) In the proposed TAC, Plaintiff seeks to add an additional cause of action for fraudulent concealment against Defendants. (Ibid.)

The cause of action for fraudulent concealment in the proposed TAC alleges, inter alia, that “[o]n or about 11:00 am on March 8, 2021 at Kaiser’s Baldwin Hills-Crenshaw medical offices, DEVIN and ARTI met with EMEL for DEVIN’s wellness visit. During the visit, ARTI expressed her concern about Devin’s hunched posture…EMEL listened to ARTI’s concerns and conducted an examination of DEVIN, including of DEVIN’s back. In DEVIN’s medical records, EMEL noted that he observed DEVIN had ‘mild lumbar scoliosis’ and diagnosed DEVIN with ‘LUMBAR SCOLIOSIS’.” (Ganesan Decl., ¶ 4, Ex. B, ¶¶ 66-67.) The cause of action alleges that “EMEL did not disclose this diagnosis to DEVIN or ARTI…Instead, EMEL intentionally deceived them as to the cause of DEVIN’s hunched posture, claiming that it was due to the way DEVIN used his cell phone, and that it could be corrected by DEVIN holding up his phone when in use.” (Ibid., ¶¶ 68-69.)

As an initial matter, Defendants assert that “Plaintiff did not file this motion with enough notice for electronic service. Plaintiff is late by one (1) day.” (Opp’n at p. 6:25.) As noted by Defendants, “[u]nless otherwise ordered or specifically provided by law, all moving and supporting papers shall be served and filed at least 16 court days before the hearing.” ((Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).) Sixteen court days before the August 16, 2024 hearing is July 25, 2024. In addition, “[a]ny period of notice, or any right or duty to do any act or make any response within any period or on a date certain after the service of the document, which time period or date is prescribed by statute or rule of court, shall be extended after service by electronic means by two court days…” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1010.6, subd. (a)(3)(B).) The proof of service attached to the instant motion indicates that the motion was served by electronic service on July 24, 2024. However, two court days prior to July 25, 2024 is July 23, 2024. In the reply, Plaintiff acknowledges that the motion was untimely served, “due to Plaintiff’s counsel’s inadvertent calendaring oversight.” (Reply at p. 8:1.)

In the opposition, Defendants also assert that “[t]his motion is prejudicial to Defendants because there is no excuse or justification offered for the delay.” (Opp’n at p. 2:7-8.) Defendants note that this action was filed on February 22, 2022, and that the instant motion was filed on July 24, 2024, over two years later. Plaintiff’s motion does not appear to provide any justification for the delay in filing the instant motion. Defendants cite to Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 486, where the Court of Appeal found that “our independent review of the record reveals no abuse of discretion in the denial of the request for leave to amend. Magpali’s amendment was proposed on the eve of trial, nearly two years after the complaint was originally filed. He did not give an explanation for leaving the Act claim out of the original complaint or bringing the request to amend so late.” Plaintiff’s reply also does not appear to discuss the delay in filing the instant motion.

Moreover, the Court notes that Plaintiff’s counsel’s supporting declaration does not appear to specify “[w]hy the amendment is necessary and proper,” “[w]hen the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered,” or “[t]he reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subds. (b)(2)-(4).) As set forth above, California Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subdivision (b) is mandatory.[1]

In light of the foregoing, the Court denies Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a third amended complaint.  

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for leave to file a third amended complaint is denied, without prejudice. Plaintiff is to provide notice of this Order.¿

PLAINTIFF’S REQUEST FOR DEFAULT JUDGMENT

 

           

Plaintiff Torrance Flite Park, LLC (“Plaintiff”) requests entry of default judgment against Defendants Donald Orval Shaw and Advanced Tactics, Inc. Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $125,467.38, comprising $119,716.33 in damages, $5,051.05 in interest, and $700.00 in costs.  

The Court notes a few defects with the submitted default judgment package.

First, paragraph 8 of the Complaint in this action alleges that “[t]he following causes of action are attached and the statements above apply to each (each complaint must have one or more causes of action attached):…Breach of Contract.” (Compl., p. 2., emphasis in original.) The Court notes that “[i]t is well established a default judgment cannot properly be based on a complaint which fails to state a cause of action against the party defaulted because, as Witkin explains, [a] defendant who fails to answer admits only facts that are well pleaded.” (Falahati v. Second, Item 6(b)(2) of Plaintiff’s request for court judgment (Form CIV-100) states that the request was mailed to, inter alia, “Daniel Orval Shaw.” (Emphasis added.) This appears to be a typo, as the defendant in this action is Donald Orval Shaw.

Rules of Court, rule 3.1806; Kahn v. Lasorda's Dugout, Inc.¿(2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1118, 1124.)¿

 OSB INVESTMENT, LLC,

                        Plaintiff,

            vs.

 EDAN DAHAN, et al.,

                        Defendants.

Case No.:

24STCV06029

Hearing Date:

August 16, 2024

Hearing Time:    2:00 p.m.

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANTS EDEN DAHAN, ISRAEL DAHAN, AND BUILDERS GREEN REMODELING, INC.’S DEMURRER TO PLAINTIFF OSB INVESTMENT, LLC’S FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

 

Background

On March 11, 2024, Plaintiff OSB Investment, LLC (“Plaintiff”) filed this action against Defendants Edan Dahan, Israel Dahan, and Builders Green Remodeling, Inc.

On April 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”), alleging causes of action for (1) fraud, (2) breach of fiduciary duty, and (3) conversion.

Eden Dahan, Israel Dahan, and Builders Green Remodeling, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”) now demur to the second cause of action of the FAC. Plaintiff opposes.

Discussion

A.    Legal Standard

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. ((Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” ((C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) For the purpose of testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. ((Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-967.) A demurrer “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” ((Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.)  

B.    Allegations of the FAC

In the FAC, Plaintiff alleges that “[t]his is an action to recover more than half a million dollars in funds stolen by Defendants during a real estate joint venture involving the purchase, remodel/construction for eventual sale of a residential property.” (FAC, ¶ 12.)

“Plaintiff is and has been engaged in real estate development, most typically in the role of financing. Defendants have held themselves out as being top-rate builders and construction professionals, with skill and experience in residential remodels and complete builds.” (FAC, ¶ 13.) “Plaintiff and Defendants had done real estate joint venturers in the past, and in doing so, they previously formed a limited liability company called 2695 Hidden Valley, LLC” (“HVLLC”). (FAC, ¶ 13.) After the completion of an unrelated HVLLC project, all shares in that entity were transferred back to Defendants. (FAC, ¶ 14.)

The property at issue in this action is “6643 Miurlands Drive, La Jolla, California, 92037” (the “Property”). (FAC, ¶ 13.) Plaintiff alleges that “[t]he terms of the Joint Venture Agreement were straightforward: a. OSB would provide the downpayment funds in the amount of $579,500.00; b. Defendants would handle the day-to-day construction/remodel of the residence on the Property; c. On completion of the construction, the Property would be listed for sale and sold to the highest bidder; d. On sale, OSB would receive the downpayment funds back in first position, and the parties would split the remaining profits.” (FAC, ¶ 13.)

“[B]y February 13, 2020, OSB had wired or otherwise provided the total sum of $579,500.00 to Great Pacific Escrow to purchase the Property.” (FAC, ¶ 16.) “On February 19, 2020, escrow in fact closed and HVLLC became the legal owner of the Property.” (FAC, ¶ 17.)

“As the project moved forward, OSB would later learn that, on information and belief, Defendants used proceeds earmarked for this project for other projects of their own, or for other purposes not involving Plaintiff. Plaintiff learned this because at some point, also unbeknownst to Plaintiff, Defendants refinanced the Property and took those proceeds for themselves.” (FAC, ¶ 19.) “Additionally, Defendants were continually behind on their construction obligations, and continually claiming that the project needed more funds, and thus as a natural course, the project was overbudget and behind schedule.” (FAC, ¶ 19.)

“Ultimately Defendants, being the sole membership interest holders in HVLLC, sold the Property to a third-party buyer without ever disclosing this information to OSB.” (FAC, ¶ 21.) Plaintiff alleges that “in doing so Defendants literally and brazenly stole each and every dollar of profit resulting from the sale, including the deposit money provided by OSB, leaving OSB with nothing. All the while OSB continued to believe the project was behind but was progressing, and thus was unaware of the sale until in and around May of 2021.” (FAC, ¶ 21.)

Plaintiff alleges that “[u]ltimately, in engaging in the acts set forth herein without the knowledge or consent of Plaintiff, Defendants stole: a. $579,500.00, which was the total amount of the purchase deposit provided by OSB; b. Approximately $370,000.00 from the refinancing proceeds; and c. $180,250, which represents 50% of the profits, after deducting the OSB down payment.” (FAC, ¶ 23.)

C.    Second Cause of Action for Breach of Fiduciary Duty  

In the second cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, Plaintiff alleges that “[a]s a result of the joint venture agreement created by and between the parties hereto, a fiduciary duty exists between OSB and Defendants with respect to their rights, obligations and duties to each other in connection with the operation of the joint venture, disposition of the Property and distribution of the proceeds from sale of the Property.” (FAC, ¶ 38.) Plaintiff alleges that “Defendants and each of them have breached their fiduciary duty to OSB by, amongst other things, engaging in acts and omissions to Plaintiff’s detriment, converting to their own account money and other consideration related to the Property including the deposit funds provided by OSB, the refinancing fees taken by Defendants, and the profits that OSB was entitled to on sale of the Property.” (FAC, ¶ 39.)

“The elements of a claim for breach of fiduciary duty are (1) the existence of a fiduciary relationship, (2) its breach, and (3) damage proximately caused by that breach.” ((O'Neal v. Stanislaus County Employees’ Retirement Assn. (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 1184, 1215.) In the demurrer, Defendants assert that “Plaintiff has failed to state facts sufficient to establish the first element, namely, the existence of a fiduciary relationship.” (Demurrer at p.  7:26-28.) 

As set forth above, Plaintiff alleges in the second cause of action that “[a]s a result of the joint venture agreement created by and between the parties hereto, a fiduciary duty exists between OSB and Defendants...” (FAC, ¶ 38, emphasis added.) Defendants assert that “Plaintiff’s allegations demonstrate that no joint venture existed between Plaintiff and Defendants.” (Demurrer at p. 8:13-14.)

Defendants cite to Cochrum v. Costa Victoria Healthcare, LLC (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 1034, 1053, where the Court of Appeal noted that “[a] joint venture…is an undertaking by two or more persons jointly to carry out a single business enterprise for profit. There are three basic elements of a joint venture: the members must have joint control over the venture (even though they may delegate it), they must share the profits of the undertaking, and the members must each have an ownership interest in the enterprise.” (Internal quotations and citation omitted.)

Defendants assert that here, Plaintiff does not allege that it had any ownership interests in the alleged joint venture. Defendants assert that to the contrary, Plaintiff alleges that “after the completion of the unrelated HVLLC project, all shares in that entity were transferred back to Defendants, and thus Plaintiff was without legal ownership interest in the Property.” (FAC, ¶ 14.) Plaintiff alleges that “[b]ecause OSB was not a member or interest holder in HVLLC, OSB had no visibility or access into any of HVLLC’s transactions.” (FAC, ¶ 19.)

In the opposition, Plaintiff does not appear to address or dispute Defendants’ assertion that Plaintiff does not allege that it had any ownership interests in the alleged joint venture. The Court agrees with Defendants that Plaintiff does not appear to allege that “the members…each have an ownership interest in the enterprise.” ((Cochrum v. Costa Victoria Healthcare, LLC, supra, 25 Cal.App.5th at p. 1053)

Defendants also assert that “Plaintiff has failed to satisfy the element of control required for a joint venture.” (Demurrer at p. 10:1-2.) Defendants assert that “Plaintiff clarifies numerous times throughout the FAC that Plaintiff did not exercise any control over the LLC, Flipping Project, or the Property.” (Demurrer at p. 10:9-10.) Defendants note that Plaintiff alleges, inter alia, that “Plaintiff was without legal ownership interest in the Property,” and that “[g]iven the structure of the relationship, OSB and its principals were not directly involved in the day-to-day operations at the Property, but instead trusted its partners to fulfill their obligations in a timely, competent manner…” (FAC, ¶¶ 14-15.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendants were “the sole membership interest holders in HVLLC.” (FAC, ¶ 21.) As noted by Defendants, Plaintiff further alleges that “Defendants had complete control over HVLLC and thus Plaintiff OSB had no access to or control over the books and records of this entity, and accordingly was not a part of or aware of any financial transactions that Defendants would cause HVLLC to engage in, or that the Defendants would otherwise undertake in the name of HVLLC.” (FAC, ¶ 18.)

Defendants assert that the “allegations…establish that Plaintiff simply provided money for the Flipping Project and, thereafter, did not exercise any control over anything.” (Demurrer at p. 11:1-2.) In the opposition, Plaintiff does not appear to address or dispute Defendants’ assertion that Plaintiff failed to satisfy the element of control required for a joint venture. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff does not appear to allege that “the members…have joint control over the venture.” ((Cochrum v. Costa Victoria Healthcare, LLC, supra, 25 Cal.App.5th at p. 1053.)

In light of the foregoing, the Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action of the FAC, with leave to amend.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the Court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the second cause of action of the FAC, with leave to amend.
