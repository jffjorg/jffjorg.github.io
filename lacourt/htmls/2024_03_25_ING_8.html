Case Number: 20STCV09554    Hearing Date: March 25, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿ 

¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                    March 25, 2024¿¿ 

¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                     20STCV09554 

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                           Maria Lopez, et al. v. Matrix Service, Inc., et al.                       .¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTIES:               Defendant Matrix Service, Inc.¿ 

¿¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Defendant, Torrance Refining Company, LLC

¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                           September 9, 2024¿ 

¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                                  (1) Ex Parte Application of defendant, matrix Service, Inc., for an order compelling answers to Deposition Questions and Production of Documents by Orlando Carbajal

¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                     (1) GRANTED.  A party is entitled to ask the verifier of discovery responses if the verifier knows whether a diligent search and reasonable inquiry were undertaken to ensure that all responsive documents in the party’s possession, custody or control have been produced

¿¿ 

¿ 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿ 

¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿ 

¿ 

This action concerns the wrongful death of decedent Gamaliel Gonzalez, who was working inside an oil storage tank at Torrance Refinery, when a jack stand his crew was using malfunctioned under weight, causing parts of the jack stand to break apart under high velocity, striking him in the head. Decedent Gamaliel Gonzalez was employed by J.T. Thorpe, a company engaged in the business of oil tank maintenance.

 

On May 8, 2020, Plaintiffs Maria Lopez and Martin Gonzalez filed the operative first amended complaint for negligence (wrongful death) and strict liability against Matrix Service, Inc. (“Matrix”) and Torrance Refining Company LLC (“TRC”). TRC owns Torrance Refinery and the tank where the accident occurred. Matrix is alleged to have worked on the design and manufacture of the jack stand that malfunctioned and caused the accident.

 

On September 17, 2020, Plaintiffs Sonia Soto, individually and as successor in interest to The Estate of Gamaliel Gonzalez, as well as Jayden Salas, Giancarlo Salas, and Christian Gonzalez filed a complaint alleging various causes of action against Matrix and TRC.

 

On December 22, 2023, Matrix notes that it served a Notice of Deposition of Orlando Carbajal and Request for Production of Documents, which contained nine (9) separate document requests. Matrix contends that through meet and confer processes and due to availability of the parties, the deposition of Deponent continued to March 8, 2024. However, Matrix argues that at no time did TORC object to the production of documents contained in the Notie of Deposition for Deponent.

 

On February 15, 2024, the Court issued an order denying Matrix’s Motion to Issue Sanctions, in part, on condition that TORC serve verifications for a variety of written discovery responses that TORC had never verified. Matrix further notes that on February 28, 2024, pursuant to this Court’s order, TORC served verifications for a variety of written discovery responses, all of which were verified by Deponent. Furthermore, Matrix notes that at his deposition, Deponent confirmed that the signatures on the verifications were his.

 

However, despite this, Matrix contends that on March 8, 2024, when the Deponent’s deposition proceeded, TORC’s counsel refused to allow deponent to answer several questions and, instead, instructed Deponent not to answer such questions. For example, Matric notes the following back and forth during Deponent’s deposition:

 

Q. For example in request for production 82 it asks for TORC's policies procedures and policies in place at the time of the incident relating to Torrance Refining Company's management with the use of compressed air equipment on the premises. The further response essentially states that there are no other documents other than what's been produced previously as exhibits A through D. And my question you to is when you signed these verifications did you review the documents that were produced as exhibits A through D?

 

MS. HARWELL: Mr. Watts, this was done with assistance of legal counsel both in-house and external this is not an appropriate question for Mr. Carbajal I'm going to instruct him not to answer.

¿II.ANALYSIS¿ 

¿ 

            Preliminarily, this Court notes that it is not inclined to rule on this motion based on TORC’s “lack of urgency” argument given the looming Motion for Summary Judgment Opposition. Instead, because of said looming Motion for Summary Judgement Opposition, this Court approached Matrix’s motion as if it were a regularly noticed motion.

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

A party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling a deponent to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent’s control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, if the deponent fails to answer or produce such information. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480(a).) If  a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 2025.480(a).)  If the court determines that the answer or production sought is subject to discovery, it shall order that the answer be given or the production be made on the resumption of the deposition.  (CCP § 2025.480(i).)  “This rule applies to subpoenas for production of documents at a deposition and also to business records subpoenas ....”  (Weil & Brown, ¶ 8:609.1 [citing Code Civ. Proc, § 2025.480(b)].) 

 

B.    Discussion

 

The moving papers here show that Deponent – who verified TORC’s discovery responses – refused to answer questions at his deposition because he was instructed not to answer such questions by his counsel. Matrix argues that such instructions were without merit and a misuse of the discovery process.  Matrix contends that a “verification is “in effect a declaration that the party has disclosed all information available to [it].” (Dayo v. Kilbourne (1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 782.) Further, pursuant to Melendrez v. Superior Ct. (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 1343 (“Melendres”) “[a]s a general matter, when an individual verifies discovery responses, further discovery can be directed to that individual to determine the sources of the initial response.” (Melendrez, supra, 215 Cal.App.4th at 1352.)

 

The court in Melendrez addressed the situation regarding when an attorney verifies a responses as an officer or agent of a corporate party and held that doing so “will constitute a limited waiver of the attorney-client and work product privileges, during any subsequent discovery from that attorney concerning the identity of the sources of the information contained in the response.”  (Id. at 1351.) The court in Melendrez found that “the reason for this waiver should be obvious. As a general matter, when an individual verifies discovery responses, further discovery can be directed to that individual to determine the sources for the initial responses.”  (Id. at 1352.) Therefore, the Melendrez court recognized that a party has the right to discover the sources of responses from a corporation where an individual verifies the response. The attorney client privilege protects communications, not the sources that were searched in order to obtain it.  Moreover, Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.250 expressly waives the attorney-client privilege “during any subsequent discovery from that attorney concerning the identity of the sources of the information contained in the response,” which expressly recognizes attorney client privilege does not apply to the identity of sources of information.  It appears that Plaintiff merely seeks to identify the sources of the documents produced in discovery, whether the deponent had reviewed the documents prior to signing them, and how the deponent gained his information and belief that the matters alleged in the discovery responses were true. All of these inquiries seem to be permissible under Melendrez.  It may well be that the corporate verifier here will testify that he does not know or that he himself did not review the documents produced, but the instructions not to answer were improper and the Court overrules those objections.

 

In Defendant TORC’s opposition, they do not oppose the motion on its merits, but does so procedurally. Here, as noted above, the Court understands that the ex parte application is brought on very short and arguable untimely notice, but the Court briefly continued the hearing to facilitate TORC’s written opposition and Court’s consideration of the same.  With respect to the opposition’s assertion of a lack of exigency, the March 28 date for filing opposition to the pending MSJ provides that exigency, particularly since the deposition of this witness was apparently postponed by TORC.  With respect to the fact that Matrix only attaches the rough transcript rather than a certified transcript in final form, there is no showing that the rough transcript fails to capture the nature of the questions and answers that are the subject of this motion.  With respect to the argument that the ex parte application fails to state the  date on which Matrix seeks to schedule the deposition, the Court will address that issue at the March 25 hearing.  TORC will be permitted to present oral argument as to the substantive issues of why it instructed its Deponent not to answer the questions about his verifications.

 

However, because this Matrix’s motion is in line with the precedent set in Melendrez, this Court finds good cause to grant this motion, and require further deposition of TORC’s deponent prior to the filing of Matrix’s motion for summary judgment opposition being required to be filed.

 

IV. CONCLUSION¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

For the foregoing reasons, Matrix’s motion is tentatively GRANTED.
