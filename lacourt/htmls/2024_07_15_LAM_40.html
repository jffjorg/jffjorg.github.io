DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 22STCV06139    Hearing Date: July 15, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

5007 HOLDINGS, LLC, a California limited liability company,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

JUN I. YOON, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jun I. Yoon Trust UDT August 15, 1994; and DOES 1 to 10,

                        Defendants.

 Case No.:          22STCV06139

 Hearing Date:   7/15/24

 Trial Date:        N/A

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Defendant Jun I. Yoon, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jun I. Yoon Trust UDT August 15, 1994’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions of $4,150; and

Defendant Jun I. Yoon, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jun I. Yoon Trust UDT August 15, 1994’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Sanctions of $4,150; and

Defendant Jun I. Yoon, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jun I. Yoon Trust UDT August 15, 1994’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Requests for Production, Set One, and Request for Sanctions of $4,150; and

Defendant Jun I. Yoon, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jun I. Yoon Trust UDT August 15, 1994’s Motion to Compel the Deposition of Plaintiff, and Request for Sanctions of $4,150

 

 

Background

Plaintiff 5007 Holdings, LLC sues Defendants Jun I. Yoon, an individual, and as Trustee of the Jun I. Yoon Trust UDT August 15, 1994 (“Yoon”) and Does 1 to 10 pursuant to an August 7, 2023 Second Amended Complaint (SAC) alleging claims of (1) Breach of Contract and (2) Misrepresentation.

The claims arise from allegations that Plaintiff 5007 Holdings—a successor-in-interest borrower on a loan made pursuant to a promissory note (as amended) and deed of trust secured by real property owned by Plaintiff in Marina Del Rey, California—was overcharged interest and late fee payments, as well as attorney fees charged in relation to loan renegotiations with Defendants Yoon, the lender, and that Yoon made misrepresentations in relation to the principal and interest owned on the loan.

 

Motions to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories, Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production

            Defendant Yoon served Plaintiff with Special Interrogatories (Set One), Form Interrogatories (Set One) and Requests for Production (Set One) (“Written Discovery”) on February 16, 2024.  (Knox Dec., ¶2.)  Plaintiff did not serve any responses.  (Id. at ¶3.)

            On April 12, 2024, Defendant served and filed these motions to compel. A facially valid proof of service shows service by electronic service on Plaintiff’s counsel at the email address on the Third Amended Complaint filed February 7, 2024.  Plaintiff filed no opposition as of July 12, 2024.

Defendant Yoon’s motions to compel responses without objection to the Written Discovery are granted pursuant to CCP §§2030.290 and 2031.300.  Plaintiff is ordered to serve responses without objection within 20 days. 

 

Motion to Compel Deposition

Defendant Yoon served Plaintiff with a deposition notice on February 21, 2024 for deposition on March 21, 2024.  (Knox Dec., ¶4.)  Plaintiff did not serve objections, failed to appear at deposition, has failed to make any witness available despite reassurances that it would do so and has failed to provide a firm deposition date.  (Knox Dec., ¶¶4-6.) 

On April 12, 2024, Defendant served and filed this motion to compel. A facially valid proof of service shows service by electronic service on Plaintiff’s counsel at the email address on the Third Amended Complaint filed February 7, 2024.  Plaintiff filed no opposition as of July 12, 2024.

Defendant Yoon’s motion to compel deposition of Plaintiff is granted pursuant to CCP §2025.450.  Plaintiff is ordered to appear for deposition within 30 days. 

Sanctions

Defendant Yoon asks that the Court impose sanctions of $4,150 for all four discovery motions.  Defense counsel spent 5.5 hours in total on the four motions and estimated spending an additional 3 hours reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition, drafting reply papers and preparing and attending hearing on the motions for a total of 8.5 hours @ $460 per hour.  Defense counsel also incurred $240 in filing fees.  (Knox Dec., ¶10.) 

No oppositions or replies were filed.  As such, the sanctions are reduced to $3,230 for 5.5 hours motion prep and 1 hour hearing attendance and $240 in filling fees.  Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel are ordered to pay sanctions in the amount of $3,230 within 20 days. 

Conclusion

Defendant Yoon’s Motion to Compel Responses to Special Interrogatories (Set One), Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories (Set One) and Motion to Compel Responses to Requests for Production (Set One) from Plaintiff are granted pursuant to CCP §2030.290 and 2031.3100.  Plaintiff is ordered to serve responses without objection within 20 days. 

Defendant Yoon’s Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff is granted pursuant to CCP §2025.450. Plaintiff is ordered to appear for deposition within 30 days at a date to be mutually agreed or, if Plaintiff fails to meet and confer, at a date of Defendant’s choosing no less than 10 days from today’s order.

Defendant The People of the State of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation’s Demurrer to First Amended Complaint.

 

 

Background

Pleadings

Plaintiff Apex Development, Inc. (Apex) sues Defendant The People of the State of California (the State), acting by and through the Department of Transportation (erroneously sued as “California Department of Transportation, a department of the State of California”) and Does 1 through 20 pursuant to a February 9, 2024 First Amended Complaint alleging claims of (1) quasi-contract—restitution and (2) Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations.

The claims arise from the following allegations. Apex rents multiple airspace leases from the California Department of Transportation (CalTrans), which are typically adjacent to freeway onramps and exists or under elevated portions of freeways, including a CalTrans lot located under the Interstate 10 freeway in Los Angeles, California at the intersection of 14th Street and Lawrence, Los Angeles 90021, subject an airspace lease [No. 07-LAX010-0012] (the Property). In relation to the Property, Apex has invested $139,899 to improve the lot, including by cleaning debris and other refuse from the Property, making repairs to the Property due to CalTrans freeway retrofitting, making safety repairs to paving and crack filler on the Property, and making repairs to the fence on the Property. Apex had reported these issues to CalTrans prior to making repairs, but CalTrans failed to act and offered no contribution toward possible expenses, forcing Apex to incur the above costs. Apex later requested restitution for its expenses maintaining the Property, which CalTrans rejected. As a result, in May 2023, Apex filed a government claim. Shortly prior to this, in April 2023, a CalTrans employee named “Daryll” entered the Property without Apex’s permission and directed Apex’s tenants not to pay rent to Apex. Apex complained of Darryll’s actions with CalTrans, which dismissed Apex’s claims and continued to send Darryll and other employees to enter the Property without notifying Apex and instructing Apex’s tenants to not pay rent to Apex.

Motion Before the Court

On March 26, 2024, the State filed a demurrer to the first and second causes of action of the FAC based on sufficiency of pleading.

As of July 12, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

The State’s demurrer is now before the Court.

 

Demurrer

Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) This device can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) To sufficiently allege a cause of action, a complaint must allege all the ultimate facts—that is, the facts needed to establish each element of the cause of action pleaded. (Committee on Children’s Television, Inc. v. General Foods Corp. (1983) 35 Cal.3d 197, 212, superseded by statute as stated in Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 242.) Thus, “[t]o survive a [general] demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.) In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

Demurrer, Complaint, First and Second Causes of Action, Quasi-Contract—Restitution and Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations: SUSTAINED, without leave to amend.

After review, the Court SUSTAINS the State’s demurrer.

The Court initially notes that the demurrer was served on Plaintiff Apex’s counsel via email to the same email address appearing in the Complaint. (Demurrer, Proof of Service [service by email to mdattaray@dattaraylaw.com]; cf. FAC, p. 1 [caption page containing same email address for Plaintiff’s counsel].)

Despite service, no opposition has been filed.

Moving to the merits, as with the prior demurrer to the original complaint, the Court finds that one argument is availing as to both causes of action: the Complaint fails to cite a statute permitting Apex to sue a public entity for unjust enrichment or intentional interference with contractual relations. (See Demurrer, pp. 4-5, 7-8.) “Except as otherwise provided by statute,” “(a) [a] public entity is not liable for an injury, whether such injury arises out of an act or omission of the public entity or a public employee or any other person.” (Gov. Code, § 815, subd. (a).) A review of the Complaint shows that no statute is referenced. Rather, the two counts stated in the Complaint are tort claims—and unjust enrichment is not a claim at all, as discussed below. It therefore follows that the Complaint’s claims are not sufficiently stated. This is particularly the case where “to state a cause of action against a public entity, every fact material to the existence of its statutory liability must be pleaded with particularity” (Lopez v. Southern Cal. Rapid Transit Dist. (1985) 40 Cal.3d 780, 795, citations omitted), including the existence of a statutory duty, where “[d]uty cannot be alleged simply by stating ‘defendant had a duty under the law’; that is a conclusion of law, not an allegation of fact” (Searcey v. Hemet Unified School Dist. (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 792, 902, citations omitted).

In addition, as to the quasi-contract cause of action, applicable law holds that no such claim may be brought against a public entity.  “As a general rule, a public entity cannot be sued on a implied in law or quasi-contract theory, because such a theory is based on quantum meruit or restitution considerations which are outweighed by the need to protect and limit a public entity’s contractual obligations.”  (Lundeen Coatings Corp. v. Department of Water & Power (1991) 232 Cal.App.3d 816, 831, fn 9.) 

The State’s demurrer is thus SUSTAINED.

The Court previously granted Plaintiff leave to amend despite its failure to file an opposition.  Plaintiff has again failed to file an opposition to Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC.  Plaintiff’s FAC failed to cure the defects raised by the prior demurrer, i.e. the failure to cite any statutory basis for liability.  As such, Plaintiff fails to establish that the defects can be cured with leave to amend.  (Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.)  In addition, Plaintiff’s failure to oppose may be deemed an abandonment of the claims alleged in the FAC.  (Herzberg v. County of Plumas (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 1, 20.)  For these reasons, leave to amend is denied. 

Conclusion

Defendant The People of the State of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation’s Demurrer to Complaint is SUSTAINED, without leave to amend.  Defendant The People of the State of California, acting by and through the Department of Transportation, to file a proposed order of dismissal within 20 days.
