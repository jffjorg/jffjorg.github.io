DEPARTMENT F51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F51, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2251.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 23CHCV01719    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: F51

SEPTEMBER 12, 2024

 

MOTIONS TO COMPEL DISCOVERY RESPONSES

(Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, Requests for Admission, and

Requests for Production of Documents, Set One)

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23CHCV01719

  

Motions Filed: 5/2/24, 5/6/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Frank Rosas, Jr. (“Plaintiff”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant FCA US, LLC (“Defendant”)

NOTICE: OK

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: Orders compelling Defendant’s verified, objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Admission (“RFAs”), Set One. Plaintiff further requests that the Court order monetary sanctions against Defendant and/or its counsel in the combined amount of $11,040.00.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s first set of discovery requests within 30 days. The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $510.00, payable within 45 days.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff brings this action under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civil Code § 1790 et seq.) for a vehicle he purchased on or around 2/16/22, for which Defendant issued the manufacturer’s express warranty. (Compl. ¶¶ 7, 9.) Plaintiff alleges that the subject vehicle exhibited “defects, nonconformities, maladjustments or malfunctions relating to, inter alia, screen glitches in and out when in use, center of cluster displays water spots behind screen, cover of instrument panel is deformed from factory, and harsh clunk felt from drivetrain,” and Defendant “were unable or unwilling to make the SUBJECT VEHICLE conform to the applicable warranties.”  (Id. at ¶¶ 13–16.)

 

On 6/14/23, Plaintiff filed her complaint, alleging against the Defendant and the dealership the following causes of action: (1) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; (3) Violation of Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2; and (4) Negligent Repair (against the dealership defendant). On 7/18/23, Defendants filed their answers.

 

On 12/13/23, Plaintiff served her first set of Form Interrogatories, RFPs, Special Interrogatories, and RFAs on Defendant. (Decl. of Laura R. Schwartz ¶ 2.) On 5/2/24, Plaintiff filed the instant motions to compel Defendant’s responses thereto. On 8/12/24 and 8/14/24, Defendant filed its oppositions thereto. On 9/6/24, Plaintiff filed his replies.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A propounding party may move for an order compelling a response to interrogatories if the responding party fails to serve a timely response within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.290, subd. (b); 2030.260, subd. (a).) Furthermore, the responding party who fails to serve a timely response “waives any right to exercise the option to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product” unless the Court grants it relief upon motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290, subd. (a).)

 

A propounding party may move for an order compelling a response to a request for production of documents if the responding party fails to serve a timely response within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2031.300, subd. (b); 2031.260, subd. (a).) Furthermore, the responding party who fails to serve a timely response “waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product” unless the Court grants it relief upon motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

 

A propounding party may move for an order deeming the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted if the responding party fails to serve a timely response within 30 days. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2033.280, subd. (b); 2033.250, subd. (a).) Furthermore, the responding party who fails to serve a timely response “waives any objection to the requests, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product” unless the Court grants it relief upon motion. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (a).) “The court shall make this order, unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.” (Id. at subd. (c).)

 

Here, as Plaintiff electronically served his first set of discovery requests on Defendant on 12/13/23, the last day for Defendant to respond was 1/15/24. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1010.6.) Defendant argues that the instant motions are moot because on 8/12/24 and 8/13/24, Defendant served verified responses to Plaintiff’s discovery requests. (Decl. of Gregory G. Brezovec ¶ 5.) Plaintiff argues in reply that because Defendant has included objections in its discovery responses, the responses are therefore not code-compliant, and the Court should grant the instant motions. (Pl.’s Reply 6:1–3.)

 

The Court agrees, and reiterates its above finding that Defendant failed to timely respond to Plaintiff’s validly-served discovery requests. The Court therefore finds that by 1/15/24, Defendant had waived any objections to the discovery requests.

 

Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motions to compel Defendant’s objection-free discovery responses are granted.

 

However, based on the “substantial compliance” in responding to the Requests for Admissions, the request to deem them admitted is denied. 

 

 

Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.290, subd. (c).)

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.300, subd. (c).)

 

“It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2033.280, subd. (c).) Additionally, “the court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030, subd. (a).)

 

Here, Plaintiff requests $2,760.00 in monetary sanctions to be imposed on Defendant and/or its attorneys per motion, totaling $11,040.00. This amount encompasses: (1) 2 hours of Plaintiff’s attorney’s time spent drafting each motion; and (2) 4 hours spent reviewing each of Defendant’s oppositions, drafting replies, and appearing for each hearing, at counsel’s hourly rate of $450.00 per hours. (Schwartz Decl. ¶ 12.) Plaintiff also seeks to recover $60.00 in filing fees per motion. (Ibid.)

 

Defendant argues in opposition that sanctions should not be awarded, as “there is simply no justification for sanctions in this case against FCA US given good faith efforts to address its mistake by counsel.” (Def.’s Opp. 5:15–16.) Plaintiff disagrees, arguing that Defendant “failed to timely respond to an authorized method of discovery, failed to respond to the repeated attempts by Plaintiff to obtain responses without the need for a discovery motion, and failed to respond to the numerous offers by Plaintiff to withdraw the motions in return for obtaining the responses.” (Pl.’s Reply 5:26–6:1.)

 

In granting the instant motions, the Court finds it reasonable to award Plaintiff sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $510.00. 

 

CONCLUSION 

The motions are granted. Defendant is ordered to provide objection-free responses to Plaintiff’s first set of discovery requests within 30 days. The Court imposes sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $510.00 within 45 days. 

 


Case Number: 23CHCV02619    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: F51


SEPTEMBER 12, 2024

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER DISCOVERY RESPONSES

(Requests for Production of Documents, Set One)

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 23CHCV02619

 

Motion filed: 5/22/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiffs Anne Hawley and William Hawley, individually and as Successors in Interest to Decedent, Christopher Hawley (collectively, “Plaintiffs”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Magic Mountain LLC (“Defendant”)

NOTICE: OK

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order compelling Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiffs’ Requests for Production of Documents (“RFPs”), Set One, Nos. 1, 3, 16–18, 20–21, 26–27, 29–37, 41, 45–46, 48–50, and 52–55.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is granted in part. Defendant ordered to provide further code-compliant responses and a privilege log, as set forth below, to RFP Nos. Nos. 1, 3, 16–18, 20–21, 26–27, 29–37, 45–46, 48–50, and 52–55 within 30 days. The Court imposes monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $500.00, payable within 45 days.

 

Defendant is reminded that all documents “must be text searchable when technologically feasible without impairment of the document’s image.” (CRC 2.256(b)(3).) Failure to comply with these requirements in the future may result in papers being rejected, matters being placed off calendar, matters being continued so documents can be resubmitted in compliance with these requirements, documents not being considered and/or the imposition of sanctions.

 

BACKGROUND

 

This is a wrongful death action wherein Plaintiffs are the parents of decedent Christopher Hawley (“Decedent”). Plaintiffs allege that on 6/23/22, Decedent visited Six Flags Magic Mountain amusement park, located at 26101 Magic Mountain Parkway in Valencia, California, and rode the X2 roller coaster, after which he sustained a brain injury resulting in his death. (FAC ¶¶ 1, 24–32.) Plaintiffs allege that Defendant “owns, operates, manages, and/or controls Six Flags Magic Mountain.” (Id. at ¶ 9.)

 

On 8/30/23, Plaintiffs filed their complaint, alleging against seven named defendants the following causes of action: (1) Strict Liability – Design Defect; (2) Strict Liability – Failure to Warn; (3) Negligent Product Design; (4) Negligent Failure to Warn; (5) Negligence; and (6) Premises Liability. On 3/14/24, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint (“FAC”), alleging the same causes of action.

 

On 9/19/23, Plaintiffs served Defendant with their RFPs, Set One. (Ex. A to Decl. of Brian Priestley.) On 11/27/23, Defendant served its responses thereto. (Ex. E to Priestley Decl.) On 3/11/24, Defendant served further responses to the subject RFPs. (Exs. O, P to Priestley Decl.)

 

On 5/22/24, Plaintiffs filed the instant motions to compel Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiffs’ RFPs, Set One, Nos. 1, 3, 16–18, 20–21, 26–27, 29–37, 41, 45–46, 48–50, and 52–55. On 8/30/24, Defendant filed its opposition thereto. On 9/6/24, Plaintiffs filed their reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

California law requires a responding party to respond to each request for production of documents with either a statement of compliance, a representation that the party lacks the ability to comply, or an objection to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.210, subd. (a).) If the response includes an objection to the demand in part, it must (1) identify the particular document that falls within the category of the request to which the objection is being made, and (2) expressly set forth the extent of, and specific ground for, the objection. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240, subd. (b).)¿

 

A propounding party may move for an order compelling further response to a request for production if it decides that: “(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete; (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive; or (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (a).) The demanding party must take the initiative to obtain a judicial determination of the validity of any objection by moving to compel a further response, but the objecting party has the burden of justifying the objection. (See, e.g., Coy v. Superior Court of Contra Costa County (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220.)

 

A.    Meet and Confer 

 

Plaintiffs’ counsel declares that beginning on 12/21/23, counsel for the parties met and conferred on several occasions regarding Defendant’s responses to Plaintiffs’ RFPs and a stipulated protective order but were unable to informally resolve the issues. (Priestley Decl. ¶¶ 9–29.) Therefore, the Court finds that counsel has satisfied the preliminary meet and confer requirement under Code of Civil Procedure sections 2031.310, subdivision (b)(2).

 

B.     Timeliness

 

“A motion to compel further response … [must] be made within 45 days of the service of the response.” (Standon Co. v Superior Court (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 898, 902.) “Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (c) [emphasis added].)

 

Here, as a preliminary matter, Defendant argues that Plaintiffs have waived their right to bring the instant motion because the agreed-upon deadline to file the motion was 5/2/24, and the motion was filed on 5/22/24. (Def.’s Opp. 2:14–17, citing Ex. W to Priestley Decl.)

 

Plaintiffs assert in reply that “months-worth of meet and confer efforts between counsel for Plaintiffs and counsel for Magic Mountain, Mary Bevins, culminated in an April 11, 2024 express written agreement in which Ms. Bevins agreed to extend Plaintiffs’ deadline to file this Motion until 30 days after Magic Mountain produced certain additional documents that had already been promised in prior supplemental discovery responses. (Pls.’ Reply 4:12–16, citing Ex. X to Priestley Decl.)

 

Defendant argues that “Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(c) requires a specific date certain so that there is no ambiguity in whether the court has jurisdiction to rule upon the merits of a motion to compel further responses.” (Id. at 5:5–7.) Defendant further contends that “Plaintiffs’ position, if followed, it would lead to an absurd result, in that Plaintiffs would have been able to file a motion to compel whenever Magic Mountain produced documents (as opposed to supplemental responses).” (Id. at 5:10–12.)

 

Plaintiffs argue in reply that “it would be absurd to allow a party who agreed in-writing to an extension to renege on that agreement without merit or legal support.” (Pls.’ Reply 6:11–13.) “The law was designed to foster agreement between the parties, not restrict the means by which parties could reach such agreements. Indeed, the terms of the agreement reached in this case between counsel for Plaintiffs and Ms. Bevins were as specific as the circumstances permitted, given that the parties were making progress and moving towards a further production.” (Id. at 6:1–5.)

 

The Court agrees with Plaintiffs, and finds that Defendant’s counsel expressly agreed, in writing, to a reciprocal extension allowing Plaintiffs to file the instant motion to compel 30 days after Defendant produced additional documents. (Ex. X to Priestley Decl.) Here, counsel for the parties continued to meet and confer regarding the merits of the instant motion even after it was filed. (Priestley Reply Decl. ¶¶ 3–6.) Accordingly, the Court finds that the instant motion is not untimely.

C.    Defendant’s Responses

 

Plaintiffs seek to compel Defendant’s further responses to Plaintiffs’ RFPs, Set One, Nos. 1, 3, 16–18, 20–21, 26–27, 29–37, 41, 45–46, 48–50, and 52–55.

 

1.      RFP Nos. 1, 3, and 50

 

“RFP Nos. 1, 3, and 50 ask for all documents regarding the incident, Christopher Hawley, and Magic Mountain’s investigations of the incident, respectively.” (Pls.’ Mot. 11:9–10.) Here, Defendant’s responses raise both the attorney-client and attorney work product privileges. (Pls.’ Sep. Stmt.) However, Plaintiffs assert that Defendant has refused to produce a privilege log relating to these requests. (Pls.’ Mot. 11:20–23.)

 

“If an objection is based on a claim of privilege or a claim that the information sought is protected work product, the response shall provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of that claim, including, if necessary, a privilege log.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240, subd. (c)(1).) “The purpose of a ‘privilege log’ is to provide a specific factual description of documents in aid of substantiating a claim of privilege in connection with a request for document production. … The purpose of providing a specific factual description of documents is to permit a judicial evaluation of the claim of privilege.” (Hernandez v. Superior Court (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 285, 292.)

 

In its opposition, Defendant asserts that “documents related to attorney-client communications, attorney work-product, and consulting expert documents were the only items withheld from production” in response to the subject RFPs. (Def.’s Opp. 6:17–18.) However, as Plaintiffs note, “Plaintiffs have never suggested that Magic Mountain must produce privileged materials. Rather, Plaintiffs have steadfastly requested a privilege log so that they and the Court have enough information to evaluate the merits of Magic Mountain’s withholdings.” (Pls.’ Reply 6:27–7:1.)

 

The Court notes, as Plaintiffs also observe, that “instead of addressing the specific issues laid out by Plaintiffs in their moving papers, Magic Mountain spends the bulk of its Opposition describing the various documents that it has produced, while failing to offer any believable justification for its refusal to produce other relevant, responsive documents.” (Id. at 3:16–19.) As Defendant offers no explanation nor justification for its refusal to produce a privilege log, despite conceding that they withheld purportedly privileged documents from production, the Court finds that further responses to Plaintiffs’ RFP Nos. 1, 3, and 50 are warranted.

 

2.      RFP Nos. 20 and 37

 

“Request No. 20 asked Magic Mountain to produce documents reflecting its insurance policies that may cover the damages sought by Plaintiffs.” (Pls.’ Mot. 11:27–12:3.) “Request No. 37 asked Magic Mountain to produce documents pertaining to injuries that occurred on X2 from the date it opened to the public, May 24, 2008, to present.” (Id. at 12:4–6.) Plaintiffs argue that here, “for both requests, Magic Mountain improperly attempts to unilaterally limit scope without justification.” (Id. at 12:9–10.)

 

In response to RFP No. 20, Defendant limited its production to “the relevant declaration page in effect at the time of the incident.” (Pls.’ Sep Stmt. p. 19.) In response to RFP No. 37, Defendant “agreed to produce responsive documents only insofar as they concerned ‘complaints limited to head injury’ for X2 ‘from June 22, 2019 to June 23, 2022.’” (Pls.’ Mot. 12:6–8.) As Plaintiffs note, Defendant does not address this issue in its opposition. Accordingly, the Court finds that further responses to Plaintiffs’ RFP Nos. 20 and 37 are warranted.

 

3.      RFP Nos. 48–50

 

RFP Nos. 48–50 seek “documents regarding testing, inspections, and investigations of X2 and Christopher Hawley’s death conducted by or on behalf of Magic Mountain since June 23, 2022.” (Pls.’ Mot. 13:19–21.) Defendant objected to each subject RFP on the basis “that this request calls for the discovery of post-remedial measures.” (Pls.’ Sep. Stmt.)

 

“When, after the occurrence of an event, remedial or precautionary measures are taken, which, if taken previously, would have tended to make the event less likely to occur, evidence of such subsequent measures is inadmissible to prove negligence or culpable conduct in connection with the event.” (Evid. Code § 1151.) Here, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s Evidence Code section 1151 objections are inapplicable in this case, where strict liability and punitive damages are alleged. (Pls.’ Mot. 13:22–28, citing Ault v. International Harvester Co. (1974) 13 Cal.3d 113, 117; Hilliard v. A.H. Robbins (1983) 148 Cal.App.3d 374, 400–401.)

 

Here, as Plaintiffs observe, the “Opposition offers no substantive rebuttal to Plaintiffs’ Motion on this issue.” (Pls.’ Reply 9:1.) Accordingly, the Court agrees with Plaintiffs and finds that here, where strict liability and punitive damages are alleged against Defendant, Defendant’s Evidence Code section 1151 objections are without merit. Accordingly, further responses to Plaintiffs’ RFP Nos. 48–50 are warranted.

 

4.      RFP Nos. 16, 18, and 21

 

RFP Nos. 16 and 18 … seek documents concerning policies and procedures in place at the park, and RFP No. 21 … seeks documents concerning the design of X2.” (Pls.’ Mot. 14:10–12.) Here, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant’s responses to the subject RFPs are deficient because the statements of compliance therein are incomplete and fail to state whether Defendant is in possession of all responsive documents. (Id. at 14:13–24; Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.220.) “Specifically, Magic Mountain does not indicate whether its purported compliance for each request is complete, nor does it attest to the sufficiency of its search for responsive documents.” (Id. at 14:13–14.)

 

While Defendant argues in reply that “based on Magic Mountain’s reasonable and diligent search, no other responsive documents exist in Magic Mountain’s possession, custody, or control,” the Court finds no such representation stated in either Defendant’s initial or supplemental responses to the subject RFPs. (Def.’s Opp. 7:11–13.) Accordingly, the Court finds that further code-compliant responses to Plaintiffs’ RFP Nos. 16, 18, and 21 are warranted.

 

5.      RFP Nos. 30–32, 41, 45–46, 48–49, 52, and 55

 

Plaintiffs further argue that Defendant’s boilerplate objections made in response to RFP Nos. 30–32, 41, 45–46, 48–49, 52, and 55 are without merit. “The requests at issue seek the following documents that are plainly relevant to Plaintiffs’ claims in this case … : testing operational modifications made to X2 [RFP No. 30]; complaints Magic Mountain received or was otherwise made aware of regarding the operation and safety of X2 [RFP Nos. 31-32]; documents identifying employees responsible for advertising/promoting the park [RFP No. 41]; documents relating to spine, neck, head, or brain injuries sustained by passengers of Fourth Dimension roller coasters, including X2 [RFP Nos. 45-46]; testing and inspections of X2 undertaken by or on behalf of Magic Mountain since the date of the incident [RFP Nos. 48-49]; social media posts regarding X2 posted by or on behalf of Magic Mountain since the date of the incident [RFP No. 52]; and downloads or print-outs from the programmable logic controller of X2 on the day of the incident [RFP No. 55].” (Pls.’ Mot. 15:6–15.)

 

Plaintiffs assert that the subject RFPs are relevant because “Plaintiffs in this case claim Christopher Hawley died as a result of riding the X2 roller coaster, a ride that is defectively designed and that failed to warn potential passengers of the severe risks it posed, risks which were known to and not acted upon by Magic Mountain.” (Pls.’ Mot. 16:14–17.)

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that RFP Nos. 30–32, as drafted, are overbroad because they “seek any complaints regarding the X2, in any manner, over a 16 year time period. The requests are not tailored [to] the complaints related to guests claiming head or brain injuries, after riding. As drafted, the requests seek discovery regarding all customer guest relations complaint[s], any incidents related to guests in the queue lines who never rode the ride, and any litigation complaints.” (Def.’s Opp. 13:25–14:4.) However, upon review of RFP Nos. 31 and 32, the Court finds that the requests seek only those complaints regarding either the operation or safety of X2 from 12/2/07 to present. Accordingly, the Court finds that RFP Nos. 30–32 are sufficiently tailored to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.

 

Defendant further argues that RFP No. 49 is overbroad as written, seeking “all DOCUMENTS concerning inspections, if any, undertaken by YOU or on YOUR behalf since the INCIDENT.” (Pls.’ Sep. Stmt. p. 56.) As Defendant observes, “Plaintiffs’ request is not limited to the X2 roller coaster, or any claims raised by Plaintiffs in this case.” (Def.’s Opp. 14:7–8.) Accordingly, the Court finds that RFP No. 49 is overbroad, and narrows the scope of the request to the X2 roller coaster.

 

Finally, Defendant argues that RFP Nos. 41 and 52 seek irrelevant documents because “there is no evidence that any advertisements, social media posts, or marketing influenced Christopher Hawley’s decision to ride the X2, in any manner.” (Id. at 15:2–3.) “Moreover, the names and contact information for employees may be obtain[ed] through interrogatories. Yet, Plaintiffs’ request, as drafted, seeks the entire employees’ personnel file.” (Id. at 15:7–16:8.) The Court agrees with Defendant to the extent that the identities of employees responsible for advertising and promotion of the amusement park may be obtained through interrogatories. Accordingly, the Court finds that further responses to RFP No. 41 are not warranted. While the Court notes that Plaintiffs do not address Defendant’s scope/relevance objections on reply, the Court finds that RFP No. 52 seeks documents relating to post-remedial measures, which, as earlier discussed, are relevant and permissible in the instant action. Accordingly, the Court finds that further responses to RFP No. 52 are warranted.

6.      RFP Nos. 26–27, 33–36, and 53–54

 

Plaintiffs assert that “in response to requests (a) seeking documents concerning the operational approval of X2 and its predecessor, X (RFP Nos. 26-27), (b) notices of violation and/or citations received from any governmental entity regarding the operation or safety of X2 (RFP Nos. 33-36), and (c) communications between Magic Mountain and Cal-OSHA regarding Mr. Hawley’s incident and prior related injuries sustained by passengers of X2 (RFP Nos. 53-54), Magic Mountain refuses to comply, claiming the documents are ‘public records and are equally available’ to Plaintiffs.” (Pls.’ Mot. 18:1–7.) Plaintiffs argue that “this is not an appropriate objection nor basis for Magic Mountain’s refusal to comply. Just because a document may or may not be available by way of a FOIA request does not make it ‘equally available’ for purposes of discovery, nor does it shield Magic Mountain from complying with its duty to produce responsive documents within its own possession, custody, or control.” (Id. at 18:9–13.)

 

The Court notes that Defendant does not address this issue in its opposition. Instead, Defendant contends that it produced all responsive documents in its possession, custody and control in response to the subject RFPs. However, as stated above, the Court finds no such representation stated in either Defendant’s initial or supplemental responses to the subject RFPs. Accordingly, the Court finds that further responses to RFP Nos. 26–27, 33–36, and 53–54 are warranted.

7.      Email Communications

 

Finally, Plaintiffs argue that “the most pervasive issue in Magic Mountain’s responses is that it has not produced a single email or other communication between its employees, between it and Six Flags, or between it and third-parties, in response to any of the Requests identified in this Motion.” (Pls.’ Mot. 19:1–4.) “Magic Mountain’s failure to produce a single email—despite Plaintiffs’ ability to prove responsive emails exist—renders its responses incomplete, and calls into question Magic Mountain’s representation that it conducted a diligent search and reasonable inquiry.” (Id. at 19:20–22.)

 

In opposition, Defendant argues that it “has produced all emails, other than emails containing work product or attorney client-communications.” (Def.’s Opp. 11:13–14.) “Plaintiffs’ speculation that additional emails should exist are negated by Magic Mountain’s responses that indicate Defendant Responding Party has produced all known responsive documents other than privileged attorney-client communications, attorney work-product, and consulting expert work-product documents.” (Def.’s Opp. 11:19–22.) However, the Court observes that this representation is made only in Defendant’s responses to RFP Nos. 1, 3, and 50. Furthermore, as set forth above, the Court finds that Defendant has failed to provide a privilege log setting forth the basis of the privileges asserted by Defendant with respect to the withheld emails. Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that to the extent that Defendant intends to withhold any responsive documents on the basis of any privilege, Defendant is ordered to produce a privilege log providing further details.

 

D.    Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction … against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (h).) Additionally, “the court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030, subd. (a).)

 

Here, in granting the instant motions, the Court finds it reasonable to impose $500.00 in monetary sanctions against Defendant. The Court notes Plaintiffs’ contention that Defendant produced additional documents on 6/20/24, but that the additional document production remains insufficient to resolve the issues raised in the instant motion. (Pls.’ Reply 10:12–13.)

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted in part. Defendant ordered to provide further code-compliant responses and a privilege log, as set forth above, to RFP Nos. Nos. 1, 3, 16–18, 20–21, 26–27, 29–37, 45–46, 48–50, and 52–55 within 30 days. The Court imposes monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $500.00, payable within 45 days.


Case Number: 24CHCV02068    Hearing Date: September 13, 2024    Dept: F51

SEPTEMBER 12, 2024

 

DEMURRER

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 24CHCV02068

 

Demurrer filed: 8/16/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendants Alfredo Ayala; and Elizabeth Grijalva, in pro per (collectively, “Defendants”)

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Crystal Arellano, in pro per (“Plaintiff”)

NOTICE: OK 

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: Defendants demur to Plaintiff’s entire complaint.


TENTATIVE RULING: The unopposed demurrer is overruled. Defendants to file their answer to Plaintiff’s complaint within 5 days.

 

BACKGROUND 

 

This is an unlawful detainer action in which Plaintiff alleges that in January 2020, she entered into a written lease agreement with Defendants, whereby Defendant agreed to pay Plaintiff $2,500.00 in monthly rent for the leased premises located at 13739 Louvre Street, Pacoima, CA 91331. (Compl. ¶¶ 3a, 6.) On 5/6/24, Plaintiff served Defendants with a 3-day notice to pay rent or quit, at which time Defendants allegedly owed Plaintiff $45,375.00 in unpaid rent. (Id. at ¶¶ 9a, 12.)

 

On 6/4/24, Plaintiff filed her complaint. On 8/16/24, Defendants filed the instant demurrer. No opposition has been filed to date.

 

ANALYSIS

 

As a general matter, a party may respond to a pleading against it by demurrer on the basis of any single or combination of eight enumerated grounds, including that “the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)

 

“A demurrer tests the pleading alone, and not the evidence or facts alleged.” (E-Fab, Inc. v. Accountants, Inc. Servs. (2007) 153 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1315.) As such, the court assumes the truth of the complaint’s properly pleaded or implied factual allegations. (Ibid.) The only issue a demurrer is concerned with is whether the complaint, as it stands, states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Here, Defendants demur to the entire complaint on the basis that it is not verified as required under Code of Civil Procedure section 1166, subdivision (a)(1).

 

A.    Meet-and-Confer

 

Before filing its demurrer, “the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41, subd. (a).) The demurring party must file and serve a meet and confer declaration stating either: “(A) The means by which the demurring party met and conferred with the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer, and that the parties did not reach an agreement resolving the objections raised in the demurrer;” or “(B) That the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer failed to respond to the meet and confer request of the demurring party or otherwise failed to meet and confer in good faith.” (Id. at subd. (a)(3).)

 

Here, Defendants make no showing that they attempted to meet and confer with Plaintiff to informally resolve the issues raised in the demurrer. However, “a determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41, subd. (a)(4).)

 

B.     Verification

 

Here, Defendants argue that Plaintiff has not verified her complaint, in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 1166, subdivision (a)(1). (Dem. 5:13.) A complaint for unlawful detainer must “be verified and include the typed or printed name of the person verifying the complaint.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1166, subd. (a)(1).)

 

While Plaintiff has not filed any opposition to the instant motion, the Court notes, upon review of the complaint, that Plaintiff has signed a verification on page 4 of the complaint. Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff has satisfied the verification requirement set forth by Code of Civil Procedure section 1166, subdivision (a)(1). Accordingly, and as Defendants do not raise any other issue on demurrer, the unopposed demurrer is overruled.

 

CONCLUSION 

 

The unopposed demurrer is overruled. Defendants to file their answer to Plaintiff’s complaint within 5 days.
