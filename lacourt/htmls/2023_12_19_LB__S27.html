Case Number: 22LBUD00619    Hearing Date: December 19, 2023    Dept: S27

Plaintiff filed this unlawful detainer action on 5/05/22.  After much litigation, Defendant vacated the premises on 9/24/23.  Because Defendant vacated the premises, the Court took the trial re: possession off calendar on 9/25/23. 

 

At this time, Plaintiff moves to amend the complaint to state a claim for monetary damages as an unlimited civil, rather than unlawful detainer, action.  The motion is premised on CCP §§1952.3, 472, 473, and 464, which collectively permit the relief sought. 

 

Any opposition to the motion was due on or before 12/06/23.  The Court has not received timely opposition to the motion, and will not consider untimely opposition papers.  The motion is granted.  Plaintiff is ordered to file a separate copy of the amended complaint within ten days.  Defendant is ordered to file a responsive pleading within thirty days thereafter.

 

The parties are reminded that there is a Case Management Conference on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the above motion.  The Court asks the parties to make arrangements to appear remotely at the CMC and hearing on the motion. 


Case Number: 23LBCV00221    Hearing Date: December 19, 2023    Dept: S27

Plaintiff, Christina Alexandra Arnao filed this action against Defendant, Mercedes-Benz, USA for Violation of the Song-Beverly Act.  Plaintiff filed her complaint on 2/06/23. 

 

On 8/09/23, the parties appeared at a Case Management Conference.  At the hearing, the Court specially set a hearing on a motion to compel arbitration for 12/19/23.  Moving papers, per CCP §1005, were due on or before 11/27/23. 

 

For reasons that are not clear, Defendant reserved a new hearing date on a motion to compel arbitration for 7/09/24 and filed moving papers on 12/05/23, after moving papers were due in order for the motion to be timely heard on 11/27/23.  The Court presumes this was done, at least in part, because the California Supreme Court has granted review of the Court of Appeals’ ruling in Ochoa v. Ford Motor Company (2023) 89 Cal.App.5th 1324. 

 

The Court will not rule on the motion to compel arbitration at this time, as it does not appear the parties intend for the specially scheduled hearing to go forward.  There is a Case Management Conference also on calendar today.  At the CMC, the Court will discuss a new hearing date for the motion to compel arbitration with the parties. The Court will not permit Defendant to delay this action for another seven months. 

 

The parties are reminded that there is a CMC on calendar concurrently with the hearing on the motion to compel arbitration.  The Court asks the parties to make arrangements to appear remotely at the conference.


Case Number: 23LBCV00483    Hearing Date: December 19, 2023    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Marco-Joseph Valdez filed this action against Defendants, Dean C. Logan and Does 1-50 on 3/21/23.  The crux of the complaint is that Logan and Doe Defendants refused to accept a mechanic’s lien for recording, which caused him damages.  The complaint includes causes of action for knowingly concealing verified evidence from being produced upon an inquiry authorized by law based on Penal Code §135 (first and second causes of action), and practicing fraud and deceit against my testimony as a witness in the public court of record based on Penal Code §133 (third cause of action); the complaint seeks declaratory and injunctive relief based on the above alleged violations, and also seeks compensatory and punitive damages.    

 

  1. Demurrer to Complaint

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP § 430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP § 430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP § 430.41(a)(3).)

 

b.     Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he called Plaintiff and left a detailed message about the nature of his case, which was to fulfill the meet and confer requirements, but Plaintiff did not return the call.  Defendant therefore satisfied the meet and confer requirement, and the Court will rule on the demurrer on its merits. 

 

c.     Grounds for Demurrer

Defendant argues the complaint is subject to demurrer on numerous grounds, each of which will be discussed below. 

 

d.     Opposition

Opposition to the demurrer was due on or before 12/06/23.  The Court has not received timely opposition to the demurrer, and will not consider late-filed opposition to the demurrer. 

 

e.     Government Tort Claims Act

Generally, no suit for money or damages may be brought against a government entity (or against a government employee acting in the scope of employment) unless and until a timely claim has been presented pursuant to the Government Claims Act (Gov.C. § 810 et seq.) and either acted upon or deemed rejected by the passage of time. Gov.C. §§ 945.4, 950.2, 912.4; see DiCampli-Mintz v. County of Santa Clara (2012) 55 Cal.4th 983, 989-990; Le Mere v. Los Angeles Unified School Dist. (2019) 35 Cal.App.5th 237, 246.

 

Compliance with the claim filing requirement is an essential element of a damages cause of action against a government entity. Consequently, the complaint must contain at least a general allegation that plaintiff timely complied. Otherwise, the complaint is subject to general demurrer for failure to state a cause of action.  Rubenstein v. Doe No. 1 (2017) 3 Cal.5th 903, 906, 221 CR3d 761, 762; Willis v. City of Carlsbad (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 1104, 1119.

 

Plaintiff alleges, at ¶1 of his complaint, that Defendant was performing “official duties at the county clerk of Los Angeles county (sic)” when the acts complained of occurred.  Plaintiff failed, however, to allege compliance with the Government Tort Claims Act.  The demurrer on this ground is therefore sustained.

 

f.      Statutory Basis for Claims

Defendant correctly notes that public entities can only be held liable for statutory violations.  Gov Code §815.  Defendant contends this also applies to public employees, citing §815.2.  §815.2, however, clarifies whether and when a public entity is vicariously liable for the tort of its employee.  Defendant failed, therefore, to show that a claim against a public employee must have a statutory basis.

 

g.     Exercise of Discretion

Defendant next argues he cannot be held liable for the acts complained of because they were acts of discretion, for which he is not liable per §820.2.  Defendant goes on to argue that §27201(a) permits him to refuse to record a document if the document violates recording requirements, if fees are not paid, or if critical information is not provided.  Defendant contends the determination of whether these requirements are met is a discretionary one, and therefore he is immune from liability.  Defendant fails to cite any authority for the position that the determination under §27201(a) is a discretionary one.  Absent such authority, the demurrer on this ground is overruled.

 

h.     Inapplicable Statutes

Defendant next argues the statutes that form the basis of Plaintiff’s two causes of action, Penal Code §§133 and 135, do not apply to the facts of the case. 

 

Penal Code §133 provides, “Every person who practices any fraud or deceit, or knowingly makes or exhibits any false statement, representation, token, or writing, to any witness or person about to be called as a witness upon any trial, proceeding, inquiry, or investigation whatever, authorized by law, with intent to affect the testimony of such witness, is guilty of a misdemeanor.”

 

§135 provides, “A person who, knowing that any book, paper, record, instrument in writing, digital image, video recording owned by another, or other matter or thing, is about to be produced in evidence upon a trial, inquiry, or investigation, authorized by law, willfully destroys, erases, or conceals the same, with the intent to prevent it or its content from being produced, is guilty of a misdemeanor.”

 

Defendant correctly notes that Plaintiff fails to allege his document was intended for use in a trial, proceeding, inquiry, or investigation, or that it was about to be produced in evidence in a trial, inquiry, or investigation.  The demurrer on this ground is therefore also sustained.

 

i.      Injunctive Relief

Defendant argues the necessary showing to entitle a person to injunctive relief is not made.  Defendant is largely arguing the standard to be applied at trial, not the standard on demurrer.  Regardless, however, the injunctive relief claim is premised on the underlying Penal Code violation claims, both of which fail, and therefore the injunctive relief claim fails along with the substantive claims.

 

j.      Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer is sustained on the grounds set forth above. 

 

k.     Leave to Amend

It is not up to the Court to figure out how the complaint can be amended to state a cause of action.  Rather, the burden is on the plaintiff to show in what manner he or she can amend the complaint, and how that amendment will change the legal effect of the pleading.  Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349; Hendy v. Losse (1991) 54 Cal.3d 723, 742.

 

A plaintiff may not amend as a matter of right, and in order to be in a position to complain upon appeal of an abuse of discretion in sustaining a demurrer without leave to amend, he must ask permission to amend, unless it is made to appear that he had no opportunity to ask such permission.  Williams v. Hinckley (1930) 109 Cal.App. 574, 576.

 

In light of the lack of opposition, leave to amend is denied.

Case Number: 23LBCV01215    Hearing Date: December 19, 2023    Dept: S27

  1. Background Facts

Plaintiff, Howard Schneider filed this action against Defendant, Tina Marie Johnson for damages arising out of an automobile vs. bicycle collision.      

 

  1. Motion to Compel Further Responses

a.     Discovery Dispute

Plaintiff propounded FROGs on Defendant.  Defendant responded. The parties met and conferred, which resulted in amended responses.  Despite additional attempts to meet and confer, the parties were unable to resolve issues relating to FROGs 12.4, 15.1, and 17.1.   Plaintiff therefore moves to compel further responses to these FROGs.

 

b.     12.4

Defendant indicates, in opposition to the motion, that she concedes her response is deficient and she will supplement her response.  The motion to compel is therefore moot as to FROG 12.4.

 

c.     15.1

FROG 15.1 asks Defendant to provide information to support each special or affirmative defense asserted in the answer.  Defendant consistently states that she was driving slowly and carefully at the time of the accident, that the parties to the action and the individual listed in the collision report are the witnesses to support her defense, and that the traffic collision report supports her defense.  Additionally, with respect to some of the defenses, she states that she has no information, witnesses, or documents to support her defense, but that discovery is ongoing and she will supplement the responses as such information becomes available. 

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses, citing Liberty Mutual Fire Ins. Co. v. LcL Administrators, Inc. (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 1093 in support of his position.  In Liberty Mutual, the defendant, in its answer and cross-complaint, accused the plaintiff insurance company of various fraudulent and improper practices.  Despite repeated court orders requiring the defendant to provide further responses to FROGs 15.1 and 17.1, the defendant never provided any substantive information to support its contentions.  The trial court ultimately struck its answer and cross-complaint, and the Court of Appeals affirmed. 

 

The Liberty Mutual situation was distinct from the instant situation in two respects.  First, while the allegations at issue in Liberty Mutual were objective in nature (the insurance company either did or did not engage in practices that were illegal or fraudulent), automobile accidents have a subjective component that cannot be reduced to specific identifiable facts, witnesses, and evidence.  Second, in Liberty Mutual, the trial court specifically found that the defendant had conducted sufficient discovery and had sufficient documents and other evidence in its possession to provide responses to the FROGs, but continued to refuse to do so.  Conversely, in our case, Defendant has not yet conducted Plaintiff’s deposition or IME, which are the most likely ways Defendant will learn of information to support her affirmative defenses. 

 

The motion to compel a further response is denied.  Defendant has provided all information she currently has in her possession.  Plaintiff is free to propound supplemental interrogatories, and/or to take any legally available action in the future to preclude Defendant from introducing facts, witnesses, or evidence not identified in discovery.  He has not, however, shown that the responses are deficient at this time, and the Court will not order supplemental responses.

 

d.         17.1

Similarly, FROG 17.1 seeks information to support Defendant’s denials of various RFAs.  The RFAs collectively seek an admission that Plaintiff did nothing to contribute to the accident and Defendant was solely at fault for the accident.  Defendant has stated, in response to each FROG, that she was driving slowly and carefully at the time of the accident, and that she has no other facts, witnesses, or evidence available at this time to support her denials. 

 

Again, Plaintiff is free to use this discovery in any legally permissible manner in connection with the litigation, but he has not shown the further responses are necessary under the circumstances.  The motion is denied.
