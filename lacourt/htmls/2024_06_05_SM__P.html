DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20SMCV01557    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Mardirossian v. Howarth, Case No. 20SMCV01557

Hearing date June 5, 2024

Defendant Howarth’s Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication

 

Mardirossian’s First Amended Complaint alleges: (1) nuisance; (2) trespass; and (3) battery against his neighbor Howarth, who moves for summary judgment/adjudication.

 

Evidentiary Objections

Mardirossian’s objections numbers 4, 5, and 11-13 to the Howarth declaration are OVERRULED. Mardirossian’s objections 1-2 SUSTAINED (relevance); objections 3, 6-9 SUSTAINED (hearsay); objection 10 SUSTAINED (improper legal opinion); objection 14-15 (lack of authentication). Mardirossian’s objections to the supplemental declaration submitted with the reply brief are OVERRULED.

 

Defects in Mardirossian’s Separate Statement

Howarth argues Mardirossian’s separate statement is defective because he does not respond to the material facts stated by Howarth. Mardirossian did not fail to respond to the actual material facts. As to trespass (facts 15-25), Mardirossian failed to dispute or indicates a fact is disputed but does not cite evidence. The Court will deem Howarth’s material facts 15-25 undisputed.

 

Pertinent Allegations of the FAC

Mardirossian alleges: On Howarth’s adjacent property, there are trees, brush, bushes, shrubs, and landscaping, which is unreasonably intrude onto Mardirossian’s property and unreasonably interferes with the use or enjoyment of his property. FAC, ¶ 8. Excessive leaves and plant debris cause increased risk of harm, including possible falling branches and increased spreading of wildfire. FAC, ¶ 8. A portion of Howarth’s fencing, located on the west side of Mardirossian’s property, extends beyond the legal boundaries of Howarth’s property and encroaches onto Mardirossian’s property. FAC, ¶ 8.

 

First Cause of Action for Nuisance

Mardirossian alleges Howarth “created a dangerous condition of overgrown trees, brush, bushes, shrubs, and landscaping causing, among other things, intruding branches and plant growth onto [Mardirossian’s] property, excessive accumulation of leaves and debris on [Mardirossian's] property, and the potentially dangerous conditions of falling branches and spreading of wildfire onto [Mardirossian] property.” FAC, ¶ 11.

 

To state a cause of action for nuisance, plaintiff “must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of its property. Second, the invasion of the plaintiff’s interest in the use and enjoyment of the land must be substantial, i.e., it caused the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage. Third, the interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, it must also be unreasonable, i.e., it must be of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.” Today’s IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1176.

 

“A de minimus interference with the enjoyment of land does not constitute a nuisance.” Smith v. David (1981) 120 Cal.App.3d 101, 114. Thus, to succeed on a nuisance cause of action, a plaintiff must show that an invasion was substantial. San Diego Gas & Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1996) 13 Cal.4th 893, 938. Additionally, to constitute a nuisance, “[t]he interference with the protected interest . . . must also be unreasonable.” Id.

 

“Trees whose branches extend over the land of another are not nuisances, except to the extent to which the branches overhang the adjoining land. To the extent they are nuisances, the person over whose land they extend may cut them off or have his action for damages . . . .” Crance v. Hems (1936) 17 Cal.App.2d 450, 451-52. “[I]t has been held that no landowner has a cause of action from the mere fact that the branches of an innoxious tree, belonging to an adjoining landowner, overhanging his premises, his right to cutoff the overhanging branches being considered a sufficient remedy.” Bonde v. Bishop (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 1, 6.

 

As to the fear of a future injury, a private nuisance claim is not sufficient where it based “solely [on] the fear of a future injury.” Koll-Irvine Center Property Owners Ass’n. v. County of Orange (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1041-42.

 

Howarth’s Evidence

Mardirossian could not identify a date or photograph when a branch from a tree fell onto his property. HSF 11. Mardirossian produced two photographs showing leaves on his property. HSF 3; Begland Decl., Exh. 2. The leaves have not stopped him from using the tennis court. HSF 6; Begland Decl., Ex. 1 at p. 210:13-21, 225:5-226:7; HSF 7; Begland Decl., Ex. 1 at p. 226:2-7. There have only been one or two instances where Mardirossian has not had time to blow the leaves off the tennis court and was unable to use the tennis court. HSF 8; Begland Decl., Ex. 1 at p. 227:4-10. Mardirossian could not identify facts supporting the allegation of increased risk of fire but testified overgrown trees “could be fuel to a fire.” HSF 13; Begland Decl., Ex. 1 at p. 151:24-152:15. Howarth “continued to have the trees in [the] area that [he] own[s] or control trimmed back regularly.” Howarth Decl., ¶ 17.

 

Mardirossian’s Evidence

Mardirossian produced photographs of the trees and conditions. Feit Decl., ¶ 7; Ex. 6. After a nearby 2019 fire, Mardirossian sent a letter to neighbors encouraging them to take additional fire protection measures. Mardirossian Separate Statement of Additional Material Facts (MAMF) 1-3; Feit Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 39:10-16. The fire came within 300 feet of Howarth’s residence. MAMF 2; Feit Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 40:5-7. Mardirossian informed neighbors of the need for fire prevention measures, such as cutting foliage. MAMF 3; Feit Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 40:18-42:10, 248:6-16.

 

Howarth has an obligation to trim branches which create the risk of falling or risk of fire. MAMF 7; Feit Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 111:2-17, 112:4-8, 112:22, 112:25-114:4, 115:4-15. Howarth’s trees are overgrown, and Mardirossian cleaned his backyard and tennis court due to the leaves. MAMF 10; Feit Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 242:18-243:8, 243:18-22. Mardirossian stated the leaves interfere with his use and enjoyment of the property. MAMF 10; Feit Decl., Ex. 4 at p. 243:18-22.

 

Analysis Re: Nuisance

Howarth argues falling leaves and overhanging branches are not actionable bases for a nuisance claim, relying on Bonde v. Bishop (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 1 for the proposition that “a landowner has no right of action against a neighbor for leaves falling from the neighbor’s tree(s) on to the landowner’s property, because the plaintiff has a right to time the branches that overhang his property to the property line.” The portion of Bonde, supra, 112 Cal.App.2d 1 cited states “no landowner has a cause of action from the mere fact that the branches of an innoxious tree, belonging to an adjoining landowner, overhang his premises, his right to cut off the overhanging branches being considered a sufficient remedy” was not the holding of the case and is dicta. Bonde, supra, 112 Cal.App.2d 1, 6.

 

In Bonde, defendants’ tree limb fell, damaging plaintiffs’ garage and fence, and branches dropped onto the roof, driveway and patio. Bonde, supra, 112 Cal.App.2d 1, 3. Plaintiffs were required to clean debris. Bonde, supra, 112 Cal.App.2d 1, 3. Bonde found the branches were a nuisance because “[t]he fact that an overhanging branch did fall, the age of the oak tree, the evidence of some decay, indicates that there is some danger of the overhanging limbs falling.” Bonde, supra, 112 Cal.App.2d 1, 4.

 

Bonde does not state a landowner lacks a right of action against a neighbor for falling leaves or overhanging branches. The issue was not falling leaves, but falling tree limbs and branches. Howarth’s reliance on Crance v. Hems (1936) 17 Cal.App.2d 450 is also inapposite, as that case concerned the growth of tree roots across a boundary line. Crance v. Hems, supra, 17 Cal.App.2d 450, 451.

 

Mardirossian’s citation to Clary v. City of Crescent City (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 274 is inapposite, as that case involved a public nuisance and concerned overgrown weeds and rubbish on vacant lots. Clary, supra, 11 Cal.App.5th 274, 277. Mardirossian’s reliance on Thain v. City of Palo Alto (1962) 207 Cal.App.2d 173, 177 is also inapposite, as that case challenged a municipal weed abatement ordinance.  

 

Fear of “potentially dangerous conditions of falling branches and spreading of a wildfire onto [Mardirossian’s] property” does not constitute an actionable nuisance. FAC, ¶ 11. The fact that a wildfire may occur or that a branch may fall is not an actionable basis for a nuisance cause of action. Koll-Irvine, supra, 24 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1041-42. Unlike plaintiffs in Bonde, Mardirossian presented no evidence that a tree branch has fallen on his property. The fact that overhanging branches are present, without more, does not rise to the level of a nuisance.

 

As to the risk of fire, Mardirossian presented no substantial evidence, such as an expert declaration, to establish the branches and leaves create the potentially dangerous condition of the spreading of a wildfire (or increase the risk beyond what the non-overhanging foliage presents). Nor is fear of increased fire damage actionable nuisance.

 

Falling leaves, however, are actionable as a nuisance. Mardirossian presented evidence the leaves fell on his property, forcing him to clean his tennis court. Mardirossian testified the accumulating leaves interfere with his use and enjoyment of his property. Mardirossian employed a gardener to clean the leaves from his tennis court. Mardirossian presented evidence that the leaves come from a tree on Howarth’s property. There are triable issues of fact as to whether the falling leaves constitute a nuisance. Denied as to the nuisance cause of action.

 

Second Cause of Action for Trespass

Mardirossian alleges Howarth “caused the entry onto [Mardirossian’s] property of overgrown tree branches, brush, bushes, shrubs, other similar landscape features, and excessive accumulation of leaves and debris, without Plaintiff’s consent or permission. FAC, ¶ 13. Mardirossian alleges “it was recently learned that a portion of [Howarth’s] fencing, located on the westerly side of [Mardirossian’s] property, extends beyond the legal boundaries of [Howarth’s] property, and trespasses and encroaches onto [Mardirossian's] property, without the consent of [Mardirossian].” FAC, ¶ 13.

 

Howarth argues the trespass claim fails because overhanging branches and falling leaves do not constitute trespass and, with respect to the allegedly encroaching fence, Howarth lacks the required intent, since he did not install the fence. Apparently, also, Howarth recently moved the fence.

 

“The elements of trespass are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or control of the property; (2) the defendant’s intentional, reckless, or negligent entry onto the property; (3) lack of permission for the entry or acts in excess of permission; (4) harm; and (5) the defendant’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing the harm.” Ralphs Grocery Co. v. Victory Consultants, Inc. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 245, 262. “The essence of the cause of action for trespass is the ‘unauthorized entry’ onto the land of another. Such invasions are characterized as intentional torts, regardless of the actor’s motivation.” Miller v. National Broadcasting Co. (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 1463, 1480. “[T]he intent required as a basis for liability as a trespasser is simply an intent to be at the place on the land where the trespass allegedly occurred.” Id. “Ratification is the voluntary election by a person to adopt in some manner as his own an act which was purportedly done on his behalf by another person, the effect of which, as to some or all persons, is to treat the act as if originally authorized by him.” Rakestraw v. Rodriguez (1972) 8 Cal.3d 67, 73. A plaintiff alleging a trespass cause of action must show interference with his exclusive possessory rights. McBride v. Smith (2018) 18 Cal.App.5th 1160, 1174.

 

Howarth’s Evidence

The fence was constructed before Howarth purchased the property in 1996; it is unknown who constructed it. HSF 20-21. There was a 2000 lot line adjustment for the boundary line on the west side of Mardirossian’s property. HSF 22-23. Mardirossian testified that, at the time he purchased the property, neither Howarth nor any other person gave him any assurance that the Safety Fence is on the property boundary between the Howarth Property and the Mardirossian Property. HSF 24. Mardirossian testified he assumed the fence was on the property boundary but did not obtain a survey. HSF 25. The Fence was moved off the Mardirossian Property at Howarth’s expense. Howarth Supp. Decl., ¶¶ 6-7; Ex. 1-2.

 

Mardirossian’s Evidence

Howarth’s survey shows a portion Howarth’s wrought iron fence encroaches onto his property. MAMF 14-15; Feit Decl., Ex. 2, Lai Decl. Howarth admitted the fencing encroaches onto and prevents Mardirossian from accessing some trees. MAMF 16-18; Feit Decl., Ex. 3 at p. 116:21-117:11, 118:8-16, 164:1-9, 166:4-8. Mardirossian granted Howarth time to relocate the fencing to avoid litigating the issue. MAMF 20-23; Feit Decl., ¶ 6, Ex. 5.

 

Analysis as to Trespass

There is a triable issue of material fact as to whether the presence of falling leaves constituting a trespass. There is evidence the trees are on Howarth’s property and interfere with Mardirossian’s use and enjoyment of the tennis court. As to overgrown branches, Mardirossian presented no evidence the presence of overhanging branches interfered with his possessory rights.

 

Concerning the fence, Howarth presented evidence in reply that the has been moved from the Mardirossian Property. Based on this, it appears the issue regarding the fencing is moot. Given that there is still an issue of triable fact as to falling leaves, summary adjudication is denied.

 

Third Cause of Action for Negligence

The third cause of action for negligence is premised on “[Howarth’s] fencing [trespassing] onto [Mardirossian’s] property, overgrown tree branches, brush, bushes, shrubs, other similar landscape features, intruding branches and plant growth onto [Mardirossian’s] property, excessive accumulation of leaves and debris on [Mardirossian’s] property, and the potentially dangerous conditions and unreasonable increase of harm from falling branches and spreading of wild fire onto [Mardirossian’s] property. FAC, ¶ 16. “The elements of any negligence cause of action are duty, breach of duty, proximate cause, and damages.” Peredia v. HR Mobile Services, Inc. (2018) 25 Cal.App.5th 680, 687. The fear of harm from falling branches and the spreading of wildfire, without more, does not give rise to a negligence claim under Koll-Irvine, supra, 24 Cal.App.4th 1036, 1041-42.

 

Howarth argues he has no duty to prevent branches overhanging another’s property. Howarth’s citation to Bonde, supra, 112 Cal.App.2d 1 is inapposite, as that case did not address a negligence cause of action or state a rule of law that a landowner has no duty to prevent branches from overhanging onto another’s property. Howarth argues he has no duty to prevent leaves from falling onto Mardirossian’s property but does not present legal authority to support such proposition.

 

Howarth argues the falling leaves have not caused actionable damages, citing Fields v. Napa Mill Co. (1958) 164 Cal.App.2d 442, 447 for the proposition that “a negligent act is not actionable unless it results in injury to another.” “Nominal damages, to vindicate a technical right, cannot be recovered in a negligence action, where no actual loss has occurred.” Fields, supra, 164 Cal.App.2d at 448. Mardirossian presented evidence falling leaves caused him and his gardener to spend time and effort cleaning the court. There is an issue as to negligence, preventing the court from granting the motion for summary adjudication. Denied.

 

Motion DENIED.


Case Number: 22SMCV02853    Hearing Date: June 5, 2024    Dept: P

Tentative Ruling

Hsieh v. Augustus, et al., Case No. 22SMCV02853

Hearing date June 5, 2024

Defendants’ Motion to Vacate Judgment

Plaintiff Hsieh filed this unlawful detainer action against defendants. On July 28, 2023 the parties entered into a stipulated judgment, wherein Hsieh would dismiss the action in exchange for defendants’ payment of past rent, holdover damages and costs. Upon receipt of the settlement, the court dismissed the action and retained jurisdiction pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure §664.6.

Because the Augustuses failed to abide by the terms of this settlement, Hsieh moved to enforce the settlement, which the court granted on August 25, 2023. Judgement was entered on the same day. Defendants move to vacate the judgment.

Defendants argue the judgment was obtained through extrinsic fraud or mistake and based on Hsieh’s attorney’s purported misconduct. They assert ownership of the property was not properly disclosed because, based on public records, a limited liability company holds legal title to the subject property. Id.; Augustus Decl. ¶ 4, Exh. 1. Second, they assert investigations by the Los Angeles City Health Department and County of Los Angeles Building and Safety were not disclosed. Third, they contend Hseih’s attorney made false statements to the court and concealed facts from the Augustuses. Augustuses Decl. ¶ 6.

 

“Extrinsic fraud usually arises when a party is denied a fair adversary hearing because he has been ‘deliberately kept in ignorance of the action or proceeding, or in some other way fraudulently prevented from presenting his claim or defense.’ ” In contrast, the term “extrinsic mistake” is “broadly applied when circumstances extrinsic to the litigation have unfairly cost a party a hearing on the merits. [Citations.] ‘Extrinsic mistake is found when [among other things] ... a mistake led a court to do what it never intended....’ ” ”   Bae v. T.D. Service Co. (2016) 245 Cal.App.4th 89, 97-98 (citations omitted).

 

Defendants fail to show evidence of extrinsic fraud or mistake. First, they were not deliberately kept ignorant of the proceedings or prevented from presenting claims or defenses because they were permitted to conduct discovery. They would have been able to discover facts relating to any investigation concerning the property. Second, the issue of ownership was known prior to the settlement, and Hsieh amended the complaint, stating the trust as the owner. Third, the moving papers fail to articulate how Hsieh’s attorney is alleged to have committed a fraud upon the court. The conclusory statements do not warrant the relief requested. DENIED.
