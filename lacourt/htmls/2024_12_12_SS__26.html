Case Number: 23STLC05961    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: 26

Cal. Automobile Ins. Co. v. Benson Crane, inc. et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Plaintiff California Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings against Defendant David Allen Benson dba Benson Crane is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.  

ANALYSIS:

On September 15, 2023, Plaintiff California Automobile Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed this action for automobile subrogation against Defendant David Allen Benson dba Benson Crane (erroneously sued as Benson Crane, Inc.) (“Defendant”). Defendant filed an answer on January 24, 2024. On August 14, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission Admitted and Request for Monetary Sanctions against Defendant. (Minute Order, 08/14/24.)

Plaintiff filed the instant Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings against Defendant on September 9, 2024. No opposition to the Motion has been filed to date.

Discussion

Plaintiff moves for judgment on the pleadings, the standard for which is essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer. That is, under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Bezirdjian v. O’Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321-322, citing Schabarum v. California Legislature (1998) 60 Cal.App.4th 1205, 1216.) Matters which are subject to mandatory judicial notice may be treated as part of the complaint and may be considered without notice to the parties. Matters which are subject to permissive judicial notice must be specified in the notice of motion, the supporting points and authorities, or as the court otherwise permits. (Id.)  The motion may not be supported by extrinsic evidence. (Barker v. Hull (1987) 191 Cal.App.3d 221, 236.)

While a statutory motion for judgment on the pleadings brought pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 438, et seq. must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration, there is no such requirement for a motion for judgment on the pleadings brought pursuant to the common law. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 439 [moving party must file declaration demonstrating an attempt to meet and confer in person or by telephone, at least five days before the date a motion for judgment on the pleadings is filed].)

Plaintiff’s Motion is accompanied by a request for judicial notice of the matters deemed admitted in Plaintiff’s Motion to Deem Requests for Admission, Set One, Admitted, and this Court’s August 14, 2024 order deeming the Requests for Admission admitted against Defendant. The Court takes judicial notice of these admissions pursuant to Cal. Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (d). (Cal. Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d); Cloud v. Northrop Grumman Corp. (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 995, 999; Evans v. California Trailer Court, Inc. (1994) 28 Cal.App.4th 540, 549) [holding that the court may take judicial notice of matters that cannot be reasonably controverted, including “admissions and concessions.”].)

The admissions in the Request for Admissions directly contradict the general denial and affirmative defenses asserted in Defendant’s Answer. The admissions admit that Defendant, during the operation of a crane, caused an electrical surge on the property of Plaintiff’s insured that caused damage to the property. (RJN, Exh. 2 at Exh. A, Request for Admission Nos. 1-4.) They also admit that Defendant failed to act with reasonable care in the operation of the crane and as a result, Defendant caused damage to a power line on the insured’s property, and to the property. (Id. at Request for Admission Nos. 5-7.) The admissions admit that Defendant’s negligence caused the insured, and subsequently, Plaintiff to incur damages of $11,888.23. (Id. at Request for Admission Nos. 8-10.) Finally, Defendant admits that the affirmative defenses asserted in the Answer lack merit and evidentiary support. (Id. at Request for Admission No. 11.)

By this Motion, Plaintiff has demonstrated that it served Defendant with Requests for Admissions that effectively establish the truth of the allegations in the Complaint, as detailed above. The admissions establish the facts upon which Plaintiff based its Complaint and that Defendant has not alleged a defense to Plaintiff’s Complaint in the Answer. Therefore, judgment on the pleadings is appropriate against Defendant.

Conclusion

Plaintiff California Automobile Insurance Company’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings against Defendant David Allen Benson dba Benson Crane is GRANTED. PLAINTIFF IS TO FILE AND SERVE A PROPOSED JUDGMENT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.

Case Number: 24STCP02499    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: 26

Fitterer v. Magana et al.

TENTATIVE RULING:

Petitioner Brian L. Fitterer’s Petition to Declare Mobilehome Abandoned is DENIED. 

ANALYSIS:

On August 8, 2024, Petitioner Brian L. Fitterer dba Laurel Canyon Mobile Estates (“Petitioner”) filed the instant Petition to Declare Mobilehome Abandoned against Respondents Juan Magana and Irma Magana (“Respondents”). Respondents filed a verified answer on October 1, 2024.

Discussion

Pursuant to Civil Code section 798.61, Petitioner seeks a court order deeming Respondents’ mobilehome, (“the Mobilehome”) located at 9051 Laurel Canyon Blvd., #24, Sun Valley, California (“the Space”), to be abandoned, and awarding Petitioner rent due on the Mobilehome site from October 1, 2021 to July 31, 2024 in the amount of $47,946.40, plus daily accruing damages of $35.71 per day from August 1, 2024 through judgment, and court costs and attorney’s fees. (Pet., Prayer at ¶¶2-4.)

A. Determination of “Abandoned Mobilehome”—Civ. Code § 798.61(a)(1)

Pursuant to Civil Code, section 798.61, subdivision (a)(1), an “abandoned mobilehome” means a mobilehome about which all of the following are true:

(A) It is located in a mobilehome park on a site for which no rent has been paid to the management for the preceding 60 days. 

(B) It is unoccupied.

(C) A reasonable person would believe it to be abandoned. 

(D) It is not permanently affixed to the land.

(Civ. Code, § 798.61, subd. (a)(1).) The Petition is supported by the declaration of Melina Miramontes, Petitioner’s regional manager. Miramontes declares that the Mobilehome was previously occupied by Respondents’ daughter, Griselda Magana, pursuant to a written agreement with Petitioner. (Pet., Miramontes Decl., ¶2.) Then, from “March 1, 2024, and at all times continuing thereafter to the date of this Declaration the subject Mobilehome has been unoccupied.” (Id. at ¶5.) The mobilehome park manager posted a Notice of Belief of Abandonment on the Mobilehome on May 31, 2024. (Id. at ¶6 and Exhs. 1-2.) A person alleging to be Respondent Irma Magana responded to the Notice of Belief of Abandonment but refused to pay the past due rent and utilities and incurring storage fees despite being provided notice of the same. (Id. at ¶8.) Petitioner explains Respondents’ burden at the hearing:

(Civ. Code, § 798.61, subd. (a)(1).) The Petition is supported by the declaration of Melina Miramontes, Petitioner’s regional manager. Miramontes declares that the Mobilehome was previously occupied by Respondents’ daughter, Griselda Magana, pursuant to a written agreement with Petitioner. (Pet., Miramontes Decl., ¶2.) Then, from “March 1, 2024, and at all times continuing thereafter to the date of this Declaration the subject Mobilehome has been unoccupied.” (Id. at ¶5.) The mobilehome park manager posted a Notice of Belief of Abandonment on the Mobilehome on May 31, 2024. (Id. at ¶6 and Exhs. 1-2.) A person alleging to be Respondent Irma Magana responded to the Notice of Belief of Abandonment but refused to pay the past due rent and utilities and incurring storage fees despite being provided notice of the same. (Id. at ¶8.) Petitioner explains Respondents’ burden at the hearing:

If, at the hearing, the petitioner shows by a preponderance of the evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome has been satisfied and no party establishes an interest therein at the hearing and tenders all past due rent and other charges, the court shall enter a judgment of abandonment, determine the amount of charges to which the petitioner is entitled, and award attorney's fees and costs to the petitioner. For purposes of this subdivision, an interest in the mobilehome shall be established by evidence of a right to possession of the mobilehome or a security or ownership interest in the mobilehome.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 798.61, subd. (d)(2).) In their answer, Respondents dispute that they have abandoned the Mobilehome. Respondents contend that Petitioner took away their possession of the Mobilehome via an unlawful detainer action against a subtenant (presumably Griselda Magana) in December 2023 and by thereafter changing the locks on February 8, 2024. (Answer, p. 2:3-9 and Exh. 3.) Respondent Irma Magana sent Petitioner a certified letter on February 21, 2024 requesting access to the Mobilehome. (Id. at p. 2:9-10 and Exhs. 4-5.) On April 2, 2024, NSLA Neighborhood Legal Services sent a letter advising Petitioner that it was in violation of Mobilehome Residency Law by denying Respondents the right to sell the Mobilehome and/or pay off any outstanding debt owed to Petitioner. (Id. at p. 2:10-15 and Exh. 6.) Respondents sent Petitioner’s counsel a letter by certified mail on June 13, 2024 stating that the Mobilehome had not been abandoned and that Respondents have attempted to make payments that were refused. (Id. at p. 2:17-24 and Exh. 10.)

Due to this conflicting evidence the Court cannot make a finding the Mobilehome is “abandoned” within the meaning of Civil Code, section 798.61, subdivision (a)(1). Respondents sent Petitioner multiple letters regarding their access to, and possession of, the Mobilehome before and after the Notice of Belief of Abandonment was posted. Petitioner’s only acknowledgement of this communication is the park manager’s declaration that “[w]e have received notice that a person who alleges to be Irma Magana, one of the Respondents, has responded to the Notice of Abandonment but has repeatedly refused to pay the past due rent and utilities and incurring storage fees, despite being provided with a full ledger listing all past due amounts owed.” (Motion, Miramontes Decl., ¶8.) No copy of the ledger, nor proof of its service on Respondents, is attached. A reasonable person would not believe that Respondents have abandoned the Mobilehome under these facts.

B.         Notice of Belief of Abandonment—Civ. Code § 798.61(b)

Civil Code, section 798.61, subdivision (b) requires:

“After determining a mobilehome in a mobilehome park to be an abandoned mobilehome, the management shall post a notice of belief of abandonment on the mobilehome for not less than 30 days, and shall deposit copies of the notice in the United States mail, postage prepaid, addressed to the homeowner at the last known address and to any known registered owner, if different from the homeowner, and to any known holder of a security interest in the abandoned mobilehome. This notice shall be mailed by registered or certified mail with a return receipt requested.”

(Civ. Code, § 798.61, subd. (b).) Petitioner attached a Notice of Belief of Abandonment to the Petition, which was served on Respondents by posting and certified mail on May 31, 2024 and June 3, 2024, respectively. (Pet., Exhs. 2-3.) Therefore, Petitioner has satisfied the requirements of Civil Code, section 798.61, subdivision (b). 

C.        Petition for Judicial Declaration—Civ. Code § 798.61(c)

Civil Code, section 798.61(c)(1) states:

“Thirty or more days following posting pursuant to subdivision (b), the management may file a petition in the superior court in the county in which the mobilehome park is located, for a judicial declaration of abandonment of the mobilehome. A proceeding under this subdivision is a limited civil case.  Copies of the petition shall be served upon the homeowner, any known registered owner, and any known person having a lien or security interest of record in the mobilehome by posting a copy on the mobilehome and mailing copies to those persons at their last known addresses by registered or certified mail with a return receipt requested in the United States mail, postage prepaid.”  (Emphasis added.)

(Civ. Code, § 798.61, subd. (c)(1).) Sun Valley is located within the County of Los Angeles, which is within this Court’s jurisdiction. The instant Petition was filed more than 30 days after the Notice of Abandonment was posted. Petitioner has shown proper service of the Petition and initial Notice of Hearing on Respondents. (Proofs of Service, filed 08/16/24 and 08/21/24.)

D.        Charges, Attorneys’ Fees, and Costs

“If, at the hearing, the petitioner shows by a preponderance of the evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome has been satisfied and no party establishes an interest therein at the hearing and tenders all past due rent and other charges, the court shall enter a judgment of abandonment, determine the amount of charges to which the petitioner is entitled, and award attorney's fees and costs to the petitioner.” (Civ. Code, § 798.61, subd. (d)(2).) As discussed above, Petitioner has not shown by a preponderance of the evidence that the criteria for an abandoned mobilehome is satisfied.

Also, Petitioner’s evidence also does not demonstrate how it came to determine the amount Respondents are claimed to owe. No copy of the ledger, nor proof of service of the same, purportedly provided to Respondents is attached to the Petition. Also, for the period during which this amount accrued, starting in October 2021, it is unclear if Griselda Magana owed Petitioner some of the rent that they are seeking against Respondents. Respondents attach a letter to Petitioner from NSLA Neighborhood Legal Services that refers to the unlawful detainer action against Griselda Magana, LASC Case No. 23PDUD00017. The Court, on its own motion, takes judicial notice that judgment has been entered in LASC Case No. 23PDUD00017 against Griselda Magana in the amount of $25,000.00. (LASC Case No. 23PDUD00017, Judgment dated 12/13/24.)

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Petitioner Brian L. Fitterer’s Petition to Declare Mobilehome Abandoned is DENIED.

Case Number: 24STLC04737    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: 26

Hagop J. Markarian Corp. v. New Directions for Youth, Inc. et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING: 

Plaintiff Hagop J. Markarian Corporation’s Motion to Enforce Terms of Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release; to Enter Judgment Against Defendant; for Prejudgment Interest; and For Attorney’s Fees and Costs is GRANTED. JUDGMENT IS TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT CHRIS RAMOS IN THE AMOUNT OF $4,563.00 PRINCIPAL, TEN (10) PERCENT INTEREST FROM THE DUE DATE OF EACH PAYMENT, ATTORNEY’S FEES OF $4,180.00 AND COSTS OF $427.30

ANALYSIS:

On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff Hagop J. Markarian Corporation (“Plaintiff”) filed this action for breach of contract and common counts against Defendant New Directions for Youth, Inc. (“Defendant”). On September 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed a notice of settlement. Two days later, Plaintiff filed a copy of the parties’ settlement agreement with a request for dismissal and retention of jurisdiction under Code of Civil Procedure section 664.6. The Court dismissed the action pursuant to the stipulation on September 17, 2024.

On October 9, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant Motion to Enforce Terms of Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release; to Enter Judgment Against Defendant; for Prejudgment Interest; and For Attorney’s Fees and Costs. To date, no opposition has been filed.

Discussion

Plaintiff brings the instant motion pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, section 664.6, which states in relevant part: 

If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.

(Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (a).) Prior to January 1, 2021, “parties” under section 664.6 meant the litigants themselves, not their attorneys.  (Levy v. Superior Court (1995) 10 Cal.4th 578, 586.) The current statute provides that “parties” includes “an attorney who represents the party” and an insurer’s agent. (Code Civ. Proc., § 664.6, subd. (b).) The settlement must include the signatures of the parties seeking to enforce the agreement, and against whom enforcement is sought. (J.B.B. Investment Partners, Ltd. v. Fair (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 974, 985.) The settlement agreement complies with the statutory requirements set forth above because it was signed by both parties and their attorneys. (Motion, Markarian Decl., Exh. 8, p. 6.)

Furthermore, the request for retention of jurisdiction must be made in writing, by the parties, before the action is dismissed for the Court’s retention of jurisdiction to conform to the statutory language. (Wackeen v. Malis (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 429, 433 [“If, after a suit has been dismissed, a party brings a section 664.6 motion for a judgment on a settlement agreement but cannot present to the court a request for retention of jurisdiction that meets all of these requirements, then enforcement of the agreement must be left to a separate lawsuit.”].) The parties’ request for retention of jurisdiction complies with these requirements because it was made in writing to the Court before the action was dismissed. (Motion, Markarian Decl., Exh. 8, ¶15.)

The settlement provides that Defendant would pay Plaintiff $10,126.00 through three installments on August 6, 2024, September 1, 2024, and September 30, 2024. (Id. at Exh. 8, ¶2.) The settlement agreement also provides that in the event of Defendant’s default, Plaintiff may move to enforce the settlement agreement, and be awarded ten percent interest, attorney’s fees and costs. (Id. at ¶¶4, 9.) Payments of $5,563.00 were made towards the settlement, after which Defendant defaulted. (Id. at ¶10; Grossburg Decl., Exh. 11.) Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff is entitled to entry of judgment against Defendant in the amount of $4,563.00 principal ($10,126.00 - $5,563.00), ten (10) percent interest from the due date of each payment, attorney’s fees of $4,180.00 and costs of $427.30. (Id. at ¶¶11-16.)

Conclusion

Plaintiff Hagop J. Markarian Corporation’s Motion to Enforce Terms of Settlement Agreement and Mutual Release; to Enter Judgment Against Defendant; for Prejudgment Interest; and For Attorney’s Fees and Costs is GRANTED. JUDGMENT IS TO BE ENTERED IN PLAINTIFF’S FAVOR AND AGAINST DEFENDANT CHRIS RAMOS IN THE AMOUNT OF $4,563.00 PRINCIPAL, TEN (10) PERCENT INTEREST FROM THE DUE DATE OF EACH PAYMENT, ATTORNEY’S FEES OF $4,180.00 AND COSTS OF $427.30.

Case Number: 24STLC05279    Hearing Date: December 12, 2024    Dept: 26

Lee v. wall et al.,

TENTATIVE RULING:

Defendant Leslie Wall, an individual’s Motion to Vacate Entry of Default is GRANTED. THE DEFAULT ENTERED ON SEPTEMBER 25, 2024 IS HEREBY VACATED. DEFENDANT LESLIE WALL, AN INDIVIDUAL, IS TO FILE AND SERVE THEIR ANSWER TO THE COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.                                                                                                                               

ANALYSIS: 

On July 25, 2024, Plaintiff Christine Lee (“Plaintiff”) filed the instant action for breach of lease agreement against Defendant Leslie Wall, individually (“Defendant Wall”), and Leslie Wall doing business as W2K Properties, LLC (“Defendant W2K”). Following Defendants’ failure to file a responsive pleading, the Court entered their default on September 25, 2024 and October 11, 2024.

The instant Motion to Vacate Default and Default Judgment was filed on October 28, 2024 by “Leslie Wall, an individual dba W2K Properties, Inc.” Plaintiff filed a late opposition on December 4, 2024.

Discussion

As an initial matter, it is unclear which Defendant filed the instant Motion. There is no defendant named “Leslie Wall, an individual dba W2K Properties, Inc.” Also, the Motion was filed in propria persona. However, a corporate entity cannot represent itself in court. (Clean Air Transport Systems v. San Mateo County Transit Dist. (1988) 198 Cal.App.3d 576, 578 (citing Merco Constr. Engineers, Inc. v. Municipal Court (1978) 21 Cal.3d 724, 729-730).) This rule applies to all entities regarded as separate from their owners, including partnerships and unincorporated associations. (See Clean Air Transport Systems, supra, 19 Cal.App.3d at 578.) Therefore, the Court will consider the Motion as being brought by Defendant Wall.

Defendant Wall moves to vacate the entry of default and quashing service of the Summons and Complaint.

Code of Civil Procedure section 473(b)

Defendant Wall moves to vacate the entry of default on multiple grounds, include Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b). Regarding relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (b), an application for relief must be made no more than six months after entry of the order from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of party fault attesting to their excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) The motion must also be accompanied by a copy of the moving defendant’s proposed pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b).) This can be corrected if the defendant submits a proposed responsive pleading by the hearing date. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (b); Carmel, Ltd. v. Tavoussi (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 393, 403.)

The Motion was timely filed and served one month after entry of default against Defendant Wall and is supported by a declaration of fault that explains Defendant Wall submitted the Summons and Complaint to their attorney, The Law Offices of Gary Saunders, on August 9, 2024. (Motion, Wall Decl., ¶1.) Defendant Wall expected that their attorney would follow up and respond to the action. (Ibid.) This declaration sufficiently demonstrates that Defendant Wall’s default was the result of their surprise that The Law Offices of Gary Saunders did not respond to the action. The Motion is also accompanied by a copy of Defendant Wall’s proposed answer to the Complaint. (Id. at Exh. A.)

Plaintiff untimely opposition supports a finding of excusable mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect by Defendant Wall. Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration corroborates that communicated with an attorney who purportedly represented Defendant Wall prior to entry of default but who did not file a responsive pleading, resulting in entry of default. (Opp., Uss Decl., ¶¶8-9.) Thereafter, Defendant Wall’s purported attorney communicated that Defendant Wall would be acting in pro per. (Id. at ¶¶10-11.) At that point, however, the default had already been entered. Therefore, the default entered on September 25, 2024 against Defendant Wall is hereby vacated.

Other statutory grounds for relief

The Motion also refers to other statutory grounds for relief, including Code of Civil Procedure section 473.5, section 473, subdivision (d), and 418.10. However, the Motion does not explain how any of these statutes applies to the facts set forth in Defendant Wall’s supporting declaration. Section 473.5 pertains to lack of actual notice of the action, which Defendant Wall admits to having. Section 473, subdivision (d) pertains to void judgments or orders, but the Motion does not explain why the entry of default in this action was void. In fact, the Motion does not provide any authority regarding the request to quash service of the Summons and Complaint under 418.10. Therefore, the request to for relief under these statutes, including to quash service, is denied.

Finally, the Motion refers to res judicata and collateral estoppel without providing any legal authority regarding those doctrines and their applicability to the facts of this action.

Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Leslie Wall, an individual’s Motion to Vacate Entry of Default is GRANTED. THE DEFAULT ENTERED ON SEPTEMBER 25, 2024 IS HEREBY VACATED. DEFENDANT LESLIE WALL, AN INDIVIDUAL, IS TO FILE AND SERVE THEIR ANSWER TO THE COMPLAINT WITHIN 20 DAYS OF THIS ORDER.
