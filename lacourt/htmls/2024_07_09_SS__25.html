Case Number: 22STLC07845    Hearing Date: July 9, 2024    Dept: 25

Tentative Ruling

Commissioner Karine Mkrtchyan

Department 25

Confidential – Court-Privileged Document

Hearing Date:                         Tuesday, July 9, 2024

Case Name:                             FRED LOYA INSURANCE COMPANY v. ISAAC H. MCIVER; and DOES 1 to 100

Case No.:                                22STLC07845

Motion:                                   Motion for Summary Judgment

Moving Party:                         Plaintiff Fred Loya Insurance Company   

Responding Party:                   Unopposed

Notice:                                    IMPROPER. The proof of service is defective, and Plaintiff did not provide proof that the motion was served on interested parties as directed by the Court’s June 6, 2024 Minute Order.  

Recommended Ruling:           Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED WTHOUT PREJUDICE.     

 

                                               

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 21, 2022, Plaintiff Fred Loya Insurance Company (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Isaac H. McIver (“Defendant”) and Does 1 to 100, alleging causes of action for: (1) Subrogation Claim; and (2) Indebtedness.

 

The Complaint alleges the following: on November 23, 2019, Plaintiff had in effect an insurance policy with Auri Rocha Rocha (the “Insured”) providing automobile insurance. (Complaint, ¶ 8.) Defendant rear-ended the Insured. (Complaint, ¶ 9.) Due to Defendant’s negligence, Plaintiff made payments under the Insured’s policy for the repair of the property in the sum of $10,545.63. (Complaint, ¶ 10.) Plaintiff demands judgment against Defendant in the sum of $10,545.63. (Complaint, ¶ 11.)  

 

On June 21, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer to the Complaint.  

 

On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff filed Motions Compelling Answers to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories, Set One and Deeming Requests for Admission, Set One, as admitted with an accompanying Motions for Sanctions.

 

            On February 7, 2024, the Court granted the motions. (02/07/24 Minute Order.) As to the request for admissions, the Court indicated that “Plaintiff is entitled to an order deeming the Requests for Admission, Set One, admitted against Defendant.” (02/07/24 Minute Order at p. 4.) The Court also ordered Defendant to serve responses to Plaintiff’s Form Interrogatories within 20 days of service of the Court’s order, and ordered Defendant and his counsel, jointly and severally, to pay monetary sanctions to Plaintiff’s counsel in the sum of $1,920.00. (02/07/24 Minute Order at p. 6.)

 

            On March 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served the instant Motion for Summary Judgment. The motion is made on the grounds that Defendant has admitted to the truth of all pertinent issues and, as such, there are no triable issues of act as to liability or damages.

 

            On June 6, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the motion for summary judgment. (06/06/24 Minute Order.) Having reviewed and considered the moving papers, the Court found that “proof of service of the instant motion to [b]e defective.” (06/06/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) ANALYSIS

 

I.          Summary Judgment   

A.                Legal Standard

            The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119. “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67.

In the context of a motion for summary judgment, “[a] plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).) “Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exist as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(1).)

“The summary judgment statute [is] not intended nor can it be used as a substitute for existing methods in the trial of issues of fact.” Travelers Indemn. Co. v. McIntosh (1952) 112 Cal.App.2d 177, 182. The summary judgment “procedure is drastic and should be used with caution.” House v. Lala (1960) 180 Cal.App.2d 412, 415. “Any doubt in granting summary judgment should be exercised against the moving party.” Ibid.

B.        Discussion   

            “Subrogation is the insurer’s right to be put in the position of the insured, in order to recover from third parties who are legally responsible to the insured for a loss paid by the insurer.” Plut v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Co. (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 98, 104, citation omitted. “Where a subrogation provision exists, an insurer may recoup its payments directly from the tortfeasor or from the proceeds of the insured’s action against a tortfeasor.” Ibid., citation omitted.

            Plaintiff presents the following undisputed material facts: on or about November 23, 2019, Plaintiff had in effect an insurance policy with Auri Rocha Rocha. (UMF No. 1.) Defendant negligently rear-ended the vehicle of Plaintiff’s insured. (UMF No. 2.) As a direct and proximate cause of Defendant’s negligence, Plaintiff made payments under the Insured’s policy for the repair of the property in the sum of $10,543.63. (UMF No. 3.) There is now due and owing from Defendant to Plaintiff the total sum of $10,543.63. (UMF No. 4.)

            Although Plaintiff has presented the aforementioned undisputed material facts, the Court cannot grant the motion for summary judgment as Plaintiff failed to “file supplemental papers addressing the deficiency . . . with respect to proper proof of service of the motion.” (06/06/24 Minute Order at p. 2.) The Court reminds Plaintiff that “[d]ue process requires a party . . . be given adequate notice . . . .” Fenn v. Sherriff (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 1466, 1481.

Due to Plaintiff’s failure to comply with the Court’s June 6, 2024 minute order, the instant motion for summary judgment is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

II.        Conclusion

           

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion for summary judgment is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

On May 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served the instant Motion to Deposit Bond Amount, Cancel and Exonerate Bond, Discharge Liability, for Attorney Fees and Costs, and to be Dismissed. Pursuant to the motion, Plaintiff seeks an order “(1) awarding [Plaintiff] attorney fees of $5,392.00 and $775.05 for costs under section 386.6 of the Code of Civil Procedure; (2) authorizing [Plaintiff] to deposit with the clerk of the court the applicable amount of the $15,000 contractor’s disciplinary bond number PR2725532 . . . it issued on behalf of [Defendant] RCH . . . net of awarded fees and costs, with requested $6,167.05 for fees and costs resulting in a net deposit of $8,832.95; (3) cancelling and exonerating the Bond in the full amount of $15,000; (4) releasing and discharging [Plaintiff] from liability to all persons for all breaches of the Bond including costs and attorney fees that may be awarded as costs; and (5) dismissing [Plaintiff] with prejudice upon [Plaintiff’s] deposit of $8,832.95 with the clerk of this court.” (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:27-2:9.)

MOVING PARTY POSITION

            In its opposition to the motion, Defendant RCH raises the following arguments: (1) the exoneration of the Bond is premature and should remain in full force and effect; (2) there is only one claim on the Bond and therefore CCP § 386(b) is inapposite; and (3) an award of attorney’s fees is premature.     

 

 

 

REPLY

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Depositing Bond Amount, Cancelling and Exonerating the Bond, Discharging Liability, Attorneys’ Fees and Costs, and Dismissing Plaintiff     

A.                Legal Standard/Applicable Law

“In interpreting the terms of a contract of suretyship, the same rules are to be observed as in the case of other contracts.” (Civ. Code § 2837.) “A surety or guarantor is one who promises to answer for the debt, default, or miscarriage of another, or hypothecates property as security therefor.” (Civ. Code § 2787.)

According to Civ. Code § 7071.5, a contractor’s bond is of the benefit of the following:

(a) A homeowner contracting for home improvement upon the homeowner’s personal family residence damaged as a result of a violation of this chapter by the licensee.

(b) A property owner contracting for the construction of a single-family dwelling who is damaged as a result of a violation of this chapter by the licensee. That property owner shall only recover under this subdivision if the single-family dwelling is not intended for sale or offered for sale at the time the damages were incurred.

(c) A person damaged as a result of a willful and deliberate violation of this chapter by the licensee, or by the fraud of the licensee in the execution or performance of a construction contract.

(Civ. Code, § 7071.5, subds. (a)-(c).)   

“A bond given as a condition of a license or permit shall be continuous in form, remain in full force and effect, and run concurrently with the license or permit period and any and all renewals, or until cancellation or withdrawal of the surety from the bond.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.440.)

Under CCP § 995.430, a bond remains in force and effect until the earliest of the following events:

(a) The sureties withdraw from or cancel the bond or a new bond is given in place of the original bond.

(b) The purpose for which the bond was given is satisfied or the purpose is abandoned without any liability having been incurred.

(c) a judgment of liability on the bond that exhausts the amount of the bond is satisfied.

(d) The term of the bond expires. Unless the statute providing for the bond prescribes a fixed term, the bond is continuous.

(Code Civ. Proc. § 995.430, subds. (a)-d).)

“[W]henever conflicting claims are or may be made upon a person for or relating to personal property, or the performance of an obligation, or any portion thereof, such person may bring an action against the conflicting claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims. The order of substitution may be made and the action of interpleader may be maintained, and the applicant or interpleading party discharged from liability to all or any of the conflicting claimants, although their titles or claims have not a common origin, or are not identical but are adverse to and independent of one another.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (a).)

“Any person, firm, corporation, association or other entity against whom double or multiple claims are made, or may be made, by two or more persons which are such that they may give rise to double or multiple liability, may bring an action against the claimants to compel them to interplead and litigate their several claims.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (b).) “The applicant or interpleading party may deny liability in whole or in part to any or all of the claimants.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (b).) “The applicant or interpleading party may join as a defendant in such action any other party against whom claims are made by one or more of the claimants or such other party may interplead by cross-complaint; provided, however, that such claims arise out of the same transaction or occurrence.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (b).)

“A party to an action who follows the procedure set forth in Section 386 . . . may insert in his motion, petition, complaint, or cross complaint a request for allowance of his costs and reasonable attorney fees incurred in such action. In ordering the discharge of such party, the court may, in its discretion, award such party his costs and reasonable attorney fees from the amount in dispute which has been deposited with the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386.6, subd. (a).)  

B.        Discussion   

            Plaintiff argues that it is a mere stakeholder in this dispute with no interest in the sum in dispute. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at p. 3:10-11.) Plaintiff “moves to deposit the $15,000 bond amount, net of allowed attorney fees and costs; to cancel and exonerate the Bond; for [Plaintiff’s] liability to be discharged; and for it to be dismissed without prejudice upon deposit.” (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at p. 3:11-14.)

 

 

 

1.         Evidence in Support of the Motion

            In support of the motion, Stacie L. Brandt (“Brandt”) declares the following: on January 22, 2024, she sent an e-mail to counsel for Defendants Orlando and RCH notifying them that Plaintiff intended to deposit $15,000.00, net of fees and costs, with the court, and asking if they were amendable to stipulating to reduce the fees and costs of deposit. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 2.) Receiving no response from either attorney, on May 4, 2024, counsel wrote to them again offering the option of stipulating to deposit. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 2.) On May 10, 2024, she received a response from Defendant Orlando’s counsel that they would stipulate, but by then the instant motion was mostly drafted, as she responded on May 14, 2024. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 2.)

            Plaintiff incurred attorneys’ fees of 11 hours at $245.00 for associates and $295.00 for partner and $703.40 in costs prior to the filing of the motion which consisted of filing the interpleader, serving the Defendants, and following up with Defendants about resolution and depositing the bond amount. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 3; Ex. 2.) Attorneys’ fees of 8.6 hours were incurred to prepare the instant motion and accompanying declarations. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 3.) In sum, Plaintiff requests total attorneys’ fees and costs of $5,932.00. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 3.) Brandt sets forth her hourly rate of $295.00 and the hourly rate of Brandon T. Molloy of $245.00 per hour, and the time spent by counsel in this action. (Brandt Decl., ¶ 3.)

            Juston Hinton (“Hinton”) also presents a declaration in support of the motion. Mr. Hinton declares that Plaintiff issued the Bond. (Hinton Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. 1.) Defendant Orlando claims $15,000.00 from the Bond, which Defendant RCH disputes. (Hinton Decl., ¶ 3.) Plaintiff is a mere stakeholder with no interest in the amounts claimed or in any portion thereof. (Hinton Decl., ¶ 4.)

2.         Depositing the Bond

            The parties do not dispute that Plaintiff can deposit the Bond amount with the Court. The Court, however, finds that Plaintiff’s reliance on CCP § 386(b) is inapposite. Also, CCP § 386(c) provides that “[a]ny amount which a plaintiff . . . admits to be payable may be deposited by him with the clerk of the court at the time of the filing of the complaint . . . in interpleader without first obtaining an order of the court therefor.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 386, subd. (c).)

            Thus, the Court finds that Plaintiff can deposit the Bond amount with the Court.

3.         Cancelling and Exonerating the Bond and Other Relief

            While the Court has found that Plaintiff can deposit the Bond, the Court does not find merit in Plaintiff’s argument that the Bond should be cancelled and exonerated upon deposit because the purpose for which the Bond was given has not been satisfied.

            First, the Court notes that Plaintiff did not provide any admissible evidence that the purpose for the Bond has been satisfied. Moreover, Defendant Orlando denied any damage as did Defendant RCH in their respective Answers to the Complaint. Thus, there remains a dispute as to whether the purpose for which the Bond was given has been satisfied. Plaintiff has not withdrawn or canceled the Bond, the Bond term has not expired, and there has been no judgment of liability on the Bond. Thus, none of the conditions set forth in CCP § 995.430 have been met to cancel and exonerate the Bond.

The Court also finds that Plaintiff’s citation to Karton v. Ari Design & Construction, Inc. (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 734 is inapposite as such case concerned a motion for attorneys’ fees.

Additionally, the Court notes that in the Complaint, Plaintiff moves for cancellation and exoneration of the Bond pursuant to CCP § 995.430(c). (Complaint, ¶ 18.) Such section of the Code of Civil Procedure states that a bond will remain in force and in effect until “[a] judgment of liability on the bond that exhausts the amount of the bond is satisfied.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 995.430, sudb. (c).) Here, however, there has been no judgment of liability on the Bond.

The Court therefore DENIES WITHOUT PREJUDICE Plaintiff’s request to cancel and exonerate the Bond. As such, the Court finds it inappropriate to dismiss Plaintiff from this action at this time and DENIES such request WITHOUT PREJUDICE due to the fact that Plaintiff has not made a showing to exonerate and cancel the Bond.

Thus, Plaintiff’s request for discharging of liability is inappropriate at this time as is Plaintiff’s request for attorneys’ fees and costs and such requests are therefore DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.  

Accordingly, given that Plaintiff has not made the requisite showing for all of its requested relief, the motion is GRANTED IN PART.

The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to deposit the Bond amount with the Court.

The Court DENIES WITHOUT PREJUDICE Plaintiff’s requests to cancel and exonerate the Bond, to be released and discharged from all liability, for attorneys’ fees and costs, and to be dismissed from this action with prejudice.

II.        Conclusion

           

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s Motion to Deposit Bond Amount, Cancel and Exonerate Bond, Discharge Liability, for Attorney Fees and Costs, and to be Dismissed is GRANTED IN PART.

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to deposit the Bond amount with the Court.

 

The Court DENIES WITHOUT PREJUDICE Plaintiff’s requests to cancel and exonerate the Bond, to be released and discharged from all liability, for attorneys’ fees and costs, and to be dismissed from this action with prejudice.

BACKGROUND

On April 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants Vladimir Polyakov, D.D.S., and Vladamir Polyakov, D.D.S., Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”) for Dental Malpractice, Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress, and Intentional Misrepresentation.

 

On May 14, 2024, Defendants filed a Demurrer and Motion to Strike the FAC.

 

On May 7, 2024, the Court entered an order relating Reah Sybirski v. Nikou Zarabian, LASC Case No. 23STLC05564, with this action being deemed the lead case. (05/07/24 Minute Order.)

 

On May 20, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Latrice A.G. Byrdsong sitting in Department 25 at Spring Street Courthouse.

 

On May 28, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint to Include Claim for Punitive Damages in Complaint and to Reclassify Under Unlimited Jurisdiction. On May 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed an amended motion of the same. On June 11, 2024, Defendants opposed the motion. On June 18, 2024, Plaintiff replied.

 

On June 3, 2024, this action was reassigned to the Honorable Karine Mkrtchyan sitting in Department 25 at Spring Street Courthouse.

On June 20, 2024, Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint was Sustained, with 20-days leave to amend the Complaint.  On June 20, 2024, Defendants’ Motion to Strike Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint was Granted, with 20-days leave to amend. 

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            Plaintiff indicates having recently discovered information that would support claims for intentional torts and punitive damages. Plaintiff contends amendment will facilitate resolution of all disputes between the parties and that Defendants will suffer no prejudice if amendment is permitted, as these claims are already plead in the First Amended Complaint and discovery has just begun. Plaintiff contends to have discovered evidence showing Defendants’ conduct was highly unusual and improper.

 

OPPOSITION

 

            In opposition, Defendants contend that permitting amendment would only delay formal resolution of this matter and would not further justice. Defendants contend that if the Court is inclined to grant leave to amend, the Court should condition such leave on precluding Plaintiff from reclassifying this case or adding a prayer for punitive damages to compensate Defendants for any inconvenience. Defendants contend Plaintiff’s new facts were due to unexcused delay, since Plaintiff admits in the moving papers that she was aware of the documents giving rise to these new facts when she filed the Complaint.

 

            Defendants then contend that Plaintiff’s motion is procedurally deficient for failure to file a proof of service, and for not including a copy of the proposed amended complaint with the moving papers. Defendants contend Plaintiff’s motion does not state which allegations are proposed to be added or where in the proposed amended pleading they are to be located, and in fact no new additional allegations are included. Defendants also note that Plaintiff did not attach the report of Dr. Reznick to her declaration, which inhibits Defendants’ ability to determine whether the evidence is proper. Defendants contend the Court must treat Plaintiff the same as if she were represented by an attorney. Defendants further contend that Plaintiff’s declaration fails to satisfy the requirements of Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b), of the California Rules of Court, such as not indicating why the proposed amendment is necessary or proper or when the facts giving rise to the proposed new allegation were discovered, but then inconsistently claims she was aware of the report from Dr. Reznick before she filed the Complaint.

 

Defendants contend Plaintiff’s motion fails to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 425.13 and fails to show a substantial probability of prevailing on her punitive damages claim. Defendants also contend Plaintiff failed to provide expert evidence to support her negligence claim or any other claim.

 

REPLY

 

            On reply, Plaintiff contends she filed a proof of service with the Amended Motion on May 29, 2024, as it was inadvertently omitted from the original motion filed on May 28, 2024. Plaintiff then contends the FAC is attached to the motion and that no additions or deletions are requested to the FAC. Plaintiff contends the purpose of this motion was only meant to satisfy the procedural requirements of Code of Civil Procedure 425.13, and that the FAC otherwise already sufficiently pleads a basis for punitive damages. Plaintiff also contends the proposed FAC is based on information and evidence that supports claims for intentional misrepresentation and intentional infliction of emotional distress against Defendants. Plaintiff further contends the motion satisfies the requirements of Code of Civil Procedure section 425.13 and that her declaration details when she discovered that amendment was necessary.

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Discussion

A.                Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint to Include Claim for Punitive Damages

            The trial court has discretion to allow amendments to pleadings in the furtherance of justice. (Code Civ. Proc., § 473, subd. (a)(1).) “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) “There is a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the pleadings at any stage of the proceeding.” Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945, citation omitted. “An application to amend a pleading is addressed to the trial judge’s sound discretion.” Ibid., citation omitted. “In any action for damages arising out of the professional negligence of a health care provider, no claim for punitive damages shall be included in a complaint or other pleading unless the court enters an order allowing an amended pleading that includes a claim for punitive damages to be filed. The court may allow the filing of an amended pleading claiming punitive damages on a motion by the party seeking the amended pleading and on the basis of the supporting and opposing affidavits presented that the plaintiff has established that there is a substantial probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim pursuant to Section 3294 of the Civil Code.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.13, subd. (a).)

A party seeking leave to amend must attach a copy of the proposed pleading to the motion for leave to amend. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(1).) A motion for leave to amend must also state what allegations are proposed to be added or deleted to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number the additional or deleted allegations or located. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(2)-(3).) Under Rule 3.1324, subdivision (b) of the California Rules of Court, a separate declaration must accompany a motion for leave to amend and must specify: (1) the effect of the amendment; (2) why the amendment is necessary and proper; (3) when the facts giving rise to the amended allegations were discovered; and (4) the reasons why the request for amendment was not made earlier. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (b).)

The Court finds that Plaintiff’s motion does not comply with Rule 3.1324 of the California Rules of Court. Defendants correctly note that there is no proposed amended complaint attached to the motion or served with the motion. In fact, Plaintiff admits that there are no new allegations to be added and contends the FAC is otherwise sufficient as is. (See Reply, 4:7-15.) Furthermore, on June 20, 2024, the Court sustained Defendants’ First Amended Complaint to the second cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress and third causes of action for intentional misrepresentation with 20 days leave to amend.  Thus, there is no operative pleading before the Court. 

 

While the Court understands Plaintiff intended to comply with Code of Civil Procedure section 425.13 to add punitive damage allegations by first seeking permission from the Court to do so, the proposed amended pleading still needs to formally demand punitive damages. “(a) A complaint or cross-complaint shall contain both of the following:… (2) A demand for judgment for the relief to which the pleader claims to be entitled. If the recovery of money or damages is demanded, the amount demanded shall be stated.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.10, subd. (a).) The proposed amended pleading must also specify the location of those allegations in the proposed amended pleading. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subd. (a)(2)-(a)(3).

 

Thus, the Court finds that the Amended Motion is deficient. Furthermore, the subject motion is moot in light of the Court’s June 20, 2024 ruling.  The Court acknowledges that Plaintiff is representing herself in pro per; however, pro per litigants “are held to the same standards as attorneys.” Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543. 

Plaintiff’s request for leave to amend is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE.

B.        Motion to Reclassify

“(a) The plaintiff, cross-complainant, or petitioner may file a motion for reclassification within the time allowed for that party to amend the initial pleading. The defendant or cross-defendant may file a motion for reclassification within the time allowed for that party to respond to the initial pleading. The court, on its own motion, may reclassify a case at any time. A motion for reclassification does not extend the moving party's time to amend or answer or otherwise respond. The court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification, regardless of any fault or lack of fault, if the case has been classified in an incorrect jurisdictional classification.

 

(b) If a party files a motion for reclassification after the time for that party to amend that party's initial pleading or to respond to a complaint, cross-complaint, or other initial pleading, the court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification only if both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) The case is incorrectly classified.

(2) The moving party shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.”

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subds. (a)-(b).)

 

            The Court finds that the Amended Motion contains no arguments in support of reclassification of this action from limited civil to unlimited civil. “Contentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority. [Citation.]” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.) The Court will not speculate as to the reasons Plaintiff moved for reclassification. Additionally, in light of the Court’s June 20, 2024 order, there is no operative pleading before the Court.

 

Accordingly, the Court DENIES the Motion to Reclassify without prejudice. 

 

 

II.        Conclusion

 

            In sum, Plaintiff’s Amended Motion for Leave to Amend Complaint to Include Claim for Punitive Damages in Complaint and to Reclassify Under Unlimited Jurisdiction is DENIED.

BACKGROUND

 

This action arises from alleged dental malpractice. On August 29, 2023, Plaintiff Reah Sybirski (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint for Dental Medical Malpractice against Defendant Nikou Zarabian (“Defendant”). The Complaint is handwritten and is set forth on pleading paper.

On May 7, 2024, the Court entered an order relating the instant action to Reah Sybirski v. Vladimir Polyakov., LASC Case No. 23STLC05562 (the “Polyakov Action”), with the Polyakov Action being deemed the lead case. (05/07/24 Minute Order.)  

 

On May 9, 2024, after hearing oral argument, the Court continued the motion in part and denied the motion in part. (05/09/24 Minute Order at p. 4.) The Court continued the hearing on the motion for leave to amend to June 20, 2024, so that Plaintiff could file and serve a supplemental declaration in compliance with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324. (05/09/24 Minute Order at p. 4.) As to Plaintiff’s request for reclassification, the Court denied Plaintiff’s request to reclassify this action to an unlimited jurisdiction action. (05/09/24 Minute Order.)

 

On May 13, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served the instant Amended Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint and to Reclassify Case Under Unlimited Civil Jurisdiction (the “Amended Motion”). Pursuant to the Amended Motion, Plaintiff seeks an order granting leave to file a First Amended Complaint. Plaintiff also seeks an order reclassifying this case under unlimited civil jurisdiction on the grounds that: (1) damages exceed the jurisdictional limits of the Court; and (2) pursuant to CCP § 1008(a), the Court inadvertently overlooked the majority of the damages included in the proposed First Amended Complaint and the motion is timely made.

 

On May 20, 2024, this action was reassigned from Department 26 to Department 25 at Spring Street Courthouse.  

 

On June 11, 2024, Defendant filed and served an opposition to the Amended Motion, to which Plaintiff replied on June 12, 2024.   

 

On June 20, 2024, after hearing oral argument and taking the motion for leave to amend under submission, the Court granted Plaintiff’s Amended Motion for Leave to File a First Amended Complaint. (06/20/24 Minute Order.) The Court ordered Plaintiff to file a clean copy of the proposed First Amended Complaint by July 9, 2024. (06/20/24 Minute Order.)

 

As to the Amended Motion to Reclassify Case Under Unlimited Civil Jurisdiction, the Court continued the hearing on such motion to July 9, 2024. (06/20/24 Minute Order at pp. 2-3.) The Court ordered the parties to submit supplemental briefs by the end of the day on July 2, 2024. (06/20/24 Minute Order at p.3.) The parties were ordered to meet and confer via telephone regarding damages by the end of the day on June 28, 2024. (06/20/24 Minute Order at p. 3.)  

 

On July 2, 2024, Defendant filed and served a declaration of its counsel, Shirley Carpenter Bridwell, Esq. in opposition to Plaintiff’s motion to reclassify this action as unlimited. Also, on July 2, 2024, Plaintiff filed and served a supplemental brief in support of her motion for reconsideration and to reclassify this case under limited jurisdiction.

 

Given that the Court has already granted Plaintiff’s motion for leave to amend, the Court’s analysis below will only address whether: (1) reconsideration of the Court’s ruling on the motion to reclassify is warranted; and (2) reclassification of this action is appropriate.  

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

            As to reclassification, Plaintiff argues that reclassification of this case under unlimited jurisdiction is necessary and submits invoices to the supplemental brief. 

 

OPPOSITION

 

            Defendant asserts that the motion to reclassify was denied and was not continued to June 20, 2024. Defendant further argues that the damages that Plaintiff is claiming in this case overlap with the damages claimed in the related case in Reah Sybirski v. Vladimir Polyakov, LASC 23STLC05562.

 

 

 

 

REPLY

 

            On reply, Plaintiff argues that the request to reconsider reclassification under unlimited jurisdiction should be granted in its entirety.  

 

ANALYSIS

 

I.          Discussion

A.        Request for Reconsideration

            Although the Court previously denied Plaintiff’s request to reclassify this action as an unlimited civil jurisdiction action pursuant to its May 9, 2024 order, Plaintiff asks the Court to reconsider its May 9, 2024 order which denied her request to reclassify this action.

            Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(a) “requires that a motion for reconsideration be based on new or different facts, circumstances, or law.” New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212. “A party seeking reconsideration must also provide a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce the evidence at an earlier time.” Ibid. “A trial court’s ruling on a motion for reconsideration is reviewed under the abuse of discretion standard.” Ibid., citation omitted.  

            California Code of Civil Procedure, Section 1008(a) provides that “[w]hen an application for an order has been made to a judge, or to a court, and refused in whole or in part, or granted, or granted conditionally, or on terms, any party affected by the order may, within 10 days after service upon the party of written notice of entry of the order . . . [may] make application to the same judge or court that made the order, to reconsider the matter and modify, amend, or revoke the prior order.” “The party making the application shall state by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1008, subd. (a).) A trial court has discretion with respect to granting a motion for reconsideration. New York Times Co. v. Superior Court, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212. 

            “The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.” New York Times Co. v. Superior Court, supra, 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-13, citation omitted. A “party seeking reconsideration must provide not only new evidence but also a satisfactory explanation for the failure to produce that evidence at an earlier time.” Mink v. Superior Court (1992) 2 Cal.App.4th 1338, 1342. “If the trial court believes reconsideration is warranted, it can amend, modify or revoke its previous order.” Glade v. Glade (1995) 38 Cal.App.4th 1441, 1457, citation omitted. In the context of motions for reconsideration, there is “a strict requirement of diligence.” Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.    

B.        Appropriateness of Reclassification  

            “The plaintiff . . . may file a motion for reclassification within the time allowed for that party to amend the initial pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (a).) “The court, on its own motion, may reclassify a case at any time.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (a).) “A motion for reclassification does not extend the moving party’s time to amend . . . or otherwise respond.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (a).) Where a motion for reclassification is filed after the time for a party to amend its initial pleading, “the court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification only if both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The case is incorrectly classified. (2) The moving party shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.040, subd. (b)(1)-(2).)

“[A] civil case in which the damages claimed are less than $25,000 or less is a limited civil case.” AP-Colton LLC v. Ohaeri (2015) 240 Cal.App.4th 500, 505. “In a limited civil action, the judgment cannot exceed $25,000.” Ibid. “For reclassification of a case from a limited civil case to an unlimited civil case, a fee shall be charged as provided in Section 70619 of the Government Code. This reclassification fee shall be in addition to any other fee due for that appearance or filing in a limited civil case.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 403.060, subd. (a).) “The fee for reclassification of a case from a limited civil case to an unlimited civil case under Section 403.060 of the Code of Civil Procedure is one hundred forty dollars ($140).” (Gov. Code § 70619.)

“[A] matter may be reclassified as a limited civil action when (i) the absence of jurisdiction is apparent before trial from the complaint, petition, or related documents, or (ii) during the course of pretrial litigation, it becomes clear that the matter will necessarily result in a verdict below the superior court’s jurisdictional amount.” Ytuarte v. Superior Court (2005)129 Cal.App.4th 266, 276, internal quotations omitted. “[T]he test [is] . . . whether lack of jurisdiction is clear . . . [or] virtually unattainable.” Id. at p. 277, citation omitted, internal quotations omitted. “This standard involves an evaluation of the amount fairly in controversy, not an adjudication of the merits of the claim, and . . . requires a high level of certainty that [the] damage award will not exceed $25,000.” Ibid. “The [trial] court may believe it highly unlikely that plaintiff will recover the amount demanded, but this is not enough to defeat jurisdiction, unless it appears to a legal certainty that plaintiff cannot recover the amount [of the] demand[].” Ibid. “[T]he superior court must deny the motion to reclassify the case as limited (and thus keep the matter in the unlimited civil court) unless it appears to a legal certainty that the plaintiff’s damages will necessarily be less than $25,000.” Ibid. 

Effective January 1, 2024, a limited civil action is an action where the amount in controversy does not exceed $35,000. (Code Civ. Proc., § 85, subd. (a).) “A civil action or proceeding other than a limited civil case may be referred to as an unlimited civil case.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 88.) 

Analysis as to Reclassification

The Court may, on its own motion, reclassify an action at any time pursuant to CCP §403.040(a).

Defendant’s counsel, Shirley Carpenter Bridwell (“Bridwell”), states the following: Plaintiff was recently granted leave to file her First Amended Complaint and such pleading claims that Defendant failed to properly diagnose and treat her dental condition. (Bridwell Decl., ¶ 2; Ex. A.) Plaintiff claims damages as a result of Defendant’s treatment of her teeth 2, 4, and 31 for infection and possible fractures. (Id., ¶ 2; Exs. A-B.) Plaintiff’s past medicals are $7,879.00, which is well below the $35,000.00 to reclassify this case to unlimited. (Id., ¶ 3.) Defendant’s counsel then sets forth Plaintiff’s estimated future costs, which overlap between what Plaintiff is claiming in this action and the Polyakov Action. (Id., ¶¶ 3-4.) The reason given for the overlap of future medicals in this case and the Polyakov Action is that Plaintiff is not in control of treatment she is still receiving, but there is no reason why the future medicals in this case cannot be determined by only treatment required to fix teeth 2, 4, and 31 for infection and possible fractures. (Id., ¶ 6.) In the Polyakov Action, Plaintiff is claiming damages to tooth 3 as well, and is claiming infection caused by Defendant and Dr. Polyakov, which means there is an overlap between what Plaintiff is claiming in damages in both cases. (Id., ¶ 7.) Defendant argues that there is an overlap between the future damages in this action and the Polyakov Action and thus Plaintiff’s calculation of future damages is questionable. (Id., ¶¶ 8-9.) Defendant’s counsel attests that Plaintiff fails to show that her damages will exceed $35,000.00 in this action. (Id., ¶ 9.)

In her declaration in support of the motion, Plaintiff declares that her current and future damages exceed the jurisdictional limits of this Court. (Sybirski Decl., ¶ 2.) Specifically, Plaintiff states that her past medical totals $10,264.00; future medical totals $20,250.00; pain and suffering is greater than $80,000.00; and emotional distress is greater than $80,000.00. (Id. ¶ 2(a)-(d).) Plaintiff declares that she has not included any damages from any other action in her damages claimed in the instant action. (Sybirski Decl. in Support of Supplemental Brief, ¶ 2.) Plaintiff states that her damages in this action alone, and not for any other action she has filed, exceed $35,000.00. (Id., ¶ 1.) Plaintiff attests that her “damages include at least $10,264 in past medical, at least $20,250 in future medical, [and] at least $80,000 in pain and suffering and emotional distress damages.” (Id.) Dr. Jay Reznick, D.M.D., M.D., informed Plaintiff that if her laser treatments proved unsuccessful, she would need to have the crowns, implants, and bone grafts completed on teeth numbers 2, 4, and 31. (Id., ¶¶ 3-4.)

            The Court finds that reclassification of this action from a limited civil jurisdiction action to an unlimited civil jurisdiction action is appropriate. The proposed First Amended Complaint indicates that due to the alleged negligent dental work of Defendant, Plaintiff will incur estimated future treatments exceeding $20,250.00. (Bridwell Decl., Ex. A at ¶ 24.) Plaintiff alleges that she has already paid Defendant $5,799.00 for the dental procedures. (Bridwell Decl., Ex. A at ¶ 12.) Plaintiff alleges that Defendant negligently performed dental work on her three molars. (Bridwell Decl., Ex. A at ¶¶ 6, 12, 25-34.) Plaintiff also paid $2,080.00 to another dental provider for infection removal procedures due to Defendant’s alleged negligent dental work. (Bridwell Decl., Ex. A at ¶ 21.) Plaintiff alleges that she has sustained mental, physical, and nervous pain due to Defendant’s actions in an amount that exceeds $80,000.00. (Bridwell Decl., Ex. A at ¶ 31.)

            Based on the proposed First Amended Complaint—which the Court granted Plaintiff leave to file—the Court finds that it is possible that Plaintiff’s damages will exceed $35,000.00. The Court acknowledges the defendant’s argument that the damages in this case overlap with the damages in the related case.  Plaintiff disputes this and declares that she has not included any damages from any other action in this instant action. The Court reminds the parties that “[t]he [trial] court may believe it highly unlikely that plaintiff will recover the amount demanded, but it is not enough to defeat jurisdiction, unless it appears to a legal certainty that plaintiff cannot recover the amount [of the] deman[d].” Maldonado v. Superior Court (1996) 45 Cal.App.4th 397, 401, emphasis in original. Therefore, it does not appear, to legal certainty, that Plaintiff cannot recover more than $35,000.00. 

            Accordingly, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for reclassification.

II.        Conclusion

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Amended Motion to Reclassify Case Under Unlimited Civil Jurisdiction is GRANTED.

 

 The Court reclassifies this action from a limited civil jurisdiction action to an unlimited civil jurisdiction action. Plaintiff is ordered to pay the reclassification fee of $140.00 pursuant to CCP § 403.060(a) before this action will be reclassified to an unlimited civil jurisdiction matter.  

 

            Moving party is ordered to give notice.

A.                Legal Standard

Superior Court (2012) 202 Cal.App.4th 1333, 1339.
