Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

ricardo flores ;

 

Plaintiff,

 

 

vs.

 

 

pine enterprises, llc , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

19STCV31868

 

 

Hearing Date:

October 31, 2023

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to code of civil procedure section 128.7

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                Plaintiff Ricardo Flores

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants Pine Enterprise, LLC and Tony Chang

Motion for Attorney’s Fees and Costs Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure Section 128.7

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Ricardo Flores (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order awarding sanctions in favor of Plaintiff and against defendants Pine Enterprise, LLC, and Tony Chang (“Defendants”) in the amount of $1,760 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7 on the ground that Defendants “intentionally delayed filing their Motion for Summary Adjudication.”  (Notice of Mot., p. 2:1-3.)  Specifically, Plaintiff appears to contend that Defendants’ second motion for summary adjudication, which was heard by the court and denied on October 12, 2023, was frivolous.  (Mot., p. 2:23-25.)

Code of Civil Procedure “[s]ection 128.7 applies only in limited circumstances.  It ‘authorizes trial courts to impose sanctions to check abuses in the filing of pleadings, petitions, written notices of motion or similar papers.’  [Citation.]  Under that authority, trial courts may issue sanctions, including monetary and terminating sanctions, against a party for filing a complaint that is legally or factually frivolous.”  (Kumar v. Ramsey (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 1110, 1120 [internal citation omitted].)  “A claim is factually frivolous if it is ‘ “not well grounded in fact” ’ and it is legally frivolous if it is ‘ “not warranted by existing law or a good faith argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law.” [Citation.]’  [Citation.]  ‘In either case, to obtain sanctions, the moving party must show the party’s conduct in asserting the claim was objectively unreasonable.  [Citation.]  A claim is objectively unreasonable if “any reasonable attorney would agree that [it] is totally and completely without merit.”  [Citation.]’”  (McCluskey v. Henry (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 1197, 1205 [internal citation omitted].)  Sanctions under this section “may consist of, or include, directives of a nonmonetary nature . . . .”  (Code Civ. Proc., §¿128.7, subd. (d).)   

The court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that Defendants’ second motion for summary adjudication was either factually frivolous or legally frivolous.  Although Plaintiff has argued that “a reasonable attorney would agree” that the motion was without merit due to (1) there having been no additional fact discovery following the court’s denial of the first motion, and (2) various issues taken with the evidence submitted by Defendants, Plaintiff has not presented sufficient evidence or argument showing that Defendants’ motion was not well grounded in fact or not warranted by existing law or a good faith argument for the modification or extension of existing law.  (Kumar, supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 1120.)  The court further finds that Plaintiff has not presented sufficient evidence or argument showing that Defendants presented their motion for an improper purpose, such as to harass Plaintiff or cause unnecessary delay or a needless increase in the cost of litigation.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (b)(1).)

Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff has not met his burden to show that Defendants’ second motion for summary adjudication was brought for an improper purpose or was factually or legally frivolous and therefore denies Plaintiff’s motion.

The court denies Defendants’ request for an award of monetary sanctions against Plaintiff pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7, subdivision (h), because (1) a request “for sanctions under this section shall be made separately from other motions or requests[,]” and     (2) Defendants made their request for sanctions under this subdivision against Plaintiff in their opposition papers (i.e., not separate from other motions).[1]  (Code Civ. Proc., § 128.7, subd. (c)(1).) 

ORDER

            The court denies plaintiff Ricardo Flores’s motion for attorney’s fees and costs pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 128.7.

The court orders defendants Pine Enterprise, LLC and Tony Chang to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

defendants’ demurrer to first amended complaint

 

MOVING PARTIES:              Defendants Lil Wave Financial Inc., d/b/a Superior Loan Servicing (erroneously sued separately as Lil Wave Financial Inc. and Superior Loan Servicing), and Funding Rush, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:        Unopposed

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

The court considered the moving papers filed in connection with this demurrer.  No opposition papers were filed.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs Shai Harris, as successor trustee of the H. McClenon Trust dated December 12, 2019 and as trustee of the ADH Harris Trust dated January 14, 2020 (“Plaintiffs”), filed the operative First Amended Complaint in this action on January 3, 2023, against defendants Teresa Gorman Martinez and Ruben Martinez, as trustees of the GM Defined Benefit Pension Plan, Asset Default Management, Inc., Lil Wave Financial, Inc., Superior Loan Servicing, and Funding Rush, Inc.  Plaintiffs allege one cause of action for the administering of an unconscionable contract or clause under U.C.C. section 2-302, alleging that Plaintiffs have been damaged by the defendants’ “administration of an adhesion contract that is unconscionable, as described in U.C.C. § 2-302.”  (FAC, p. 1:24-25.)

Defendants Lil Wave Financial, Inc., d/b/a Superior Loan Servicing (erroneously sued separately as Lil Wave Financial, Inc. and Superior Loan Servicing) and Funding Rush, Inc. (collectively, “Defendants”) move the court for an order sustaining their demurrer to Plaintiffs’ First Amended Complaint.

The court sustains Defendants’ demurrer to the cause of action for the administering of an unconscionable contract or clause under Uniform Commercial Code section 2-302 because it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since there is no affirmative cause of action for unconscionability, which is a defense to the enforcement of a contract.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Jones v. Wells Fargo Bank (2003) 112 Cal.App.4th 1527, 1539 [explaining that there is no cause of action for unconscionability under California Civil Code section 1670.5 because “that doctrine is only a defense to contract enforcement”]; Hines v. US Bankcorp. (C.D. Cal. 2021) 2021 WL 3923248 at *2 [“Though section 2-302 of the UCC has been codified in California, see Cal. Civ. Code § 1670.5, ‘unconscionability “operates as a defense to the enforcement of a contract of adhesion”” and the plaintiffs had not provided authority showing that it is an affirmative cause of action].)  Plaintiffs have not filed opposition papers with the court showing that (1) unconscionability is a valid cause of action, or (2) the facts alleged in the First Amended Complaint are sufficient to support any other legal theory. 

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)¿ The court finds that Plaintiffs have not met their burden to show how they could amend their pleading to render it sufficient and therefore sustains the demurrer without leave to amend.

 

 

ORDER

The court sustains defendants Lil Wave Financial, Inc., d/b/a Superior Loan Servicing (erroneously sued separately as Lil Wave Financial, Inc. and Superior Loan Servicing) and Funding Rush, Inc.’s demurrer to plaintiffs Shai Harris, as successor trustee of the H. McClenon Trust dated December 12, 2019 and as trustee of the ADH Harris Trust dated January 14, 2020’s First Amended Complaint without leave to amend.

The court orders that defendants Lil Wave Financial, Inc., d/b/a Superior Loan Servicing (erroneously sued separately as Lil Wave Financial, Inc. and Superior Loan Servicing) and Funding Rush, Inc. are dismissed with prejudice from this case.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 581, subd. (f)(1).)

The court orders defendants Lil Wave Financial, Inc., d/b/a Superior Loan Servicing (erroneously sued separately as Lil Wave Financial, Inc. and Superior Loan Servicing) and Funding Rush, Inc. to give notice of this ruling.

 

IT IS SO ORDERED.

[Tentative] Order RE:

 

plaintiff’s motion to disqualify counsel

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Plaintiff Eduardo Luna          

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association, Inc., and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association, Inc. (sued as Doe defendant 1)

Motion to Disqualify Counsel

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.[1]

DISCUSSION

Plaintiff Eduardo Luna (“Plaintiff”) moves the court for an order disqualifying Shawn M. Olson and the Olson Law Firm (“Counsel”) as counsel for defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association, and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association (sued as Doe defendant 1) (collectively, “Defendants”), or any other party in this action.

Plaintiff moves for this relief on the ground that (1) the Complaint alleges derivative claims on behalf of defendants Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association (sued as Doe 1) (collectively referred to as “Villa HOA”); (2) the breach of fiduciary duty causes of action are alleged against members of the board of Villa HOA; and (3) the derivative claims make the Villa HOA entities nominal defendants and plaintiffs in this action, such that Counsel is “representing both Plaintiffs and Defendants in the same lawsuit.” (Notice of Mot., p. 2, ¶¶ 1-3.)

“A trial court’s authority to disqualify an attorney derives from the power inherent in every court ‘[t]o control in furtherance of justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto.’”  (People ex rel. Dept. of Corporations v. SpeeDee Oil Change Systems, Inc. (1999) 20 Cal.4th 1135, 1145.)  “The paramount concern [when resolving disqualification motions] must be to preserve public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar.”  (Ibid.) 

The court finds that (1) the Complaint does not allege facts sufficient to support any claims brought derivatively on behalf of Villa HOA, and (2) therefore the argument and evidence presented by the parties do not show that disqualification of Counsel based on concurrent representation is required.

As set forth above, Plaintiff asserts that disqualification is required because Counsel is deemed to be representing both plaintiffs and defendants in this action since the Complaint alleges derivative claims on behalf of Villa HOA.  (Notice of Mot., p. 2, ¶¶ 1-3; Mot., pp. 1:8-11 [“The complaint raises derivative claims, so the [Villa HOA entities] actually stand[] in the position as a plaintiff[,]” but one attorney cannot represent “both sides in that situation”], 2:3-4, 2:6 [“Disqualification is Mandatory Based [o]n The Derivative Claims”].)  Specifically, Plaintiff asserts that there exist the following conflicts: (1) between defendants Dawn Kuykendall (“D. Kuykendall”) and Jerilyn Kuykendall Rivetti (“J. Rivetti”), on the one hand, and Villa HOA on the other hand, based on the first two causes of action alleging that the individual defendants breached the fiduciary duties to Villa HOA, and (2) between defendants Rodney Owens, BJD Services, LLC, and Brian McWilliams, on one hand, and Villa HOA on the other hand, on the ground that the individual defendants are alleged to have aided and abetted D. Kuykendall and J. Rivetti’s breaches of fiduciary duties to the Villa HOA entities.  (Mot., p. 3:4-8, 5:6-20.)

Thus, Plaintiff’s motion is solely based on the theory that the Complaint alleges derivative claims on behalf of the Villa HOA entities, which has the legal effect of including them in this lawsuit as both plaintiffs and nominal defendants, such that Counsel is concurrently representing parties adverse to each other.  However, the court finds that the Complaint does not allege facts sufficient to constitute derivative claims.

“An action is derivative, that is, in the corporate right, ‘ “if the gravamen of the complaint is injury to the corporation, or to the whole body of its stock and property without any severance or distribution among individual holders, or it seeks to recover assets for the corporation or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.” ’”  (Everest Investors 8 v. McNeil Partners (2003) 114 Cal.App.4th 411, 425.) 

First, the court finds that the first, second, fifth, and sixth causes of action do not state facts sufficient to support derivative claims brought on behalf of Villa HOA because those causes of action (1) are expressly brought by the plaintiff(s) in their individual or personal capacities, and (2) do not seek to recover damages suffered by the corporations.  (Compl., pp. 5:14-16 [first cause of action is brought “By Plaintiffs Personally”] [emphasis added], 7:8-10 [second cause of action is brought “By Plaintiffs Personally”] [emphasis added], 9:24-26 [fifth cause of action is brought “By Plaintiff Mr. Luna” and against Villa HOA], 11:2-4 [sixth cause of action is brought “By Plaintiff Luna”]; Compl., ¶¶ 17-19, 24-25, 38, 43-45.) 

Second, although the third and fourth causes of action appear to allege facts that would support derivative claims on behalf of Villa HOA for damages suffered by the corporations, these causes of action are expressly brought “By Plaintiffs Personally” and do not state that they are derivative claims on behalf of the Villa HOA entities.  (Compl., ¶¶ 29-30 [alleging that certain of the defendants breached their fiduciary duties to Villa HOA by failing to seek competitive bidding for maintenance], 33-34 [alleging aiding and abetting of the breaches of fiduciary duties]; Compl., pp. 8:4-6, 9:5-7; Cal. Rules of Ct., rule 2.112 [“Each separately stated cause of action . . . must specifically state: [¶¶] (3) The party asserting it if more than one party is represented on the pleading”].)  Moreover, the Complaint’s prayer asserts relief in favor of the plaintiffs and does not seek relief on behalf of Villa HOA.  (Compl., p. 11:22-23 [“Plaintiffs seek a judgment against Defendants”] [emphasis added].)

The court therefore finds that the gravamen of each cause of action of the Complaint is not “injury to the corporation,” and further finds that no cause of action “seeks to recover assets for [Villa HOA] or to prevent the dissipation of its assets.”  (Everest Investors 8, supra, 114 Cal.App.4th at p. 425.)  Thus, because (1) Plaintiff seeks relief solely on the ground that the Complaint seeks derivative claims, but (2) the Complaint does not allege facts sufficient to constitute derivative claims brought on behalf of Villa HOA, the court finds that the pleadings and evidence do not show the existence of a conflict of interest based on concurrent representation that requires the disqualification of Counsel.

ORDER

The court denies plaintiff Eduardo Luna’s motion to disqualify counsel.

The court orders defendants BJD Services, LLC, Dawn Kuykendall, Jerilyn Rivetti Kuykendall, Brian McWilliams, Rodney Owens, Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association, and Villa Patrician Home Owner’s Association (sued as Doe defendant 1) to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
