Case Number: 21TRCV00786    Hearing Date: July 2, 2024    Dept: B

MILESTONE EQUIPMENT COMPANY LLC, et al. vs NEXXGEN ENTERPRISES, INC., et al.

Case No. 21TRCV00786

This matter is before the Court on an Order to Show Cause re Entry of Default Judgment.  Plaintiff has submitted materials to support the default judgment against the two remaining defendants, Nexgen Enterprises, Inc (“Nexgen”) and 3 Ocelots, Incorporated (“3 Ocelots”).  The Court shall enter judgment as to Defendant Nexgen and 3 Ocelots with certain additional corrections to the proposed Judgment.

1.  The proposed judgment needs to be revised to add the language from JUD-100:

Defendants were properly served with a copy of the summons and complaint.

Defendants failed to answer the complaint or appear and defend the action within the time allowed by law.

Defendants' defaults were entered by the clerk upon plaintiff's application.

Assuming that the above is added to the Proposed judgment, the Court shall enter judgment against Nexgen in all other respects as requested in the Proposed Judgment lodged March 22, 2024.

The Court also finds that the attorneys fees requested, while substantial, are reasonable given the totality of the facts surrounding this case.

 

Plaintiff ordered to modify the Proposed Judgment as noted and resubmit it within 10 days.

Hearing Date:                          July 2, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Compel Deposition of Plaintiff

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

The motion to compel deposition is GRANTED. 

Plaintiff Esther Barbara Batindira Kibazze is ordered to appear for deposition within twenty days either remotely or in person at a mutually agreeable date and time.

Plaintiff is ordered to pay sanctions to defendant in the amount of $487.50, within thirty days.

BACKGROUND

            On March 6, 2023, plaintiff Esther Barbara Batindira Kibazze filed a complaint against 24 Hour Fitness USA, Inc. for general negligence and premises liability based on an incident that occurred on October 17, 2022.  She alleges that while she was exercising at the gym, she was seriously injured while using the arm and shoulder press machine when the 100 lb plate tumbled down and injured her.

On June 23, 2023, defendant filed an answer.

On June 28, 2023, the parties stipulated, and the court ordered that the prayer for punitive damages be stricken.

On March 28, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            CCP §2025.450(a) provides:  “If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, . . , without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document . . . described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent’s attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document . . . described in the deposition notice.”

CCP §2025.450(b) provides, “A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:

(1)        The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.

(2)        The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”

CCP §2025.230 states:  “If the deponent named is not a natural person, the deposition notice shall describe with reasonable particularity the matters on which examination is requested. In that event, the deponent shall designate and produce at the deposition those of its officers, directors, managing agents, employees, or agents who are most qualified to testify on its behalf as to those matters to the extent of any information known or reasonably available to the deponent.”

DISCUSSION

Defendant requests that the Court compel plaintiff to appear for her deposition.

            Defendant explains that on November 29, 2023, defendant served a notice of taking plaintiff’s deposition and demand for production of documents and things for January 9, 2024.  The deposition was continued to March 5, 2024, at the request of plaintiff’s counsel.  The deposition was then taken off calendar because plaintiff’s counsel was purportedly having difficulties with his client.  On April 4, 2024, defendant served another notice of taking deposition for April 22, 2024.  Plaintiff failed to appear for her deposition and a certificate of non-appearance was taken.  On April 29, 2024, defense counsel emailed and called plaintiff in an effort to meet and confer and requested a response by May 3, 2024.  Defense counsel states that they have not received any communication from plaintiff.

            There is no opposition.

            The Court finds that the notice of deposition was properly served, and that plaintiff failed to appear.  Plaintiff did not serve objections. 

            The motion is thus GRANTED.

            As for sanctions, defendant requests $1,365.  The request assumed that there was time devoted to preparing a reply.  There was no need for that.  The Court finds that $487.50 ($195/hr. x 2.5 hrs.) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against plaintiff.

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED. 

Plaintiff is ordered to appear for her deposition within twenty days either remotely or in person at a mutually agreeable date and time.

Plaintiff is ordered to pay sanctions to defendant’s attorney in the amount of $487.50, within thirty days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                          July 2, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Torrance Care Center West, Inc.

Responding Party:                  None

Motion for an Order to Deem Admitted Requests for Admission

            The Court considered the moving papers.

 RULING

The motion is GRANTED.  The Court orders that the truth of any matters specified in defendant’s requests for admission be deemed admitted.

Plaintiffs are ordered to pay a monetary sanction to defendant in the amount of $490 within 30 days.

BACKGROUND

On June 13, 2023, Lonnie Lee Gipson and Betty Gipson, ind. and as guardian ad litem of Lonnie Lee Gipson, filed a complaint against Torrance Care Center West, Inc. for (1) statutory elder abuse/neglect, (2) violation of Patient’s Bill of Rights and Health and Safety Codes, (3) negligence, and (4) unfair business practices.

On March 18, 2024, the court sustained defendant’s demurrer to the first, second, and fourth causes of action with lave to amend.  To date, plaintiffs have not filed a FAC.  The court granted plaintiffs’ counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Pursuant to CCP § 2033.280(b), a party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).  “Failure to timely respond to RFA does not result in automatic admissions.  Rather, the propounder of the RFA must ‘move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted, as well as for a monetary sanction’ under § 2023.010 et seq.”  Weil & Brown, Civ. Proc. Before Trial, ¶ 8:1370, citing CCP § 2033.280(b).  The court “shall” grant the motion to deem RFA admitted, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”  CCP § 2033.280(c).

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Torrance Care Center West, Inc. requests that the Court deem admitted the truth of the matters in defendant’s Requests for Admission, Set One, as against plaintiffs.

            Defendant asserts that on December 11, 2023, defendant served plaintiffs with written discovery.  Responses were due by January 15, 2024.  Subsequent thereto, defendant extended the time for plaintiffs to provide responses on three separate occasions, with the last extension granted on January 30, 2024, with a due date of April 15, 2024.  On May 14, 2024, defense counsel attempted to contact Betty Gipson by phone and sent a meet and confer letter by email and registered letter, offering to provide an extension to May 22, 2024.  After receiving no response, defense counsel attempted to meet and confer and left a voice mail message.  To date, defense counsel has not received responses.

            There is no opposition.

            The Court finds that defendant properly served written discovery and plaintiffs failed to timely serve responses.

            Accordingly, the motion is GRANTED.

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

It is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction on the party or attorney whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated a motion to deem them admitted. CCP § 2033.280(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

            Defendant requests $1,060 in sanctions against plaintiffs.  The Court finds that $490 ($215/hr. x 2 hrs., filing fee) is a reasonable amount to be imposed against plaintiffs.

ORDER

The motion is GRANTED.

The Court orders that the truth of any matters specified in defendant’s requests for admission be deemed admitted.

The Court orders that plaintiffs pay a monetary sanction to defendant in the amount of $490 within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

DEL AMO BEHAVIORAL HEALTH SYSTEM,

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Del Amo Behavioral Health System

Responding Party:                  Plaintiff Rafael Ortega

Demurrer to Complaint

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND to allege two separate causes of action, one based on medical malpractice and one for wrongful death.

BACKGROUND

            On March 5, 2024, plaintiff Rafael Ortega, individually and as successor in interest for Nadia Monique Ortega, filed a complaint against Del Amo Behavioral Health System for a single cause of action.  The caption to the Complaint reads “Complaint for Damages for Medical Malpractice Wrongful Death/Successor in Interest.”  In the body of the Complaint it is labeled “First Cause of Action for Medical Negligence by Plaintiff.”

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

Defendant demurs to the sole cause of action for medical negligence/wrongful death on the grounds that it is uncertain and does not comply with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.112.

The complaint alleges that decedent Nadia Ortega was an involuntary in-patient at Del Amo Hospital beginning on December 5, 2022, after she was transferred from another hospital for a Welf. & Inst. §5150 hold because she was in danger to herself and or others and or was gravely disabled; to wit, could not provide her own food, shelter, or clothing.  Complaint, ¶9.  From the time of admission on December 5, 2022 to the time of her death on December 10, 2022, plaintiff was under the custody and care of defendant pursuant to an involuntary hold under Welf. & Inst. Code §5150 extended by Welf. & Inst. Code §5250.  Decedent was diagnosed as bipolar and exhibited manic episodes.  Medications were provided to plaintiff’s decedent including Haldol, Ativan, and Benadryl.  Id., ¶10.  On December 10, 2022, at 06:45 hrs., decedent was found unresponsive; attempts at resuscitation were unsuccessful.  She was pronounced deceased at 07:07 hrs.  Id., ¶11.  The medical, nursing, and ancillary health care providers, all agents and employees of defendants, fell below the standard of practice in the community for similar health care providers under similar circumstances.  Defendants inappropriately and negligently provided care and treatment and monitored her welfare, which resulted in her death.  Id., ¶12.

Defendant argues that the cause of action is uncertain “as to what is alleged by each plaintiff and the claims asserted by the two plaintiffs.”  Defendant contends that although plaintiff is asserting a single cause of action, it “appears to encompass two causes of action and includes allegations of medical negligence/survivor action as well as wrongful death.” 

In opposition, plaintiff contends that he clearly set forth his standing as heir and successor in interest, the theory of recovery, and the damages claimed, and thus, plaintiff argues, he has provided sufficient notice to defendant of plaintiff’s claim, which is for medical malpractice.

In reply, defendant reiterates its argument that “each cause of action should be separately stated” and “must identify the parties asserting the claim” in compliance with Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 2.112, contending that “plaintiff must either omit one of the plaintiffs, or clarify that there are two causes of action.”

Although the Defendant’s challenge is a demurrer for uncertainty, Defendant argues that the pleading is defective as it fails to comply with California Rule of Court 2.112.  This argument perhaps should have been presented by way of a Motion to Strike pursuant to C.C.P. § 436(b) as the pleading was not “drawn in conformity with the laws of this state, a rule of court, ….”  However, the Court is granted its own authority to strike pleadings on its own motion.  The result is the same, whether the attack is by way of demurrer or motion to strike.  The Pleading is defective.

The Court notes the benefit to not only the Defendant, but to the Plaintiff and the Court of addressing at this point in time the deficiency in the cause(s) of action asserted.  Clarity as to the potential claim or claims that are being asserted is of value for all, and particularly for Plaintiff.  Different claims require different elements and different jury instructions.  Different claims may give rise to different defenses.  Having clarity from the beginning of the case, so that the parties may properly research the claims, conduct discovery on the relevant factual issues raised by the claims and defenses, and, if ultimately necessary, propose jury instructions on each of the distinct legal claims asserted is valuable.  Hence the Court is rather surprised that Plaintiff has not chosen to take advantage of the demurrer in order to prepare a First Amended Complaint that clearly identifies the claim or claims that he is attempting to set forth.

Because the Complaint fails to set forth the cause or causes of action sufficiently and separately the Court shall sustain the demurrer both for uncertainty and because it does not plead the claim or claims sufficiently to alert the Defendant to the claim that must be defended.

The demurrer is thus SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

ORDER

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH TWENTY DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND to separately allege each cause of action that the Plaintiff desires to assert, whether one (as presently identified) or two or more.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
