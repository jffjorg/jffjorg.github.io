DEPARTMENT 27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV00797    Hearing Date: June 17, 2024    Dept: 27

Hon. Lee S. Arian, Dept 27

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS

Hearing Date: 6/17/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV00797 ARLET TALAMANTES vs DAVID ALEXANDER MELENDEZ

Moving Party: Defendants David and Mauricio Melendez

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER AND REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS ARE DENIED.

 

On June 12, 2024, the Court heard oral arguments relating to the timeliness of the present motion to compel further responses.  The Court continued the motion to June 17, 2024, to further consider the timeliness arguments, as well as the substantive arguments raised by the motion.  It now finds that under the plain language of the statute, the present motion was timely filed. 

Plaintiff seeks to compel further responses to the following 2 requests:

REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 28: Please produce the raw Electronic Data Recorder information downloaded from the 2019 Fiat 500, bearing CA license plate number 8NDN707 on January 9, 2024.

REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION NO. 29: Please produce any and all photos taken of the 2019 Fiat 500, bearing CA license plate number 8NDN707 on January 9, 2024.

Defendant makes the following objections to Plaintiff’s requests:

Responding Party objects to this Request on the following grounds: (1) It is vague and ambiguous. (2) It is premature. (3) It seeks to violate the attorney-client privilege and attorney work product doctrine. (4) It calls for premature expert discovery. The time for expert discovery has not yet arrived. Experts are scheduled to be designated in this case on May 23, 2024.

Defendant's main argument is that, similar to witness statements obtained by opposing counsel, EDR data and photographs, although they are not mental impressions or case strategies (which enjoy absolute protection), are nonetheless protected by the qualified work product privilege. Defendant  relies on the reasoning in Coito v. Superior Court (2012) 54 Cal.4th 480, 496-97:

First, when an attorney obtains through discovery a witness statement obtained by opposing counsel through his or her own initiative, such discovery undermines the Legislature's policy to ‘[p]revent attorneys from taking undue advantage of their adversary's industry and efforts.’” (§ 2018.020, subd. (b).) Even when an attorney exercises no selectivity in determining which witnesses to interview, and even when the attorney simply records each witness's answer to a single question (“What happened?”), the attorney has expended time and effort in identifying and locating each witness, securing the witness's willingness to talk, listening to what the witness said, and preserving the witness's statement for possible future use. An attorney who seeks to discover what a witness knows is not without recourse. The attorney is free to interview the witness for himself or herself to find out what information the witness has that is relevant to the litigation. …Absent a showing that a witness is no longer available or accessible, or some other showing of unfair prejudice or injustice (§ 2018.030, subd. (b)), the Legislature's declared policy is to prevent an attorney from free-riding on the industry and efforts of opposing counsel (§ 2018.020, subd. (b)). (

Second, a default rule authorizing discovery of witness statements procured by an attorney would impede the Legislature's intent ‘to encourage [attorneys] to prepare their cases thoroughly and to investigate not only the favorable but the unfavorable aspects of those cases.’ (§ 2018.020, subd. (a).) If attorneys must worry about discovery whenever they take a statement from a witness, it is reasonably foreseeable that fewer witness statements will be recorded and that adverse information will not be memorialized. As Justice Kane observed below, without work product protection, ‘no meaningful privacy exists within which an attorney may have sufficient confidence to thoroughly investigate and record potentially unfavorable matters.’ This result would derogate not only from an attorney's duty and prerogative to investigate matters thoroughly, but also from the truth-seeking values that the rules of discovery are intended to promote. Accordingly, we hold that a witness statement obtained through an attorney-directed interview is, as a matter of law, entitled to at least qualified work product protection.

When there is qualified work product, the burden is on the party seeking discovery to establish adequate reasons to justify production, such as unavailability or inaccessibility of the witnesses. 

Here, the EDR data and photographs, similar to witness statements, are factual in nature. Defendant expended significant efforts to acquire the EDR data and photographs: arranging for access to the vehicle, hiring an expert to download the EDR data, hiring a professional to take the photos, determining which parts of the car to photograph, and ensuring that these materials were preserved for potential future use.  Thus, these items are entitled to qualified work product protection.

 

Plaintiff is not without recourse. The vehicle in question is Plaintiff’s car, and Plaintiff has unrestricted access to it. Plaintiff has the ability to download the EDR data and take photographs of the car. Plaintiff can directly obtain the same factual information without relying on Defendant’s efforts. Plaintiff has not demonstrated any unavailability or inaccessibility of the car, the EDR data, or the ability to take photographs, which are essential to justify production under the qualified work product doctrine.

 

Moreover, Plaintiff’s reply did not address Coito v. Superior Court or the qualified work product privilege as asserted by Defendant. Plaintiff argues that the EDR data and photographs are not mental impressions or case strategies; however, these arguments pertain to absolute work product privilege, which is not the basis of Defendant's argument.

 

Given the accessibility of the vehicle to Plaintiff, the lack of adequate reasons to justify the production, and the protection afforded by the qualified work product privilege, Defendant has raised meritorious objections and Plaintiff's motion to compel is DENIED. Sanctions are likewise DENIED.

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION; MOTION TO EXTEND TIME OF DEPOSITION; REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 6/17/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 21STCV28557 MARIA ELENA ESPINOZA vs LUIS CORNEJO, et al.

Moving Party: Defendants Luis Cornejo and On Track Transportation

Responding Party: Plaintiff 

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITION IS DENIED; MOTION TO EXTEND TIME OF DEPOSITION IS GRANTED; REQUESTS FOR SANCTIONS IS DENIED

 

Background

 

Defendants have taken Plaintiff’s deposition three times, but each session was cut short by Plaintiff and had to be rescheduled. When Defendants scheduled a fourth deposition, Plaintiff refused to attend. Defendants now move the Court to compel Plaintiff to attend her fourth deposition and to permit an additional five hours of deposition beyond the seven-hour time limit.

 

Legal Standard 

 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450

¿¿¿¿ 

Any party may obtain any discovery of information, documents, land, property, or electronically stored information so long as the discoverable matter is not privileged, is relevant to the subject matter and can lead one to admissible evidence.¿(Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450¿provides in pertinent part the following:¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

“(a) If, after service of a deposition notice, a party to the action or an officer, director, managing agent, or employee of a party, or a person designated by an organization that is a party under Section 2025.230, without having served a valid objection under Section 2025.410, fails to appear for examination, or to proceed with it, or to produce for inspection any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice, the party giving the notice may move for an order compelling the deponent's attendance and testimony, and the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

(b) A motion under subdivision (a) shall comply with both of the following:¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿ 

(1) The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the production for inspection of any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing described in the deposition notice.¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

(2) The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040¿or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. 

  

Where a motion to compel a party’s appearance and testimony at deposition is granted, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of the party who noticed the deposition and against the deponent, unless the court finds the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)  On motion of a party who, in person or by attorney, attended at the time and place specified in the deposition notice in the expectation that the deponent’s testimony would be taken, the court shall impose a monetary sanction in favor of that party and against the deponent.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.450, subd. (g)(2).) 

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 2025.290

 

Code of Civil Procedure Section 2025.290 requires courts to allow additional time, beyond the seven-hour limit, to depose a witness, unless the court, in its discretion, determines that the deposition should be limited for some reason. (Certainteed Corporation v. Sup. Ct. (2014) 222 Cal.App.4th 1053, 1062.)  

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2025.290 not only authorizes a judge to allow additional time to depose a witness in these circumstances, but requires the judge to do so, unless the judge determines that the deposition should be limited for another reason, such as the deponent's medical condition or to protect any party, deponent, or other natural person or organization from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, oppression, undue burden, or expense. (CCP § 2025.290(c); Certainteed, supra, 222 CA4th at 1062.) 

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff's sole argument in opposition to Defendants’ motion to compel is that Defendants did not fulfill the meet and confer requirement prior to filing the motion.

 

The Court disagrees. On April 11, 2024, Defendants requested to continue the deposition since it was terminated prematurely by Plaintiff. Plaintiff did not attempt to meet and confer, instead stating that if Defendants wanted to depose Plaintiff for the fourth time, they could file a regularly noticed motion, which Plaintiff would oppose. The email exchanges show that Defendants attempted to reach an informal resolution, but Plaintiff urged Defendants to file a motion. The Court finds that Defendants have met their meet and confer obligations prior to filing the motion. Furthermore, communications between the parties after the motion was filed showed that the parties seem to be at an impasse on whether Defendants are entitled to five additional hours of deposition or just two, as proposed by Plaintiff, beyond the statutory 7-hour limit.

 

Although on April 11, 2024, Defendants made an informal request for Plaintiff’s deposition, Defendants did not serve Plaintiff with a deposition notice for Plaintiff’s fourth deposition. A motion to compel deposition can only be made after service of a deposition notice. The Court cannot grant the present motion to compel deposition when no deposition notice was served on Plaintiff.

Time calculated for the 7-hour rule is time on the record. As for the length of the deposition, Plaintiff did not present any reason why the deposition should not be allowed to exceed the seven-hour limit. The opposition only contests the present motion on the basis of the meet and confer requirement. Given that Plaintiff is claiming in excess of $1 million in damages alleging traumatic brain injuries and the presence of a Spanish interpreter is slowing down the deposition, Defendants’ request for an additional five hours is reasonable and therefore granted.

The parties are to meet and confer on Plaintiff’s deposition dates given the new time limit. The motion is granted in part and denied in part.

 

 The Court denies sanctions as requested by both parties, finding that both parties acted with substantial justification.

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT 

Hearing Date: 6/17/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 23STCV26107 JAMES PHILLIP THOMAS, et al. vs CITY OF LOS ANGELES, et al.

Moving Party: DEFENDANTS GARDEN VIEW AND WILSON SUMMIT HOMEOWNERS ASSOCIATION; SUBDIVISION MANAGEMENT CONSULTANTS; GWEN TIBBS

Responding Party: Plaintiff and City of Los Angeles

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling:

 

HOA DEFENDANTS’ MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS DENIED.

GARDEN VIEW’S MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT IS GRANTED.

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff James Phillip Thomas alleges that on June 24, 2023, he tripped and fell due to a mis-leveled portion of the sidewalk at 4299 Via Marisol in the Monterey Hills area of Los Angeles. Defendants Wilson Summit Homeowners Association, Subdivision Management Consultants and Gwen Tibbs (collectively, the “HOA Defendants”) own and maintain the property abutting the sidewalk in question. Defendant Garden View, Inc. is the landscaping company contracted by the HOA Defendants to provide landscaping services. Plaintiff sued both the HOA Defendants and Garden View for negligence and premises liability. Both the HOA Defendants and Garden View now separately move the court for summary judgment against Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

 

HOA Defendants’ objections to evidence submitted by the City of Los Angeles: Nos. 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, and 7 are overruled.

 

HOA Defendants’ objections to evidence submitted by Plaintiff: Nos. 3, 9, 10, 12, and 15 are overruled.

 

Plaintiff’s objection to evidence submitted by Defendant Garden View: Nos. 6, 13, 14, 16, 17, and 19 are overruled.

 

The parties did not make any other objections to evidence that was material to Court’s ruling on the Motion for Summary Judgment. (CCP § 437c(q).)

 

LEGAL STANDARD¿ 

¿ 

In reviewing a motion for summary judgment, courts must apply a three-step analysis: “(1) identify the issues framed by the pleadings; (2) determine whether the moving party has negated the opponent’s claims; and (3) determine whether the opposition has demonstrated the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”¿(Hinesley v. Oakshade Town Center (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 289, 294.) 

 

“[T]he initial burden is always on the moving party to make a prima facia showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.”¿(Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1519.)¿A defendant moving for summary judgment or summary adjudication “has met his or her burden of showing that a cause of action has no merit if the party has shown that one or more elements of the cause of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to the cause of action.”¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).)¿If the moving party fails to carry its burden, the inquiry is over, and the motion must be denied. (See Id.; see also Consumer Cause, Inc. v. SmileCare (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 454, 468.)¿Even if the moving party does carry its burden, the non-moving party will still defeat the motion by presenting evidence of a triable issue of material fact. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849-50.) 

 

To meet this burden of showing a cause of action cannot be established, a defendant must show not only “that the plaintiff does not possess needed evidence” but also that “the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence.”¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 854.)¿It is insufficient for the defendant to merely point out the absence of evidence.¿(Gaggero v. Yura (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 884, 891.)¿The defendant “must also produce evidence that the plaintiff cannot reasonably obtain evidence to support his or her claim.”¿(Ibid.)¿The supporting evidence can be in the form of affidavits, declarations, admissions, depositions, answers to interrogatories, and matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿(Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at p. 855.) 

 

“Once the defendant … has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff … to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2).) The plaintiff may not merely rely on allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists, but instead, “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action.”¿(Ibid.)¿“If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.”¿(Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467.) 

 

The court must “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve all doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party,” including “all inferences reasonably drawn therefrom.”¿(Yanowitz v. L’Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028, 1037; Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at pp. 844-45.) “On a summary judgment motion, the court must therefore consider what inferences favoring the opposing party a factfinder could reasonably draw from the evidence.¿While viewing the evidence in this manner, the court must bear in mind that its primary function is to identify issues rather than to determine issues.¿[Citation.]¿Only when the inferences are indisputable may the court decide the issues as a matter of law.¿ If the evidence is in conflict, the factual issues must be resolved by trial.”¿(Binder v. Aetna Life Ins. Co. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 832, 839.)¿Further, “the trial court may not weigh the evidence in the manner of a factfinder to determine whose version is more likely true.¿[Citation.]¿Nor may the trial court grant summary judgment based on the court’s evaluation of credibility. [Citation.]” (Id. at p. 840; see also Weiss v. People ex rel. Department of Transportation (2020) 9 Cal.5th 840, 864 [“Courts deciding motions for summary judgment or summary adjudication may not weigh the evidence but must instead view it in the light most favorable to the opposing party and draw all reasonable inferences in favor of that party”].) 

 

SIDEWALK ACCIDENT DECISIONS DOCTRINE

 

Defendants rely on the “Sidewalk Accident Decisions” doctrine (the “sidewalk doctrine”), which governs property adjacent to a sidewalk. The sidewalk doctrine is based on case law explaining that sidewalks are public and thus adjacent landowners owe no duty to users of the sidewalk unless that property owner created the hazardous condition. Streets and Highways Code §5610 provides that owners of lots fronting on a public street shall maintain any sidewalk in a condition that will not endanger persons and not interfere with the public convenience in the use of those works.  Pursuant to Jones v. Deeter (1984) 152 Cal.App.3d 798, 803, the duty imposed by this ordinance is owed to the city and not to persons using the sidewalk. “Under section 5610 the abutting owner bears the duty to repair defects in the sidewalk, regardless of whether he has created these defects. It was felt, however, that it would be unfair for such an owner to be held liable to travelers injured as a result of sidewalk defects which were not of the owner’s making. Thus, the ‘Sidewalk Accident Decisions’ doctrine arose; this doctrine holds that the abutting property owner is not liable in tort to travelers injured on the sidewalk unless the owner somehow creates the injurious sidewalk condition.” (Deeter, 152 Cal.App.3d at 803.)

 

While generally a landowner is not liable for harm resulting from conditions or activities outside his property, on adjoining land or on the highway, street, or sidewalk, the landowner may be liable if the landowner created the defect or exercised dominion or control over the adjoining land (Jordan v. City of Sacramento (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1490-1491; Corcoran v. San Mateo (1953) 122 Cal.App.2d 355, 359; Schaefer v. Lenahan (1944) 63 Cal.App.2d 324, 326.)¿In settings where the abutting owners exercised control over the property such as by planting trees or habitually trimming or caring for them, these abutting owners have the duty to maintain the trees in a safe condition to make sure the roots do not cause a tripping hazard. (Alcaraz v. Vece (1997) 14 Cal.4th 1149, 1162.)

 

Furthermore, “a defendant need not own, possess and control property in order to be held liable; control alone is sufficient.” (Id.) A person is required to maintain “land in their possession and control in a reasonably safe condition.” (Ann M. v. Pacific Plaza Shopping Center (1993) 6 Cal.4th 666, 674.)¿ In Alcaraz, the court held that because the defendant maintained the lawn surrounding the meter box, a reasonable trier of fact could infer that the defendant exercised control over that strip of land and had a duty to warn others of the hazardous condition of the meter box.¿(Alcaraz v. Vece, 14 Cal.4th at 1162.)

 

MOVING PARTIES’ INITIAL BURDEN 

Both moving parties have met their initial burden of showing that they did not own or breach any duty of care to Plaintiff. The HOA Defendants demonstrated that the sidewalk where the incident occurred is owned and maintained by the city, and that the tree alleged to have caused the displacement of the sidewalk is also controlled by the city (HOA UMF 7, 10). Additionally, a permit is required to trim the tree or perform other maintenance tasks on it (HOA UMF 11). Similarly, Garden View presented evidence that the tree in question is maintained and controlled by the city (Garden View UMF 5-6). Garden View did not perform any services on the tree in question (Garden View UMF 18); its services in the general area near the tree consist only of leaf blowing and shrub trimming (Garden View UMF 17). There is a sprinkler near the tree, but it is oriented to direct water away from the tree. Both parties demonstrated that they neither owned nor controlled the sidewalk in question, nor did they maintain the tree adjacent to the sidewalk whose roots caused the displacement.

TRIABLE ISSUE OF FACT

        HOA Defendants

Plaintiff introduced a declaration from his expert Kay Greeley, who opined that water from a sprinkler, shooting up the hill behind the tree, migrated downward, thereby overwatering the tree and leading to the overgrowth of roots which caused the displacement of the sidewalk. (Plf’s Ex. B, Greeley Decl. ¶¶ 9-11.) The court agrees with the HOA Defendants that Plaintiff's expert's declaration regarding this cause is speculative. However, the HOA's reply seemed to overlook Plaintiff's strongest evidence that "the drainage pipe next to the tree well wall, terminating into the river rock, caused concentrated drainage of water immediately adjacent to the subject tree and sidewalk, leading to increased root growth and more voids underneath the sidewalk. The offset sidewalk at the incident location is direct evidence of voids (underneath the sidewalk and above the soil) caused by tree roots uplifting the sidewalk slab." (Greeley Decl. ¶ 14). It is Plaintiff's expert's opinion that the drainage pipe caused concentrated drainage immediately next to the tree well wall and sidewalk, exacerbating tree root growth, which led to the sidewalk uplift (Greeley Decl. ¶15). Unlike the speculative claims about water migrating back down the slope, Plaintiff's expert's statement regarding the drainage pipe appears plausible and not merely speculative to the court.  Given that the Court is required to draw all inferences in favor of the non-moving party, it appears this evidence is sufficient to rebut Plaintiff’s prima facie case.

Guy Stiver, City of Los Angeles’ expert, also reached a similar conclusion relating to the drainage from the pool. Stiver “observed water drainage from pool equipment had flowed over the concrete floor edge and onto HOA slope. At the east end of the pool equipment room water drainage had eroded the slope causing a sizable 4 feet wide by 2 feet deep gulley. At this location, I observed a clear one half inch drain pipe with a continuous flow of water I estimated at .2 gallon per minute. At the west end of the equipment room concrete floor I observed another one half inch diameter clear pipe with only drips of water.” (Guy Stiver Decl. ¶ 12.) If the clear pipe, with an estimated .2 gallons per minute flow, is allowed to flow continuously for 24 hours, an estimated 288 gallons of water would be deposited into the gulley. (Id. ¶13.) Stiver opined that “[t]he extra drainage water most likely applied over years, increased root growth contributing to the lifting of the concrete panel that lead to the alleged trip and fall incident. This assertion is backed up by the evidence that the combined widths (4 inches total) of the wall cracks at the subject location almost equals the combined widths of wall cracks (4.5 inches total) at the other five planter tree well locations” (Id. ¶15.)  Photographs taken by the experts show that the pool equipment room is directly above the tree with water coming out of the pool drainage pipe leading to the tree either through the black drainage pipe or through a gulley formed by the flow of water.

Therefore, Plaintiff and Defendant City of LA have raised a triable issue of fact relating to whether the HOA Defendants caused the uplift of the sidewalk under the sidewalk doctrine through the drainage from its pool equipment.

Garden View

As a preliminary issue, the City of Los Angeles did not file an opposition against Garden View’s motion for summary judgment. Garden View's situation differs from that of the HOA Defendants because it is not the owner of the adjacent property but rather a third party contracted by the property owner to perform landscaping work. Defendant Garden View argues that it owed no duty of care to Plaintiff. However, "[t]he duty question must be decided on a case-by-case basis, but each case must be governed by the general rule that everyone is required to use ordinary care to prevent causing injury to others." (Bloomberg v. Interinsurance Exchange (1984) 162 Cal.App.3d 571, 575.) "A defendant who enters upon an affirmative course of conduct affecting the interests of another is regarded as assuming a duty to act, and will be liable for negligent acts or omissions." (Id.) As in Bloomberg, the allegations against Defendant center around the negligent performance of a contractual duty. Similarly here, one source of Defendant’s duty stems from the negligent performance of its contractual duty under its agreement with the HOA. The other source of duty stems from the sidewalk doctrine where Garden View can be held liable if it created the displacement in question, or retained control over the sidewalk or the tree at issue. As demonstrated above, Garden View has shown that it does not control the sidewalk or the tree in question, and by extension, did not create the displacement, as Garden View did not maintain, trim, or water the tree. The onus is now on Plaintiff to raise a triable issue of fact.

Ownership And Control of The Sidewalk

The only evidence introduced by Plaintiff regarding Garden View’s ownership and control of the sidewalk in question is the testimony of the City of Los Angeles’ Person Most Qualified (PMQ), James Hawkins, who stated in his deposition, "I don’t know" when asked if "the City controlled the sidewalk." (Exhibit C to Plaintiffs’ Evidence – City of Los Angeles Deposition (PMQ James Hawkins), pgs. 37:18 – 22. [Compendium pg. 29.]) Under CCP § 437c(p)(2), the opposing party is required to set forth specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists. Statements such as "I don’t know" do not constitute specific facts. Thus, Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact relating to Garden View’s control over the sidewalk in question.

Ownership And Control of The Tree

With regard to Garden View’s control of the specific tree at issue, Plaintiff introduced a document showing that the city has an easement 4 feet into the HOA’s property, which includes the tree in question. Plaintiff’s expert interpreting the easement declares that the property, including the tree, is owned by the HOA but under the control of the city (Plf’s Ex. C). This arrangement is corroborated by the fact that the HOA does not inspect or maintain the tree. (Tibbs Depo. 75:20-25, 76:23-25.)

In situations where ownership and control are distinct, case law has established that control is the key determinant of liability. The court in Low v. City of Sacramento (1970) 7 Cal.App.3d 826,832 clarified that under common law, the responsibility for maintaining the safe condition of land lies with the possessor, defined as the entity that both occupies and controls the property. The court states that “in identifying the party vulnerable to a verdict, control dominates over title.” (Id.) “The crucial element is control.” (Schwartz v. Helms Bakery Ltd. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 232, 239) The Low court determined that even if an easement holder has control over an area, the underlying fee holder who actively manages the property, such as by maintaining landscaping, retains the power to prevent or remedy hazards and is therefore liable. (Low, 7 Cal.App.3d at 834.) In the current case, whether the HOA or Garden View owned the land that includes the tree is not the dispositive factor. The key determinant for assessing liability is who actually maintained and controlled the property.

Application

The evidence presented by Plaintiff does not substantiate his claim that Garden View controlled the tree in question. Plaintiff attempted to utilize a statement from Gwen Tibbs, CEO of Subdivision Management, asserting that Garden View maintains the common areas, which allegedly included the tree at issue. (Exhibit A to Plaintiffs’ Evidence – Declaration of Gwen Tibbs, ¶¶ 3, 9.) However, in the same declaration, Tibbs clarified that neither she nor Garden View maintains the tree, and it is actually maintained by the city (Tibbs Decl. ¶¶ 6-8).

Plaintiff also references the easement of the property, as detailed in the Declaration of John S. Henderson. (Exhibit C to Plaintiff’s Evidence, ¶¶ 2, 3, 4 and its Exhibits 1 and 2). Plaintiff’s own expert testified that the tree is controlled by the city and owned by the HOA. Low has made clear that control and not ownership is the determining factor in assessing liability.

Plaintiff introduced expert testimony from Kay Greeley regarding the placement of a drainage pipe near the tree's roots as evidence of Garden View’s control of the tree. (Exhibit B to Plaintiffs’ Evidence – Declaration of Kay Greeley, P.E., R.L.A., ¶¶ 15, 17, 19, 21.) However, evidence presented by Plaintiff himself shows that the pipe was neither installed nor maintained by Garden View but had existed prior to Garden View’s contract with the HOA. (Plf’s Ex. D, Deposition of Garden View, Inc. (PMK Blake Meahl) 208:6-25.)

Plaintiff introduced testimony of Garden View’s PMK, who testified that for trees higher than 10 feet, they would get a tree service. (Exhibit D to Plaintiff’s Evidence – Garden View, Inc. Deposition (PMK Blake Meahl – Part 1), 136:13 – 137:6. [Compendium pgs. 51 – 52.]) However, this does not apply to the specific tree in question. Plaintiff needs to show that Garden View’s scope of service includes the tree at issue, which he has not.

Lastly, Plaintiff points to Garden View placing river rocks outside of the brick barrier enclosing the tree. However, the court does not find this action to constitute control over the tree in question. First, the rocks were placed outside the barrier surrounding the tree and no rocks were placed inside the barrier.  Second, a one-time placement of the rocks in response to the directive of the HOA to which it had no input (Plf’s Ex. D, Depo of Blake Meahl 59:1-21.) does not constitute systematic control sufficient to establish liability under the sidewalk decision doctrine.

Plaintiff failed to show that maintenance of the tree at issue was required under the control or that, in practice, Defendant Garden View actually performed regular maintenance of the tree. Thus, Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact relating to Garden View’s control over the tree.

Garden View’s Negligent Performance and Creation of the Displacement

Plaintiff’s contention that Garden View caused the alleged displacement and Garden View’s negligent performance are based on the same two theories. The first theory involves the black drainage pipe connecting the property to the area immediately next to the barrier of the tree. As analyzed above, the drainage from the pool through the drainage pipes might have contributed to the overgrowth of the roots leading to the displacement at issue. However, evidence showed that Garden View was not involved in the installation of the drainage pipes. Garden View’s PMK testified that the black drainage pipe was not installed by Garden View and is likely to have existed since the development of the property. (Plf’s Ex. D, Meahl Depo 208:6-25.) Furthermore, under Garden View’s contract with the HOA, Garden View does not perform any services for drainage pipes. (Garden View’s Ex. A.) This is to be expected given that the drain pipes relate to drainage from the pool equipment located directly above the tree and is unrelated to landscaping. Plaintiff failed to raise a triable issue of fact both under the contract and in actual practice that Defendant Garden View is involved in any way with the drainage pipe in question.

Plaintiff’s second theory is that due to the slope behind the tree, water from sprinklers shooting up the slope migrates downwards, causing overwatering of the tree and leading to the displacement. Plaintiff’s expert points to the difference in vegetation on the slope between 2014 and 2015, indicating bare areas as evidence of overwatering and drainage issues. (Greeley Decl. ¶ 9.) However, the expert admits that overwatering and drainage issues are just one of many potential causes for the bare areas. (Id.) The expert, based on a lack of discovery responses, also speculates that river rock was added in 2019 to stabilize the bottom of the slope. (Id.) The expert further concludes that due to the slope, raised irrigation heads shooting water onto the slope migrated downward to the tree, encouraging more rapid growth of the tree. (Id. ¶ 10.) The expert did not state that he measured or observed how much water migrates backward, how much is captured by the existing vegetation, how much is dispersed to other areas, how much water runs off onto the streets, and how much of the water is blocked off by the brick barrier surrounding the trees. Without establishing these factual foundations, the conclusion that water migration caused overwatering, which led to the displacement, is speculative and insufficient to raise a triable issue of fact. A party cannot avoid summary judgment based on mere speculation and conjecture…but instead must produce admissible evidence raising a triable issue of fact. (Crouse v. Brobeck, Phleger & Harrison (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1509, 1524.) “If the plaintiff cannot do so, summary judgment should be granted.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal. App. 4th 463, 467.)  

CONCLUSION

HOA Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is denied. Garden View’s motion for summary judgment is granted.

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS 

Hearing Date: 6/17/24 

CASE NO./NAME: 22STCV27661 BRIAN WALKER, et al. vs DAVID M ODEGARD , et al.

Moving Party: Defendant David Odegard 

Responding Party: Unopposed

Notice: Sufficient 

Ruling: MOTIONS FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS AGAINST PLAINTIFFS BRYAN WALKER AND STEVE MALANG AS TO DEFENDANT DAVID ODEGARD ARE GRANTED

 

On November 14 and 17, 2023, this Court ordered Plaintiffs Walker and Malang to respond to Defendants’ first sets of Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Requests for Production by December 14, 2023.

The Court’s order also sanctioned Plaintiff Walker and his counsel in the amount of $1,000, payable to Defendant by December 4 and 7, 2023.

On February 14, 2024, Defendants filed a Motion for Terminating Sanctions based on Plaintiff’s failure to respond to discovery and pay the sanctions ordered by the Court. The motion was set to be heard on March 12, 2024. Plaintiff failed to file an opposition, but counsel appeared at the hearing and advised the Court that he failed to oppose the motion due to his health conditions.

Counsel also advised the Court that he had already provided responses to discovery and that he would be substituting or associating in counsel due to his health conditions and that terminating sanctions were not warranted. Based on this, the Court denied the motion, ordered counsel to pay the sanctions, and to file a motion for substitution of attorney or notice of association of counsel within 20 days of March 18, 2024.

Defendant alleges Plaintiffs’ counsel has not been truthful to the Court as discovery responses allegedly served on Defendant have not been received. Furthermore, upon examining the record, the Court sees that no motion for substitution of attorney or notice of association of counsel has been filed. Plaintiffs’ attorney did not file an opposition to this motion for terminating sanctions, and Plaintiffs did not attend the FSC set for June 3, 2024.

It is undisputed that Plaintiffs did not file the motion for substitution. Defendant asserts that he has not received the discovery responses that were allegedly served, and Plaintiffs did not contest this assertion. Further, there was already a prior motion for termination where the Court gave Plaintiffs the benefit of the doubt and an opportunity to comply with the Court’s orders. Additionally, Plaintiffs did not oppose the present motion and did not attend the FSC, appearing to have abandoned the case. It appears to the Court that a lesser sanction would not curb Plaintiffs’ abuse. Thus, Defendant’s motion for terminating sanctions is granted.[1]  Because the Court deems that sanction sufficient, it denies the requests for monetary sanctions.
