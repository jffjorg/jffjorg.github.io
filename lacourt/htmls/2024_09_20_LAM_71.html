DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

        All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDEPT71@lacourt.org. Do not click on the email address, either copy and paste it or type it into your email.  Include the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant, cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.


            Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words "SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    


            If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via Court-Connect. If the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling, the Court will take the  matter off calendar.
                          
            Note that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative ruling as the order of the court.   
 

            If you submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.   

Case Number: 23STCV01565    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

SARA VAN HORN, et al., 

 

         vs.

 

THE WOMAN’S CLUB OF HOLLYWOOD, CALIFORNIA, et al.

 Case No.:  23STCV01565

 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 20, 2024

 

Plaintiffs Sara Van Horn’s and Patrick Van Horn’s motion for a protective order is denied. 

 

Plaintiffs Sara Van Horn (“Sara”) and Patrick Van Horn (“Patrick”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) move for a protective order.  (Notice of Motion, pgs. 1-2; C.C.P. §§2025.420, 2030.090(a).)  Plaintiffs also move for sanctions up to and including sanctions striking Defendants The Woman’s Club of Hollywood, California (“WCOHC”) and Rosemary Lord’s (“Lord”) (collectively, “Defendants”) pleadings and seeking costs and fees associated with the Motion in an amount no less than $10,000.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2; C.C.P. §§2031.060, 2023.030; CRC, Rule 3.1152.)

 

Background

On June 27, 2024, Plaintiffs filed the instant motion for protective order.  On September 6, 2024, Defendants filed their opposition.  As of the date of this hearing no reply has been filed.

 

Meet and Confer

          C.C.P. §2031.060 provides, in part:

When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

 

(C.C.P. §2031.060(a), emphasis added.)

Plaintiffs’ counsel failed to submit a meet and confer declaration in compliance with C.C.P. §2016.040.  (See Decl. of Holliday.)  Plaintiffs’ counsel’s declaration does not state facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the matter outside of court.  (C.C.P. §§2016.040, 2031.060(a).)  Therefore, the Court denies the instant motion.

 

Conclusion

Plaintiffs’ motion for protective order is denied.

Case Number: 24STCV07725    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

OYEKUNLE JEGEDE, 

 

         vs.

 

90018 COLLECTION LLC, et al.

 Case No.:  24STCV07725

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  September 20, 2024

 

Moving Defendant 90018 Collection, LLC’s motion for a protective order is denied.

Moving Defendant’s request for sanctions is denied.

 

Defendant 90018 Collection, LLC (“90018”) (“Moving Defendant”) moves for a protective order quashing discovery propounded by pro per Plaintiff Oyekunle Jegede (“Jegede”) (“Plaintiff”).  (Notice of Motion, pgs. 1-2; C.C.P. §§2017.020, 2023.280, 2023.090, 2023.300.)  Moving Defendant moves on the grounds that Plaintiff’s Special Interrogatories, Set One (“SROG”), Requests for Admission, Set One (“RFA”), and Requests for Production of Documents Set One (“RFP 1”) and Set Two (“RFP 2”) (collectively, “Plaintiff’s Discovery”) served on July 11, 2024, and July 13, 2024, seeks information about the rights held by other tenants (for which Plaintiff has no standing to complain), contracts and financial data having nothing to do with Plaintiff’s lease, and evidence of alleged other unrelated “bad acts” by 90018 to prove 90018 is sufficiently bad to deserve being sued here. (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.) 

Moving Defendant argues such discovery demands are overbroad, unfocused and seek information that is not reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence and violate 90018’s right to privacy under the California Constitution.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.)  Moving Defendant also requests an award of attorney’s fees of $6,370.00 and costs in the sum of $80.97 for a total of $6,450.97 that were unreasonably incurred to bring the matter before the Court on the grounds that reasonable resolutions were offered to Plaintiff, (either deferral or narrowing of the requests) which he rejected.  (Notice of Motion, pg. 2.)

 

Background

Plaintiff filed his initial complaint in this matter on March 27, 2024, against Moving Defendant and Non-moving Defendants Winston Cenac, Trustee of the Island Boy Trust (“Cenac”), Jason Penneton (“Penneton”), Shelby Ring (“Ring”), the Law Offices of Rebecca Hufford Cohen (“Law Office”), and On Time (“On Time”) (collectively, “Defendants”) for four causes of action: (1) illegal eviction; (2) illegal theft; (3) discrimination; and (4) fraud.

On August 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed the operative first amended complaint (“FAC”) against Defendants alleging seven causes of action: (1) wrongful eviction; (2) discrimination (violation of FEHA); (3) breach of contract; (4) conversion; (5) negligence; (6) fraud and misrepresentation; and (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress, arising from his tenancy at a studio apartment located at 238 ½ Market St., Venice, CA (“Subject Property”).  (See FAC ¶2.)

On July 1, 2024, Moving Defendant filed the instant motion.  On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed his opposition.  On September 13, 2024, Moving Defendant filed its reply.  On September 16, 2024, Plaintiff filed a sur-reply.[1]

 

Meet and Confer

          C.C.P. §2031.060 provides, in part:

When an inspection, copying, testing, or sampling of documents, tangible things, places, or electronically stored information has been demanded, the party to whom the demand has been directed, and any other party or affected person, may promptly move for a protective order. This motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.

 

(C.C.P. §2031.060(a), emphasis added.)

Moving Defendant’s counsel declares that on June 27, 2024, she met and conferred with Plaintiff regarding his Complaint and discovery requests, but that the matters presented in this motion were not resolved.  (See Decl. of Windler ¶¶5-14, Exh. 6.)  Moving Defendant’s counsel’s declaration states facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve the matter outside of court.  (C.C.P. §§2016.040, 2031.060(a).)  Therefore, the Court will consider the instant motion.

 

Legal Standard

C.C.P. §2031.060(b) provides that “[t]he court, for good cause shown, may make any order that justice requires to protect any party or other person from unwarranted annoyance, embarrassment, or oppression, or undue burden and expense.”  (C.C.P. §2031.060(b).)

The decision upon whether to enter a protective order lies within the sound discretion of the court.  (Raymond Handling Concepts Corp. v. Superior Court (1995) 39 Cal.App.4th 584, 588, 591; Meritplan Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (1981) 124 Cal.App.3d 237, 242.)  Moving parties have the burden to show good cause for protective order.  (Emerson Electric Co. v. Superior Court (1997) 16 Cal.4th 1101, 1110.)   

 

          Discussion

Moving Defendant’s motion for a protective order is denied.  Moving Defendant failed to meet its burden to demonstrate they need protection from Plaintiff’s Discovery Requests.  

First, Moving Defendant’s argument that Plaintiff’s discovery is premature is unavailing. The right to discovery generally does not depend on the status of the pleadings. i.e., the case need not be “at issue.”  (Mattco Forge, Inc. v. Arthur Young & Co. (1990) 223 Cal.App.3d 1429, 1436 [upholding sanctions for refusal to make discovery because demurrer pending].)

Deficiencies in the pleadings generally do not affect either party’s right to conduct discovery. Thus, for example, discovery may continue after a demurrer has been sustained with leave to amend, although no amended complaint has yet been filed—i.e., no valid complaint need be on file.  (Budget Finance Plan v. Superior Court (1973) 34 Cal.App.3d 794, 797.)

Second, Moving Defendant does not have a right to privacy protected by the California Constitution; Article I, §1 of the California Constitution protects the rights of “people” only.  (SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 755; see Ameri-Medical Corp. v. Workers’ Compensation Appeals Board (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1287; Zurich American Insurance Co. v. Superior Court (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1485, 1504 [corporation acknowledged that it had no right of privacy under the California Constitution].)  While corporations do have a right to privacy, it is not a constitutional right.  The corporate right to privacy is a lesser right than that held by human beings and is not considered a fundamental right.  (Ameri-Medical Corp., 42 Cal.App.4th at pgs. 1287-1288.)

Because the corporate privacy right is not constitutionally protected, the issue presented in determining whether Plaintiff’s discovery requests infringe that right is resolved by a balancing test.  The discovery’s relevance to the subject matter of the pending dispute and whether the discovery “appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence” is balanced against the corporate right of privacy.  (Hecht, Solberg, Robinson, Goldberg & Bagley LLP v. Superior Court (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 579, 595.)  Doubts about relevance generally are resolved in favor of permitting discovery. (Id.)

Here, Moving Defendant’s records regarding the sale or transfer of the multi-unit property Defendant Cenac owned that includes the Subject Property (i.e. RFP 2 Nos. 2, 3) is reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence because Plaintiff’s FAC pertains to the ownership of the property and the legal authorization of Cenac to take possession of the Subject Property.  (See FAC ¶4.)

Third, Moving Defendant fails to demonstrate Plaintiff’s discovery requests are burdensome and oppressive.

Based on the foregoing, Moving Defendant’s motion for protective order is denied.

 

Sanctions

In light of this Court’s ruling on the motion, Moving Defendant’s request for sanctions is denied.

 

Conclusion

Moving Defendant’s motion for protective order is denied.

Moving Defendant’s request for sanctions is denied.

Moving Party to give notice.
