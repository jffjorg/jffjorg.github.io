Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling must send an email to alhdeptx@lacourt.org advising the Court and all counsel of their intention to submit.  Unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties.  If all parties submit on the Court’s tentative ruling, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling unless otherwise ordered by the Court.  This may be done by incorporating verbatim the Court’s tentative ruling.

The Court has considered the Declaration of Angel M. Baker concerning the October 31, 2024 Order to Show Cause re: Dismissal (Settlement).  The OSC hearing is continued to January 28, 2025 at 8:30 a.m. in Department X of the Alhambra Courthouse.

Plaintiff is to give notice.


Case Number: 23AHCV01210    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: X


RELIEF REQUESTED

Defendants move to strike and demur to the first amended complaint.

 

Plaintiff also filed an ex parte application for more time to prepare for the Case Management Conference and/or trial.

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff sued the storage facility where he rented storage units until the facility told him to move his property out of storage.  The first amended complaint has nine causes of action: (1) breach of contract, (2) unfair business practice, (3) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (4) physical, psychological, and financial abuse of a disabled elder person, (5), unfair fraudulent business practice, (6) discrimination, (7) intentional infliction of emotional distress, (8) declaratory relief, and (9) injunctive relief.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendants’ demurrer filed against the FAC is SUSTAINED.  The motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

Plaintiff’s ex parte application is denied where there is currently no case management conference scheduled, nor is there a trial date scheduled.

 

LEGAL STANDARD


Demurrer

 

A general demurrer may be taken to a complaint where “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(e).)  A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a).)  The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

 

Additionally, a special demurrer to a complaint may be brought on the ground the pleading is uncertain, ambiguous, or unintelligible.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(f);¿Beresford Neighborhood Ass’n. v. City of San Mateo¿(1989) 207 Cal.App.3d 1180, 1191.)  A demurrer based on uncertainty is disfavored and will be strictly construed even when the pleading is uncertain in some respects.  (Khoury v.¿Maly’s¿of California, Inc.¿(1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)  A demurrers based on uncertainty are “granted only if the pleading is so incomprehensible that a defendant cannot reasonably respond.”  (Lickiss v. Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 1125, 1135.)

 

Motion to Strike

 

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike a pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).)  The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.  (Ibid., at § 436, subd. (a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with California law, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Ibid., at § 436, subd. (b).)  An immaterial or irrelevant allegation is one that is not essential to the statement of a claim or defense; is neither pertinent to nor supported by an otherwise sufficient claim or defense; or a demand for judgment requesting relief not supported by the allegations of the complaint.  (Ibid., at § 431.10, subd. (b).)  The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice.  (Ibid., at § 437.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

The FAC alleges unfair termination of a rental contract due to Plaintiff’s status as a homeless, disabled, and elderly individual.  The FAC alleges that Plaintiff rented storage units on January 2, 2021, and June 1, 2021.  On April 26, 2023, Defendant Ryan Martinetz approached Plaintiff with a notice of 30 days to vacate and notified Plaintiff to move his property out of storage by May 31, 2023.

 

Demurrer

 

Preliminarily, the FAC is uncertain because it does not specify which defendant or defendants each cause of action is asserted against, and it fails to clearly articulate the wrongful acts and their legal basis.

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Contract

The elements of a claim for breach of contract are: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)  In addition, the complaint must demonstrate damages proximately caused by the breach.  (St. Paul Ins. v. American Dynasty (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1060.)

 

The FAC attached a copy of the contract.  Attached to the FAC as Exhibit 11 is a contract for the rental agreement for storage units #261 and #334.  The parties to the rental agreement are the Operator (Defendant Raymond Avenue Self Storage) and Plaintiff.  Defendant Martinetz is not a party to this contract.  The FAC alleges Defendants breached the contract by having a non-working elevator in the facility and unlawfully disposing of Plaintiff’s personal property by auctioning off his items.

 

Defendants demur, arguing that the rental agreement was between Plaintiff and Defendant Rogerson, not Defendant Martinetz, and Plaintiff failed to allege that he performed under the agreement by paying rent.

 

Here, the FAC alleges the Operator issued a 30 day notice to vacate, stating that the reason is because of some food that Plaintiff threw away in the trash can.  Attached to the FAC as Exhibit 7 is the notice to vacate.  The letter states: “According to the rules and regulations of your rental agreement, no food or beverages are to be stored in the units.  On April 26th you came in to store more items in your unit, but we found food products in the trash bins we gave you.  Therefore, we request that you remove all of your belongs [sic] from Unit #261 & 334 by May 31, 2023.”  (FAC, Exh. 7.)  The demurrer argues that food in the trash can attract mice and vermin and that is why Defendant Rogerson terminated Plaintiff’s lease.  However, the FAC asserts that he had asked for a plastic cart “that day just to throw away what I could consider trash, or junk” and that he “did not see any post sign stating any kind of prohibition for throwing any kind of food.”  (FAC, pp. 14-15.)

 

Upon review of the rental agreement terms regarding food, paragraph 23 of the rental agreement states: “If Customer is in default under this Agreement, or for any other reason, Operator may terminate this Agreement by giving Customer fifteen (15) days written notice.”  Paragraph 10 of the agreement limits use of the space and facility: “The storage of food and any perishable goods is strictly prohibited.”

 

Considering the allegations and exhibits attached to the complaint, Plaintiff fails to state sufficient facts to constitute a cause of action for breach of contract.

 

The demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Second Cause of Action: Unfair Business Practice

“ ‘By proscribing “any unlawful” business practice, “[Business and Professions Code] section 17200 ‘borrows’ violations of other laws and treats them as unlawful practices” that the [UCL] makes independently actionable.’  [Citation.]  ‘An unlawful business practice under [Business and Professions Code] section 17200 is “ ‘an act or practice, committed pursuant to business activity, that is at the same time forbidden by law.  [Citation.]’ ” ‘ ” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1361.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges Defendant Rogerson knew that a surprising 30 day notice to vacate would be difficult for Plaintiff and Defendant Rogerson has experience dealing like this to obtain profit, and Rogerson Capital Management also refused a request granting an extension to the move-out date.  These facts do not constitute an unlawful business practice.

 

The demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED. 

 

Third Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

“Under California law, every contract includes an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.”  (Prager University v. Google LLC (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 1022, 1039.)  The covenant “exists merely to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made.”  (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349.)

 

Plaintiff does not allege that Defendants breached the contract or frustrated his rights to receive the benefits of the agreement.  The FAC alleges the refusal to grant an extension to move out, or to agree to mediate with Loyola Law School, and lack of consideration of Plaintiff’s medical conditions, show a lack of good faith.  These allegations are insufficient to sustain this cause of action.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s third cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Fourth Cause of Action: Physical, Psychological and Financial Abuse of a Disabled Elder Person

“The Elder Abuse Act’s heightened remedies are available only in limited circumstances.   plaintiff must prove, by clear and convincing evidence, that a defendant is liable for either physical abuse under section 15610.63 or neglect under section 15610.57, and that the defendant committed the abuse with ‘recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.’ ”  (Winn v. Pioneer Medical Group, Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 148, 156.)  “To recover the enhanced remedies available under the Elder Abuse Act from a health care provider, a plaintiff must prove more than simple or even gross negligence in the provider’s care or custody of the elder.”  (Carter v. Prime Healthcare Paradise Valley LLC (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 396, 405.)  “In order to obtain the Act’s heightened remedies, a plaintiff must allege conduct essentially equivalent to conduct that would support recovery of punitive damages.”  (Covenant Care, Inc. v. Superior Court (2004) 32 Cal.4th 771, 789.)

 

The FAC alleges that receiving a 30 day notice to vacate disturbed him when he read it and has been suffering emotionally and has more symptoms which affect his body.  He alleges he has lost a great amount of his personal property.  However, the FAC does not establish how the Defendant acted with recklessness, oppression, fraud, or malice.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for elder abuse is SUSTAINED.

 

Fifth Cause of Action: Unfair Fraudulent Business Practice

For the reasons stated in the second cause of action for Unfair Business Practice, Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for “unfair fraudulent business practices” also fails.  The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Sixth Cause of Action: Discrimination

Under the Unruh Act, “[a]ll persons within the jurisdiction of this state are free and equal . . . and no matter what their . . . disability [or other protected characteristic] . . . are entitled to the full and equal accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, or services in all business establishments of every kind whatsoever.”  (Civ. Code, § 51; see also CACI No. 3066.)  A cause of action under Unruh consists of the following elements: (1) the defendant denied the plaintiff access to full and equal accommodations, advantages, facilities, privileges, or services in a business establishment; (2) the plaintiff’s membership in a protected class was a motivating factor for this denial; and (3) defendants’ wrongful conduct caused plaintiff to suffer injury, damage, loss, or harm.  (See Wilkins-Jones v. County of Alameda (2012) 859 F.Supp.2d 1039, 1048.)  A plaintiff establishing an Unruh violation that is not also an ADA violation must establish that the discrimination was intentional.  (Harris v. Capital Growth Investors XIV (1991) 52 Cal.3d 1142, 1172.)

 

The FAC asserts discrimination where Defendant Rogerson told Plaintiff not to use the rear door on the second floor of the facility, even though there was a notice on the elevator that all customers could use the rear door because the elevator was out of service.  The FAC asserts that not being able to use the rear door could have cost him being able to take his personal property out of the facility.  The FAC also asserts that the operator Rogerson was harassing and provoking Plaintiff.

 

The allegations on the face of the complaint do not meet the elements of a discrimination cause of action under the Unruh Act.  They do not show that Plaintiff’s membership in a protected class was a motivating factor for this denial, or that Defendant’s wrongful conduct caused Plaintiff to suffer injury, damage, loss, or harm.

 

The demurrer to the sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Seventh Cause of Action: Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

“ ‘ “[T]o state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress a plaintiff must show: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress by the defendant’s outrageous conduct.” ’ ” (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.)

 

The FAC alleges that after receiving the notice to vacate, he started to experience distress for what he was facing as the notice was made with the purpose to mislead him into an unexpected situation.  It also alleges the elevator was not working during the entire month of May, which was the month for him to vacate his storage units, and thus he had to take his property without any help and by using the stairs.  It also alleges some of Plaintiff’s personal items were missing, for which the police wrote a report about.

 

The allegations of Defendant’s conduct, of issuing a notice to vacate, with a facility with a nonworking elevator, do not meet the definition of outrageous conduct by the defendant.  It also is not apparent that Defendant intended to cause, or recklessly disregarded the probability of causing, emotional distress.  The allegations are insufficient to sustain a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

 

Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Eighth Cause of Action: Declaratory Relief and Ninth Cause of Action: Injunctive Relief

Plaintiff’s eighth and ninth causes of action fail.  “To qualify for declaratory relief under section 1060, plaintiffs were required to show their action (as refined on appeal) presented two essential elements: ‘(1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the rights or obligations of a party.’ ”  (Lee v. Silveira (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 527, 546.)  “A trial court may grant a preliminary injunction upon a showing that (1) the party seeking the injunction is likely to prevail on the merits at trial, and (2) the ‘interim harm’ to that party if an injunction is denied is greater than ‘the [interim] harm the [opposing party] is likely to suffer if the ... injunction is issued.’  [citation.]”  (Integrated Dynamic Solutions, Inc. v. VitaVet Labs, Inc. (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 1178, 1183.)

 

Here, Plaintiff does not state the basis for declaratory or injunctive relief.  Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s eighth and ninth causes of action is SUSTAINED.

 

Motion to Strike

Defendants request the Court to strike Plaintiff’s request for treble damages as none of the causes of action allow for the recovery of treble damages.  The Court finds that the FAC does not assert the basis of the treble damages sought.  Thus, the motion to strike is GRANTED.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendants’ demurrer filed against the FAC is SUSTAINED in its entirety.  The motion to strike is GRANTED.

Plaintiff is granted leave to amend the Complaint.  The Complaint must be filed within 30 days of Defendants’ service of a notice of ruling.

Case Number: 24NNCV01992    Hearing Date: October 31, 2024    Dept: X


RELIEF REQUESTED

Defendant demurs to all nine causes of action in Plaintiff’s complaint.

 

BACKGROUND

The complaint alleges Defendant expressed an interest in hiring Plaintiff’s construction company to build a house and the parties negotiated the terms and price of the project and provided Plaintiff with a $1,000 deposit as a demonstration of his commitment to the project. Approximately one week after entering the contract, Defendant contacted Plaintiff to inform him that he would not proceed with Plaintiff’s company and would award the project to a friend instead. Six months later, Defendant decided to re-engage Plaintiff and reaffirm his commitment. Around October 13, 2023, Defendant made his first contractual payment to Plaintiff.  Then, Defendant failed to make a payment per the contract and instead proposed an alternative arrangement suggesting that Plaintiff complete sink installation and tree removal for a certain payment but Plaintiff refused.  Defendant unilaterally cancelled the contract and barred Plaintiff from entering the construction site.

 

Plaintiff’s complaint alleges (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Quantum Meruit (3) Promissory Estoppel, (4) Unjust Enrichment, (5) Breach of The Implied Covenant of Good Faith And Fair Dealing, (6) Fraudulent Inducement, (7) Negligent Misrepresentation, (8) Conversion, and (9) Unfair Business Practices – Bus. and Profs. Code section 17200.

 

TENTATIVE RULING

Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth and eighth causes of action are SUSTAINED with leave to amend.  Defendant’s demurrer to the first, second, third, fourth, sixth, seventh, and ninth causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

A general demurrer may be taken to a complaint where “[t]he pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).)  A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿  (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)  In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.30(a).)¿  The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.  (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)

 

DISCUSSION

Defendant demurs to all nine causes of action in the complaint.

 

(1) Breach of Contract

 

The elements of a claim for breach of contract are: “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.”  (Oasis West Realty, LLC v. Goldman (2011) 51 Cal.4th 811, 821.)  In addition, the complaint must demonstrate damages proximately caused by the breach.  (St. Paul Ins. v. American Dynasty (2002) 101 Cal.App.4th 1038, 1060.)

 

The demurrer’s only argument against the first cause of action is that the complaint does not include a copy of the contract, nor does it state the contract terms verbatim or in substance, leaving the relevant provisions ambiguous and uncertain as to whether they meet the formal requirements of section 7159 of the Business and Professions Code.  Business and Professions Code section 7159 “identifies the projects for which a home improvement contract is required, outlines the contract requirements, and lists the items that shall be included in the contract, or may be provided as an attachment.”  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 7159(a).)  A “home improvement contract” is defined as follows:

 

“[A]n agreement, whether oral or written, or contained in one or more documents, between a contractor and an owner or between a contractor and a tenant, regardless of the number of residence or dwelling units contained in the building in which the tenant resides, if the work is to be performed in, to, or upon the residence or dwelling unit of the tenant, for the performance of a home improvement, as defined in Section 7151, and includes all labor, services, and materials to be furnished and performed thereunder, if the aggregate contract price specified in one or more improvement contracts, including all labor, services, and materials to be furnished by the contractor, exceeds five hundred dollars ($500).   

 

When taking the allegations in the complaint as true, the alleged contract does not appear to be for home improvement.  Although the complaint does not attach a written contract, it pleads its terms regarding a payment schedule (Compl., ¶ 8), and pleads that Defendant made the first payment (Compl., ¶ 11).  Plaintiff pleads facts sufficient to state a breach of contract cause of action against Defendant.

 

The demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first cause of action.

 

(2) Quantum Meruit, (3) Promissory Estoppel, and (4) Unjust Enrichment

 

“The elements for a claim of unjust enrichment are receipt of a benefit and unjust retention of the benefit at the expense of another.  The theory of unjust enrichment requires one who acquires a benefit which may not justly be retained, to return either the thing or its equivalent to the aggrieved party so as not to be unjustly enriched.”  (Lyles v. Sangadeo-Patel (2014) 225 Cal.App.4th 759, 769, quotation marks and citations omitted.)  Notably, “[u]njust enrichment is not a cause of action”; it is simply “a restitution claim.”  (Hill v. Roll International Corp. (2011) 195 Cal.App.4th 1295, 1307; see also Melchior v. New Line Productions, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 779, 793 [“there is no cause of action in California for unjust enrichment”].)

 

The elements of promissory estoppel are: (i) a clear and unambiguous false promise; (ii) defendant’s knowledge of the falsity of the promise; (iii) plaintiff’s reasonable and foreseeable reliance; (iv) defendant’s failure to perform; and (v) injury to plaintiff as a result of the reliance. (Thompson v. Int’l Alliance of Theatrical Stage Employees and Moving Picture Machine Operators (1964) 232 Cal.App.2d 446; Aceves v. U.S. Bank, NA (2011) 192 Cal.App.4th 218, 231.)

 

The demurrer argues quantum meruit or quasi-contractual recovery is not available when the parties have an actual agreement covering compensation.  The demurrer argues that similarly, contract and promissory estoppel claims are mutually exclusive - promissory estoppel is only available in the absence of a valid and enforceable contract.  Further, the demurrer argues, an unjust enrichment claim does not lie where the parties have an enforceable express contract. Since the complaint alleges that Plaintiff, on behalf of Da Hong Construction, and Defendant entered into a contract, the demurrer concludes that Plaintiff’s claims for quantum meruit, promissory estoppel, and unjust enrichment should be dismissed.

 

The complaint alleges “Plaintiff provided the services and materials with the expectation of payment, as explicitly agreed upon in the Contract.  Defendant’s failure to make the payment of $80,820 due upon the completion of the specified construction milestone constitutes a failure to fulfill the implied promise to pay for services rendered.”  (Compl., ¶ 28.)  Plaintiff has alleged a valid cause of action for quasi-contract seeking restitution and unjust enrichment.  The demurrer as to the second and fourth causes of action is OVERRULED.

 

Plaintiff alleges false promise, Plaintiff’s reliance, and Plaintiff’s damages.  (Compl., ¶¶ 33-37.)  Plaintiff alleges how he reasonably relied on Defendant’s promises to his detriment.  “Plaintiff mobilized substantial resources, including labor, materials, and equipment, to commence and continue the construction project based on Defendant’s promises of payment as outlined in the Contract.”  (Compl., ¶ 34.)

 

Plaintiff has alleged facts sufficient to state a cause of action for promissory estoppel.  The demurrer is as to the third cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

(5) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

The demurrer argues that the fifth cause of action is superfluous when it merely repeats the allegations for breach of contract, as it does not create any additional liability.  Defendant points to case law that supports this position, stating that a breach of the contract may also constitute a breach of the implied covenant, but insofar as the party’s actions are directly actionable as a breach of a contract term, a claim that merely realleges that breach as a violation of the covenant is superfluous.  Therefore, Defendant argues, Plaintiff failed to allege sufficient facts.

 

Every contract contains an implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing that neither party will do anything to interfere with the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement. (Howard v. American Nat’l Fire Ins. Co. (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 498, 528.)  The precise nature and extent of the duty depends on the nature and purpose of the underlying contract and the parties’ legitimate expectations arising from the contract.  (Ibid.)  “A breach of the implied covenant of good faith is a breach of the contract … and breach of a specific provision of the contract is not … necessary to a claim for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing.”  (Thrifty Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230, 1244.)  However, “if the allegations [of breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing] do not go beyond the statement of a mere contract breach and, relying on the same alleged acts, simply seek the same damages or other relief already claimed in a companion contract cause of action, they may be disregarded as superfluous as no additional claim is actually stated.”  (Careau & Co. v. Security Pacific Business Credit, Inc. (1990) 222 Cal.App.3d 1371, 1395.)

 

Upon review of the complaint, the Court finds that the underlying factual allegations relied upon in Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is identical to those alleged in his first cause of action for Breach of Contract.  Accordingly, the demurrer to the fifth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

(6) Fraudulent Inducement

 

“A promise to do something necessarily implies the intention to perform; hence where a promise is made without such intention, there is an implied misrepresentation of fact that may be actionable fraud.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 638.)  To establish a claim of fraudulent inducement, one must show that the defendant did not intend to honor its contractual promises when they were made.  (Tenzer v. Superscope, Inc. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 18, 30.)

 

The demurrer argues the complaint only asserts that Defendant had no intention of fulfilling its obligations under the contract, but the complaint itself contradicts this by acknowledging that the defendant complied with all obligations until the seventh progress payment.  Defendant argues Plaintiffs fail to present any factual basis suggesting that Defendant lacked intent to perform the contractual promises when the contract was entered into.  

 

The demurrer to the sixth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

(7) Negligent Misrepresentation

 

Negligent misrepresentation is a form of deceit, the elements of which consist of (1) a misrepresentation of a past or existing material fact, (2) without reasonable grounds for believing it to be true, (3) with intent to induce another’s reliance on the fact misrepresented, (4) ignorance of the truth and justifiable reliance thereon by the party to whom the misrepresentation was directed, and (5) damages.  (Fox v. Pollack (1986) 181 Cal.App.3d 954, 962.)

 

The demurrer asserts Plaintiffs fail to allege any specific facts indicating how Defendant knew there would be a dispute regarding performance prior to entering into the agreement with Plaintiffs or what specific representations Defendant made that are alleged to be false and misleading.

 

The complaint alleges (1) throughout the negotiation and execution of the contract, Defendant made specific representations to Plaintiff regarding his intention and ability to pay for the construction services and materials as per the agreed payment schedule, (2) Defendant either knew or should have known that he would not fulfill the payment obligations, (3) Defendant intended for Plaintiff to rely on these representations in deciding to enter into the Contract and commence work, (4) Plaintiff justifiably relied on Defendant’s representations in entering into the Contract and performing the construction work, and (5) Plaintiff has suffered significant financial harm and operational disruption—incurring substantial costs for labor, materials, and other expenses necessary to fulfill his obligations under the contract.  (Compl., ¶¶ 61-62, 64-66.)

 

The demurrer as to the seventh cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

(8) Conversion

 

Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another.  (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.)  The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.  (Ibid.)

 

The demurrer asserts Defendant’s alleged failure to make an $80,820 payment upon completion of a specified construction milestone does not constitute conversion, as there was no taking of intangible property.

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the “non-payment was not a mere breach of contract but amounted to Defendant asserting unauthorized control over the funds, which were contractually and rightfully owed to Plaintiffs.”

 

Normally, money cannot be converted.  (McKell v. Washington Mutual, Inc. (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1491.)  But money can be the subject of a conversion action when a specific sum capable of identification is involved.  (See SP Investment Fund I LLC v. Cattell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 898, 907.)

 

The simple failure to pay money owed does not constitute conversion, otherwise the tort of conversion would swallow the category of contract claims based on the “mere contractual right of payment.  (Voris v. Lampert (2019) 7 Cal.5th 1141, 1151.)  Cases recognizing claims for the conversion of money “typically involve those who have misappropriated, commingled, or misapplied specific funds held for the benefit of others.”  (Id., at p. 1152.)

 

Here, this case involves the simple failure to pay money owed.  Thus, the allegations in the complaint do not constitute conversion.  The demurrer to the eighth cause of action is SUSTAINED with leave to amend.

 

(9) Unfair Business Practices – Bus. and Profs. Code section 17200.

 

California’s unfair competition law (“UCL”) prohibits any unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice.  (Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200 et seq.)  A claim may be brought under the UCL by a person who has suffered injury in fact and has lost money or property as a result of unfair competition.  (Bus. & Prof. Code, § 17204).  To establish standing under the UCL, a plaintiff must (1) establish a loss or deprivation of money sufficient to qualify as injury in fact, i.e., economic injury, and (2) show that the economic injury was the result of, i.e., caused by, the unfair business practice that is the gravamen of the claim.  (See Kwikset Corp. v. Superior Court (2011) 51 Cal.4th 310, 337.)

 

The complaint states that the unlawful practices include, (1) failing to make the required payment of $80,820 upon completion of the specified construction milestone, (2) proposing an alternative payment arrangement not contemplated by the contract and, upon Plaintiff’s refusal, unilaterally canceling the Contract and barring Plaintiff from the construction site, (3) making false and misleading representations to Plaintiff regarding Defendant’s intention and ability to fulfill his payment obligations under the contract, and (4) intentionally depriving Plaintiff of the value of the services, labor, and materials provided for the construction project.  (Compl., ¶ 73.)

 

The demurrer asserts “the subject contract is a home improvement contract in which Defendant hired Plaintiff’s construction company to build a house, and the parties negotiated the terms and price of the project (Complaint ¶6).  There is not a scintilla of evidence that Defendant engaged in the ‘practice’ denounced by the statute.”

 

In opposition, Plaintiff asserts that its complaint details how Defendant issued assurances of financial stability and prompt payment that were unfounded, which Plaintiffs relied upon to their detriment.  Further, Plaintiffs assert these actions were part of a broader strategy to exploit the contractual relationship and financial dealings to Plaintiffs’ disadvantage, illustrating a pattern of conduct that could be deemed unfair and fraudulent under California law.

 

The Court finds Plaintiff has alleged activity that could be considered unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent.  At the very least, Plaintiff has alleged Defendant intentionally deprived Plaintiff of the value of the services, labor, and materials provided for the construction project.

 

The demurrer to the ninth cause of action is OVERRULED.

 

CONCLUSION

Defendant’s demurrer to the fifth and eighth causes of action are SUSTAINED with leave to amend.  Defendant’s demurrer to the first, second, third, fourth, sixth, seventh, and ninth causes of action are OVERRULED.

 

Plaintiff is to file an amended complaint as to the fifth and eighth causes of action within 20 days of Defendants’ mailing of notice of ruling.
