DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV11207    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Adam Dolewski filed this action against Defendants, Dollar Tree Stores, Inc., Berkshire Bakersfield, LLC, and Brentwood Group, LLC for premises liability and negligence arising out of an incident that occurred at a Dollar Tree location in Long Beach.  Plaintiff alleges Defendants’ negligence caused him to be confined in a freezer for a substantial period of time, which caused him injuries and damages. 

 

Defendants filed a cross-complaint against Hill Phoenix, Inc. and Hill Phoenix WIC, LLC, the entities that manufactured the subject freezer.  Thereafter, Plaintiff amended his complaint to include a claim for manufacturing defects against Hill Phoenix. 

 

2.     Motion for Sanctions

a.     Relief Requested

Hill Phoenix seeks terminating sanctions against Dollar Tree due to its destruction of the freezer in which the incident occurred.  Specifically, it seeks an order dismissing Dollar Tree’s cross-complaint against it, as well as an order that Dollar Tree must indemnify it to the extent Plaintiff ultimately prevails on any claim against it.  Alternatively, Hill Phoenix seeks issue, evidentiary, and/or monetary sanctions against Dollar Tree.

 

b.     Chronology

The chronology of events leading up to the filing of this motion is not disputed.  Plaintiff filed his lawsuit on 3/23/21.  Dollar Tree cross-complained against Hill Phoenix on 1/11/23.  Hill Phoenix swiftly communicated a desire to inspect the freezer, which Dollar Tree agreed to.  On 2/03/23, an unrelated arsonist set fire to the subject store.  Hill Phoenix became aware of the fire, and made Dollar Tree’s attorney aware of the fire on 4/16/23.  Around the same time, unaware of the litigation, Dollar Tree’s construction department dismantled and disposed of the freezer. 

 

c.     Law Governing Spoliation of Evidence

“Spoliation” is the destruction or significant alteration of evidence, or the failure to preserve property for another’s use as evidence, in pending or future litigation.  There is no tort liability for intentional spoliation of evidence committed by a party to the action to which the evidence is relevant and which is, or reasonably should have been, discovered by the spoliation victim (opposing party) before conclusion of the underlying action.  (Cedars-Sinai Med. Ctr. v. Superior Court (1998) 18 Cal.4th 1, 17–18 [defendant in medical malpractice action allegedly destroyed plaintiff's medical records].)  Nor is a tort remedy available for negligent spoliation committed by a party to the action.  (Coprich v. Superior Court (2000) 80 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1089–1090.)

 

There are adequate non-tort remedies for such misconduct, including: discovery sanctions, including exclusion of evidence on affected issue, striking pleadings and rendering default judgment, or contempt; Evidence Code section 413 presumption that destroyed evidence would be adverse to spoliator; CACI 204, and BAJI 2.03 to the same effect; etc.  (Cedars-Sinai Med. Ctr., supra, 18 Cal.4th at p. 10.)  There is uncertainty of harm resulting from the destruction of evidence.  It involves a speculative assessment on whether the evidence would have affected the outcome and if so, the amount of damage caused thereby.  (Id., at p. 14.) Public policy is against creating additional tort remedies for litigation-related misconduct because it perpetuates litigation and burdens judicial resources. (Id., at p. 9.) 

 

The Discovery Act does not specifically prohibit the intentional destruction of relevant evidence (“spoliation”) before a lawsuit has been filed or before a discovery request.  See Dodge, Warren & Peters Ins. Services, Inc. v. Riley (2003) 105 CA4th 1414, 1419.  Courts disagree whether spoliation is sanctionable under the Discovery Act; some cases authorize discovery sanctions for spoliation absent any discovery request: “Destroying evidence in response to a discovery request after litigation has commenced would surely be a misuse of discovery within the meaning of section 2023 as would such destruction in anticipation of a discovery request.” Cedars-Sinai Med. Ctr. v. Sup.Ct. (Bowyer) (1998) 18 C4th 1, 12 (dictum); see Williams v. Russ (2008) 167 CA4th 1215, 1227—terminating sanction imposed for allowing destruction of relevant records.  Other courts reject sanctions for spoliation on the ground that the Discovery Act authorizes sanctions only “(t)o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method or provision of this title,” and no discovery method applies to spoliation before a discovery request.  CCP § 2023.030 (emphasis added); New Albertsons, Inc. v. Sup.Ct. (Shanahan) (2008) 168 CA4th 1403, 1430–1431. 

 

Where there is evidence of suppression, the court may instruct the jury: “You may consider whether one party intentionally concealed or destroyed evidence. If you decide that a party did so, you may decide that evidence would have been unfavorable to that party.” CACI 204; see also BAJI 2.03; see Cedars-Sinai Med. Ctr. v. Sup.Ct. (Bowyer) (1998) 18 C4th 1, 11–12.

 

d.     Analysis

The crux of Dollar Tree’s opposition is that there is no evidence that it acted willfully.  Hill Phoenix, however, argues that such evidence is not necessary.  Hill Phoenix relies on Williams, supra, which the Court has read and considered at length.  In Williams, a client was suing his attorney for breach of fiduciary duty and related claims.  Prior to litigation, the attorney turned over the client files pursuant to the client’s request.  The client then rented a storage facility for the sole purpose of housing the files.  The client chose and copied some of the files that supported his claims, but did not copy the entire file.  Thereafter, the client ceased paying for the storage facility, and received multiple notices that the contents of the facility would be destroyed if payment was not received.  Despite the notifications, the client continued to fail to pay, and ultimately the files were destroyed.  The trial court granted the attorney’s request for terminating sanctions, and the court of appeals affirmed.  Importantly, the trial court found myriad of the client’s factual contentions, including that he had not received notice that the files would be destroyed, that he believed someone at the attorney’s office had copied the files, and that he had fallen behind on the rent due to back surgery and financial difficulties, all to be unsupported by the evidence submitted.

 

Hill Phoenix argues the instant case is similar because Dollar Tree, in choosing to have absolutely no policy ensuring evidence in ongoing litigation is preserved, must accept the consequences of the resulting destruction of the freezer.  It contends intent to destroy evidence is not necessary, only intent to do the thing that results in the destruction of the evidence. 

 

A full reading of Williams, however, is in line with the general authority holding that terminating sanctions should be used sparingly, and only in the most egregious of circumstances.  The crux of Williams was that the client did something so egregious, and so clearly likely to lead to the destruction of necessary evidence, that no other sanction would be appropriate. 

 

The Court finds such sanctions is not appropriate in this case for three reasons.  First, the evidence of what Dollar Tree did wrong is that it failed to have a policy in place to prevent spoliation of evidence.  There was, however, no reason to suspect the freezer was going to be in a fire and thereafter destroyed.  The Court finds this is not nearly as egregious as the spoliation in Williams and the other cited cases.  Second, Dollar Tree has offered to permit Hill Phoenix the full raw metadata of its own experts’ inspection of the freezer.  The Court is hopeful that Plaintiff will offer the same.  Third, the Court is unclear on whether it can order the sanction sought – Hill Phoenix seeks an order not just striking the cross-complaint, but also requiring Dollar Tree to indemnify Hill Phoenix in the event Plaintiff prevails on any claims against it.  Hill Phoenix has not cited authority permitting that remedy to be imposed in the context of a terminating sanction.   

 

Notably, Dollar Tree concedes a jury instruction under CACI 204 would be appropriate.  The Court finds such a jury instruction is the most appropriate redress for the wrong that occurred.  The jury can be fully apprised of the facts relating to the destruction of the freezer, and the jury can then determine whether to draw any inferences from the destruction of the freezer. 

The alternative request for issue, evidentiary, and/or monetary sanctions is also denied.  Again, the Court finds Dollar Tree did not engage in egregious misconduct, and finds a jury instruction is the most appropriate means of addressing the loss of the freezer.

b.     Law Governing Consolidation

CCP §1048 grants discretion to the trial courts to consolidate actions involving common questions of law or fact.  The trial court's decision will not be disturbed on appeal absent a clear showing of abuse of discretion.  (Feliner v. Steinbaum (1955) 132 Cal.App.2d 509, 511.)  A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.  The purpose of consolidation is to avoid unnecessary costs or delay, avoid duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both action, and avoid inconsistent results by hearing and deciding common issues together.  (See Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485.)  Each case presents its own facts and circumstances, but the court generally considers the following: (1) timeliness of the motion: i.e., whether granting consolidation would delay the trial of any of the cases involved; (2) complexity: i.e., whether joining the actions involved would make the trial too confusing or complex for a jury; and (3) prejudice: i.e, whether consolidation would adversely affect the rights of any party. (See State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1956) 47 Cal.2d 428, 430–431.)  In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should weigh whether the common issues predominate over the individual issues and whether any risks of jury confusion or prejudice to the parties outweighs the reduction in time and expense that would result from consolidation. (Todd-Stenberg v. Shield (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978.) 

 

Consolidation is a procedure for uniting separate lawsuits for trial, where they involve common questions of law or fact and are pending in the same court.  (See CCP § 1048.)  The purpose is to enhance trial court efficiency (i.e., to avoid unnecessary duplication of evidence and procedures); and to avoid the substantial danger of inconsistent adjudications (i.e., different results because tried before different juries, or a judge and jury, etc.).  (See Todd-Stenberg v. Dalkon Shield Claimants Trust (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978–979.)  There are two types of consolidation under §1048: (1) a consolidation for purposes of trial only, where the two actions remain otherwise separate, and (2) a complete consolidation or consolidation for all purposes, where the two actions are merged into a single proceeding under one case number and result in only one verdict or set of findings and one judgment.  (Hamilton v. Asbestos Corp. (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1127, 1147.)  The trial court's decision will not be disturbed on appeal absent a clear showing of abuse of discretion. 

 

A consolidation of actions does not affect the rights of the parties.  The purpose of consolidation is merely to promote trial convenience and economy by avoiding duplication of procedure, particularly in the proof of issues common to both actions.  (See Estate of Baker (1982) 131 Cal.App.3d 471, 485.)  In deciding whether to grant a motion to consolidate, the court should weigh whether the common issues predominate over the individual issues and whether any risks of jury confusion or prejudice to the parties outweighs the reduction in time and expense that would result from consolidation. (Todd-Stenberg v. Shield (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 976, 978.) 

c.     Analysis

Plaintiffs did not file a notice of related cases before they filed this motion.  Instead, they filed their notice of related cases on 5/23/24, the same day Defendants filed their opposition to the motion.  The Court finds the cases are related, and 24LBCV00458 is transferred to Department S27 for all further proceedings. 

 

Because the cases are now related, the issue of whether or not to consolidate them is now ripe for determination.  The crux of Defendants’ opposition is that Plaintiffs have improperly brought both a cause of action (battery) and a dismissed defendant (Dr. Jonnala) back into the action, and have also added a new defendant (Dr. White) into the action.  It is unclear why Plaintiffs named Dr. Jonnala in 24LBCV00458, but Plaintiffs’ notice of motion expressly indicates that any defendant dismissed from 21STCV19312 by way of summary judgment will also be dismissed from 24LBCV00458; indeed, dismissal of Dr. Jonnala was filed on 5/30/24.

 

As will be discussed further below, this court will hear the demurrer in 24LBCV00458 prior to trial, and if any cause of action has been improperly pled, it will be dismissed accordingly. 

 

The biggest issue is the fact that Dr. White is named as a defendant in 24LBCV00458, but was never named in 21STCV19312.  This means Dr. White will necessarily not be prepared for the currently scheduled trial date of 9/30/24 in 21STCV19312. 

 

The Court finds the need to have both the wrongful death trial and the trial of the survival claims held together far outweighs any prejudice that will occur as a result of the need to continue the trial date.  The motion to consolidate is therefore granted.

Case Number: 22LBCV00049    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: S27

Willow propounded the discovery that forms the basis of this motion on 12/26/23.  SEJ served responses on 1/29/24.  On 2/21/24, Willow sent a meet and confer letter.  On 3/12/24, SEJ sent an additional meet and confer letter and offered an extension of time to further respond in exchange for an extension of time to file motions to compel further responses.  Willow filed these motions on 4/08/24. 

 

On 5/24/24, SEJ filed opposition to the motions.  Notably, the opposition was one day late, likely due to the Memorial Day holiday on 5/27/24.  The Court has read and considered the opposition despite the one-day delay in filing, but asks SEJ to ensure timely filing in the future.  The crux of the opposition is that the motions were not filed within the 45-day limit, and therefore they must be denied.

 

The Court has reviewed the evidence in this regard and finds the motions were not timely filed.  Willow’s attorney declares that the parties, on 3/12/24, appeared at a hearing on SEJ”s attorney’s motion to be relieved as counsel, and at that time, the Court extended the deadline to file motions to compel further responses until 4/08/24.  See Declaration of Stephanie C. Goldstein, ¶12.  As SEJ correctly notes in opposition to the motion, nothing in the Court’s minute order indicates there was an extension of time to file motions to compel, and there was no notice of ruling so indicating.  Additionally, SEJ’s attorney declares he telephonically conferred with SEJ’s prior attorney (who was seeking to be relieved on 3/12/24), and the prior attorney told him there was no discussion concerning discovery at the hearing.  The Court has no recollection of any discussion of a discovery motion extension at the 3/12/24 hearing.

 

Importantly, pursuant to Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1409-1410, the prior version of the Discovery Act permitted the court to extend the 45-day limit to file discovery motions, but the current version of the Discovery Act omits that ability, permitting an extension ONLY on stipulation of the parties.  Thus, even if the Court found in favor of Willow on the evidentiary issue, the purported extension would have no effect, and the motions would still be untimely. 

 

The motions are summarily denied as untimely.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Senator Corner LLC filed this action against Defendant, Hans Peter Koch for commercial unlawful detainer, alleging failure to pay rent due on the subject premises.    

 

2.     Motion to Enforce Settlement

Plaintiff moves to enforce the parties’ settlement pursuant to CCP §664.6.  The Court has the following concerns, each of which must be addressed at the hearing before the Court will grant the motion. 

 

First, Plaintiff’s notice of motion indicates the motion is based on the moving papers and the declarations of Leon Adhoot and Debra Friedman.  There is no declaration of Debra Friedman with the moving papers.  There is a declaration of Leon Adhoot, and the declaration states, at ¶3, that a copy of the parties’ stipulation for entry of judgment is attached as Exhibit A.  There are no exhibits attached to the declaration.

 

The Court has, however, located a copy of the stipulation, which was filed on 9/29/23.  The stipulation, at ¶2, indicates the parties have agreed that Defendant will cede possession of the premises, plus pay past due rent, holdover damages, attorneys’ fees, and costs by way of installment payments.  Despite the stipulation indicating possession would be ceded, Plaintiff’s motion, page 2, lines 6-7, indicates Defendant remains in possession of the premises.  Plaintiff must clarify. 

 

At ¶4, the stipulation states that a writ of possession will issue immediately, but no lockout will occur before 10/01/13.  Clearly, this date is wrong.  The parties likely meant 10/01/23, as the stipulation was filed on 9/29/23.  In any event, the Court wishes, again to clarify whether a writ of possession has been issued and whether a lockout has occurred.

 

At ¶6a, the stipulation indicates judgment will be entered “now.”  This contrasts with what Plaintiff appears to be doing, which is having judgment entered upon default, which would have properly been ¶6b, not ¶6a.  Regardless, it appears entry of judgment is proper per the parties’ stipulation.

 

At ¶7, the stipulation states, “File is to be sealed and remain sealed as long as there is no breach.  File can be unsealed with a declaration from plaintiff attorney.”  There has, to date, been no motion to seal the file, and the Court notes that the file is not sealed.  Plaintiff must clarify whether such motion will be filed and when. 

 

If Plaintiff addresses each of these issues to the satisfaction of the Court, the Court will grant the motion.  If Plaintiff fails to do so, the motion will be denied without prejudice and Plaintiff will be required to re-file the moving papers and cure the defects.
