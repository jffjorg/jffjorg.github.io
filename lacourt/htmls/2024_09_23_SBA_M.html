Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

 https://lacourt.portalscloud.com/VCourt/

 

 

*Parties with a fee waiver on file may be eligible to appear at no/reduced cost


Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.



Case Number: 23TRCV00057    Hearing Date: September 23, 2024    Dept: M

LOS ANGELES SUPERIOR COURT – SOUTHWEST DISTRICT

 

 

Honorable Gary Y. Tanaka                                                           Monday, September 23, 2024

Department M                                                                                                                        Calendar No. 12

 

 

 

 

PROCEEDINGS

 

            Ronald Williams v. Torrance Memorial Medical Center, et al.

            23TRCV00057

1.      Ronald Williams’ Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Six, and Request for Sanctions

2.      Ronald Williams’ Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center

3.      Ronald Williams’ Motion to Compel Answer to Deposition Question [and Other Numerous Requests]

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Ronald Williams’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Six, and Request for Sanctions is deemed moot, in part, and denied, in part.

 

            Ronald Williams’ Motion to Compel the Deposition of Defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center and Request for Sanctions is denied.

 

            Ronald Williams’ Motion to Compel Answer to Deposition Question [and Other Numerous Requests Including Sanctions] is denied.

 

            Background

 

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on January 6, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff’s wife, Chionese Agee, died on November 9, 2022, after surgery at defendant’s hospital. Plaintiff alleges the following cause of action: 1. Wrongful Death.

 

            Motion to Compel Further Responses to Special Interrogatories

 

Where responses to interrogatories have been served but the requesting party believes that they are deficient because the answers are evasive or incomplete, or, because an objection is without merit, that party may move for an order compelling a further response.  CCP § 2030.300(a).  Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days after service of verified responses in question, or any verified supplemental responses.  CCP § 2030.300(c).  The motion must be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration in compliance with CCP § 2016.040.  CCP § 2030.300(b).

 

Meet and Confer

 

Plaintiff set forth a meet and confer declaration in substantial compliance with CCP §§ 2031.310(b)(2) and 2016.040.  (Declaration, Neil Howard ¶¶ 11-15.)

 

Motion

 

Plaintiff moves for an order for further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Six, Numbers 161 to 186 from Defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center. The special interrogatories at issue simply seek facts that may lead to the discovery of admissible evidence in this action.

 

On June 18, 2024, Defendant served further verified responses. Therefore, the motion to compel further responses to Special Interrogatories, Set Six, is deemed moot.

 

Sanctions

 

Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is denied. It is true that further responses were only served after Plaintiff incurred the time and expense to file the motion to compel. Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states: “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.” However, the meet and confer discussions reveal that Plaintiff filed the motion prior to the time that the parties agreed that further responses would have been provided. Therefore, the request for monetary sanctions is denied.

 

Motion to Compel Deposition

 

The party noticing the deposition may move for an order compelling appearance at the deposition and production of documents, pursuant to the deposition notice, from the party deponent who fails to appear or produce materials requested in the deposition notice, and who has not served a valid objection under § 2025.410(a).  CCP § 2025.450(a). 

 

“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  CCP § 2025.450(b)(2). Plaintiff set forth a meet and confer declaration stating that Plaintiff contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. ((Declaration, Neil Howard ¶¶ 9-10.)

 

Plaintiff moves for an order compelling Defendant TMMC to produce twelve different deponents.  (See, page 2 to 4 of the notice of motion for the detailed narrative of the different categories of deponents that Plaintiff seeks to depose for this medical negligence action.)  The motion is made on the grounds that the deponents failed to appear for the noticed date of the depositions and Defendant has failed to make these deponents available for deposition. Plaintiff also seeks sanctions.

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel deposition is granted, in part. The deponents are ordered to appear for their depositions at a date and time that can be agreed upon by the parties.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendant’s request for sanctions is denied. The Court notes that the depositions were noticed with dates unilaterally chosen by the moving party. Courtesy and consideration should be provided in scheduling depositions to attempt to obtain dates that are amenable to all parties. This is especially true when considering the sheer number of depositions that were noticed.

 

Motion to Compel Further Deposition Testimony

 

“If a deponent fails to answer any question or to produce any document, electronically stored information, or tangible thing under the deponent's control that is specified in the deposition notice or a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move the court for an order compelling that answer or production.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480, subd. (a).) 

 

“This motion shall be made no later than 60 days after the completion of the record of the deposition, and shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2025.480(b).)  The motion must be accompanied by a separate statement pursuant to Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345(a)(4) and (5).

 

Where a deposition question seeks discovery of privileged information, the deponent or his or her counsel must interpose a specific objection in order to preserve the privilege or work product protection.  CCP § 2025.460(a).  Refusing to answer a deposition question on any other ground is improper.  See Stewart v. Colonial Western Agency, Inc. (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 1006, 1015.  Irrelevance alone is an insufficient ground to justify a deponent’s refusal to answer a deposition question.  Id. A refusal to answer is only justified if the questions require the disclosure of privileged matter or if the questions are manifestly irrelevant or calculated to harass.  Id.

 

Meet and Confer

 

Defendant filed a meet and confer declaration setting forth a reasonable good faith attempt at an informal resolution of each issue presented by the motion in compliance with CCP § 2025.480(b) and CCP § 2016.040.  (Decl., Neil Howard, ¶ 49.) 

 

Motion to Compel Further Deposition Testimony


Plaintiff moves for an order “to compel answer to deposition question, an order compelling Amy Letwin, R.N. to appear for a second deposition session, an order compelling defendant Torrance Memorial Medical Center to pay all costs associated with the second deposition session; and request for monetary sanctions against Defendant, Torrance Memorial Medical Center. This motion is based upon the grounds that: 1. Good cause exists for Amy Letwin, R.N. to be compelled to answer the deposition question; 2. Good cause exists for Amy Letwin, R.N. to be compelled to appear again for deposition; 3. Good cause also exists for counsel to be admonished not to interrupt the deposition and seek to coach the deponent or suggest answers; 4. Good cause also exists for counsel to be admonished not to take a break when a question is pending; 5. Good cause exists for counsel to be admonished for instructing the witness not to answer on grounds other than privilege, privacy, trade secrets or other matters statutorily or constitutionally exempt from discovery; 6. Good cause exists for counsel to be admonished for instructing the witness not to answer through unfounded objections, (see Los Angeles Superior Court Rule 3.26, and Appendix 3.A(e)(8); and 7. Good cause exists for this Court to appoint a referee to supervise the second session of the deposition of Amy Letwin, R.N.” (Notice of Motion, page 2, lines 3-23.)

 

Issue 1: Defense counsel wanted to take a break during the deposition when Plaintiff counsel did not want to take a break.

 

The motion to compel further answers is denied.

 

First, technically, this issue does not constitute a situation where a deponent refused to answer a question. Instead, Plaintiff counsel believes that no break should have been obtained because of Plaintiff’s counsel suspicion that Defense counsel wanted to further “coach” the deponent on how to answer the question. There is no authority presented to show that CCP 2025.480 governs this situation since again this is not a situation where the deponent refused to answer the question. The parties are generally tasked to act civilly in the context of a deposition and obtain consensus as to when breaks are needed during a deposition.

 

Issue 2: Questions and answers regarding active bleeding.

 

The motion to compel further answers is denied.

 

There is no instance where the deponent refused to answer a question and no instance where Defense counsel instructed the deponent not to answer the question. The objections that were asserted were appropriate with the caveat that the deponent then was free to answer the question. Instead, it is clear that Plaintiff counsel became argumentative with the deponent when he believed that the deponent was not answering the question in a manner that he believed was proper or consistent with the facts as Plaintiff counsel believed to be true. This caused a needless dispute between the parties’ counsel. The motion as to Issue 2 is denied.

 

The motion to compel further answers to deposition questions as well as all the other requests sought by Plaintiff, including the appointment of a referee, is denied.

 

Plaintiff’s request for monetary sanctions is denied.

 

Finally, the Court notes that Plaintiff has on calendar at least 11 additional motions to compel. The Court would simply advise the parties to review the discovery disputes, perhaps reconsider how the parties have been acting toward each other and attempt to obtain a resolution to the disputes without the necessity for Court intervention.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

PROCEEDINGS

 

Jalula Roberts v. Torrance Post Acute, LLC, et al.

            24TRCV00364

1.      Jalula Roberts’ Motion for Trial Preference

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Jalula Roberts’ Motion for Trial Preference is granted.  

 

Background

           

            Plaintiff filed the Complaint on January 31, 2024. April 19, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Plaintiff suffered injuries while in the care of Defendants.  Plaintiffs set forth the following causes of action: 1. Negligence 2. Elder Abuse 3. Violation of Health and Safety Code 1430.  

 

            Motion for Preferential Trial Date

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 36 states, in relevant part:

“(a) A party to a civil action who is over 70 years of age may petition the court for a preference, which the court shall grant if the court makes both of the following findings:

(1) The party has a substantial interest in the action as a whole.

(2) The health of the party is such that a preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing the party's interest in the litigation.

(f) Upon the granting of such a motion for preference, the court shall set the matter for trial not more than 120 days from that date and there shall be no continuance beyond 120 days from the granting of the motion for preference except for physical disability of a party or a party's attorney, or upon a showing of good cause stated in the record. Any continuance shall be for no more than 15 days and no more than one continuance for physical disability may be granted to any party.”

 

CCP § 36.5 states: “An affidavit submitted in support of a motion for Preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36 may be signed by the attorney for the party seeking preference based upon information and belief as to the medical diagnosis and prognosis of any party. The affidavit is not admissible for any purpose other than a motion for preference under subdivision (a) of Section 36.”

 

“[T]he decision to grant or deny a preferential trial setting rests at all times in the sound discretion of the trial court in light of the totality of the circumstances.” Salas v. Sears, Roebuck & Co. (1986) 42 Cal.3d 342, 344. “Where a party meets the requisite standard for calendar preference under subdivision (a), preference must be granted. No weighing of interests is involved.” Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 535 (granting the mandatory motion for trial preference and disregarding Defendant’s arguments that the Court must balance the interests of the parties, and, further commenting that arguments regarding due process concerns were baseless). “We conclude that section 36, subdivision (a), as construed by this court in Rice v. Superior Court (1982) 136 Cal.App.3d 81 [185 Cal.Rptr. 853], is mandatory and absolute in its application and does not allow a trial court to exercise the inherent or statutory general administrative authority it would otherwise have.” Koch-Ash v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 689, 692 (reversing the trial Court’s order to stay an action which was entitled to a mandatory trial preference and commenting that when an individual qualifies for a preference under Section 36(a) no discretion is afforded to the Court to deny a preference).

 

Motion for Preference

 

Plaintiff moves for a trial preference pursuant to CCP § 36(a), based on Plaintiff’s age and health condition.  

 

Plaintiff submitted evidence that Plaintiff is over the age of 70 and that Plaintiff has a substantial interest in this action.  (Decl., Janine A. Mitchell, ¶¶ 1-8; Ex. 1.)  Plaintiff submitted adequate evidence that her health is such that a trial preference is necessary to prevent prejudicing Plaintiff.  (Id. at ¶¶ 1-8.)  “The standard under [Code of Civil Procedure Section 36] subdivision (a), unlike under subdivision (d), which is more specific and more rigorous, includes no requirement of a doctor’s declaration.” Fox v. Superior Court (2018) 21 Cal.App.5th 529, 534. Unlike other areas of the law, a declaration setting forth the health of the party in support of a motion for preference under CCP § 36(a) may be made by a conclusory and hearsay declaration from counsel.  Here, Plaintiff’s counsel adequately complied by setting forth a conclusory hearsay declaration as to the client’s health.

 

As noted above, an order granting Plaintiff’s motion for trial preference is mandatory upon a showing by Plaintiff that she has met the requirements of Section 36(a).  There is no weighing of interests involved.

 

Defendant’s opposition contends that Plaintiff’s counsel’s declaration is conclusory. However, as noted above, for purposes of this motion, Plaintiff may submit a conclusory declaration from her counsel. The Court finds that the declaration is competent and sufficient to testify as to Plaintiff’s health considering the incredibly low evidentiary threshold that has been established by case authority.

 

Defendant also raises due process concerns regarding its ability to timely prepare for trial. In ruling on a motion for preference, the Court may take into consideration whether an earlier trial date may deny a party sufficient time to adequately prepare for trial.  Peters v Superior Court (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 218, 227. The Court determines that Defendant’s due process concerns are speculative, at this time. In addition, the Court can certainly consider the parties’ arguments should they be raised at a later time regarding potentially accelerating discovery periods or shortening notice periods on dispositive motions.

 

Therefore, Plaintiff’s Motion for Preferential Trial Setting is granted.
