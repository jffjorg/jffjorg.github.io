Case Number: 22TRCV01219    Hearing Date: December 30, 2024    Dept: 8

Tentative Ruling¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

HEARING DATE:                    December 30, 2024

¿¿¿ 

CASE NUMBER:                      22TRCV01219

¿¿ 

CASE NAME:                           Jane Doe #1, et al. v. Defendant Doe School District, et al.

¿¿ 

MOVING PARTY:                Defendant, Palos Verdes Peninsula Unified School District

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiffs, Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, Jane Doe 4, and Jane Doe 5.

¿¿ ¿¿ 

TRIAL DATE:                       January 27, 2025

¿¿ ¿¿¿ 

MOTION:¿                                  (1) District’s Motion for Leave to File First Amended Cross-Complaint

                                                (2) Ex Parte Application to Continue trial

¿¿ 

Tentative Rulings:                     (1) DENIED.

                                                (2) Discuss, since Plaintiff has not had time to file a response to the ex parte application filed on December 27

 

¿¿ 

 

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

On November 14, 2022, Plaintiffs, Jane Doe 1, Jane Doe 2, Jane Doe 3, Jane Doe 4, and Jane Doe 5 (collectively “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint against Defendants, Defendant Doe School District (“Defendant”), and Defendant DOES 2 through 100. On April 11, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”). On July 18, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Negligent Supervision/Failure to Warn; and (3) Negligent Retention/Hiring. 

 

            Now, Defendant, Palos Verdes Peninsula Unified School District (“District”) files a Motion for Leave to File a Cross-Complaint (“XC”) on the following grounds: (1) “facts have come to light that establish good cause for the District seeking leave to file its XC adding Beverly Wilshire Travel as ROES 1; (2) the District will be severely prejudiced if not permitted to file this XC at this time; and (3) granting leave to file this XC will serve the interests of justice.

 

B. Procedural¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

On December 4, 2024, District filed a Motion for Leave to Amend and file XC.  On December 16, 2024, Plaintiffs filed an opposition motion. On December 20, 2024, District filed a reply brief.  

 

¿II. ANALYSIS

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

  Code of Civil Procedure section 428.50 provides: 

 

“(a) A party shall file a cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint. 

 

(b) Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial. 

 

(c)¿A party shall obtain leave of court to file any cross-complaint except one filed¿within¿the time¿specified in subdivision (a)¿or¿(b).¿ Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.” 

 

(Code Civ. Proc., § 428.50.)  

 

“A party who fails to plead a cause of action subject to the requirements of this article, whether through oversight, inadvertence, mistake, neglect, or other cause, may apply to the court for leave to amend his pleading, or to file a cross-complaint, to assert such cause at any time during the course of the action. The court, after notice to the adverse party, shall grant, upon such terms as may be just to the parties, leave to amend the pleading, or to file the cross-complaint, to assert such cause if the party who failed to plead the cause acted in good faith.  This subdivision shall be liberally construed to avoid forfeiture of causes of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 426.50.) (Emphasis added.) 

 

The Court of Appeals has explained: “The legislative mandate is clear. A policy of liberal construction of section 426.50 to avoid forfeiture of causes of action is imposed on the trial¿court. A motion to file a cross-complaint at any time during the course of the action must be granted unless bad faith of the moving party is demonstrated where forfeiture would otherwise result. Factors such as oversight, inadvertence, neglect, mistake or other cause, are insufficient grounds to deny the motion unless accompanied by bad faith.” (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank¿(1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98–99.) “‘‘Bad faith,’ is defined as ‘[t]he opposite of ‘good faith,’ generally implying or involving actual or constructive fraud, or a design to mislead or deceive another, or a neglect or refusal to fulfill some duty or some contractual obligation, not prompted by an honest mistake . . ., but by some interested or sinister motive[,] . . . not simply bad judgment or negligence, but rather . . . the conscious doing of a wrong because of dishonest purpose or moral obliquity; . . . it contemplates a state of mind affirmatively operating with furtive design or ill will.  [Citation.]’  [Citations.]’  [Citation.]”  (Id. at 100.) 

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).) 

 

B.    Discussion

 

District files this Motion for Leave to File Amended Cross-Complaint on the grounds that it states that Gary Poe’s declaration on December 2, 2024, confirmed the involvement of an entity known as Beverly Wilshire Travel. (Declaration of Harry W. Harrison (“Harrison Decl.”), ¶ 5, Exhibit C.) Thus, District seeks to name Beverly Wilshire Travel as Roe 1 in its already existing cross-complaint as it reasonably believes that Beverly Wilshire Travel may properly bear liability for the events giving rise to this lawsuit and any damages allegedly sustained by Plaintiff. District argues that granting its motion is in the interest of justice and judicial economy so that a single jury can be able to fully evaluate and render a verdict on liability and damages. In making its arguments, District relies on Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94 (“Silver Organizations”).

In Silver Organizations, the Court of Appeals explained that “The legislative mandate is clear. A policy of liberal construction of section 426.50 to avoid forfeiture of causes of action is imposed on the trial court. A motion to file a cross-complaint at any time during the course of the action must be granted unless bad faith of the moving party is demonstrated where forfeiture would otherwise result. Factors such as oversight, inadvertence, neglect, mistake or other cause, are insufficient grounds to deny the motion unless accompanied by bad faith.” (Silver Organizations Ltd. v. Frank¿(1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 94, 98–99.) “‘‘Bad faith,’ is defined as ‘[t]he opposite of ‘good faith,’ generally implying or involving actual or constructive fraud, or a design to mislead or deceive another, or a neglect or refusal to fulfill some duty or some contractual obligation, not prompted by an honest mistake . . ., but by some interested or sinister motive[,] . . . not simply bad judgment or negligence, but rather . . . the conscious doing of a wrong because of dishonest purpose or moral obliquity; . . . it contemplates a state of mind affirmatively operating with furtive design or ill will.  [Citation.]’  [Citations.]’  [Citation.]”  (Ibid. at 100.) 

In Silver Organizations, the plaintiffs had brought an action on a promissory note against defendants, who postponed retaining an attorney while they attempted to settle the action. (Silver Organizations, supra, ) 217 Cal.App.3d at 94.) After settlement negotiations proved unsuccessful, the case was set for trial. (Ibid.) The defendants quickly sought counsel, but that representation was belatedly denied due to an asserted conflict of interest issue. (Id. at 101.) Promptly, defendants pursued other legal representation for trial, finding an attorney to represent them who successfully moved for a slight trial continuance (43 days). (Ibid.) During that time, the attorney pursued investigation and formal discovery, including the deposition of one of the plaintiffs. (Id. at 98.) In doing so, the attorney not only discovered additional grounds for defending his clients, but also found grounds to file a cross-complaint which was expeditiously filed, and subsequently denied. (Id. at 101.) The Court of Appeals found that the trial court erred in its denial, stating that because  “the trial court [did] not provide[] a reason for its denial of a motion to file a compulsory cross-compliant,” they implied its denial was based on a finding that the moving party has not acted in good faith. (Id. at 100.) However, the Court of Appeals review of the entire record “failed to reveal [to them], directly or inferentially, any substantial evidence of bad faith by the appellants.” (Ibid.) The First District Court found that “[l]ooking at the entire period between the filing of the complaint and the denial of the section 426.50 motion, a time frame of less than six months, we [found] noting in appellants’ words or conduct remotely suggesting dishonest purpose, moral obliquity, sinister motive, furtive design or ill will.” (Ibid.) The Court acknowledged that while it could have been “argued that appellants, acting as their own counsel, may have been guilty of neglect, inadvertence or oversight, thereby causing delay, section 426.50 expressly disallows denial of amotion based on these grounds. There must be bad faith and this record fails to demonstrate that element.” (Id. at 101.)

Here, this Court finds District’s reliance on Silver Organizations unavailing. Although unpublished, the Court turns the Parties’ attention to the First District Court’s subsequent 2014 decision of U.S. Bank National Association v. Singh (2014 WL 5796367) (“Singh”). There, the very Court of Appeal that decided Silver Organization, distinguished Singh when finding that, despite a trial court’s failure to “make express findings on the issue” of whether there had been a showing of bad faith, “substantial evidence support[ed] an implied finding that the [defendants] filed the cross-compliant in bad faith and for the improper purpose of delaying…” (p. 4.) The First District Court of Appeals stated that “[t]he cross-complaint was filed only days after the Singhs asked the court to continue the hearing on U.S. Bank’s motion for summary judgment and only weeks before the scheduled trial date. The Singhs concede[d] that the proposed cross-complaint [was] based, at least partially, on facts that occurred before U.S. Bank filed this action, and the Singhs were aware of these facts months before they filed their motion for leave. Indeed, many of the legal theories set forth in the Singhs’ proposed cross-complaint were also asserted in the answer the Singhs filed 18 months earlier…While these facts may support inferences other than bad faith, such as inadvertence and neglect, on substantial-evidence review we must defer to inferences favoring the trial court’s ruling.” (p. 5.)

Similarly to Singh, this Court finds that substantial evidence supports an implied finding that District is acting in bad faith. First, the Court recognizes that this is not the first time District is filing a motion for leave to file cross-complaint. As noted in the opposition brief, the trial date was initially set for September 24, 2024, but on July 17, 2024, District filed an ex parte application to continue trial, citing “new evidence” regarding World Horizons Institute, a party it claimed helped plan the Europe Trip and was indispensable to the case. In fact, District’s ex parte motion states, “evidence recently obtained through preparation for the deposition of District’s person most qualified (held on June 21, 2024) led to the discovery of a potentially indispensable party, namely World Horizons Institute, the travel agency responsible for conducting the Europe Trips at issue in this case.” (Ex Parte Application, p. 6.) In footnote 1 of the ex parte application, District stated that “[t]his information was not readily available previously because Plaintiffs have all alleged in pleadings, responded in discovery and testified in their respective depositions that the Europe Trip was sponsored by Rolling Hills High School.”  (Ex Parte Application, p. 6, n.1.) This Court continued the trial based on District’s contentions. Despite this, Plaintiffs argue that District has not conducted any discovery regarding World Horizons Institute.

If the District was reviewing evidence during the preparation for the deposition of their person most qualified on June 21, 2024, the Court wonders why District would not also review Volume II of Poe’s deposition taken September 28, 2023. In that deposition, Poe confirmed that when he arranged the trips between 1978 and 1989, that he used Beverly Wilshire Travel. (Declaration of Jennifer R. Liakos (“Liakos Decl.”), ¶ 4, Exhibit 3, p. 215:17-23.) The Court is unable to review the rest of the deposition transcripts as they are not included, but the following question posed to Poe appears to be even more information regarding Beverly Wilshire Travel, which would align with the information District claims they just received from Poe’s December 2, 2024 declaration. Certainly, the deposition testimony from September 28, 2023, “confirms [Beverly Wilshire Travel’s] involvement during the pertinent time period,” the stated reason District has for just now seeking to add them in as ROE 1. (Harrison Decl., ¶ 5.) Thus, based on this reasoning alone, as stated in the moving declaration, the Court does not find good cause to grant District leave to amend as the declaration confirms nothing that was not already known about Beverly Wilshire Travel over a year prior. District even concedes, in the 5th paragraph of Harrison’s declaration, that Beverly Wilshire Travel has been previously referenced during the course of litigation.

Plaintiffs’ opposition brief also highlights the other times Beverly Wilshire Travel have been referenced in this litigation. On April 17, 2024, Jane Doe 2 produced a copy of the 1983 Europe Trip Itinerary and Travel Plan in her Responses to the District’s Request for Production of Documents as JD2_0014-JD2_0039. (Liakos Decl., ¶ 6, Exhibit 4.) Additionally, Stephanie Simon, a third-party witness who attended the 1983 Europe Trip referenced the same Beverly Hills travel document in her September 2024 deposition, and it was attached as an exhibit. (Liakos Decl., ¶¶ 9-10, Exhibits 7-8.) Plaintiffs confirm that District’s attorneys even asked questions of both Garry Poe and Stephanie Simon about Beverly Wilshire Travel’s involvement in planning the trip. Thus, District’s answer including an affirmative defense for indemnity, ROEing in a separate travel agency, not conducting any discovery on that travel agency, and attempting to ROE in a separate travel agency a day before the motion for summary judgment hearing, and with trial next month militate against yet another trial continuance when the identity of Beverly Wilshire Travel was known throughout litigation.

            Lastly, this Court holds that this motion can be denied on procedural grounds as well. Harrison’s declaration was required to specify the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).) Here, while District claims to have “confirmed” something from Poe’s December 2, 2024 declaration, the Harrison Declaration fails to state the date of discovery and reason for delay. Confirmation of an already known fact is not discovery of that fact.

A.    Legal Standard

 

Court approval is required for all settlements of a minor’s claim. (Prob. Code §§ 3500, 3600, et seq.; Code Civ. Proc. § 372.) “‘[W]ithout trial court approval of the proposed compromise of the ward’s claim, the settlement cannot be valid.  [Citation.] [¶] Nor is the settlement binding [on the minor] until it is endorsed by the trial court.’” (Pearson v. Proc., § 372, subd. (a)(1).) Alternatively, the petitioner may file a declaration demonstrating that he or she has a right to compromise the minor’s claim under Cal. Probate Code section 3500.  Pursuant to California Rules of Court (“CRC”) Rule 7.950, a petition for court approval of a compromise of a pending action for a minor “mut be verified by the petitioner and must contain a full disclosure of all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise, covenant, settlement, or disposition.” 

 

Regarding the substance of the Petition, to obtain court approval of the settlement of a minor’s claims, the petitioner must file a complete and “verified petition for approval of the settlement and must disclose ‘all information that has any bearing upon the reasonableness of the compromise.’ [Citations.]” (Barnes v. Western Heritage Ins. Co. (2013) 217 Cal.App.4th 249, 256; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 7.950.) (Italics added.)

Petition notes that they understand that if the compromise proposed in this petition is approved by this Court and consummated, the claimant will never be able to recover any more compensation from the settling defendants even if the claimant’s injuries turn out to be more serious than they now appear.

 

Further, the Petition notes that Defendant, Academy for Early Learning agreed to the settlement of a lump sum payment of $15,000. This Petition notes that the claimant’s medical expenses – including medical expenses paid by petitioner, Medicare, Medi-Cal, and private insurers – to be paid or reimbursed from proceeds of the settlement of judgment include: (1) Total medical expenses before any reductions: $445.79; (2) Total medical expenses paid: $445.79; (3) Total of negotiated, contractual, or statutory reductions, if any: $111.44; and (5) Total amount of statutory or contractual liens, if any: $334.35.

 

The Petition also accounts for claimant’s attorney’s fees and all other expenses (except for medical expenses), including expenses advanced by claimant’s attorney or paid or incurred by petitioner, to be reimbursed from proceeds of settlement of judgment. The Petition asserts that the name, branch, and address of each depository are specified in attachment 18b(2) which states: Wels Fargo – 400 S. Market St., Inglewood, CA 90301.

 

The Court GRANTS this motion as all information provided in the moving papers tends to bear upon the reasonableness of the compromise.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

¿ For the foregoing reasons, all three of Plaintiff’s Petition for Minor’s Compromise is GRANTED.

A.    Legal Standard

 

To be eligible for admission pro hac vice, the applicant must be admitted to practice before a U.S. court or the highest court of any state or territory, must not be a California resident, must be associated with a member of the California bar, must have noticed the California State Bar in San Francisco at least 21 calendar days before the hearing, and must have paid $50.00 to the State Bar. Further, pursuant to California Rules of Court, Rule 9.40 the moving papers must state: (1) The applicant's residence and office address; (2) The courts to which he has been admitted to practice and the dates of such admission; (3) That the applicant is a member in good standing in those courts; (4) That the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (5) The title of the court and cause in which he has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of such application and whether or not it was granted; and (6) The name, address and telephone number of the active member of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.

I. BACKGROUND¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

A. Factual¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿ 

            On March 19, 2024, Plaintiff, Larry Turner (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant, Elite Group Enterprises and EMA Risk Services. On April 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) alleging the following causes of action: (1) Motor Vehicle Negligence; (2) General Negligence; and (3) Intentional Tort.

                                                                                

            Now, Plaintiff files a Motion for Leave to File a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on the grounds that since the filing of the FAC, Plaintiff’s counsel states to have uncovered crucial mistakes in the FAC that service of summons and complaint to both Defendants would be incomplete. Plaintiff also claims to be moving on the grounds Plaintiff seeks to amend the pleading to add causes of action and legal theories based on uncovered evidence.

¿¿ 

On November 18, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Motion for Leave to Amend and file Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”). To date, no opposition brief has been filed.

 

 

¿II. ANALYSIS

 

A.    Legal Standard

 

 Leave to amend is permitted under Code of Civil Procedure section 473, subdivision (a) and section 576. The policy favoring amendment and resolving all matters in the same dispute is “so strong that it is a rare case in which denial of leave to amend can be justified. . ..” “Although courts are bound to apply a policy of great liberality in permitting amendments to the complaint at any stage of the proceedings, up to and including trial [citations], this policy should be applied only ‘where no prejudice is shown to the adverse party . . .. [citation].  A different result is indicated ‘where inexcusable delay and probable prejudice to the opposing party’ is shown. [Citation].” (Magpali v. Farmers Group (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)  

 

A motion for leave to amend a pleading must also comply with the procedural requirements of California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324, which requires a supporting declaration to set forth explicitly what allegations are to be added and where, and explicitly stating what new evidence was discovered warranting the amendment and why the amendment was not made earlier. The motion must also include (1) a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, (2) specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added, and (3) a declaration specifying the effect, necessity and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery and reasons for delay. (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324, subds. (a), (b).) 

 

B.    Discussion

 

Plaintiff states that he seeks to amend the pleading as follows: (1) add Does 1 to 20; (2) Correct mistake on item number 5, a. Defendant Elite Group Enterprises, LLC to (5) Other: a limited liability company; (3) Correct mistake on item number 5, b. remove EMA Risk Services, and remove (5) other: Insurance Company; (4) Add item number 6, a. 1 to 10, inclusive, and b. 11 to 20, inclusive; (5) Correct mistake on item number 14, a. (2) remove punitive damages; (6) Add item number 15, and MV-2 (b-f); and GN-1; (7) Add attachments One (1) Cause of Action-Motor Vehicle and Two (2) Cause of Action-General Negligence to Complaint that were not previously attached to the original complaint.

 

Plaintiff explains that since filing the FAC, he has uncovered crucial mistakes in the FAC that service of summons and complaint to both Defendants would be incomplete. Further, Plaintiff has attempted personal service of the original summons and complaint on Defendant, David Joseph Tapia, but has not been successful. Plaintiff also seeks to amend the complaint to add causes of action and legal theories based on the above evidence. Plaintiff confirms that Defendants will not be prejudiced by the granting of this motion, and that the amendments are not barred by the statute of limitations.

 

            The court DENIES this motion on the grounds that Plaintiff has failed to comply with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324. Although Plaintiff has included specifications by reference to pages and lines the allegations that would be deleted and added as well as providing a copy of the proposed and numbered amendment, Plaintiff has failed to include a declaration stating the effect, necessity, and propriety of the amendments, date of discovery, and reason for delay. The Court acknowledges that the Notice attached to the Plaintiff’s moving papers includes, in the caption, that the moving papers include a Declaration of Catalina L. Gracia, however, there is no such declaration included. As such, the Court DENIES the motion without prejudice for failure to comply with California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1324

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion for Leave to File his Second Amended Complaint is DENIED, without prejudice.

On June 24, 2024, Plaintiffs, Christin Jesus Alegria and Paola Jannette Chaname (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed a complaint, in pro per, against Defendants, Hui Q. On August 15, 2024, Plaintiffs responded in pro per with their responses. However, Defendant argues that instead of properly answering the discovery, Plaintiffs objected to each and every request/interrogatory.  Defendants thus filed motions to compel further responses.

 

            On December 3, 2024, this Court granted Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs’ Further Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Form Interrogatories, and Requests for Monetary Sanctions in a lowered amount. This Court also DENIED Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs’ Further Responses to Requests for Admission.

 

            Now, Plaintiffs file a Motion to Reconsider this Court’s granting of the Motion to Compel Further Responses to Defendant’s Demand for Production as well as this Court’s granting of Defendant’s Request for Monetary Sanctions in the amount of $2,500.

 

B. Procedural  

 

            On December 6, 2024, Plaintiffs filed this Motion for Reconsideration. On December 13, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition brief. To date, no reply brief has been filed.

 

II. ANALYSIS ¿ 

¿ 

A.    Legal Standard  

 

When a court granted or denied a prior application for an order in whole or in part, the original party making the application may make an additional application for the same order when it based upon “new or different facts, circumstances, or law in which case it shall be shown by affidavit what application was made before, when and to what judge, what order or decisions were made, and what new or different facts, circumstances, or law are claimed to be shown.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(b).) If a party fails to comply with Code of Civil Procedure § 1008(b), the court may revoke or set aside on ex parte motion any order following the application. (Id.)  If a change in law warrants reconsideration of a prior order entered, the court may make a motion on its own and enter a new order. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(c).) An application that fails to comply with CCP § 1008 cannot be considered. (Code Civ. Proc. § 1008(e).) 

 

Section 1008 is “the clear legislative intent to restrict motions to reconsider circumstances where a party offers the court some fact or authority that was not previously considered by it” and could not have been considered by it.¿ (Gilberd v. AC Transit (1995) 32 Cal.App.4th 1494, 1500.)¿ Section 1008 is the exclusive means for modifying, amending or revoking an order.¿ That limitation is expressly jurisdictional.”¿ (Id. at p. 1499.)¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

“The burden under section 1008 is comparable to that of a party seeking a new trial on the ground of newly discovered evidence: the information must be such that the moving party could not, with reasonable diligence, have discovered or produced it at the trial.”¿ (New York Times Co. v. Superior Court (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 206, 212-13.)¿ There is a strict requirement or diligence on the moving party; the moving party must present a satisfactory explanation for failing to provide the evidence or different facts earlier.¿ (Garcia v. Hejmadi (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 674, 690.)¿ Evidence obtained after a is not ground for reconsideration where there was no showing why evidence could not have been obtained earlier.¿ (Jones v. P.S. Develop. Co., Inc. (2008) 166 Cal.App.4th 707, 725, overruled on other grounds in Reid v. Google, Inc. (2010) 50 Cal.4th 512, 530 [discussing evidence discovered after a hearing on a motion for summary judgment].)¿

 

B.    Discussion

 

In their moving papers, Plaintiffs highlight that they relied on Defense Counsel, Larry Rothman’s September 25, 2024 correspondence letter that granted them until October 18, 2024, to provide verified responses to discovery requests. Thus, Plaintiffs contend that Larry Rothman’s October 7, 2024 filing of the Motions to Compel Further were premature. Moreover, Plaintiffs state that they complied with the October 18, 2024 deadline.

 

In Defendant’s opposition brief, he asserts that these “new” facts and/or circumstances, are not in fact new. Although Plaintiffs’ untimely opposition brief was filed on the same day the Motions to Compel Further were heard (supposedly because Plaintiffs claim to not have known about this hearing until December 2, 2024), the Court nonetheless considered the opposition brief in making its ruling. The Court agrees that these facts do not constitute “new facts and/or circumstances” under Code of Civil Procedure section 1008, subdivision (a).

 

Here, although the Court acknowledges that Plaintiffs relied on Defense Counsel’s correspondence, and believed they were abiding by an extended deadline, the Court emphasizes that Defense Counsel had no way of knowing this. In offering to extend the deadline to serve Plaintiffs’ responses, Defense Counsel also requested an extension of time to file the Motions to Compel Further. Having received no response from Plaintiffs, Defense Counsel abided by his deadline.

 

 

III. CONCLUSION¿ 

 

            Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ Motion for Reconsideration is DENIED. The Court’s December 3, 2024 order is to stand, and Plaintiffs are required to provide verified further responses, without objection, as well as the production of the responsive documents as to Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Demand for Production and Form Interrogatories on or before December 31, 2024. Plaintiffs are also required to pay Defense Counsel sanctions in the amount of $2,500 on or before December 31, 2024.

 

            Defenant is ordered to give notice.
