Judge Joel L. Lofton, Department X

(2)

MOTIONS TO COMPEL DISCOVERY (SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE AND SET TWO, REQUEST FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENT, SET ONE AND SET TWO)

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1) Plaintiff The Bird and The Bear LLC

                                                (2) Defendant International Mac Construction Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY:     (1) Defendant International Mac Construction Inc.

                                                (2) Plaintiff The Bird and The Bear LLC

 

SERVICE:                             (1) Filed May 21, 2024

                                                (2) Filed April 8 and 19, 2024

 

OPPOSITION:                      (1) Filed May 30, 2024

                                                (2) Filed May 30, 2024

 

REPLY:                                 (1) N/A

                                                (2) N/A

 

RELIEF REQUESTED       (1) An order relieving Plaintiff of its deemed admissions (made by 5/8/2024 Court Order) and permitting it to serve a new or amended response to Defendant’s Request for Admissions, Set One.

(2) An order compelling Plaintiff to provide objection-free responses to Defendant’s discovery requests, and sanctions.       

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to be relieved from deemed admissions is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to respond to Defendant’s Request for Admissions, Set One, within 10 days.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel discovery and request for sanctions are DENIED.        

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Relief from deemed admissions

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.300 provides in relevant part: “[t]he court may permit withdrawal or amendment of an admission only if it determines that the admission was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect, and that the party who obtained the admission will not be substantially prejudiced in maintaining that party's action or defense on the merits.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.300, subd, (b), emphasis added.) This includes “deemed admissions” ordered by the court under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280(b), as well as admissions expressly made by a party.  (Wilcox v. Birtwhistle (1999) 21 Cal.4th 973, 979.) The trial court’s discretion in ruling on a motion to withdraw or amend an admission is not unlimited, but must be exercised in conformity with the spirit of the law and in a manner that serves the interests of justice.  (New Albertsons, Inc. v. Superior Court (2008) 168 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1420.)

 

Compel discovery (interrogatories and requests for production of document)

 

A court is permitted to intervene if a party fails to timely serve responses to discovery requests. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290; 2031.300;¿Sinaiko¿Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants¿(2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 403.) A party that fails to serve a timely response to the discovery request waives any objection to the request, including one based on privilege or the protection of attorney work product. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(a), 2031.300(a);¿Sinaiko¿Healthcare Consulting, Inc., supra, 148 Cal.App.4th (“Sinaiko”), at 404.) The propounding party may move the court for orders compelling responses to interrogatories and demands for inspection. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(b), 2031.300(b);¿Sinaiko at 404.)

  

If a propounding party moves for and obtains a court order compelling a response, the court shall impose monetary sanctions against the party failing to timely respond to interrogatories and demands for inspection unless that party acted with substantial justification or the sanction would otherwise be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c);¿Sinaiko at 404.)¿ 

 

DISCUSSION

 

Relief from deemed admissions

 

On January 11, 2024, Plaintiff’s previous counsel had a life-threatening health emergency requiring immediate heart surgery and became unable to represent Plaintiff abruptly. (Declaration of Phillip R. Geurts (“Geurts Decl.”) ¶¶ 5-7, 11; Declaration of Larry Finch (“Finch Decl.”) ¶ 6.) Plaintiff commenced a diligent search for new counsel, which was difficult because the case is in the later stages of discovery. (Finch Decl.¶¶ 8-9.) During the time Plaintiff was seeking new counsel, Defendant served on Plaintiff’s ex-counsel two sets of discovery. (Geurts Decl.¶ 10; Finch Decl.¶ 10.) None of these were served on Plaintiff, and service was rejected by Plaintiff’s former counsel. (Geurts Decl.¶ 10; Finch Decl.¶ 11.) Plaintiff was not served with any subsequent motions or other papers related to compelling those requests. (Geurts Decl.¶ 10; Finch Decl.¶ 13.) Because Plaintiff had no knowledge or understanding about how to respond to any of the Requests for Admission, Set One, or any of the other discovery requests for that matter, Plaintiff failed to serve timely responses. Plaintiff diligently sought new counsel, which he was able to secure within about three months after abruptly losing its former attorney. (Finch Decl.¶ 17.)

 

Defendant will not be prejudiced by relieving Plaintiff of the deemed admissions. A trial date has not been set. Allowing Plaintiff to respond to the admissions serves the public policy in favor of deciding cases on their merits.

 

Plaintiff’s motion is GRANTED.

 

Compel discovery (interrogatories and requests for production of document)

 

On February 15, 2024, Defendant MAC through the Ryu Law Firm (Defendant MAC’s

prior counsel), propounded Special Interrogatories, Form Interrogatories, Set One, and Request for Production of Documents, Set One, on Plaintiff through the Geurts Law Firm (Plaintiff’s former counsel) via electronic service. (See, Porto Decl., Exhibits A, B, C and D.) On March 11, 2024, Defendant MAC through counsel, Magarian & DiMercurio, APLC, propounded Form Interrogatories, Set Two and Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, on Plaintiff through the Geurts Law Firm (Plaintiff’s former counsel) via electronic service. (See, Porto Decl., Exhibits E and F.) Plaintiff failed to timely respond, and Defendant filed the instant motions.

 

            In opposition, Plaintiff’s new counsel declares that Plaintiff has since served the requested discovery responses. (Chen Decl., ¶ 13.) Once a party has provided responses to the discovery sought, the moving party’s motion is moot. (Sinaiko at 408-409.) Defendants did not deny this. If Defendants are unsatisfied with Plaintiff’s discovery responses, they may file motions to compel further.

 

            Accordingly, Defendant’s five motions to compel discovery are denied as MOOT.

 

Sanctions

 

            Defendant requests sanctions in connection with its motions. The Court finds that the circumstances rendering Plaintiff’s previous counsel unable to represent Plaintiff “make the imposition of the sanction unjust” within the meaning of CCP sec. 2031.300(c). Plaintiff’s new counsel only substituted into this case on about May 8, 2024. (Chen Decl., ¶ 4.)

 

CONCLUSION

 

            Plaintiff’s motion to be relieved from deemed admissions is GRANTED. Plaintiff is ordered to respond to Defendant’s Request for Admissions, Set One, within 10 days.

 

            Defendant’s motion to compel discovery and request for sanctions are DENIED.

BACKGROUND

            This case arises out of Plaintiffs Jennifer Penez (“Penez”) and Jose Holguin’s (“Holguin”) (collectively “Plaintiffs”) wrongful death claim as successors-in-interest to Jazlyn Katana Holguin (“Decedent”). Plaintiffs allege that leased property located at 2358 Caldero Lane, Montrose, CA 91020 from Defendants Heewon Chung and Mi Seon Chung (“the Chungs”).

Plaintiffs allege that the pool located in the backyard of the Subject Property was improperly maintained and lacked adequate safety measures. The swimming pool was not equipped with any enclosure that isolated it from the residence; there was no pool cover; the doors of the residence that provided direct access to the swimming pool were not equipped with any alarms; the doors of the residence that provided direct access to the swimming pool were not self-closing or self-latching; and the swimming pool was not equipped with any other safety protections that would prevent unauthorized entry into the swimming pool or alert Plaintiffs of any unauthorized entry.  

Plaintiffs further allege that Defendant Del Rey Pool Plastering, Inc. (“Del Rey”) was hired to replaster the pool and Defendant Leslie’s Poolmart, Inc. (“Leslie’s”) was hired to install a pool pump. Plaintiffs allege that each performed work without obtaining a permit in violation of County of Los Angeles Code of Ordinances Title 26. Plaintiffs allege that Del Rey and Leslie’s failed to acknowledge the gross lack of safety protections for the swimming pool, failed to warn Plaintiff of the lack of adequate safety precautions, and/or failed to provide notice to Plaintiffs, or the Chungs of the drowning prevention requirements of California’s Swimming Pool Safety Act. Plaintiffs allege that pursuant to California’s Swimming Pool Safety Act, when a building permit is required for the construction or remodeling of a swimming pool, any person engaging in the permitted work on the swimming pool must give the consumer notice of the drowning prevention safety requirements enumerated therein.

Plaintiffs allege that Decedent fell into the pool on February 9, 2021, and drowned because of the lack of safety features at the Subject Property. Plaintiffs allege that Decedent was declared dead on February 16, 2021.

            Plaintiffs filed a first amended complaint (“FAC”) on March 28, 2022, alleging three causes of action for (1) negligence/premises liability (wrongful death), (2) negligence (wrongful death), and (3) survival action.

In addition, Plaintiffs’ application for good faith settlement determination as to the settlement with Defendants Del Rey Pool Plastering, Inc. Alfredo Preciado, and Jose Maria Preciado is GRANTED.

LEGAL STANDARD

 

CCP § 877.6, subdivision (a)(1), provides, in relevant part, that, on noticed motion, “[a]ny party to an action wherein it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co-obligors on a contract debt shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith of a settlement entered into by the plaintiff . . . and one or more alleged tortfeasors or co-obligors . . . .”  “A determination by the court that the settlement was made in good faith shall bar any other joint tortfeasor or co-obligor from any further claims against the settling tortfeasor or co-obligor for equitable comparative contribution, or partial or comparative indemnity, based on comparative negligence or comparative fault.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (c).  Although a determination that a settlement was in good faith does not discharge any other party from liability, “it shall reduce the claims against the others in the amount stipulated” by the settlement.  Code Civ. Proc., § 877, subd. (a).

 

“The party asserting the lack of good faith shall have the burden of proof on that issue.”  Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (d).

 

In Tech-Bilt, Inc. v. Woodward-Clyde & Associates (1985) 38 Cal.3d 488, 499, the California Supreme Court identified the following nonexclusive factors courts are to consider in determining if a settlement is in good faith under § 877.6: “a rough approximation of plaintiffs’ total recovery and the settlor's proportionate liability, the amount paid in settlement, the allocation of settlement proceeds among plaintiffs, and a recognition that a settlor should pay less in settlement than he would if he were found liable after a trial.  Other relevant considerations include the financial conditions and insurance policy limits of settling defendants, as well as the existence of collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct aimed to injure the interests of nonsettling defendants.” 

 

The evaluation of whether a settlement was made in good faith is required to “be made on the basis of information available at the time of settlement.”  Tech-Bilt, Inc., supra, 38 Cal.3d at p. 499. “‘[A] defendant’s settlement figure must not be grossly disproportionate to what a reasonable person, at the time of the settlement, would estimate the settling defendant’s liability to be.’ [Citation.]”  Ibid.

 

“The party asserting the lack of good faith, who has the burden of proof on that issue (§ 877.6, subd. (d)), should be permitted to demonstrate, if he can, that the settlement is so far ‘out of the ballpark’ in relation to these factors as to be inconsistent with the equitable objectives of the statute.  Such a demonstration would establish that the proposed settlement was not a ‘settlement made in good faith’ within the terms of section 877.6.”  Id. at pp. 499-500.

 

In City of Grand View Terrace v. Superior Court (1987) 192 Cal.App.3d 1251, 1261, the court provided the following guidance regarding a motion for a good faith settlement determination: 

 

This court notes that of the hundreds of motions for good faith determination presented for trial court approval each year, the overwhelming majority are unopposed and granted summarily by the trial court.  At the time of filing in many cases, the moving party does not know if a contest will develop.  If each motion required a full recital by declaration or affidavit setting forth a complete factual response to all of the Tech-Bilt factors, literally thousands of attorney hours would be consumed and inch-thick motions would have to be read and considered by trial courts in an exercise which would waste valuable judicial and legal time and clients’ resources. . . . That is to say, when no one objects, the barebones motion which sets forth the ground of good faith, accompanied by a declaration which sets forth a brief background of the case is sufficient. 

 

If the good faith settlement is contested, section 877.6, subdivision (d), sets forth a workable ground rule for the hearing by placing the burden of proving the lack of good faith on the contesting party.  Once there is a showing made by the settlor of the settlement, the burden of proof on the issue of good faith shifts to the nonsettlor who asserts that the settlement was not made in good faith.  If contested, declarations by the nonsettlor should be filed which in many cases could require the moving party to file responsive counterdeclarations to negate the lack of good faith asserted by the nonsettling contesting party. 

 

Id., at 1260-1261 [citation omitted].

 

DISCUSSION

 

I.                Motion to Challenge Good Faith of Del Rey and Plaintiffs’ Settlement

 

Plaintiffs filed an application for a determination that their settlement with Defendant Del Rey for $1,000,000 is in good faith under Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6.

 

Defendant Leslie’s has filed a motion to challenge the good faith determination. Leslie’s first argues that the application was not timely filed or served because the application served with incomplete, as it did not contain any of the evidentiary documents referenced by and incorporated into the application including the written settlement agreement between Plaintiffs and Del Rey and the declarations of the Preciados. These documents were not served upon counsel of Defendant until March 8, 2024, when they were emailed under the caption of “Notice of Errata.” Defendant contends that the extremely late service of the documents upon which the application relies occurred only four days prior to the statutory deadline to file the present motion and is prejudicial.

Any party to an action in which it is alleged that two or more parties are joint tortfeasors or co- obligors shall be entitled to a hearing on the issue of the good faith settlement entered into by the plaintiff and one or more alleged tortfeasors. (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (a)(1).) “In the alternative, a settling party may give notice of settlement to all parties and to the court, together with an application for determination of good faith settlement and a proposed order.

The application shall indicate the settling parties, and the basis, terms, and amount of the settlement. The notice, application, and proposed order shall be given by certified mail, return receipt requested, or by personal service. Proof of service shall be filed with the court. Within 25 days of the mailing of the notice, application, and proposed order, or within 20 days of personal service, a nonsettling party may file a notice of motion to contest the good faith of the settlement. If none of the nonsettling parties files a motion within 25 days of mailing of the notice, application, and proposed order, or within 20 days of personal service, the court may approve the settlement.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (a)(2).)

However, the application filed and served contained the requirements under Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (a)(2), such as the settling parties, the basis, terms and amount of settlement. Although the attachments were missing, a written settlement agreement need not be executed and presented as long as the court and all the parties are timely advised of the important terms of the settlement. (Alcal Roofing & Insulation v. Superior Court (1992) 8 Cal. App. 4th 1121, 1127.) Here, Defendant does not argue that it was unaware of the important terms of the settlement on the application alone without the attachments. In addition, the statute does not require that a declaration of insurance be provided such that the failure to include the declaration would require a denial of the application. Moreover, while Defendant argues the late filing of the attachments was prejudicial in that it did not prepare a comprehensive motion to contest the application, Defendant has nevertheless briefed its motion on the merits, and the Court is unable to determine how Defendant has been prejudiced.

 

Next, the court notes that Leslie’s contests the motion for a good faith settlement determination, and as a result, “ha[s] the burden of proof on that issue.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (d).)

 

Leslie’s provides evidence that Plaintiffs are seeking economic and non-economic damages, including $265,187.08 in medical bills and $1.75 million in lost wages, and an unspecific claim for a lifetime of lost earnings on behalf of Decedent. (Blarcom Decl., ¶¶ 4-10; Exhs. 3-6.) Leslie’s also states that Plaintiffs made a $12,000,000 settlement demand to Leslie’s, evidencing their intent to seek tens of millions of dollars. (Id., ¶¶ 9-10.)

 

Leslie’s argues that Plaintiffs entire theory of liability hinges upon whether Del Rey and Leslie’s were required to obtain building permits for the services performed on the pool. Leslie’s contends that Del Rey performed the greatest amount of work at the pool, and its work could have required a permit. Leslie’s thus argues that its proposed settlement is simply insufficient as a matter of law given the damages sought by Plaintiffs and its potential liability.

The court finds that Leslie’s has not shown that Del Rey’s settlement agreement with plaintiffs for $1 million is so far ‘out of the ballpark’ in relation to its proportionate share of plaintiffs’ known damages at the time of settlement.

Although Leslie’s points to Plaintiffs settlement demand of $12 million, Leslie’s does not cite to any authority for the proposition that a settlement demand reflects a plaintiff’s total recovery. There may be strategic reasons for making a settlement demand. Thus, Leslie’s own evidence shows that Plaintiffs’ approximate total recovery this far is a little more than $2 million, and pursuant to Leslie’s own argument, Plaintiffs claims against Del Rey and Leslie’s rest on speculation. Further, Leslie’s argues that its work did not require a building permit. It further argues that Del Rey provided more work and Del Rey’s work could have triggered a need for a building permit. Importantly, Leslie’s does not argue that Del Rey did need a building permit. As such, based on the arguments presented, Del Rey’s settlement for $1 million is half of Plaintiff’s known damages and therefore is not out of the ballpark in relation to its proportionate share of liability, which appears to be attenuated.

 

Leslie’s further argues that Del Rey failed to provide evidence of its financial condition. The court notes that the Del Rey defendants provided evidence that $1 million is their policy limits. (Notice of Errata, Preciado Decl., ¶¶ 2-3.) Moreover, recent authority provides no such evidence is required.  (Cahill, supra, 194 Cal.App.4th at 968 (“As SDGE notes, in support of their section 877.6 motion, Owners did not present any evidence of their financial condition or liability insurance policy limits. However, that omission did not preclude the trial court from finding the April 2009 settlement was made in good faith. Tech–Bilt does not require settling defendants to present such evidence.”  (citation omitted)).)  Additionally, the settlement amount is reasonable “without ‘discounting’ that amount based on any purported financial insolvency or insurance limitations.”  (Ibid.)

 

Leslie’s argues that the application fails to provide the court with how the proposed settlement is to be allocated among the plaintiffs, and does not provide sufficient information as to whether there was any collusion, fraud, or tortious conduct between Plaintiffs and Del Rey.

In opposition, Plaintiffs provide that all settling parties were represented by counsel, and the negotiations were conducted at arm’s length. (Brotman Decl., ¶ 6.)

As to allocation, the court notes that the purpose of a motion for good faith settlement is to determine “whether the amount of the settlement is within the reasonable range of the settling tortfeasor's proportional share of comparative liability for the plaintiff's injuries.”  (Tech-Bilt, Inc., supra, 38 Cal.3d at p.499.)  Another purpose “underlying section 877.6 is to ease court congestion by providing an incentive to settle the entire underlying litigation.”  (Britz, Inc. v. Dow Chemical Co. (1999) 73 Cal.App.4th 177, 184.)  The purpose of a hearing for good faith settlement is not to specifically allocate how the settlement funds are distributed.  (Dole Food Co., Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 242 Cal.App.4th 894, 919 [Finding that section 877.6 does not require damages to be allocated for approval of a good faith settlement].) As explained by the Supreme Court, “the fact a nonsettling defendant may challenge the agreement's assigned value should not be interpreted as giving such defendant a right to a mini-trial on the valuation issue. The nature, extent and the procedure regarding any such challenge is left to the discretion of the trial court.”  (Abbott Ford, Inc. v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 858, 879, Fn. 23.)   

 

Moreover, the only relevance of the allocation of damages is that as to future offset due to settlement credits.  “[S]ettlement credits are predicated on the principle that while a plaintiff is entitled to full compensation for his or her proven injuries, he or she is not entitled to a ‘double recovery’ of these damages.”  (Schreiber v. Lee¿(2020) 47 Cal.App.5th 745, 760; see also Tavaglione v. Billings¿(1993) 4 Cal.4th 1150, 1158–1159 [“Regardless of the nature or number of legal theories advanced by the plaintiff, he is not entitled to more than a single recovery for each distinct item of compensable damage supported by the evidence. [Citation.] Double or duplicative recovery for the same items of damage amounts to overcompensation and is therefore prohibited.”].) 

 

However, at this point, such a determination of offset is premature.  “The reduction of an award pursuant to this section is normally applied one of two ways: (1) by the jury after evidence is submitted to it of the settlement with instructions to reduce the award by the amount paid in settlement [Citation]; or (2) by the court when the settlement is not presented to the jury [Citation].”  (May v. Miller¿(1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 404, 409.)  No jury trial has occurred and thus offset may not even be applicable as – for example – Leslie’s may prevail. 

 

As a result, Leslie’s motion to challenge the good faith settlement determination of the settlement between Plaintiffs and Del Rey is denied. In turn, Plaintiffs’ application for a good faith settlement determination is granted.

 

II.              Motion to Challenge Good Faith of the Chungs and Plaintiffs’ Settlement

 

The Chungs filed an application for a determination that their settlement with Plaintiffs for $300,000 is in good faith under Code of Civil Procedure section 877.6.

 

Leslie’s challenges the good faith of the settlement. As Leslie’s contests the motion for a good faith settlement determination, it “ha[s] the burden of proof on that issue.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 877.6, subd. (d).)

Leslie’s contends that the settlement is so far out of the ballpark in relation to the Chungs’ liability. Leslie’s argues that they are the only defendants in this case that owed a duty of care to affirmatively provide anti-drowning features at the Property as they are the Owners of the property and owed a duty of care to keep it safe.

Leslie’s provides evidence that Plaintiffs are seeking economic and non-economic damages, including $265,187.08 in medical bills and $1.75 million in lost wages, and an unspecific claim for a lifetime of lost earnings on behalf of Decedent. (Blarcom Decl., ¶¶ 4-10; Exhs. 3-6.) Leslie’s also states that Plaintiffs made a $12,000,000 settlement demand to Leslie’s, evidencing their intent to seek tens of millions of dollars. (Id., ¶¶ 9-10.)

 

In opposition, the Chung Defendants provide evidence that Plaintiffs were responsible for all pool maintenance under the lease agreement. (Chung Decl., Exh. 2.) They also argue that the argument that any additional safety measures would have prevented the accident is purely speculative. The Chung Defendants also argue the property was built in 1956 and the pool is not subject to the Swimming Pool Safety Act. The Chung Defendants also contend that Plaintiff Penez was on a conference call for work with headphones on when Decedent somehow got into the pool. Plaintiff had given Decedent a phone to occupy her and the battery had died. (Schroder Decl., ¶ 7.) Moreover, the Chungs argue that Plaintiffs never requested any safety prevention measures be installed, and thus, they were not on notice of any dangerous condition. “A plaintiff alleging injuries based on a dangerous condition must prove the defendant¿either: (1) created the dangerous condition, or (2)¿knew or should have known of the dangerous condition.”¿ (See¿Peralta v. Vons Companies, Inc.¿(2018) 24 Cal.App.5th 1030, 1036; see also¿Ortega v. Kmart Corp.¿(2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1206.)¿¿

The court finds that the settlement of $300,000 is not so out of the ballpark of Plaintiffs’ known damages of approximately $2 million in relation to the Chung Defendants’ liability in light of the potential lack of notice issues and potential comparative negligence of Plaintiffs. The court notes that settlements have been upheld as being in good faith in cases with a $25,000 settlement in a $5 million case, a $30,000 settlement in a $1 million case, a $65,000 settlement in a $7 million case, and a $50,000 settlement in a $1.425 million case. (See Cahill v. San Diego Gas & Elec. Co. (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 939, 968 [citing Bay Development, Ltd. v. Superior Court (1990) 50 Cal.3d 1012; Wysong & Miles Co. v. Western Industrial Movers (1983) 143 Cal.App.3d 278; Wilshire Ins. Co. v. Tuff Boy Holding, Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 627].)

Leslie’s further argues that the Chungs failed to provide evidence of their financial condition. The court notes that there is evidence that the settlement amount is for the policy limits. (Dorr Decl., ¶ 6; Chung Decl., ¶ 13.) Chung further declares that he cannot contribute to the settlement above and beyond payment of his insurance policy limits. In an effort to contribute as much as possible, Chung’s insurance company agreed to pay the policy limits and reimbursement of $7,950 for Plaintiffs’ portion of the mediation fee with the Hon. Richard Stone and to pay $1,000 in med pay. (Id., ¶ 15.) The Chungs do not have additional excess or umbrella insurance that would provide coverage. (Id., ¶ 13.) Moreover, recent authority provides no such evidence is required.  (Cahill, supra, 194 Cal.App.4th at 968 (“As SDGE notes, in support of their section 877.6 motion, Owners did not present any evidence of their financial condition or liability insurance policy limits. However, that omission did not preclude the trial court from finding the April 2009 settlement was made in good faith. Tech–Bilt does not require settling defendants to present such evidence.”  (citation omitted)).)  Additionally, the settlement amount is reasonable “without ‘discounting’ that amount based on any purported financial insolvency or insurance limitations.”  (Ibid.)

Next, as Leslie’s argued above with respect to its motion to challenge the settlement between Plaintiffs and Del Rey, there is no information as to how the settlement is to be allocated between Plaintiffs. Nevertheless, as stated above, allocation is not required. (Dole Food Co., Inc. supra, 242 Cal.App.4th at 919.) Further, any offsetting issue is premature.

            Lastly, the Chungs’ counsel’s declaration states that the parties mediated this case with the Hon. Richard Stone in July 2023. Defendants and Plaintiffs continued negotiations after mediation and thereafter reached the settlement that is before the court. (Schroder Decl., ¶ 11.) There was no collusion in this settlement. (Id., ¶ 12.)

As a result, Leslie’s motion to challenge the good faith settlement determination of the settlement between Plaintiffs and the Chungs is denied. In turn, the Chungs’ application for a good faith settlement determination is granted.

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Defendant Leslie’s Poolmart, Inc.’s motions to challenge the food faith settlement determination are DENIED. In turn, Defendants Heewon Chung and Mi Seon Chung’s application for a good faith settlement determination is GRANTED. In addition, Plaintiffs’ application for good faith settlement with Defendants Del Rey Pool Plastering, Inc. Alfredo Preciado, and Jose Maria Preciado is GRANTED.
