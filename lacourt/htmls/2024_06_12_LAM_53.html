Case Number: 21STCV10823    Hearing Date: June 12, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

john christian lukes ;

 

Plaintiff,

wells fargo bank, n.a. , et al.;

 

Defendants.

21STCV10823

June 12, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion to compel compliance with court order and for issue and evidentiary sanctions or, alternatively, monetary sanctions

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.   

 

RESPONDING PARTY:       Plaintiff John Christian Lukes

Motion to Compel Compliance with Court Order and for Issue and Evidentiary Sanctions or, in the alternative, Monetary Sanctions

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.’s evidentiary objections as follows:

Objection No. 1 is sustained.

Objection No. 2 is overruled.

DISCUSSION

Defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order                  (1) compelling plaintiff John Christian Lukes (“Plaintiff”) to comply with the court’s September 6, 2023 order; (2) directing Plaintiff to pay monetary sanctions to the court in the amount of $1,500 pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 177.5; (3) awarding monetary sanctions in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiff in the amount of $9,440; and (4) imposing issue and evidence sanctions against Plaintiff to preclude him from introducing or referencing any argument or evidence (i) of a purported contract with Defendant that was formed, communicated, or memorialized in writing, (ii) that the May 28, 2010 email, alleged in the First Amended Complaint (¶ 21), exists, and (iii) that the statements by Sloan and Silk to Plaintiff in 2015 regarding the portfolio were false.  Defendant moves for this relief on the ground that Plaintiff has not complied with the court’s September 6, 2023 order.

If a party engages in the misuse of the discovery process, the court may impose issue or evidence sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subds. (b), (c).)  The court may impose an issue sanction (1) “ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process[,]” or (2) that prohibits “any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (b).)  “The court may impose an evidence sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.030, subd. (c).)  “Discovery sanctions should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.  [Citation.]  If a lesser sanction fails to curb misuse, a greater sanction is warranted: continuing misuses of the discovery process warrant incrementally harsher sanctions until the sanction is reached that will curb the abuse.”  (Victor Valley Union High School District v. Superior Court of San Bernardin County (2023) 91 Cal.App.5th 1121, 1158 [internal quotations and citations omitted].)

On September 6, 2023, the court issued an order granting in part, inter alia, Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff’s further responses to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents.[1]  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, p. 6:4-5.)  The court ordered Plaintiff (1) to serve on Defendant further written responses to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, numbers 1-12, 16, 20-42, 44-49, 51-59, and 62-71; (2) to produce to Defendant all documents and things in Plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to those requests; (3) to use the search terms set forth in the proposed search terms list attached to the email from Rachel Lowe dated January 4, 2023; (4) to produce to Defendant all of the materials produced in response to Defendant’s document demands in electronic form with associated metadata; (5) to produce a privilege log in compliance with Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.240; and (6) to pay monetary sanctions to Defendant.  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, p. 6:6-20.)  The court ordered Plaintiff to provide the responses to discovery within 45 days of the date of the court’s order, and to pay sanctions within 60 days of the date of the order.  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, pp. 6:6-12, 7:15-16.) 

The court notes that, although Defendant asserts that the court’s November 8, 2023 order on the parties’ stipulation extended Plaintiff’s deadline to respond, the court disagrees.  (Mot., p. 8:13-14; Lowe Decl., ¶ 18; Lowe Decl., Ex. K.)  While the stipulation states that Plaintiff “requested 30 additional days to supplement Plaintiff’s responses” to the discovery, the stipulation does not state that Defendant stipulated to that extension, and the order does not extend Plaintiff’s deadline to respond.  (Lowe Decl., Ex. K, Nov. 8, 2023 Stip. & Order, pp. 2:23-26, 5:1-12.)  Thus, the court finds that Plaintiff was required to serve his supplemental responses and to produce responsive documents no later than 45 days after the court’s September 6, 2023 order.  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, p. 6:6-12.)

Defendant produced evidence, in support of its moving papers, establishing that Plaintiff did not comply with the court’s September 6, 2023 order.  Specifically, as of January 10, 2024 (the date Defendant filed this motion) – i.e., more than 45 days after the court’s September 6, 2023 order – Plaintiff had “not provided any supplemental written responses to [Defendant’s] Requests for Production or produced any additional documents, ESI, or metadata[.]”  (Lowe Decl., ¶ 24.)

In his opposition, Plaintiff does not dispute that he has not fully complied with the court’s order compelling him to serve written responses and to produce responsive documents to certain of Defendant’s document demands.  Instead, Plaintiff has merely (1) presented evidence establishing that he has provided a privilege log to Defendant, and (2) asserted that he needs additional time to comply with the order.  (Leonardo Decl., ¶¶ 13 [counsel informed Defendant’s counsel that he was unable to comply with the October 23, 2023 production deadline and requested a 30-day extension], 17-18 [counsel provided an initial privilege log on March 16, 2024 and an updated log on April 23, 2024], 20 [“While some progress has been made on Lukes’ emails the project is overwhelming”]; Opp., p. 7:14-16 [Plaintiff and counsel “are working toward the production of the ESI for his email”].)  

Thus, the court finds that Defendant has met its burden to show that Plaintiff has engaged in the misuse of the discovery process by (1) disobeying the court’s September 6, 2023 order compelling him to provide discovery, and (2) failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery (i.e., Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.010, subds. (g), (d).)  The court further finds that the less severe sanction of monetary sanctions, imposed by the court against Plaintiff in its September 6, 2023 order, has not produced compliance with the court’s order or the discovery process.  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, p. 7:15-16; Victor Valley Union High School District, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1158 [“The discovery statutes evince an incremental approach to discovery sanctions, starting with monetary sanctions and ending with the ultimate sanction of termination”] [internal quotations and citations omitted].)  The court therefore finds that Defendant has met its burden to show that evidence sanctions are warranted.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.030, subds. (b), (c), 2031.310, subd. (i) [“if a party fails to obey an order compelling further response, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, [or] an evidence sanction” and may also impose a monetary sanction in lieu of or in addition to that sanction].)

First, the court denies Defendant’s request to impose issue or evidence sanctions that would preclude Plaintiff from introducing or referencing argument and all evidence in support of his allegations that (1) there exists a contract between Plaintiff and Defendant, (2) the May 28, 2010 email exists, and (3) the statements made by Timothy Sloan and Stephen Silk in 2015 regarding the portfolio were false, because such sanctions are not narrowly tailored to the prejudice caused by Plaintiff’s failure to serve written responses and to produce responsive documents to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents.  (Victor Valley Union High School District, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1158 [in choosing sanctions, the court should attempt to tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery].)

Second, the court grants Defendant’s request to impose an evidence sanction precluding Plaintiff from introducing any writings (as that term is defined in Evidence Code section 250), including electronically stored information, relating to any contract alleged in Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint.

In the court’s September 6, 2023 order, the court (1) found that Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, number 2 (i) included an incomplete statement of compliance, and (ii) included objections that were without merit, and therefore (2) ordered Plaintiff to serve a further written response and to produce responsive documents to Defendant’s Requests for Production, Set One, number 2, which requested the production of all documents and communications relating to “any contract alleged in” Plaintiff’s complaint.  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, pp. 2:16-20, 6:6-12; Lowe Decl., Ex. C, Pl. Responses to Requests for Production, p. 3:25-27; Def. Sep. Statement, p. 12:13-15.)  Plaintiff did not serve further written responses to this document demand and did not produce responsive documents thereto as ordered by the court in its September 6, 2023 order.  (Lowe Decl., ¶ 24.)   Thus, the court finds that, in order to remedy the prejudice suffered by Defendant as a result of Plaintiff’s failure to serve a further written response and to produce responsive documents to this document demand, Plaintiff should be precluded from introducing writings related to any contract alleged in his First Amended Complaint.  (Victor Valley Union High School District, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1158.)

Third, the court grants Defendant’s request to impose an evidence sanction precluding Plaintiff from introducing any writings (as that term is defined in Evidence Code section 250), including electronically stored information, relating to Plaintiff’s allegation in paragraph 21 of his First Amended Complaint that “Plaintiff [John Christian Lukes] sent an email to [Timothy J.] Sloan confirming the terms of the Contract to which they had agreed[,]” including the alleged May 28, 2010 email and any written responses thereto.  (FAC ¶¶ 16 [defining “Contract” to be the finder’s fee contract formed between Cervenka and Lukes and Defendant], 21 [alleging the existence of the May 28, 2010 email].)

In the court’s September 6, 2023 order, the court (1) found that Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, number 22 (i) included an incomplete statement of compliance, and (ii) included objections that were without merit, and therefore (2) ordered Plaintiff to serve a further written response and produce responsive documents to Defendant’s Requests for Production, Set One, number 22, which requested the production of all documents and communications relating to Plaintiff’s allegation that “‘Plaintiff sent an email to Sloan confirming the terms of the Contract to which they agreed,’ including the alleged May 28, 2010 email and any responses thereto.”  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, pp. 2:16-20, 6:6-12; Lowe Decl., Ex. C, Pl. Responses to Requests for Production, p. 11:23-26; Def. Sep. Statement, p. 77:10-13.)  Plaintiff did not serve further written responses to this document demand and did not produce responsive documents thereto as ordered by the court in its September 6, 2023 order.  (Lowe Decl., ¶ 24.)   Thus, the court finds that, in order to remedy the prejudice suffered by Defendant as a result of Plaintiff’s failure to serve a further written response and to produce responsive documents to this document demand, Plaintiff should be precluded from introducing writings relating to the May 28, 2010 email.  (Victor Valley Union High School District, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1158.)

The court notes that, in Plaintiff’s opposition, Plaintiff asserts that he “testified that he does not have the email because he maintains that his email account was hacked, [and] that certain emails including that one were deleted and others are altered.”  (Mot., p. 5:18-19; Leonardo Decl., ¶ 20.)  However, Plaintiff did not (1) provide competent evidence in support of that assertion (including a copy of the transcript of Plaintiff’s deposition), or (2) serve on Defendant a further written response stating that he is unable to comply with that demand after conducting a diligent search and reasonable inquiry because the particular item has been destroyed, lost, misplaced, or stolen, or is no longer in his possession, custody, or control.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.230.)  The court further notes that Plaintiff’s initial and amended responses to this document demand did not include such a response.  (Lowe Decl., Ex. B, p. 5:25-28 [Pl. September 14, 2022 response to Request No. 22]; Lowe Decl., Ex. C, pp. 11:27-12:6 [Pl. October 21, 2022 amended response to Request No. 22].)

Fourth, the court grants Defendant’s request to impose an evidence sanction precluding Plaintiff from introducing any writings (as that term is defined in Evidence Code section 250), including electronically stored information, relating to Plaintiff’s allegation in paragraph 34 of the First Amended Complaint that “the statements by [Timothy J.] Sloan and [Stephen] Silk to Plaintiff in 2015 regarding the Portfolio transaction [as alleged in paragraphs 30 and 31 in the First Amended Complaint] were false.”

In the court’s September 6, 2023 order, the court (1) found that Plaintiff’s response to Defendant’s Requests for Production of Documents, Set One, number 35 (i) included an incomplete statement of compliance, and (ii) included objections that were without merit, and therefore (2) ordered Plaintiff to serve a further written response and to produce responsive documents to Defendant’s Requests for Production, Set One, number 35, which requested the production of all documents and communications relating to Plaintiff’s allegation in paragraph 34 of Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint “that ‘the statements by Sloan and Silk to [Plaintiff] in 2015 regarding the Portfolio were false.’”  (Sep. 6, 2023 Order, pp. 2:16-20, 6:6-12; Lowe Decl., Ex. C, Pl. Responses to Requests for Production, p. 17:22-25; Def. Sep. Statement, p. 137:20-23.)  Plaintiff did not serve further written responses to this document demand and did not produce responsive documents thereto as ordered by the court in its September 6, 2023 order.  (Lowe Decl., ¶ 24.)   Thus, the court finds that, in order to remedy the prejudice suffered by Defendant as a result of Plaintiff’s failure to serve a further written response and to produce responsive documents to this document demand, Plaintiff should be precluded from introducing writings relating to his allegation that Timothy Sloan and Stephen Silk’s statements to him in 2015 regarding the portfolio were false.  (Victor Valley Union High School District, supra, 91 Cal.App.5th at p. 1158.)

Fifth, the court grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions.[2]  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.030, subd. (a), 2031.310, subd. (i).)  The court finds that $8,150 ((4 hours x $750 hourly rate for attorney Lowe) + (1 hour x $650 hourly rate for attorney Crain) + (10 hours x $450 hourly rate for attorney Bolender)) is a reasonable amount of sanctions to impose against Plaintiff.  (Lowe Decl., ¶¶ 27-30.)

            Sixth, the court denies Defendant’s request that the court further order Plaintiff to comply with the September 6, 2023 order because the court has already issued an order compelling Plaintiff to provide the discovery set forth therein.

Seventh, the court denies Defendant’s request that the court impose sanctions against Plaintiff in the amount of $1,500, payable to the court, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 177.5.

Finally, the court recognizes that this motion concerns the production of electronically stored information.  However, while “the court shall not impose sanctions on a party or any attorney of a party for failure to provide electronically stored information that has been lost, damaged, altered, or overwritten as the result of the routine, good faith operation of an electronic information system[,]” Plaintiff did not present argument or evidence establishing that the safe harbor provisions apply.[3]  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2031.310, subds. (j)(1), 2023.030, subd. (f)(1).)

ORDER

            The court grants in part defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.’s motion to compel compliance with court order and for issue and evidence sanctions or, alternatively, monetary sanctions as follows.

            The court grants defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.’s motion for evidence sanctions against plaintiff John Christian Lukes.

            The court orders that plaintiff John Christian Lukes is prohibited from introducing into evidence any writings (as that term is defined in Evidence Code section 250), including electronically stored information, relating to: 

(1) any contract alleged in plaintiff John Christian Lukes’s First Amended Complaint, filed January 24, 2022;

(2) plaintiff John Christian Lukes’s allegation in paragraph 21 of his First Amended Complaint that “Plaintiff [John Christian Lukes] sent an email to [Timothy J.] Sloan confirming the terms of the Contract to which they had agreed[,]” including the alleged May 28, 2010 email and any written responses thereto; and

(3) plaintiff John Christian Lukes’s allegation in paragraph 34 of the First Amended Complaint that “the statements by [Timothy J.] Sloan and [Stephen] Silk to Plaintiff [John Christian Lukes] in 2015 regarding the Portfolio transaction [as alleged in paragraphs 30 and 31 in the First Amended Complaint] were false.”

            The court grants defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.’s motion for monetary sanctions against plaintiff John Christian Lukes.

            The court orders plaintiff John Christian Lukes to pay monetary sanctions to defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. in the amount of $8,150 within 30 days of the date of this order.

            The court denies all other relief requested in defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A.’s motion.

            The court orders defendant Wells Fargo Bank, N.A. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
