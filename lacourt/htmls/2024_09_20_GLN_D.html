DEPARTMENT D LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20GDCV00728    Hearing Date: September 20, 2024    Dept: D

TENTATIVE RULING

Calendar: 2
Date: 9/20/2024
Case No: 20 GDCV00728 Trial Date: October 21, 2024
Case Name: King Cobra Construction, PC v. Nieves, et al. 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO FILE CROSS-COMPLAINT

Moving Party: Defendants Probuild Company, LLC and Dixieline Builders Fund Control, Inc.  
Responding Party: Plaintiff Corazon Nieves    

RELIEF REQUESTED:
Leave to file a cross-complaint and have the First Amended Cross-Complaint be deemed filed.  
RELEVANT FACTS:
The complaint in the lead case in these consolidated cases is brought by plaintiff King Cobra Construction, P.C. (King Cobra) which alleges that it is a licensed general contractor, and in 2019 entered into a written agreement with defendants Corazon S. Nieves, Trustee of the Corazon Revocable Trust Dated October 10, 2013, to act as the general contractor for a project located on 3080 Hollywell Place in Glendale, which property was owned by defendant.  There were also approved change orders and extra work performed that improved the property.

Plaintiff alleges that it performed all of the conditions of the agreement, but defendant materially breached the terms of the agreement by failing to pay for the work performed and owes plaintiff no less than the sum of $288,000 plus interest and prompt payment penalties.  The complaint alleges causes of action for breach of written contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, indebitatus assumpsit, account stated, foreclosure of mechanic’s lien and declaratory relief. 

Defendant Nieves, individually, and as trustee, has filed a cross-complaint in the lead case against King Cobra and its principal, Haroutyan Keshishyan, as cross-defendants, alleging that the construction contract between the parties did not contain any provision for payment of construction supervision fees to cross-defendants, or payment of profits, and that there were no executed change orders. The cross-complaint alleges that to finance the construction, Corazon obtained a loan from U.S. Bank (US Bank) (the Construction Loan), disbursements from which were handled by Dixieline Builders Fund Control, Inc.  (Dixieline).  The cross-complaint alleges that as of the time of the filing of this action, US Bank and Dixieline have paid to King Cobra the total sum of $613,501.00 out of the original contact price, and there remains $39,499.00 in escrow on hold until the instant litigation is resolved. 

Cross-complainant alleges that during the construction, Keshishyan alerted Nieves that disbursements were being held up by Dixieline because Dixieline required additional or specific forms to be completed, which Keshishyan offered to complete to spare cross-complainant the trouble of reviewing and completing the forms.  Cross-complainant alleges that cross-complainant would sign the forms completed by cross-defendants, and was not permitted to review the documents, and was assured that cross-complainant would have the opportunity to review the documents and make adjustments or corrections later in the process.  The cross-complaint alleges that unbeknownst to cross-complainant, the Dixieline Standard Cost Breakdown included a provision for “supervision” fees of $35,000 and “profit” of $60,000, which were not discussed or agreed upon by the parties. 

The cross-complaint alleges that over the course of the construction, Keshishyan began requesting that Nieves pay King Cobra directly for monies to fund the construction, separate and apart from the Construction Loan, representing that such would expedite completion, and that defendants would agree to an accounting for cross-defendants to reimburse any overpayment from reimbursement received from Dixieline.  The cross-complaint alleges that cross-complainant ultimately paid cross-defendants direct payments of $332,579.22, and that cross-defendants have been paid a total sum of $946,080.22 from the Construction Loan and direct payments.  The cross-complaint alleges that in April of 2020, after cross-complainant refused an offer to sell the property as is to a buyer located by cross-defendants to resolve cross-complainant’s growing liquidity problems, cross-complainant refused to make any further direct payments and demanded that cross-defendants complete construction and perform an accounting.  The cross-complaint alleges that cross-defendants then abandoned construction and took with them personal property belonging to cross-complainant, including appliances and fixtures, with the approximate value of $25,000.  Cross-defendant King Cobra then recorded a mechanic’s lien and sued cross-complainant in this lawsuit.  The cross-complaint in the lead case alleges causes of action for breach of written contract, breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing, conversion, fraud, elder financial abuse, unfair competition, unjust enrichment, and declaratory relief.

The consolidated case, originally Case Number 22 GDCV00004, was consolidated with the lead case on July 29, 2022, with all further matters to be handled in Case Number 20 GDCV000728.  Because of the timing of the consolidation, some of the pertinent papers were filed in Case Number 22 GDCV00004, including the operative First Amended Complaint, and can be located there.   The First Amended Complaint in the case was brought by Nieves as plaintiff, on behalf of herself and as attorney in fact for her brother, who allegedly co-owns the subject property.  Plaintiff alleges that defendants U.S. Bank National Association (US Bank), and its servicers and agents defendant Dixieline Builders Fund Control (Dixieline) and defendant Pro Build Company, LLC (ProBuild) were negligent in issuing payments to the construction company King Cobra in connection with the construction project at 3600 Hollywell Place in Glendale because work was not performed in a workmanlike fashion, and defendants failed to properly inspect and verify that the construction work was properly done.      

On August 12, 2022, the court heard a demurrer and motion to strike in connection with the FAC brought by defendants US Bank and US Bancorp.   The demurrer was overruled as to the first cause of action for negligence, second cause of action for breach of contract, and fourth cause of action for breach of contract.  The demurrer was sustained with leave to amend to the sixth cause of action for unfair business practices.  The motion to strike allegations of pain and suffering and emotional distress and claims for attorney’s fees was granted without leave to amend at the concession of plaintiff in the opposition papers.  Plaintiff was permitted ten days leave to amend the sixth cause of action only.  No further amended complaint has been filed.  
 
On August 29, 2022, plaintiff filed a Request for Dismissal with prejudice of the “6th Cause of Action (Unfair Business Practices) only—as to all Defendants.”  The dismissal was entered as requested on August 29, 2022.  

On July 28, 2023, the court heard a motion for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication brought by defendant US Bank. 

The motion for summary judgment was denied.  The motion for summary adjudication was granted as to the fourth cause of action for breach of contract under the USB-DX Agreement.

On March 8, 2024, the court heard two motions for summary judgment or in the alternative summary adjudication on the consolidated complaint, one brought by defendant Dixieline, and one brought by Probuild.  The motions for summary judgment were denied. The motion for summary adjudication brought by Dixieline Builders was granted as to the fourth cause of action brought by plaintiff Nieves for breach of contract under the Master Services Agreement between U.S. Bank and Dixieline. 

The file shows that on June 13, 2024, the parties filed a Stipulation for an order granting Probuild and Dixieline leave to file a cross-complaint against Barry Simonetti and Res-Com Inspection Services, Inc.  Pursuant to the stipulation, the court signed and filed the order the same date. 

The cross-complaint was filed on July 9, 2024.   The cross-complaint alleges that cross-defendants Barry Simonetti and Res-Com Inspection Services, Inc. entered into a Real Estate Services Consulting Agreement with Dixieline in May of 2011 concerning inspections of the project wherein cross-defendants agreed to provide contractual indemnity to Dixieline.  The cross-complaint alleges causes of action for contractual indemnity, implied/equitable indemnity, contribution, and declaratory relief.  
 
ANALYSIS:
Defendants and cross-complainants Dixieline and Probuild seek to file a first amended cross-complaint to add allegations to the July 9, 2024, inspectors cross-complaint against Nieves and King Cobra, for contractual indemnity and breach of duty to defend, and to include these parties to the existing equitable indemnity, contribution and declaratory relief causes of action.

Cross-complainants argue that this is a compulsory cross-complaint, as it arises out of the same nexus of operative facts as the main consolidated action.   

Under CCP § 428.50:
“(a) A party shall file a cross-complaint against any of the parties who filed the complaint or cross-complaint against him or her before or at the same time as the answer to the complaint or cross-complaint. 
(b) Any other cross-complaint may be filed at any time before the court has set a date for trial.
(c)  A party shall obtain leave of court to file any cross-complaint except one filed within the time specified in subdivision (a) or (b).  Leave may be granted in the interest of justice at any time during the course of the action.” 

It is held that relief under the permissive provisions of the statute is appropriate when a defendant seeks to file a cross-complaint for indemnity.  Platt v. Coldwell Banker Real Estate Services (1990) 217 Cal.App.3d 1439, 1444.  This procedural posture is not a situation where the indemnity claims will be lost if not asserted in this matter.  Hence, although the parties here apply the bad faith standards generally applicable to compulsory cross-complaints, the amendments to the cross-complaint here are in fact not compulsory. 

In this case, the case has already been set for trial, so it is in the court’s discretion to grant leave “in the interest of justice.”  Cross-complainants argue that there has been no bad faith in seeking to file the cross-complaint against Nieves and King Cobra at this time, without directly arguing why it would be in the interests of justice to do so.   However, the moving papers argue that the circumstances giving rise to the need for amendment to include Nieves and King Cobra in the cross-complaint arose following a deposition of King Cobra’s principal on July 9, 2024, which was attended by a newly hired attorney for Dixieline and Probuild, John Goodman.  Goodman was prompted by the lack of documents produced to review the relevant documents.  Consequently, he discovered a strongly worded indemnity provision in the Fund Control Agreement signed by Nieves and King Cobra.  The indemnity clause was connected with a newly discovered side deal between the parties for additional funds to be paid during construction which had not been disclosed previously.  [Goodman Decl., paras. 5, 6].   Counsel determined that the indemnity clause was likely triggered by this failure to disclose, and promptly began meeting and conferring with counsel concerning stipulating to the amendment.  Nieves and King Cobra would not stipulate to the filing of an amended cross-complaint.  Mr. Goodman then prepared a motion to amend cross-complaint, which was filed and served on July 29, 2024.  [Goodman Decl., paras. 6-8, Ex. C].  

The court finds that, although the indemnity claims would not be lost if not asserted in this action and could be asserted in a separate action after liability is determined in this matter, it is far more convenient to have all these issues determined together in the same action.  

The motion also argues that there are no new parties, as Nieves and King Cobra are already parties to this action.  The motion also points out that there is no prejudice because no factual issues are added requiring additional discovery.  The only new issues are contractual and legal issues concerning contractual and equitable indemnity obligations.   The motion also points out that although the trial date was then set for October 21, 2024, that trial date would likely have to be pushed back in any event due to the recent addition of the cross-defendant inspectors.   In fact, the court, pursuant to plaintiff’s recent ex parte application, has continued the trial to April 7, 2025. 

Plaintiff Nieves has filed an opposition to the motion.  The opposition argues that Dixieline and Probuild have not acted with diligence in seeking leave to amend, and that the claims are not warranted in the cross-complaint and, to the extent they are viable, can be resolved by way of Nieves’ complaint. 

The showing discussed above demonstrates that sufficient diligence has been shown in pursuing the newly discovered claims.  Also, the arguments concerning the merits of the claims are not persuasive given the liberality of pleading claims for indemnity and contribution and the circumstance here that the newly added inspectors and Nieves and King Cobra may be jointly liable for the alleged inspection deficiencies adds to the reasons to allow for the amended cross-complaint.  It is not specified how such claims are already encompassed in the other pleadings, and if there is some overlap.  However, there is no harm in permitting the assertion of direct relief in case there is no comparable affirmative relief sought elsewhere in the various pleadings here in an answer or some form of a complaint or cross-complaint. 
  
Overall, the court has a strong interest in having all of these matters tried together and would generally disfavors having cross-complainant be forced to bring another civil action against a potentially responsible party on indemnity and contribution theories.  There is also the assertion of a duty to defend, which would likely be related to this action, or the issue could be addressed at the close of this action.  

Nieves argues that there will be prejudice if this motion is granted, as the amendment will result in a delay of the trial in this matter, and undoubtedly increase the burden of discovery.  As noted above, the current trial date has been continued pursuant to plaintiff’s recent ex parte application.  Such a continuance has been made with a continuance of the discovery cut off dates to correspond to the new trial date, so that any necessary discovery can be obtained.  It is not specified what new discovery would be required.  Also, there is no argument that due to a delay in asserting the new claims there has been deterioration or disappearance of evidence, which is ordinarily the type of prejudice which would justify denying a motion such as this one on the ground of prejudice.   

The interests of justice here favor the judicial efficiency of the alleged liability and indemnity and contribution issues between the parties being determined and apportioned in a single proceeding.  The motion accordingly is granted.   

RULING:
Motion of Dixieline Builders Fund Control, Inc. and Probuild Company, LLC for Leave to File Compulsory Cross-Complaint is GRANTED in the interest of justice.  
Moving parties are ordered to e-file their signed First Amended Cross-Complaint by the close of business this date.

TENTATIVE RULING

Calendar: 10
Date: 9/20/2024
Case No: 23GDCV02249 Trial Date:  Not Set
Case Name: Samvel Khachatryan v. Dory Cabinta Macatlang

MOTION FOR RECLASSIFICATION OF MATTER TO LIMITED JURISDICTION

Moving Party: Defendant Dory Cabinta Macatlang
Responding Party: Plaintiff Samvel Khachatryan

RELIEF REQUESTED: 
Order reclassifying the subject matter to a limited jurisdiction. 

CAUSES OF ACTION: from Complaint   
1) Motor vehicle negligence
2) Negligence
SUMMARY OF FACTS: 
Plaintiff Samvel Khachatryan alleges that on November 15, 2021 on the 5 freeway northbound near the Western Avenue exit in Glendale, defendant Dory Cabinta Macatlang negligently operated and owned a motor vehicle. Plaintiff alleges that traffic suddenly came to a stop and plaintiff’s vehicle was rear-ended by defendant, and that as a result of the impact plaintiff sustained bodily injuries and property damage. 
Plaintiff alleges that plaintiff has suffered wage loss, loss of use of property, hospital and medical expenses, general damage, property damage and loss of earnings capacity. No damages sum is alleged in the complaint. There is no statement of damages in the file. 
On July 19, 2024, this matter came on for hearing, but no party appeared. The court’s tentative indicated the motion would be granted and noted that Plaintiff had not filed an opposition. 
On July 26, 2024, the matter was continued to September 20, 2024 by nunc pro tunc order. The court indicated that pleadings are to be filed pursuant to statute. 
Plaintiff filed an opposition to this motion to reclassify on August 16, 2024.

ANALYSIS:
Defendant seeks relief under CCP § 403.040(b), which provides, in pertinent part:
“If a party files a motion for reclassification after the time for that party to ...respond to a complaint, ... the court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification only if both of the following conditions are satisfied:
(1) The case is incorrectly classified.
(2) The moving party shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.” 
The California Supreme Court in construing when a case could be transferred to a municipal court under previous CCP § 396 held that a matter could be transferred when it would “necessarily” result in a verdict below the jurisdictional amount. (Walker v. Superior Court (1991) 53 Cal.3d 257, 262.) The Court stated that this standard “requires a high level of certainty that a damage award will not exceed $25,000 and is not satisfied by a finding that such an award is merely ‘unlikely’ or ‘not reasonably probable’.” (Walker, at 269.)
Walker authorizes the trial court to conduct a pretrial hearing to obtain information about whether the amount of a judgment to be rendered will justify reclassification. (See Walker, at 268; Stern v. Superior Court (2003 2nd Dist.) 105 Cal.App.4th 223, 231.)
The appellate court reviews a reclassification order “according to an abuse of discretion standard, i.e., whether the court made an informed decision within its discretion or whether the order exceeded the bounds of reason.” (Stern at 231; citing Walker at 272.)
As an initial matter, Defendant here argues that this matter should be reclassified to a limited jurisdiction matter, as discovery has shown that the amount in controversy does not exceed $35,000. 
This argument is based on the current version of CCP section 86(a)(1), which provides:
“(a) The following civil cases and proceedings are limited civil cases:
(1) A case at law if the demand, exclusive of interest, or the value of the property in controversy amounts to thirty-five thousand dollars ($35,000) or less.” 
The statute was revised effective January 1, 2024 to increase the jurisdictional minimum from $25,000 to $35,000. This case was filed on October 24, 2023. Case law interpreting former versions of jurisdictional statutes when the limits were adjusted has held that where there is a statutory change of jurisdiction of a court and no clearly expressed intention to the contrary, the amended statute will be held to operate prospectively rather than retrospectively. (Dillon v. Superior Court in and for Los Angeles County (1950, 2nd Dist.) 98 Cal.App.2d 437, 439.) The statute here does not include any express language indicating that the new jurisdictional minimum was intended to apply retroactively, as opposed to prospectively, that is, for cases filed on or after January 1, 2024. Thus, the court applies the $25,000 minimum, as opposed to the $35,000 as urged in the moving papers.
Defendant here argues that according to plaintiff’s own representations the total amount of plaintiff’s special damages for the medical treatment he received is $5,190, and the estimate for the property damage to his vehicle is $6,817.36. 
Defendant relies on plaintiff’s responses to Form Interrogatories, in which plaintiff indicates that the medical treatment received consisted of consultation and treatment with a medical doctor, costing $4,790, and x-rays costing $400, with no medication prescribed. (Ex. A, Responses to Form Interrogatories Nos. 6.4, 6.5.) When asked if there are any other medical services necessitated by the injuries, plaintiff responded, “No.” (Ex. A, Response to Form Interrogatory No. 6.6.)
Plaintiff’s responses also indicate that a written estimate for property loss is $6,817.36 (Repair Estimate, Exh. B), and that plaintiff does not attribute any loss of income or earning capacity to the incident, or other damages. (Ex. A, Responses to Form Interrogatories Nos. 7.2, 8.1, 9.1.)
Plaintiff filed an opposition, however, Plaintiff failed to file proof of service indicating he served Defendant with the opposition. Defendant has not filed a reply to indicate she has received notice of the opposition, but the court assumes defendant’s attorney received the opposition because no statement of non-opposition is filed.
In opposition, Plaintiff argues that he may require additional treatment (Zakaryan Decl., Exh. A), and he claims pain and suffering. (Id., ¶ 8.) Plaintiff also argues that his vehicle was a total loss and was valued at $11,270. Further, Plaintiff argues that his economic damages are not known yet because Medicare has not given any details as to the final lien. (Id., ¶ 7.)
Indeed, Plaintiff’s discovery responses that were submitted by Defendant in connection with this motion, provide support for most of Plaintiff’s arguments in opposition. Plaintiff’s response to Form Interrogatory 6.3 indicates that Plaintiff suffers from constant pain, which indicates he is making a claim for general damages. (Defendant’s Ex. A, Response to Form Interrogatories 6.3.) Further, Response to Form Interrogatory 6.7 indicates that Plaintiff’s health care provider advised that he may require future or additional treatment. (Defendant’s Ex. A, Response to Form Interrogatories 6.7.) 

But, the court notes that Plaintiff offers no evidence whatsoever for his argument that the vehicle was valued at $11,270.

In any event, the court can transfer an action to limited jurisdiction if “it becomes clear that the matter will ‘necessarily’ result in a verdict below the superior court jurisdictional amount.” (Walker at 262.)“‘This standard requiresa high level of certaintythat a damage award will not exceed $25,000 and is not satisfied by a finding that such an award is merely 'unlikely' or 'not reasonably probable.' " (Id. at 269, italics added.) 
As Plaintiff may require future treatment, and has a claim for pain and suffering, it does not appear to a high level of certainty that Plaintiff cannot recover more than the jurisdictional amount, especially considering that Plaintiff has a claim for general damages, which are not subject to precise measurement, and thus, the possibility exists that Plaintiff’s total damages could exceed the jurisdictional limit. (Singer v. Superior Court (1999) 70 Cal. App. 4th 1315, 1320.)

RULING: 
Based on the foregoing, the motion to reclassify is DENIED.
