DEPARTMENT 58 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV01605    Hearing Date: September 27, 2024    Dept: 58

Judge Bruce G. Iwasaki

Department 58

Hearing Date:              September 17, 2024

Case Name:                 Elisa Ortego v. Magic Laundry Services, Inc.  

Case No.:                    22STCV01605

Motion:                       Motion to Vacate Order Compelling Arbitration Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 1281.98 and Request for Monetary Sanctions  

Moving Party:             Plaintiff Elisa Ortego

Responding Party:      Defendant Magic Laundry Services, Inc.  

 

Tentative Ruling:      The Motion to Vacate Order Compelling Arbitration is granted.  The Request for Monetary Sanctions is taken under submission.

                                     

 

Background

 

In this employment action, Plaintiff Elisa Ortego (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint on January 13, 2022, alleging a single PAGA cause of action complaint against her former employer, Defendant Magic Laundry Services, Inc. (“Defendant”). The PAGA claim incorporated numerous Labor Code violations.

 

On September 28, 2023, Defendant filed a motion to compel arbitration pursuant to the parties’ arbitration agreement. Plaintiff opposed the motion to compel arbitration.

 

On October 23, 2023, after hearing oral argument, the Court granted the motion to compel arbitration and stayed this action pending the outcome of arbitration. (10/23/23 Minute Order.)  

 

On July 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed a motion to vacate the order compelling arbitration pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, on the grounds that Defendant failed to timely pay outstanding arbitration fees. Plaintiff also seeks monetary sanctions against Defendant in the amount of $30,560.50.

 

On September 4, 2024, Defendant filed an opposition to the motion, to which Plaintiff replied on September 10, 2024.

 

Judicial Notice

 

            The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request for judicial notice. (Evid. Code, §§ 452, 453.)

 

 

Legal Standard

 

“Sections 1281.97 and 1281.98 each prescribe procedures for payment and remedies for nonpayment of arbitration fees and costs by the drafting party, i.e., the company or business that included a predispute arbitration provision in a contract with a consumer or employee.” (Williams v. West Coast Hospitals, Inc. (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 1054, 1065.) “Both sections provide that a drafting party who fails in its obligation to pay fees and costs required to initiate or continue the arbitration within 30 days after the due date is in material breach of the arbitration agreement, is in default of the arbitration, and waives its right to compel the employee or consumer to proceed with that arbitration as a result of the material breach.” (Id. at p. 1066.) “Consequently, even where an arbitration has commenced, the employee or consumer may unilaterally elect[,] among other alternatives, to [w]ithdraw the claim from arbitration and proceed in a court of appropriate jurisdiction.” (Ibid., emphasis in original.) “If the consumer elects to proceed in court to commence or resume litigation, the consumer must . . . seek vacatur of a prior order compelling arbitration and staying the litigation.” (Ibid.)  

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98 is concerned with the payment of fees and costs during the pendency of arbitration. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(a)(1).) “The arbitration provider shall provide an invoice for any fees and costs required for the arbitration proceeding to continue to all of the parties to the arbitration. The invoice shall be provided in its entirety, shall state the full amount owed and the date that payment is due, and shall be sent to all parties by the same means on the same day.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(a)(2).) “To avoid delay, absent an express provision in the arbitration agreement stating the number of days in which the parties to the arbitration must pay any required fees or costs, the arbitration provider shall issue all invoices to the parties as due upon receipt. Any extension of time for the due date shall be agreed upon by all parties.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 1281.98(a)(2).) “[L]ate payment as provided in section 1281.98 constitutes a ‘material breach’ without regard to any additional considerations.” (De Leon v. Juanita’s Foods (2022) 85 Cal.App.5th 740, 749.)  

 

Should an employee move to vacate an order compelling arbitration and succeeds on such motion, “the court shall impose sanctions on the drafting party in accordance with Section 1281.99.” (Espinoza v. Superior Court (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 761, 774-75.) “Section 1281.99, in turn, states that the court shall impose a monetary sanction against a drafting party in the form of the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees and costs incurred by the employee or consumer as a result of the material breach.” (Ibid.)  

 

“The FAA [Federal Arbitration Act] was enacted to override judicial hostility to enforcing arbitration agreements.” (Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc. (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 222, 239.) “The FAA embodies a strong federal policy favoring arbitration.” (Ibid.) “When the FAA applies to an agreement . . . the substantive provisions of the FAA preempt state law to the extent that state law actually conflicts with federal law or operates as an obstacle to accomplishing the purpose of the FAA.” (Id. at p. 240.) “The FAA also contains procedural provisions” which “do not apply in state court.” (Ibid.) “The procedural provisions of California arbitration laws apply in California courts by default.” (Ibid.) “Parties can avoid preemption by expressly agreeing to apply state law to their agreements, whether state substantive law, state procedural law, or both.” (Ibid.) “If parties expressly agree to apply the CAA, or agree to apply California law, including California’s arbitration rules, then the state arbitration laws will not be preempted by the FAA.” (Ibid.) “When the FAA applies to an arbitration agreement, it will, however, preempt state substantive law that conflicts with the policies of the FAA.” (Ibid.) “When an agreement falls within the scope of the FAA and the parties have not expressly elected California law . . . the FAA preempts the portion of section [1281.98] that requires findings of material breach and a waiver of the right to arbitrate as a matter of California law.” (Id. at p. 242.)

 

“The Legislature enacted section 1281.97 along with Section 1281.98. The former provision concerns fees due at the initiation of arbitration. Otherwise, the provisions are analogous, and courts analyze them similarly.” (Cvejic v. Skyview Capital, LLC (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 1073, 1077.)

 

Discussion

 

Plaintiff contends that Defendant’s failure to pay arbitration fees is a material breach of the arbitration agreement. Plaintiff further argues that Keeton v. Tesla, Inc. (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 26 confirms that CCP § 1281.98 is not preempted by the Federal Arbitration Act (“FAA”).

 

In opposition to the motion, Defendant neither disputes that it materially breached the arbitration agreement by timely failing to pay arbitration fees nor does it dispute that Plaintiff is entitled to sanctions. The crux of Defendant’s opposition is that the FAA and not the California Arbitration Act (“CAA”) governs the outcome of the motion. Defendant contends that Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc., supra, 102 Cal.App.5th 222 requires denial of the motion. Alternatively, Defendant argues that the case should be stayed pending the California Supreme Court’s resolution of the issue of the FAA preempting CCP § 1281.98.

 

Pertinent Language of the Arbitration Agreement

 

Of significance, the Arbitration Agreement states at the outset that it “is governed by the Federal Arbitration Act (9 U.S.C. §§ 1, et seq.).”  The Agreement provides that Plaintiff and Defendant mutually “agree that any legal dispute or controversy covered by this Agreement, or arising out of, relating to or concerning the scope, formation, validity, enforceability or breach of this Agreement, shall be resolved by final and binding arbitration in accordance with the Employment Arbitration Rules of the American Arbitration Association (“AAA Rules”) then in effect, and not by court or jury trial . . . .” (Portillo Decl., Ex. B, p. 1, ¶ 1.) The Agreement states that the parties “shall follow the AAA Rules applicable to initial filing fees . . . .” (Portillo Decl., Ex. B., p. 1, ¶ 1.) Discovery concerning the arbitration will be conducted in accordance with AAA Rules. (Portillo Decl., Ex. B., p. 1, ¶ 1.)

 

Defendant’s payment was late; its excuses for lateness are inadmissible.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel, Erin R. Hutchins (“Hutchins”) provides a declaration in support of the motion. Ms. Hutchins relevantly states the following: On February 14, 2024, AAA confirmed receipt of Plaintiff’s filing fees and that Plaintiff’s filing requirements had been met, and soon thereafter the parties mutually selected Sonya Goodwin, Esq. as the arbitrator. (Hutchins Decl., ¶ 6.) An Arbitration Management Conference occurred on May 23, 2024 with the Arbitrator. (Hutchins Decl., ¶ 9.) On May 31, 2024, the AAA emailed to Defendant’s counsel an invoice in the amount of $22,500.00 for the “Arbitrator’s Compensation Deposit #1” and the invoice stated that the balance was “due upon receipt.” (Hutchins Decl., ¶ 10, Ex. A.)  Accordingly, payment was due June 30, 2024. Defendant did not pay the invoice until July 11, 2024. (Hutchins Decl., ¶11.)

 

Defendant’s counsel, Richard B. Mojica (“Mojica”), states that Defendant mailed a check to the AAA for the amount of the May 31, 2024 invoice on or about June 20, 2024. (Mojica Decl., ¶ 4.) The check was not received due to mail theft that occurred at the mailbox where the check was deposited for mailing. (Mojica Decl., ¶ 5.) On July 11, 2024, the AAA informed defense counsel that payment had not been received and, that day, Defendant made an electronic payment. (Mojica Decl., ¶ 7.) Defendant was current on all fees owing to the AAA prior to Plaintiff filing the instant motion. (Mojica Decl., ¶ 8.)

 

Plaintiff neither objects to nor disputes Mojica’s declaration. If the Court accepted the declaration, the Court would be tempted to conclude that Defendant’s lateness – due to alleged criminal conduct beyond its control – was not a material breach. The cases suggest, however, the contrary.  Several recent decisions have held that the statutory language and history set forth a bright line rule, and no considerations of whether Plaintiff was prejudiced or whether the arbitration was delayed should be considered.  (De Leon v. Juanita’s Foods, supra, 85 Cal.App.5th 740, 753 [“statute’s language establishes a simple bright-line rule that a drafting party’s failure to pay outstanding arbitration fees within 30 days after the due date results in its material breach of the arbitration agreement”]; Doe v. Superior Court (2023) 95 Cal.App.5th 346 [writ issued against trial court’s denial of employee’s motion to vacate arbitration when employer was required to effect payment on October 3, but mailed on Friday, September 30, such that arbitrator received it on October 5]; Espinoza v. Superior Court (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 761, 771 [Legislature intended courts to apply payment deadlines strictly even if the delay is “inadvertent, brief, and did not prejudice plaintiff”].) It can be argued, however, that this case is not like the situation of a party sending payment too close to the deadline as in Doe.

 

            That said, the Court declines to accept the Mojica declaration because it is riddled with inadmissibility and to receive it would permit a miscarriage of justice. (Evid. Code, § 353.) Ordinarily, of course, the failure to object waives the objection. (Leonardini v. Shell Oil Co. (1989) 216 Cal.App.3d 547, 584.)  But this is an attorney declaration with no foundation regarding who at Defendant’s office mailed the payment to the arbitrator, when, and how.  It provides no evidence of personal knowledge regarding the theft of the mailbox.  The key parts of the declaration are based on information and belief, which is inadmissible. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 5.111(b)(2); Kellett v. Kellett (1934) 2 Cal.2d 45, 48.)

 

            Even if the Court accepted the evidence regarding Defendant’s late payment, the appellate authorities interpret this statute strictly.  The Court concludes that Defendant materially breached the Arbitration Agreement.

 

The FAA does not preempt section 1281.98.

 

There is a split of authority in the Court of Appeal whether Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98 is preempted by the Federal Arbitration Act.  Recognizing this issue will be resolved by higher authorities, the Court must choose among closely analyzed, if divergent, decisions. 

 

In Gallo v. Wood Ranch USA, Inc. (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 621, plaintiff Sunny Gallo signed an arbitration agreement as a condition of employment with defendant Wood Ranch. (Id. at p. 630.) The arbitration agreement specified “that the arbitrator is to apply California and/or federal substantive law to determine issues of liability and damages regarding all claims, but is to look to the California Arbitration Act . . . to conduct the arbitration and any pre-arbitration activities.” (Ibid.) In Gallo, “[t]he employee handbook reinforce[d] the parties’ agreement that they [would] look to the California Arbitration Act, including its procedural provisions, to conduct the arbitration and any pre-arbitration activities.” (Ibid.) Wood Ranch moved to compel arbitration and the trial court granted the motion. (Id. at p. 631.) Wood Ranch was not timely in paying initial arbitration fees and Gallo moved to vacate the trial court’s order compelling arbitration. (Id. at p. 632.) The trial court granted the motion and rejected Wood Ranch’s argument that section1281.97 was preempted by the FAA. (Ibid.)

 

As a matter of first impression our Second District, Division Two, affirmed the trial court’s order vacating the order compelling arbitration. (Gallo v. Wood Ranch, Inc., supra, 81 Cal.App.5th 621, 629.) The Gallo court held that sections 1281.97, 1281.98, and 1281.99 were not preempted by the FAA “because the procedures they prescribe further—rather than frustrate—the objectives of the FAA to honor the parties’ intent to arbitrate and preserve arbitration as a speedy and effective alternative forum for resolving disputes.” (Ibid.) The Gallo court noted that there was no federal policy favoring arbitration under a certain set of procedural rules and that states had liberty to enact some rules affecting arbitration even though such rules will be arbitration-specific. (Id. at p. 639.) Sections 1281.97 and 1281.99 were deemed to not be preempted by the FAA because they neither prohibited nor discouraged arbitration but merely “define[d] the procedures governing the date by which the party who drafted an agreement to arbitrate against an employee or consumer must pay the initial fees and costs to arbitration, and [specified] the consequences of untimely payment.” (Id. at p. 641.) Thus, Code of Civil Procedure sections 1281.97 and 1281.98 were deemed “akin to a statute of limitation.” (Ibid.)  

 

Two months later in Espinoza v. Superior Court (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 761, Division One of the Second District relied on Gallo to conclude that section 1281.97 is not analogous to state claw preempted by the FAA in other cases.  (Id. at p. 783.) Unlike Gallo, the arbitration agreement in Espinoza did not broadly incorporate the Californica Arbitration Act, but the court deemed the CAA applicable by default.  (Id. at p. 786.) Accordingly, the court rejected the argument that the FAA preempted section 1281.97.

 

In Hohenshelt v. Superior Court (2024) 99 Cal.App.5th 1319, Division 8 of the Second District also relied on Gallo to hold that Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98 is not preempted by the FAA. Justice Wiley dissented.  He pointed out that the delay in contract performance might merit sanctions but questioned why arbitration itself must be “abolished.”  Pointing out that the U.S. Supreme Court has repeatedly “rebuked California state law that continues to find new ways to disfavor arbitration,” (Id. at p. 1327.) Justice Wiley stated that the California statute singles out arbitration agreements for disfavored treatment:  “No other contracts are voided on a hair-trigger basis due to tardy performance. Only arbitration contracts face this firing squad.” (Id. at p. 1328.)

 

In neither Gallo, Espinoza, nor Hohenshelt, does it appear that the parties expressly incorporated FAA either substantively or procedurally in the arbitration agreement. But in

Hernandez v. Sohnen Enterprises, Inc. (2024) 102 Cal.App.5th 222, the arbitration agreement incorporated the FAA and that “[d]uring arbitration, the parties could conduct discovery and bring motions under the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure.” (Id. at p. 231.)  In Hernandez, Division Eight parted company with other panels in the Second District to find that section 1281.97 of the CAA did not apply, and if it did, the FAA preempted it. (Id. at p. 238.) The employer in Hernandez failed to pay arbitration costs within 30 days of the due date and the employee filed a motion to vacate arbitration under section 1281.97. (Id. at p. 230.) The trial court granted the employee’s motion to vacate arbitration and the employer appealed. (Ibid.) On appeal, the employer argued that the FAA governed the parties’ arbitration agreement and that the FAA preempted CCP § 1281.97. (Ibid.)

 

The Hernandez majority agreed with the employer and held that “the arbitration agreement . . . [was] governed by the FAA, including both the substantive and procedural provisions of the FAA, rather than California’s arbitration laws.” (Hernandez, supra, 102 Cal.App.5th 222, 230.) The Hernandez court found that the substantive and procedural provisions of the FAA applied to the arbitration agreement at issue because the agreement stated that it was “governed by the FAA” and that such statement was “broad, encompassing both the procedural and substantive provisions of the FAA.” (Id at p. 241.) The court reasoned that the arbitration agreement consistently referred to provisions in the FAA and the parties expressly agreed in the agreement and via stipulation that the Federal Rules of Civil Procedure would apply to the arbitration. (Ibid.) And importantly, there was “no provision explicitly referring to California law in the agreement.” (Ibid.)

 

In its preemption analysis, Hernandez court also found that section 1281.97 does not further the goals of the FAA by encouraging or facilitating arbitration because it imposes “a higher standard for enforcement of arbitration agreements in consumer and employee disputes” by allowing an employee to avoid arbitration where there is a minor, inadvertent, or consequential delay. (Id. at p. 243-44.) The Hernandez court stated a rule of law that “unless the parties have expressly selected California’s arbitration provisions to apply to their agreement,” the FAA preempts the portion of section 1281.97 that dictates findings of material breach and waiver. (Id. at p. 244.) The Hernandez court reversed the order granting the motion to vacate. (Id. at p. 230.)

 

Justice Baker dissented. He noted that the opinion created a conflict with “many other Court of Appeal decisions,” and opined that the arbitration agreement was “too ambiguous” to conclude that the FAA applies. He questioned whether the majority’s application of an “equal-treatment principle” in which arbitration agreements are not treated differently from other contracts, uses meaningful comparisons. He concluded that it was a “stretch too far” to say that the FAA is “offended by a state law that requires prompt payment of arbitration fees.”  (Hernandez, supra,102 Cal.App.5th at p. 248.)

 

In Keeton v. Telsa, Inc. (2024) 103 Cal.App.5th 26, appellant Tesla appealed a trial court order finding that Tesla materially breached the parties’ arbitration agreement under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.98, and granting appellee Keeton’s motion to vacate arbitration. The parties’ arbitration agreement stated “that all disputes regarding . . . employment [would] be resolved by binding arbitration conducted by the Judicial Arbitration and Mediation Services, Inc. (JAMS) under the current rules of JAMS for employment disputes.” (Id. at p. 30-31.) Nothing in the arbitration agreement incorporated the FAA.

 

On appeal, Tesla argued that the trial court erred in finding that the FAA did not preempt section 1281.98. (Keeton, supra, 103 Cal.App.5th at p. 30.)  In Keeton, the First District noted that “our high court has yet to decide whether the FAA preempts section 1281.98, [but] the Second District concluded in Gallo that an almost identical statute—section 1281.97—was not preempted by the FAA.” (Id. at p. 35-36.) The Keeton court held that the FAA does not preempt section 1281.98 and that:  “arbitration-specific state procedural rules are not preempted where the parties agreed to arbitrate by those rules and the rules are consistent” with the objective of the FAA. The Keeton court indicated that even where parties do not explicitly incorporate the CAA therein, “the procedural provisions of the CAA apply in California courts by default.” (Id. at p. 38.) The Keeton court held that “application of section 1281.98 . . . would not undermine the federal policy to ensure the enforceability, according to their terms, of private agreements to arbitrate.” (Id. at p. 38.) The Keeton court affirmed the trial court order granting the motion to vacate arbitration. (Id. at p. 42.)

.   .   .

 

The division among the appellate decisions reflects a difference not about the benefits of arbitration, but on whether legislative efforts to curb abuses of arbitration promote or impede arbitration as an efficient method of dispute resolution.  For some, requiring the party that pays its fees late to forfeit the arbitral forum is extreme, and can be addressed with other sanctions.  On the other hand, the party that pays its fees late ordinarily has no excuse for being late.  It drafted the arbitration agreement, has litigated whether arbitration will be compelled, and receives ample time to get the ball rolling.  One can imagine other time limits that courts would deem too short. One can imagine a different regime without a bright line, but a balancing test of whether a delay is unreasonable, or whether prejudice from delay resulted.  There will be cases, like this one, in which the asserted reason for the delay is beyond the paying party’s control, and, if the evidence were sufficient, might justify extraordinary relief. 

 

The Court finds that arguments on the two sides of the preemption issue, which will in due course be resolved by our Supreme Court, well stated. On this issue, judicial modesty is in order. The Court defers to the Legislature on the drawing of lines.  The Court defers to the majority of Second District opinions finding no FAA preemption.  The Court concludes that section 1281.98 does not burden arbitration in general, because clear rules for facilitating prompt arbitral resolution will, in the main, make that method fairer and more favored, even if, in individual cases like this one, arbitration is forfeited.

 

Conclusion

 

The Court vacates its order compelling arbitration in this matter.  The Court takes under submission Plaintiff’s motion for sanctions.
