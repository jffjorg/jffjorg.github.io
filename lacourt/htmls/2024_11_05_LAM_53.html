Tentative rulings are sometimes, but not always, posted. The purpose of posting a tentative ruling is to to help focus the argument. The posting of a tentative ruling is not an invitation for the filing of additional papers shortly before the hearing.


Case Number: 20STCV46671    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 53

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles – Central District

Department 53

 

 

annette turner , et al.;

 

Plaintiffs,

 

 

vs.

 

 

aids healthcare foundation , et al.;

 

Defendants.

Case No.:

20STCV46671

 

 

Hearing Date:

November 5, 2024

 

 

Time:

10:00 a.m.

 

 

 

[tentative] Order RE:

 

defendant’s motion for order upholding “confidential” designation of documents produced in discovery

 

 

MOVING PARTY:                 Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation      

 

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Plaintiffs Annette Turner and Tammy Davis

Motion for Order Upholding “Confidential” Designation of Documents Produced in Discovery

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation’s evidentiary objections, filed on October 30, 2024, as follows:

Objections Nos. 1-12 are overruled.

DISCUSSION

Defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation (“Defendant”) moves the court for an order upholding the “confidential” designation of the following videos and documents produced in discovery by Defendant, to which plaintiffs Annette Turner and Tammy Davis (“Plaintiffs”) have objected: (1) two videos depicting a female tenant creating a disturbance and possibly experiencing mental health issues, one of which was taken while she was at the door to her unit in the hallway and the other of which was taken in the lobby at the Madison Hotel, (2) one video depicting a physical altercation between a tenant and a possible trespasser who may have committed theft, which was taken in a residential hallway at the Madison Hotel, and (3) documents that consist of complaints and reports made by tenants concerning alleged sexual harassment, sexual assault, gender discrimination, or violent criminal conduct at the Madison Hotel.  (Eastman Decl., ¶¶ 7-8, 10.)

On March 18, 2021, the parties lodged with the court a Stipulated Protective Order.  On March 22, 2021, the court entered an order approving the Stipulated Protective Order.  (Mar. 22, 2021 Protective Order, p. 11:1-9.)  Pursuant thereto, in the event that a party objects to a confidential designation, counsel for the designating party shall have 30 days to either (a) agree in writing to de-designate the documents, or (b) file a motion with the court seeking to uphold any or all of the designations.  (Mar. 22, 2021 Protective Order, ¶ 6.)

The court finds that Defendant has met its burden to establish the applicability of the confidential designation of the subject videos and documents because Defendant has shown that they consist of videos and information that concern the privacy rights of the non-parties.  (Mar. 22, 2021 Protective Order, ¶ 6 [“The Designating Party shall have the burden on any Designation Motion of establishing the applicability of its ‘Confidential’ designation”].)

“The party asserting a privacy right must establish a legally protected privacy interest, an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy in the given circumstances, and a threatened intrusion that is serious.  [Citation.]  The party seeking information may raise in response whatever legitimate and countervailing interests disclosure serves, while the party seeking protection may identify feasible alternatives that serve the same interests or protective measures that would diminish the loss of privacy.  A court must then balance these competing considerations.  [Citations.]”  (Williams v. Superior Court (2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 552.)

Defendant has presented evidence establishing that (1) there is a legally protected privacy interest belonging to Defendant’s residents in (i) the acts taking place in the building in which they live, and (ii) the reports made to Defendant regarding acts of violence committed at the Madison Hotel, and (2) the tenants had an objectively reasonable expectation of privacy under the circumstances.  (Williams, supra, 3 Cal.5th at p. 552; Eastman Decl., ¶¶ 7-8, 10 [describing videos and documents].)  The court disagrees with Plaintiffs’ contention that the tenants did not have a reasonable expectation of privacy because the subject conduct, even if occurring in hallways or lobbies at the Madison Hotel, took place in the building in which the tenants lived.  The court further finds that Plaintiffs have not shown that all of the subject documents are in the public record or are publicly available.

Plaintiffs have, in opposition, asserted that, if they use the videos of the female tenant, they will blur out her face, and will further redact the names of the crime victims in the incident reports.  (Harings Decl., ¶¶ 7 [“Plaintiffs are willing to blur out the subject woman’s face”], 9.)  The court also notes that Plaintiffs’ counsel states, in her declaration, that (1) some of the documents were filed in another action, and (2) some of the documents were created by Plaintiffs.  (Harings Decl., ¶ 9.)  However, Plaintiffs did not specifically state which documents that are the subject of this motion were previously filed in another action.  Moreover, although Plaintiffs’ counsel states that certain of the documents were created by Plaintiffs and objects to those being designated as confidential, counsel did not specifically identify which pages were created by Plaintiffs and instead has cited to, generally, the documents that are the subject of this motion.  (Ibid. [as to pages AHF-Turner 1135-1396, stating that those include “documents created by my own clients—I recognize my client’s handwriting and her report (AHF-Turner 1135-1396)”].)

The court finds, after weighing the privacy rights of the non-parties to this action and the parties’ suggestions regarding the treatment of the discovery (i.e., Plaintiffs’ suggestions to redact identifying information and Defendant’s request that the confidential designation be upheld), that, in order to protect the privacy interests identified by Defendant, it is appropriate to uphold the confidential designation of the protective order.  The court therefore grants Defendant’s motion.

            The court denies Plaintiffs’ request for monetary sanctions against Defendant.

ORDER

            The court grants defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation’s motion for order upholding “confidential” designation of documents produced in discovery.           

            The court orders that the following videos and documents produced by defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation in this action shall be designated as “Confidential” pursuant to the protective order entered by the court in this action on March 22, 2021: (1) the video with the bates number “AHF-TURNER 01367,” (2) the video with the bates number “AHF-TURNER 01368,” (3) the video with the bates number “AHF-TURNER 01394,” and (4) the documents with the bates numbers “AHF-TURNER 1135-1396” and “AHF-TURNER 1567-1717.”

The court orders counsel for defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation and plaintiffs Annette Turner and Tammy Davis to meet and confer, no later than November 15, 2024, to discuss and determine whether any of the documents within bates numbers “AHF-TURNER 1135-1396” and “AHF-TURNER 1567-1717” that were created by plaintiffs Annette Turner and Tammy Davis should not be designated as confidential pursuant to the protective order entered by the court on March 22, 2021, based on plaintiffs Annette Turner and Tammy Davis’s objections to such documents being designated as confidential.

            The court orders defendant AIDS Healthcare Foundation to give notice of this ruling.

RESPONDING PARTIES:    Defendants and cross-complainants Nicholas Fuentes and Old Earl Scheib Paint and Body

Special Motion to Strike Portions of Cross-Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with this motion.

EVIDENTIARY OBJECTIONS

The court rules on plaintiffs and cross-defendants Reza Safaie and Aaron Trucking, Inc.’s evidentiary objections, filed on October 28, 2024, as follows:

Objections Nos. 1-3 are sustained.

DISCUSSION

Plaintiffs and cross-defendants Reza Safaie and Aaron Trucking, Inc. (“Cross-Defendants”) have filed a special motion to strike the fourth cause of action for defamation per se alleged by defendants and cross-complainants Nicholas Fuentes and Old Earl Scheib Paint and Body (“Cross-Complainants”) in their Cross-Complaint pursuant to Code Civil Procedure section 425.16, also known as the anti-SLAPP (“strategic lawsuit against public participation”) statute. 

¿“The anti-SLAPP procedures are designed to shield a defendant’s constitutionally protected conduct from the undue burden of frivolous litigation.”¿ (Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal.5th 376, 393.)¿ “The anti-SLAPP statute does not insulate defendants from any liability for claims arising from the protected rights of petition or speech.¿ It only provides a procedure for weeding out, at an early stage, meritless claims arising from protected activity.”¿ (Id. at p. 384.)¿¿“Resolution of an anti-SLAPP motion involves two steps.¿ First, the defendant must establish that the challenged claim arises from activity protected by section 425.16.¿ If the defendant makes the required showing, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to demonstrate the merit of the claim by establishing a probability of success.”¿ (Ibid. [citation omitted].)¿ The California Supreme Court has “described this second step as a ‘summary-judgment-like procedure.’¿ The court does not weigh evidence or resolve conflicting factual claims.¿ Its inquiry is limited to whether the plaintiff has stated a legally sufficient claim and made a prima facie factual showing sufficient to sustain a favorable judgment.¿ It accepts the plaintiff’s evidence as true, and evaluates the defendant’s showing only to determine if it defeats the plaintiff’s claim as a matter of law.¿ ‘[C]laims with the requisite minimal merit may proceed.’”¿ (Id. at pp. 384-385 [citations omitted].)¿ 

1.     Public Interest Exemption (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.17)

As a threshold matter, the court notes that, in their opposition papers, Cross-Complainants assert that the public interest exemption applies, which the court must first consider before proceeding to the two-step analysis under section 425.16.  (Opp., pp. 5:1-6:4; Takhar v. People ex rel. Feather River Air Quality Management Dist. (2018) 27 Cal.App.5th 15, 24 [“‘Before engaging in this two-step analysis, a court must consider any claims by the plaintiff that a statutory exemption contained in section 425.17 applies’”] [internal citation omitted].)

“The public interest exemption to the anti-SLAPP statute is set forth in section 425.17, subdivision (b).”  (Takhar, supra, 27 Cal.App.5th at p. 25.)  Pursuant to that exemption, “[s]ection 425.16 does not apply to any action brought solely in the public interest or on behalf of the general public if all of the following conditions exist: [¶] (1) The plaintiff does not seek any relief greater than or different from the relief sought for the general public or a class of which the plaintiff is a member.  A claim for attorney’s fees, costs, or penalties does not constitute greater or different relief for purposes of this subdivision.  [¶]  (2) The action, if successful, would enforce an important right affecting the public interest, and would confer a significant benefit ,whether pecuniary or nonpecuniary, on the general public or a large class of persons.  [¶]  (3) Private enforcement is necessary and places a disproportionate financial burden on the plaintiff in relation to the plaintiff’s stake in the matter.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.17, subd. (b).)

The court finds that Cross-Complainants have not met their burden to establish that the public interest exemption applies.  (Takhar, supra, 27 Cal.App.5th at p. 24 [“‘A plaintiff has the burden to establish the applicability of th[e] [public interest] exemption’”] [internal citation omitted]; Code Civ. Proc., § 425.17, subd. (b).)  

Cross-Complainants have brought this cause of action not on behalf of the general public or in the public interest, but on behalf of themselves, and to that end, seek from Cross-Defendants (1) damages for their alleged business losses, and (2) punitive damages.  (Cross-Compl., ¶ 41.)  They have not sought relief on behalf of the general public, or a class of which Cross-Complainants are members.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.17, subd. (b)(1).)  Cross-Complainants, although arguing that they “are attempting to curb the abuses created by Cross-Defendants from filing false reports to law enforcement and other government authorities[,]” have not presented adequate authority, argument, or evidence to establish that their cause of action for defamation is “brought solely on the public interest or on behalf of the general public” as required.  (Opp., p. 5:25-27; (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.17, subd. (b).)

The court therefore finds that the public interest exemption does not apply.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.17, subd. (b); Klem v. Access Ins. Co. (2017) 17 Cal.App.5th 595, 611 [“The exception ‘applies only when the entire action is brought in the public interest’”]; Takhar, supra, 27 Cal.App.5th at p. 25 [“‘If individualized relief is sought, a plaintiff [or cross-complainant] must satisfy the requirements of the anti-SLAPP statute in order for the action to proceed’”] [internal citation omitted].)

2.     First Prong: Protected Activity

Courts analyze special motions to strike under a two-step approach.¿ “Initially, the moving defendant bears the burden of establishing that the challenged allegations or claims ‘aris[e] from’ protected activity in which the defendant has engaged.”¿ (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1061.)¿ “[T]he statutory phrase ‘cause of action…arising from’ means simply that the defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.¿ [Citation.]¿ In the anti-SLAPP context, the critical point is whether the plaintiff’s cause of action itself was based on an act in furtherance of the defendant’s right of petition or free speech.”¿ (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 78.)¿ The moving defendant will meet this burden by demonstrating that the plaintiff’s claim fits one of the categories outlined in Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16, subdivision (e).¿ (Ibid.)¿¿ 

The protected acts in furtherance of a defendant’s right of petition or free speech include:¿ 

  1. Any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; or¿ 
  1. Any written or oral statement made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law; or¿ 
  1. Any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest; or¿¿ 
  1. Any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.¿¿ 

(Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16, subd. (e).)¿¿ 

The court finds that Cross-Defendants have met their burden to show that the fourth cause of action for defamation arises from protected activity.  (Park, supra, 2 Cal.5th at p. 1061 [moving defendant or cross-defendant bears the first burden of showing that the claims arise from protected activity in which the defendant has engaged].) 

Cross-Complainants have based their fourth cause of action for defamation on the following three claims alleged in the Cross-Complaint: (1) “defamation [committed by Cross-Defendants] ensued by including in the Complaint how Cross-Complainants are liars, thieves, frauds, and scammers, despite the known fact that Cross-Defendants were negligent in retrieving their vehicle which caused incurring storage costs and fees” (Cross-Compl., ¶ 38 (second)[1]);    (2) Cross-Defendants “made these statements to the Los Angeles County District Attorney’s Office and the Department of Motor Vehicles Investigator which resulted in Cross-Complainant’s place of business to be swarmed with police vehicles, which caused Cross-Complainant’s customers to be increasingly concerned” (Cross-Compl., ¶ 39); and (3) “Cross-Defendants additionally blatantly inferred moral turpitude on Cross-Complainants by constantly referring in all court filings referencing case XNWLA099026-01” (Cross-Compl., ¶ 40).  The court finds that all of the claims alleged arise from protected activity.

First, the court finds that the claims that are based on Cross-Defendants’ statements about Cross-Complainants, made in Cross-Defendants’ court filings (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 38 (second), 40), arise from Cross-Defendants’ statements and writings made before a judicial proceeding.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e)(1); Navellier v. Sletten (2002) 29 Cal.4th 82, 90 [“A claim for relief filed in federal district court [or other court] indisputably is a statement or writing made before a . . . judicial proceeding”].)

Second, the court finds that the claim that is based on Cross-Defendants’ statements about Cross-Complainants made to the Los Angeles County District Attorney’s Office and the Department of Motor Vehicles Investigator (Compl., ¶ 39) arises from Cross-Defendants’ protected activity in making statements made before an official proceeding since (1) Cross-Complainants have alleged that Cross-Defendants reported to government entities (i.e., the Los Angeles County District Attorney’s Office and the Department of Motor Vehicles) that Cross-Complainants had stolen the subject vehicle (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 18, 39), and (2) those allegations show that the purpose of the statements was to solicit investigations by the District Attorney’s Office and the Department of Motor Vehicles’ investigator, which constitutes protected activity.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (e)(1); ComputerXpress, Inc. v. Jackson (2001) 93 Cal.App.4th 993, 1009 [finding that the filing of a complaint with the Securities and Exchange Commission was a statement before an official proceeding, and explaining that “[i]n the analogous context of the privilege under Civil Code section 47 for a statement in an official proceeding, the California Supreme Court has observed that the term ‘official proceeding’ has been interpreted broadly to protect communications to or from governmental officials which may precede the initiation of formal proceedings.’  [Citation.]  Thus, communications to an official administrative agency . . . designed to promote action by that agency is as much a part of the official proceeding as a communication made after the proceedings had commenced”] [internal quotation marks, citations, and emphasis omitted].)

The court notes that Cross-Complainants have argued, in their opposition papers, that the alleged conduct is not subject to the anti-SLAPP statute because (1) the filing of a false report to law enforcement officials is not protected, and (2) Cross-Defendants made false statements to law enforcement and other government authorities.  (Opp., p. 6:23-26; Lefebvre v. Lefebvre (2011) 199 Cal.App.4th 696, 703 [“Neither the federal nor the state constitutional right of petition or free speech encompasses a right to file a false crime report”] [emphasis in original].)  However, the court finds that Cross-Complainants have not shown that Cross-Defendants’ reports to the Los Angeles County District Attorney’s Office and the Department of Motor Vehicles were false and therefore fall outside of the scope of the anti-SLAPP statute.[2] 

 

 

3.     Second Prong: Probability of Prevailing

The second prong places the burden on the plaintiff to establish that there is a probability the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b).)  “To meet this burden under the second prong of the statute, ‘the plaintiff “must demonstrate that the complaint is both legally sufficient and supported by a sufficient prima facie showing of facts to sustain a favorable judgment if the evidence submitted by the plaintiff is credited.”  [Citations.]’”  (Maleti v. Wickers (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 181, 200.)  “The plaintiff must show that its claim has ‘at least “minimal merit.” ’”  (Ibid.)  “The second step burden of establishing a probability of prevailing is not high.  [Citation.]  It consists of a summary-judgment-like procedure.”  (Billauer v. Escobar-Eck (2023) 88 Cal.App.5th 953, 964.)

First, the court notes that Cross-Defendants have argued, in their moving papers, that the fourth cause of action is barred by the litigation privilege.  As set forth above, ordinarily, it is the burden on the plaintiff or cross-complainant on the second prong to establish the probability of prevailing on the merits.  (Billauer, supra, 88 Cal.App.5th at p. 964.)  However, “[d]uring the second prong of a court’s anti-SLAPP analysis, a defendant bears the burden of proving a privilege’s applicability.”  (Neurelis, Inc. v. Aquestive Therapeutics, Inc. (2021) 71 Cal.App.5th 769, 794; Billauer, supra, 88 Cal.App.5th at p. 965 [addressing first the issue of whether the subject communications were absolutely privileged under Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b).)  The court finds that Cross-Defendants have not met that burden.

“Civil Code section 47, subdivision (b) renders absolutely privileged communications made as part of a ‘judicial or quasi-judicial proceeding.’  [Citations.]  ‘The usual formulation is that the privilege applies to any communication (1) made in judicial or quasi-judicial proceedings; (2) by litigants or other participants authorized by law; (3) to achieve the objects of the litigation; and (4) that have some connection or logical relation to the action.’”  (Neurelis, Inc., supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 794.)  

While the court acknowledges that Cross-Defendants have cited authority discussing the litigation privilege, Cross-Defendants did not present adequate argument and analysis establishing that the litigation privilege applies to bar Cross-Complainants’ claims for defamation in this action.  (Neurelis, Inc., supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 794.)  For example, Cross-Defendants did not present sufficient argument establishing that each element of the privilege has been met and applies to each of the three claims alleged in support of this cause of action, including argument and authority establishing that (1) the statements made to the District Attorney’s Office and Department of Motor Vehicles constitutes a communication made in a judicial or quasi-judicial proceeding, and (2) the communications made to those entities and in the Complaint were made to achieve the objects of the litigation.[3]  (Neurelis, Inc., supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 794 [noting that the defendant “insist[ed] it has done enough to prove the applicability of the privilege simply by pointing to the citizen privilege.  It has not”].)  Cross-Defendants similarly did not address each element of the litigation privilege as applied to the claims relating to the allegations made in their Complaint.

Second, the court finds that Cross-Complainants have not met their burden to produce evidence, which the court must accept as true, to make a prima facie showing that their cause of action for defamation has minimal merit.  (Billauer, supra, 88 Cal.App.5th at pp. 964 [plaintiff or cross-complainant has the burden to establish minimal merit], 966 [proceeding to consider whether the plaintiff satisfied her burden of showing a probability of success on the merits after concluding that the litigation privilege does not apply].)

“Libel per se is when the communication is defamatory without the need for explanatory matter.”  (Billauer, supra, 88 Cal.App.5th at p. 966; Civ. Code, § 45a.)  The elements of defamation are “(a) a publication that is (b) false, (c) defamatory, and (d) unprivileged, and that (e) has a natural tendency to injure or that causes special damage.”  (Sanchez v. Bezos (2022) 80 Cal.App.5th 750, 763 [internal quotation marks and citations omitted].)

The court finds that Cross-Complainants have not presented adequate argument or evidence establishing that they can make a prima facie showing of the element of falsity.  (Sanchez, supra, 80 Cal.App.5th at p. 763.)  As set forth above, Cross-Complainants have based their defamation claims on the allegations that Cross-Defendants falsely stated that Cross-Complainants “are liars, thieves, frauds, and scammers” regarding the alleged theft of the subject Thunderbird, and that they have inferred moral turpitude by referring to Case No. XNWLA099026-01 before it has been adjudicated.  (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 18, 38 (second), 39-40). However, the court finds that Cross-Complainants have not presented competent evidence to make a prima facie showing that these statements were false.

As to the statements that Cross-Complainants stole the Thunderbird (Cross-Compl., ¶¶ 18, 38 (second) 39), Cross-Complainants did not present evidence, such as a declaration from Cross-Complainants, to show that they did not steal or unlawfully take this vehicle.  Further, as set forth above, the court has sustained Cross-Defendants’ evidentiary objections to the evidence submitted in support of the opposition papers (i.e., the Department of Motor Vehicles’ investigation report) because counsel did not properly authenticate that exhibit and because the statements therein are inadmissible hearsay.  However, even if the court had overruled the objection to that exhibit and considered it in ruling on this motion, the court finds that the investigation report does not support a prima facie showing that these statements were false, because the investigation report states only that the investigator concluded that “there was no crime nor violations in regard to the transactions involving the 2007 Bentley,” and therefore does not show that Cross-Complainants made false statements regarding the “claims of theft in regards to the ’65 Thunderbird[.]”[4]  (Chuah Decl., PDF p. 15 [emphasis added]; Cross-Compl., ¶ 38 (second) [emphasis added].)

As to the alleged statements made regarding the references to Case No. XNWLA099026-01 (Cross-Compl., ¶ 40), Cross-Complainants have not shown that any such statements were false.  Cross-Complainants did not, for example, present argument or evidence to make a prima facie showing that the case did not involve Cross-Complainants’ moral turpitude.  Moreover, Cross-Complainants do not dispute that such a case had been filed against them.  (Cross-Compl., ¶ 40 [alleging that the case “has not been adjudicated”]; Chuah Decl., ¶ 7 [stating that Case No. XNWLA099026-01 was dismissed on August 2, 2024].)

Thus, the court finds that Cross-Complainants have not met their burden to make a prima facie showing that the fourth cause of action for defamation per se has minimal merit and therefore grants Cross-Defendants’ special motion to strike that cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b)(1); Neurlis, Inc., supra, 71 Cal.App.5th at p. 783 [if the plaintiff fails to meet its burden on the second prong, the court will strike the claim].)

4.     Attorney’s Fees

Cross-Defendants request an award of $24,735 in attorney’s fees.  The court finds that Cross-Defendants have established a lodestar figure of $17,390 and therefore awards them attorney’s fees in that amount.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).)

“[A] prevailing defendant on a special motion to strike shall be entitled to recover that defendant’s attorney’s fees and costs.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1); Frym v. 601 Main Street LLC (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 613, 619 [“‘[A]ny SLAPP defendant who brings a successful motion to strike is entitled to mandatory attorney fees’”].)  “[T]he fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. . . . .¿ The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.¿ The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.”¿ (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095 (internal citations omitted).)¿ “[T]he verified time statements of the attorneys, as officers of the court, are entitled to credence in the absence of a clear indication the records are erroneous.”¿ (Horsford v. Board of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal.App.4th 359, 396.)¿¿ 

Cross-Defendants have submitted the declarations of their attorneys, in which counsel attest to their qualifications, skill, and experience.  (Hartman Decl., ¶ 23; Semnar Decl., ¶¶ 11-17.)  The court finds that (1) a reasonable hourly rate for attorney Hartman is $600, and (2) a reasonable hourly rate for attorney Semnar is $650.  (Ibid.)  The court finds that the total amount of 28.7 hours expended in connection with preparing the special motion to strike is reasonable.  (Hartman Decl., ¶¶ 16-20; Semnar Decl., ¶¶ 7-8.)  Thus, the court finds that Cross-Defendants have established a lodestar amount of $17,390 ((25.3 hours x $600 hourly rate for Hartman) + (3.4 hours x $650 hourly rate for attorney Semnar). 

The court denies Cross-Complainants’ request for attorney’s fees.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).)

ORDER

            The court grants plaintiffs and cross-defendants Reza Safaie and Aaron Trucking, Inc.’s special motion to strike.

            The court orders that the fourth cause of action for defamation per se, alleged in defendants and cross-complainants Nicholas Fuentes and Old Earl Scheib Paint and Body’s Cross-Complaint, is stricken.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (b).)

            The court orders defendants and cross-complainants Nicholas Fuentes and Old Earl Scheib Paint and Body to pay attorney’s fees in the amount of $17,390 to plaintiffs and cross-defendants Reza Safaie and Aaron Trucking, Inc. within 30 days of the date of this order.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 425.16, subd. (c)(1).)

            The court orders plaintiffs and cross-defendants Reza Safaie and Aaron Trucking, Inc. to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.

(1)   Demurrer to Complaint

(2)   Motion to Strike Portions of Complaint

The court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers filed in connection with the demurrer and motion to strike.

BACKGROUND

Plaintiffs Julieann Berg (“Berg”) and Terri James (“James”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) filed this breach of warranty of habitability action on April 8, 2024 against defendants Syliva and Harry Naman Heritage Properties, LLC, Naman Family Investments, LP, Chad Naman, as trustee of the Chad Naman 2021 Trust, Kimberly Naman, as trustee of the Kimberly Naman 2021 Trust, and Amy Ruggles, as trustee of the Jennifer Naman 2021 Trust.[1] 

Defendants Syliva and Harry Naman Heritage Properties, LLC, Naman Family Investments, LP, Chad Naman, as trustee of the Chad Naman 2021 Trust, Kimberly Naman, as trustee of the Kimberly Naman 2021 Trust (“Defendants”) now move the court for an order     (1) sustaining their demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth, sixth, and 12th causes of action, and (2) striking from Plaintiffs’ Complaint the requests for punitive damages and supporting allegations.

DEMURRER

The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to plaintiff James’s fifth cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since plaintiff James (1) is not required to allege that she was actually or constructively evicted to maintain this cause of action, and (2) has alleged that Defendants interfered with her right to quiet enjoyment of the property (Compl., ¶ 107).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Ginsberg v. Gamson (2012) 205 Cal.App.4th 873, 898 [“breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment can be understood as a title encompassing claims for wrongful eviction, and also claims in which the tenant’s use of the premises is disturbed, but the tenant remains in possession”] [emphasis added]; Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1300 [“In recent years, the covenant of quiet enjoyment has been expanded, and in this state, for example, it insulates the tenant against any act or omission on the part of the landlord, or anyone claiming under him, which interferes with a tenant’s right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy”] [internal quotation marks and citations omitted]; Nativi v. Deutsche Bank National Trust Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 261,392 [“some authorities recognize that a tenant may sue for breach of the covenant while remaining in possession”].)

The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action for nuisance because (1) it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action (Compl., ¶¶ 25, 29, 114), and (2) the court finds that it is not duplicative of the negligence cause of action.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Lynch v. Peter & Associates, Engineers, Geologists, Surveyors, Inc. (2024) 104 Cal.App.5th 1181, 1198 [“Even if the nuisance claim relies on the same facts about lack of due care supporting the negligence claim, it does not mean the claims are identical or duplicative . . . .  Also, assessing a nuisance claim in this manner ignores the fact that a duty between defendant and plaintiff need not exist to maintain a nuisance claim”].)

            The court overrules Defendants’ demurrer to Plaintiffs’ 12th cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress because it states facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action since Plaintiffs have alleged facts establishing that (1) Defendants’ conduct, in failing to address the mold growing in Plaintiffs’ units, causing Plaintiffs to suffer health and respiratory issues (Compl., ¶¶ 45, 68, 70), despite their knowledge and Plaintiffs’ complaints about the condition of their units (Compl., ¶¶ 38-40, 43, 44-48, 51, 55, 168), was “so extreme as to exceed all bounds of decency in a civilized community[,]” and (2) Plaintiffs suffered severe emotional distress (Compl., ¶ 173).  (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e); Berry v. Frazier (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1258, 1273 [elements of intentional infliction of emotional distress].)

MOTION TO STRIKE

Defendants move the court for an order striking (1) Plaintiffs’ requests for punitive damages (Compl., ¶¶ 72, 74, 84, 103, 111, 118, 140, 174; Compl., Prayer, ¶ 5), and (2) the supporting allegations (Compl., ¶¶ 66, 73, and pp. 15:17-23 (erroneously labeled ¶ 53), 16:2-3 (erroneously labeled ¶ 55)).

The court grants Defendants’ motion to strike plaintiff James’s request for punitive damages, as alleged in support of the fifth cause of action for breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment only (Compl., ¶ 111), because that cause of action, as alleged by plaintiff James, sounds in contract and therefore cannot support a request for punitive damages.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a); Ginsberg, supra, 205 Cal.App.4th at pp. 901 [“These cases do not support the proposition that a tort claim arises out of the breach of the covenant of quiet enjoyment, when there has been no eviction and the tenant remains in possession of the premises”], 902 [“when the landlord has breached the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, but the tenant remains in possession of the premises, the tenant’s remedy is to ‘sue for breach of contract damages’”]; Spinks v. Equity Residential Briarwood Apartments (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1030-1031 [characterizing breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment to be a cause of action “sounding in contract”].)

The court denies Defendants’ motion to strike the remaining allegations and requests for punitive damages because Plaintiffs have alleged sufficient facts establishing that (1) Defendants were guilty of malice or oppression based on the allegations that Defendants failed to remediate the mold that was visible in Plaintiffs’ units throughout their tenancies, including after Plaintiffs advised Defendants’ agent, Ora Maschiach, of the adverse physical symptoms that they suffered as a result thereof (Compl., ¶¶ 38, 40, 42-45, 50), and (2) Defendants’ authorization, ratification, or act of oppression and malice was on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of Defendants (Compl., pp. 15:16-16:10.)  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Civ. Code, § 3294, subds. (a), (b); King v. U.S. Bank National Association (2020) 53 Cal.App.5th 675, 711 [discussing malice and despicable conduct within the meaning of Civil Code section 3294].)

The burden is on the plaintiff “to articulate how it could amend its pleading to render it sufficient.”¿ (Palm Springs Villas II Homeowners Assn., Inc. v. Parth (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 268, 290.)¿ To satisfy that burden, a plaintiff “must show in what manner he can amend his complaint and how that amendment will change the legal effect of his pleading.”¿ (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.)  The court finds that plaintiff James has not met her burden to articulate how she can amend her request for punitive damages, made in connection with the fifth cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, to render it sufficient against Defendants.  The court therefore grants Defendants’ motion to strike that request (Compl., ¶ 111) without leave to amend.

ORDER

            The court overrules defendants Syliva and Harry Naman Heritage Properties, LLC, Naman Family Investments, LP, Chad Naman, as trustee of the Chad Naman 2021 Trust, Kimberly Naman, as trustee of the Kimberly Naman 2021 Trust’s demurrer to plaintiffs Julieann Berg and Terri James’s Complaint.

            The court grants defendants Syliva and Harry Naman Heritage Properties, LLC, Naman Family Investments, LP, Chad Naman, as trustee of the Chad Naman 2021 Trust, Kimberly Naman, as trustee of the Kimberly Naman 2021 Trust’s motion to strike the request for punitive damages as alleged by plaintiff Terri James in support of the fifth cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, as set forth in paragraph 111 of the Complaint.

            The court denies all other relief requested in defendants Syliva and Harry Naman Heritage Properties, LLC, Naman Family Investments, LP, Chad Naman, as trustee of the Chad Naman 2021 Trust, Kimberly Naman, as trustee of the Kimberly Naman 2021 Trust’s motion to strike.

            The court orders defendants Syliva and Harry Naman Heritage Properties, LLC, Naman Family Investments, LP, Chad Naman, as trustee of the Chad Naman 2021 Trust, Kimberly Naman, as trustee of the Kimberly Naman 2021 Trust to file an answer to plaintiffs Julieann Berg and Terri James’s Complaint no later than 10 days from the date of this order.

            The court orders plaintiffs Julieann Berg and Terri James to give notice of this ruling.

IT IS SO ORDERED.
