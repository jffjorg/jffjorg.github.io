Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

TENTATIVE RULING


Lashawna Nadreau’s Motions to Compel Briana Flowers’ and Brianna Hollens’ Responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Demand for Inspection and Production of Documents, Set One, and for Monetary Sanctions are granted, in part, and denied, in part.

 

The Court notes that no notice was provided of the hearing date of April 24, 2024. Thus, the tentative ruling is conditioned upon all parties waiving the defect in notice.

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint was filed on January 13, 2023. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiffs were involved in a motor vehicle accident with Defendant.  

 

Motions to Compel

 

CCP § 2030.290 states: “If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the following rules apply…The party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (CCP § 2030.290(b).) 

 

CCP § 2031.300 states: “If a party to whom a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed fails to serve a timely response to it, the following rules shall apply: The party making the demand may move for an order compelling response to the demand.”  (CCP § 2031.300(b)).

 

Defendant served upon Plaintiffs Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Demand for Inspection and Production of Documents, Set One, on September 21, 2023. (Decls., Adam A. Chavez, ¶¶ 5-9.)  Defendant contends that no responses were served to the requests. (Id.) Plaintiffs failed to file any written oppositions to the motions.

 

Therefore, the motions to compel responses to Form Interrogatories, Set One, Special Interrogatories, Set One, and Demand for Inspection and Production of Documents, Set One, are granted.

 

Plaintiffs are ordered to serve responses without objections within 10 days of this date.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendant’s requests for monetary sanctions are denied. Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040 states, in relevant part: “A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.”

 

Here, the notices of the motions failed to identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanctions are sought and failed to specify the type of sanctions sought.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Calsafe Research Center, Inc. v. The Napoleon Co., et al.

23TRCV00310

1.      Calsafe Research Center, Inc.’s Motion to Approve Settlement    

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Calsafe Research Center, Inc.’s Motion to Approve Settlement is granted.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on February 3, 2023. Plaintiff alleges the following facts. Defendant manufactures, packages, distributes, markets, and/or sells consumer products that contain lead. Plaintiff seeks to cause Defendant to warn consumers in California that they are being exposed to lead pursuant to Proposition 65 - Health and Safety Code § 25249.5. Plaintiff requests an injunction and civil penalties.

 

Motion to Approve Settlement

 

CCP § 664.6 states: “If parties to pending litigation stipulate, in a writing signed by the parties outside the presence of the court or orally before the court, for settlement of the case, or part thereof, the court, upon motion, may enter judgment pursuant to the terms of the settlement. If requested by the parties, the court may retain jurisdiction over the parties to enforce the settlement until performance in full of the terms of the settlement.”

 

Health & Saf. Code, § 25249.7(f)(4) states, in relevant part:

“If there is a settlement of an action brought by a person in the public interest under subdivision (d), the plaintiff shall submit the settlement, other than a voluntary dismissal in which no consideration is received from the defendant, to the court for approval upon noticed motion, and the court may approve the settlement only if the court makes all of the following findings:

(A) The warning that is required by the settlement complies with this chapter.

(B) The award of attorney's fees is reasonable under California law.

(C) The penalty amount is reasonable based on the criteria set forth in paragraph (2) of subdivision (b).

 

Plaintiff contends that on or about September 21, 2023, the parties finalized the terms of a settlement and executed a proposed consent judgment which was forwarded to the attorney general for reporting as required by the applicable statute. (Decl., Joseph R. Manning, ¶¶ 9-10, Ex. B.)  The Court finds that the injunctive relief that is required by the settlement complies with Proposition 65, codified in Health and Safety Code Section 25249.5 et seq. The Court finds that the award of attorneys’ fees of $18,000 is reasonable and that the penalty amount of $2,000.00 is reasonable under the criteria set forth in Section 25249.7(b)(2).

 

Thus, the Court grants the Motion to Approve Settlement and enters judgment in accordance with terms of the consent judgment.

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Kenneth E. Follmar, III, et al. v. Selene Finance, LP, et al.

24TRCV00924

1.      Kenneth E. Follmar, III, et al.’s Application for a Preliminary Injunction

 

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Kenneth E. Follmar, III, et al.’s Application for a Preliminary Injunction is denied pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 526.

 

Background

 

Plaintiffs’ Complaint was filed on March 18, 2024. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. Plaintiffs obtained a first lien mortgage loan secured by the property located at 44 Buckskin Lane, Rolling Hills Estates, CA 90274, through a Deed of Trust, recorded on May 5, 2016. Plaintiffs suffered from financial hardship. On or around July 12, 2022, Plaintiffs entered into a loan modification agreement with Bank of America. Thereafter, Bank of America transferred the servicer rights to Selene Finance (“Selene”). Plaintiffs requested a repayment plan with Selene, but Selene did not offer a plan that Plaintiffs could manage. MTC Financial Inc. recorded a notice of default. Prior to recording the NOD, Defendants did not contact Plaintiff to assess the borrower’s financial situation and explore options for the borrower to avoid foreclosure, as required by Civ. Code § 2923.5(b). Defendants did not provide a single point of contact as required by Civ. Code § 2923.7.

 

Plaintiffs allege the following causes of action: 1. Violation of Civ. Code § 2923.5; 2. Violation of Civ. Code § 2923.7; 3. Declaratory Relief; 4. Violations of Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, et seq.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

Defendant’s request for judicial notice is granted. The Court may take judicial notice of recorded documents.  Evid. Code, § 452, subds. (c), (h); Yvanova v. New Century Mortg. Corp. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 919, 924, fn. 1.

 

Application for Preliminary Injunction

 

            The legislature designed the California Homeowner Bill of Rights Act (“HBOR”) to provide plaintiffs the ability to obtain an injunction to enjoin foreclosure when there is a material violation of HBOR.  With the first through second causes of action, Plaintiffs have alleged HBOR claims. 

 

            Civ. Code, § 2924.19 states, in material part:

            “(a)(1) If a trustee's deed upon sale has not been recorded, a borrower may bring an action for injunctive relief to enjoin a material violation of Section 2923.5, 2924.17, or 2924.18.

            (2) An injunction shall remain in place and any trustee's sale shall be enjoined until the court determines that the mortgage servicer, mortgagee, beneficiary, or authorized agent has corrected and remedied the violation or violations giving rise to the action for injunctive relief. An enjoined entity may move to dissolve an injunction based on a showing that the material violation has been corrected and remedied.

            (b) After a trustee's deed upon sale has been recorded, a mortgage servicer, mortgagee, beneficiary, or authorized agent shall be liable to a borrower for actual economic damages pursuant to Section 3281, resulting from a material violation of Section 2923.5, 2924.17, or 2924.18 by that mortgage servicer, mortgagee, beneficiary, or authorized agent where the violation was not corrected and remedied prior to the recordation of the trustee's deed upon sale. If the court finds that the material violation was intentional or reckless, or resulted from willful misconduct by a mortgage servicer, mortgagee, beneficiary, or authorized agent, the court may award the borrower the greater of treble actual damages or statutory damages of fifty thousand dollars ($50,000).”

 

            In addition, pursuant to CC § 2924.12(a)(1): “If a trustee's deed upon sale has not been recorded, a borrower may bring an action for injunctive relief to enjoin a material violation of Section 2923.55, 2923.6, 2923.7, 2924.9, 2924.10, 2924.11, or 2924.17.”  In addition, injunctive relief is a remedy available under the third cause of action for Violation of Business & Professions Code § 17200.  Bus. & Prof. Code § 17203.

           

            “In determining whether to issue a preliminary injunction, the trial court considers two related factors: (1) the likelihood that the plaintiff will prevail on the merits of its case at trial, and (2) the interim harm that the plaintiff is likely to sustain if the injunction is denied as compared to the harm that the defendant is likely to suffer if the court grants a preliminary injunction.  The latter factor involves consideration of such things as the inadequacy of other remedies, the degree of irreparable harm, and the necessity of preserving the status quo.”  14859 Moorpark Homeowner’s Assn. v. VRT Corp. (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 1396, 1402.

 

            The court finds that Plaintiffs failed to meet their burden to demonstrate a likelihood that Plaintiffs will prevail on the merits of their case at trial.  Plaintiffs failed to establish that Defendants committed a material violation of HBOR.  Thus, Plaintiffs have not met their burden to establish entitlement to injunctive relief to enjoin a material violation of Civ. Code Sections 2923.5 and 2923.7.  CC § 2924.19(a)(3); CC § 2924.12(a)(1). 

 

            Plaintiffs allege material violations of Sections 2923.5 and 2923.7 based on the self-serving conclusory declaration of Plaintiff Kenneth Follmar.  However, the conclusions of violations of these Sections are materially controverted with competent evidence by Defendant.

 

            Defendant has provided evidence to show that Defendant complied with Section 2923.5. Section 2923.5(b) states that a notice of default may not be recorded until a loan servicer has contacted or, with due diligence, attempted to contact the borrower to discuss foreclosure avoidance options. Here, Defendant did send Plaintiffs letters regarding foreclosure avoidance options that contained the HUD toll-free telephone number and made numerous attempts to speak to Plaintiffs by telephone. (Decl., Nik Fox, ¶¶ 12-30; Exs. 4-16.)  In addition, in numerous correspondences, Defendant identified a single point of contact in compliance with CC § 2923.7. (Id.) Defendant’s submitted significant evidence supported with accompanying documents showing material compliance with Sections 2923.5 and 2923.7. Again, Defendant’s evidence refutes Plaintiff’s own self-serving declaration of alleged violations.

 

            As to the non-HBOR causes of action, pursuant to Bus. & Prof. Code § 17200, Plaintiffs failed to demonstrate a likelihood of success on the merits.  Plaintiffs failed to establish standing due to the underlying default in their loan obligations. To establish standing for purposes of the UCL claim, Plaintiffs must provide evidence to show a causal link between their economic injury and Defendant’s alleged wrongful acts.  Jenkins v. JP Morgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 497 (disapproved on other grounds in Yvanova v. New Century Mortg. Corp. (2016) 62 Cal.4th 919).  Here, Plaintiffs submitted no competent evidence to establish such a causal link. The declaratory relief cause of action is based on an alleged existing controversy pursuant to the alleged HBOR violations, and thus fails to establish a likelihood of success on the merits for purposes of injunctive relief.  

 

            Thus, Plaintiffs failed to demonstrate that they are likely to succeed on the merits.  Therefore, the interim harm that Defendant may suffer if the injunction is granted is greater than Plaintiffs’ relative harm. 

 

Thus, the application for a preliminary injunction is denied.

 

The temporary restraining order is hereby dissolved.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
