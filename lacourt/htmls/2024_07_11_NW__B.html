DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Attorneys who elect to submit on these published tentative rulings, without making an appearance at the hearing, may so notify the Court by communicating this to the Department's staff before the set hearing time. See, e.g., CRC Rule 324(b).

Case Number: 19STCV28184    Hearing Date: July 11, 2024    Dept: B

HEARING DATE:    Fri., July 12, 2024                               JUDGE /DEPT:        Moskowitz/Dept. B

CASE NAME:           Juan Ramon Fuentes Hernandez. v. Manuel DeJesus Puga et al

CASE NUMBER:     19STCV28184

PROCEEDINGS:     MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants, DNELEVETS, INC. and MANUEL DEJESUS PUGA

RESP. PARTY:         None

 

MOTION FOR LEAVE TO AMEND TO FILE FIRST AMENDED ANSWER

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            The Court GRANTS the motion for leave to amend to allow Defendants to file a First Amended Answer. Defendants are ordered to file the First Amended Answer within 20 days of this order.

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

            Plaintiff Juan Ramon Fuentes Hernandez filed the instant lawsuit alleging causes of action for negligence and negligence per se on August 9, 2019. On October 24, 2019, Defendants filed their Answer.

             On May 23, 2024, Defendants filed their Motion for Leave to Amend to File First Amended Answer.

 

            On June 7, 2024, the matter was assigned to Judge Karen Moskowitz in Department B of the Van Nuys Courthouse East.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

            “The court may, in furtherance of justice, and on any terms as may be proper, allow a party to amend any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 473(a)(1).) “Any judge, at any time before or after commencement of trial, in the furtherance of justice, and upon such terms as may be proper, may allow the amendment of any pleading or pretrial conference order.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 576.) Specifically, there is a right to amend “to correct inadvertent misstatements of facts or erroneous allegations of terms.” (Berman v. Bromberg (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 936, 945.)

 

            “A motion to amend a pleading before trial must . . . [s]tate what allegations in the previous pleading are proposed to be deleted, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the deleted allegations are located; and [s]tate what allegations are proposed to be added to the previous pleading, if any, and where, by page, paragraph, and line number, the additional allegations are located.”¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(a).)¿ The declaration must also specify the amendment’s effect, why it is necessary and proper, when the facts supporting the amended allegations were discovered, and why the request was not made earlier.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1324(b).)¿

 

            Discussion

           

            Defendants seek to amend their Answer to include Proposition 213, Last Clear Chance, and reasonable and necessary medical expenses affirmative defenses.

 

            As a preliminary matter, the Court notes Defendants failed to include the courthouse address in the Notice of Motion. (Cal. Rul. of Court 3.1110 (b)(1).) Therefore, Plaintiff was not properly noticed. However, the case was reassigned on June 7, 2024, and notice was sent to Plaintiff on June 7, 2024, and June 18, 2024. As of July 10, 2024, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition.

 

            Here, Defendants have complied with California Rules of Court Rule 3.1324. (Hughes Decl. ¶¶ 3-8.) Moreover, Defendants provided a copy of the proposed First Amended Answer. (Id. at ¶ 14; Ex. 1.) Defendants would be severely and irreparably harmed if they are not allowed to file their First Amended Answer to assert primary and meritorious affirmative defenses in this matter. (Id.  at ¶ 13.) Notably, Plaintiff has not filed an opposition showing that he would be prejudiced by the amendment. Therefore, the Court grants the motion.

           

III.              Conclusion & Order

 

            The Court grants the Motion for Leave to Amend. Defendants are ordered to file the First Amended Answer within 20 days of this order.

BACKGROUND

 

On October 1, 2019, Plaintiff, Araceli Sanchez (“Plaintiff”), filed this action against Defendant, Alicia Rivera (“Defendant”) for damages arising from a motor vehicle accident.

 

Defendant now moves for the Court to impose terminating, evidentiary, issue sanctions, and /or monetary sanctions against Plaintiff on the grounds that Plaintiff has violated  prior court orders concerning her neurological examination, failed to provide documents relating to a subsequent September 4, 2018 accident (presumedly the result of spoliation of evidence), engaged in other forms of discovery abuse, and failed to pay monetary sanctions. Plaintiff opposes.

 

Trial is set for July 23, 2024.

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

As a preliminary matter, the court finds that the instant motion is untimely. Pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.020, “any party shall be entitled as a matter of right to complete discovery proceedings on or before the 30th day, and to have motions concerning discovery heard on or before the 15th day, before the date initially set for the trial of the action.” In this instance, trial is set for July 23, 2024. Therefore, any motion concerning discovery had to have been heard on or before July 8, 2024.

 

In her reply, Defendant claims that the instant motion is not a discovery motion. (Reply at pg. 5.) However, this argument is not persuasive because Defendant requests the Court to issue sanctions due to Plaintiff’s purported discovery abuses. Consequently, the statutory deadline set forth under Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.020 applies. Such discovery deadlines are jurisdictional. (See Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.4th 1403, 1409.) Defendant could have either sought to have the instant hearing advanced or moved pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.050 to have this matter heard, but Defendant chose to do neither.

 

Moreover, Defendant insists that the deadline pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 2024.030 should apply because Plaintiff obstructed Defendant’s medical expert from completing the medical examination, rendering the matter an expert witness issue. (Reply at pg. 5.) This argument is also not persuasive because the sanctions that Defendant seeks goes beyond expert discovery, and it seeks to curtail Plaintiff’s ability to admit certain evidence into the record.

 

Accordingly, because the instant motion is untimely, the motion is denied.

MOTION TO CONTINUE TRIAL DATE

(CRC Rule 3.1332)

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On April 28, 2022, Plaintiff Diana Bernabe (“Bernabe”) filed an action against Defendant Walgreen Co. (“Walgreen”) for premises liability arising out of an alleged slip and fall accident.

 

On July 12, 2023, pursuant to the Stipulation of the parties, the Court continued the trial date from October 26, 2023, to February 23, 2024.  On January 3, 2024, pursuant to the stipulation of the parties, the Court once again continued the trial date from February 23, 2024, to August 12, 2024.

 

On May 30, 2024, Defendant Walgreen filed the instant Motion to Continue Trial (“Motion”).

 

            On June 6, 2024, the matter was assigned to Judge Karen Moskowitz in Department B of the Van Nuys Courthouse East.  Plaintiff was ordered to give notice to all parties in this action within 10 days of service of this notice and file proof of service thereof within 12 days of this notice.  The Court notes that Plaintiff has failed to file proof of service of the Notice of Case Reassignment.

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Although continuances of trials are disfavored, each request for a continuance must be considered on its own merits.  (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(c).)  The Court may grant a continuance only on an affirmative showing of good cause requiring the continuance.  (Ibid.)  Good cause includes the unavailability of an essential lay or expert witness, party, or trial counsel; “the substitution of trial counsel, but only where there is an affirmative showing that the substitution is required in the interests of justice;” the addition of a new party; a party’s excused inability to obtain evidence; or a significant, unanticipated change in the case.  (Ibid.)

 

Furthermore, the Court may look to the following factors in determining whether a trial continuance is warranted:

 

(1) The proximity of the trial date;

(2) Whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial due to any party;

(3) The length of the continuance requested;

(4) The availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance;

(5) The prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance;

(6) If the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay;

(7) The court's calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials;

(8) Whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial;

(9) Whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance;

(10) Whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and

(11) Any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.

 

(Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332(d)(1)-(11).)

Defendant Walgreen moves for an order continuing the trial date from August 12, 2024, to October 29, 2024, or any other date thereafter.

The Court considers the circumstances of the instant case and the arguments presented in the Motion.  Although the trial date has been continued on two previous occasions, due to the unavailability of essential witnesses, the proximity of the trial date and the brief continuance requested, as well as Plaintiff’s lack of opposition, and the need for additional discovery, the Court finds that there is good cause to continue the trial.

 

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Defendant Walgreen Co.’s Motion to Continue Trial Date is GRANTED.

CASE NAME:           Eduardo Galeno v. Pacific Mechanical Contractor, et al.

CASE NUMBER:     22STCV14248                                   

 

PROCEEDINGS:     MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:   Defendants Pacific Mechanical Contractors and John Venegas

RESP. PARTY:         None

 

MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

(CCP §§ 2023.010, et seq.)

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendants Pacific Mechanical Contractors, et al.’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.  Plaintiff Eduardo Galeno’s Complaint is dismissed with prejudice.

 

Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions is also GRANTED in the amount of $721.65 to be paid by Plaintiff Eduardo Galeno within thirty (30) days of the Court’s Order.

 

 

ANALYSIS:

 

I.                    Background

 

On April 28, 2022, Plaintiff Eduardo Galeno (“Galeno”) filed an action against Defendants Pacific Mechanical Contractors (erroneously sued as “Pacific Mechanical Contractor”) (“Pacific”) and John Venegas (“Venegas”) (collectively “Defendants”) for motor vehicle and general negligence.

 

 

On May 10, 2024, Defendant Pacific filed the instant Motion for Terminating Sanctions (“Motion”).  No opposition has been filed.  On July 3, 2024, Defendant filed a Notice of Non-Opposition to the Motion.

 

            On June 6, 2024, the matter was assigned to Judge Karen Moskowitz in Department B of the Van Nuys Courthouse East.  Plaintiff was ordered to give notice to all parties in this action within 10 days of service of this notice and file proof of service thereof within 12 days of this notice.  The Court notes that Plaintiff has failed to file proof of service of the Notice of Case Reassignment.

 

 

II.                 Legal Standard

 

Where a party willfully disobeys a discovery order, courts have discretion to impose terminating, issue, evidence, or monetary sanctions.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2023.010(d), (g), 2023.030; R.S. Creative, Inc. v. Creative Cotton, Ltd. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 495.)

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.040 requires that “[a] request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.”  Furthermore, the notice of motion shall be supported by a memorandum of points and authorities, and accompanied by a declaration setting forth facts supporting the amount of any monetary sanction sought.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.040.)

 

Monetary sanctions may be imposed “ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct…unless [the Court] finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.030(a).)

 

Issue sanctions may be imposed “ordering that designated facts shall be taken as established in the action in accordance with the claim of the party adversely affected by the misuse of the discovery process. The court may also impose an issue sanction by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from supporting or opposing designated claims or defenses.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.030(b).)

 

Evidence sanctions may be imposed “by an order prohibiting any party engaging in the misuse of the discovery process from introducing designated matters in evidence.”  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.030(c).)

 

In more extreme cases, the Court may also impose terminating sanctions by “striking out the pleadings or parts of the pleadings,” “staying further proceedings,” “dismissing the action, or any part of the action,” or “rending a judgment by default” against the party misusing the discovery process.  (Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.030(d).)  The court should look to the totality of the circumstances in determining whether terminating sanctions are appropriate.  (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77¿Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)  Ultimate discovery sanctions are justified where there is a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.  (Van Sickle v. Gilbert (2011) 196¿Cal.App.4th 1495, 1516.)  “[A] penalty as severe as dismissal or default is not authorized where noncompliance with discovery is caused by an inability to comply rather than willfulness or bad faith.”  (Brown v. Sup. Ct. (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 701, 707.)  “Although in extreme cases a court has the authority to order a terminating sanction as a first measure [citations], a terminating sanction should generally not be imposed until the court has attempted less severe alternatives and found them to be unsuccessful and/or the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective.”  (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible and Tract Society of New York, Inc. (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604-605.)

 

In City of Los Angeles v. PricewaterhouseCoopers, LLC, the Court ruled that Code of Civil Procedure §§ 2023.010 and 2023.030 list the available discovery sanctions but do not independently authorize a court to impose specific sanctions.  (PriceewaterhouseCoopers (2022) 84 Cal.App.5th 466, 503-504.)  Thus, a court’s authority to impose the sanctions specified in §§ 2023.010 and 2023.030 must arise from a statute governing a particular method of discovery.  (Ibid.)

 

III.              Discussion

 

Defendants Pacific and Venegas move for terminating sanctions against Plaintiff Galeno or in the alternative, issue and evidentiary sanctions.  Defendants also request an order imposing monetary sanctions in the amount of $921.65 against Plaintiff.  (Ibid.)

 

The Court notes that during the pendency of the instant action, it has granted the following motions filed by Defendants against Plaintiff:

 

(1)   Motion for Order Deeming Admitted Truth of Facts and Imposing Monetary Sanctions, granted on April 3, 2024;

(2)   Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Interrogatories, granted on April 3, 2024;

(3)   Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Responses to Production of Documents, granted on May 8, 2024.

 

According to defense counsel, the Court’s orders were served on Plaintiff; however, Plaintiff has not responded to the discovery or paid any of the sanctions ordered by the Court.  (Shepard II Decl. ¶ 5.)  Additionally, as the Court has deemed the Requests for Admission admitted, Plaintiff has admitted that Defendants were not negligent or liable, and Plaintiff did not incur any damages.  (Ibid. at ¶ 6.)  Defendants also request monetary sanctions as follows: 2.3 hours spent on preparing the instant Motion, 1 hour to reply to an anticipated opposition and prepare for the hearing, and another hour to appear at the hearing, at a billing rate of $200.00, as well as $61.65 in filing and service fees, for a total of $921.65.  (Shepard Decl. ¶¶ 7-10.)

 

Defendants argue that “Plaintiff has willfully and consistently misused the discovery process by continually refusing to respond to written discovery” and directly disobeying the Court’s orders.  (Mot. p. 3.)  Moreover, Plaintiff’s failure to pay monetary sanctions demonstrates that lesser sanctions are not effective in compelling Plaintiff to comply with discovery.  (Ibid.)

 

            Plaintiff has not opposed the Motion.

 

Code of Civil Procedure § 2030.290 governs the imposition of sanctions in regard to failure to respond to interrogatories.  Section 2030.290(c) states that:

 

The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey an order compelling answers, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to that sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

(Code of Civ. Proc. § 2030.290(c), emphasis added.)

 

Similarly, Code of Civil Procedure § 2031.300 governs the imposition of sanctions in regard to failure to respond to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling.  Section 2031.300(c) states:

 

… the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. If a party then fails to obey the order compelling a response, the court may make those orders that are just, including the imposition of an issue sanction, an evidence sanction, or a terminating sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010). In lieu of or in addition to this sanction, the court may impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010).

 

(Code of Civ. Proc. § 2031.300(c), emphasis added.)

 

Based on §§ 2030.290(c) and 2031.300(c), the Court has authority to impose any of the sanctions specified in § 2030.030.

 

In determining whether terminating sanctions are appropriate in the instant case, the Court considers the totality of the circumstances, including whether there has been a willful discovery order violation, a history of abuse, and evidence showing that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with discovery rules.

 

Here, the Court finds that Defendant has presented sufficient evidence to support its request for terminating sanctions.  Plaintiff has failed to respond to Requests for Admission, Interrogatories, and Requests for Production.  Furthermore, the Court’s order compelling compliance with these discovery requests and monetary sanctions have not been effective.  The Motion is unopposed, and Plaintiff has not demonstrated any justification for his failure to comply with discovery.

 

The Court also finds $721.65 in monetary sanctions to be appropriate, as no reply has been filed.

 

IV.              Conclusion & Order

 

For the foregoing reasons,

 

Defendants Pacific Mechanical Contractors, et al.’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.  Plaintiff Eduardo Galeno’s Complaint is dismissed with prejudice.

 

Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions is also GRANTED in the amount of $721.65 to be paid by Plaintiff Eduardo Galeno within thirty (30) days of the Court’s Order.

 

Moving parties are ordered to give notice.

2. Meet and Confer

Prior to filing a demurrer or motion to strike, the demurring/moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer/motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer/motion to strike. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 430.41, 435.5.)¿¿¿ 

The Court finds that Barker Management has not fulfilled this requirement prior to filing the instant demurrer. (See Matta Decl., ¶¶2-5, Ex. A.) However, the Court notes that Barker Management attempted to meet and confer but received no response from Plaintiffs’ counsel. (Id.) Thus, the Court will exercise its discretion and rule on the merits below.

 

3. Demurrer to Complaint

a. Legal Standard

“The primary function of a pleading is to give the other party notice so that it may prepare its case [citation], and a defect in a pleading that otherwise properly notifies a party cannot be said to affect substantial rights.” (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240.)¿¿¿ 

            “A¿demurrer¿tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Ivanoff v. Bank of America, N.A.¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 719, 725.) The Court looks to whether “the complaint alleges facts sufficient to state a cause of action or discloses a complete defense.” (Id.) The Court does not “read passages from a complaint in isolation; in reviewing a ruling on a demurrer, we read the complaint ‘as a whole and its parts in their context.’ [Citation.]” (West v. JPMorgan Chase Bank, N.A. (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 780, 804.) The Court “assume[s] the truth of the properly pleaded factual allegations, facts that reasonably can be inferred from those expressly pleaded and matters of which judicial notice has been taken.” (Harris, supra, 56 Cal.4th p. 240.) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer may be brought under Code of Civil Procedure Section 430.10, subdivision (e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted or under section 430.10, subdivision (a), where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading. All other grounds listed in Section 430.10, including uncertainty under subdivision (f), are special demurrers. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 92(c).)¿¿¿ 

            Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)¿¿ 

 

b. Discussion

            Barker Management demurs to the first, third, fourth, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, eleventh, twelfth, and thirteenth causes of action on the grounds that (1) they fail to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action and (2) are uncertain.

 

First Cause of Action for Battery

“The essential elements of a cause of action for battery are: (1) defendant touched plaintiff, or caused plaintiff to be touched, with the intent to harm or offend plaintiff; (2) plaintiff did not consent to the touching; (3) plaintiff was harmed or offended by defendant's conduct; and (4) a reasonable person in plaintiff's position would have been offended by the touching.” (So v. Shin (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 652, 669.)

The Complaint alleges as follows:

On or about April 26, 2020, Plaintiffs began their lease at Innes Heights Apartments, where they were provided Unit #206. (Compl., ¶14.) Immediately after moving into the unit, Plaintiff LELAH BANKS was bitten by an unknown pest, but Plaintiffs were unsure of what exactly was occurring in their unit or the cause of the bites. (Id. at ¶15.) Nonetheless, Plaintiff LATANNYA BANKS reported the pest problem to the property manager, Defendant JUPITER. (Id.) Despite the report, no inspection of the apartment was made. (Id.) Plaintiffs later discovered at the end of May that these bites were from carpet beetles upon discovering them. (Id. at ¶16.) Subsequently, Plaintiffs sought medical care for their injuries at an urgent care facility and were both prescribed topical creams and oral antibiotics for their carpet beetle injuries. (Id. at ¶17.) Furthermore, despite multiple requests of assistance, Defendant JUPITER did not send an exterminator to the apartment for over six months. (Id. at ¶18.) Approximately six months after Plaintiffs’ first request for carpet beetle treatments, Defendant JUPITER finally sent an exterminator to the Plaintiffs’ home. (Id. at ¶19.) However, the apartment management did not adequately treat Plaintiffs apartment and the pest issues have persisted as of the date of this Complaint. (Id.) Also, because the exterminator visits were scheduled on such short notice, Plaintiffs were unable to prepare their home for the treatments, so the exterminator did not properly address the carpet beetle infestation. (Id.) Moreover, the exterminator only set up traps around the apartment and did not spray the unit. (Id.)

On or about November 5, 2020, Plaintiff LATANNYA BANKS reported the pest infestation

to the Health Department to which Defendant JUPITER scheduled an inspection with the Health Department with canines regarding bedbugs not carpet beetles. (Id. at ¶¶20-21.) As such, Plaintiff LATANNYA BANKS independently hired a pest control company and on or about November 10, 2020, they found evidence of carpet beetles and spiders and scheduled the carpet beetle extermination for November 11, 2020. (Id. at ¶22.) Pest control services returned once a month to perform

three treatments. (Id. at ¶23.) Likewise, Plaintiff LATANNYA BANKS repeatedly requested for the carpeting in the apartment to be removed, however, Defendant JUPITER did not comply with the Plaintiff’s request and  denied the existence of carpet beetles. (Id. at ¶24.) Defendant JUPITER also claimed that she contacted the pest control company that the Plaintiffs had independently used to treat the apartment and that the company did not find carpet beetles in the apartment, even though the pest control company’s report states otherwise. (Id. at ¶26.)To this day, the apartment management has not removed the contaminated carpeting in Plaintiffs’ home and Plaintiff LATANNYA BANKS frequently finds dead carpet beetles when she vacuums her apartment. (Id.)

On or about June of 2021, housing case manager Heather Hampton, a representative of the APLA Health agency, sent two notices to Defendant JUPITER regarding the substandard living conditions of the Plaintiffs and Plaintiffs’ request to remove the carpeting in the apartment. (Id. at ¶27.) Defendant JUPITER did not respond to either notice. (Id.) The carpet beetle issue at the Plaintiffs’ home is still ongoing as Plaintiffs still receive fresh bites to this day. (Id. at ¶28.) “As a result of these multiple incidents, Plaintiffs incurred various expenses, including medical costs, contaminated food, independently purchased foggers, pesticide sprays, at home essential oil remedies, hotel costs, the cost of their monthly rent while living in an uninhabitable residence, bedding, a newly purchased violin, the replacement cost of their furniture and clothing, along with other belongings exposed to the infestation at the Apartment, and other costs.” (Id. at ¶29.)

Here, the Complaint does state sufficient facts to support the battery cause of action. Plaintiffs allege Defendants conduct of failing to address the carpet beetle issue for months caused intentional and reckless unconsented harmful or offensive contact with Plaintiffs’ persons. (Id. at ¶41.) Such contact was the proximate and legal cause of Plaintiffs’ injuries and damages. (Id. at ¶43-45.) The Complaint also specifically alleges that Plaintiffs injuries resulted in Plaintiffs seeking medical intervention, where they were given medications for the carpet beetle bites and have been placed on breathing machines. (Id. at ¶40.) As such, Defendants have been put on adequate notice of the battery claim Plaintiffs have brought against them.

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the first cause of action.

 

Third Cause of Action for Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

“The elements of a cause of action for IIED are as follows: (1) defendant engaged in extreme and outrageous conduct (conduct so extreme as to exceed all bounds of decency in a civilized community) with the intent to cause, or with reckless disregard to the probability of causing, emotional distress; and (2) as a result, plaintiff suffered extreme or severe emotional distress.” (Berry v. Frazier (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1258, 1273.)

Here, the Complaint does state sufficient facts to support the IIED cause of action. Plaintiffs allege Defendants’ conduct in failing to adequately address the carpet beetle issue for six months and more despite being shown photographs of the pest issue, receiving medical documentation concerning the bites caused by the carpet beetles, and being given notices from the Health Department to address the issue was extreme and outrageous. () Plaintiffs further allege Defendants’ conduct caused them severe emotional distress. Thus, Defendants have been put on adequate notice of the IIED claim Plaintiffs have brought against them.

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the third cause of action.

Fourth, Fifth, and Tenth Cause of Actions Re: Breach of Warranty of Habitability

The Court notes that Defendants combine their arguments for the fourth, fifth, and tenth causes of action. Thus, the Court addresses them all at once below.

 

Pursuant to Civil Code Section 1941, “The lessor of a building intended for the occupation of human beings must, in the absence of an agreement to the contrary, put it into a condition fit for such occupation, and repair all subsequent dilapidations thereof, which render it untenantable, except such as are mentioned in section nineteen hundred and twenty-nine.” (Cal. Civ. Code, § 1941.)

“A dwelling shall be deemed untenantable for purposes of Section 1941 if it substantially lacks any of the following affirmative standard characteristics or is a residential unit described in Section 17920.3 or 17920.10 of the Health and Safety Code....” (Cal. Civ. Code, § 1941.1(a).) “Any building or portion thereof including any dwelling unit, guestroom or suite of rooms, or the premises on which the same is located, in which there exists any of the following listed conditions to an extent that endangers the life, limb, health, property, safety, or welfare of the public or the occupants thereof shall be deemed and hereby is declared to be a substandard building: [¶](12) Infestation of insects, vermin, or rodents as determined by a health officer or, if an agreement does not exist with an agency that has a health officer, the infestation can be determined by a code enforcement officer, as defined in Section 829.5 of the Penal Code, upon successful completion of a course of study in the appropriate subject matter as determined by the local jurisdiction.” (Cal. Health & Safety Code, § 17920.3(a).)

 “The elements of such an affirmative claim are the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises' habitability, notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant's discovery of the condition, the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency, and resulting damages.” (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1297.)

Here, the Complaint does state sufficient facts to support the fourth, fifth, and tenth causes of action. Plaintiffs allege they were still subject to substandard living conditions in Unit #206 due to the infestation of carpet beetles as of the date of the Complaint. (Compl., ¶¶Id.16-29, 72.) Plaintiffs also allege the pest control services report found evidence of carpet beetles and spiders. (Id.) Plaintiffs further allege she sent photographs of the carpet beetles to Defendant. (Id.) Plaintiffs allege they sent medical documentation regarding the carpet beetles to Defendants and Defendants were sent two notices from the Health Department to remove the carpet because of the infestation. (Id.) As such, Defendants have been put on adequate notice of the breach of habitability claims Plaintiffs have brought against them.

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the fourth, fifth, and tenth causes of action.

 

Sixth Cause of Action for Violation of Business & Professions Code §§ 17200 et seq.

“To bring a UCL claim, a plaintiff must show either an (1) ‘unlawful, unfair, or fraudulent business act or practice,’ or (2) ‘unfair, deceptive, untrue or misleading advertising.’” (Adhav v. Midway Rent A Car, Inc. (2019) 37 Cal.App.5th 954, 970.)” A plaintiff alleging unfair business practices under these statutes must state with reasonable particularity the facts supporting the statutory elements of the violation.” (Khoury v. Maly's of California, Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 619.)

Here, the Complaint fails to state sufficient facts to support the unfair business competition law cause of action. Plaintiffs merely allege Defendants’ conduct has the capacity to deceive consumers. (Compl., ¶116.) Plaintiffs further allege it was unlawful and unfair for Defendants’ to collection rent payment from them. (Id. at ¶ 114.) However, Plaintiffs fail to allege facts demonstrating/explaining the manner in which Defendants’ conduct constitute unfair, unlawful, and deceptive business practices. As such, Defendants have not been adequately put on notice in regard to this claim to properly defend against it.

Therefore, the demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days Leave to Amend as to the sixth cause of action.

 

Seventh Cause of Action for Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment

“In the absence of language to the contrary, every lease contains an implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, whereby the landlord impliedly covenants that the tenant shall have quiet enjoyment and possession of the premises.” (Andrews v. Mobile Aire Estates (2005) 125 Cal.App.4th 578, 588.) “The covenant of quiet enjoyment “insulates the tenant against any act or omission on the part of the landlord, or anyone claiming under him, which interferes with a tenants right to use and enjoy the premises for the purposes contemplated by the tenancy.” (Id.)

Here, the Complaint does state facts sufficient to support the breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment cause of action. Plaintiffs allege the carpet beetle issue caused them and their guests respiratory problems and severe allergies. (Compl., ¶24.) Plaintiffs have to rely on breathing machines. (Id.) Plaintiffs guest experienced coughing and wheezing due to the condition of the Unit. (Id.) Plaintiffs also allege having to replace furniture, food, stay in hotels, and clothing due to the carpet beetle infestation. (Id. at ¶29.) Plaintiffs further allege these damages and injuries were caused by Defendants failure to eradicate the carpet beetle issue after Plaintiffs made numerous complaints. (Id. at ¶124.) As such, Defendants have been put on adequate notice of the breach of covenant of quiet enjoyment claim Plaintiffs have brought against them.

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the seventh cause of action.

 

Eighth Cause of Action for Violation Civil Code § 1941.3

“On and after July 1, 1998, the landlord, or his or her agent, of a building intended for human habitation shall do all of the following: (1) Install and maintain an operable dead bolt lock on each main swinging entry door of a dwelling unit…(2) Install and maintain operable window security or locking devices for windows that are designed to be opened. Louvered windows, casement windows, and all windows more than 12 feet vertically or six feet horizontally from the ground, a roof, or any other platform are excluded from this subdivision…(3) Install locking mechanisms that comply with applicable fire and safety codes on the exterior doors that provide ingress or egress to common areas with access to dwelling units in multifamily developments. This paragraph does not require the installation of a door or gate where none exists on January 1, 1998.”

In their opposition, Plaintiffs have admitted the Complaint does allege allegations to support this cause of action. However, Plaintiffs’ counsel contends the cause of action cites the wrong Civil Code Section due to counsel’s error and seeks leave to amend. Plaintiffs’ counsel purports the eight cause of action should by for violation of Civil Code Section 1942.3.

Therefore, the demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days Leave to Amend as to the eighth cause of action.

 

 

Ninth Cause of Action for Violation of Civil Code § 1942.4

“A landlord of a dwelling may not demand rent, collect rent, issue a notice of a rent increase, or issue a three-day notice to pay rent or quit pursuant to subdivision (2) of Section 1161 of the Code of Civil Procedure, if all of the following conditions exist prior to the landlord’s demand or notice: (1) The dwelling substantially lacks any of the affirmative standard characteristics listed in Section 1941.1 or violates Section 17920.10 of the Health and Safety Code, or is deemed and declared substandard as set forth in Section 17920.3 of the Health and Safety Code because conditions listed in that section exist to an extent that endangers the life, limb, health, property, safety, or welfare of the public or the occupants of the dwelling.” (Cal. Civ. Code, § 1942.4(a)(1).)

Here, the Complaint does state sufficient facts to support the ninth cause of action for violation of Civil Code Section 1942.4. As discussed above, Plaintiffs allege they were subjected to substandard living conditions in Unit #206 due to the infestation of carpet beetles as of the date of the Complaint. (Compl., ¶¶16-29, 72, 136.) Plaintiffs also allege the pest control services report found evidence of carpet beetles and spiders. (Id.) Plaintiffs further allege she sent photographs and medical documentation concerning the carpet beetles to Defendants. (Id.) Plaintiffs further allege Defendants were sent two notices from the Health Department to remove the carpet because of the infestation. (Id.) Plaintiffs also allege despite these reports and notices, Defendants failed to eradicate the carpet beetle issue and continued to demand and collect rent payment. (Id. at ¶ 144.) As such, Defendants have been put on adequate notice of this cause of action Plaintiffs have brought against them .

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the ninth cause of action.

 

Eleventh Cause of Action for Private Nuisance

To establish a cause of action for private nuisance, “First, the plaintiff must prove an interference with his use and enjoyment of its property. Second, the invasion of the plaintiff's interest in the use and enjoyment of the land must be substantial, i.e., it caused the plaintiff to suffer substantial actual damage. Third, the interference with the protected interest must not only be substantial, it must also be unreasonable, i.e., it must be of such a nature, duration, or amount as to constitute unreasonable interference with the use and enjoyment of the land.” (Today's IV, Inc. v. Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transportation Authority (2022) 83 Cal.App.5th 1137, 1176.)

Here, the Complaint does state sufficient facts to support the private nuisance cause of action. As explained above, Plaintiffs allege the carpet beetle issue caused them and their guests respiratory problems and severe allergies resulting in Plaintiffs reliance on breathing machines. (Compl., ¶¶24, 155.) Plaintiffs also allege their guests experienced coughing and wheezing due to the condition of the Unit when they would come to visit. (Id.) Plaintiffs also allege having to replace furniture, food, stay in hotels, and clothing due to the carpet beetle infestation due to Defendants failure to eradicate the carpet beetle issue for more than six months after Plaintiffs made numerous complaints. (Id. at ¶¶29, 159.) On the face of the Complaint, a jury could reasonably infer that Defendants’ conduct was unreasonable in duration and nature. As such, Defendants have been put on adequate notice of the private nuisance claim Plaintiffs have brought against them.

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the eleventh cause of action.

 

Twelfth Cause of Action for Public Nuisance

“[I]n determining whether a particular activity constitutes a public nuisance, a court considers three elements: (1) the proscribed act, (2) whether the result of the act interferes with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property, and (3) whether the act affects a sufficient number of persons.” (People v. McDonald (2006) 137 Cal.App.4th 521, 535.)

Here, the Complaint fails to state sufficient facts to support the public nuisance claim. Although Plaintiffs allege that their guests would experience coughing and wheezing when they visited Plaintiffs’ unit, there are no facts alleging the guests were also bitten by the carpet beetle. Similarly, there are no facts alleging any other tenants experienced the same issue alleged in the Complaint. Nonetheless, the pleading standard is not high and Plaintiffs have not shown they are incapable of amending the Complaint.

Therefore, the demurrer is SUSTAINED with 20 days Leave to Amend as to the twelfth cause of action.

 

Thirteenth Cause of Action for Breach of Contract

“Establishing that claim requires a showing of “(1) the existence of the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to the plaintiff.” (D’Arrigo Bros. of California v. United Farmworkers of America (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 790, 800.) “Further, the complaint must indicate on its face whether the contract is written, oral, or implied by conduct.” (Otworth v. Southern Pac. Transportation Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452, 458–459.)

Here, the Complaint does state sufficient facts to support the breach of contract cause of action. Plaintiffs allege they entered into a written contract for the lease of the Unit at Defendants’ subject property, specifically Unit #206. (Compl., ¶¶73, 167-168.) Plaintiffs performed in accordance with the contract terms including payment of the lease price for the unit. (Id. at ¶¶169-170.) Defendants’ breached the lease agreement by failing to provide a habitable unit and their breach caused Plaintiffs direct and proximate damages. (Id. at ¶¶171-172.) As such, Defendants have been put on notice of the breach of contract claim Plaintiffs have brought against them.

Therefore, the demurrer is OVERRULED as to the thirteenth cause of action.

 

4. Motion to Strike

a. Legal Standard

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 435(b)(1).)¿The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436;¿Stafford v. Shultz¿(1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

 

 

b. Discussion

            Barker Management moves to strike paragraphs 46, 64, 71, 93, 109, 129, 135, 149 of the Complaint and the prayer for relief paragraphs 9 and 13 at pp. 30. The motion is made on the grounds that the Complaint fails to state sufficient facts to support a claim for punitive damages and attorneys’ fees.

            Punitive Damages

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the¿defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.) The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Id.) A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged, if proven, would not support a finding that the defendant acted with malice, fraud or oppression. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California (2010) 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63.)¿¿¿

¿“Malice” is defined in section 3294(c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (See, e.g., Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.)¿¿“Fraud” is defined in section 3294(c)(3) as “an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.”¿

To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.) Rather, a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he [or she] knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.” (Id. at 90). Further, the allegations must be sufficient for a reasonable jury to conclude that Defendant’s conduct was “despicable” defined as “base, vile or contemptible.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725.)¿

As discussed above, the Complaint states sufficient facts to support the first, third, fourth, fifth, seventh, and tenth causes of action, which relate to the cited paragraphs for punitive damages. Plaintiffs allege Defendants were aware of the carpet beetle infestation through Plaintiffs’ own reports, photographs, pest control services report, medical documentation, and two notices from the Health Department. (Compl., ¶¶16-29.) Plaintiffs further alleged as of the date of the Complaint the carpet beetle infestation had not be abated. (Id.) Plaintiffs also alleged they suffered bites from the carpet beetles and respiratory issues resulting in the prescription of topical creams and reliance on a breathing machine. (Id.) A reasonable jury could find that Defendants’ conduct constitutes a willful and conscious disregard for the rights of Plaintiffs.

Therefore, the motion is DENIED on the punitive damages ground.

 

            Attorneys’ Fees

Pursuant to Civil Code Section 1942.4(b)(2), “The prevailing party shall be entitled to recovery of reasonable attorney’s fees and costs of the suit in an amount fixed by the court.” (Cal. Civ. Code, § 1942.4(b)(2).)

As mentioned above, the Complaint alleges sufficient facts to support the ninth cause of action for violation of Civil Code Section 1942.4. As such, Plaintiffs would be entitled to reasonable attorneys’ fees is they ultimately prevail on this claim at trial. Therefore, the motion is DENIED on the attorneys’ fees  ground.
