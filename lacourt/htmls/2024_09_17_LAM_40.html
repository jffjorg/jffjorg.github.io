DEPARTMENT 40 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 40 - JUDGE ANNE RICHARDSON - LAW AND MOTION RULINGS
The Court issues tentative rulings on certain motions.The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) email Dept 40 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (smcdept40@lacourt.org) with a copy to the other party(ies) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no email is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. 



Case Number: 21STCV13188    Hearing Date: September 17, 2024    Dept: 40

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Department 40

 

MARICRUZ SANTIAGO,

                        Plaintiff,

            v.

SUBARU OF AMERICA, INC.; SUBARU OF GLENDALE; and DOES 1 through 10, inclusive,

                        Defendants.

 

 Case No.:          21STCV13188

 Hearing Date:   September 17, 2024

 Trial Date:        n/a

 [TENTATIVE] RULING RE:

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees, Costs and Expenses

 

I. Background

This is a lemon law action arising from Plaintiff Maricruz Santiago (Santiago) purchasing a 2015 Subaru Forester, which was manufactured and distributed by Defendant Subaru of America, Inc. (Subaru).

On January 26, 2024, Santiago accepted Subaru’s Code of Civil Procedure section 998 Offer of Compromise (998 Offer) in the amount of $80,000 plus Santiago’s costs, expenses and attorney fees. (Baker Decl., ¶ 91, Ex. 4.)

On April 11, 2024, Santiago filed the instant motion for attorney’s fees.

On July 9, 2024, Subaru opposed the motion.

On July 15, 2024, Santiago replied.

 

II. Motion

A. Requests for Judicial Notice

Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of a series of rulings on fee motions in other Song-Beverly cases.

The Court may take judicial notice of records of any court of record of the United States. (Evid. Code § 452(d)(2).) The request for judicial notice is GRANTED to the extent of their existence. However, the Court does not take judicial notice of the truth of the matters within the documents. (See C.R. Tenet Healthcare Corp. (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1094, 110.) 

B. Evidentiary Objections

Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Jacqueline Bruce Chinery are SUSTAINED as to Nos. 1, 2 and 3.

C. Legal Standard

Attorneys’ fees are allowed as costs when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc, § 1033.5, subd. (a)(10)(B).)  In a lemon law action, costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees, may be recovered by a prevailing buyer under the Song-Beverly Act. (See Civ. Code, § 1794 subd. (d).) 

Section 1794 provides: “If the buyer prevails in an action under this section, the buyer shall be allowed by the court to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code, § 1794.) 

Thus, the statute includes a “reasonable attorney’s fees” standard.  The attorney bears the burden of proof as to “reasonableness” of any fee claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1033.5 subd. (c)(5).) This burden requires competent evidence as to the nature and value of the services rendered. (Martino v. Denevi (1986) 182 Cal.App.3d 553, 559.) “Testimony of an attorney as to the number of hours worked on a particular case is sufficient evidence to support an award of attorney fees, even in the absence of detailed time records.” (Ibid.) 

A plaintiff’s verified billing invoices are prima facie evidence that the costs, expenses, and services listed were necessarily incurred. (See Hadley v. Krepel (1985) 167 Cal.App.3d 677, 682.) “In challenging attorney fees as excessive because too many hours of work are claimed, it is the burden of the challenging party to point to the specific items challenged, with a sufficient argument and citations to the evidence. General arguments that fees claimed are excessive, duplicative, or unrelated do not suffice.” (Lunada Biomedical v. Nunez (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 459, 488, quoting Premier Med. Mgmt. Sys., Inc. v. California Ins. Guarantee Ass’n (2008) 163 Cal.App.4th 550, 564.) The Court has discretion to reduce fees that result from inefficient or duplicative use of time. (Horsford v. Bd. of Trustees of California State Univ. (2005) 132 Cal. App. 4th 359, 395.) 

In determining a reasonable attorney fee, the trial court begins with the lodestar, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate. (Warren v. Kia Motors America, Inc. (2018) 30 Cal.App.5th 24, 36.) The lodestar may then be adjusted based on factors specific to the case in order to fix the fee at the fair market value of the legal services provided. (Ibid.) These facts include (1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, (2) the skill displayed in presenting them, (3) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys, (4) the contingent nature of the fee award. (Ibid.) 

D. Analysis

Santiago moves for an award of attorney fees, costs, and expenses in the amount of $189,690.57, consisting of: (1) $127,619.00 in attorney fees for Strategic Legal Practices, APC (SLP); (2) a 1.35 multiplier enhancement on the attorney fees ($44,666.65); (3) $13,904.92 in costs and expenses for SLP; and (4) an additional $3,500.00 for Santiago’s counsel to review Subaru’s Opposition, draft the Reply, and attend the hearing on this Motion. (Notice of Motion, p. i; Civ. Code §§ 1794 subd. (d), 998.)

Subaru argues that the Santiago’s counsel padded their fees, spent excessive amounts of time performing tasks and billed excessive amounts for documents already drafted in other cases. (Opp. p. 11.) Subaru thus requests that Santiago’s attorney’s fees be denied in their entirety, or in the alternative, be reduced to $14,659.00, or a lesser amount. (Opp. p. 11.)

1.     Prevailing Party

Santiago seeks an award of attorney’s fees pursuant to a settlement agreement reached by the parties. On January 26, 2024, Santiago accepted Subaru’s 998 Offer for $80,000.00 plus costs, expenses and attorney’s fees pursuant to Civ. Code section 1794 subd. (d). (Baker Decl. ¶ 91; Ex. 4.)

It is undisputed that Santiago is the prevailing party in this action and entitled to a reasonable amount in attorney fees.

2.     Reasonableness of Hourly Rates

“The reasonable hourly rate is that prevailing in the community for similar work.” (PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095.) “The experienced trial judge is the best judge of the value of professional services rendered in [her] court.” (Ibid.)

Santiago seeks to recover attorney fees for 19 attorneys and one law clerk (Canton) who worked on the case:

(1) Mani Arabi at $495/hr, (2) Angel Baker at $595/hr, (3) Neal Butala at $460/hr, (4) Christopher Campbell at $495/hr, (5) Eve Canton at $295/hr, (6) Jason Clark at $550/hr, (7) Tionna Carvalho at $570/hr in 2023 and then $595/hr in 2024, (8) Mark Gibson at $475/hr, (9) Jared Kaye at $395/hr, (10) Regina Liou at $475/hr, (11) David Lunn at $620/hr, (12) Ian McCallister at $595/hr, (13) Bryan Miller at $595/hr, (14) David Rodriguez at $595/hr, (15) Ezra Ryu at $385/hr, (16) Nino Sanaia at $425/hr, (17) Artin Sodaify at $410/hr, (18) Rosy Stoliker at $395/hr, (19) Rabiya Trimizi at $375/hr in 2023 and then $395/hr, in 2024, and (20) Oliver Tomas at $610/hr. (Shahian Decl. ¶¶ 44-83, Ex. 18.)

Santiago’s counsel demonstrates that the attorney’s rates are commonly accepted within their community of practice in the relevant area of law. (Shahian Decl.  ¶¶ 44-83.)

Subaru’s counsel argues that the billing rates are unreasonable considering the non-complex nature of the case. (Opp. pp. 4-5.) In line with Mikhaeilpoor v. BMW of North America, LLC (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 240, Subaru argues that all rates should be reduced to $350/hr. (Opp. p. 5.)

Here, the attorney’s hourly rates varied from $375/hr to $610/hr. (Shahian Decl. ¶¶ 44-83, Ex. 18.) These rates are reasonable within the Los Angeles community for work of this nature. (Shahian Decl. ¶¶ 44-83.)

3.     Reasonableness of Hours Incurred

“A trial court assessing attorney fees begins with a touchstone or lodestar figure, based on the ‘careful compilation of the time spent and reasonable hourly compensation of each attorney ... involved in the presentation of the case.” (Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1321.) “The reasonableness of attorney fees is within the discretion of the trial court, to be determined from a consideration of such factors as the nature of the litigation, the complexity of the issues, the experience and expertise of counsel and the amount of time involved. The court may also consider whether the amount requested is based upon unnecessary or duplicative work.” (Wilkerson v. Sullivan (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

Santiago seeks to recover attorney’s fees for 261.15 hours litigating this matter. (Mot. p. 10.) Santiago has submitted billing records. (Shahian Decl., Ex. 18.)

            Subaru objects to several of Santiago’s requested fees on the grounds that Santiago’s counsel billed excessive hours for routine tasks and/or billed unnecessary tasks (Opp. pp. 5-10.)

            First, Subaru objects to 2.3 hours ($1,092.50) billed on April 5, 2021, to draft Santiago’s Complaint on the grounds that it is the standard complaint filed in every case. (Opp. p. 5.)

            Second, Subaru objects to 4.9 hours ($2,254.00) billed on July 20, 2021, to draft responses and document production for written discovery on the grounds that the responses are templated. (Opp. p. 5.)

Third, Subaru objects to 31.1 hours ($13,316.50) billed between August 15, 2022, August 16, 2022, August 19, 2022, January 10, 2023, and March 22, 2023, to litigate various motions to compel discovery responses all of which the Court denied in their entirety as moot. (Opp. p. 6.)

Fourth, Subaru objects to 8.4 hours ($4,470.00) billed between July 16, 2021, July 17, 2021, July 20, 2021, September 1, 2021, November 13, 2023, December 5, 2023, December 7, 2023, December 11, 2023, and January 26, 2024, to communicate with their client. Subaru also objects to 11.2 hours ($10,494.00) block billed for tasks that also included communicating with the client. Subaru objects to these entries on the grounds that they are unnecessary and excessive. (Opp. p. 6.)

Fifth, Subaru objects to 4.6 hours ($2,737.00) billed on July 12, 2023, to draft and finalize motions in limine on the grounds that the motions are the templated. (Opp. p. 6.)

Sixth, Subaru objects to various entries on the grounds that they are duplicative. Subaru objects to 6.2 hours ($3,689.00) billed on July 24, 2023, 4.9 hours ($2,915.50) billed on November 29, 2023, 2.1 hours ($1,249.50) billed on November 30, 2023, and 2.8 hours ($1,666.00) and 2.4 hours ($1,188.00) billed on December 5, 2023, all to draft and finalize oppositions to motions in limine. (Opp. pp. 6-7.) Subaru also objects to 3.5 hours ($1,662.50) billed on August 1 and 2, 2023, 1.2 hours ($744.00) billed on August 2, 2023, and 11.4 hours ($4,674.00) billed November 20 and 21, 2023, all to draft and revise joint trial documents. (Opp. p. 7.)

Seventh, Subaru objects to 3.9 hours ($2,320.50) billed on November 30, 2023, December 2 and 4, 2023 to analyze trial documents and prepare for trial, and to 8.5 hours ($5,057.50) billed on December 9, 2023 to prepare to argue motions in limine and depositions by an attorney that had not worked on the matter during the previous two years. (Opp. p. 7.)

Eighth, Subaru objects to 5.3 hours ($2,517.50) billed between July 28, 2023, and July 31, 2023, to draft a notice to appear at trial on the grounds that the notice is templated. (Opp. p.7.)

Ninth, Subaru objects to 2.4 hours ($948.00) billed on August 6, 2023 and 1.1 hours ($654.50) billed on November 30, 2023 to analyze discovery on the grounds that time is excessive. (Opp. p. 8.)

Tenth, Subaru objects to multiple entries on the grounds that they are block billed. (Opp. pp. 8-9.)

Finally, Subaru objects to 7.5 hours ($4,462.50) in connection with the present motion and (Opp. p. 10.)

The trial court has an obligation to award only those attorneys’ fees that are reasonable.¿ See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1096 (PLCM Group); see also Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.  “When a ‘voluminous fee application’ is made, the court may … ‘make across-the-board percentage cuts either in the number of hours claimed or in the final lodestar figure.’” (Kerkeles v. City of San Jose (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 88, 102.)

Here, the number of hours spent on this matter are excessive. Santiago seeks to recover 261.15 hours billed by 19 different attorneys. 19 attorneys certainly suggest overstaffing. This was a non-complex lemon law case and Santiago’s lawyers are well versed in this field. This is demonstrated by the dozens of similar lemon law cases Santiago references involving Strategic Law Partners, many of which were handled by the same attorneys from Strategic Law Partners who worked on this case. (See Shahian Decl. ¶¶ 44-83, Exs. 1-17.) Not all of the time requested is reasonable for routine lemon law tasks. (See, e.g. Shahian Decl. Ex. 18 [4.9 hours to draft discovery responses, 31.1. hours to draft moot motions to compel discovery, 18.4 hours to draft oppositions to motions in limine, 5.3 hours to draft a notice to appear at trial.] Santiago’s counsel also appears to have overbilled for work on the instant motion (7.5 hours) and anticipated cost of reviewing any opposition and drafting a reply ($3,500.00). (See Shahian Decl. Ex 18) Moreover, block billing can exacerbate the vagueness of an attorney’s fees request and support a court’s finding that time entries were inflated and non-compensable Christian Research Institute v. Alnor (2008) 165 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1325-1326.)

Thus, Santiago’s billing should be reduced. Upon review and consideration of Santiago’s submitted billing statements, the Court finds that a 35% reduction is appropriate. Total attorney’s fees, up through and including counsel’s work on the instant motion and time to reply and attend the hearing thereon, shall thus be $85,227.35. ($127,619.00 + $3,500 = $131,119.00; $131,119.00 x 35% = $45,891.65; $131,119.00 - $45,891.65 = $85,227.35)

4.     Multiplier

While the lodestar reflects the basic fee for comparable legal services in the community, it may be adjusted based on various factors, including “(1) the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill displayed in presenting them; (2) the extent to which the nature of the litigation precluded other employment by the attorneys; (3) the contingent nature of the fee award” and (4) the success achieved. (Serrano v. Priest (1977) 20 Cal.3d 25, 49.)

Santiago additionally requests a lodestar multiplier enhancement of 1.35 based on counsel obtaining an excellent outcome, the risks posed by the litigation, and the delay of payment. (Mot. pp. 13-14.)

Here, a lodestar multiplier is not appropriate. This is a straightforward lemon law case with no noted complicating factors. Nothing before the Court indicates that the case precluded other employment by Santiago’s counsel. Any contingency risk factor is already accounted for in the hourly rates.

Thus, the request for a lodestar multiplier is denied.

5.     Costs

Santiago requests a total of $13,904.92 in costs and expenses for Strategic Legal Partners. (Mot. p. i; Shahian Decl. Ex. 18.) Subaru does not object to the costs.

Thus, Santiago’s request for $13,904.92 in costs is granted. 

III. Conclusion

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees, Costs and Expenses is GRANTED in part. The Court awards $85,227.35 in attorney’s fees and $13,904.92 in costs and expenses to Strategic Legal Partners for a total of $99,132.27 in costs and fees.

Defendant Brink’s Global Services, Inc. Demurrer to Plaintiff Stephen Newland’s Third Amended Complaint

Plaintiff’s Motion for an Order Imposing Issue or Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions against Defendant Brink’s Global Services, Inc.

 

 

I. Background

A. Pleadings

Plaintiff Stephen Newland (Newland) sues Defendants Tyler Gallagher, Leah Donoso, Christian Howard, Regal Assets, LLC (Regal Assets), Doe 1 Brink’s Incorporated (Brink’s) and Does 2-20 pursuant to a June 26, 2024 Third Amended Complaint (TAC) alleging claims of: (1) Fraud (Intentional or Reckless Misrepresentation), (2) Fraud (False Promises), (3) Fraud (Concealment), (4) Conversion, (5) Breach of Fiduciary Duty, and (6) Breach of Contract (7) Breach of Contract (Third Party Beneficiary) against all Defendants; and (8) Breach of Deposit [Bailment] against Brink’s and Does 2-10.

The claims arise from allegations that between August 24 and 25, 2018, Newland was enticed to and did purchase silver and gold coins from Regal Assets—a suspended California LLC that operated as the alter ego of Defendants Gallagher, Donoso, and Howard, alleged as co-conspirators, as well as agents of Regal Assets. Through Defendant Donoso, Regal Assets represented that it would secure Newland’s coins with Brink’s pursuant to a Precious Metals Storage Agreement (the Agreement) through which Brink’s would store precious metals purchased from Regal Assets. Newland alleges having purchased the coins based on Howard’s representations as to the coins’ characteristics and based on the alleged agreement with Brink’s. Newland paid $15,007.67 for the coins, which were allegedly stored with Brink’s, but for which Regal Assets persistently failed to turn over account verification reports, which Newland requested on various occasions. Later, Newland requested that the coins be transferred to him from Brink’s, making his requests directly to Brink’s and to Regal Assets’ agents Donoso and Howard. In October 2022, Brink’s finally responded to Plaintiff Newland, asserting that it had transferred the silver and gold coins to “Defendants ‘several months ago.’” Plaintiff Newland states that he never received the coins from Defendants Regal Assets, Gallagher, Donoso or Howard.

B. Motions Before the Court

On July 25, 2024, Brinks demurred to the TAC’s seventh and eighth causes of action.

On September 4, 2024, Newland filed an opposition to the demurrer.

On September 10, 2024, Brinks replied.

On July 26, 2024, Newland filed a motion for an order imposing issue or terminating sanctions and monetary sanctions against Brink’s Global Services, Inc.

On September 4, 2024, Brinks filed an opposition to the motion for sanctions.

On September 10, 2024, Newland replied.

Brink’s demurrer and Newland’s motion for sanctions are now before the Court.

 

II. Demurrer

A.    Meet and Confer

The Court notes that Defendant has met the meet and confer requirement pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41. (Lagas Decl., ¶¶ 4-5.)

B.    Legal Standard

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747; see Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) This device can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) “To survive a [general] demurrer, the complaint need only allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action; each evidentiary fact that might eventually form part of the plaintiff’s proof need not be alleged.” (C.A. v. William S. Hart Union High School Dist. (2012) 53 Cal.4th 861, 872.)

In testing the sufficiency of the cause of action, the demurrer admits the truth of all material facts properly pleaded. (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital Dist. (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 966-67.) A demurrer, however, “does not admit contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Daar v. Yellow Cab Co. (1967) 67 Cal.2d 695, 713.) When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228, disapproved on other grounds, Jones v. Lodge at Torrey Pines Partnership (2008) 42 Cal.4th 1158, 1162.) The face of the complaint includes exhibits attached to the complaint. (Frantz v. Blackwell (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 91, 94.) If facts appearing in the exhibits contradict those alleged, the facts in the exhibits take precedence. (Holland v. Morse Diesel Intern., Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1443, 1447, superseded by statute on other grounds as stated in White v. Cridlebaugh (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 506, 521.)

C.    Analysis

1.     Seventh Cause of Action, Breach of Contract (Third Party Beneficiary): SUSTAINED

“A contract is a voluntary and lawful agreement, by competent parties, for a good consideration, to do or not to do a specified thing.” (Robinson v. Magee (1858) 9 Cal. 81, 83.) “To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant’s breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.” (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.) Implicit in the element of damage is that the defendant’s breach caused the plaintiff’s damage.” (Troyk v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1352, italics omitted.)

In their demurrer, Brink’s argues that Newland’s breach of contract claim fails because the SAC does not allege facts sufficient to show that Brink’s breached the Agreement with Regal Assets or that Newland was a third-party beneficiary to the Agreement.

In opposition, Newland argues that Code of Civil Procedure section 2017.210 entitles him to obtain discovery of Brink’s insurance policy. He argues that the insurance policy will be probative on whether Newland is a third-party beneficiary under the Agreement. The opposition does not otherwise address how Brink’s breached the Agreement.

Here, Newland alleges that Brink’s permitted the removal of his coins from the vault without requiring Regal Assets to adhere to all the terms of the Precious Metals Storage Agreement. He alleges that his losses were caused by Brink’s failure to abide by the terms of the Agreement and failure to hold Regal Assets to its duties under the Agreement. Newland alleges that he is a third-party beneficiary under the Agreement. However, accepting all the factual assertions in the TAC, including the exhibits, as true, Newland has failed to allege facts sufficient to state a cause of action for breach of contract. “Implicit in the element of damage is that the defendant’s breach caused the plaintiff’s damage.” (Troyk v. Farmers Group, Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1305, 1352, italics omitted.)

Newland points to specific sections of the Agreement that he alleges Brink’s or Regal Assets did not adhere to. (TAC pp. 20-23, Ex. D) Upon review of the unredacted sections, and accepting the assertions as true, the alleged breaches did not cause Newland’s loss. The sections of the Agreement that Newland references are not related to Brink’s obligations when releasing the coins from its vault. The sections reference various responsibilities Brink’s had while the coins were in Brink's possession. However, the TAC does not allege that Newland’s damages occurred while the coins were in Brink’s possession. Allegations that Brink’s breached the Agreement regarding its obligations while the coins were in its possession are immaterial to a loss that occurred after the coins were no longer in Brink’s possession.

Additionally, because the allegations do not support a cause of action for breach of contract, the Court need not address the question of whether Newland is a third-party beneficiary to the contract.  

Accordingly, Brink’s demurrer is SUSTAINED.

2.     Eighth Cause of Action, Breach of Deposit [Bailment]: SUSTAINED

A bailment is called a deposit within the Civil Code. It is defined as “[a] voluntary deposit […] made by one giving to another, with his consent, the possession of personal property to keep for the benefit of the former, or of a third party.” (Civ. Code, § 1814.) Under California law, “a bailment for the benefit of both parties … is a bailment for hire, and imposes on the bailee the duty to use ordinary care with respect to the bailed property.” (Gerbert v. Yank (1985) 172 Cal.App.3d 544, 551.) “A bailment ‘is made by one giving to another, with his consent, the possession of personal property to keep for the benefit of the former, or of a third party.’” (McKell v. Washington (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 1457, 1491, citing Civ. Code, § 1814; see Gebert v. Yank, supra, at p. 551; see, e.g., McKell v. Washington, supra, at p. 1491 [“Plaintiffs allege[d] … [a] breach of bailment cause of action [when they alleged] that they ‘delivered to Washington Mutual, and Washington Mutual agreed to hold for the benefit of plaintiffs, money to pay the cost of underwriting, tax services and wire transfer services,” which Washington Mutual breached when it “paid only a portion of the money to the third party vendors and kept a portion for itself”].)

The TAC alleges that Brink’s breached its duties as a bailee by “permitting Defendants or unknown third parties to remove Newland’s coins from Brink’s SLC Vault.” (TAC ¶ 127.) However, the allegations in the TAC and the terms of the Agreement attached to the TAC do not show how Brink’s release of the coins to Regal Assets was improper. Regal Assets is named as the “Customer” in the Agreement. (TAC, Ex. D., Preamble) The Customer is permitted to withdraw metals from the account with Brink’s. (TAC, Ex. D., § 3 [setting out the process by which the Customer may withdraw metals from the account with Brink’s].) Nowhere in the contractual terms does the Agreement impose an obligation on Brink’s to consult with third parties who have an interest in the stored metals prior to allowing Regal Assets to withdraw those metals pursuant to section 3 of the Agreement. The Agreement only provides that the metals would be released to the Customer—Regal Assets—upon following the proper procedure, and here, the TAC alleges that Brink’s released the coins to Regal Assets. (TAC, ¶ 117.) Thus, the TAC fails to allege facts sufficient to show that Brink’s failed to act within the appropriate standard of care for a bailee.

Accordingly, Brink’s demurrer is SUSTAINED.

 

III. Motion for an Order Imposing Issue or Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions: DENIED

A.    Requests for Judicial Notice

The Court declines Plaintiff’s request to take judicial notice of the excerpts from Brink’s Company’s 10-K Forms because they are not relevant to the discovery abuses alleged in this motion. (Evid. Code § 450.) (Lockley v. Law Office of Cantrell, Green, Pekich, Cruz & McCort (2001) 91 Cal.App.4th 875, 882 [“Judicial notice is the recognition and acceptance by the court, for use by the trier of fact or by the court, of the existence of a matter of law or fact that is relevant to an issue in the action without requiring formal proof of the matter.”].)  

B.    Evidentiary Objections

1.     Defendant Brink’s Objections to Plaintiff Newland’s Declaration

Not ruled on as not material to the disposition of this motion.

2.     Defendant Brink’s Objections to Plaintiff Newland’s Proposed Findings of Fact

Not ruled on as not material to the disposition of this motion.

C.    Legal Standard

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 permits California courts to impose issue or evidence sanctions for discovery misuses, which are defined by section 2023.010 of the Code of Civil Procedure. (See Peat, Marwick, Mitchell & Co. v. Superior Court (1988) 200 Cal.App.3d 272, 288 [The court’s inherent power to curb abuses and promote fair process extends to the preclusion of evidence].) 

Terminating sanctions are drastic sanctions that should be imposed sparingly and only when it is clear that the party to be sanctioned has left no viable alternatives. (See Dept. of Forestry & Fire. Prot. v. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 191, disapproved on other grounds in Presbyterian Camp & Conf. Ctrs, Inc. v. Superior Court (2021) 12 Cal.5th 493; Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Soc’y (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 566, 604.) The court should consider the totality of the circumstances before ordering terminating sanctions, including (1) whether the conduct of the party was willful, (2) the detriment to the party propounding discovery, and (3) the number of formal and informal attempt to obtain the discovery. (Los Defensores, Inc. v. Gomez (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 377, 390.)

D.    Analysis

Newland moves for an order imposing issue sanctions, or in the alternative, terminating sanctions and for an order awarding monetary sanctions in the amount of $12,757.50 against Brink’s and Christensen Hsu & Sipes, LLP, based on discovery abuses related to the Court’s May 13, 2024 order regarding Category Nos. 9 and 11 in Newland’s Request for Production, Set One (RFP).

On June 12, 2024, Brink’s submitted First Amended Responses to the RFP’s. (Mot. Ex. G.) On August 9, 2024, Brink’s submitted Second Amended Responses to the RFP’s. (Opp. Ex. 3.) Upon review, the amended responses comply with the May 13, 2024 Court Order. (See, Mot. Ex. G, Opp. Ex. 3 and Minute Order, May 13, 2024.)

            In the motion, Newland again argues that the insurance documents he seeks are relevant to showing that Newland is a third-party beneficiary under the Precious Metals Storage Agreement. As discussed above, because Newland has failed to state a breach of contract cause of action against Brink’s, whether Newland is a beneficiary to the contract is irrelevant. Thus, there has been no misuse of the discovery process warranting issue, termination or monetary sanctions.

            Accordingly, Newland’s motion for sanctions is DENIED.

IV. Conclusion

The Defendant Brink’s Global Services, Inc.’s Demurrer to Plaintiff Stephen Newland’s Third Amended Complaint is SUSTAINED, without leave to amend.

The Plaintiff Stephen Newland’s Motion for an Order Imposing Issue or Terminating Sanctions and Monetary Sanctions against Defendant Brink’s Global Services, Inc. is DENIED.
