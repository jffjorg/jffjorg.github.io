DEPARTMENT 51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

1. If you wish to submit on the tentative ruling, please email the clerk at SMCdept51@lacourt.org (and “cc” all other parties in the same email) and notify all other parties in advance that you will not be appearing at the hearing.  Include the word "SUBMISSION" in all caps in the subject line and include your name, contact information, the case number, and the party you represent in the body of the email. If you do not have access to the internet, you may call the clerk at (213) 633-0351.

 

If you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear at the hearing, the opposing party may nevertheless appear and argue the motion, and the Court may decide not to adopt the tentative ruling. Please note that the tentative ruling is not an invitation, nor an opportunity, to file any further documents relative to the hearing in question which are not authorized by statute or Rule of Court.

 

2. For any motion where no parties submit to the tentative ruling in advance, and no parties appear at the motion hearing, the Court may elect to either adopt the tentative ruling or take the motion off calendar, in its discretion.

3. DO NOT USE THE ABOVE EMAIL FOR ANY PURPOSE OTHER THAN TO SUBMIT TO A TENTATIVE RULING.  The Court will not read or respond to emails sent to this address for any other purpose.

 




Case Number: 22STCV39334    Hearing Date: November 5, 2024    Dept: 51

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Upinder S. Kalra, Department 51

 

HEARING DATE:   November 5, 2024                                         

 

CASE NAME:           Andrew Stafford, et al. v. Christine Fadly

 

CASE NO.:                22STCV39334

 

MOTION TO COMPEL DEPOSITIONS OF PLAINTIFFS

 

MOVING PARTY:  Defendant/Cross-Complainant Christine Fadly

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): None as of October 28, 2024.

 

REQUESTED RELIEF:

 

1.      An Order compelling the depositions of each Plaintiff;

2.      An Order awarding $1,900 in sanctions.

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

1.      Motion to Compel Depositions of Plaintiffs is GRANTED;

2.      Plaintiffs are ORDERED to appear for deposition on November 15, 2024;

3.      Plaintiffs are ORDERED to pay $1,310.00 in sanctions to Defendant counsel by November 15, 2024.

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

 

On December 19, 2022, Plaintiffs Andrew Stafford and Alexa Greger (Plaintiffs) filed a Complaint against Defendant Christine Fadley (Defendant) with nine causes of action for: (1) Private Nuisance, (2) Intentional Influence to Vacate, (3) Termination of Estate, Civil Code § 789.3 (4) Business and Professions Code §§ 17200, (5) Slander/Defamation, (6) Breach of Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment, (7) Breach of Implied Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing, (8) Violation of California Civil Code § 1946.2, and (9) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress. Plaintiffs allege that they were tenants of the Subject Property, which was owned and/or managed by Defendant. During Plaintiffs time at the Subject Property, Defendant locked the gate to prevent Plaintiffs from entering. Additionally, Defendant turned off electrical power and glued the locks shut to further prevent Plaintiffs from entering the Subject Property. Defendant also attempted to increase Plaintiffs’ rent by 37%, increasing it from $2,700 to $2,700, or stating that Plaintiffs have to vacate. 

 

On May 25, 2023, the court SUSTAINED Defendant’s Demurrer as to the 5th Cause of Action, with leave to amend, and OVERRULED as to the remaining causes of action. The court also DENIED Defendant’s Motion to Strike. 

 

On June 12, 2023, Plaintiffs filed a First Amended Complaint (FAC) with the same causes of action as the Complaint alleged in the caption, however, the Eighth Cause of Action states it is for Conversion of Private Property.  

 

On November 13, 2023, the court OVERRULED Defendant’s demurrer to the FAC and DENIED Defendant’s Motion to Strike. 

 

On December 5, 2023, Defendant filed an Answer. LEGAL STANDARD:

 

Where a would-be deponent fails to appear, proceed with, or produce documents for deposition in a duly noticed deposition notice, the noticing party may move for an order to compel said deponent’s attendance, testimony, and/or production for inspection of any document described in the deposition notice. (Code Civ. Proc., (CCP) §§ 2025.230, 2025.450 (a).)

  

ANALYSIS:

 

Meet and Confer

 

A motion to compel a deposition must “be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition…by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.” (CCP § 2025.450, subd. (2).) Plaintiffs argue that Defendant insufficiently met and conferred because they did not schedule an IDC with this court.

 

Here, Defendant met the meet and confer requirement. According to the Declaration of Nathanael Cade (Cade Decl.), Defendant attempted to coordinate taking Plaintiffs’ depositions with Plaintiffs’ counsel numerous times between April 3, 2024 and July 12, 2024. (Cade Decl. ¶ 3.) Defendant then attempted to meet and confer about Plaintiffs’ objections to the deposition notices Defendant did send out. (Cade Decl. ¶¶ 6, 7.) The court is not persuaded by Plaintiffs’ argument that failing to meet and confer to schedule an IDC, in this instance, is dispositive. Indeed, Plaintiffs note in their opposition that they “stated they would provide Plaintiffs’ availability for deposition by the end of [May 22, 2024]” and did not do so and that Defendants had been reaching out to Plaintiffs “but were unable to reach the Plaintiffs.” (Opp. 2:3-8.) In fact, the court found good cause not to proceed with an IDC for the instant dispute. (October 14, 2024 Minute Order.)

 

Accordingly, this requirement is met.

 

Compel Deposition[1]

 

Defendant contends that Plaintiffs have ignored numerous requests to schedule their depositions – even after stating they would provide availability by the end of May 22, 2024. Defendant further contends that Plaintiffs did not serve a valid objection to the deposition notice and, as such, Defendant incurred the cost to take a Notice of Non-Appearance. Plaintiffs do not directly address this in their opposition but seem to argue that there is no rush to take Plaintiffs’ depositions due to the August 18, 2025 trial date.

 

Here, Defendant is entitled to depose Plaintiffs. First, Defendant has demonstrated that Plaintiffs have been dilatory in setting their depositions. Second, Plaintiffs’ argument that there is no rush to take the depositions is not well taken. Plaintiffs provide no authority supporting their contention that a “late” trial date excuses their failure to provide deposition availability to Defendant. The opposite is true. “A lawyer shall not intentionally, repeatedly, recklessly or with gross negligence fail to act with reasonable diligence in representing a client.” (Rules of Prof. Conduct, rule 1.3(a).) This requires a lawyer “not [to] neglect or disregard, or unduly delay a legal matter entrusted to the lawyer.” (Id. at subd. (b).) This applies to the lawyer’s client and to the courts. (Lopez v. Larson (1979) 91 Cal.App.3d 383, 400 [discussing an attorney’s workload.])

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiffs’ depositions.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendant contends that sanctions are warranted because Plaintiffs have ignored Defendant’s repeated requests to take Plaintiffs’ deposition. Defendant requests 11 hours at $500 per hour in sanctions which includes the meet and confer time and bringing this motion. Plaintiffs argue sanctions are not warranted.

 

A court shall impose monetary sanctions if the motion to compel is granted unless the one subject to sanctions acted with substantial justification or other circumstances would make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (CCP § 2025.450, subd. (g)(1).)  Additionally, “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” (CCP § 2023.030(a).)

 

The Court awards reasonable attorneys’ fees according to the lodestar.¿ See PLCM Group v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1096; see also Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1132.¿ 

 

Here, sanctions are warranted.[2] It is abundantly clear that Plaintiffs are misusing the discovery process because it is undisputed that they have had six months to schedule their depositions with Defendant and have failed to do so.[3] (Cade Decl., Exhibit 1, Exhibit 5; Opp. 1:27-2:28.) However, applying the lodestar, Defendant’s fee request is unreasonable. First, 11 hours in bringing this straightforward motion is excessive. Second, Defendant’s request for the Certificates of Non-Appearance is unreasonable because the deposition date was unilaterally set, Plaintiffs served an objection indicating they would not attend, and counsel did have a phone call wherein Defendant knew Plaintiffs did not intend to appear on May 29, 2024 and informed Plaintiffs’ counsel that they would proceed with the non-appearances should counsel fail to provide alternate deposition availability. (Cade Decl., Exhibit 1.) Finally, the court is unaware of authority allowing a sanction award encompassing meet and confer efforts that are tangentially related to the discovery motion. As such, the court reduces the requested amount from 11 hours to 2.5 hours at counsel’s rate of $500 per hour as well as the $60 motion fee for the instant motion.

 

Accordingly, the court GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the amount of $1,310.00.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            For the foregoing reasons, the Court decides the pending motion as follows:

 

4.      Motion to Compel Depositions of Plaintiffs is GRANTED;

1.      Plaintiffs are ORDERED to appear for deposition on November 15, 2024;

2.      Plaintiffs’ counsel is  ORDERED to pay $1,310.00 in sanctions to Defendant counsel by November 15, 2024.

Moving party is to give notice.
