DEPARTMENT O LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 18VECV00128    Hearing Date: December 3, 2024    Dept: O

 

                                                 

 

 

 

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES – NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

JUANA MENDOZA; FABIAN VARGAS; AND EDMUNDO VARGAS (SUING BY HIS GUARDIAN AD LITEM, JUANA MENDOZA),

 

Plaintiffs,

vs.

NORTH HILLS WILLS APTS, INC.,

 

Defendant.

Case No.: 18VECV00128

ORDER GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO VACATE THE COURT’S JULY 5, 2024, ORDER AWARDING PLAINTIFFS ATTORNEY’S FEES AND COSTS

 

I.        INTRODUCTION

Plaintiffs Juana Mendoza, Fabian Vargas, and Jose Edmundo Vargas (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) brought this action against their former landlord, Defendant North Hills Wills Apts., Inc. (“Defendant”) alleging three causes of action: (1) Breach of the Warranty of Habitability; (2) Negligent Maintenance; and (3) Nuisance.

Following a jury trial that spanned several days, the jury returned a verdict in favor of Plaintiffs as to all three of their causes of action and awarded damages in the amount of $14,400 to Juana Mendoza, $8,100 to Fabian Vargas, and $7,500 to Jose Edmundo Vargas.

On July 5, 2024, the Court awarded Plaintiffs’ attorney’s fees (“Order”) after Defendant did not file an opposition to Plaintiff’s properly noticed motion.

Defendant now seeks to vacate to vacate the Order.  

II.      PROCEDURAL HISTORY

On October 26, 2018, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint against Defendant.

On December 6, 2018, Defendant filed its Answer to the Complaint.

On February 6, 2024, the jury trial commenced.

On February 20, 2024, the jury returned with a verdict in favor of all three Plaintiffs as to all three of their causes of action in the amounts listed above.

On May 6, 2024, the Court entered judgment in favor of Plaintiffs.

On July 5, 2024, the Court granted Plaintiffs’ Attorney’s Fees Motion in part.

On October 25, 2024, Defendant filed the instant Motion to Vacate the Court’s July 5, 2024, Order Awarding Plaintiffs Attorney’s Fees and Costs (“Motion”).

On November 15, 2024, Plaintiffs filed their Opposition.

On November 22, 2024, Defendant filed its Reply.

III.     ANALYSIS

On July 5, 2024, this Court granted Plaintiffs’ Attorney’s Fees Motion in part. Defendant did not oppose or appear at the hearing. Defendant now requests this Court vacate its Order granting Plaintiffs’ attorney’s fees due to its counsel’s mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect, pursuant to § 473(b). (See Declaration of Laura Adler (“Ms. Adler”) filed October 25, 2024 (“Adler Decl.”).) Defendant requests this relief pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b)’s mandatory relief provision or, in the alternative, under its discretionary relief provision. (See Motion, pp. 5-7.)  

Ms. Adler declares her firm’s legal department has undergone structural changes as a result of the appointment of a Receiver. (See Adler Decl., ¶ 4.) She also declares the attorney assigned to the present case left the firm at the end of August of this year. (See id.) “As explained in the Declaration of Laura Adler filed with the motion, the original failing to calendar and appear at the June 7, 2024, was compounded by the internal tumult surrounding the appointment of the Receiver and the departure of Mr. Whitaker.” (Reply, pg. 3.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue the Motion is procedurally defective as the Court’s Order must be appealed under Code of Civil Procedure § 904.1 and, even if Code of Civil Procedure § 473 applies, “there is nothing or no facts to support a basis for mistake or excusable neglect.” (Opposition, pp. 4-5.)

The Court finds Ms. Adler has met her burden of proof to demonstrate mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b). As an initial matter, while the Court’s Order can be appealed under Code of Civil Procedure § 904.1, it does not have to be appealed under § 904.1. (See Lakin v. Watkins Associated Industries (1993) 6 Cal.4th 644, 656.) Mandatory relief is also not appropriate in this instance, as mandatory relief under Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b) is limited to defaults, default judgments, and dismissals. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 473(b).) Discretionary relief is, however, appropriate. “As the California Supreme Court recently observed: The discretionary relief provision of section 473, subdivision (b) applies to any judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding. Thus, for example, the failure of counsel to meet a procedural deadline is a proper subject for section 473 relief.” (Huh v. Wang (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 1406, 1419 (internal quotations and citations omitted).) The Court recognizes Plaintiff’s counsel’s firm did not formally go into receivership until after the hearing on Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees. However, firms do not go into receivership overnight; financial and structural problems like those that lead to a receivership usually simmer until they boil over. Thus, the Court is willing to give Ms. Adler’s declaration the benefit of the doubt coming from an officer of the court that her firm’s failure “to calendar and appear at the June 7, 2024, [hearing] was compounded by the internal tumult surrounding the appointment of the Receiver and the departure of Mr. Whitaker.” (Reply, pg. 3.) This showing is sufficient for the Court to grant Defendant’s Motion pursuant to its ability to award discretionary relief under Code of Civil Procedure § 473(b).

IV.     CONCLUSION

          Based on the foregoing, the Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s Motion to Vacate its Order granting Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorney’s Fees. Defendant shall file its Opposition to Plaintiffs’ Motion for Attorney’s Fees forthwith and the Court shall reset Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorney’s Fees for hearing on January 2, 2025, at 8:30 a.m.

III.    DEMURRER

A.      Legal Standard

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint). (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)[2] It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true. (See Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.) 

“We treat the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law. We accept the factual allegations of the complaint as true and also consider matters which may be judicially noticed.” (Mitchell v. California Department of Public Health (2016) 1 Cal.App.5th 1000, 1007 (citation omitted); see also Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co. (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604 [“the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].) Allegations are to be liberally construed. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 452.) A demurrer may be brought if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10(e).) 

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.) “[T]he court gives the complaint a reasonable interpretation, and treats the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded.” (Id.) In testing the sufficiency of the complaint, the court must assume the truth of (1) the properly pleaded factual allegations; (2) facts that can be reasonably inferred from those expressly pleaded; and (3) judicially noticed matters. (See Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.) A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. (See SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) No other extrinsic evidence can be considered. (See Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) Accordingly, “[w]hether the plaintiff will be able to prove the pleaded facts is irrelevant to ruling upon the demurrer.” (Stevens v. Superior Court (1986) 180 Cal.App.3d 605, 609-10.) 

A demurrer can only be sustained when it disposes of an entire cause of action. (See Poizner v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 119; Kong v. City of Hawaiian Gardens Redev. Agency (2003) 108 Cal.App.4th 1028, 1046.) Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (See Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) However, “[i]f there is any reasonable possibility that the plaintiff can state a good cause of action, it is error to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend.” (Youngman v. Nevada Irrigation Dist. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 240, 245).  

B.      The Parties Properly Met and Conferred.

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a), emphasis added.) A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 430.41(a)(3).)

         Demurring Defendant’s counsel filed a declaration attesting to his written and telephonic meet and confer efforts. (See Declaration of Christopher James filed September 23, 2024, ¶¶ 3-8.) Demurring Defendants have fulfilled their meet and confer obligations.    

C.      Analysis 

1.              Plaintiff Fails to State a Breach of Contract Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178 (cleaned up).)

Demurring Defendants argue two contracts are relevant to Croitoru and OZ: the Parent AOAs [Amended Operating Agreements] and the Investment Agreement. (See Demurrer, pg. 5; see also FAC, ¶¶ 50-51, Exs. E-G.) Demurring Defendants argue OZ is not a party to the Parent AOAs and that Plaintiff fails to allege Croitoru breached any part of the Parent AOAs, as no “provision obligated Croitoru ‘to replace FMB’s former financial funding avenues’ or ‘personally fund[] or secur[e] funding to relieve Plaintiff of his personal liabilities in FMB,’” and because Plaintiff does not allege “that he sought or expected some unperformed managerial function from Croitoru.” (Demurrer, pp. 5-6, quoting FAC, ¶¶ 62, 65.)

As to the Investment Agreement, Demurring Defendants argue Plaintiff is not a party to that agreement and does not have standing to enforce it. (See Demurrer, pg. 6.) Even if he was party to the Investment Agreement, “the FAC still fails to identify any provision [of the Investment Agreement] requiring further contributions.” (Id. (emphasis added).)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues he is an intended third-party beneficiary of “these agreements” and that these agreements are related and therefore must be interpreted together. (Opposition, pg. 5.)

Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts to allege a breach of contract cause of action against Demurring Defendants. OZ is not a party to the Parent AOAs and thus the cause of action fails against OZ. As Demurring Defendants note, Plaintiff has not alleged how Croitoru has breached the Parent AOAs. Alleging Croitoru entered into the Parent AOAs “in exchange for either personally funding or securing funding to relieve Plaintiff of his personal liabilities in FMB” is not equivalent to a breach of the Parent AOAs themselves. (See FAC, ¶ 65.) The undisputed factual distinction between OZ and Croitoru regarding OZ’s status as a non-party to the Parent AOA’s leads the Court to conclude that while Plaintiff is entitled to another opportunity to amend his breach of contract action regarding Croitoru and the Parent AOAs, it is not entitled to the same opportunity as to OZ and the Parent AOAs. Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Demurring Defendants demurrer as to the first cause of action for breach of the Parent AOAs WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Croitoru but WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to OZ.

Plaintiff is also not a party to the Investment Agreement, which Plaintiff appears to acknowledge in his Opposition by arguing his standing to enforce the contract only as a third-party beneficiary. But, assuming arguendo Plaintiff’s third-party beneficiary status, Plaintiff has still failed to allege how Demurring Defendants breached the Investment Agreement in any way, much less how Plaintiff was damaged by such breach. In contrast to Plaintiff’s allegations that “Croitoru executed a Membership Interest Investment Agreement to supply this $100 million to the FMB Entities in exchange for a 25% equity stake (the ‘Investment Agreement’),” the Investment Agreement itself refers to Demurring Defendants’ $100 million investment in the past tense only. (FAC, ¶ 50, Ex. E, Recitals(C) and 2(a) (“Investor made”, “following the Investor investing Hundred Million Dollars”, “The Company hereby agrees to and does irrevocably sell….25% of the shares in the Company, in exchange for concession to defer the principal payment date”).) Facts appearing in an exhibit attached to a complaint are given precedence over inconsistent allegations in the complaint. (See Moran v. Prime Healthcare Mgmt., Inc. (2016) 3 Cal.App.5th 1131, 1145-1146.) Thus, as currently pleaded, Plaintiff’s breach of contract cause of action alleging the Demurring Defendants’ breach the Investment Agreement fails to state a claim upon which relief can be granted. And although the Court remains skeptical Plaintiff can cure the defect in light of language in the Investment Agreement quoted above, the Court will give Plaintiff an opportunity to amend the cause of action as to this theory.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first cause of action for breach of contract with respect to the Investment Agreement WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to both Demurring Defendants.

2.             Plaintiff Fails to State a Fraud or Misrepresentation Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

“The elements of fraud, which give rise to the tort action for deceit, are (1) a misrepresentation, (2) with knowledge of its falsity, (3) with the intent to induce another’s reliance on the misrepresentation, (4) justifiable reliance, and (5) resulting damage.” (Conroy v. Regents of Univ. of Cal. (2009) 45 Cal. 4th 1244, 1255.)

“[F]raud must be specifically pleaded. This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.” (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.) In addition, a cause of action of fraud against a corporation requires a plaintiff to allege the names of persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when. (See Tarmann v. State Farm Mut. Auto. Ins. Co. (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 157-58.) 

Demurring Defendants first argue most of the allegations contained in Plaintiff’s second and third causes of action for fraud and misrepresentation, respectively, are not fraudulent misrepresentations. (See Demurrer, pg. 7.) “The only purported representation attributed to Croitoru or OZ LA is that ‘they were able to and would … Provide $100,000,000.00 in funding to the FMB Entities as outlined in the Investment Agreement.’” (Id., quoting FAC, ¶ 118.) But the Investment Agreement, Demurring Defendants argue, makes no such representations, and Plaintiff does not allege Croitoru or OZ made oral representations to that effect. (See Demurrer, pg. 7.)

In opposition, Plaintiff addresses only Demurring Defendants’ false representations regarding “their intent and ability to provide $100 million in funding,” arguing these allegations are sufficient to satisfy the elements of fraud. (Opposition, pg. 5.)

Plaintiff falls short of the mark because he has failed to state facts with sufficient specificity to support either his fraud or misrepresentation causes of action. As an initial matter, Plaintiff’s second and third causes of action contain many allegations unrelated to fraud and Plaintiff, by addressing only his allegations in Opposition that relate to the $100 million in funding, appears to concede that such allegations are superfluous to support his fraud causes of action. (See FAC, ¶¶ 76, 123.) Furthermore, as discussed above, the Investment Agreement contradicts Plaintiff’s allegations that Demurring Defendants would “provide funding pursuant to the Investment Agreement” and “[p]rovide $100,000,000.00 in funding to the FMB Entities as outlined in the Investment Agreement.” (FAC, ¶¶ 76(l), 118(a).) And Plaintiff never specifically alleges Demurring Defendants promised to provide $100 million in funding outside of the Investment Agreement, via oral representations or otherwise. (See FAC.)

The only other relevant allegations are too general to meet the level of specificity required to maintain causes of action for fraud and/or misrepresentation. For example, Plaintiff alleges Defendants collectively (with no specific mention of Croitoru or OZ) assured “Plaintiff with the same false fraudulent promise of imminently forthcoming funds” after the execution of the Parent AOAs and alleges “Pinto, Zaga, Gilinski, OZ and Croitoru…[k]nowingly misrepresenting, falsely representing, or concealing Pinto, Zaga, Gilinski’s intentions and/or ability to provide funding pursuant to the agreements described hereinabove[.]” (FAC, ¶¶ 53, 123(j).) These allegations are not sufficiently specific to state a cause of action for fraud or misrepresentation.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s second and third causes of action for fraud and misrepresentation, respectively.

3.             Plaintiff Fails to State a Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing or Breach of Fiduciary Duty Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

a.             Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

Every contract imposes upon each party a duty of good faith and fair dealing in its performance and its enforcement.” (Hicks v. E.T. Legg & Associates (2001) 89 Cal.App.4th 496, 508.) “[T]he scope of conduct prohibited by the covenant of good faith is circumscribed by the purposes and express terms of the contract.” (Id. at 509.) “The covenant of good faith and fair dealing… exists… to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made.” (Guz v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349.) “The covenant is read into contracts and functions ‘as a supplement to the express contractual covenants, to prevent a contracting party from engaging in conduct which (while not technically transgressing the express covenants) frustrates the other party’s rights to the benefits of the contract.’” (Thrifty Payless, Inc. v. The Americana at Brand, LLC (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1230, 1244 (quoting Racine & Laramie, Ltd. v. Department of Parks & Recreation (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 1026, 1031-32).) The implied covenant “is designed to effectuate the intentions and reasonable expectations of parties reflected by mutual promises within the contract.” (Nein v. HostPro, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 833, 852.)¿¿¿ 

Demurring Defendants again argue OZ is not a party to the Parent AOAs and Plaintiff is not a party to the Investment Agreement. (See Demurrer, pg. 8.) Demurring Defendants acknowledge Croitoru is a party to the Parent AOAs but argues Plaintiff’s allegations create “obligations and benefits not contemplated in those contracts” such as Croitoru’s obligation to actively participate in the management of the FMB Entities and to “blindly pump cash into FMB to keep it afloat.” (Id. at 8-9, citing FAC, ¶ 127.)

In opposition, Plaintiff again argues he is a third-party beneficiary to the Investment Agreement, this time adding “[t]he FAC alleges that the parties’ mutual understanding was that Croitoru, through OZ, would ensure the provision of additional capital to stabilize the FMB entities. This mutual understanding is demonstrated by Croitoru’s acceptance of 25% equity, suggesting ongoing obligations that transcend a mere deferral of prior investments.” (See Opposition, pg. 6.) Plaintiff also attempts to bring OZ under the umbrella of the “agreements” by first alleging OZ is a “third person who has colluded with a fiduciary,” by next arguing OZ had “implied obligations arising from conduct”,” and, finally, by arguing OZ’s “liability under agency and participating doctrines.” (Opposition, pp. 6-7.)

None of Plaintiff’s kitchen sink arguments are persuasive. First, Plaintiff has not alleged facts sufficient to demonstrate he is a third-party beneficiary of the Investment Agreement. “[C]ontracting parties must clearly manifest their intent to benefit the third party. ‘The fact that [a third party] is incidentally named in the contract, or that the contract, if carried out according to its terms, would inure to his benefit, is not sufficient to entitle him to demand its fulfillment. It must appear to have been the intention of the parties to secure to him personally the benefit of its provisions.’” (Kalmanovitz v. Bitting (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 311, 314 (original italics, citation omitted).) Determining third-party beneficiary status requires a Court to examine “the express provisions of the contract at issue, as well as all of the relevant circumstances under which the contract was agreed to, in order to determine not only (1) whether the third party would in fact benefit from the contract, but also (2) whether a motivating purpose of the contracting parties was to provide a benefit to the third party, and (3) whether permitting a third party to bring its own breach of contract action against a contracting party is consistent with the objectives of the contract and the reasonable expectations of the contracting parties. All three elements must be satisfied to permit the third-party action to go forward.” (Goonewardene v. ADP, LLC (2019) 6 Cal.5th 817, 829-830 (emphasis added).) Third parties “who are only incidentally or remotely benefitted” by the contract may not enforce it. (Harper v. Wausau Ins. Co. (1997) 56 Cal.App.4th 1079, 1087.) Thus, “[t]he fact that [the third party] is incidentally named in the contract, or that the contract, if carried out according to its terms, would inure to his benefit, is not sufficient to entitle him to demand its fulfillment. It must appear to have been the intention of the parties to secure to him personally the benefit of its provisions.” (Eastern Aviation Group, Inc. v. Airborne Express, Inc. (1992) 6 Cal.App.4th 1448, 1452 (cleaned up).)

Here, Plaintiff’s allegations remain in direct contradiction to the terms of the Investment Agreement and, as discussed above, the terms of the Investment Agreement control. On its face, the Investment Agreement confers a benefit on Demurring Defendants and “FMB”: Demurring Defendants receive equity in exchange for delaying repayment of prior $100 million investments. (FAC, ¶ 50, Ex. E.) Plaintiff may have received a benefit as a result of the execution of the Investment Agreement by virtue of his role in the FMB Entities, though the terms of the Investment Agreement contradict the specific benefit Plaintiff claims he was to receive, but such a benefit does not appear to be the intention of the Investment Agreement. Thus, Plaintiff only appears to have incidentally benefitted from the contract and, according to the law, may not enforce it.

The Court will not read additional terms into the Investment Agreement, as Plaintiff asks this Court to do in his Opposition.[3] Plaintiff is not a party to the Investment Agreement nor is he, as alleged, an intended third-party beneficiary. Plaintiff therefore cannot allege breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing as to the Investment Agreement, as such a cause of action first necessities a contract between the parties.  

The Court also disregards Plaintiff’s other attempts to bootstrap OZ to the Parent AOAs. As discussed above, since OZ is not a party to the Parent AOAs, Plaintiff cannot allege OZ has breached those agreements. Thus, Plaintiff’s allegation OZ breached the covenant of good faith and fair dealing as to the Parent AOAs fails.

The only remaining agreement on which Plaintiff’s breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing can stand are the Parent AOAs to which only Croitoru is a party. To that end, Plaintiff alleges Croitoru (and other defendants) were required to “[a]ctively participate in the management and strategic direction of the FMB Entities…[c]ontribute time, effort, and resources to support the growth and operation of the FMB Entities…[and] [p]rovide funding and financial support as necessary to maintain the financial stability and success of the FMB Entities.” (FAC, ¶ 127.) Plaintiff also alleges Croitoru (and other defendants) unfairly interfered with Plaintiff’s right to receive the benefits of the Agreements by “[f]ailing to actively participate in the management and strategic direction of the FMB Entities as required[; f]ailing to obtain financing to effectuate the financial restructuring of FMB so as to relieve Plaintiff of debt personally guaranteed by him[;]…[w]ithholding the necessary funding and financial support, contrary to their obligations under the Investment Agreement[; and]… [m]isrepresenting their intentions and abilities to provide support, thereby undermining the financial stability of the FMB Entities in order to force Plaintiff to accept detrimental conditions.” (FAC, ¶ 130.)

As an initial matter, the Court disregards Plaintiff’s allegations as they relate to the Investment Agreement for the reasons previously stated. As for the remainder, Plaintiff’s allegations fail to state a cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing as it relates to Croitoru and the Parent AOAs. Plaintiff twice alleges Croitoru was responsible for and failed to participate in the management and direction of the FMB Entities, but such responsibility is specifically assigned to Gilinski and Zaga, not Croitoru. (See, e.g., FAC, ¶ 50, Ex. F, 4.1.) Unlike Gilinski and Zaga, who were “primarily… responsible to strategic direction of the company [sic], contribute or arrange for capital for the operation of the Company and support the company in any specific task as well as to support the Company’s growth” pursuant to Section 4.1 of the Parent AOAs, Croitoru had no similarly specific responsibilities.

Plaintiff also alleges Croitoru failed to contribute time and effort into the growth and operation of the FMB Entities without stating the business demanded more than what Croitoru did. This omission matters because section 4.1 stated in only the broadest and most general terms the Members “anticipated that each Member will be contributing time and effort to the business of the Company consistent with the demand of the business.” (See id. (emphasis added).) Likewise, the Parent AOAs do not create an obligation by Croitoru to effectuate financing to relieve Plaintiff of personal debt nor do they reflect affirmative representations by Croitoru about his ability to provide support. The Parent AOAs mandate additional capital contributions, but only once the “Members, by Unanimous Consent, determine that (a) additional capital is required for legitimate business purposes of the Company” and, even then, “each Member shall contribute, or cause to be contributed, to the capital of the Company such additional sums, in proportion to each Member's Percentage Interest or as otherwise approved by the Unanimous Consent of the Members.” (See, e.g., FAC, ¶ 50, Ex. F, 2.2)

In short, Plaintiff’s allegations that Croitoru breached the covenant of good faith and fair dealing implied in the Parent AOAs are either beyond the scope of the Parent AOAs, unsupported by additional factual allegations, or directly conflict with the attached exhibits. “The covenant of good faith and fair dealing, implied by law in every contract, exists merely to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made. The covenant thus cannot be endowed with an existence independent of its contractual underpinnings. It cannot impose substantive duties or limits on the contracting parties beyond those incorporated in the specific terms of their agreement.” (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349-350 (original italics, cleaned up).)

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Demurring Defendants demurrer as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing of the Parent AOAs WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Croitoru but WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to OZ. However, the Court SUSTAINS Demurring Defendants demurrer as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing of the Investment Agreement WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to both Demurring Defendants.

b.             Breach of Fiduciary Duty

Despite only titling his fourth cause of action as “Breach of the Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing”, Plaintiff also alleges an entirely separate breach of fiduciary duty claim in his fourth cause of action. (See FAC, ¶¶ 133-140.) Specifically, Plaintiff alleges Croitoru and other defendants “breached both the duty of loyalty and duty to use reasonable care by knowingly conducting the Internal Contract [not further defined] and by intentionally withholding this information from Plaintiff.” (FAC, ¶ 134.) Plaintiff also alleges Croitoru and other defendants breached the duty of loyalty and lists a number of allegations in support, including but not limited to, “[t]aking Partnership property, profit, or benefit for their personal benefit, including any tax advantages and/or tax credits” and engaging “in a pattern of deception and concealment, notably in their failure to disclose critical information to Plaintiff, including but not limited to the internal contracts, tax and financial information, and financial dealings that directly impacted Plaintiff’s interests and the overall well-being of the FMB Entities.” (FAC, ¶ 136(a), (d).)

To state a cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty, a plaintiff must allege a fiduciary duty, breach of that duty, and damage caused by the breach. (See Charnay v. Cobert (2006) 145 Cal.App.4th 170, 182.) “[B]efore a person can be charged with a fiduciary obligation, he must either knowingly undertake to act on behalf and for the benefit of another, or must enter into a relationship which imposes that undertaking as a matter of law.” (City of Hope Nat'l Med. Ctr. v. Genentech (2008) 43 Cal.4th 375, 386 (internal quotations omitted).) “[E]xamples of relationships that impose a fiduciary obligation to act on behalf of and for the benefit of another are a joint venture, a partnership, or an agency. But, those categories are merely illustrative of fiduciary relationships in which fiduciary duties are imposed by law.” (Cleveland v. Johnson (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1339 (internal citation and quotations omitted.)

Demurring Defendants again argue OZ is not a member of the “FMB Parents” and so owes no duties to Plaintiff. (See Demurrer, pg. 9.) Demurring Defendants argue Plaintiff’s list of duty of loyalty allegations are not attributed to any one defendant and that only two of the listed allegations “could generously be interpreted as directed at Croitoru: (i) trying to obtain tax advantages (an apparent reference to investing through OZ LA) and (ii) inducing Plaintiff to enter the Investment Agreement and Parent AOAs. Neither could possibly constitute a fiduciary breach by Croitoru, as both pre-date Croitoru’s membership in the FMB Parents.” (Id.) In any case, Demurring Defendants argue, structuring investments to seek a tax benefit does not breach the duty of loyalty, and Croitoru did not owe Plaintiff a duty of loyalty before becoming a member of the FMB Parents. (See id. at 9-10.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the FAC sufficiently alleges Croitoru owed Plaintiff fiduciary duties and that OZ and Croitoru were integral to the scheme to defraud Plaintiff and breach fiduciary duties. (See Opposition, pg. 7.)

The Court finds Plaintiff has failed to allege a breach of fiduciary duty claim on behalf of Croitoru and OZ. OZ is not a member of the entities at issue here and thus owes no duty to Plaintiff as presently pleaded. As to Croitoru, most of Plaintiff’s listed alleged breaches are not attributed to any individual defendants and so conclusory that it is unclear who, for example, “used Partnership information for their personal benefit” or engaged, facilitated, or “otherwise participated in transactions where they had an adverse interest against the partnership, including representing parties with interests adverse to the partnership in transactions[.]” (FAC, ¶ 136.) The remainder of the listed allegations that could be attributed to Croitoru specifically (Paragraph 136(a), (d), (h), and (j)) all relate to allegations that predate Croitoru’s equity interest. (See FAC, ¶¶ 39 (discussing alleged attempts to gain tax advantages and failing to disclose information to Plaintiff), and 50-51 (discussing the alleged $100 million promise and failure to perform).) Thus, if Plaintiff is going to succeed on his breach of fiduciary duty claim against Croitoru for any of those actions, he must clarify the basis of Croitoru’s fiduciary duty to him during that period and how Croitoru breached it.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s fourth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty as to both Croitoru and OZ.

4.             Plaintiff Fails to State a Breach of Fiduciary Duty Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

The Court reincorporates its breach of fiduciary legal standard from the previous section.

Here, Plaintiff again alleges breach of fiduciary duty, but under the “Trust-Fund Doctrine.” (FAC, ¶ 113.) Plaintiff alleges the “FMB Entities” became insolvent in or about 2023, that Plaintiff was a creditor of the FMB Entities, and that “Pinto, Gilinski, Zaga, and Croitoru, as managers and members, immediately owed a fiduciary duty to the creditors of the FMB Entities, including Plaintiff.” (Id.)

“The scope of any extra-contractual duty owed by corporate directors to the insolvent corporation’s creditors is limited in California, consistently with the trust-fund doctrine, to the avoidance of actions that divert, dissipate, or unduly risk corporate assets that might otherwise be used to pay creditors claims.” (Berg & Berg Enterprises, LLC v. Boyle (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 1020, 1041 (emphasis in original).) This duty is a fiduciary duty. (See id., 178 Cal.App.4th at 1040.)

Demurring Defendants argue Plaintiff’s FAC is uncertain as to a breach of fiduciary duty via the Trust-Find Doctrine. (See Demurrer, pg. 10.) “It is impossible to tell, however, which of these entities was insolvent, whether Plaintiff was indeed a ‘creditor’ of such entity, and whether Defendants had any officer/director relationship with such entity.” (Id.) Either way, Demurring Defendants argue, Plaintiff has not properly alleged Croitoru and OZ’s breach of fiduciary duty. (See id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues he has properly plead his fifth cause of action because he has alleged he was a creditor and the FMB Entities were insolvent. (See Opposition, pg. 8.)

As discussed above, Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts to state a breach of fiduciary cause of action pursuant to the Trust-Fund Doctrine against the Demurring Defendants. Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action for breach of fiduciary duty.

5.             Plaintiff Fails to State an Open Book Account Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

A cause of action for an open book account requires the following: (1) a detailed statement which constitutes the principal record of one or more transactions between a debtor and a creditor arising out of a contract or some fiduciary relation; (2) that shows the debits and credits in connection therewith, and against whom and in favor of whom entries are made; (3) that was entered in the regular course of business as conducted by such creditor or fiduciary; (4) that was kept in a reasonably permanent form and manner; and (5) that was kept in a bound book, on a sheet or sheets fastened in a book or to backing but detachable therefrom, on a card or cards of a permanent character, or is kept in any other reasonably permanent form and manner. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 337a; Tsemetzin v. Coast Federal Savings & Loan Assn. (1997) 57 Cal.App.4th 1334, 1343.)

         Plaintiff has failed to state sufficient facts to state a cause of action for open book account because he hasn’t alleged any of the necessary elements of an open book account cause of action, including the most foundational element of such a cause of action: that Demurring Defendants have a debtor-creditor relationship with Plaintiff as an individual.  

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s sixth cause of action for open book account.

6.             Plaintiff Fails to State a Books and Records Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

Corporations Code § 1601 provides: “(a) The accounting books and records and minutes of proceedings of the shareholders and the board and committees of the board of any domestic corporation, and of any foreign corporation keeping any such records in this state or having its principal executive office in this state, shall be open to inspection upon the written demand on the corporation of any shareholder or holder of a voting trust certificate at any reasonable time during usual business hours, for a purpose reasonably related to such holder’s interests as a shareholder or as the holder of such voting trust certificate. The right of inspection created by this subdivision shall extend to the records of each subsidiary of a corporation subject to this subdivision. (b) Such inspection by a shareholder or holder of a voting trust certificate may be made in person or by agent or attorney, and the right of inspection includes the right to copy and make extracts. The right of the shareholders to inspect the corporate records may not be limited by the articles or bylaws.”

Corporations Code § 1602 reads: “Every director shall have the absolute right at any reasonable time to inspect and copy all books, records and documents of every kind and to inspect the physical properties of the corporation of which such person is a director and also of its subsidiary corporations, domestic or foreign. Such inspection by a director may be made in person or by agent or attorney and the right of inspection includes the right to copy and make extracts. This section applies to a director of any foreign corporation having its principal executive office in this state or customarily holding meetings of its board in this state.”

Corporations Code § 1603 reads in pertinent part: “(a) Upon refusal of a lawful demand for inspection, the superior court of the proper county, may enforce the right of inspection with just and proper conditions.”

Here, Plaintiff alleges “Defendants had control and oversight over the FMB Entities’ financial resources” but deprived “Plaintiff of critical financial and tax records so that he would be able to prepare his tax returns, resulting in financial loss and significant liability and tax exposure.” (FAC, ¶ 129.) Thus, as currently pleaded, the Court construes Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action to relate only to the “FMB Entities.” But Plaintiff has not named the “FMB Entities” as defendants and thus cannot demand to inspect the records of the non-party “FMB Entities” via the named parties in this action, including the Demurring Defendants.

         Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s seventh cause of action for books and records of the FMB Entities.

7.             Plaintiff Fails to State an Aiding and Abetting Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

To state a cause of action for aiding and abetting, a plaintiff must allege the following: (1) Person aids and abets commission of intentional tort knowing the other’s conduct constitutes breach of duty; or giving substantial assistance or encouragement to the other to so act; or in accomplishing a tortious result and (2) the person’s own conduct, separately considered, constitutes breach of duty to the third person. (See Das v. Bank of America, N.A. (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 727, 744.)

Demurring Defendants argue Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action fails “because it does not identify which Defendant supposedly aided and abetted which claim, or through which actions… It alleges no facts of when or how Croitoru and OZ LA provided substantial assistance to any tort.” (Demurrer, pg. 12.)

Plaintiff argues in opposition “the FAC has specifically alleged that Croitoru, through OZ, entered into agreements that were integral to the Defendants’ scheme to divert assets and harm Plaintiff’s interests, including the Investment Agreement where Croitoru failed to provide promised funding, which exposed Plaintiff to significant liabilities.” (Opposition, pp. 9-10.)

Plaintiff has failed to state sufficient facts to state an aiding and abetting cause of action. As Demurring Defendants highlight, Plaintiff alleges Pinto, Zaga, Gilinski, Croitoru, OZ, and Mosdot have “substantially assisted, knowingly provided and encouraged each other in committing the following acts” and again proceeds to list a series of acts not attributed to any particular Defendant. (FAC, ¶ 135.) As such, it is unclear who was committing what intentional tort and how Croitoru was aiding and abetting the individual committing said tort. Plaintiff also cannot maintain an aiding and abetting cause of action against OZ, as a corporation, as OZ is not a “person” who can aid in the commission of an intentional tort.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s eighth cause of action for aiding and abetting WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Croitoru but WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to OZ.

8.             Plaintiff Fails to State an Express Indemnity Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

The elements of a cause of action for express indemnity are (1) a contract defining the obligation for one party to make good as to a loss another party incurred and (2) the occurrence of loss the other party incurred or of some other legal consequence of conduct of another party. (See Rossmoor Sanitation, Inc. v. Pylon, Inc. (1975) 13 Cal.3d 622, 628.) “Indemnity is a contract by which one engages to save another from a legal consequence of the conduct of one of the parties, or of some other person.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2772.) “A defendant may properly bring a declaratory cross-complaint for indemnity in the original tort action even though ‘for statute of limitations purposes, the defendant's indemnity action does not accrue until he has suffered actual loss through payment.’” (Allen v. Southland Plumbing (1988) 201 Cal.App.3d 60, 65 (citations omitted).)

Plaintiff has not stated sufficient facts to state a cause of action for breach of express indemnity. There is no indemnity provision in the Parent AOAs and Plaintiff is not a party to the Investment Agreement, as discussed above. (See FAC, ¶¶ 50-51, Exs. E-G.)

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s ninth cause of action for express indemnity.

9.             Plaintiff Fails to State a Conversion Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another. The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff’s ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant’s conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.” (Lee v. Hanley (2015) 61 Cal.4th 1225, 1240.) 

Demurring Defendants argue OZ never obtained any ownership interest from Plaintiff and Plaintiff voluntarily transferred his rights in the FMB Entities. (See Demurrer, pg. 13.) Demurring Defendants also argue “any claim that such assent was fraudulently induced sounds in fraud and must be pleaded with particularity.” (Id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues the cause of action is not alleged against OZ and that any discussion of Plaintiff’s voluntary assent goes impermissibly beyond the scope of the facts in the demurrer. (See Opposition, pg. 11.)

Plaintiff has not alleged sufficient facts to state a cause of action for conversion against Demurring Defendants. As an initial matter, though Plaintiff does not name OZ in the header of his tenth cause of action, Plaintiff does, however, name OZ in the substance of the cause of action. (See FAC, ¶ 147.) OZ, per Plaintiff’s allegations, did not obtain ownership interest in the FMB Entities and thus could not have converted any of Plaintiff’s interest.

The Court also finds a discussion of the voluntary nature of the alleged conversion to be within the four corners of the FAC. Plaintiff specifically alleges he agreed to a dilution of his equity (via the expansion of others’ equity), but that his agreement was given only upon the promise that additional funds would be provided. (See FAC, ¶¶ 51-52.) “[T]he law is well settled that there can be no conversion where an owner either expressly or impliedly assents to or ratifies the taking, use or disposition of his property.” (Farrington v. A. Teichert & Son, Inc. (1943) 59 Cal.App.2d 468, 474.) The fact that no additional funds were allegedly provided does not render Plaintiff’s interest converted but does give rise to other potential tort claims.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s tenth cause of action for conversion WITH LEAVE TO AMEND as to Croitoru but WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to OZ.

10.          Plaintiff Fails to State a Quantum Meruit Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

“Quantum meruit refers to the well-established principle that ‘the law implies a promise to pay for services performed under circumstances disclosing that they were not gratuitously rendered.’” (Children’s Hospital Central Cal. v. Blue Cross of Cal. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1260, 1274.) Quantum meruit “applies ‘[w]here one obtains a benefit which he may not justly retain.... The so-called ‘contract implied in law’ in reality is not a contract. [Citations.] ‘Quasi-contracts, unlike true contracts, are not based on the apparent intention of the parties to undertake the performances in question, nor are they promises. They are obligations created by law for reasons of justice.’ [Citation.]” (McBride v. Boughton (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 379, 388, fn 6.)¿ 

“To recover on a claim for the reasonable value of services under a quantum meruit theory, a plaintiff must establish both that he or she was acting pursuant to either an express or implied request for services from the defendant and that the services rendered were intended to and did benefit the defendant.” (Ochs v. PacifiCare of Cal. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 782, 794 (internal citation omitted).)

Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for quantum meruit against Demurring Defendants because he doesn’t allege he was acting on a request for services or that he rendered any services whatsoever to Demurring Defendants. Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s eleventh cause of action for quantum meruit.

11.           Plaintiff Fails to State a Declaratory Relief Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

A cause of action for declaratory relief requires the following elements: (1) person interested under a written instrument or a contract or (2) a declaration of his or her rights or duties (a) with respect to another or (b) in respect to, in, over or upon property; and (3) an actual controversy. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 1060.)   

Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for declaratory relief. Plaintiff is not a party to the Investment Agreement and Plaintiff has not properly alleged Croitoru’s breach of the Parent AOAs, as discussed above. Thus, Plaintiff has not alleged an actual controversy and cannot maintain his twelfth cause of action as presently pled.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s twelfth cause of action for declaratory relief.

12.          Plaintiff Fails to State a Specific Performance Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

To state a cause of action for specific performance, a plaintiff must allege the following: (1) Contract breach; (2) inadequacy of a legal remedy; (3) underlying contract reasonable and supported by adequate consideration; (4) existence of a mutuality of remedies; (5) contractual terms sufficiently definite to know what it is to enforce; and (6) substantial similarity between requested performance and promised performance. (See Real Estate Analytics, LLC v. Vallas (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 463, 472; see also Patel v. Liebermensch (2008) 45 Cal.4th 344, 349 (if contract terms are certain enough, judges carry into effect ascertainable, reasonable intentions of the parties).)

Plaintiff has failed to state sufficient facts to state a cause of action for specific performance as Plaintiff has not properly alleged Demurring Defendant’s breach of contract. Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s thirteenth cause of action for specific performance.

13.          Plaintiff Fails to State an Intentional Interference with Prospective Economic Interests or Intentional Interference with Current Business Advantage Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

The elements of a claim for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage are: (1) an economic relationship between the plaintiff and some third party, with the probability of future economic benefit to the plaintiff; (2) the defendant’s knowledge of the relationship; (3) intentional acts on the part of the defendant designed to disrupt the relationship; (4) actual disruption of the relationship; and (5) economic harm to the plaintiff proximately caused by the acts of the defendant. (See Korea Supply Co. v. Lockheed Martin Corp. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1134, 1153.) A plaintiff need not allege a defendant intended to disrupt, but may allege they knew the interference would be substantially certain. (See San Jose Constr. v. S.B.C.C. (2007) 155 Cal.App.4th 1528, 1544.) A plaintiff must also demonstrate the defendant engaged in an independently wrongful act. (See id. at p. 1158.) “[A]n act is independently wrongful if it is unlawful, that is, if it is proscribed by some constitutional, statutory, regulatory, common law, or other determinable legal standard.” (Id. at p. 1159.) 

The elements of a claim for intentional interference with a contract are: (1) valid contract between plaintiff and third party; (2) defendant’s knowledge of that; (3) defendant's intentional acts designed to induce disruption of the relationship; (4) actual disruption; and (5) resulting damage. (See Reeves v. Hanlon (2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1148.)

Demurring Defendants argue OZ is not mentioned in either of Plaintiff’s fourteenth and fifteenth causes of action. (See Demurrer, pg. 15.) Demurring Defendants argue “Plaintiff names a handful of entities (for the first time), but provides no context or information regarding the prospective relationship (or its likelihood of success)…nor any existing contract or supposed breach…” (Id.) Demurring Defendants further argue Plaintiff’s allegations fail to mention their knowledge of Plaintiff’s existing relationships and how failing to provide funding was directed at disrupting those relationships. (See id.)

In opposition, Plaintiff argues his FAC “identifies Plaintiff’s economic relationships with entities such as PMS Financial, Peak Servicing, Loan Oak, and RTI Financials” and that “Croitoru, through OZ, was an active participant in the scheme to undermine Plaintiff’s economic and business relationships.” (Opposition, pp. 13-14 (emphasis added).)

Plaintiff has simply failed to state a cause of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage or with a contract against Demurring Defendants. Plaintiff’s allegations concerning his relationship with the various entities he lists and Demurring Defendants’ knowledge of his relationship with those entities is far too attenuated and speculative. Allegations of Demurring Defendants’ participation in a general scheme to take over the FMB Entities is not enough to show Demurring Defendants participated in that scheme in order to disrupt Plaintiff’s specific relationships with PMS Financial, Loan Oak, RTI Financials, and/or Peak Servicing. Plaintiff seems to acknowledge the unspecified nature of his allegations in his Opposition, saying he had a relationship with entities such as PMS Financial, Peak Servicing, Loan Oak, and RTI Financials. However, that is not sufficient to state a cause of action for intentional interference with either prospective economic advantage or contract. Plaintiff must identify which entities he had relationships with (and the nature of those relationships), that each defendant knew about those relationships, and how their individual conduct was designed to disrupt those relationships, did so disrupt them, and that such disruption damaged him.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s fourteenth and fifteenth causes of action for intentional interference with prospective economic advantage and with contract.

14.          Plaintiff Fails to State an Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress Cause of Action Against Demurring Defendants.

“The elements of the tort for intentional infliction of mental distress are: (1) outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) intention to cause or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress, (3) severe emotional suffering and (4) actual and proximate causation of emotional distress.” (Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 921.)  

To satisfy the element of extreme and outrageous conduct, a defendant’s conduct “‘must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in a civilized society.’” (Moncada v. West Coast Quartz Corp. (2013) 221 Cal.App.4th 768, 780.)¿“Behavior may be considered outrageous if a defendant (1) abuses a relation or position which gives him power to damage the plaintiff’s interest; (2) knows the plaintiff is susceptible to injuries through mental distress; or (3) acts intentionally or unreasonably with the recognition that the acts are likely to result in illness through mental distress.” (McDaniel v.¿Gile¿(1991) 230 Cal.App.3d 363, 372.)¿“Severe emotional distress means emotional distress of such substantial quality or enduring quality that no reasonable person¿in civilized society should be expected to endure it.” (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co.¿(1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 1004.)¿“[T]he plaintiff must allege with great specificity the acts which he or she believes are so extreme as to exceed all bounds of that usually tolerated in civilized community.” (Vasquez v. Franklin Management Real Estate Fund, Inc. (2013) 222 Cal.App.4th 819, 832.)¿¿ 

Plaintiff has failed to state a cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress. Plaintiff again refers only to Defendants generally, failing to specify how each Defendant individually contributed to his emotional distress. Plaintiff alleges “[s]pecific acts of misconduct by Defendants have included, but are not limited to, threats, intimidation, harassment, and other forms of egregious conduct” which have in turn led to the loss of Plaintiff’s health insurance and salary and an increase in Plaintiff’s personal debt. (FAC, ¶¶ 184-185.) Plaintiff does not describe any other specific instances of Defendants’, or Demurring Defendants’, alleged intentional infliction of emotional distress.

The Court is then left to sift through Plaintiff’s prior 180 disjointedly numbered paragraphs to find evidence of Croitoru or OZ’s conduct that might be considered outrageous. As discussed above, the Investment Agreement does not demonstrate Demurring Defendants owed but failed to provide additional funding. This cannot be the basis for Plaintiff’s intentional infliction of emotional distress claim.

Plaintiff does, however, allege “[w]hen Defendants realized that Plaintiff would not agree to sign the latest documents, Pinto, Zaga, Gilinski, Croitoru and Menachem Pinto began a campaign of physical and verbal threats against Plaintiff and his family, putting unrelenting pressure on Plaintiff for one goal – to force Plaintiff to ultimately sign the November 2023 agreements and relinquish his interest in the FMB Entities and leave.” (FAC, ¶ 57.) Verbal and physical threats could certainly qualify as outrageous conduct. But, in his Opposition, Plaintiff completely abandons these allegations of threats and physical violence. The extreme and outrageous conduct is based only on “Defendants’ actions—making substantial funding commitments to induce Plaintiff to reduce his equity and assume personal liabilities, followed by a deliberate failure to provide the promised funds.” (Opposition, pg. 14.) General allegations concerning Demurring Defendants’ participation in a scheme to take over the FMB Entities are insufficient to constitute outrageous conduct.

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS WITH LEAVE TO AMEND Demurring Defendants’ Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s sixteenth cause of action for intentional infliction of emotional distress.

IV.    MOTION TO STRIKE

         A.      Legal Standard

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 436(a).) The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (See id. at § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (See id. at § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (See id. at § 437.)

B.      The Motion to Strike Is Granted In Part and Denied In Part.

Demurring Defendants seek to strike all allegations concerning punitive damages, all references to attorneys’ fees, all references to damages to the FMB Entities, and all references to emotional distress for breach of contract. (See Motion.)

As an initial matter, the Court DENIES as moot Demurring Defendants’ Motion as it pertains to causes of action affected by the Court’s sustaining the Demurrer in its entirety. More specifically, the Court DENIES as moot the Motion as to Nos. 1-7 (under Punitive and Exemplary Damages), Nos. 1-3 (under Damages to the FMB Entities), and No. 1 (under Emotional Distress Damages).

The Court GRANTS Demurring Defendants’ Motion as it pertains to Plaintiff’s punitive damages request for his second, third, fourth, fifth, eighth, tenth, fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth causes of action. Punitive damages may be recovered upon a proper showing of malice, fraud, or oppression. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 3294(a).) “Malice” is defined as conduct intended to cause injury to a person or despicable conduct carried on with a willful and conscious disregard for the rights or safety of others. (See Turman v. Turning Point of Cent. Cal., Inc. (2010) 191 Cal.App.4th 53, 63.) “Oppression” means despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship, in conscious disregard of the person’s rights. (See id.) “Fraud” is an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known by defendant, with intent to deprive a person of property, rights or otherwise cause injury. (See id.) Conclusory allegations, devoid of any factual assertions, are insufficient to support a conclusion that parties acted with oppression, fraud or malice. (See Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal.App.4th 1033, 1042.) As discussed in the Court’s analysis of the Demurrer, the Court does not find Plaintiff’s allegations against Demurring Defendants, as currently alleged, amount to a showing of malice, fraud, or oppression.

The Court also GRANTS Demurring Defendants’ Motion as it pertains to Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees request for his first, second, third, fifth, eighth, ninth, tenth, eleventh, fourteenth, fifteenth, and sixteenth causes of action. “A party is entitled to an award of¿attorney¿fees¿if there is a specific authorization therefor by statute or private agreement.” (Beverly Hills Properties v. Marcolino (1990) 221 Cal.App.3d Supp. 7, 10.) Plaintiff has alleged no basis to recover fees from Demurring Defendants. Although the Investment Agreement contains an attorneys’ fees provision, Plaintiff is not a party to it.

The Court also GRANTS Demurring Defendants’ Motion as it pertains to “Page 48, Line 2 (Prayer ¶ 3; Prayer for the First Cause of Action for Breach of Contract): ‘For lost profits and loss of business damages’ as to Croitoru and OZ LA.” (Motion, No. 4 (under Damages to the FMB Entities).) Plaintiff is suing in his individual capacity and cannot recover for damages to the FMB Entities as they are not Plaintiffs or even parties to the instant action.

Accordingly, the Court DENIES IN PART and GRANTS IN PART Demurring Defendants’ Motion in accordance with its above analysis WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

(See California Rules of Court, rule 2.112; see also Campbell v. Rayburn (1954) 129 Cal.App.2d 232, 235.)

 

The FAC is misnumbered at various points to such an extent that it makes references to specific paragraphs virtually useless. It jumps from Paragraph 76 to 110 on pages 27 and 28, contains two different paragraphs 111 and 112 on pages 28 and 29, then jumps from Paragraph 120 to a third different Paragraph 111 on page 36, at which point the paragraphs continue through 122 before jumping to Paragraph 141 and back to 123 on page 38. The FAC then resumes sequentially until Paragraph 163, which incorporates paragraphs 1 to 167, even though 164-168 are never alleged because the FAC jumps yet again to Paragraph 169.

 

As a result of these issues, the Court will refer to the paragraphs as they are erroneously numbered and trust that the context of each citation will make clear which paragraph its analysis refers because the Court has organized its analysis by cause of action.

 

Of slightly lesser concern given the length of the FAC and number of exhibits attached to it, Plaintiff hasn’t properly electronically bookmarked each exhibit separately.
