DEPARTMENT T LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STCV43701    Hearing Date: January 3, 2025    Dept: T

Mari Hartoonian vs Ashley Furniture Industries, Inc.

 

Motion:                                   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents

Moving Party:                         Defendant Ashley Furniture Industries, Inc.  

Responding Party:                  None

Tentative Ruling:                    Granted
 

BACKGROUND

 

On December 4, 2019, Plaintiff Mari Hartoonian (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendant Ashley Furniture Industries, Inc. (“Defendant”) for negligence, premises liability, and products liability. Plaintiff alleges she was injured at an Ashley Furniture store in Burbank after she sat on a coffee table which then collapsed, causing her injuries.

 

On October 11, 2024, Defendant served Request for Production, Set Three on Plaintiff, which contains Request numbers 49 and 50. (See Chung Decl., ¶ 3, Exh. A.)

 

Defendant now moves to compel Plaintiff to provide further responses to its Request for Production, Set Three numbers 49-50. No opposition has been filed.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Defendants argue responses to Requests 49 and 50 should be compelled because Plaintiff failed to serve any responses.  

 

OPPOSITION

           

            No opposition has been filed.  

 

REPLY


No reply has been filed.       

ANALYSIS

As an initial matter, Defendant has moved to compel further responses to Request for Production, Set Three. However, the Notice of Motion references Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.290, which pertains to Request for Admissions, and section 2031.310, which refers to compelling further responses to Request for Production. The supporting memorandum also references section 2031.310. (See Motion, at p. 6.)

Defendant asserts that Plaintiff has not responded to Request for Production, Set Three, and has not responded to its meet and confer letter. (Chung Decl., ¶ 8, Exh. F.) Therefore, it appears Defendant is seeking to compel an initial response to the discovery, as opposed to a further response.

Nevertheless, the Court notes that on October 24, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Substitution of Attorney substituting her original counsel, Laurence Ring, for Tom Antunovich (“Antunovich”). No Association of Counsel was filed. On October 31, 2024, the Court granted and signed an order relieving Antunovich as Plaintiff’s counsel. On that same day, Antunovich filed a proof of service of the Order Relieving Counsel on Plaintiff.

Plaintiff has not appeared in this case since her counsel was relieved, nor has she filed a substitution of attorney. Therefore, Plaintiff is currently self-represented.

Nevertheless, the proof of service for this motion shows that it was served electronically on Laurence Ring, Esq., Plaintiff’s original attorney. Defendant’s exhibit F shows some correspondence between Defendant’s counsel and Laurence Ring. However, because the docket shows that Plaintiff is self-represented, Defendant has not shown that it provided adequate notice of this motion. Therefore, based on lack of notice, the Court is inclined to deny this motion on procedural grounds.

In the event Defendant shows that notice was proper, and Laurence Ring is Plaintiff’s counsel, the Court will address the merits of the motion below, construing it as a motion to compel initial responses under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.300.

A.    Motion to Compel

The Court notes that on September 25, 2024, the Court denied Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to Requests for Production, numbers 25 and 26, which sought documents supporting Plaintiff’s loss of earnings claim and lost wages claim. (Min. Order, 9/25/24.) The motion was denied as untimely because it was not noticed within 45 days after the responses were served on May 20, 2020. (Ibid.; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (c).)

Responses to demands for inspection, copying, testing or sampling must be made within 30 days after service of the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).) Where a party fails to serve a timely response, the party making the demand may move for an order compelling a response to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (b).) Additionally, “[t]he party to whom the demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling is directed waives any objection to the demand, including one based on privilege or on the protection for work product under Chapter 4 (commencing with Section 2018.010).” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

Here, Defendant’s Request for Production, Set Three was served on October 11, 2024. Defendant asserts Plaintiff has not responded.

Request number 49 seeks the following: “Copies of your income tax returns, state and federal, for the years 2017 through the present, whether filed individually or jointly with another person. Documents should also include, but are not limited to, W2 forms, 1099 forms, worksheets, schedules, and any other documents used in preparation of your income tax returns.”   (Chung Decl., Exh. A.)

Request number 50 seeks the following: “Cash registers, canceled checks, bank statements, deposit slips, travelers’ checks, and cashiers’ check acquisition records, and any other documents reflecting deposits, withdrawals, and/or exchanges of funds at any bank or financial institution for all accounts (a) held by you, individually or jointly with another person, or (b) standing in the name of any corporation, partnership, joint venture, trust, or other entity in which you have had any interest from January 2017 to the present.” (Chung Decl., Exh. A.)

Regarding request 49 for tax returns, California recognizes a privilege against the forced disclosure of federal and state tax returns. (Schnabel v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 704, 719-20; see also Webb v. Standard Oil Co. of Cal. (1957) 49 Cal.2d 509, 513-14.) The purpose is to encourage truthful declarations in tax returns, without fear they will be used against the taxpayer. (Sav-On Drugs, Inc. v. Superior Court (1975) 15 Cal.3d 1, 6.) However, the privilege may be waived in three situations: “(1) there is an intentional relinquishment [Citation], (2) the ‘gravamen of [the] lawsuit is so inconsistent with the continued assertion of the taxpayer's privilege as to compel the conclusion that the privilege has in fact been waived’ [Citation], or (3) a public policy greater than that of confidentiality of tax returns is involved [Citation.]” (Schnabel, supra, 5 Cal.4th at 721 [quoting Sammut v. Sammut (1980) 103 Cal.App.3d 557, 560].)

As discussed further in Schnabel, the public policy exception has been applied to proceedings enforcing child support obligations. (Schnabel, supra, 5 Cal.4th at 722.)

Notably, in Brown v. Superior Court (1977) 71 Cal.App.3d 141, the Court of Appeal applied the privilege to a personal injury case where the plaintiff was claiming lost income. There, the court found that W-2 forms “constitute an integral part of the return” and were subject to the privilege where it had not been waived by plaintiff. (Id. at p. 143-44.)

However, because Plaintiff failed to provide timely responses, the Court deems the tax return privilege waived. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

Therefore, because timely responses were not served, the motion to compel is granted.

B.     Sanctions

The court shall impose a monetary sanction against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to a demand for inspection, copying, testing, or sampling, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).)

Here, because Defendant has not requested sanctions in the Notice of Motion, the Court declines to award sanctions. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2023.040 [“A request for a sanction shall, in the notice of motion, identify every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought, and specify the type of sanction sought.”].) 

RULING

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production of Documents is GRANTED.     


Case Number: 22STCV02646    Hearing Date: January 3, 2025    Dept: T

Elpidio Rios vs CP V Cumulus, LLC, et al.  

 

Motion:                                   Demurrer to Plaintiff’s First Amended Complaint

Moving Party:                         Defendants CP V Cumulus, LLC, Carmel Partners Inc., CP Construction West Inc., Carmel Management, LLC, MWL Solutions, Inc., and Mario William Larrabure dba MWL General Contracting

Responding Party:                   Plaintiff Elpidio Rios

Tentative Ruling:                    Sustained with leave to amend

 

BACKGROUND

 

On July 10, 2024, Plaintiff Elpidio Rios (“Plaintiff”) filed the operative first amended complaint (“FAC”) alleging negligence based on a construction site injury near the intersection of La Cienega and Jefferson Boulevards in Los Angeles. Specially, Plaintiff alleges that on January 24, 2020, he was working for a subcontractor that was hired by some or all of Defendants, to perform certain work at the premises. On that day, Plaintiff was walking on the premises when an unsecured piece of plywood slipped out underneath him, causing him to fall through an opening 12 feet high. (FAC, ¶ 27-28.)

 

Defendants are entities that are alleged to own and operate the subject premises and construction project. (FAC, ¶ 17-18.)

 

            Defendants CP V Cumulus, LLC, Carmel Partners Inc., CP Construction West Inc., Carmel Management, LLC, MWL Solutions, Inc., and Mario William Larrabure dba MWL General Contracting (“Defendants”) now demur to the FAC. Plaintiff opposes and Defendants reply.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Defendants demur to the FAC arguing that the alleged facts show the action is barred by the Privette doctrine and workers’ compensation exclusivity. Defendants argue the FAC does not sufficiently allege that Defendants retained control of the site or that there were concealed hazards under the Privette exceptions.  

 

OPPOSITION

           

            Plaintiff first argues that an affirmative defense such as the Privette doctrine, cannot be raised in a demurrer. Next, Plaintiff argues the FAC does not allege that Defendants hired Plaintiff’s employer, and therefore, it cannot be determined from the pleadings that Privette applies. Finally, Plaintiff argues the FAC sufficient alleges the Privette exceptions.

 

REPLY

 

            Again, Defendants argue that Plaintiff alleges insufficient facts of the Privette exceptions.           

ANALYSIS

I.                   Demurrer Legal Standard

The primary function of a pleading is to give the other party notice so that it may prepare its case [citation], and a defect in a pleading that otherwise properly notifies a party cannot be said to affect substantial rights.” (Harris v. City of Santa Monica (2013) 56 Cal.4th 203, 240.) “A demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the factual allegations in a complaint.” (Ivanoff v. Bank of America, N.A. (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 719, 725.) It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party's pleading (complaint, answer or cross-complaint).  (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 422.10, 589; see Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purposes of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true.  (Donabedian, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.)

The general rule is that the plaintiff need only allege ultimate facts, not evidentiary facts. (Doe v. City of Los Angeles (2007) 42 Cal.4th 531, 550.) “All that is required of a plaintiff, as a matter of pleading, even as against a special demurrer, is that his complaint set forth the essential facts of the case with reasonable precision and with sufficient particularity to acquaint the defendant with the nature, source and extent of his cause of action.” (Rannard v. Lockheed Aircraft Corp. (1945) 26 Cal.2d 149, 156-157.)

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318; Donabedian, supra, 116 Cal.App.4th at 994.)  No other extrinsic evidence can be considered.  (Ion Equip. Corp. v. Nelson (1980) 110 Cal.App.3d 868, 881 [error for court to consider facts asserted in memorandum supporting demurrer]; see also Afuso v. United States Fid. & Guar. Co. (1985) 169 Cal.App.3d 859, 862 [disapproved on other grounds in Moradi-Shalal v. Fireman’s Fund Ins. Cos. (1988) 46 Cal.3d 287] [error to consider contents of release not part of court record].) A demurrer can be utilized where the “face of the complaint” itself is incomplete or discloses some defense that would bar recovery.  (Guardian North Bay, Inc. v. Superior Court (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 963, 971-72.) 

 

II.                Request for Judicial Notice

The Court grants Defendant’s request for judicial notice of exhibit 1, the FAC in this case. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).)

III.             Meet and Confer

Code of Civil Procedure section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a)(3).)

The Declaration of Amar M. Hatti, Defendants’ counsel, states the following: “[o]n August 23, 2024, I telephoned the attorney for plaintiff Elpidio Rios, Christopher Ghahreman. We met and conferred regarding the second amended complaint in this matter and the basis for the concurrently-filed demurrer, including application of the Privette doctrine. We could not come to an agreement on the demurrer.” (Hatti Decl., ¶ 2.)

Therefore, the meet and confer requirement is met.

IV.             Privette Doctrine

Ordinarily, the person hiring an independent contractor is not liable to the contractor's employees for workplace injuries.¿ The independent contractor's employer's liability for such injuries is limited by workers' compensation, as is the liability of the party who hired the contractor.¿ (Privette v. Superior Court (1993) 5 Cal.4th 689, 693, 700-02; Camargo v. Tjaarda Dairy (2001) 25 Cal.4th 1235, 1244-1245; Toland v. Sunland Housing Group, Inc. (1998) 18 Cal.4th 253, 264-70.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

The Supreme Court has held that it would be unfair to impose liability on the hiring person when the liability of the contractor, the one primarily responsible for its worker’s on-the-job injuries, is limited to providing workers’ compensation coverage.¿ As such, even if there is evidence that the hirer was directly liable for the plaintiff’s injuries, recovery is not available under the peculiar risk doctrine.¿ (Privette, supra, 5 Cal.4th at 693, 700-02.)¿¿ 

¿¿ 

Workers’ compensation “ ‘is the exclusive remedy against an employer for injury or death of an employee.’ [Citation.]” … In Privette, the Supreme Court held that “an independent contractor’s employee should not be allowed to recover damages from the contractor’s hirer, who ‘is indirectly paying for the cost of [workers’ compensation] coverage, which the [hired] contractor presumably has calculated into the contract price.’ [Citation.]” [Citation.]¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

The Privette holding was based on the principle that the hirer of an independent contractor generally has “ ‘ “ ‘no right of control as to the mode of doing the work contracted for.’ ” ’ ” [Citation.] Precisely because the hirer “has no obligation to specify the precautions an independent hired contractor should take for the safety of the contractor’s employees, ... [a]bsent an obligation, there can be no liability in tort.” [Citation.]¿¿¿ 

¿¿ 

(Alvarez v. Seaside Transportation Services LLC (2017) 13 Cal.App.5th 635, 640.)¿ Moreover, “[t]he Privette line of decisions establishes a presumption that an independent contractor’s hirer ‘delegates to that contractor its tort law duty to provide a safe workplace for the contractor’s employees.’ [Citation.]”¿ (Id. at 642.)¿In SeaBright Ins. Co. v. US Airways, Inc. (2011) 52 Cal.4th 590, “the Supreme Court extended Privette to service contracts, [holding] that the duty to provide a safe working environment is implicitly and presumptively delegated in all independent contractor agreements.” (Vargas v. FMI, Inc. (2015) 233 Cal.App.4th 638, 645.)  

 

A.                Exceptions to Privette

The California Supreme Court has identified two limited circumstances to overcome the presumption under Privette. (Gonzalez v. Mathis (2021) 12 Cal.5th 29, 38.)

 “First, in Hooker v. Department of Transportation (2002) 27 Cal.4th 198, 115 Cal.Rptr.2d 853, 38 P.3d 1081 (Hooker), [the Court] held that a hirer may be liable when it retains control over any part of the independent contractor's work and negligently exercises that retained control in a manner that affirmatively contributes to the worker's injury. [Citation.] Second, in Kinsman v. Unocal Corp. (2005) 37 Cal.4th 659, 36 Cal.Rptr.3d 495, 123 P.3d 931 (Kinsman), [the Court] held that a landowner who hires an independent contractor may be liable if the landowner knew, or should have known, of a concealed hazard on the property that the contractor did not know of and could not have reasonably discovered, and the landowner failed to warn the contractor of the hazard. [Citation.]”

(Gonzalez, supra, 12 Cal.5th at p. 38.)  

V.                Discussion

Here, the FAC states the following relevant allegations:

“On or about January 24, 2020, Plaintiff was working for a subcontractor (hired by some or all Defendants) to perform certain work at the Premises.” (FAC, ¶ 27.) “[A]t all relevant times alleged herein, Defendants actively participated in and/or retained control over the construction, installation, assembly, erection, design, modification, repair, management, supervision, monitoring, control, inspection safety and/or maintenance of the Premises, including, without limitation, the improvements and structures therein, the subject wood flooring, and the area where Plaintiff was injured.” (Id., ¶ 29.) Plaintiff alleges Defendants retained control because they “supervised and managed the construction and collaborated seamlessly every step of the way in construction”. (Id.)

Furthermore, the FAC alleges: “[t]he hazardous and unsafe condition of the subject wood flooring, Plaintiff’s work site and the Premises was latent and/or concealed in that their dangerous nature was not apparent to the Plaintiff or other persons similarly situated.” (FAC, ¶ 38.) “Defendants knew, or should have known, that such condition of the subject wood flooring/deck was unsafe and dangerous, and failed to take reasonable safety precautions or to provide adequate warnings or safeguards to prevent injury to the Plaintiff and others.” (Id., ¶ 42.)

First, Plaintiff fails to show that an affirmative defense cannot be the basis for a demurrer, especially given that Plaintiff cites cases involving demurrers on a statute of limitations defense. (See Opp., 4; Committee for Green Foothills v. Santa Clara County Bd. of Supervisors (2010) 48 Cal.4th 32, 42; Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403].)

Second, Plaintiff argues the face of the FAC does not show that Defendants hired Plaintiff’s employer. However, Plaintiff insufficiently explains how the allegation that “Plaintiff was working for a subcontractor (hired by some or all Defendants) to perform certain work at the Premises” (FAC, ¶ 27) precludes Privette. Based on this allegation, Plaintiff falls under the Privette doctrine because he worked for a subcontractor hired to perform work. (See Tverberg v. Fillner Construction, Inc. (2010) 49 Cal.4th 518, 528-29 [barring recovery for independent contractor hired by subcontractor]; Toland, supra, 18 Cal.4th at p. 270 [barring recovery for employee of subcontractor].)

Here, the FAC fails to specifically state facts that Defendants negligently exercised control over Plaintiff’s work, which affirmatively created or contributed to Plaintiff’s fall from the unsecured plywood.  On the issue of the concealed hazard exception to the Privette doctrine, which appears to be the theory pursued, as noted above, it applies if the hirer failed to warn of a hazard of which it knew, or should have known, the contractor did not know of the hazard, and the contractor could not have reasonably discovered this hazardous condition. (Kinsman, supra, 37 Cal.4th 659 at 664, fn. omitted.)  Under this exception, the hirer’s delegation of tort duties can be seen as “ineffective” because the independent contractor cannot protect its workers against a hazard it does not know about and could not reasonably discover.  (Sandoval v. Qualcomm Incorporated (2021) 12 Cal.5th 256, 271.)  Here, the FAC does not sufficiently allege facts that Defendants had knowledge of, or should have known of the unsecured plywood, and that it was not readily apparent to Plaintiff or the subcontractor he was working for.   

Therefore, because the FAC states insufficient facts of the Hooker and Kinsman exceptions, the demurrer is sustained with leave to amend.

RULING

Defendants’ demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND.

Alison Zatta vs William James Hill, et al.

 

Motion:                                   Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two and Request for Monetary Sanctions

Moving Party:                         Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc.  

Responding Party:                   None

Tentative Ruling:                    Grant

 

BACKGROUND

 

On February 15, 2022, Plaintiff Alison Zatta (“Plaintiff”) filed a complaint against Defendants William James Hill, Uber Technologies, Raiser LLC, Raiser-CA LLC, and Does 1 to 50 for negligence based on a motor vehicle accident that occurred on February 18, 2020.

 

On May 14, 2024, Defendant Uber Technologies, Inc. (“Defendant”) served Plaintiff with Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two. (Sharma Decl. ¶ 3, Exh. 1.) Responses were due June 15, 2024. Plaintiff failed to serve timely responses.

 

After Defendant filed a motion to compel, Plaintiff served responses on September 26, 2024. (Sharma Decl. ¶ 7.)

 

On November 6, 2024, Defendant filed the instant motion, seeking to compel further responses to Requests for Production of Documents, Set Two, numbers 4 to 6. Defendant also seeks monetary sanctions. No opposition has been filed.

 

MOVING PARTY POSITION

 

Defendant argues the responses to Requests 4-6 are deficient because the objections lack merit and fail to show how the work product objection applies.

 

OPPOSITION

           

            No opposition has been filed.  

 

REPLY


No reply has been filed.           

ANALYSIS

I. Compel Further Responses to Request for Production

Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(a) provides that on receipt of a response to a request for production of documents, the demanding party may move for an order compelling further responses if:¿ 

¿ 

(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete.¿ 

(2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.¿ 

(3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.¿¿ 

 

A response consisting of an objection must specify with particularity the document being objected to, and, if based on privilege, must provide sufficient factual information for other parties to evaluate the merits of this claim, including if necessary, a privilege log. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240 (b), (c).) 

¿ 

The motion must set forth specific facts showing good cause for the discovery and include a meet and confer declaration. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(b).)

 

“Unless notice of this motion is given within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing, the demanding party waives any right to compel a further response to the demand.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(c).)¿¿¿ 

 

A.    Meet and Confer

Defendant’s counsel, Pragya Sharma, declares that she sent two correspondences for Plaintiff to provide further responses, on October 9, 2024 and October 25, 2024. Plaintiff failed to respond to the meet and confer effort. (Sharma Decl. ¶ 8, Exh. 2, 3.) Therefore, the meet and confer requirement is met. 

B.     Requests 4-6

Requests 4-5 seek all contracts and agreements between Plaintiff or Plaintiff’s counsel, and Plaintiff’s medical treatment providers. Request 6 seeks all communications between Plaintiff’s counsel, anyone employed by counsel, and Plaintiff’s medical treatment providers.

In response, Plaintiff asserted the following objections to each Request:

“Objection: The request is oppressive and burdensome because it is vague, ambiguous, and unintelligible so as to make a response impossible without speculation as to the meaning of the question. Consequently the question is oppressive and harassing and without a foundational showing of competence. . . . The request seeks information which is protected from disclosure by the attorney’s work product privilege.”

The discovery requests appear to seek relevant materials. Additionally, they are not so vague and ambiguous that Plaintiff cannot reasonably respond. Plaintiff also has not filed an opposition and fails to set forth evidence that the documents are subject to the work product doctrine. Moreover, the record shows that the responses were untimely; therefore, any privilege or objection based on work product has been waived. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).)

In light of the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s objections are without merit. Accordingly, the motion to compel further is granted. 

C.    Sanctions

The court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310 (h).)

 

Here, Defendant seeks $600 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff, $400 of which was incurred in bringing the moving papers and $200 for preparing a reply and appearance at the hearing. (See Sharma Decl. ¶ 9.) Although no reply was necessary, the Court still finds the requested sanctions to be reasonable.  Therefore, the Court grants $600 in monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.

 

RULING

Defendant’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, numbers 4-6 is GRANTED. Plaintiff shall provide further responses within 20 days.

The Court further grants Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions in the amount of $600 against Plaintiff. Said monetary sanctions shall be paid to counsel for Defendant within 20 days.

Case Number: 24NNCV02267    Hearing Date: January 3, 2025    Dept: T

Antonio Ruvulcaba vs Volkswagen Group of America, Inc., et al.

Motion: Motion to Disqualify The Lemon Pros, LLP 
Moving Party: Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc.  
Responding Party: Plaintiff Antonio Ruvulcaba 
Tentative Ruling: Deny 

BACKGROUND

This is a Song-Beverly action which was filed on June 14, 2024 by Plaintiff Antonio Ruvulcaba (“Plaintiff”). The case involves a 2018 Volkswagen Atlas, which Plaintiff purchased on February 17, 2019. (Complaint ¶ 13.) Plaintiff is represented by counsel from the firm The Lemon Pros, LLP.  

 Defendant Volkswagen Group of America, Inc. (“Defendant”) now moves to disqualify The Lemon Pros, LLP from representing Plaintiff. Plaintiff opposes and Defendant replies. 

MOVING PARTY POSITION
 
Defendant argues that The Lemon Pros. LLP should be disqualified because its attorney Anthony Paul Greco, is a former attorney of Defendant, who obtained confidential and privileged information about Defendant’s litigation strategies. Defendant has used the firm Squire Patton Boggs (US) LLC (“Squire”) to defend against Song-Beverly actions since 2004; Squire formerly employed Anthony Paul Greco (“Greco”) as an attorney for almost three years. Defendant argues that while working for Squire, Greco defended more than 100 lemon law cases for Volkswagen and was highly involved in developing and implementing litigation strategies. 

Defendant also argues the conflict should be imputed to the entire firm because Greco substantially participated in Defendant’s representation, and it received no notice of Greco’s representation afterward. 

OPPOSITION

Plaintiff argues the motion should be denied because Defendant fails show that the current representation is substantially similar to Greco’s former representation. 

Next, Plaintiff contends even if there is a conflict, it should not be imputed to The Lemon Pros. LLP because Greco was properly screened from the instant action and any cases involving Defendant. 

REPLY

Defendant argues Plaintiff’s counsel sent a letter after this motion was filed, purporting to comply with the ethical screening procedures, and questions its veracity.   
SUR-REPLY

On December 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a document asking the Court to strike Defendant’s Reply Declaration of Sean P. Conboy. If the Court will consider the Conboy declaration, Plaintiff asks for leave to file a sur-reply. Plaintiff also separately filed evidentiary objections to the Conboy Reply Declaration. 

ANALYSIS

I. Legal Standard for Motion to Disqualify  

A. Attorney Disqualification 

“Code of Civil Procedure section 128, subdivision (a)(5) gives courts the power to order a lawyer's disqualification…[A] trial court's decision concerning a disqualification motion will not be disturbed absent an abuse of discretion…’” (See DCH Health Services Corp. v. Waite (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 829, 831-832.)¿¿ 
 
The duty of loyalty is at stake when an attorney’s representation of one client is adverse to the interests of another current client.¿(Flatt v. Superior Court (1994) 9 Cal.4th 275, 284.)¿“Disqualification in cases of successive representation is based on the prohibition against ‘employment adverse to a [. . .] former client where, by reason of the representation of the [. . .] former client, the [attorney] has obtained confidential information material to the employment.’”¿(See H.F. Ahmanson & Company v. Salomon Brothers, Inc. (1991) 229 Cal.App.3d 1445, 1451.)¿¿ 
 
A former client need not establish that the attorney actually possesses confidential information to disqualify the former attorney.¿ (H.F. Ahmanson & Co., supra, 229 Cal.App.3d at 1452.)¿ “It is enough to show a ‘substantial relationship’ between the former and current representation.”¿ (Id.)¿ “It is the possibility of the breach of confidence, not the fact of an actual breach that triggers disqualification.”¿ (In re Marriage of Zimmerman (1993) 16 Cal.App.4th 556, 563; H.F. Ahmanson & Co., supra, 229 Cal.App.3d at 1452.)¿¿¿ 
 
“[T]he attorney’s possession of confidential information will be presumed only when ‘a substantial relationship has been shown to exist between the former representation and the current representation, and when it appears by virtue of the nature of the former representation or the relationship of the attorney to his former client confidential information material to the current dispute would normally have been imparted to the attorney . . . .’”¿ (H.F. Ahmanson & Co., supra, 229 Cal.App.3d at 1454.)¿ Disqualification of the attorney from representing the second client is mandatory where the substantial relationship test is met and access to confidential information is presumed.¿ (Flatt v. Superior Court (1994) 9 Cal.4th 275, 283.)¿

“[T]he question whether an attorney should be disqualified in a successive representation case turns on two variables: (1) the relationship between the legal problem involved in the former representation and the legal problem involved in the current representation, and (2) the relationship between the attorney and the former client with respect to the legal problem involved in the former representation.” (Jessen v. Hartford Casualty Ins. Co. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.) “If the relationship between the attorney and the former client is shown to have been direct—that is, where the lawyer was personally involved in providing legal advice and services to the former client—then it must be presumed that confidential information has passed to the attorney and there cannot be any delving into the specifics of the communications between the attorney and the former client in an effort to show that the attorney did or did not receive confidential information during the course of that relationship. As a result, disqualification will depend upon the strength of the similarities between the legal problem involved in the former representation and the legal problem involved in the current representation.” (Id.)
 
“[A] ‘substantial relationship’ exists whenever the ‘subjects’ of the prior and the current representations are linked in some rational manner.”¿ (Jessen v. Hartford Casualty Ins. Co. (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 698, 711 (citing Flatt, supra, 9 Cal.4th at 283).)¿ The “subject” of a representation includes “information material to the evaluation, prosecution, settlement or accomplishment of the litigation or transaction given its specific legal and factual issues.”¿ (Id. at 712-13.)¿ “[S]uccessive representations will be ‘substantially related’ when the evidence before the trial court supports a rational conclusion that information material to the evaluation, prosecution, settlement or accomplishment of the former representation given its factual and legal issues is also material to the evaluation, prosecution, settlement or accomplishment of the current representation given its factual and legal issues.”¿ (Id. at 713.)¿¿However, “Jessen did not adopt a ‘playbook approach’ to the substantial relationship test or create ‘a lifetime prohibition against representation adverse to a former client.’ [Citation.]” (Khani v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 916, 921.) “The attorney's acquisition of general information about the former client's ‘overall structure and practices’ would not of itself require disqualification unless it were found to be ‘material’—i.e., directly in issue or of critical importance—in the second representation. [Citation.] The same is true about information such as the first client's ‘litigation philosophy’ or ‘key decision makers.’ [Citation.]” (Id. [internal citations omitted].) 

B. Firm Disqualification
 
“[W]hether disqualification of the entire firm is automatic is an open question.”¿ (California Self-Insurers’ Security Fund v. Superior Court (2018) 19 Cal.App.5th 1065, 1077.)¿ As was noted by the Court of Appeal in Kirk v. First American Title Ins. Co. (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 776 and discussed in California Self-Insurers’ Security Fund, the case law regarding whether vicarious disqualification is automatic and absolute is mixed.¿ (California Self-Insurers’ Security Fund, supra, 19 Cal.App.5th at 1075; Kirk, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 799.)¿ 

The Kirk Court summarized the history of the law of vicarious disqualification and found that Flatt did not create an absolute rule of vicarious disqualification and that “the state of the law is as initially expressed by the appellate courts: (1) a case-by-case analysis based on the circumstances present in, and the policy interests implicated by, the case; (2) tempered by the Henriksen [v. Great American Savings & Loan (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 109] rule that vicarious disqualification should be automatic in cases of a tainted attorney possessing actual confidential information from a representation, who switches sides in the same case.”¿ (Kirk, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 800; California Self-Insurers’ Security Fund, supra, 19 Cal.App.5th at 1076 (quoting Kirk).)¿ The Kirk Court further indicated that vicarious disqualification is the general rule and that courts should presume knowledge is imputed to all members of a tainted attorney’s law firm, but that, “in the proper circumstances, the presumption is a rebuttable one, which can be refuted by evidence that ethical screening will effectively prevent the sharing of confidences in a particular case.’”¿ (Kirk, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 801; California Self-Insurers’ Security Fund, supra, 19 Cal.App.5th at 1076 (quoting Kirk).)¿ 

“The showing must satisfy the trial court that the [tainted attorney] has not had and will not have any involvement with the litigation, or any communication with attorneys or [ ] employees concerning the litigation, that would support a reasonable inference that the information has been used or disclosed.” (Kirk, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 810.) “The specific elements of an effective screen will vary from case to case, although two elements are necessary: First, the screen must be timely imposed; a firm must impose screening measures when the conflict first arises. It is not sufficient to wait until the trial court imposes screening measures as part of its order on the disqualification motion.” (Ibid.) “Second, it is not sufficient to simply produce declarations stating that confidential information was not conveyed or that the disqualified attorney did not work on the case; an effective wall involves the imposition of preventive measures to guarantee that information will not be conveyed.” (Ibid.) “The typical elements of an ethical wall are: [1] physical, geographic, and departmental separation of attorneys; [2] prohibitions against and sanctions for discussing confidential matters; [3] established rules and procedures preventing access to confidential information and files; [4] procedures preventing a disqualified attorney from sharing in the profits from the representation; and [5] continuing education in professional responsibility.” (Id. at 810-11.) However, not all elements need to be established. (Id. at 811.) 

II. Request for Judicial Notice

Matters are ordinarily subject to judicial notice when they are “reasonably beyond dispute.” (Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp.¿(2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 97, 113.) As a result, a court can take judicial notice of the existence of a document. However, it cannot take judicial notice of the truth of statements within that document if they are reasonably disputable. (Id.) The Court of Appeal has approached judicial notice of deposition testimony differently. Some courts have held that the truth of statements within deposition transcripts can never be judicially noticed. (Garcia v. Sterling¿(1985) 176 Cal.App.3d 17, 22.) Others have held statements within deposition transcripts can be judicially noticed only if they are statements by the plaintiff or his agent which are inconsistent with the pleadings. (Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.) A third approach allows judicial notice “only in those instances where there is not or cannot be a factual dispute concerning that which is sought to be judicially noticed.” (Cruz v. County of Los Angeles¿(1985) 173 Cal.App.3d 1131, 1134; Joslin v. H.A.S. Ins. Brokerage¿(1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 369, 374–75.)  
The Court grants the request for judicial notice of the existence of exhibits A and C as court records. (Evid. Code, § 452, subd. (d).) The Court denies the request for the judicial notice of exhibits B, D, and E because the information contained within are not reasonably beyond dispute. 

III. Evidentiary Objections

The Court rules as follows on Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to the Declaration of Sean Conboy: Objection 1 is sustained. Objections 2 through 17 are overruled. 

Declaration of Nicholas Johnson: Objections 18 to 26 are overruled. Objection 27 is sustained.

IV. Discussion

Here, Greco represented Defendant in more than 100 cases from July 2021 to March 2024 in Song-Beverly and other breach of warranty actions. (Johnson Decl. ¶ 2-3; Conboy Decl. ¶ 4.) Defendant’s Senior Counsel, Breach of Warranty and Single Incidents, Nicholas Johnson, also declares that he regularly communicated with Greco several times a day. (Johnson Decl. ¶ 5.) Greco also attended and led meetings to discuss Defendant’s strategy. (Id. ¶ 6.) Johnson further declares that “[Greco] handled every aspect of Volkswagen’s defense on the matters where he was the day-to-day handling. He developed and recommended strategies, reviewed confidential and privileged documents, prepared responses to discovery, prepared Volkswagen corporate representatives to testify at depositions (and defended the depositions), and prepared discovery and dispositive motions.” (Id. ¶ 7.)

Greco also helped prepare Defendant’s corporate executives and experts for depositions. (Johnson Decl. ¶ 9-11.) Notably, in January 2024, Greco attended and participated in a national conference at Volkswagen’s headquarters which was held to exchange nationwide litigation strategies for breach of warranty cases. (Id. ¶ 16.)

In opposition, Plaintiff provides a declaration from Greco, stating that after viewing the complaint in this case, he was never involved in any case involving the same vehicle or repairs at issue. (Greco Decl. ¶ 3.) 

The Court finds there is evidence that Greco primarily worked on Song-Beverly and breach of warranty actions; Plaintiff does not dispute this fact, nor disputes that Greco represented Defendant from July 2021 to March 2024. Therefore, this matter involves a successive relationship since it is undisputed that Greco represented Defendant in previous actions. Therefore, the issue in this motion turns on whether the “evidence before the trial court supports a rational conclusion that information material to the evaluation, prosecution, settlement or accomplishment of the former representation given its factual and legal issues is also material to the evaluation, prosecution, settlement or accomplishment of the current representation given its factual and legal issues.” (See Jessen, supra, 111 Cal.App.4th at p. 713.) 

Plaintiff relies on Khani v. Ford Motor Co. (2013) 215 Cal.App.4th 916, where the Court of Appeal reversed the granting of a disqualification motion involving an attorney who had represented Ford in 150 cases over a three-year period, which included lemon law cases. (Id. at p. 919.) There, the court determined the evidence was insufficient for disqualification because Ford had not established that any confidential information obtained would be at issue in the present case, that the same vehicle was at issue, or that Ford had policies, practices, or procedures generally applicable to the evaluation, settlement or litigation of lemon law cases at the time that were unchanged. (Id. at p. 922.) The court then held that disqualifying an attorney because the former and current cases involve claims under the same statute, does not make them substantially related. (Id.) Moreover, the court concluded that disqualifying an attorney based on playbook information in prior lemon law cases is insufficient without showing its materiality. (Id.) 

Considering the principles discussed in Khani, Defendant’s declarations offer few specific facts of the legal and factual issues in this case, and the over 100 cases that Greco defended between July 2021 to March 2024. Instead, the declarations focus on generalizations, such as “Anthony Greco has knowledge of privileged and confidential information that will be directly relevant to this litigation.” (Johnson Decl. ¶ 20.) However, Greco defended the depositions of Defendant’s person most qualified and expert Jeffrey Calhoun in the Pittacora and Shah cases respectively. The Court took judicial notice of the complaints in these cases which both alleged Song-Beverly violations; Pittacora involved a 2022 Volkswagen Atlas and Shah involved a 2021 Volkswagen Golf. The vehicle at issue in this case is a 2018 Volkswagen Atlas. Still, here, there is likely insufficient evidence of the specific factual and legal issues; like in Khani, the general facts that Greco received Defendant’s playbook information from defending previous Song-Beverly actions is insufficient without showing it is material to this case. 

Nevertheless, even if the declarations contained sufficient facts, the motion would still be denied because Plaintiff shows that Greco was properly screened. Here, Plaintiff sets forth declarations from Michael Saeedian and Jorge L. Acosta, both partners at The Lemon Pros, LLP. Saeedian declares that Greco was hired on October 14, 2024. (Saeedian Decl. ¶ 2.) Although Saeedian determined there was no conflict issue, out of an abundance of caution, Greco was screened from all matters involving Volkswagen. (Id.) Saeedian further declares that Greco is “categorically excluded from all communications, discussions, and meetings regarding cases involving Volkswagen.” (Id. ¶ 4.) Greco has his own private office with a door and his own paralegal who also does not have access to any cases involving Volkswagen. (Id. ¶ 5.) Two days after Greco was hired, Saeedian sent a firm-wide email informing all attorneys and staff of these screening measures and prohibiting them from showing or discussing any cases involving Volkswagen with Greco. (Id. ¶ 6.) Greco also does not receive any portion of fees received from cases involving Volkswagen. (Id. ¶ 7.) The Declaration of Jorge Acosta also confirms that on October 14, 2024, he was instructed to restrict Greco’s access to cases involving Defendant within the firm’s case management software. (Acosta Decl. ¶ 7.) Greco has confirmed this information in his own declaration. (Greco Decl. ¶ 4-7.) 
Based on the foregoing information, the Court concludes that the screening measures were sufficient. (See Kirk, supra, 183 Cal.App.4th at 810-11.) Plaintiff’s evidence shows Greco was timely screened when he was hired, that he is physically separated from other attorneys in the firm with his own office and paralegal, that all staff were informed not to discuss cases involving Defendant with Greco, that Greco cannot share in profits related to Defendant’s cases, and that Greco is prevented from accessing information about Defendant’s cases within the firm’s management software. Therefore, the elements enumerated in Kirk are sufficiently shown; Plaintiff has rebutted the presumption that any of Greco’s knowledge is imputed to the firm. Though Defendant questions whether the screening procedures described were actually applied, it offers no direct contrary evidence. Therefore, the motion to disqualify is denied.

Given the disposition of the ruling, the Court denies Plaintiff’s request for leave to file a sur-reply and declines to rule on Plaintiff’s evidentiary objections to Defendant’s reply declaration. 

RULING

Defendant’s Motion to Disqualify The Lemon Pros, LLP is DENIED.
