DEPARTMENT B LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV00815    Hearing Date: September 9, 2024    Dept: B

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. B

 

JOSE GOMEZ,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

23TRCV00815

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] RULING

 

 

MIGUEL HERNANDEZ,

 

 

 

Defendant.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                         September 9, 2024

 

Moving Parties:                      Defendant Miguel Hernandez

Responding Party:                  None

(1)   Motion to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories

(2)   Motion to Compel Responses to Production of Documents and Things

 

            The Court considered the moving papers.  No opposition was filed.

RULING

            The motions are GRANTED.

Plaintiff Jose Gomez is ordered to respond without objections to defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set One) within twenty days.

Plaintiff Jose Gomez is ordered (1) to serve on defendant a verified response without objections to defendant’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) and (2) to produce all documents and things in plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to defendant’s request, within twenty days.  

 

 

The Court orders that plaintiff Jose Gomez and plaintiff’s attorney of record, Jesse L. Halpern, Esq. (SBN 37962), jointly and severally, pay $620 in sanctions to defendant, within 30 days.

BACKGROUND

On March 20, 2023, plaintiff Jose Gomez filed a complaint against Miguel Hernandez for motor vehicle negligence based on an incident that occurred on June 9, 2021 in Inglewood.  

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Interrogatories

            If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  CCP §2030.290(b).  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905-906. 

            Request for Production of Documents

            Where there has been no timely response to a CCP §2031.010 demand, the demanding party must seek an order compelling a response.  CCP §2031.300.  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  So, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded.  There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses.  Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.  Where the motion seeks only a response to the inspection demand, no showing of "good cause" is required.  Weil & Brown, Civil Procedure Before Trial, 8:1487. 

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Miguel Hernandez requests that the Court compel plaintiff Jose Gomez to respond to the initial form interrogatories and request for production of documents.

            Defendant asserts that on April 28, 2023, defendant propounded discovery requests on plaintiff.  Responses were due by May 30, 2023.  On December 12, 2023, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter to plaintiff’s counsel but did not receive a response.  As of the filing date of the motions, defense counsel had not received responses.

            The Court finds that the discovery requests were properly served, and that plaintiff failed to serve responses by the due date and thus has waived objections.  Further, although not required, defense counsel sent a meet and confer letter to plaintiff’s counsel.

The motions are thus GRANTED.  

            Sanctions

Under CCP § 2023.030(a), “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. . . . If a monetary sanction is authorized by any provision of this title, the court shall impose that sanction unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  Under CCP § 2023.010, an example of the misuse of the discovery process is “(d) Failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.”

Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel unless the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.”  CCP §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).

Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1348(a) states:  “The court may award sanctions under the Discovery Act in favor of a party who files a motion to compel discovery, even though no opposition to the motion was filed, or opposition to the motion was withdrawn, or the requested discovery was provided to the moving party after the motion was filed.”

Defendant request $1310 for each motion.  The Court finds that $620 ($250/hr. x 2 hrs., $120 in filing fees) is a reasonable amount to be awarded against plaintiff and plaintiff’s attorney of record in total for both motions.

ORDER

The motions are GRANTED.

Plaintiff Jose Gomez is ordered to respond without objections to defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set One) within twenty days.

Plaintiff Jose Gomez is ordered (1) to serve on defendant a verified response without objections to defendant’s Request for Production of Documents (Set One) and (2) to produce all documents and things in plaintiff’s possession, custody, or control, which are responsive to defendant’s request, within twenty days.  

The Court orders that plaintiff Jose Gomez and plaintiff’s attorney of record, Jesse L. Halpern, Esq. (SBN 37962) pay $620 in sanctions to defendant, within 30 days.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

RULING

BACKGROUND

            On June 5, 2024, plaintiff Willow List filed a complaint against defendant Unify Financial Federal Credit Union for (1) violation of Civil Code §2923.5, (2) violation of Civil Code §2924.9, (3) wrongful foreclosure, and (4) unfair business practices, violation of Bus. & Prof. Code §17200.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Under CCP § 1281, a “written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and revocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”

Under CCP § 1281.2, “On petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: . . . (b) Grounds exist for rescission of the agreement. . . .”

“[W]hen a petition to compel arbitration is filed and accompanied by prima facie evidence of a written agreement to arbitrate the controversy, the court itself must determine whether the agreement exists and, if any defense to its enforcement is raised, whether it is enforceable.  Because the existence of the agreement is a statutory prerequisite to granting the petition, the petitioner bears the burden of proving its existence by a preponderance of the evidence.  If the party opposing the petition raises a defense to enforcement—either fraud in the execution voiding the agreement, or a statutory defense of waiver or revocation (see §1281.2(a), (b))—that party bears the burden of producing evidence of, and proving by a preponderance of the evidence, any fact necessary to the defense.”  Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal. 4th 394, 413.

Petitions to compel arbitration are summary proceedings in which the court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence.  Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal. 4th 951, 972.  Unlike a ruling on a motion for summary judgment, the Court is charged with resolving any factually disputed issues unless there is no evidentiary support for the claims of the party opposing the petition.  Id. at 973.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Unify Financial Federal Credit Union requests an order compelling plaintiff to binding arbitration and to stay the proceeding.

            The complaint alleges that on August 30, 2013, plaintiff obtained a mortgage loan on the subject property at 22934 Nadine Cir. B, Torrance from defendant in the amount of $250,000, memorialized by a Deed of Trust.  The maturity due date is September 1, 2043.  The trustee is T.D. Service Company.  Complaint, ¶9.  On June 2, 2023, a Notice of Delinquent Assessment against the property was recorded.  Id., ¶10.  On October 6, 2023, a Notice of Default and Election to Sell Under a Deed of Trust was recorded.  Id., ¶11.  On January 24, 2024, a Notice of Trustee’s Sale was recorded.  The sale date was set for February 27, 2024, was continued to May 2, 2024, and the property was sold on that date.  Id., ¶12.  Plaintiff was only working with the lender defendant.  In addition to the modification, plaintiff listed her home so she could get her property value.  The agent was working with defendant to get all the documents, and their agent insists he did just that.  Id., ¶13. 

            The complaint further alleges that plaintiff would have qualified for the Cares Act.  She is not fit to move her stuff herself as she is 69 years old and weights 82 pounds.  Plaintiff submitted the documents for defendant to review, and to discuss what was needed to move forward.  Plaintiff received nothing in writing and no acknowledgement letters.  Id., ¶14.  There was no SPOC.  They did try to get her to log in their system as the RMA is located on their web page.  Id., ¶15.  There was no 30-day approval or denial.  The realtor was made aware that the lender will not stop the date and that all the documents were not received.  Id., ¶16.

            Existence of an agreement to arbitrate

“As stated in Cione v. Foresters Equity Services, Inc. (1997) 58 Cal. App. 4th 625, 634 ‘The right to arbitration depends upon contract; a petition to compel arbitration is simply a suit in equity seeking specific performance of that contract.  There is no public policy favoring arbitration of disputes that the parties have not agreed to arbitrate.’”  Lopez v. Charles Schwab & Co., Inc. (2004) 118 Cal. App. 4th 1224, 1229.

Defendant asserts that there exists an arbitration agreement between the parties.  Defendant Credit Union explains that it is not a bank.  Rather it is a wholly member-owned, not-for-profit cooperative, which provides account and loan services to its members.  Defendant exists to serve its members’ financial interests.  Jack Jennings decl., ¶4.  Plaintiff became a member of the Credit Union in 1987.  Since joining, plaintiff has been an active member, having obtained a credit card and many loans through the Credit Union.  Jennings decl., ¶7.  On January 10, 2003, plaintiff signed a new Membership Application, agreeing to be subject to the Credit Union’s Truth-in-Savings and Electronic Services Disclosure and Agreement, including all amendments thereto.  Jennings decl., ¶8, Exh. 2.  On December 13, 2012, plaintiff executed a Membership Invitation/Signature Card, agreeing that her member account would be governed by the Credit Union’s Account Agreement.  Jennings decl., ¶9, Exh. 3.  The Credit Union has updated the Account Agreement on several occasions over the years.  In 2015, the Credit Union updated the Account Agreement to include an Arbitration Agreement.  Since 2015, the Credit Union has also updated the Arbitration Agreement.  Jennings decl., ¶11, Exh. 5.  Plaintiff was provided with the full text of the Arbitration Agreement when a copy of the Membership Booklet was mailed to each member on August 1, 2018.  Jennings decl., ¶13.

The Arbitration Agreement states, in part (at para. 39):  “You and the Credit Union agree that we shall attempt to informally settle any and all disputes arising out of, affecting, or relating to your accounts, or the products or services the Credit Union has provided, will provide or has offered to provide to you, and/or any aspect of your relationship with the Credit Union (hereafter referred to as the ‘Claims’).  If that cannot be done, then you agree that any and all Claims that are threatened, made, filed or initiated after the Effective Date (defined below) of this arbitration section, even if the Claims arise out of, affect or relate to conduct that occurred prior to the Effective Date, shall, at the election of either you or us, be resolved by binding arbitration administered by the American Arbitration Association (‘AAA’) in accordance with its applicable rules and procedures for consumer disputes (‘Rules’), whether such Claims are in contract, tort, statute, or otherwise.”

            Defendant argues that plaintiff accepted the Credit Union’s offer to arbitrate by failing to opt out and continuing to use her account and thereby consenting to the terms of the Arbitration Agreement.  Jennings decl., ¶¶15-16.

The Court finds that defendant has met its burden of proving the existence of an arbitration agreement.  

Scope

Defendant asserts that the complaint raises claims relating to plaintiff’s account and relationship with the Credit Union and thus the Arbitration Agreement encompasses plaintiff’s dispute.

The Court finds that the broadly-worded Arbitration Agreement (“any and all disputes arising out of, affecting, or relating to your accounts, or the products or services the Credit Union has provided, will provide or has offered to provide to you, and/or any aspect of your relationship with the Credit Union”) covers plaintiff’s causes of action as they arise out of her account, loan, and relationship with defendant.

Enforceability

Defendant argues that any claims that the Arbitration Agreement is unconscionable are meritless. 

There is no opposition.

The motion is GRANTED.

 

ORDER

            The motion is GRANTED.

            The case is STAYED.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.
