DEPARTMENT F47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F47, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2247.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 22CHCV01021    Hearing Date: May 23, 2024    Dept: F47

Dept. F47

Date: 5/23/24                                                                          

Case #22CHCV01021

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY’S FEES

 

Motion filed on 11/29/23.

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Los Angeles Federal Credit Union

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Jessica J. Martin aka Jessica Jna Baker

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: An order awarding Plaintiff attorney’s fees in the amount of $16,975.00 as the prevailing party after judgment on a contract claim. 

 

RULING: The motion is granted as set forth below.    

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arose out of Plaintiff Los Angeles Federal Credit Union’s (Plaintiff) claims that Defendant Jessica J. Martin aka Jessica Jna Baker (Defendant) defaulted on two loans made by Plaintiff to Defendant.  On 10/31/22, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendant for: (1) Breach of Contract, (2) Money Lent, (3) Claim and Delivery, (4) Breach of Contract and (5) Money Lent.  On 1/5/23, Defendant, representing herself, filed an answer to the complaint.

 

On 11/13/23, Defendant did not appear for trial and judgment was entered in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant in the principal amount of $63,731.20, plus 4% interest, as to the vehicle loan; and for the principal amount of $4,094.39 plus 12% interest, as to the personal line of credit loan.  (See 11/13/23 Minute Order; 11/28/23 Judgment).  Additionally, the Court ordered that costs were to be determined by the timely filing of a Memorandum of Costs and attorneys’ fees to be determined/awarded per motion.  Id.

 

On 11/29/23, Plaintiff filed and served the instant motion seeking an order awarding Plaintiff attorney’s fees in the amount of $16,975.00 as the prevailing party after judgment on a contract claim.  Defendant has not opposed or otherwise responded to the motion. 

 

ANALYSIS

 

Civil Code 1717 provides, in relevant part:

 

“(a) In any action on a contract, where the contract specifically provides that attorney's fees and costs, which are incurred to enforce that contract, shall be awarded either to one of the parties or to the prevailing party, then the party who is determined to be the party prevailing on the contract, whether he or she is the party specified in the contract or not, shall be entitled to reasonable attorney's fees in addition to other costs.

Where a contract provides for attorney's fees, as set forth above, that provision shall be construed as applying to the entire contract, unless each party was represented by counsel in the negotiation and execution of the contract, and the fact of that representation is specified in the contract.

 

Reasonable attorney's fees shall be fixed by the court, and shall be an element of the costs of suit.

 

Attorney's fees provided for by this section shall not be subject to waiver by the parties to any contract which is entered into after the effective date of this section. Any provision in any such contract which provides for a waiver of attorney's fees is void.

 

(b)(1) The court, upon notice and motion by a party, shall determine who is the party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section, whether or not the suit proceeds to final judgment. Except as provided in paragraph (2), the party prevailing on the contract shall be the party who recovered a greater relief in the action on the contract. The court may also determine that there is no party prevailing on the contract for purposes of this section.” 

 

When a party obtains an unqualified victory by completely prevailing on, or defeating, all contract claims and the contract provides for attorneys’ fees, Civil Code 1717 entitles the prevailing party to recover reasonable attorneys’ fees.  Scott Co. of California (1999) 20 C4th 1103, 1109; Hsu (1995) 9 C4th 863, 865-866

 

Both of the contracts which underlie the claims on which Plaintiff prevailed at trial include attorney fee provisions which provide that Defendant agrees to pay for all court costs and reasonable attorney’s fees incurred in the course of collecting amounts owed under the contracts.  (See Anaya Decl. ¶3, Ex.A ¶8, Ex.B, p.2 “Collection Costs”).

 

As the prevailing party on the contract claims based on contracts which contained attorney fee provisions, Plaintiff is entitled to recover its reasonable attorney’s fees.

 

The Court finds that the $350 per hour claimed by Plaintiff’s counsel to be reasonable.  (Anaya Decl. ¶4).  However, the Court finds that Plaintiff is not reasonably entitled to recover the fees incurred in relation to Plaintiff’s failed motion for summary judgment and failed motion to compel deposition.  (See Anaya Decl. ¶5; 6/27/23 Minute Order denying MSJ; 9/21/23 Minute Order denying Motion to Compel Further Deposition).  As such, the Court has deducted 13.4 hours from the 48.5 hours claimed.  Plaintiff is awarded $12,285.00 in reasonable attorney’s fees (35.1 hours multiplied by $350/hour).  (See Anaya Decl. ¶5).

 

CONCLUSION

 

The motion is granted.  Plaintiff is awarded $12,285.00 in attorney’s fees against Defendant.

DEMURRER & MOTION TO STRIKE TO THE ORIGINAL COMPLAINT

 

Demurrer & Motion to Strike filed on 2/2/24.

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant PMI Cielo LLC

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Jana Burkholder

NOTICE: ok

 

Demurrer is to the 1st-6th and 8th-15th causes of action:

            1.  Tortious Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability – Violations of Civil Code 1941

            2.  Breach of Contract – Civil Code 3300, et seq.

            3.  Breach of Quiet Enjoyment – Civil Code 1927

            4.  Private Nuisance – Civil Code 3501, et seq.

            5.  Premises Liability – Civil Code 1714

            6.  Retaliation

            7.  Negligence

            8.  Retaliatory Eviction

            9.  Constructive Eviction

            10. Violation of Civil Code 1940.2

            11. Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

            12. Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress

            13. Violation of Business and Professions Code 17200, et seq.

            14. Fraud

            15. Violation of Civil Code 1950.5

 

RELIEF REQUESTED IN MOTION TO STRIKE: An order striking portions of the complaint regarding punitive damages; physical and mental discomfort in the 2nd cause of action; and request for monetary damages in the 13th cause of action.

 

RULING: The demurrer is overruled, in part, sustained with leave to amend, in part, and sustained without leave to amend, in part, as set forth below.  The motion to strike is granted without leave to amend.  A First Amended Complaint is due within 30 days. 

 

SUMMARY OF FACTS & PROCEDURAL HISTORY

 

This action arises out of Plaintiff Jana Burkholder’s (Plaintiff) tenancy from on or about 3/28/20 to on or about 5/15/23 in an apartment allegedly owned, controlled and/or managed by Defendant PMI Cielo LLC (Defendant).  Plaintiff alleges that during her tenancy the apartment suffered from numerous habitability issues that negatively impacted her health and eventually led to her moving out of the apartment.

 

On 9/12/23, Plaintiff filed this action against Defendant for: (1) Tortious Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability – Violations of Civil Code 1941; (2) Breach of Contract – Civil Code 3300, et seq.; (3) Breach of Quiet Enjoyment – Civil Code 1927; (4) Private Nuisance – Civil Code 3501, et seq.; (5) Premises Liability – Civil Code 1714; (6) Retaliation; (7) Negligence;

(8) Retaliatory Eviction; (9) Constructive Eviction; (10) Violation of Civil Code 1940.2;

(11) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (12) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (13) Violation of Business and Professions Code 17200, et seq.; (14) Fraud; and (15) Violation of Civil Code 1950.5.  The parties were unable to resolve the issues Defendant has with the complaint; therefore, on 2/2/24, Defendant filed and electronically served the instant demurrer to the 1st – 6th and 8th – 15th causes of action in the complaint on the grounds that the complaint fails to state sufficient facts to constitute any of these causes of action and each of these causes of action is uncertain.  CCP 430.10(e), (f).  Defendant also filed and served a motion to strike portions of the complaint regarding punitive damages (¶15, p.7:6; ¶66, p.13:21-23; ¶75, p.14:24-27; ¶94, p.17:8-9; ¶95, p.17:10-16; Prayer ¶C, p.18:7-8); physical and mental discomfort in the 2nd cause of action (¶28, p.9:3-5); and request for monetary damages in the 13th cause of action (¶86, p.16:5-11).

 

The demurrer and motion to strike were originally scheduled for hearing on 4/11/24.  On 4/3/24, 3 court days late, Plaintiff filed and electronically served an opposition to the demurrer and a non-opposition to the motion to strike.  See CCP 1005(b).  Due to the late filing and service of the opposition and lack of reply, the hearing on the demurrer and motion to strike were continued to 5/23/24 to allow for the filing of a reply.  On 5/8/24, Defendant filed and served a reply to the opposition to the demurrer. 

 

ANALYSIS    

 

As noted above, Plaintiff has filed a non-opposition to the motion to strike.  Additionally, in the opposition to the demurrer, Plaintiff withdraws the 8th, 10th, 12th and 13th causes of action in the complaint.  Therefore, the Court will not address those arguments in the analysis.

 

1st cause of action – Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

 

In the demurrer/moving papers, Defendants cites no authority to support its argument that Plaintiff must specify which of the two lease agreements alleged in the complaint underlies this cause of action.  (See Demurrer, p.11:20-5).  Further, based on the allegations in the complaint regarding when certain conditions occurred which are incorporated into the cause of action, it can be discerned which lease was in place during the conditions complained of.  (See Complaint ¶¶9-11, 17-18, 20).     

 

2nd cause of action – Breach of Contract

 

The elements of a breach of contract cause of action are: (1) the existence of a contract, (2) the plaintiff’s performance or excuse for non-performance, (3) defendant’s breach and (4) resulting damage.  Otworth (1985) 166 CA3d 452, 459. 

 

Defendant concedes that Plaintiff has alleged the existence of two lease agreements which are attached as Exhibits A and B to the complaint.  (See Demurrer, p.12:18).  As with the breach of implied warranty of habitability claim, based on the allegations regarding when certain conditions occurred which are incorporated into this cause of action, Defendant can ascertain which lease/contract is at issue for each complaint.  (See Complaint ¶¶9-11, 25).  Similarly, these incorporated allegations are sufficiently specific to establish how Defendant allegedly breached the contracts/leases at issue.  (Id. and ¶27).

 

3rd cause of action – Breach of Quiet Enjoyment

 

As with the 1st and 2nd causes of action, the allegations incorporated into this cause of action are sufficient to establish which of the two leases underlie this claim.  It is also noted that the second lease renewed on a month-to-month basis after the expiration of the fixed term and, therefore, was in place when Plaintiff alleges she was constructively evicted on 5/15/23.  (See Complaint , Ex.B ¶3).  Since this cause of action did not arise until Plaintiff’s alleged constructive eviction, it is not entirely duplicative of the breach of contract claim.  Petroleum Collections, Inc. (1975) 48 CA3d 841, 846. 

 

4th cause of action – Private Nuisance

 

The basis for the private nuisance cause of action is unclear.  The complaint cites civil code 3501 which merely sets forth the remedies (a civil action or abatement) for a private nuisance.  The opposition contends that the claim is brought pursuant to Civil Code 1927 which already forms the basis for the 3rd cause of action.  (See Opposition, p.4:24-25).  The allegations in the cause of action also appear to be based on the same allegations as the 3rd cause of action making the claim unnecessarily duplicative.  See El Escorial Owners’ Association (2007) 154 CA4th 1337, 1348-1349; (Complaint ¶¶34-36).   

 

5th cause of action – Premises Liability

 

Plaintiff concedes that the elements of a premises liability cause of action are the same as for negligence: duty, breach, causation and damages.  (See Opposition, p.5:14-15).  Plaintiff fails to address the fact that the allegations in the premises liability cause of action are essentially the same those in the 7th cause of action for negligence, which is not challenged in this demurrer.  As such, the 5th cause of action appears to be unnecessarily duplicative.  See El Escorial Owners’ Association, supra.    

 

6th cause of action – Retaliation

 

While Plaintiff has withdrawn the 8th cause of action for retaliatory eviction, it appears that Plaintiff intends to state such a claim within the 6th cause of action as the opposition argues that “California recognizes two independent sources for the doctrine of retaliatory eviction, one statutory and one common law.”  (See Opposition, p.5:28-p.6:1).  Plaintiff then cites to Civil Code 1942.5(d) (mistakenly referred to as 1945(d) in the opposition) which provides:

 

“Notwithstanding subdivision (a), it is unlawful for a lessor to increase rent, decrease services, cause a lessee to quit involuntarily, bring an action to recover possession, or threaten to do any of those acts, for the purpose of retaliating against the lessee because the lessee has lawfully organized or participated in a lessees' association or an organization advocating lessees' rights or has lawfully and peaceably exercised any rights under the law. In an action brought by or against the lessee pursuant to this subdivision, the lessee shall bear the burden of producing evidence that the lessor's conduct was, in fact, retaliatory.”

 

However, in the 6th cause of action, Plaintiff cites Civil Code 1942.5(a) and (c).  (See Complaint, p.11:7-8).  As such, the basis for the claim is unclear – is Plaintiff making a claim for retaliatory/constructive eviction or is Plaintiff claiming Defendant retaliated against her is some other way (i.e., Civil Code 1942.5(c) relates to reporting or threatening to report the lessee or individuals associated with the lessee to immigration).   

 

9th cause of action – Constructive Eviction

 

While the opposition argues that this cause of action is a separate and independent cause of action brought pursuant to Civil Code 1940.2, based on the allegations in the complaint, it is not clear how this claim differs from the 6th cause of action which Plaintiff claims in the opposition is one for retaliatory eviction.  As such, the claims appear to be duplicative. 

 

11th cause of action – Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress

 

The elements of an intentional infliction of emotional distress cause of action are: (1) extreme and outrageous conduct by the defendant; (2) the defendant’s intention of causing or reckless disregard of the probability of causing emotional distress; (3) the plaintiff’s suffering severe or extreme emotional distress; and (4) actual and proximate causation of the emotional distress injuries by the defendant's outrageous conduct.  Hughes (2009) 46 C4th 1035, 1050.  To be considered “severe,” the emotional distress must be “of such substantial quantity or enduring quality that no reasonable (person) in a civilized society should be expected to endure it.” Fletcher (1970) 10 CA3d 376, 397.  To be considered “outrageous,” a defendant’s conduct must be so extreme as to exceed all bounds tolerated in a civilized community.  Id.

 

Plaintiff has failed to allege facts which show the type of extreme and outrageous conduct by Defendant which is necessary to support a claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  As noted above, the bases for Plaintiff’s retaliation/retaliatory eviction and constructive eviction claims are uncertain.  Additionally, Plaintiff has alleged that Defendant attempted repairs regarding habitability complaints.

 

14th cause of action – Fraud

 

The elements of a fraud cause of action are: (1) a knowingly false representation by the defendant; (2) an intent to deceive or induce reliance; (3) justifiable reliance by the plaintiff; and (4) resulting damages.  See Service by Medallion, Inc. (1996) 44 CA4th 1807, 1816; Wilhelm (1986) 186 CA3d 1324, 1332.  A fraud claim must be pled with factual specificity including facts showing “how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.” Lazar (1996) 12 C4th 631, 645.  As against a corporate entity, the plaintiff must plead facts alleging “the names of the persons who made the allegedly fraudulent representations, their authority to speak, to whom they spoke, what they said or wrote, and when it was said or written.”  Id. 

 

Even with the incorporation of the prior allegations, Plaintiff has failed to plead the fraud claim with the requisite factual specificity.  It is not clear exactly what misrepresentations were made to Plaintiff, when, who made the misrepresentations and that person’s or persons’ authority to speak on behalf of Defendant.  (See Complaint ¶90). 

 

15th cause of action – Violation of Civil Code 1950.5

 

Plaintiff merely alleges that Defendant has failed to return the security deposit in violation of Civil Code 1950.5 but fails to state what specific conduct of Defendant violated Civil Code 1950.5 (i.e., Did Defendant fail to provide Plaintiff with an itemized statement indicating why the security deposit was not returned? Was the itemization incorrect? Etc.)  See Civil Code 1950.5(b), (e), (g); (Complaint ¶¶16, 92).

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on Plaintiff’s withdrawal of these causes of action, the demurrer as to the 8th, 10th, 12th and 13th causes of action is sustained without leave to amend. 

 

The demurrer as to the 1st, 2nd and 3rd causes of action is overruled.

 

The demurrer as to the 4th, 5th, 6th, 9th, 11th, 14th and 15th causes of action is sustained with leave to amend.  Due to the liberal policy of allowing leave to amend and because this is only the original complaint, Plaintiff is given the opportunity to try to cure the defects in these causes of action.

 

Due to Plaintiff’s notice of non-opposition to the motion to strike, Defendant’s request to strike  portions of the complaint regarding punitive damages (¶15, p.7:6; ¶66, p.13:21-23; ¶75, p.14:24-27; ¶94, p.17:8-9; ¶95, p.17:10-16; Prayer ¶C, p.18:7-8); physical and mental discomfort in the 2nd cause of action (¶28, p.9:3-5); and request for monetary damages in the 13th cause of action (¶86, p.16:5-11) is granted without leave to amend.

 

A First Amended Complaint is due within 30 days.
