Background

 

Plaintiffs Marci Higer (“Higer”) and Brad¿Waisbren¿(“Waisbren” and collectively “Plaintiffs”) were tenants at the storage facility located at 3421 Gillespie in Acton, CA (the “Property”).¿ On August 03, 2017, a tumultuous rainstorm resulted in flooding on the Property, damaging Plaintiff’s stored chattels.¿ On February 02, 2018, Plaintiffs filed a Government Tort Claim with Defendant County of Los Angeles (“COLA”), which was ultimately denied on May 21, 2018.¿ 

 

¿On November 18, 2018, Plaintiffs filed their initial Complaint against COLA and Defendants Los Angeles County of Regional Planning, Los Angeles County Department of Public Works, Los Angeles County Flood Control District, and Los Angeles County Waterworks District No. 37, Acton, alleging ten (10) cause of action sounding in (1)¿Negligence, (2) Inverse Condemnation; (3) Public Nuisance; (4) Private Nuisance; (5) Premises Liability; (6) Premises Liability–Willful Failure to Warn (Civ. Code § 846); (7)¿ Trespass; (8) Intentional Infliction of Emotional Distress; (9) Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress (“NIED”); (10) Dangerous Condition of Public Property–Gov. Code §§ 830–840.6.¿ Plaintiffs’¿principal¿contention is that the flooding on the property was caused and exacerbated by the design, construction, and maintenance of the county’s neighboring roads and flood water infrastructure, which allegedly carried large amounts of storm runoff directly to Plaintiffs’ property as a de facto catch basin.¿ 

 

On June 10, 2019, prior to any Answer from any defendant, Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint (“FAC”), alleging ten (10) causes of action sounding in (1)¿Inverse Condemnation, (2) Negligence; (3) Public Nuisance; (4) Private Nuisance; (5) Premises Liability; (6) Trespass; (7)¿Premises Liability–Willful Failure to Warn (Civ. Code § 846) ; (8) NIED; (9) Dangerous Condition of Public Property (Gov. Code §§ 830–840.6); and (10)Violations of the California Public Records Act Request, Gov. Code § 6250¿et al.¿ Plaintiff named only two of the previously named defendants in the initial complaint, COLA and Defendant Los Angeles County Flood Control District (“LACFCD” and¿collectively “Defendants”).¿ 

 

On December 12, 2019, the Court sustained Defendants’ Demurrer to Plaintiff’s FAC, with leave to amend.¿ Plaintiff filed their Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) on January 10,¿2020¿alleging four (4) causes of action for (1) Inverse Condemnation (2) Risk of Peculiar Kind (Gov. Code § 815.6); (2) Dangerous Condition of Public Property¿(Gov. Code §§ 830–840.6); and Violation of the California Public Records Act (Gov. Code § 6250).¿ 

 

¿On July 09,¿2020¿the Court sustained Defendants’ Demurrer to the first, second, and third causes of action, with leave to amend and overruled the demur as to the fourth cause of action.¿ Plaintiffs filed the Third Amended Complaint (“TAC”) on August 03,¿2020¿realleging four (4) causes of action for (1) Inverse Condemnation (2) Risk of Peculiar Kind (Gov. Proc. §§ 2025.250 and 2025.260 for reference in this determination, as these statutes appear to be in pari materia with the “reasonable place” requirement in section 2031.030(c)(3). “ ‘Statutes are considered to be in pari materia when they relate to the same person or thing, to the same class of persons [or] things, or have the same purpose or object. Characterization of the object or purpose is more important than characterization of subject matter in determining whether different statutes are closely enough related to justify interpreting one in light of the other.’ ” (People v. Honig (1996) 48 Cal.App.4th 289, 327.) Here, Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.030(c)(3) requires the location of an inspection demand to be “reasonable,” and section 2025.250 places geographic limitations on how far away a deposition may be held from a defendant's residence or place of business. Proc. § 2025.250, the Legislature placed a limit of 150 miles on how far a business entity that is a party can be made to travel for its deposition. Both sets of statutes therefore speak to the same basic “object or purpose.” (Honig, supra, 48 Cal.App.4th at p. 327.) “When statutes are in pari materia, they should be construed together as one statute. [Citations.] So interpreted, ‘all parts of a statute should be read together and construed in a manner that gives effect to each, yet does not lead to disharmony with the others.’ ” (Ibid.) i.                    Terminating Sanctions

 

The imposition of terminating sanctions is a drastic measure. (See¿Deyo¿v. Kilbourne¿(1978) 84 Cal.App.3d 771, 793 (“The sanction of dismissal or the rendition of a default judgment against the disobedient party is ordinarily a drastic measure which should be employed with caution. [Citation] However, there is no question that a court is empowered to apply the ultimate sanction against a litigant who persists in the outright refusal to comply with his discovery obligations. [Citation] The refusal to reveal material evidence is deemed to be an admission that the claim or defense is without merit. [Citations]”).)¿In general,¿a terminating sanction should generally not be imposed until the court has attempted less severe alternatives and found them to be unsuccessful and/or the record clearly shows lesser sanctions would be ineffective. (Lopez v. Watchtower Bible & Tract Society of New York, Inc.¿(2016)¿246 Cal. App. 4th 566, 604-05.) In Lopez, the Court ordered¿Lopez¿to produce documents and repeated this order over “several subsequent hearings.” (Id. at 605.) However, “terminating sanctions are to be used sparingly, only when the trial court concludes that lesser sanctions would not bring about the compliance of the offending party.” (R.S. Creative, Inc., supra, 75 Cal.App.4th 486, 496.) Additionally, “ ‘where a violation is willful, preceded by a history of abuse, and the evidence shows that less severe sanctions would not produce compliance with the discovery rules, the trial court is justified in imposing the ultimate sanction.’ [Citations.]” Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 992.)  

 

When determining if a terminating sanction is appropriate, trial courts consider the totality of the circumstances: conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery. (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246 [“Lang”].) Lang provides a review of cases in which trial courts issued terminating sanctions. “In each case, the offending party spent months avoiding or evading discovery.” (Ibid.)  

 

Here, the Court’s Order was not adhered to; however, at this time, the electronic documents have been turned over. Ultimately, some of the outstanding discovery has been turned over. This case has been plagued by discovery issues from both parties and there have been a number of attempts to rectify the discovery issues in front of this Court. However, as to the underlying specific discovery alone, the Court addressed the issue in the form of Motions to Compel Further, which led to a new briefing schedule for the Motion for Summary Judgment, and, now, these motions requesting terminating sanctions. The Court notes that, pursuant to its Courtroom Rules, IDCs are held prior to the filing of any motion to compel further. The Court has also previously only imposed monetary sanctions. As such, it is not appropriate to impose terminating sanctions.

 

Accordingly, the requests for orders issuing terminating sanctions against Defendants is denied.

 

ii.                  Contempt Sanctions

 

A violation of a discovery order, just like any other court order, is punishable as a contempt of court, and contempt sanctions are available against parties, attorneys, and nonparty witnesses to achieve the objectives of discovery. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §2023.030(e).) A contempt sanction consists either of the imposition of a fine not exceeding $1,000 or imprisonment not exceeding 5 days, or both. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1218(a).) In addition, a person adjudged guilty of contempt for violating a court order may be ordered to pay to the party initiating the contempt proceeding the “reasonable attorney's fees and costs incurred” in connection with the contempt proceeding. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §1218(a).)

 

Case precedent provides that a contempt sanction is seldom used to enforce discovery orders and is inappropriate in all but the most extreme situations in which “the court's dignity is truly compromised and no other suitable penalty can be found.” (In re de la Parra (1986) 184 Cal.App.3d 139, 144-45.)

 

Contempt sanctions are inappropriate for the matter before this Court.

 

Accordingly, the requests for orders issuing contempt sanctions against Defendants is denied.

 

iii.                Evidentiary Sanctions

 

The Court notes that a motion for issue or evidentiary sanctions requires a separate statement and Waisbren has not complied with the requirement. (See Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1345(a)(7).) Defendants appear to take no issue with this deficiency and has responded via a Memorandum of Points and Authorities without a separate statement. The Court addresses the issue on its merits.

 

To impose an evidence sanction, there must be a failure to comply with a court order and the failure must be willful. (Biles v. Exxon Mobil Corp. (2004) 124 Cal.App.4th 1315, 1327.) “Even where nonmonetary sanctions are called for, they should be appropriate to the dereliction, and should not exceed that which is required to protect the interests of the party entitled to but denied discovery.” (Ibid.) “The sanctions the court may impose are such that are suitable and necessary to enable the party seeking discovery to obtain the objects of the discovery he seeks, but the court may not impose sanctions which are designed not to accomplish the objects of discovery but to impose punishment.” (Ibid.)
“[W]hen a party repeatedly and willfully fails to provide certain evidence to the opposing party as required by the discovery rules, preclusion of the evidence may be appropriate, even if such a sanction proves determinative in terminating the plaintiff's case.” (Ibid.) “[A] persistent refusal to comply with an order for the production of evidence is tantamount to an admission that the disobedient party really has no meritorious claim.” (Ibid.)

 

Here, there was a failure to comply with this Court’s December 12, 2024 Order. The actions of Defendants have adversely impacted the supplemental briefing schedule by the Court. Defendants have provided substantive responses only after the Court’s deadline. It appears this failure to provide such information was willful as Defendants provided (1) a “response” declaring their intent to respond on January 11, 2024, the day the Court had ordered responses due, and (2) Defendants did not provide a verified response until March 25, 2024, over a month after their own supplemental reply brief was due.

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) provides that “[t]he court may impose a monetary sanction ordering that one engaging in the misuse of the discovery process, or any attorney advising that conduct, or both pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct.” A misuse of the discovery process “include[s] . . . [d]isobeying a court order to provide discovery.” (Doppes v. Bentley Motors, Inc. (2009) 174 Cal.App.4th 967, 991.)

 

Defendant concedes that Waisbren is entitled to reasonable expenses. Defendant believes that the only expenses that are reasonable are the filing fees, presumed to be $120.00.

 

While Waisbren requests “$484.54 in sanctions against Defendant and each and every one of their attorneys of record which represents reasonable expenses incurred” (Mtn. for Terminating Sanctions RE: LACFD 18:14-16; Mtn. for Terminating Sanctions RE: COLA 18:19-21) and “per CCP §2023.050, Defendant and each and every one of their attorneys of record be sanctioned $1000.00” (Mtn. for Terminating Sanctions RE: LACFD 18:17-18; Mtn. for Terminating Sanctions RE: COLA 18:22-23), the requested $484.54 “against Defendant and each and every one of their attorneys of record” which is likely directed at the four attorneys on Defendants’ header are unsupported. (See Decl. of Wasibren in both motions at ¶ 16 [“I spent $149.54 for filing fees, $20 for parking at the Los Angeles County Law Library, $20 for copies at the Los Angeles County Law Library, $200 Lexis/Nexis Fee and $75 for travel to the Los Angeles County Law Library and the hearing on this motion and $20 for paper.”] Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.040 requires facts supporting the amount requested to be set forth.

 

The Court notes that the request for sanctions does not identify “every person, party, and attorney against whom the sanction is sought” as required under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.040. However, the notice identifies Defendants and “each and/or all of its attorneys of record.” (See both Motions for Terminating Sanctions at Notice.)

 

“Reasonable expenses may include attorney fees, filing fees, referee fees, and other costs incurred.” (Cornerstone Realty Advisors, LLC v. Summit Healthcare REIT, Inc. (2020) 56 Cal.App.5th 771, 790 (“Cornerstone”).) Cornerstone discusses three principles surrounding monetary sanctions regarding discovery: (1) compulsion under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) –  “[i]f a monetary sanction is authorized ‘the court shall impose that sanction’ unless the court finds that the person subject to the sanction either acted with substantial justification or imposition of sanctions would be unjust under the circumstances;” (2) causation as defined in Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) – “[m]onetary sanctions for engaging in misuse of the discovery process may only be imposed based on attorney fees and costs ‘incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct;’ and (3) reasonableness under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(a) – “[t]he amount of monetary sanctions is limited to the ‘reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees’ that a party incurred as a result of the discovery abuse.” (Id. at 790-91.) “The principle of reasonableness means a trial court has discretion to reduce the amount of fees and costs requested as a discovery sanction in order to reach a reasonable award.” (Id. at 791 [internal citations omitted].)

Regarding travel, it the travel for the hearing and the travel to the Los Angeles Law Library is combined. Parking relates to the Los Angeles Law Library as well. If the sole purposes for traveling to the Los Angeles Law Library was copying the Court is not inclined to allow these as costs.

Analysis

Standard for Motion for Summary Judgment/Adjudication – A party may move for summary judgment in any action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c subd. (a).)  Similarly, a party may move for summary adjudication as to one or more causes of action, if the party contends that the cause of action has no merit.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (f)(1).)  Summary adjudication will be granted if it completely disposes of a cause of action, affirmative defense, a claim for damages, or an issue of duty.  (Id.)  A motion for summary adjudication may be made in the alternative to a motion for summary judgment.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (f)(2).)  To prevail on a motion for summary judgment, the evidence submitted must show there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (c).)  

The defendant moving for summary judgment does not need to conclusively negate an element of the plaintiffs’ cause of action, but only to show “that one or more elements of the cause of action. . .cannot be established. . .or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Ridgefield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 849 (citing Code Civ. Proc. § 437c subd. (o)(2).)  Once the moving party has met the respective burden, the burden shifts to the responding party who must demonstrate that a triable issue of material fact exists.  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c subd. (p).)  The responding party must not rely on allegations or denials in the pleadings or answer alone, but “shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto.”  (Id.) 

When ruling on a summary judgment motion, the trial court must consider all inferences from the evidence, even those contradicted by the moving party’s evidence.  The motion cannot succeed unless the evidence leaves no room for conflicting inferences as to material facts; the court has no power to weigh one inference against another or against other evidence.  (Murillo v. Rite Stuff Foods, Inc. (1998) 65 Cal.App.4th 833, 841.)  In determining whether the facts give rise to a triable issue of material fact, “‘[a]ll doubts as to whether any material, triable, issues of fact exist are to be resolved in favor of the party opposing summary judgment . . . .’”  (Gold v. Weissman (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1195, 1198–1199, as modified on denial of reh'g (Feb. 10, 2004).)  “In other words, the facts alleged in the evidence of the party opposing summary judgment and the reasonable inferences there from must be accepted as true.”  (Jackson v. County of Los Angeles (1997) 60 Cal.App.4th 171, 179.)

Procedural Issues

Plaintiffs argue that Defendants’ motion for summary judgment is procedurally deficient as they have not delineated the specific causes of action in their motion nor their Separate Statement of Undisputed Facts. 

Cal. Rules of Court 3.1350(b) states that each “specific cause of action, affirmative defense, claims for damages, or issues of duty” to be stated in the motion and the separate statement in the event summary adjudication is sought. 

Cal. Rules of Court 3.1350(d)(1)(A) and (B) states that the separate statement must separately identify:

(A) Each cause of action, claim for damages, issue of duty, or affirmative defense that is the subject of the motion; and

(B)  Each supporting material fact claimed to be without dispute with respect to the cause of action, claim for damages, issue of duty, or affirmative defense that is the subject of the motion.

Plaintiffs rely on Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 169, 171, for the assertion that the Court must deny Defendants’ motion for summary judgment for their failure to address all of the material allegations of the complaint. However, Plaintiffs misconstrue Teselle, which stated that “the moving party must comply with the provisions of subdivision (b)(1) and its separate statement must address the allegations of material fact in the complaint. If … the moving parties fail to do so, the motion for summary judgment must be denied.” (Teselle, supra, 173 Cal.App.4th at 171.) While Defendants failed to delineate each cause of action in their motion and separate statement, they did not fail to comply with the requirements in Code of Civil Procedure section 437c subdivision (b)(1), which states: 

The motion shall be supported by affidavits, declarations, admissions, answers to interrogatories, depositions, and matters of which judicial notice shall or may be taken. The supporting papers shall include a separate statement setting forth plainly and concisely all material facts that the moving party contends are undisputed. Each of the material facts stated shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. The failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may in the court’s discretion constitute a sufficient ground for denying the motion.

Subdivision (b)(1) merely requires a moving party to have a separate statement setting forth all undisputed material facts in support of their motion. While the California Rules of Court specify more specific requirements for the separate statement, such as separately identifying each cause of action, Plaintiffs fail to provide any authority stating that a failure to separately identify each cause of action in the separate statement constitutes grounds for denying the motion.

Defendants rely on Biron, which requires “a showing of ‘ “a substantial cause-and-effect relationship excluding the probability that other forces alone produced the injury.” [Citations.]’ [Citation.]” (Belair, supra, 47 Cal.3d at p. 559, 253 Cal.Rptr. 693, 764 P.2d 1070.)” to impose inverse condemnation liability. (Biron, supra, 225 Cal.App.4th at 1279.) There, the court found that “the public improvement did operate perfectly, but the storm exceeded the design capacity” such that “[n]ature was a superseding cause of the flooding” and there was no substantial causation. (Id. at 859-860.) The court relied on the trial court’s findings: “[i]t found that City’s storm drain pipes were operating free of any obstructions. It found that the storm drain system did not fail, and that it performed as designed on a 10-year storm design. However, the system was overwhelmed with water because the storm was consistent with a 100-year storm, rather than a 10-year storm.” (Id. at 859.)

In opposition, Plaintiffs call into question the reliability of Defendants’ evidence, particularly the Gumbel analysis relied upon by Mr. Nasseri. As discussed above in Plaintiffs’ evidentiary objections, the Court declines to consider Mr. Nasseri’s conclusions based on the Gumbel Analysis due to Mr. Nasseri’s failure to authenticate the Los Angeles County Department of Public Works Gumbel Extreme Value Type – Rainfall Frequency Analysis – Automatic Rain Gage Results for Rain Gage No. 250 – Acton Camp attached as Exhibit 3. Further, Plaintiffs assert that in order to independently verify the Gumbel Analysis, they require all of the data used to produce that Gumbel Analysis relied upon by Defendants and Mr. Nasseri. Mr. Nasseri’s conclusions based on the Gumbel Analysis which used historical data from 1956 to 2007 show that Defendants may have evidence which Plaintiffs, as the opposing party, need to successfully oppose the motion. 

Plaintiffs also argue that it is inaccurate and inappropriate to directly compare AL487 observed data from the storm event to the 250D historical data. While AL487 is closest to the Subject Property, it has only been in operation since 2008 and does not have the length of records necessary for a Gumbel analysis. Thus, Mr. Nasseri relied on 250D historical data. However, Plaintiffs argue that the two stations are poorly correlated resulting in a severe overestimation of the return period of the Storm Event. Specifically, Plaintiffs point out that a comparison of AL487 annual maxima rainfall data to 250D annual maxima rainfall data shows that the extreme precipitation values for AL487 are considerably higher than those for 250D for both 15-minute and 1-hour events, using a point over threshold technique. (Shaffer Decl. Exhibit 1 and Exhibit 2). Plaintiffs assert that it is much more reliable to use this approach compared to simply and directly using the AL487 rainfall event value with the 250D estimated rainfall frequencies as the vastly different return periods (i.e., 4-years versus 500-years) demonstrate the unsuitability of Mr. Nasseri’s approach. (Shaffer ¶ 21.) According to Plaintiffs’ calculations, the Storm Event was under a 25-50 year event. 

In reply, Defendants challenge Plaintiffs’ evidence of a declaration from their retained expert witness and civil engineer, William Shaffer. Defendants argue that Plaintiffs failed to establish Mr. Shaffer’s minimum competence and the declaration lacks foundation to establish his expertise in flood control hydrology. Further, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs do not explain why it was inappropriate of Dr. Nasseri to perform a Gumbel analysis using the closest rain gauge that has a sufficient historical record to perform such analysis. Finally, Defendants state that Plaintiffs do not sufficiently challenge the design capacity of Defendants’ flood control infrastructure, thereby conceding that the Defendants’ infrastructure was designed to accommodate a 25-year event.

The Court finds that Defendants fail to satisfy their burden of proving no triable issue of fact as to Plaintiffs’ claims for Inverse Condemnation; Breach of Mandatory Duty (Violation of Government Code section 815.6); and Dangerous Condition of Public Property. Defendants only address the issue of causation, arguing that the flood and drainage facilities did not substantially cause Plaintiffs’ damages. However, the evidence Defendants rely upon is inadmissible since the exhibits used by Mr. Nasseri are improperly authenticated. Further, even if the conclusions based on the Gumbel Analysis was admissible, contrary to Defendants’ arguments in reply, Plaintiffs provide sufficient evidence raising a triable issue of fact as to whether the Storm Even was actually a 500 year event as Defendants claim, or a 25-50 year event as Plaintiffs claim. The Court also finds Plaintiffs’ arguments regarding why the Grumbel analysis using the rainfall data from 250D rather than AL487 to raise a triable issue of fact as to the causation element of Plaintiffs’ causes of action. The Court finds that Plaintiffs may properly rely on the opinion testimony of their expert witness William Shaffer. Mr. Shaffer testifies that he has a B.S. in Civil Engineering from Loyola Marymount University; he is a licensed Professional Civil Engineer in California; he is a Project Engineer at EEC Environmental and has been employed there since 2016; his job duties include managing a wide variety of civil and environmental engineering projects including land development, utility design, surveying, hydrologic analysis, water and wastewater treatment, environmental remediation, and construction projects with 10 years of engineering experience in these areas; and that he is familiar with the allegations made in the Fifth Amended Complaint in this case. (Shaffer Decl. ¶¶ 2-6.) The Court finds that Mr. Shaffer sufficiently established his competency to offer his expert opinion on the severity of the 2017 storm and the capacity of Defendants’ infrastructure. 

Thus, the Court DENIES Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to Inverse Condemnation; Breach of Mandatory Duty (Violation of Government Code section 815.6); and Dangerous Condition of Public Property

“YOU ARE NOT REQUIRED TO FILL OUT THIS FORM IN ORDER TO RECEIVE PUBLIC RECORDS UNDER THE CALIFORNIA PUBLIC RECORDS ACT. COMPLETING THIS FORM IS OPTIONAL. THE INFORMATION REQUESTED BELOW, HOWEVER SERVES TO ASSIST OUR EMPLOYEES IN PROCESSING YOUR PUBLIC RECORDS REQUEST.”

(Lara Decl., Exhibit 1, Pages 1 and 2). In support of this, Plaintiffs state that filling out the form is optional. Based on both Plaintiffs’ communications with Defendants regarding the CRPA requests and the fact that filling out the forms were optional, the Court finds that Plaintiffs have shown both Plaintiffs sought disclosure of public records such that Plaintiff Waisbren has standing to sue. 

Violation of the Public Records Act 

The Court next turns to whether Defendants violated the Public Records Act. 

Defendants assert that the undisputed material facts show a reasonable search was undertaken, and all reasonably identifiable responsive records were disclosed. Defendants state they did not claim that any responsive records identified in that search were withheld by exemption or privilege. Further, Defendants point out that the 5AC identifies no legitimate records nor category of records that were withheld. Defendants argue that the list of records or categories of records that Plaintiffs identify in the 5AC were disclosed, go beyond the scope of Plaintiffs’ requests, would not have been responsive to the requests, are not in Defendants’ records, or do not exist. Defendants specifically address the records that Plaintiffs specifically identify as being improperly withheld in the 5AC ¶ 263:

First, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced “[t]he permits for the grading and paving on Michigan in Acton, CA.” (5AC ¶ 263.) Defendants assert that no responsive public records were identified through Defendants’ reasonable search.

Second, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced “[t]he building plans, grading plans, feasibility studies, drainage and storm plans for Meadowlark Elementary School.” (5AC ¶ 263.) Defendants assert that if these records existed, they would not be Defendants’ records since the Acton Agua-Dulce Unified School District, that built the school and presumably has responsibility for its maintenance, is a separate legal entity not affiliated with either Defendant. Furthermore, Defendants state that this specific request was not included, nor would be responsive to anything requested in PRA requests 3860 or 3864.

Third, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced “[t]he letter referenced in Defendants Bates Stamped CPRA 0000009 which references ‘a completed letter was sent to Construction Division inquiring on why the basin hasn’t been maintained and requesting it to be cleaned.’” (5AC ¶ 263.) Defendants assert that that was in fact disclosed by Defendants on June 11, 2019.

Fourth, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced “[a]ll of the emails between Amir Ibrahim, Phil Doudar, Faye Conaway, Jemellee Cruz, Paul Melillo and Toan Duong in regards to PD 2470.” (5AC ¶ 263.) These individuals are DPW employees. However, Defendants assert that these emails were not actually requested by Plaintiff Higer in either requests, and, therefore, were not and could not have been identified through a reasonable search, and thus disclosed. Further, Defendants state these specific individual employees were not included in either PRA request because they were not known at the time, but identified through the records produced by Defendants. 

Fifth, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced claim “[a]ll of the interoffice emails in regards to the maintenance of PD2470.” (5AC ¶ 263.) Defendants assert that these emails go beyond the scope of anything requested through either PRA request 3860 or 3864. Furthermore, even if it were properly requested, Defendants state it would not have been a reasonable request under the Public Records Act, as it is overly vague, overbroad, or burdensome, and could not lead to a focused search for identifiable records.

Sixth, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced “[a]ll of the interoffice emails in regards to the maintenance of any drainage, storm drain maintenance, feasibility studies, ditch maintenance, culverts, manhole and drain maintenance reference in the CPRA request.” (5AC ¶ 263.) Like the fifth request, Defendants assert that these emails also go beyond the scope of either PRA request 3860 or 3864, and even if they were properly requested, it would not have been a reasonable request under the Public Records Act, as it is vague and overbroad, and could not lead to a focused search for identifiable records.

Seventh, Plaintiffs state Defendants have not produced “[c]opies of the routines and location maps referenced by Jemellee Cruz as referenced by her email date Thursday, April 3, 2003 regarding PD2470 and Meadowlark School – Defendants Bates Stamp 000010.” 
(5AC ¶ 263.) Defendants argue that this instance of improper withholding has obviously been identified by Plaintiffs through the records produced by Defendants, as evident by the inclusion of Defendants’ batestamp number 000010 and cannot be credibly argued that this record would have been reasonably identifiable under the scope of either request. Furthermore, Defendants state the substance of the batestamp email speculates that some record may be created in the future where DPW employee Jemellee Cruz states, “[w]e will set up the routines & location maps once we receive the transfer document,” but does not indicate that such records were ever created. 

In opposition, Plaintiffs assert that Defendants are liable for their non-responsiveness and unlawful and intentional delays to document requests under the strict timetables as provided for in the CPRA. Plaintiffs state that Defendants never claimed any exemptions such as withholding records pertaining to pending litigation and were not subject to privacy concerns. Plaintiffs also reassert the documents identified in the 5AC paragraph 263 as items which Defendants failed to produce. However, Plaintiffs do not support this with any argument or evidence. Plaintiffs further state that Defendants failed to provide all rain gauge data requested on October 6, 2023, which demonstrates their continued violation of the CPRA. Plaintiffs also state that Defendants failed to provide responsive documents within the statutory time. Plaintiffs point out that Defendants invoked their 14 day extension to respond to all three of the CPRA requests, however this was past the 10 day deadline. (Lara Decl., Exhibit 3)

In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs’ complaint about how Defendants processed the 2019 CPRA requests is irrelevant. Defendants also assert that Plaintiffs present no conflicting evidence to support a conclusion that Defendants have withheld responsive records to the 2019 CPRA requests, and specifically the five categories to which Defendants allegedly did not respond. Defendants also argue that Plaintiffs’ new allegation that Defendants are continuing to violate the CPRA goes beyond the scope of the pleadings. Finally, Defendants assert that Plaintiffs’ complaint that Defendants took 50 days to respond to their CPRA requests is irrelevant since timeliness has no remedy.

“The judicial remedy set forth in the CPRA is available only to a person or entity who is seeking disclosure of public records and only where the public entity is allegedly improperly withholding those records.” (County of Santa Clara v. Superior Court (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 119, 127.) “The Act provides no remedy for failure to timely comply with a request for records.” (Rogers v. Superior Court (1993) 19 Cal.App.4th 469, 483.) 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s untimeliness arguments to be meritless as Rogers sets forth that there is no remedy for a failure to timely comply with requests for records. Further, Plaintiffs have not supported their assertion that Defendants failed to disclose all documents responsive to the CPRA’s as listed in 5AC paragraph 263. Finally, as Defendants point out, Plaintiffs cannot rely on the October 6, 2023 request as it is beyond the scope of the pleadings. ¿ (See AARTS Productions, Inc. v. Crocker Nat’l Bank (1986) 179 Cal. App. 3d 1061, 1064-1065 [“[T]he trial court must identify the issues framed by the pleadings since it is these allegations to which the motion must respond by establishing a complete defense or otherwise showing there is no factual basis for relief on any theory reasonably contemplated by the opponent’s pleading.”].) 

Thus, the Court GRANTS Defendants’ motion for summary adjudication as to Violation of the California Public Records Act.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Compelling Further Responses to Requests for Production (“RFPs”) – “Unless otherwise limited by order of the court in accordance with this title, any party may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved in the pending action or to the determination of any motion made in that action, if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)¿For discovery purposes, information is regarded as relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating the case, preparing for trial, or facilitating settlement thereof.” (City of Los Angeles v. Superior Court¿(2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 272, 288.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further responses to a demand for inspection or production of documents (“RFP”) may be brought based on: (1) incomplete statements of compliance; (2) inadequate, evasive or incomplete claims of inability to comply; or (3) unmerited or overly generalized objections.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §¿2031.310(a).)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

Notice of the motion must be given within 45 days of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the demanding party and the responding party have agreed in writing. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).)¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿2031.310(b)(1).) In¿Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court¿(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there¿is¿“a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

If the moving party has shown good cause for the requests for production, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v.¿Sup. Ct¿(2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.)¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

“The court shall limit the scope of discovery if it determines that the burden, expense, or intrusiveness of that discovery clearly outweighs the likelihood that the information sought will lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Cal. Code of Civ.¿Proc. § 2017.020(a).) Generally, objections on the ground of burden require the objecting party to produce evidence of (a) the propounding party’s subjective intent to create burden, or (b) the amount of time and effort it would take to actually respond. (See¿West Pico Furniture Co. of Los Angeles v. Superior Court In and For Los Angeles County¿(1961) 56 Cal.2d 407, 417.) However, where discovery is obviously overbroad on its face, no such evidence is necessary. (See¿Obregon v. Superior Court¿(1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 424, 431.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Trial courts are vested with “wide discretion” to allow or prohibit discovery. (Williams v. Superior Court¿(2017) 3 Cal.5th 531, 540.)¿¿¿¿

As stated, ante, a motion to compel further production must set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc., §¿2031.310(b)(1).) In¿Digital Music News LLC v Superior Court¿(2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 216 at 224, the Court defined “good cause” as a showing that there¿is¿“a disputed fact that is of consequence in the action and the discovery sought will tend in reason to prove or disprove that fact or lead to other evidence that will tend to prove or disprove the fact.”¿¿Here, Plaintiff has shown good cause for the document demands as such documents are directly relevant to the case as Plaintiff has alleged that Defendants used Plaintiff’s internal trade secret information including but not limited to price mechanisms and business practices implemented for each client based on client needs and the amount acquired from such alleged actions. As to privacy, although not constitutionally protected, a corporation still enjoys a right to privacy in its confidential information, including financial information. (See SCC Acquisitions, Inc. v. Superior Court (2015) 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 755-56.) Whether discovery requests infringe on that right is resolved by balancing whether the discovery “ ‘appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence’ ” against the corporate right of privacy. (Id. at 756 [internal citations omitted].)

 

The RFPs directed to Tan and M Business concerning M Businesses bank statements and payments are relevant, as discussed ante. As such, the burden is on the objecting party to justify the objections. (Kirkland v.¿Sup. Ct¿(2002) 95 Cal. App.4th 92, 98.) Defendants state that a deposition would be less intrusive while citing to El Dorado Savings & Loan Assn. v. Superior Court (1987) 190 Cal.App.3d 342, 346 (“El Dorado Savings”), summarized by Defendants as “refusing to order disclosure of employee personnel file where the relevant information could be obtained through “less intrusive means, such as deposing.” (Opposition to Mtn. to Compel Further RE: M Business 4:24-5:6; Opposition to Mtn. to Compel Further RE: Tan 5:4-13.) No argument is provided as to how El Dorado Savings applies to the action at hand, rather Defendants have only provided a general statement that a deposition is a less intrusive means through which a party can obtain information. As such, Defendants have not justified their objections, which also include boilerplate objections such as overbroad, unduly burdensome, harassing, and vague and ambiguous. (See Mtn. to Compel Further RE: Tan, Separate Statement; See Mtn. to Compel Further RE: Tan, Separate Statement, Decl. Sam Donabedian, Exh. C; Mtn. to Compel Further RE: M Business, Separate Statement; Mtn. to Compel Further RE: M Business, Decl. Sam Donabedian at Exh. C.) The Court notes that Defendants’ responses use the term “interrogatory.” (See ibid.)

 

The Motion to Compel Defendant Mary Jane Tan Further Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED as to RFP No. 11 and the Motion to Compel Defendant M Business Solutions LLC Further Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED in its entirety.

 

As to RFP No. 13, directed to Tan, individually, the RFP requests for “any and all documents;” however, both parties appear to be in agreement that this response would lead to the production of bank documents. No argument is provided for other documents. A privacy privilege is implied from the right to privacy to privacy guaranteed by California Constitution, article I, section 1. Matters that fall within the zone of privacy established by the California Constitution and case law are protected unless an adequate countervailing interest is shown. (Britt v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 844, 864-865; Morales v. Superior Court (1979) 99 Cal.App.3d 283, 287.) Those matters include personal financial affairs. (SCC Acquisitions, Inc., supra, 243 Cal.App.4th 741, 754-755.) “In determining whether disclosure is required, the court must indulge in a careful balancing of the right of a civil litigant to discover relevant facts, on the one hand, and the right of the third parties to maintain reasonable privacy regarding their sensitive personal affairs, on the other. [Citation.] The court must consider the purpose of the information sought, the effect that disclosure will have on the affected persons and parties, the nature of the objections urged by the party resisting disclosure and availability of alternative, less intrusive means for obtaining the requested information. [Citation.] Based on an application of these factors, the more sensitive the nature of the personal information that is sought to be discovered, the more substantial the showing of the need for the discovery that will be required before disclosure will be permitted. [Citations.]” (Ibid.) “If an obvious invasion of interest fundamental to personal autonomy is involved, then the compelling interest test applies. If the invasion is less central, or is in bona fide dispute, then a general balancing test applies.” (Fair Employment & Housing (2002) 99 Cal.App.4th 896, 903.)

 

For the same reasons discussed, ante, Plaintiff has shown good cause and Defendants’ have not justified their objections.

 

Plaintiff has only requested documents related to payments Tan has received from Plaintiff’s past customers. As such, it appears that all transactions or payments responsive to this request would be applicable to this matter as Tan is not “forced to disclose the entirety of her bank statements.” (See Opp. to Mtn. to Compel Further RE: Tan 5:22-25.)

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Compel Defendant Mary Jane Tan Further Responses to Demand for Production of Documents, Set One is GRANTED as to RFP No. 13.

“Except as provided in subdivision (j), the court shall impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to a demand, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Cal. While the privacy objections may be justified to an extent, there does not appear to be substantial justification as Defendants have neglected to argue a variety of their objections such as overbroad, unduly burdensome, harassing, and vague and ambiguous.

 

The Court notes that the request for sanctions by Plaintiff on an RFP matter triggers Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.050 which reads, in relevant part:

 

Notwithstanding any other law, and in addition to any other sanctions imposed pursuant to this chapter, a court shall impose a one-thousand-dollar ($1,000) sanction, payable to the requesting party, upon a party, person, or attorney if, upon reviewing a request for a sanction made pursuant to Section 2023.040, the court finds any of the following:

(1) The party, person, or attorney did not respond in good faith to a request for the production of documents made pursuant to Section 2020.010, 2020.410, 2020.510, or 2025.210, or to an inspection demand made pursuant to Section 2031.010.

(2) The party, person, or attorney produced requested documents within seven days before the court was scheduled to hear a motion to compel production of the records pursuant to Section 2025.450, 2025.480, or 2031.320 that is filed by the requesting party as a result of the other party’s, person’s, or attorney’s failure to respond in good faith.

(3) The party, person, or attorney failed to confer in person, by telephone, letter, or other means of communication in writing, as defined in Section 250 of the Evidence Code, with the party or attorney requesting the documents in a reasonable and good faith attempt to resolve informally any dispute concerning the request.

 

(Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.050(a).)

 

Accordingly, the Court imposes a sanction of $1,000.00 on Defendants and their counsel of record, Mika Marie Hilaire, jointly and severally.
