DEPARTMENT 68 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV11602    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: 68

Dept. 68

Date: 1-14-25

Case 23STCV11602

Trial Date: 1-21-25

 

SUMMARY JUDGMENT/SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant, Cambria Company, LLC

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff, Wendy Viridinia Solano-Castro

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

Summary Adjudication

·         Fraudulent Concealment

·         Claim for Punitive Damages

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Wendy Viridinia Solano-Castro, et al. alleges the wrongful death of Raul Garcia-Leon caused from as a stone fabricator and installer. Plaintiffs contend working with “toxic” stone products led to the development of silicosis and death of Garcia-Leon on February 16, 2023.

 

On May 22, 2023, Plaintiff filed a 582 paragraph complaint for negligence, strict liability – warning defect, strict liability – design defect, fraudulent concealment, and breach of implied warranties. On May 31, 2023, Plaintiff a 235 page first amended complaint for negligence, strict liability – warning defect, strict liability – design defect, fraudulent concealment, and breach of implied warranties. The action names a multitude of parties comprised of suppliers, sellers, etc. The case was deemed non-complex on June 5, 2023, and assigned to Department 68 on June 7, 2023.

 

On October 11, 2023, the court sustained the demurrer to the first amended complaint brought by MS International, Inc. to the fourth cause of action with 20 days leave to amend. On October 18, 2023, the court overruled the demurrer to the fourth cause of action brought by M S International, Inc. On November 2, 2023, Caesarstone USA, Inc. filed a cross-complaint for indemnity, declaratory relief and apportionment. On December 29, 2023, Lowes Home Centers, LLC filed a cross-complaint indemnity, declaratory relief and apportionment.

 

On September 5, 2024, the court granted the motion for trial preference based on the age of Rebecca Garcia at the time of the filing of the motion.

 

RULING: Denied.

Evidentiary Objections to the Declaration of Raphael Metzger: Overruled.

Evidentiary Objections to the Declaration of Dr. Kenneth D Rosenman: Overruled.

Evidentiary Objections to the Declaration of Scott P. Brust: Overruled.

Evidentiary Objections to the Declaration of Simonetta Marshall: Overruled.

No objections by Plaintiffs on file.

 

Defendant Cambria Company, LLC (Cambria) moves for summary judgment on the entire complaint, and alternatively, summary adjudication on the claim for punitive damages. Cambria moves on a few grounds: Lack of evidence establishing causation, a complete defense under the Federal Hazards Communications Act, and sophisticated intermediary/user doctrine. Plaintiffs in opposition challenge the motion on grounds that Cambria fails to shift the burden of proof, due to the lack of sufficient competent evidence, and denial of application of the sophisticated user defense.

 

The pleadings frame the issues for motions, “since it is those allegations to which the motion must respond. (Citation.)” (Scolinos v. Kolts (1995) 37 Cal. App. 4th 635, 640-641; FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382-383; Jordan-Lyon Prods., LTD. v. Cineplex Odeon Corp. (1994) 29 Cal.App.4th 1459, 1472.) The purpose of a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication “is to provide courts with a mechanism to cut through the parties’ pleadings in order to determine whether, despite their allegations, trial is in fact necessary to resolve their dispute.” (Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.)  “Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (c), requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)

The court adheres to this standard for product liability cases, such as asbestos and crystalline silica (e.g. latent exposure leading to mesothelioma or silicosis).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.”  (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

Cambria relies on denial of any supply, manufacture or distribution of silica containing products utilized by Decedent, while employed as a fabricator. The position relies entirely on a challenge to Plaintiffs’ evidence, and otherwise offers no challenge to the allegation that Cambria engaged in the manufacture/supply/distribution of silica containing artificial stone products.

 

The court finds the subject matter analogous to asbestos litigation on the basis of latent injury caused by a dangerous product, and therefore applies the substantial factor standard in the subject action. (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1415, 1419.) “In the context of a cause of action for asbestos-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective asbestos-containing products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a “legal cause” of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In an asbestos-related cancer case, the plaintiff need not prove that fibers from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing cancer.” Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982. “[W]e conclude that the proper analysis is to ask whether the plaintiff has proven exposure to a defendant's product, of whatever duration, so that exposure is a possible factor in causing the disease and then to evaluate whether the exposure was a substantial factor.”  (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 1416.)  Exposure by a contributing party, among many, is sufficient to establish triable issues of material fact.  The calculation of the probabilities of whether even limited exposure constituted a substantial factor is more appropriately addressed before the trial court.  (Id. at pp. 1417-1419.)

Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982.)

[See Declaration of Cassidy H. Tynan, Index of Exhibits.] The conclusive argument and reliance on denial of sufficient evidence fails to shift the burden. (See McGonnell v. On the ground of sophisticated/intermediate user, the court reviews the standard. “In general, a manufacturer or supplier has a duty to warn consumers about the dangers and risks inherent in the use of its product. (Citations.) The sophisticated user defense is an exception to the general duty to warn. (Citation.) The defense concerns warnings and does not apply to a cause of action for design defect. (Citation.) ... [¶] Under the sophisticated user defense, a manufacturer is not liable to a sophisticated user of its product for failure to warn about the product's dangers if the sophisticated user knew or should have known of the dangers. (Citation.) The ‘should have known’ portion of the formulation is an objective standard. (Citation.) ... The rationale supporting the defense is that the failure to warn about risks already known to a sophisticated user usually is not a proximate cause of the user's injuries. (Citation.)” (Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th at pp. 601–602.)

Other than an admission to the hazards of silica dust, the motion lacks legal support actually addressing the standard for warning or evidence of the sufficiency of its warning, and instead offers deflection. (Webb v. Special Electric Co., Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 167, 187-188.) T

Plaintiff additionally establishes triable issues of material fact on the known hazards of silica particulate matter resulting from fabrication, the warning to Decedent’s employer, and Decedent’s employers’ sophistication. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition of Bernardino Sanchez, 159:8-16.]

[Citation.]’” (Kaldenbach v. Mutual of Omaha Life Ins. Co. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 830, 850 (internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

“There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. [Citation.]’” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) “Each of the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presupposes the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. … As a matter of common sense, such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties.” (Id. at pp. 336–337.)

 

“The requirement's purpose is to inform consumers about a product's hazards and faults of which they are unaware, so that they can refrain from using the product altogether or evade the danger by careful use.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198.) Users include persons responsible for processing materials not privy to information regarding necessary safety protocols. The withholding of said information subjects a defendant to potential fraudulent concealment liability. (Id. at pp. 1199-1200.)

 

The operative complaint presents allegations regarding knowledge of the allegedly toxic substance, crystalline silica, dangers to workers engaged in the cutting, grinding, fabrication, polishing, etc. with the product, and failure to disclose potential toxicity upon working with said products. Decedent was specifically exposed and harmed as a result of the non-disclosed hazards and continued work with the supplied product. The allegations meet the standard for fraudulent concealment. (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78-79.)

 

Under the consideration for product liability summary judgment, the court finds insufficient address of this standard by Cambria for purposes of shifting the burden. The court therefore denies the motion for summary adjudication on both the failure to shift the burden, and triable issues of material fact regarding failure to warn on allegedly known hazards caused by fabrication of products containing silica. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition of Bernardino Sanchez, 159:8-16.]

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

“‘[M]alice does not require actual intent to harm. [Citation.] Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. [Citation.] Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.’ [Citations.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)

 

Consistent with the prior part of the order, the court again finds the motion insufficiently addresses the evidence presented by Plaintiffs regarding the knowing distribution of the silica containing product, including corporate knowledge and direction for the manufacture and distribution of an allegedly harmful product. The court additionally finds triable issues of material fact on the basis of knowledge, ratification, and malice. [Index of Exhibits: Responses to Specially Drafted Interrogatories; Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition of Jon Grzeskowiak, and Cambria’s Safety Data Sheet.] (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 576–577; Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168.) The motion for summary adjudication is therefore denied in its entirety.

 

In summary, the court denies the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication.

In a[] [silicosis] case, the plaintiff need not prove that [particulate] from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing [silicosis].” (Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982.)

 

Other than citation to discovery responses regarding the basis of the complaint, including the performance of stone fabrication work without sufficient protective equipment, the court finds no substantive basis for the challenge to the insufficiency of evidence by Plaintiffs regarding the placement of Caesarstone USA manufactured and/or distributed silica containing artificial stone products on the premises of any and all of Decedent’s employers during the relevant exposure period. [See Declaration of Brian L. King, Compendium of Evidence.] The conclusive argument and reliance on denial of sufficient evidence fails to shift the burden. (See McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 81.)

 

Further, the deposition testimony of Decedent’s co-worker Domingo Ojeda-Alverdin and the declaration of its Corporate Support Manager Keuana Lillard are equally insufficient to shift the burden. The deposition testimony of Ojeda-Alverdin does not negate exposure or causation and he admittedly identifies Caesarstone slabs as ones he worked with, though Caesarstone USA makes a vague claim that it may be counterfeit without any factual or legal address. Likewise, the declaration of Lillard does not state that Caesarstone USA never manufactured in the United States, and otherwise only presents evidence that there are no records of direct sale to Decedent’s places of work.

 

The court alternatively finds triable issues of material fact based on the presence of Caesarstone vehicles and products on the premises of Decedent’s place of employment during the relevant time period, records and correspondence of sale involving Caesarstone, and evidence of manufacturing the product. Plaintiffs present possibly admissible testimony identifying Caesarstone vehicles and products for Bernadino’s shop, and invoices and sales of Caesarstone slabs to Bernadino’s shops. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow: Deposition Testimony of Cesar Quiroz, 60:13-20, 164:14-165:2, 268:6-15; 269:8-12, 626:24-627:3; Declaration of Simonetta Marshall, Exh. A.] Plaintiffs also present evidence that Caesarstone USA had a manufacturing plant in Georgia from 2015 through January of 2024 based on news articles. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exhs. 1-2.] The exposure to said silica containing stone products caused exposure and led to the silicosis diagnosis and death. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman.]

 

As to Caesarstone USA’s lack of privity argument regarding the claim for breach of implied warranty, Plaintiffs correctly cite to Peterson v. Lamb Lumber Co. (1960) 54 Cal.2d 339, 347-348, where our Supreme Court held that for purposes of breach of implied warranty liability, a worker who is injured in the course of his work by a defective product purchased by his hirer is in privity with both the manufacturer and the supplier of the product.

 

Therefore, the motion for summary judgment and motion for summary adjudication reliant on the absence of sufficient exposure, causation, and privity is therefore DENIED.

 

On Fraudulent Concealment, Caesarstone USA challenges the lack of any basis of duty to disclose any specific potential harms from the product(s). Caesarstone USA argues that Decedent did not have a fiduciary or direct transactional relationship with it, thus foreclosing any duty to disclose. Caesarstone USA relies on the deposition testimony of Ojeda-Alverdin, who testified that the stone slabs used at Bernardino Sanchez’s shop were purchased by the property owner and that the slabs used in the shop were provided by third-party contractors “Pocholo” and “El Poblano” or “Puebla.” [Compendium of Evidence, Ojeda-Alverdin Deposition Transcript.] Thus, it argues that there is no evidence that Decedent, who worked as a fabricator, purchased Caesarstone USA slabs himself or was in any way involved in ordering slabs on behalf of his employers. Defendant also relies on the declaration of Lillard, who provides that Caesarstone USA has no record of selling its products directly to Decedent’s employers. [Declaration of Keuana Lillard, ¶¶4-6.]

 

In opposition, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant failed to shift the burden and challenge the evidence presented. Plaintiffs further contend that Caesarstone USA remains liable because the Hazard Communication Standard imposed a duty on it to fully disclose the lethal hazards of its product by affixing hazard warning labels and use instructions to its artificial stone slabs and by providing safety data sheets to those purchasing the product, as well as under common law. Plaintiffs also provide possibly admissible evidence of Caesarstone USA’s CEO Arik Tendler’s knowledge of silicosis cases against it, a failure to investigate silicosis, a lack of proper warnings despite knowledge that silica can be hazardous to health, and policies Caesarstone put in place in Isreal during the manufacturing. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exh. 7, Deposition Testimony of Arik Tendler.] However, Plaintiffs have yet to show this former testimony is admissible under any hearsay exception.

 

“[T]he elements of a cause of action for fraud based on concealment are: ‘(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage. [Citation.]’” (Kaldenbach v. Mutual of Omaha Life Ins. Co. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 830, 850 (internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

“There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. [Citation.]’” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) “Each of the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presupposes the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. … As a matter of common sense, such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties.” (Id. at pp. 336–337.)

 

The court notes the standard for certain types of product potentially applicable, and considered in a demurrer on the subject cause of action brought by a different co-defendant. “The requirement's purpose is to inform consumers about a product's hazards and faults of which they are unaware, so that they can refrain from using the product altogether or evade the danger by careful use.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198.) Users include persons responsible for processing materials not privy to information regarding necessary safety protocols. The withholding of said information subjects a defendant to potential fraudulent concealment liability. (Id. at pp. 1199-1200.)

 

Paragraphs 541 through 547 of the operative complaint present allegations regarding knowledge of the allegedly toxic substance, crystalline silica, dangers to workers engaged in the cutting, grinding, fabrication, polishing, etc. with the product, and failure to disclose potential toxicity upon working with said products. Decedent was specifically exposed and harmed as a result of the non-disclosed hazards and continued work with the supplied product. [First Amend. Comp., ¶¶ 535-564.] The allegations meet the standard for fraudulent concealment. (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78-79.)

 

Under the consideration for product liability summary judgment, the court finds insufficient address of this standard by Caesarstone USA for purposes of shifting the burden. Further, as discussed in the prior part of the order, the court finds the evidence presented by Caesarstone USA to be insufficient as well. The court therefore denies the motion for summary adjudication on the failure to shift the burden, and notes there may be triable issues of material fact regarding failure to warn on allegedly known hazards caused by fabrication of products containing silica depending on the admissibility of Plaintiffs’ evidence. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exh. 7, Deposition Testimony of Arik Tendler.]

 

On punitive damages, Caesarstone USA challenges the sufficiency against the corporate entity. Caesarstone USA generally states that Plaintiffs have no evidence to support this claim and relies on the same arguments made for the fraud cause of action. Plaintiffs’ opposition lacks much legal address as well.

 

On punitive damages, Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (c) authorizes punitive damages upon a showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, which are defined as follows:

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

“‘[M]alice does not require actual intent to harm. [Citation.] Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. [Citation.] Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.’ [Citations.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)

 

Consistent with the prior part of the order, the court again finds the motion insufficiently addresses the evidence presented by Plaintiffs regarding the knowing distribution of the silica containing product, including corporate knowledge and direction for the manufacture and distribution of an allegedly harmful product. Further, Caesarstone USA presents no actual evidence in support of its contentions, only generally points to an absence of evidence without much address, and relies on its arguments regarding the fraud cause of action which fail as discussed above. [Compendium of Evidence, Responses to Specially Drafted Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents.] The court additionally notes there may be triable issues of material fact on the basis of knowledge, ratification, and malice depending on the admissibility of Plaintiffs’ evidence. [Declaration of Brian P. Barrow, Exh. 7, Deposition Testimony of Arik Tendler.] (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 576–577; Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168.) The motion for summary adjudication is therefore denied in its entirety.

 

In summary, the court denies the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication.

A defendant may alternatively move for summary judgment on grounds that Plaintiff lacks sufficient evidence, and cannot reasonably obtain necessary evidence in support of the claims.¿(Aguilar v. Atl. Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th at pp. 854-855; Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 582, 587; Andrews v. Foster Wheeler LLC (2006) 138 Cal.App.4th 96, 101; Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 573, 590.) The court adheres to this standard for product liability cases, such as asbestos and crystalline silica (e.g. latent exposure leading to mesothelioma or silicosis).

 

“When deciding whether to grant summary judgment, the court must consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers (except evidence to which the court has sustained an objection), as well as all reasonable inference that may be drawn form that evidence, in the light most favorable to the party opposing summary judgment.” (Avivi v. Centro Medico Urgente Medical Center (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 463, 467; see also Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (c).) “An issue of fact can only be created by a conflict in the evidence.  It is not created by speculation, conjecture, imagination or guesswork.”  (Lyons v. Security Pacific National Bank (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1001, 1041 (citation omitted).)

 

The court finds the subject matter analogous to asbestos litigation on the basis of latent injury caused by a dangerous product, and therefore applies the substantial factor standard in the subject action. (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 1409, 1415, 1419.) “In the context of a cause of action for asbestos-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective asbestos-containing products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a “legal cause” of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In an asbestos-related cancer case, the plaintiff need not prove that fibers from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing cancer.” Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982. “[W]e conclude that the proper analysis is to ask whether the plaintiff has proven exposure to a defendant's product, of whatever duration, so that exposure is a possible factor in causing the disease and then to evaluate whether the exposure was a substantial factor.”  (Lineaweaver v. Plant Insulation Company, supra, 31 Cal.App.4th at p. 1416.)  Exposure by a contributing party, among many, is sufficient to establish triable issues of material fact.  The calculation of the probabilities of whether even limited exposure constituted a substantial factor is more appropriately addressed before the trial court.  (Id. at pp. 1417-1419.)

 

“In the context of a cause of action for [silica]-related latent injuries, the plaintiff must first establish some threshold exposure to the defendant's defective [] products, and must further establish in reasonable medical probability that a particular exposure or series of exposures was a ‘legal cause’ of his injury, i.e., a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. In a[] [silicosis] case, the plaintiff need not prove that [particulate] from the defendant's product were the ones, or among the ones, that actually began the process of malignant cellular growth. Instead, the plaintiff may meet the burden of proving that exposure to defendant's product was a substantial factor causing the illness by showing that in reasonable medical probability it contributed to the plaintiff or decedent's risk of developing [silicosis].” (Rutherford v. Owens-Illinois, Inc. (1997) 16 Cal.4th 953, 982.)

 

Paragon relies on denial of any supply, manufacture or distribution of silica containing products utilized by Decedent, while employed as a fabricator. The position relies entirely on a challenge to Plaintiffs’ evidence, and otherwise offers no counter evidence refuting the allegation that Paragon engaged in the manufacture/supply/distribution of silica containing artificial stone products.

 

Other than citation to discovery responses regarding the basis of the complaint, including the performance of stone fabrication work without sufficient protective equipment, the court finds no substantive basis for the challenge to the insufficiency of evidence by Plaintiffs regarding the placement of Paragon manufactured and/or distributed silica containing artificial stone products on the premises of any and all of Decedent’s employers during the relevant exposure period. [See Declaration of John McMahon, Index of Exhibits.] Plaintiffs present pages and pages of factually detailed discovery regarding the claim for exposure. The conclusive argument and reliance on denial of sufficient evidence fails to shift the burden. (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc. (2002) 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 81.)

 

The court alternatively finds triable issues of material fact based on the presence of silica containing Paragon products identified at least in part as Revera and Sequel, and acquired at Bedrosians. The affiliation with Bedrosians and Paragon remains undisputed. [Declaration of Scott Brust: Deposition of Cesar Quiroz, 870:23-874:19; see Declaration of Eric Jessen.] The exposure to said silica containing stone products caused exposure and led to the silicosis diagnosis and death. [Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman.]

 

The motion for summary judgment and motion for summary adjudication reliant on the absence of sufficient causation is therefore DENIED. (McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc., supra, 98 Cal.App.4th 1098, 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co., supra, 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 81.)

 

Paragon next contends superseding conduct intervenes any causation claims. The position relies on deposition testimony whereby a co-employee admittedly removed warning labels placed on artificial stone slabs present on the premises of employer Bernadino Sanchez, and said employer failed to provide adequate respiratory protection equipment. Paragon maintains it complied with federal regulations requiring the labeling of hazardous products. Plaintiffs in opposition counters that Bernadino Sanchez was never an employer of Decedent, and was therefore not obligated to provide safety equipment. Sanchez also denies ever seeing any material data safety sheets or otherwise understanding the warning labels on the slabs in terms of silica hazards. Plaintiffs also represent triable issues of material fact exist on the defense based on “misuse” doctrine.

 

Superseding causation constitutes an exception to reliance on substantial factor causation. (Ash v. North American Title Co. (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 1258, 1277 accord Soule v. General Motors Corp. (1994) 8 Cal.4th 548, 573 (footnote 9) [“This principle is distinct from the defense of ‘superseding cause,’ which absolves a tortfeasor, even though his conduct was a substantial contributing factor, when an independent event intervenes in the chain of causation, producing harm of a kind and degree so far beyond the risk the original tortfeasor should have foreseen that the law deems it unfair to hold him responsible”].

 

“The use of the terms ‘superseding’ or ‘superseding cause’ in these opinions should not be confused with the doctrine of superseding cause which relieves an actor of liability notwithstanding that the actor was a cause in fact of the injury for which recompense was sought. ‘A superseding cause is an act of a third person or other force which by its intervention prevents the actor from being liable for harm to another which his antecedent negligence is a substantial factor in bringing about.’ (Rest.2d Torts, § 440.) In California the doctrine requires more than mere negligence on the part of the intervening actor. ‘[T]he fact that an intervening act of a third person is done in a negligent manner does not make it a superseding cause if a reasonable man knowing the situation existing when the act of the third person is done would not regard it as highly extraordinary that the third person so acted or the act is a normal response to a situation created by the defendant's conduct and the manner in which the intervening act is done is not extraordinarily negligent.’” (Doupnik v. General Motors Corp. (1990) 225 Cal.App.3d 849, 862-863.)

 

The motion itself lacks specific address of the standard, and simply cites on the existence of federal regulations regarding disclosure requirements, and deposition testimony regarding the removal of warning labels. The court declines to make the arguments for the Federal Hazards Communications Act simply based on citation to federal regulations and reiteration in reply. As further discussed below, the court also finds to the extent the position relies on deflecting responsibility for placement of silica containing stone products into commerce to Sanchez for removal of the safety tags, the court finds no legal support for the Federal Hazards Communication Act defense on this basis.

 

The removal of warning labels from slabs and failure to provide allegedly provided safety sheets to stone fabricators on the premises (challenged by Sanchez) otherwise insufficiently establishes any extraordinary negligence or otherwise altered the foreseeability of harm caused by the stone cutting operations. Paragon first and foremost relies on discovery propounded by Surface Warehouse, and other cases, which the court will not consider for purposes of the subject motion. [Declaration of John MacMahon ¶¶ 3-8, Index of Exhibits, Ex. 3-7: Responses to Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories.] Of the reviewed, admissible evidence, the court finds insufficient consideration of the standard presented. (Doupnik v. General Motors Corp., supra, 225 Cal.App.3d at pp. 862-863.)

 

With the exception of the removal of certain alleged warning literature and stickers, the deposition testimony only addresses the workplace conditions rather than any consideration of the alleged superseding conduct. [MacMahon Decl., ¶¶ 9-11, Ex. 8-9: Deposition of Domingo Ojeda-Alvedrin and Cesar Gonzalez-Quiroz.] Again, the court finds mere citation to federal regulation standards insufficiently establishes a basis for relief under the Federal Hazards Communications Act or superseding causation. Even if Paragon shifted the burden, court finds triable issues of material fact on the issue of reasonableness in the alleged conduct, including under the guise of federal regulations and the existence of any employment relationship between Sanchez and Decedent. [Id.; Declaration of Scott Brust: Deposition of Cesar Quiroz.]

 

On the third ground, Paragon challenges the action under sophisticated/intermediate user. The court reviews the standard. “In general, a manufacturer or supplier has a duty to warn consumers about the dangers and risks inherent in the use of its product. (Citations.) The sophisticated user defense is an exception to the general duty to warn. (Citation.) The defense concerns warnings and does not apply to a cause of action for design defect. (Citation.) ... [¶] Under the sophisticated user defense, a manufacturer is not liable to a sophisticated user of its product for failure to warn about the product's dangers if the sophisticated user knew or should have known of the dangers. (Citation.) The ‘should have known’ portion of the formulation is an objective standard. (Citation.) ... The rationale supporting the defense is that the failure to warn about risks already known to a sophisticated user usually is not a proximate cause of the user's injuries. (Citation.)” (Collin v. CalPortland Co. (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th at pp. 601–602.)

 

Paragon relies on the “obvious danger” standard within the sophisticated user defense: “The obvious danger rule is an objective test, and the courts do not inquire into the user's subjective knowledge in such a case. In other words, even if a user was truly unaware of a product's hazards, that fact is irrelevant if the danger was objectively obvious. ...  Thus, under the sophisticated user defense, the inquiry focuses on whether the plaintiff knew, or should have known, of the particular risk of harm from the product giving rise to the injury.” (Johnson v. American Standard, Inc. (2008) 43 Cal.4th 56, 71.)

 

While the danger of silica dust finds medical support (See Declaration of Kenneth Rosenman), Paragon relies on Decedent’s “experience” as a fabricator to comprehend the medical dangers of silica dust caused by the cutting of artificial stone. The argument provides no evidentiary support establishing the “obvious” nature of such an injury. The reply also appears to mutate the position in the motion as pivoting to “misuse,” which the court finds factually and legally unsupported. The court, again, will not consider unmade, or perhaps even new legal argument raised in the reply. The court declines to find as a matter of law that such a danger exists for purposes of ruling on the motion. The court therefore finds Paragon, again, fails to shift the burden. (See McGonnell v. Kaiser Gypsum Co., Inc., supra, 98 Cal.App.4th at pp. 1103-1104; Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Const. Co., supra, 69 Cal.App.4th at p. 81.)

 

To the extent Paragon appears to offer a sophisticated intermediary defense, the court again finds no support based on an assumption the known hazard of silica particulate matter from stone fabrication. Other than citation to the public policy and reliance on self-awareness of the hazards, the motion again lacks legal support actually addressing the standard. The court also finds no legal or evidentiary support based on reliance and deflection under uncited OSHA and Cal OSHA standards. (Webb v. Special Electric Co., Inc. (2016) 63 Cal.4th 167, 187-188.)

 

The court therefore DENIES the motion for summary judgment based on the failure to establish both insufficient evidence or a complete defense on this basis. Plaintiff additionally establishes triable issues of material fact on the known hazards of silica particulate matter resulting from fabrication. [Rosenman and Burrows Decl.]

 

On the summary adjudication, to the extent Paragon relies on the lack of causation regarding proof of Paragon products in the presence of Decedent, and superseding conduct, the court denies the motion for summary adjudication as to the first, third, fourth, and fifth causes of action.

 

On the fraudulent concealment cause of action, Paragon additionally challenges the lack of any basis of duty to disclose any specific potential harms from the failure to warn. Paragon relies exclusively on the lack of any duty to disclose due to the absence of any fiduciary duty. “[T]he elements of a cause of action for fraud based on concealment are: ‘(1) the defendant must have concealed or suppressed a material fact, (2) the defendant must have been under a duty to disclose the fact to the plaintiff, (3) the defendant must have intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with the intent to defraud the plaintiff, (4) the plaintiff must have been unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact, and (5) as a result of the concealment or suppression of the fact, the plaintiff must have sustained damage. [Citation.]’” (Kaldenbach v. Mutual of Omaha Life Ins. Co. (2009) 178 Cal.App.4th 830, 850 (internal quotation marks omitted.)

 

“There are ‘four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts. [Citation.]’” (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336.) “Each of the other three circumstances in which nondisclosure may be actionable presupposes the existence of some other relationship between the plaintiff and defendant in which a duty to disclose can arise. … As a matter of common sense, such a relationship can only come into being as a result of some sort of transaction between the parties.” (Id. at pp. 336–337.)

 

“The requirement's purpose is to inform consumers about a product's hazards and faults of which they are unaware, so that they can refrain from using the product altogether or evade the danger by careful use.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co. (2011) 198 Cal.App.4th 1187, 1198.) Users include persons responsible for processing materials not privy to information regarding necessary safety protocols. The withholding of said information subjects a defendant to potential fraudulent concealment liability. (Id. at pp. 1199-1200.)

 

The operative complaint presents allegations regarding knowledge of the allegedly toxic substance, crystalline silica, dangers to workers engaged in the cutting, grinding, fabrication, polishing, etc. with the product, and failure to disclose potential toxicity upon working with said products. Decedent was specifically exposed and harmed as a result of the non-disclosed hazards and continued work with the supplied product. The allegations meet the standard for fraudulent concealment. (Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, 78-79.)

 

Under the consideration for product liability summary judgment, the court finds insufficient address of this standard by Paragon for purposes of shifting the burden. The court therefore denies the motion for summary adjudication on both the failure to shift the burden, and triable issues of material regarding failure to warn on allegedly known hazards caused by fabrication of products containing silica. [Rosenman Decl. and Declaration of Brian Barrow.]

 

On the fifth cause of action for breach of implied warranties, Paragon again contends Sanchez was at fault for not providing safety equipment, and the products otherwise conformed to safety standards. The argument lacks evidentiary support. Citing to Plaintiffs’ complaint (See Separate Statement of Material Fact numbers 24-29) will neither establish an absence of sufficient evidence or affirmatively establish a conforming product. The single concluding sentence regarding (the rejected) sophisticated user doctrine and “Bigler-Engler doctrines discussed infra” (e.g. 19:17-20:20) raises the “direct dealings” discussion, but in context of the fraudulent concealment claim. In other words, Paragon owed no duty to disclose. For the same reasons cited in the fraudulent concealment section, the court rejects this argument as well. The court otherwise declines to consider the new argument on “privity” and “fitness” raised in the reply in that it deprives Plaintiffs of an opportunity to respond. (U.S. Roofing, Inc. v. Credit Alliance Corp. (1991) 228 Cal.App.3d 1431, 1441; Balboa Ins. Co. v. Aguirre (1983) 149 Cal.App.3d 1002, 1010.) Regardless, for the reasons stated in the fraudulent cause of action the court finds a basis for a duty of disclosure to Decedent for purposes of the instant motion and declines to find as a matter of law a lack of said duty or other basis of a complete defense. (Bigler-Engler v. Breg, Inc. (2017) 7 Cal.App.5th 276, 313-315.) The motion AND reply otherwise fail to establish application of the privity requirement in the subject context fully taking into account and accepting Sanchez as an employer. “[E]xceptions to the privity requirement have been found in cases involving foodstuffs, drugs and pesticides, substances marketed with the knowledge the purchaser may not be the ultimate consumer of the product... The strict requirement of privity has also been excused when an inherently dangerous instrumentality causes harm to a buyer's employee. ... Whether these cases are viewed as expanding the doctrine of privity or relieving a plaintiff of the obligation to demonstrate privity in favor of the emerging tort doctrine of strict liability ... the result is the same.” (Jones v. ConocoPhillips Co., supra, 198 Cal.App.4th at p. 1201.)

 

On the last item for summary adjudication, punitive damages, Paragon offers a conclusive challenge regarding the lack of evidence to support the claim for punitive damages. On punitive damages, Civil Code section 3294, subdivision (c) authorizes punitive damages upon a showing of malice, oppression, or fraud, which are defined as follows:

 

(1) “Malice” means conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.

(2) “Oppression” means despicable conduct that subjects a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.

(3) “Fraud” means an intentional misrepresentation, deceit, or concealment of a material fact known to the defendant with the intention on the part of the defendant of thereby depriving a person of property or legal rights or otherwise causing injury.

 

“‘[M]alice does not require actual intent to harm. [Citation.] Conscious disregard for the safety of another may be sufficient where the defendant is aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his or her conduct and he or she willfully fails to avoid such consequences. [Citation.] Malice may be proved either expressly through direct evidence or by implication through indirect evidence from which the jury draws inferences.’ [Citations.]”  (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1299.)

 

Consistent with the prior part of the order, the court again finds the motion insufficiently addresses the evidence presented by Plaintiff regarding the knowing distribution of the silica containing product, including corporate knowledge and direction for the manufacture and distribution of an allegedly harmful product. [Index of Exhibits, Responses to Specially Drafted Interrogatories and Requests for Production of Documents.] (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 576–577; Cruz v. HomeBase (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 168.) Such conduct establishes the right to seek punitive damages under the malice standard. The motion for summary adjudication is therefore denied in its entirety.

 

In summary, the court denies the motion for summary judgment and summary adjudication.
