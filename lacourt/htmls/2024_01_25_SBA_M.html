ALERT

Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

 

Department M strongly encourages the use of  LA CourtConnect* for ALL hearings, without need for prior approval, unless live testimony by a witness is required.

 

The contact information for LA CourtConnect* is:

 

 

 https://lacourt.portalscloud.com/VCourt/

 

 

*Parties with a fee waiver on file may be eligible to appear at no/reduced cost


Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Lisa Popovich, et al. v. City of Los Angeles, et al.     

21STCV32814

1.      Lora L. LoCoco’s Application to Appear as Counsel Pro Hac Vice for Defendants Herc Rentals Inc. and Herminio Rodriguez

2.      Anthony Steinike’s Application to Appears as Counsel Pro Hac Vice for Defendants Herc Rentals Inc. and Herminio Rodriguez      


TENTATIVE RULING

 

            Lora L. LoCoco and Anthony Steinike’s Applications to Appear as Counsel Pro Hac Vice for Defendants Herc Rentals Inc. and Herminio Rodriguez are granted.  

 

            Background

 

            Plaintiffs filed the Complaint on October 6, 2022. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. This action arises out of a collision which occurred on June 27, 2022 between a commercial truck and trailer parked on Imperial Highway and a commuter bus. Lisa Popovich was a passenger on the bus.

 

            Application to Appear Pro Hac Vice

 

            Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 9.40 states, in relevant part:

            “(d) The application must state:

            (1) The applicant's residence and office address;

            (2) The courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission;

            (3) That the applicant is a licensee in good standing in those courts;

            (4) That the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court;

            (5) The title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and

            (6) The name, address, and telephone number of the active licensee of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.

            (e) An applicant for permission to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule must pay a reasonable fee not exceeding $50 to the State Bar of California with the copy of the application and the notice of hearing that is served on the State Bar.”

 

            The applicants have met all the requirements mandated in Rule 9.40. The applications to be admitted as counsel pro hac vice are therefore granted.

 

            Lora L. LoCoco and Anthony Steinike are admitted as counsel pro hac vice in the pending matter.

 

            Defendants are ordered to give notice of this ruling.

Sylvia Riza De Lozano v. American Honda Motor Co., Inc., et al.

22TRCV01316

1.      American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Appear, Testify, and to Produce Documents at Deposition and Request for Monetary Sanctions  

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

American Honda Motor Co., Inc.’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff to Appear, Testify, and to Produce Documents at Deposition and Request for Monetary Sanctions is granted.

 

Background

 

Plaintiff filed the Complaint on November 22, 2022. This is a “Lemon Law” case brought by Plaintiff concerning a 2019 Honda Passport. Plaintiff alleges that the vehicle suffers from widespread defects, including electrical and engine defects. Defendant has been unable to repair their vehicle within a reasonable number of attempts. Plaintiff alleges the following cause of action: 1. Violation of Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty.

 

Motion to Compel Deposition

 

The party noticing the deposition may move for an order compelling appearance at the deposition and production of documents, pursuant to the deposition notice, from the party deponent who fails to appear or produce materials requested in the deposition notice, and who has not served a valid objection under § 2025.410(a).  CCP § 2025.450(a). 

 

“The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040, or, when the deponent fails to attend the deposition and produce the documents, electronically stored information, or things described in the deposition notice, by a declaration stating that the petitioner has contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance.”  CCP § 2025.450(b)(2). Defendant set forth a meet and confer declaration stating that Defendant contacted the deponent to inquire about the nonappearance. (Decl., Monica Y. Hernandez, ¶¶ 5-19.)

 

Defendant moves for an order compelling Plaintiff appear, testify, and produce documents at her deposition. The motion is made on the grounds that despite numerous continuances and schedule accommodations, deponent has failed to appear for her deposition. Defendant states that it has attempted to proceed with the deposition on numerous dates after the initial noticed date but has been thwarted each time with Plaintiff contending that she is not available. (Id.) Defendant also seeks sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel of record.

 

Plaintiff filed a limited opposition stating that she will, yet again, appear for deposition on February 21 or February 22, 2024. It does appear that the parties should have been able to obtain a date amenable to both parties for the deposition. However, since the deposition has not yet taken place and there is no dispute that Plaintiff has not appeared for her properly noticed deposition, Defendant’s motion to compel deposition is granted. In addition, sanctions are warranted because it was only after the motion was filed that Plaintiff provided the dates above. Defendant incurred attorneys’ fees in the preparation and appearance of this motion.

 

Defendant’s motion to compel deposition is granted. Plaintiff is ordered to appear for her deposition at a date and time that can be agreed upon by the parties. If no agreement can be reached, Plaintiff is ordered to appear at a reasonable date and time to be determined by the moving party.

 

Sanctions

 

Defendant’s request for monetary sanctions is granted. Sanctions are awarded in favor of Defendant and against Plaintiff and Plaintiff’s counsel in the total amount of $1,575.00. The hourly rate and time expended as set forth in the declaration of Monica Y. Hernandez is reasonable. Sanctions are payable within 30 days of this date.

 

Defendant is ordered to give notice of this ruling.

            Stephanie Jungers, et al. v. Roberto Aguilero Toledo, et al.

            23TRCV01039

1.      Stephanie Jungers, et al.’s Motion to Vacate the Dismissal      

 

 

COURT RULING

 

            Stephanie Jungers, et al.’s Motion to Vacate the Dismissal is granted.  

      

            Background

 

            Plaintiffs filed the Complaint on April 5, 2023. Plaintiffs allege the following facts. On April 8, 2021, Plaintiffs were involved in a motor vehicle accident with Defendant.

 

            Motion to Set Aside Dismissal

 

            CCP § 473(b) states, in relevant part: “The court may, upon any terms as may be just, relieve a party or his or her legal representative from a judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding taken against him or her through his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect. Application for this relief shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed therein, otherwise the application shall not be granted, and shall be made within a reasonable time, in no case exceeding six months, after the judgment, dismissal, order, or proceeding was taken.  . . .  Notwithstanding any other requirements of this section, the court shall, whenever an application for relief is made no more than six months after entry of judgment, is in proper form, and is accompanied by an attorney's sworn affidavit attesting to his or her mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect, vacate any (1) resulting default entered by the clerk against his or her client, and which will result in entry of a default judgment, or (2) resulting default judgment or dismissal entered against his or her client, unless the court finds that the default or dismissal was not in fact caused by the attorney's mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect. The court shall, whenever relief is granted based on an attorney's affidavit of fault, direct the attorney to pay reasonable compensatory legal fees and costs to opposing counsel or parties. . ..”

 

            Plaintiffs have adequately established that the dismissal was entered due to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or neglect of Plaintiffs’ counsel. Plaintiffs’ counsel states that he failed to calendar the October 25, 2023 Order to Show Cause hearing. Therefore, Plaintiffs’ counsel did not attend resulting in the dismissal of the action. (Decl., Ron A. Rosen Janfaza, ¶¶ 7-8.) Thus, the motion to set aside dismissal is granted and the case is restored to the active calendar.

 

            Plaintiffs are ordered to give notice of this ruling.
