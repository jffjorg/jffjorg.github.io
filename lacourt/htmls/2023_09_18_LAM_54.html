DEPARTMENT 54 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV19934    Hearing Date: September 18, 2023    Dept: 54

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

Khaled J. Al-Sabah,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case

No.:

 

 

20STCV19934 (Consolidated w/ 23STCV04590)

 

vs.

 

 

Tentative Ruling

 

 

Michael Edward Bass, et al.,

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date: September 18, 2023

Department 54, Judge Maurice A. Leiter

Demurrer to First Amended Cross-Complaint;

Moving Party: Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Khaled J. Al-Sabah

Responding Party: Defendant/Cross-Complainant Michael Edward Bass

 

T/R:     AL-SABAH’S DEMURRER IS SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

AL-SABAH TO NOTICE. 

 

If the parties wish to submit on the tentative, please email the courtroom at SMCdept54@lacourt.org with notice to opposing counsel (or self-represented party) before 8:00 am on the day of the hearing.

 

The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

                On July 15, 2020, Plaintiff Khaled J. Al-Sabah filed the operative first amended complaint against Defendants Michael Edward Bass, Ronald Richards, and Usman Shaikh, asserting causes of action for breach of fiduciary duty, breach of contract, theft, money had and received, conversion, and accounting. Al-Sabah alleges that Bass was to be Al-Sabah’s “representative” in the United States. Al-Sabah alleges he wired Bass $2.5 million to hire attorney Defendant Shaikh to represent Al-Sabah in an unrelated embezzlement lawsuit. Al-Sabah alleges that Bass kept $2 million of his money.

 

On December 20, 2022, Bass filed the operative third amended cross-complaint against Al-Sabah, asserting causes of action for (1) breach of written contract; (2) breach of oral contract; (3) intentional interference with prospective economic advantage; and (4) intentional interference with contractual business relations. The Court sustained Al-Sabah's demurrer to the TACC without leave to amend.

 

On March 2, 2023, Plaintiff Al-Sabah filed a new action for fraudulent conveyance against Defendant Bass in case no. 23STCV04590. This action was consolidated with 20STCV19934. On July 12, 2023, Bass filed a first amended cross-complaint in case no. 23STCV04590 against Al-Sabah for RICO violations.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A demurrer to a complaint may be taken to the whole complaint or to any of the causes of action in it.  (CCP § 430.50(a).)  A demurrer challenges only the legal sufficiency of the complaint, not the truth of its factual allegations or the plaintiff's ability to prove those allegations.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High Sch. Dist. (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 726, 732.)  The court must treat as true the complaint's material factual allegations, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  (Id. at 732-33.)  The complaint is to be construed liberally to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Id. at 733.)

 

Al-Sabah demurs to the FACC for RICO violations on the ground that it fails to state sufficient facts. RICO requires conduct of an enterprise through a pattern of racketeering activity affecting interstate commerce, and an injury caused to plaintiff’s business or property. (McMartin v. Children’s Institute International (1989) 212 Cal.App.3d 1393, 1406.) Plaintiffs are required to “‘specifically plead the time, place or nature of the alleged communications constituting racketeering activity . . . .’” (People v. Highland Federal Savings & Loan (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 1692, 1713, 1715.)

 

Bass alleges Al-Sabah engaged in racketeering activity by breaching Bass’s and Al-Sabah's alleged agreement that Al-Sabah would pay Bass $5,000,000.00 in exchange for vague services. Bass also alleges Al-Sabah engaged in racketeering activity by scheming with third-party David Ross to transfer title of a corporation owned by Bass to another third-party, David Turner.

 

These allegations are insufficient to state RICO claims against Al-Sabah. Al-Sabah's alleged breach of contract is not racketeering activity and the remaining allegations against Al-Sabah are limited and conclusory. Bass has not alleged an enterprise or pattern of racketeering activity. Nor has Bass shown how a further amended complaint would address these deficiencies.

 

Al-Sabah's demurrer is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.

The Court considers the moving papers, opposition, and reply.

 

BACKGROUND

 

On November 21, 2022, Plaintiff Saul Larner filed a complaint against Defendants Christopher Sorley and Michele A. Dobson, asserting a cause of action for malicious prosecution and SLAPP-back. Plaintiff alleges that Defendants, who were counsel for the defendants in related case 22STCV12097, maliciously brought anti-SLAPP motions in the related case. The Court denied the motions on October 25, 2022.

 

On July 7, 2023, the Court sustained Defendant Sorley’s demurrer to the complaint without leave to amend.

 

ANALYSIS

 

CCP § 128.7(b) provides that by signing and filing a pleading, an attorney “is certifying that to the best of the person’s knowledge, information, and belief, formed after an inquiry reasonable under the circumstances, all of the following conditions are met: (1) [i]t is not being presented primarily for an improper purpose, such as to harass or to cause unnecessary delay or needless increase in the cost of litigation[;] (2) [t]he claims, defenses, and other legal contentions therein are warranted by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for the extension, modification, or reversal of existing law or the establishment of new law[;] (3) [t]he allegations and other factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if specifically so identified, are likely to have evidentiary support after a reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery[;] (4) [t]he denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on a lack of information or belief.” CCP § 128.7 authorizes the court to impose appropriate sanctions upon attorneys or parties that have violated subsection (b). (CCP § 128.7(c).) 

 

CCP § 128.5 provides that “[a] trial court may order a party, the party’s attorney, or both, to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney’s fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.”  (CCP § 128.5(a).)  “‘Actions or tactics’ include, but are not limited to, the making or opposing of motions or the filing and service of a complaint, cross-complaint, answer, or other responsive pleading.”  (CCP § 128.5(b)(1).)  “‘Frivolous’ means totally and completely without merit or for the sole purpose of harassing an opposing party.”  (CCP § 128.5(b)(2).) 

 

A. Defendant Sorley’s Motion for Sanctions

 

Defendant Sorley moves for monetary[1] sanctions against Plaintiff on the ground that Plaintiff brought and maintained this action in bad faith. Defendant represents that Plaintiff was repeatedly told by counsel that there was no legal basis for Plaintiff’s claims, but Plaintiff refused to withdraw the suit. Defendant also asserts Plaintiff has engaged in various bad faith litigation tactics such as inappropriate communications, threats of additional litigation, and frivolous State Bar complaints. Defendant requests $7,600.00 in attorney’s fees.

 

The Court finds monetary sanctions appropriate. As discussed in the Court’s ruling on Sorley’s demurrer, this action is not legally tenable. Plaintiff was informed of this several times by Defendants with citations to clear statutory law. Plaintiff then refused to dismiss the action and instead engaged in threats and filed bar complaints.

 

Defendant Sorley’s motion is GRANTED.

 

B. Defendant Dobson’s Motion for Sanctions

 

Defendant Dobson moves for terminating and monetary sanctions against Plaintiff on the ground that Plaintiff brought and maintained this action in bad faith. As stated, there is no legal basis for this action. The plain language of the SLAPP-back statutory provisions state that SLAPP-back actions may be filed by a prevailing Defendant against the Plaintiff who filed the underlying SLAPP suit. They do not allow SLAPP-back actions against Defendants who unsuccessfully bring an anti-SLAPP. Plaintiff has been informed of this and has still refused to dismiss the action. Terminating and monetary sanctions are warranted here. Defendant requests $1,280.00 in attorney’s fees.

 

Defendant Dobson’s motion is GRANTED.

 


[1] Defendant’s moving papers also seek terminating sanctions. This aspect of the motion is moot as the Court entered judgment in favor of Defendant on July 19, 2023 following Defendant’s successful demurrer.

BACKGROUND

 

On April 17, 2023, Plaintiff Chad Darling filed a complaint against Defendants Arc Point Law and Mark Piesner, asserting causes of action for (1) legal malpractice; (2) breach of fiduciary duty; and (3) breach of contract. Plaintiff alleges Defendants failed to use reasonable care in representing Plaintiff in divorce proceedings.

 

ANALYSIS

 

A. Demurrer to Complaint

 

A demurrer to a complaint may be taken to the whole complaint or to any of the causes of action in it.  (CCP § 430.50(a).)  A demurrer challenges only the legal sufficiency of the complaint, not the truth of its factual allegations or the plaintiff's ability to prove those allegations.  (Picton v. Anderson Union High Sch. Dist. (1996) 50 Cal. App. 4th 726, 732.)  The court must treat as true the complaint's material factual allegations, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  (Id. at 732-33.)  The complaint is to be construed liberally to determine whether a cause of action has been stated.  (Id. at 733.)

 

Defendant demurs to the third cause of action for breach of contract on the ground that Plaintiff failed to attach a copy of the contract or plead its terms. “The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.)

 

Plaintiff alleges he and Defendants entered into a representation agreement for legal services. Plaintiff alleges Defendant failed to perform these services competently and Plaintiff was harmed as a result. This is sufficient to allege breach of contract.

 

Defendant’s demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

 

B. Motion to Strike

“Any party, within the time allowed to response to a pleading, may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part" of that pleading. (CCP § 435(b)(1).) “The Court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false or improper matter asserted in any pleading; (b) Strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the Court." (CCP § 436.)

Defendants move to strike allegations that Defendants failed to file a CCP § 473(b) motion in the underlying action, allegations regarding violations of the professional rules, and the prayer for prejudgment interest. Defendants assert they did bring a 473(b) motion in the underlying action, the allegations regarding violations of the professional rules contradict other allegations, and Plaintiff cannot recover prejudgment interest because there is no sum certain of damages.

Plaintiff does not oppose striking the 473(b) motion allegations. Plaintiff asserts Defendants failed to meet and confer regarding the allegations of violations of the professional rules and argues that there is a possibility for prejudgment interest in this case.

As Plaintiff does not oppose striking the 473(b) motion allegations, the motion is GRANTED as to those allegations. The motion is also GRANTED as to the allegations regarding violations of the professional rules as they are contradictory to other allegations. The motion is DENIED as to prejudgment interest, as a basis for prejudgment interest may be apparent later in the action.
