DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SMCV00885    Hearing Date: June 18, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

June 18, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV00885

MOTION

Demurrer to First Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant Paradigm Talent Agency, LLC

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff 8942 Wilshire, LLC

 

MOTION

 

Plaintiff 8942 Wilshire, LLC (“Plaintiff”) asserts in its First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) two causes of action against its former commercial tenant, Defendant Paradigm Talent Agency LLC (“Defendant’) for (1) breach of lease and (2) fraud. 

 

Defendant now demurs to the second cause of action for fraud for failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (e).  Plaintiff opposes the Demurrer and Defendant replies.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Defendant requests judicial notice of California Executive Order N-33-20.  Judicial notice may be taken of “Official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.”  (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (c).)  Therefore, the Court takes judicial notice of Executive Order N-33-20.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.     DEMURRER

 

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In testing the sufficiency of a cause of action, a court accepts “[a]s true all material facts properly pled and matters which may be judicially noticed but disregard contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.  [A court also gives] the complaint a reasonable interpretation, reading it as a whole and its parts in their context.”  (290 Division (EAT), LLC v. City & County of San Francisco (2022) 86 Cal.App.5th 439, 450 [cleaned up]; Hacker v. Homeward Residential, Inc. (2018) 26 Cal.App.5th 270, 280 [“in considering the merits of a demurrer, however, “the facts alleged in the pleading are deemed to be true, however improbable they may be”].)

 

Further, in ruling on a demurrer, a court must “liberally construe” the allegations of the complaint “with a view to substantial justice between the parties.”  (See Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)  

 

In summary, “[d]etermining whether the complaint is sufficient as against the demurrer on the ground that it does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, the rule is that if on consideration of all the facts stated it appears the plaintiff is entitled to any relief at the hands of the court against the defendants the complaint will be held good although the facts may not be clearly stated, or may be intermingled with a statement of other facts irrelevant to the cause of action shown, or although the plaintiff may demand relief to which he is not entitled under the facts alleged.”  (Gressley v. Williams (1961) 193 Cal.App.2d 636, 639.)

 

A.    FAILURE TO STATE A CAUSE OF ACTION

 

The elements for fraudulent misrepresentation are “(1) the defendant represented to the plaintiff that an important fact was true; (2) that representation was false; (3) the defendant knew that the representation was false when the defendant made it, or the defendant made the representation recklessly and without regard for its truth; (4) the defendant intended that the plaintiff rely on the representation; (5) the plaintiff reasonably relied on the representation; (6) the plaintiff was harmed; and (7) the plaintiff's reliance on the defendant's representation was a substantial factor in causing that harm to the plaintiff.”  (Graham v. Bank of America, N.A. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 605–606.) 

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“One of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.”  (Ibid.)

 

Here, the FAC alleges:

 

6. Landlord is the owner of the real property located at 8942 Wilshire Boulevard, Beverly Hills, California 90210 (the “Premises”).

 

7. Landlord and Tenant are parties to the July 14, 2017 written office lease agreement (as amended, “Lease”), pursuant to which Tenant leases the Premises from Landlord. A true and correct copy of the Lease, including all amendments, is attached hereto as Exhibit “1” and incorporated herein by this reference.

 

8. As required by the Lease, Tenant tendered a letter of credit as security for Tenant’s performance under the Lease (“LOC”). The Lease permits Landlord to draw on the LOC in the event of any monetary default under the Lease by Tenant

 

9. In May of 2020, Tenant’s former Chief Executive Officer, Michael Dates, contacted Landlord by phone and written correspondence to request relief from its obligation to pay rent under the Lease, due to the alleged impact of the COVID-19 Pandemic (“Pandemic”) on Tenant’s business and finances. Tenant represented to Landlord that it was in “financial distress” and that the Pandemic “caused a temporary and unprecedented disruption to all areas of Paradigm’s business.”

 

10. As part of its request for relief from its rental obligations under the Lease, Tenant provided Landlord with financial records relating to Tenant’s business and financial performance. Tenant represented to Landlord that the financial records and summaries were true and accurate. In reality, the financial records and summaries were misrepresented and inaccurate. Defendant provided financial records to Plaintiff that intentionally concealed Defendant’s pending transaction with Wasserman Media Group, LLC (“Wasserman”) regarding the sale of its assets and its pending new business relationship with Platinum Equity, LLC (“Platinum Equity”).

 

11. Ultimately, based in part on the information provided by Tenant, and in reliance on the accuracy and completeness of such information, Landlord and Tenant agreed to a modified rent schedule up to and including July of 2021. Landlord and Tenant memorialized this agreement as to rent relief and adjusted rental payment terms in a December 21, 2020 Amendment to the Lease (“December 2020 Amendment”).

 

12. Sometime in late 2020 or early 2021, and shortly after consummating the December 2020 Amendment, Tenant sold a significant portion of its business and assets, including its entire music department, for consideration estimated to be in excess of $150 million to Wasserman.

 

13. Tenant never advised Landlord of its plans to sell a significant portion of its assets, and never advised Landlord that its financial statements did not reflect this anticipated transaction. Additionally, Tenant did not provide Landlord any of the terms relating to this sale of a significant portion of Tenant’s assets, at or after the consummation of this transaction, or an accounting of the disposition of the consideration received by Tenant as a result of this transaction.

 

14. Landlord alleges, on information and belief, that the funds and consideration received by Tenant from the sale of a portion of its business and assets in 2020 or 2021, referenced above, were transferred and/or conveyed in an effort to defraud Tenant’s creditors, including without limitation Landlord here.

 

15. Tenant misrepresented its financial condition to obtain rent relief from Landlord. Tenant was aware of the pending sale of its music assets to Wasserman when it requested rent relief from Landlord. In a press release, Tenant’s principal, Sam Gores, stated that the sale to Wasserman “is a win for all parties and a vital step on the restructuring path we embarked upon more than a year ago.” (Hollywood Reporter, March 17, 2021.) This press release reveals that Tenant had decided, before demanding rent relief from Landlord in May of 2020, that Tenant would complete a major restructuring of its business, including selling valuable assets, but it elected not to advise Landlord about this sale or strategy. This information would have been of great significance to Landlord’s willingness to negotiate with Tenant regarding its obligations under the Lease. This timeline shows that Tenant intentionally misrepresented its financial condition to ensure that Landlord would grant it rent relief.

 

16. Tenant was also aware that Platinum Equity, chaired by Sam Gores’ brother Tom Gores, had taken a controlling stake in Tenant in June of 2020, but Tenant failed to disclose this information to Landlord when seeking rent relief. This means that Tenant was backed by $6 billion in individual wealth held by Tom Gores, and the many more billions of dollars held by Platinum Equity. In requesting rent relief purportedly based on the effects of the Pandemic, Tenant intentionally concealed from Landlord this new backing and its major “restructuring plan,” which likely already included a determination to reduce Tenant’s space costs, irrespective of the supposed impacts of the Pandemic. Indeed, Tenant later publicly confirmed that its desire to vacate the Premises was a conscious and intentional business strategy, spurred by the “lessons” learned during the Pandemic, and its desire to provide a “progressive hybrid work model” for its employees. (Los Angeles Times, February 9, 2022.) Tenant did not disclose any of this information to Landlord when seeking rent relief.

 

17. Less than one year after Landlord granted Tenant’s request for rent relief, in approximately July of 2021, Tenant advised Landlord that it would not satisfy its rental obligations under the Lease, and that Landlord should look to the LOC for recovery of rents due under the Lease going forward.

 

18. Upon being advised by Tenant that Tenant intended to breach the Lease, and would not pay the monetary obligations due under the Lease, Landlord engaged in a number of discussions with Tenant and its representatives, as well as representatives of Platinum Equity, which had some economic or financial interest in Tenant and in Tenant’s attempt to avoid the obligations of the Lease.

 

19. Notwithstanding the above, which left Landlord facing millions of dollars in uncollected rent, Landlord continued to discuss Tenant’s options for meeting its obligations under the Lease with Tenant and Platinum Equity, now its controlling stakeholder, all the while reserving all of Landlord’s rights under the Lease.

 

20. In late 2021, Landlord was advised that an unrelated entertainment company was interested in leasing the entire Premises that was then occupied by Tenant. Tenant and Landlord engaged in a number of discussions regarding this potential transaction, and agreed that it would be beneficial to both Landlord and Tenant if this transaction were to proceed, as the income generated by this replacement lease of the Premises would significantly mitigate the damages that Landlord had suffered, and would suffer, by virtue of Tenant’s breach and repudiation of the Lease.

 

21. Notwithstanding Landlord’s good faith efforts to accommodate a potential transaction with an unrelated company to lease all or a portion of the Premises, and thus reduce Landlord’s damages attributable to Tenant’s breach, the transaction was not consummated.

 

22. Landlord understands that Tenant has secured a new location for its business operations, even though Tenant remains liable on the Lease with Landlord.

 

23. Tenant has continuously failed to pay to Landlord the monetary amounts due under the Lease since August of 2021.

 

24. Landlord has drawn on the LOC to recover the amounts that Tenant has failed to pay to Landlord, which are due under the Lease. These draws have fully exhausted the balance under the LOC.

 

[…]

 

37. The above representations were knowingly false. In reality, Tenant was in the process of selling off significant parts of its business at the same time it was claiming business turbulence and financial distress relating to the Pandemic. Tenant sold a significant portion of its business and assets, including its entire music department in late 2020 or early 2021 to Wasserman. Based on multiple press reports, Tenant had decided, before demanding rent relief from Landlord in May of 2020, that Tenant would complete a major restructuring of its business, including selling valuable assets, but it elected not to tell Landlord about this strategy. This information would have been of great significance to Landlord’s willingness to negotiate with Tenant regarding its obligations under the Lease. This timeline shows that Tenant intentionally misrepresented its financial condition to ensure that Landlord would grant it rent relief.

 

38. Tenant was also aware that Platinum Equity chaired by Sam Gores’ brother Tom, had taken a controlling stake in Tenant in June of 2020, but it failed to disclose this information to Landlord during rent negotiations. This means that Tenant was backed by $6 billion in individual wealth held by Tom Gores, and the many more billions of dollars held by Platinum Equity. In requesting rent relief purportedly based on the effects of the Pandemic, Tenant intentionally concealed from Landlord this new backing and its major “restructuring plan,” which likely already included a determination to reduce Tenant’s space costs, irrespective of the supposed impacts of the Pandemic.

 

39. The representations regarding Tenant’s financial distress were knowingly false. The true facts are that Tenant did not require rent relief: Tenant concealed the intended sale of its assets to Wasserman and its relationship with Platinum Equity to reduce or eliminate its rent obligations under the Lease.

 

40. When Tenant made these misrepresentations, it knew them to be false and made them with the intention to deceive and defraud Landlord and to induce Landlord to act in reliance on them. Landlord justifiably and detrimentally relied on these representations by granting Tenant rent relief.

 

41. As a result of Tenant’s actions, Landlord has sustained damages in an amount exceeding the jurisdictional minimum of this Court, and that is subject to proof at the time of trial.

 

42. In undertaking the actions and conduct described above, Tenant acted with the intention to deceive and defraud Landlord and was guilty of fraud, oppression, and malice. In addition to actual damages, Landlord is entitled to an award of exemplary and punitive damages in an amount to be determined at trial.

 

 

(FAC ¶¶ 6-24; 37-42.) 

 

Thus the FAC adequately pleads with particularity the who (Defendant’s former Chief Executive Officer, Michael Dates); what (COVID-19 related financial distress requiring a rent reduction/abatement); when (May 2020); and how (phone calls and written correspondence) of the alleged fraudulent misrepresentation.  Further, the FAC provides specific details about various pending and imminent revenue streams available to Defendant when the representations were made, demonstrating Defendant knew at the time that its financial prospects were not as dire as represented to Plaintiff. 

 

Defendant argues that the allegations regarding financial statements that were allegedly provided to Plaintiff lack the requisite details.  But the specific contents of the financial statements Defendant provided to Plaintiff are generally within Defendant’s knowledge, and as such, less specificity is required regarding the specific contents of those statements.

 

Defendant further argues that Plaintiff fails to allege damages stemming from the alleged fraud.  To the contrary, Defendant alleges that in reliance upon Plaintiff’s representations about its financial situation, Defendant modified the rental agreement and accepted lower amounts of rent.

 

Whether Defendant actually made fraudulent misrepresentations about its financial situation and whether Plaintiff actually suffered the damages alleged are factual questions to be determined at later stages of the litigation.  But for pleading purposes, Plaintiff has asserted a prima facie claim for fraud against Defendant.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the reasons stated, the Court overrules Defendant’s Demurrer to the Second Cause of Action for Fraud, and orders Defendant to file and serve an Answer to the FAC on or before July 9, 2024. 

 

Defendant shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.

MOTIONS

 

            Rafi Moghadam of Halo Law, counsel for Defendants Michael Navi and Millbrook Kitchen, Inc. (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel from both Defendants, citing the clients’ unresponsiveness.  The motions are unopposed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 284 provides “[t]he attorney in an action or special proceeding may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination as follows: 1. Upon the consent of both client and attorney, filed with the clerk, or entered in the minutes; 2. Upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”

 

Procedural Requirements

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, requires:

 

(1) the motion must be made on form MC-051; (subd. (a));

 

(2) it must be accompanied by a declaration on form MC-052 stating why the motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) instead of a consent brought under section 284(1); (subd. (c));

 

(3) a proposed order on form MC-053 must be lodged with the court, specifying all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known; (subd. (e)); and

 

(4) The documents must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. (subd. (d).)

 

If the notice is served by mail or electronic service, it must be accompanied by a declaration indicating that the address served is the current address, or in the case of service by mail, that it was served on the last known address and a more current address could not be located after reasonable efforts within 30 days before filing the motion.  (Ibid.)  The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Substantive Requirements

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(a) outlines the reasons a lawyer must withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client is bringing an action, conducting a defense, asserting a position in litigation, or taking an appeal, without probable cause and for the purpose of harassing or maliciously injuring any person;

 

(2)   the representation will result in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or the State Bar Act;

 

(3)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it unreasonably difficult to carry out the representation effectively; or

 

(4)   the client discharges the lawyer.

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(b) outlines the reasons a lawyer may withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client insists upon presenting a claim or defense in litigation, or asserting a position or making a demand in a non-litigation matter, that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law;

 

(2)   the client either seeks to pursue a criminal or fraudulent course of conduct or has used the lawyer’s services to advance a course of conduct that the lawyer reasonably believes was a crime or fraud;

 

(3)   the client insists that the lawyer pursue a course of conduct that is criminal or fraudulent;

 

(4)   the client by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(5)   the client breaches a material term of an agreement with, or obligation, to the lawyer relating to the representation, and the lawyer has given the client a reasonable warning after the breach that the lawyer will withdraw unless the client fulfills the agreement or performs the obligation;

 

(6)   the client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the representation;

 

(7)   the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal;

 

(8)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(9)   a continuation of the representation is likely to result in a violation of these rules or the State Bar Act; or

 

(10)         the lawyer believes in good faith in a proceeding pending before a tribunal that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal.

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Counsel has filed forms MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053 for each Defendant.  The attorney declarations (MC-052) indicate that a motion was filed instead of filing a consent because, “Client is unresponsive and did not voluntarily consent to counsel's request to be relieved.”  (MC-052 at ¶ 2.)  As such, the Court finds that the motions comply with the Rules of Profession Conduct, rules 1.16(b)(4).) 

 

            The proofs of service indicates each of these forms was served on both clients by mail and electronic service and on counsel of record for Plaintiff electronically.  Therefore, the Court finds motion is both procedurally and substantively proper.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, the Court grants Counsel’s Motions to be Relieved as Counsel. 

 

Counsel must serve the signed orders (form MC-053), which shall include information about all future hearings and proceedings noticed by any party, or ordered by the Court, on Defendants Michael Navi and Millbrook Kitchen, Inc. and all other parties who have appeared in the action, within 10 days of the date of this Order, and file a proof of service of such.  Counsel will remain the attorney of record for Defendants until Counsel files the requisite proof of service.  (See Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1362(e).)
