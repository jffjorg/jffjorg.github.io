DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV11887    Hearing Date: October 4, 2023    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

LUIS SANCHEZ

 

         vs.

 

BRINK’S INCORPORATED, et al.

 

 Case No.:  21STCV11887

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: October 4, 2023

 

            Plaintiff’s motion for final approval of the Class Action and PAGA settlement is GRANTED.

 

            On 3/29/2021, Plaintiff Luis Sanchez (Plaintiff) on behalf of himself and other aggrieved employees filed suit against Brink’s Incorporated and Dunbar Armored, Inc., seek civil penalties pursuant to the Private Attorney General Act (PAGA), and Labor Code section 2698, et seq.

 

            On 7/13/2023, this Court granted preliminary approval of the Class Action and PAGA settlement.

 

            Now, Plaintiffs move for final approval of the class action settlement.

 

            The motion is unopposed. 

 

Discussion

 

The Class Action and PAGA Settlement Agreement and Class Action Notice (Settlement Agreement) provide for the following:

 

(1) A non-reversionary Gross Settlement Amount of $2,600,000.00 allocated to approximately 2,386 Class Members on a pro rata basis according to the number of weeks each Class Member worked during the Class Period;

 

(2) An award of up to one-third of the Gross Settlement Amount (currently $866,666.67) and up to $50,000.00 in reimbursement of costs to Plaintiffs’ Counsel for services rendered as counsel on this matter;

 

(3) Incentive Award of up to $10,000.00 to each Plaintiff ($40,000.00 in total);

 

(4) Settlement Administration fees and costs of up to $20,550.00; and

 

(5) Payment to the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency (LWDA) of $100,000.00 for civil penalties pursuant to PAGA. Seventy-five percent (75%) of this payment will be paid to the California Labor and Workforce Development Agency (LWDA Payment), and twenty-five percent (25%) will be paid to the Net Settlement Amount for distribution to Class Members.

 

Reasonableness and fairness are presumed where (1) the settlement is reached through “arms-length bargaining”; (2) investigation and discovery are “sufficient to allow counsel and the court to act intelligently”; (3) counsel is “experienced in similar litigation”; and (4) the percentage of objectors “is small.” (Dunk v. Ford Motor Co. (1996). 48 Cal. App. 4th 1794, 1802.)

 

The Court reviewed the proposed settlement for these conditions in its 7/13/2023 ruling on the motion for preliminary approval. At preliminary approval, Plaintiffs presented evidence that the Settlement is entitled to a presumption of fairness because (1) the Settlement was reached through arm’s length bargaining after formal mediation, (2) Plaintiffs conducted investigation, including both formal and informal discovery, and analyzed Class Members’ time and payroll records, and (3) Plaintiffs’ counsel are sufficiently experienced to be appointed Class Counsel. (See Plaintiffs’ Motion for Preliminary Approval filed 3/28/2023.) Since then, the final Dunk factor has been satisfied: only two Class Member have opted out, and no Class Members objected. As such, nothing has changed since preliminary approval which would now negate the presumption of fairness previously found.

 

After review, the Court also finds the attorney fees requested—$866,666.67, roughly one-third of the preliminary approved GSA, and litigation costs of $26,193.12—to be reasonable. (See Chavez v. Netflix, Inc. (2008) 162 Cal. App. 4th 43, 66, n.11 (“fee awards in class actions average around one-third of the recovery.”).

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiffs’ motion for final approval of the settlement is granted.

 

It is so ordered.

 

Dated:  October    , 2023

Case Number: 22STCV19201    Hearing Date: October 4, 2023    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MAHMUD REZA SIAMIZADE

 

         vs.

 

STEVEN MEIER, et al.

 

 Case No.:  22STCV19201 

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: October 4, 2023

 

Plaintiff’s motion to compel further is DENIED. Plaintiff is sanctioned $700.00.

 

On 6/13/2022, Plaintiff Mahmud Reza Siamizade (Plaintiff) filed suit against Steven Meier, LA Peer Surgery Center, Gholamreza Siamizade, and Masoumeh Siamizade. On 6/20/023, Plaintiff filed a third amended complaint (TAC) alleging: (1) professional negligence—wrongful death; and (2) fraud.

 

Now, Plaintiff moves to compel further responses from Defendant LA Peer (Defendant) to Special Interrogatories (Sets Two and Three).

 

Discussion

 

The discovery at issue was propounded by Plaintiff on Defendant on 5/23/2023 and 6/4/2023, respectively.

 

As a preliminary matter, the Court requires an IDC before a motion to compel further is heard on the merits.

 

Second, Plaintiff’s motion is procedurally and substantively defective. Plaintiff has not submitted a separate statement for this motion nor has he provided any factual or legal reasons for compelling further responses. (California Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(a)(2).)

 

            The Court finds sanctions against Plaintiff to be warranted. Plaintiff never contacted Defendant to have a telephonic meet and confer discussion regarding Defendant’s objections and responses to his discovery prior to filing his Motion. Not only is such meet-and-confer required, but it could have led to this procedurally defective motion being withdrawn without the need for a hearing.

 

Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff’s motion to compel further is denied. Plaintiff is sanctioned $700.00. ($350/hr x 2 hr.)

 

 

 

It is so ordered.

Case Number: BC705147    Hearing Date: October 4, 2023    Dept: 17

 

 

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

CALIFORNIA DEPARTMENT OF EDUCATION

                          

         vs.

 

GOLDEN DAYS SCHOOLS, INC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  BC705147

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  October 4, 2023

 

            The Parkers’ motion for reconsideration is DENIED.

 

            On 5/4/2018, Plaintiff California Department of Education (Plaintiff) filed suit against Golden Day Schools, Inc. and Clark Parker, alleging breach of contract and declaratory judgment.

 

            On 8/2/2023, the Court granted CDE’s motion for summary adjudication and Motion in Limine no. 1.

 

            Now, the Parkers move for reconsideration of those rulings.

 

Discussion

 

            The Parkers argue that they are entitled to reconsideration of the Court’s rulings on CDE’s motion for summary adjudication and motion in limine no. 1 based on new case law concerning when the statute of limitation starts to run where there is an administrative remedy, and because the Parkers did not have an opportunity to address or respond to arguments and evidence that CDE first made arguments in its reply briefing.

 

            After review, the Court does not find the grounds for reconsideration to exist.

 

            As to the first contention, the Parkers seek reconsideration based on Federal Energy Regulatory Commission v. Vitol Inc. (9th Cir., Aug. 18, 2023, No. 22-15584) __ F.4th __, 2023 WL 5316720 at *1, 2023 U.S. App. LEXIS 21720) (FERC v. Vitol).  However, first, this is a Ninth Circuit decision, and thus is not binding on this Court. Second, it is not persuasive authority because the relevant statute of limitations analysis here turns on CCP section 337, a state statute, whereas the analysis in FERC v. Vitol turned on the interplay between the Federal Power Act and 28 U.S.C. section 2462. As such, the facts and regulatory framework of FERC v. Vitol are different from the facts in this case. In that case, FERC was suing under the Federal Power Act, while in these cases, CDE is suing for breach of contract. The cause of action under the Federal Power Act accrued once the FERC had issued its penalty. A breach of contract action accrues when there is some act that is required under the contract that Golden Day did not perform. Golden Day did not breach those obligations until there was an obligation to repay. The obligations to repay did not occur until either (i) the Department had completed recalculations required by the OAH decision, or (ii) Golden Day had exhausted its appeal of the OAH decisions.

 

The Parkers argue that, under FERC v. Vitol, the breaches of contract relevant to Case BC705147 must have occurred as soon as the OAH issued its decision. However, the OAH decision does not indicate that Golden Day has to pay anything immediately. Rather, it says “the California Department of Education will recalculate the audit for FY 2006/2007 and FY 2007/2008, based on the Factual Findings and Legal Conclusions herein.” As noted by CDE, “Clearly implied by this language is that Golden Day does not have to do anything until that recalculation is completed. In addition, the OAH decision was on appeal. At one point, the Superior Court reversed a portion of the decision (the Court of Appeal later disagreed with this one finding of the Superior Court and affirmed all of the OAH’s decision). The statute of limitations should not run while the underlying OAH decision might be reversed.” (Opp., 15: 9-14.)

 

In sum, the Court finds that FERC v. Vitol has no persuasive application to the facts here because (1) the Court there was deciding when an cause of action accrues under the Federal Power Act, not when a cause of action accrues under any contracts; and (2) the Federal Power Act does not seem to grant defendants further appellate rights or provide for a recalculation of amounts owed, which was the case after the first OAH proceeding. In FERC v. Vitol, the defendant had an assessed penalty that was due immediately. In this case, Golden Day did not have an amount that it owed until, at the very least, that amount was recalculated by CDE. Rather, the OAG’s issuance of a decision indicated that the CDE needed to recalculate the amounts and bill those amounts to Golden Day. Golden Day’s later failure to pay those amounts, after the decision became final, is the relevant breach of contract that triggered the statute of limitations, not the issuance of the decision requiring the CDE to recalculate the amounts.

 

            As such, FERC v. Vitol is not new or different law under section 1008 because it is non-controlling authority that is distinguishable.

 

            As for their second contention, the Parkers argue the Court should reconsider its prior ruling because they say that they were not afforded an opportunity to respond to the new evidence and arguments raised by the Department’s in its reply.  In particular, the Parkers argue that CDE first raised the arguments that: (1) exhaustion of administrative remedies includes the writ proceedings to the Superior Court and the subsequent appeals, and thus— according to the CDE—the statute of limitations was tolled until the Supreme Court denied review; and (2) its breach of contract claim accrued on January 12, 2014, because that was thirty days after the CDE issued a letter demanding the amount owed pursuant to the OAH’s Decision.

 

            However, as to the first point, CDE’s motion for summary adjudication provided:

 

The Parkers have alleged 20 sparsely pled affirmative defenses, which are not adequate to put the Parkers’ contentions at issue. (Ex. 21; Ex. 23; Ex. 24.) “A plaintiff moving for summary judgment is not required to disprove affirmative defenses not sufficiently put at issue by the defendant's answer.” (Los Angeles Unified, supra, 57 Cal.App.5th at pp. 508-509; see also FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 384-385 [16 affirmative defenses which merely stated the name of the defense and that it barred recovery did not raise any issues of material fact that plaintiffs were required to disprove].) On their face, the Parkers’ affirmative defenses are inadequately pled. They state bare legal theories without facts, that are implausible or irrelevant.

 

            (Opp., 16: 19-24.)

 

            As such, the CDE’s motion was based on a contention that the Parkers’ statute of limitations defense was sparsely pled, and insufficiently supported by facts (In verified responses, the Parkers provide no facts to support their statute-of limitation defense). When the Parkers’ opposition raised (for the first time) their argument that CDE’s breach of contract cause of action accrued at an earlier time than the decision of the Court of Appeal, it was appropriate for CDE to address that new argument in its reply brief – it had no opportunity to do so previously. (Jacobs v. Coldwell Banker Residential Brokerage Co. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 438, 449.)

 

            As for the second contention, the Parkers argue that they “were not afforded an opportunity to respond to the new evidence and arguments raised by the CDE in its reply in support of its MIL. In reply to the MIL, the CDE raised for the first time the argument that the Parkers were given notice of the second OAH proceeding via a simple letter sent to their counsel in connection with this action on August 24, 2018.” (Motion to Reconsider, at pp. 12-17.) However, as noted by CDE in opposition, “the Department’s initial memorandum in support of its Motion in Limine provides a surfeit of different ways that the Parkers’ were virtually represented at the OAH proceedings and had notice of it. The notice that the Parkers reference in their Motion in Limine was filed with the Court on March 22, 2023 – months before the Parkers were required to oppose the motion in limine. (Ex. 29.) The Parkers’ opposition to the Motion in Limine raised the supposed due process limitations of the collateral estoppel. It was entirely appropriate for the Department, in its reply, to cite to evidence that was before the Court on that very issue. Replies are supposed to address the arguments in the opposition.” (Opp., 17: 18-27.)

 

            In sum, the Court finds an insufficient basis to conclude that the CDE improperly raised any argument for the first time in reply, or that there were arguments or evidence presented there which the CDE was deprived an opportunity to respond to. Notably, the Parkers did not raise any objection at the hearing nor did they ask for permission to submit responsive evidence or file a sur-reply. Moreover, the Parkers have not explained why they did not present oral argument at the hearing as to the contentions raised by the CDE in reply.

 

            The Court finds the Parkers have failed to present new facts or law under section 1008.

 

            Based on the foregoing, the Parkers’ motion for reconsideration is denied.

 

It is so ordered.
