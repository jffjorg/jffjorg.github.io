DEPARTMENT 61 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 19STLC01888    Hearing Date: January 31, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendants Yale Management Services, Inc. and A and L Associates, LLC’s Motion to Compel Responses to Form and Special Interrogatories from Plaintiff Paul Neese is GRANTED. Responses are ordered within 30 days.

 

Defendant to give notice.

 

I.                   MOTION TO COMPEL

A propounding party may demand a responding party to produce documents that are in their possession, custody or control. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010.) A party may likewise conduct discovery by propounding interrogatories to another party to be answered under oath. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.010, subd. (a).) The responding party must respond to the production demand either by complying, by representing that the party lacks the ability to comply, or by objecting to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.210.) The responding party must respond to the interrogatories by answering or objecting. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2030.210, subd. (a).) If the responding party fails to serve timely responses, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses to the production demand and interrogatories. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.)

 

A party who fails to serve a timely response to interrogatories or a demand for inspection waives any objection to the demand. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290, 2031.300.)

Defendants Yale Management Services, Inc. and A and L Associates, LLC (Defendants) served Plaintiff Paul S. Neese (Plaintiff) with Form and Special Interrogatories on August 22, 2023, with responses due on November 14, 2023, by agreement of the parties to extend the deadline multiple times. (Fischer Decl. ¶¶ 2–9.) No responses have been received. (Fischer Decl. ¶ 10.)

Defendants have shown the service of discovery, without timely response from Plaintiff. No opposition to the motion has been filed.

The motion is GRANTED.


Case Number: 20STCV22971    Hearing Date: January 31, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendants NewRez LLC dba Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing and The Bank of New York Mellon fka The Bank of New York, as Trustee for the Certificateholders of CWALT, Inc., Alternative Loan Trust 2007-6, Mortgage Pass-Through Certificates, Series 2007-6’s Motion for Judgment on the Pleadings is contingent upon the ruling on Plaintiff’s Petition to Administer Estate of Austeene George Cooper in Probate Case No. 20STPB07802. If the petition is denied, the present motion shall be GRANTED, without leave to amend. If the petition is granted, this motion shall be DENIED.

 

I.                   MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

A party may move for a judgment on the pleadings as to an entire complaint or as to a particular cause of action in a complaint. (Code Civ. Proc. § 438 subd. (c)(2)(A).) If a defendant moves for a judgment on the pleadings and argues that a complaint does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action against that defendant, then the court should grant a defendant’s motion only if the court finds as a matter of law that the complaint fails to alleges facts sufficient to constitute the cause of action. (See id., § 438 subd. (c)(1)(B)(ii); see also Mechanical Contractors Assn. v. Greater Bay Area Assn. (1998) 66 Cal.App.4th 672, 677.)

 

“The standard for granting a motion for judgment on the pleadings is essentially the same as that applicable to a general demurrer, that is, under the state of the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.” (Bezirdjian v. O’Reilly (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 316, 321.) When considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, the court not only should assume that all facts alleged in the SAC are true but also should give those alleged facts a liberal construction. (See Gerawan Farming, Inc. v. Lyons (2000) 24 Cal.4th 468, 515–516, 101 Cal.Rptr.2d 470, 12 P.3d 720.) In particular, the court should liberally construe the alleged facts “‘with a view to attaining substantial justice among the parties.’ [Citation.]” (See Alliance Mortgage Co. v. Rothwell (1995) 10 Cal.4th 1226, 1232, 44 Cal.Rptr.2d 352, 900 P.2d 601.)

 

The present motion mirrors in many respects the prior motion for judgment on the pleadings this court granted with leave to amend on December 8, 2023.Defendants NewRez LLC dba Shellpoint Mortgage Servicing and The Bank of New York Mellon fka The Bank of New York, as Trustee for the Certificate holders of CWALT, Inc., Alternative Loan Trust 2007-6, Mortgage Pass-Through Certificates, Series 2007-6 (Defendants) move for judgment on the Third Amended Complaint (TAC) on the grounds that Plaintiff Kristi Courtois’ (Plaintiff) remaining claims for elder abuse, violation of the Homeowners Bill of Rights (HBOR), Business & Professions Code § 17200 (UCL), and negligence are barred by her lack of standing.

 

As with the previous motion, Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s claim to standing rests upon her status as executor of Decedent Austeene G. Cooper’s estate, when Plaintiff has not in fact been appointed as same. Although Defendants acknowledge that Plaintiff was granted Special Letters of Administration from the probate court to prosecute the present action, they note that on August 4, 2023, the probate court denied Plaintiff’s petition for probate of will on the grounds that Plaintiff had drafted the will in which she was appointed, and therefore her petition was barred by “the conclusive presumption of fraud” under Probate Code § 21380, subd. (a) and (c). (RJN Exh. B.) In the same order, the court noted that Plaintiff’s special letters of administration had expired in June 2023, and declined to renew them. (Ibid.) Defendants therefore argue that Plaintiff’s basis for standing has failed. (Motion at pp. 5–6.)[1]

 

Plaintiff in opposition argues that the disposal of her petition to probate the will does not deprive her of standing, as she has since filed[2] a new petition to administer the Decedent’s intestate estate, supported by the declaration of Brand Cooper, Decedent’s brother and one of his heirs-at-law. (Opposition at pp. 3–4.) As with Plaintiff’s prior opposition to Defendant’s earlier motion for judgment on the pleadings, she contends that the present lack of standing may be cured if the probate court grants her petition. (Ibid.)

 

Defendants in reply contend that Plaintiff’s appointment of intestate executor is unlikely, as Probate Code § 8402 states that “a person is not competent to act as personal representative in any of the following circumstances,” thereafter listing, among other bases, “[t]here are grounds for removal of the person from office under Section 8502.” (Prob. Code § 8402, subd. (a)((3).) Probate Code § 8502, meanwhile, states that a personal representative may be removed from office for “commit[ing] a fraud on the estate.” (Prob. Code § 8502, subd. (a); Reply at p. 4.) Thus Defendants contend that Plaintiff’s new petition to be administrator of the estate is “unlikely speculation.” (Reply at p. 4.)

 

The judicially noticeable materials make clear that Plaintiff, at present, lacks standing. She has since filed a new petition to be appointed the executor of Decedent’s intestate estate. Defects in standing may be cured (Branick v. Downey Savings & Loan Assn. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 235, 243), but the resolution of Plaintiff’s new petition is uncertain. Plaintiff in her request for judicial notice contends that the hearing date on the petition is November 16, 2023.

 

Accordingly, the resolution of the present motion depends upon the resolution of Plaintiff’s petition, and this court may not grant or deny the motion until Plaintiff’s petition is decided. If the petition is denied, the motion shall be GRANTED, without leave to amend. If the petition is granted, this motion shall be DENIED.

 

 

Defendants to give notice.

 


[1] Plaintiff objects to this court’s taking notice of the probate court’s ruling, on the grounds that the request for judicial notice does not describe which facts in the ruling this court is requested to take notice of. (See Estate of Nicholas (1986) 177 Cal.App.3d 1071, 1089 [“The trial judge should always be careful to specify exactly the document or portion of a document contained in a court file of which he is taking judicial notice. Also, counsel should be required to state, with like specificity, the exact document or portion of a document from a court file or record, when requesting the court to take judicial notice of a court record.”].) This is not a persuasive objection, as Defendant asks this court to take notice of the ruling and the asserted basis therefore, which is within the scope of judicial notice. (Evid. Code § 452, subd. (c), (d) [official acts of judicial departments and court records].) This court need take no notice of the underlying truth of the presumption of fraud.

[2] The petition was filed on September 5, 2023, the day before Defendant’s present motion was filed on September 6, 2023. (Plaintiff’s RJN Exh. 1.)


Case Number: 20STCV42444    Hearing Date: January 31, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendants Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Memorial Health Services, and Desiree Thomas’s Motion to Compel Plaintiff Gergis R. Ghobrial’s Independent Mental Examination is GRANTED. The exam is set to begin at 9 a.m. or 11 a.m., depending on the time chosen by Plaintiff at the hearing on this motion. The parties are directed to meet and confer concerning Plaintiff’s hand neuropathy and accommodations for the same. No sanctions are awarded.

Defendants to give notice.

 

I.                   MOTION TO CONDUCT MENTAL EXAMINATION

 

“If any party desires to obtain discovery by a physical examination other than that described in Article 2 (commencing with Section 2032.210), or by a mental examination, the party shall obtain leave of court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310, subd. (a).) “A motion for an examination under subdivision (a) shall specify the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination, as well as the identity and the specialty, if any, of the person or persons who will perform the examination. The motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Id. subd. (b).)

“The court shall grant a motion for a physical or mental examination under Section 2032.310 only for good cause shown.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (a).) Section 2032.320 subdivision (b) requires a motion for an IME to specific “the time, place, manner, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination . . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.310 subd. (b).) Moreover, an order granting a physical or mental examination shall specify the person or persons who may perform the examination, as well as the time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.320, subd. (d).)

The California Supreme Court has held that a prior, but substantially similar, statute allowing for IMEs “defines a showing of ‘good cause’ as requiring that the party produce specific facts justifying discovery and that the inquiry be relevant to the subject matter of the action or reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (Vinson v. Superior Court (1987) 43 Cal.3d 833, 840.)

Defendants Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Memorial Health Services, and Desiree Thomas (Defendants) move to compel the independent mental examination (IME) of Plaintiff Gergis Ghobrial (Plaintiff), to be conducted by Ellen G. Stein, Ph.D. Defendants argue that Plaintiff has placed his mental condition at issue through his claim for intentional infliction of emotional distress and claiming of damages for emotional distress and other mental damages, as stated in Plaintiff’s discovery responses. (Motion Exh. 5.) Defendant presents the declaration of Dr. Stein, who testifies to her qualifications and the range of tests she expects to conduct. (Stein Decl. ¶¶ 3–6.) The examination is to take place over two half-days, with the first involving written testing, and the second involving an interview. (Stein Decl. ¶¶ 7–10.) The dates specified for the examination are February 5 and 8, 2024, at 9 a.m., at an unspecified location. Defendant proposes that the venue of examination be at “an agreeable location within the mileage restrictions set forth in Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.220.” (Motion at p. 12.)

During the meet and confer process, Plaintiff objected to the necessity of an IME, referring to the qualified medical examination (QME) of Dr. Jeffrey Coker performed during Plaintiff’s worker’s compensation case against Defendant Surgical Affiliates Management Group, Inc.  (SAMGI). (Motion Exhs. 2, 4.) Plaintiff proposed a stipulation whereby Defendants would agree “to accept the complete report of the mental examination conducted by Dr. Croker [sic] as the final assessment of Plaintiff’s claims of PTSD for the purpose of this case.” (Motion Exh. 4.) Plaintiff did not produce the report, and proposed that Defendants could review the QME report after they stipulated to its dispositive effect. (Mino Decl. ¶ 6, Exh. 2.)

Plaintiff in opposition argues that there is no good cause for the IME, given the existence of Coker’s QME report, from which he provides unauthenticated excerpts which corroborate his allegations regarding the workplace origins of his distress. (Opposition at pp. 6–8.)[1] Plaintiff also argues that Stein does not specifically identify the tests she proposes to use, or the location of the exam. (Opposition at pp. 9–10.) Plaintiff argues that Stein specializes in the wrong subject area, and does not purport to use the diverse battery of tests used by Coker. (Opposition at p. 10.) Plaintiff argues that the short window of time between the January 31 hearing date and the February 5 date of the initial examination is inadequate under Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.220. (Opposition at pp. 9–10.) Plaintiff finally asks that any exam be conducted according to a variety of conditions. (Opposition at pp. 10–13.)

The Coker QME is not a suitable replacement for the IME that Defendants propose. When a plaintiff’s mental condition is in controversy, “defendants are entitled to have petitioner examined by a psychiatrist of their own choice pursuant to an order which complies with” applicable law. (Whitfield v. Superior Court for Los Angeles County (1966) 246 Cal.App.2d 81, 83.) The moving Defendants are not parties to the workers compensation proceeding, and in any event, a QME appointed in the workers compensation context is not chosen by any party, but is selected from a panel assigned by the administrative director. (See Lab. Code § 4062.2, subd. (c).) What’s more, Plaintiff does not offer the QME for the same purpose that an IME is intended to serve. The purpose of an IME is to furnish the parties with discoverable information, and there is no authority for the proposition that the conclusions of the examiner are binding upon the litigants. Plaintiff, however, has not agreed to produce the QME report except upon the condition that Defendants be bound by its conclusions. (Motion Exh. 2 [October 27, 2023 Email], Exh. 4.) This is improper, not least of all because Coker’s conclusions might prove erroneous. Dr. Stein in a reply declaration criticizes the test battery presented by the QME report (in those excerpts that Plaintiff has made available), and opines that “the test battery produced by Dr. Coker is not one which would be relied upon by a forensic psychologist in a civil matter.” (Stein Decl. ¶¶ 2–6.) There is thus ample good cause to support the IME, even if the QME were also made available to the parties.[2]

Plaintiff’s contention that the motion does not describe the place of examination is of only superficial merit. A motion seeking an IME must include “the time, place, manner, diagnostic tests and procedures, conditions, scope, and nature of the examination.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2032.320, subd. (d).) Plaintiff neglects, however, that the omission of a proposed venue from the motion, like its omission from Defendants’ proposed stipulation during the meet-and-confer process, was an attempt to give Plaintiff the opportunity to agree to a venue of his choice, rather than have one unilaterally imposed upon him. (Motion at p. 12; Exh. 1.) Plaintiff in fact blows hot and cold as to the desired specificity of the proposed IME, on one page objecting that there is no listed venue for examination, and on another objecting (without basis) that the time of examination — required by the same statute — cannot be set until “the venue of the examination is identified.” (Opposition at p. 11.) In any event, Defendants have rectified the omission by proposing a venue in Irvine with which Dr. Stein is familiar, and which she testifies meets Plaintiff’s accessibility and medical requirements. (Stein Decl. ¶ 17.)[3]

Plaintiff’s other objections are without merit. Plaintiff argues that Dr. Stein specializes in the wrong subject area, but she testifies that she has clinical practice in a number of trauma-intensive areas, and has served as an expert witness on emotional distress issues in labor and employment matters, like the present case. (Stein Decl. ¶ 1.) Plaintiff argues that there is insufficient notice of the proposed dates of examination, but in addition to relying on the notice provision applicable to physical medical exams not at issue here (Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.220, subd. (d)), Plaintiff has been on notice of the February 5 and 9 dates of examination since the motion was filed on November 8, 2023. Plaintiff thus offers no basis to deny the motion.

The proposed conditions and amendments to the IME that Plaintiff offers in opposition are of varying quality. They are, however, all matters upon which Plaintiff could have conferred with Defendants before electing total opposition the IME, and for which Defendants indeed invited Plaintiff’s input before the motion became necessary. (Motion Exh. 2 [October 16, 2023 email].)

Some of these proposed conditions amount to little more than unnecessary directives that Defendants and Dr. Stein abide by statute. These include the proposal that Plaintiff be permitted to record the examination — a right already granted by Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.530, and applicable here — and the proposal that Plaintiff be given a copy of the report related to him and prior reports by the same examiner as to the same condition. (Opposition at pp. 10–11.) If Plaintiff desires this latter information, he may make a demand for it without leave of court as provided under Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.610.

There are other proposed conditions: that Plaintiff not be required to sign any forms; that all persons assisting the medical examiner be identified by full name and title; and that Plaintiff not be required to provide all but the most minimal personal information to the examiner. (Opposition at p. 12.) These are submitted only as terse demands, without justification or supporting authority. Plaintiff’s request that no physical examination be conducted is also needless (Opposition at p. 12), as no physical examination is proposed.

Plaintiff also proposes that all written exams be administered by audio tests and verbal answers, rather than by reading and handwriting. (Motion at p. 12.) Although not supported by any declaration, this proposal is apparently based on Plaintiff’s “neuropathy in the hand, making the proposed first day examination of 4–6 hours of written tests exceptionally challenging for the plaintiff to complete.” (Motion at p. 10.) Dr. Stein contends that rendering the entire written portion of the exam into an audio/verbal format is “not realistic,” as each written test involves potentially hundreds of test items, some with multiple response options, each of which would have to be read aloud to Plaintiff, and which would lengthen 20-minute history-form process into a two-hour process, not considering the other written tests to be administered. (Supp. Stein Decl. ¶¶ 15–16.) Dr. Stein also notes the inconsistency of Plaintiff’s objection to this mode of examination, when he must have completed similar testing to undergo the QME that he promotes as an alternative. (Supp. Stein Decl. ¶ 15.)

The court agrees with Defendants that Plaintiff’s proposed all-verbal test accommodation is impracticable for the reasons stated by Dr. Stein, and need not be adopted. However, the fact that Plaintiff’s proposal is overbroad and overly burdensome does not mean that his neuropathy cannot be accommodated in some other way. For instance, although Plaintiff’s neuropathy, as described, would make extended periods of handwriting difficult for him, he does not claim to be print-disabled, or that reading is otherwise difficult. The accommodation that Plaintiff evidently requires is in the manner of responding to the written exams, not in the manner by which the test-items are delivered to him. Thus while Plaintiff’s proposed condition furnishes no basis to deny or limit the IME order, the court directs the parties to meet and confer concerning Plaintiff’s neuropathy, and accommodations for same, prior to the examination.

Plaintiff’s proposal that the tests commence at 11 a.m. rather than 9 a.m., is also of some merit,. (Opposition at p. 12.) Defendants in their meet and confer correspondence assured Plaintiff that they were amenable to an examination venue in the “Long Beach area,” where Plaintiff resides. (Motion Exh. 2 [October 17, 23, 2023 emails].) The proposed venue here, however, is in Irvine, approximately an hour’s drive away. (Reply at p. 3.) The 11 a.m. start time may thus be an appropriate measure to take into account the increased distance proposed by Defendant. Dr. Stein does not oppose a later start time, but warns that starting the examinations later could push the tests to later in the day. (Supp. Stein Decl. ¶ 14.) The court and parties may address the relative merits of either starting time at the hearing on this motion.

The motion is therefore GRANTED. The exam is set to begin at 9 a.m. or 11 a.m., depending on the time chosen by Plaintiff at the hearing on this motion. The parties are directed to meet and confer concerning Plaintiff’s hand neuropathy and accommodations for the same.

Although Defendants seek $5,235.00 in sanctions, no sanctions are proper here. The statute governing motions to compel an IME does not contain a mandatory sanctions provision. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2032.310.) Code of Civil Procedure § 2032.410 permits a range of sanctions against a party who “is required to submit to a physical or mental examination . . . but fails to do so,” but as yet Plaintiff has not failed to attend an IME that he has been ordered to attend. No sanctions are therefore awarded.

 


[1] The Coker examination occurred on April 22, 2022. (Ghobrial Decl. ¶ 2.)

[2] Plaintiff does not dispute that his mental condition is at issue in this litigation.

[3] Plaintiff does not dispute that he is available on the dates in question.


Case Number: 21STCV33705    Hearing Date: January 31, 2024    Dept: 61

Defendant Prada USA Corp.’s Motion for to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production against Plaintiff Lindsey Sterling is GRANTED. Further responses are ordered within 30 days. Sanctions are awarded against Plaintiff and her counsel in the amount of $2,550.00.

 

Defendant to provide Notice.

 

I.                   MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER – PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS

 

“A party may demand that any other party produce . . . a document that is in the possession, custody, or control of the party on whom the demand is made.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.010(b).) The demanding party may move for an order compelling further response to the demand if the demanding party deems that (1) a statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete, (2) a representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive, or (3) an objection in the response is without merit or too general. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(a).) “The motion shall set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand,” and “[t]he motion shall be accompanied by a meet and confer declaration under Section 2016.040.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b).)

 

A motion to compel a further response to an inspection demand must set forth specific facts showing “good cause” justifying the discovery sought by the inspection demand. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310(b)(1); Kirkland v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 92, 98.) Unless there is a legitimate privilege issue or claim of attorney work product, that burden is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance. (TBG Ins. Services Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 444.) Once the moving party demonstrates good cause for the discovery, the burden is on the responding party to justify any objection or failure to fully respond to the inspection demand.  (Coy v Superior Court (1962) 58 Cal.2d 210, 220.)

 

Defendant Prada USA Corp. (Defendant) seeks to compel further responses from Plaintiff Lindsey Sterling (Plaintiff) to Requests for Production No. 69 and 70. This motion requires some factual background.

 

On March 8, 2023, this court granted Defendant’s motion to compel further responses to supplemental requests for production and  interrogatories. Plaintiff had previously provided  responses stating that she had no documents related to her search for new employment, and had stated that she had no information concerning health providers who treated her for injuries arising from Defendant’s alleged conduct. When Plaintiff later testified at deposition that she in fact had employment-search documents, and further named a number of previously unnamed health providers who had treated her for case-related injuries, Defendant served supplemental requests seeking the documents and information. Plaintiff responded to these requests only with unsupported objections, however, and the court granted Defendant’s motion to compel.

After this order, shortly before the second session of Plaintiff’s deposition in May 2023, Defendant served a resume belonging to plaintiff and, evidently, no other documents concerning her job search. (Papac Decl. ¶ 7, Exh. E.) At her deposition, Plaintiff confirmed that this was the resume she had used to seek subsequent employment after leaving Defendant, notwithstanding Defendant’s questions concerning a portion of the resume that indicated she still worked for Defendant. (Papac Decl. Exh. F at pp. 315–321.) After coming back to the deposition from lunch, Plaintiff stated that she had “one thing to say,” and her counsel stated that she wanted to “clear the record.” (Id. at p. 341–342.) Plaintiff stated that she was not “a hundred percent sure” that the resume produced “is the updated resume that I sent.” (Id. at p. 407.) Plaintiff confirmed that she had another version of the resume that did not falsely indicate that she still worked for Defendant, and that she thought she had given it to Defendant. (Id. at p. 408.) She stated that she had likely prepared the unproduced, updated resume in April or May of 2020, shortly after her termination, while preparing applications to other employers. (Id. at p. 409.)

 

Following email correspondence with Plaintiff’s counsel, Plaintiff produced another resume on September 6, 2023, accurately indicating that she no longer worked for Defendant. (Papac Decl. Exh. G, H.) It is this resume that is the subject of the motion. A week later, on September 13, 2023, Defendant served requests for production seeking “the original native electronic format” of the resume, in both PDF and word document formats, “with all metadata.” (Separate Statement.) Plaintiff responded tardily on October 24, 2023, stating, “After a reasonable and diligent search, responding party does not have any responsive documents to this request it is lost. [sic]” (Separate Statement.) After another email exchange in which Plaintiff’s counsel refused to elaborate or explain how Plaintiff had lost the resume she had served a month earlier, the present motion followed. (Papac Decl. Exh. K.)

 

Further responses are warranted to the requests at issue because Plaintiff’s responses are “inadequate, incomplete, or evasive.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310, subd. (a).) The contention that the native electronic format of the resume is “lost” is unworthy of credence. Plaintiff served the document through her attorney a week before the requests at issue were served, and Plaintiff’s prior statements of inability to comply related to the same job-search documents were later proven to be false in the proceedings leading up to this court’s order granting Defendant’s prior motion to compel.

 

Plaintiff’s opposition only provides further basis for granting the motion. First, Plaintiff confirms, repeatedly, that the resume served on September 6, 2023, was not the one to which Plaintiff referred at deposition, but was an “updated” copy of the resume prepared after that deposition took place. See, e.g., Opposition at p. 13 [“Plaintiff is not disputing that she updated her resume after her deposition on May 24, 2023.”[.] Thus the requests at issue are supported by ample good cause, as they are directed toward verifying the authenticity of the second resume, which Plaintiff now acknowledges is inauthentic.

 

Plaintiff contends that she cannot produce the native files because her laptop is broken. (Opposition at p. 2.) Yet the deposition testimony that Plaintiff relies upon for this point is from her May 2023 deposition, several months before she provided the “updated” resume. (Opposition at p. 12; Westmoreland Decl. Exh. 17 at  p. 363.) Plaintiff does not explain how the broken laptop prevents her from accessing the native files for the resume, when it did not prevent her from creating it.

 

Plaintiff also contends, falsely, that the original resume produced at Plaintiff’s deposition was a scanned copy of a physical document, and that no electronic versions of the original are available. (Opposition at pp. 10, 13.) The testimony that Plaintiff cites does not stand for this proposition; it states only that Plaintiff “printed them out,” meaning the outdated resumes that still listed her as employed with Defendant, “and that’s what I had left over.” (Westmoreland Decl. Exh. 15 at pp. 315–316.) She thus stated that she had physical copies of the resume left over, not that she lacked any electronic copies. Indeed, as Defendant notes in reply, the metadata on the original resume’s PDF file (which is how it was produced to Defendant) indicates that it originated as a word document prepared in March 2019, long before this litigation, which was modified (likely converted into a PDF) on the date of Plaintiff’s May 2023 deposition. (Papac Decl. ¶ 2, Exh. A.) The contention that Plaintiff never had an electronic copy of the resume is without basis.

 

Plaintiff therefore has in her possession the word document and PDF of the updated resume in their native format, and has simply refused to produce them. Her statements of inability to comply are, like the previous such statements brought before this court, simply false.

 

The motion is GRANTED.

 

II.                SANCTIONS

Statute provides that the court shall impose sanctions upon a party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response to interrogatories or requests for production of documents, absent substantial justification otherwise. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2030.300, subd. (d); 2031.310, subd. (h).)

 

Defendant seeks $2,550.00 in sanctions against Plaintiff and her counsel, representing ten hours of attorney work at $255 per hour. (Papac Decl. ¶ 14.)

 

In the previous motion to compel further, this court awarded sanctions only against Plaintiff’s counsel, and not Plaintiff herself, based on Plaintiff’s averment in deposition that she had produced responsive documents to her counsel, Dominique Westmoreland, which had not been passed on to Defendant. Here, however, Plaintiff’s opposition memorandum directly states that she prepared the false resume for production to Defendant. It is true that this admission is included only in the opposition memorandum authored by Westmoreland, and this opposition is so riven with flagrant misrepresentations, damning admissions, and baffling inconsistencies, that it is difficult to imagine anyone but its author approving its release. Nonetheless, “[t]here is a presumption that an attorney has authority to appear for any person for whom he professes to act.” (Wilson v. Barry (1951) 102 Cal.App.2d 778, 780.) That presumption applies to Plaintiff and Westmoreland here.

Sanctions in the amount of $2,550.00 are therefore awarded against Plaintiff and her counsel.
