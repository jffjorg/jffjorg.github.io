ALERT

Due to Coronavirus, please consider appearing by phone for Department M cases.

 

Department M strongly encourages the use of  LA CourtConnect* for ALL hearings, without need for prior approval, unless live testimony by a witness is required.

 

The contact information for LA CourtConnect* is:

 

 

 https://lacourt.portalscloud.com/VCourt/

 

 

*Parties with a fee waiver on file may be eligible to appear at no/reduced cost


Dept. M issues tentative rulings in many, but not all motion hearings. There is no set time at which tentatives are posted. Please do not call the staff to inquire if a tentative will be posted. 

If parties are satisfied with the ruling, parties may submit on the tentative. However, if an opposing party does not submit, they will be permitted to argue. Please check with the other side before calling the courtroom to submit. The staff does not keep track of which parties submitted and which did not, so please do not ask. 

If a matter is also a scheduling hearing (CMC, TSC, OSC etc) an appearance is still required even if a party submits on the tentative ruling.

Hearing Date:                         October 12, 2023

 

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Adrienne L. Spruth

Responding Party:                  None

Motion to Reclassify

 

            The court considered the moving papers.

RULING

            The motion is GRANTED.  The case is reclassified as an unlimited civil case.  Plaintiff is ordered to pay the reclassification fee.

BACKGROUND

On August 20, 2021, plaintiff Adrienne L. Spruth filed an unlawful detainer complaint (as a limited civil case) against Mark J. Spruth based on tenancy at will and a 30-day notice to quit as to property at 26202 Grayslake Road, Rancho Palos Verdes.

On October 8, 2021, the court denied defendant’s motion to quash service of summons.

            On December 17, 2021, the court sustained with leave to amend defendant’s demurrer as to proper service of the 30-day notice to quit.

            On December 22, 2021, plaintiff filed a FAC.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

            Under CCP §403.020, “(a) If a plaintiff, cross-complainant, or petitioner files an amended complaint or other amended initial pleading that changes the jurisdictional classification from limited to unlimited, the party at the time of filing the pleading shall pay the reclassification fee provided in Section 403.060, and the clerk shall promptly reclassify the case. If the amendment changes the jurisdictional classification from unlimited to limited, no reclassification fee is required, and the clerk shall promptly reclassify the case.”

            Under CCP §403.040, “(a) The plaintiff, cross-complainant, or petitioner may file a motion for reclassification within the time allowed for that party to amend the initial pleading. The defendant or cross-defendant may file a motion for reclassification within the time allowed for that party to respond to the initial pleading.  The court, on its own motion, may reclassify a case at any time.  A motion for reclassification does not extend the moving party's time to amend or answer or otherwise respond.  The court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification, regardless of any fault or lack of fault, if the case has been classified in an incorrect jurisdictional classification.

(b) If a party files a motion for reclassification after the time for that party to amend that party's initial pleading or to respond to a complaint, cross-complaint, or other initial pleading, the court shall grant the motion and enter an order for reclassification only if both of the following conditions are satisfied:

(1) The case is incorrectly classified.

(2) The moving party shows good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.

(c) If the court grants a motion for reclassification, the payment of the reclassification fee shall be determined, unless the court orders otherwise, as follows:

(1) If a case is reclassified as an unlimited civil case, the party whose pleading causes the action or proceeding to exceed the maximum amount in controversy for a limited civil case or otherwise fails to satisfy the requirements of a limited civil case under Section 85 shall pay the reclassification fee provided in Section 403.060. . . .”

DISCUSSION

            Plaintiff Adrienne Spruth requests that the court reclassify the case from limited civil jurisdiction to unlimited civil jurisdiction.

            Plaintiff asserts that the 30-day notice to quit expired on August 16, 2021, and that she is seeking holdover damages.  She filed this action in limited civil jurisdiction “believing that the case would proceed to trial quickly,” and if it had, the holdover damages would have been below $25,000.  She contends that “with the delay of these proceedings” and defendant’s continued occupancy of the subject property, the potential holdover damages exceed $25,000, as plaintiff alleges that the daily rate is $166.67.  Thus, plaintiff argues, this case no longer qualifies as a limited civil action.

            The court finds that the case is incorrectly classified, and that plaintiff has shown good cause for not seeking reclassification earlier.

            The court orders that the case is reclassified as an unlimited case.  Plaintiff is ordered to pay the reclassification fee.

            Plaintiff is ordered to give notice of ruling.

RULING

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH 20 DAYS LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 1st and 2nd causes of action in the FAC.  The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND as to the 3rd and 4th causes of action.

BACKGROUND

            On March 22, 2023, Megna Real Estate Investments, Inc. filed a complaint against Center Street Lending VIII SPE, LLC and Prestige Default Services, LLC for (1) violation of Civil Code §2923.5, (2) violation of Civil Code §2923.7, and (3) violation of Bus. and Prof. Code §17200. 

            On April 18, 2023, plaintiff filed a FAC for (1) breach of contract, (2) breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, (3) slander of title, and (4) conversion.

            On April 20, 2023, Center Street Lending filed a notice of related cases.

            On May 10, 2023, the court denied plaintiff’s ex parte application for a TRO to enjoin defendants from selling property pursuant to a trustee’s sale.

            On June 22, 2023, plaintiff filed a notice of automatic stay.

            On June 27, 2023, plaintiff filed a notice of stay of proceedings.

            On July 7, 2023, as to a notice of related case, the court found that the herein case and CVSW2302442 and CVSW2302453 are related but that the cases are not to be transferred from one county to another.  The court also stated that plaintiff filed bankruptcy but that does not stay the case.  There is no cross-complaint against plaintiff. 

            On September 12, 2023, the court granted plaintiff’s counsel’s motion to be relieved as counsel.  The court notes that there has been no proof of service filed as to the order on plaintiff.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.  Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.  “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.  Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”  SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.  “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”  Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.

DISCUSSION

            Defendant Center Street Lending VIII SPE, LLC (“Center Street Lending”) demurs to each cause of action in the FAC on the grounds that they fail to state sufficient allegations to constitute a cause of action and are uncertain.

            The FAC alleges that on April 7, 2021, plaintiff took out a loan mortgage against the property from Center Street Lending VIII SPE, LLC in the amount of $1,387,500.  FAC, ¶16.  Pursuant to the deed of trust, a lender must apply plaintiff’s payments according to the deed of trust.  Covenant (f) of the deed of trust holds that plaintiff’s payments will be applied in the following order:  (2) interest due under the Note; (3) principal due under the Note.  Id., ¶17.  Plaintiff made timely payments until November 2022.  Id., ¶18.  On December 21, 2022, Prestige, on behalf of Center Street Lending, recorded a Notice of Default against the property.  Id., ¶19.  Prestige caused the recording of the Notice of Default, showing plaintiff’s past due balance as $456,146.45.  Id., ¶20.  This is while Center Street Lending issued a mortgage statement on February 14, 2023 to plaintiff reflecting a past due balance of $280,689.97.  At the time of recording, the Notice of Default, Prestige and Center Street Lending had known plaintiff had made significant payments and had lowered the past due balance to $280,689.97.  Id., ¶21.  Despite this knowledge, Center Street Lending failed to apply plaintiff’s payment to plaintiff’s account and instead deemed the payment a partial payment and placed it into a suspense account, and wrongfully recorded the Notice of Default, thereby breaching the loan agreement between the parties.  Id, ¶22.  On March 30, 2023, Prestige, on behalf of Center Street Lending, recorded a Notice of Trustee’s Sale against the property.  Id., ¶23.

                        1st cause of action for breach of contract

“The elements of a cause of action for breach of contract are:  (1) the contract, (2) plaintiff's performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damages to plaintiff.”  Coles v. Glaser (2016) 2 Cal. App. 5th 384, 391. 

The FAC alleges that Center Street Lending is in breach of contract by applying plaintiff’s payment to a suspense account, rather than to plaintiff’s principal balance.  Pursuant to the DOT, the lender must apply plaintiff’s payments according to the DOT.  Covenant (f) of the DOT holds that plaintiff’s payments will be applied in the following order:  (2) interest due under the Note; (3) principal due under the Note.  FAC, ¶27.  Plaintiff made timely payments until November 2022.  Id., ¶28.  Prestige caused the recording of the NOD, showing plaintiff’s past due balance as $456,146.45.  Id., ¶29.  This is while, Center Street Lending issued a mortgage statement on February 14, 2023 to plaintiff reflecting a past due balance of $280,689.97.  Id., ¶30.  At the time of recording the NOD, defendants had known plaintiff had made significant payments and had lowered the past due balance to $280,689.97.  Id., ¶31.  Despite this knowledge, Center Street Lending failed to apply plaintiff’s payment to plaintiff’s account and instead, deemed the payment a partial payment and placed it into a suspense account, and wrongfully recording the NOD, thereby breaching the loan agreement between the parties.  Id., ¶32.  Plaintiff suffered actual damages, including inflated mortgage payments resulting from the breach of their agreement, damaged credit, attorney’s fees, and any other damages according to proof at trial.  Id., ¶33.

            Defendant argues that plaintiff has not alleged any provision that could have been breached.  As to “Covenant (f)” of the deed of trust, it makes no requirement as to “applying payments to interest and principal.”  See FAC, Exh. A.  Rather, Covenant (f) lists the beneficiary’s security interest and specifically references and incorporates the terms of the promissory note.  Further, defendant contends, because plaintiff is “so clearly and admittedly in default,” it has not shown actual damages resulting from any purported breach or that the alleged actions of defendant interfered with its ability to perform (i.e., make contractual payments).  Defendant further argues that plaintiff has not alleged its own performance under the deed of trust, including any payments made after November 2022.  Also, defendant asserts, the loan has matured, and plaintiff has not shown that it repaid the loan.

            The court finds that the allegations are insufficient as argued by defendant.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

                        2nd cause of action for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing

“Every contract imposes upon each party a duty of good faith and fair dealing in its performance and its enforcement.”  Hicks v. E.T. Legg & Associates (2001) 89 Cal. App. 4th 496, 508.  “[T]he scope of conduct prohibited by the covenant of good faith is circumscribed by the purposes and express terms of the contract.”  Id. at 509.  “The covenant of good faith and fair dealing . . . exists . . . to prevent one contracting party from unfairly frustrating the other party’s right to receive the benefits of the agreement actually made.”  Guz v. Bechtel National Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 349.  Allegations of breach of the covenant of good faith that do not go beyond a statement of a mere contract breach may be disregarded as superfluous.  See Bionghi v. Metropolitan Water District (1999) 70 Cal. App. 4th 1358, 1370.

            The FAC alleges that in November 2022, plaintiff tendered a timely mortgage payment as required under the DOT.  However, although defendant’s servicer confirmed application of the payment, plaintiff later learned that this payment was not applied to plaintiff’s account and was instead placed into a suspense account, thereby interfering with plaintiff’s ability to make payments on their loan according to the terms of the DOT.  In doing so, defendant breached the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing by interfering with plaintiff’s ability to make payments under its loan.  FAC, ¶37.

Defendant argues the same as under the 1st cause of action.

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient to constitute a cause of action as argued by defendant.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

           

 

                        3rd cause of action for slander of title

“The elements of the slander of title tort are (1) a publication, (2) without privilege or justification, (3) falsity, and (4) direct pecuniary loss.”  Weeden v. Hoffman (2021) 70 Cal. App. 5th 269, 293.  Civil Code §2924(d) extends the communications privilege of Section 47 to the mailing, publication, and delivery of notices of default, as well as the performance of the procedures set forth in Sections 2920 through 2944.10 regarding mortgages.  “[A] slander of title claim has been held to be subject to a litigation privilege defense.”  Id.  Where the litigation privilege provides a complete defense to a slander of title claim, such claim may be struck from the complaint.  Id. 

            The FAC alleges that within the last year, defendant willfully, wrongfully, without justification, and without privilege, published and caused to be recorded foreclosure documents and other documents that cast doubt on plaintiff’s ownership of the property.  FAC, ¶41.  As discussed supra, those documents were invalid or recorded improperly and caused doubt to be cast on plaintiff’s title to the property.  Id., ¶42.  Defendants’ publication and recording of those documents directly impaired plaintiff’s right of ownership in and to the property.  Id., ¶43.

            Defendant argues that slander of title is inapplicable to privileged communications such as the notice of default and sale.  Defendant further contends that plaintiff has not pled sufficiently that defendant was without privilege or justification in recording the notice of default and notice of sale as authorized by the deed of trust.  Also, defendant asserts, the loan has matured, and plaintiff makes no allegation that the loan was paid off.  Defendant further argues that plaintiff has failed to plead actual pecuniary loss (rather than a threat of potential loss).

            The court finds that the allegations are insufficient as argued by defendant.  Plaintiff’s claim arises from defendants’ issuance of the Notice of Default and the Notice of Trustee’s Sale, both privileged publications under Civil Code §47.  See Weeden, supra.

            The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

                        4th cause of action for conversion

A cause of action for conversion requires the following elements:  (1) plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of personal property; (2) defendant’s disposition of the property inconsistent with plaintiff’s rights; and (3) resulting damages.  Fremont Indemnity Co. v. Fremont General Corp. (2007) 148 Cal. App. 4th 97, 119. “ It is not necessary that there be a manual taking of the property; it is only necessary to show an assumption of control or ownership over the property, or that the alleged converter has applied the property to his own use.”  Spates v. Dameron Hospital Association (2003) 114 Cal. App. 4th 208, 221.

The FAC alleges that Center Street Lending wrongfully exercised control over plaintiff’s personal property.  FAC, ¶48.  Plaintiff had a right to have its payments applied toward the interest and principal payments under the Note.  Id., ¶49.  Center Street Lending substantially interfered with plaintiff’s payments by knowingly or intentionally preventing plaintiff from access to the payments when it applied the payments to a suspense account.  Id., ¶50.  Plaintiff did not consent to applying the payments toward a suspense account.  Id., ¶51.

Defendant argues that this tort “is clearly inapplicable to this case” and that plaintiff admittedly obtained the loan and agreed to the terms of the promissory note and deed of trust.  Defendant asserts that because plaintiff owes money to defendant under the terms of the loan, “it is impossible that Defendant has obtained Plaintiff’s property by a wrongful act.”

The court finds that the allegations are insufficient as argued by defendant.  The alleged facts do not support plaintiff’s “ownership or right to possession” of the payments made to defendant.

The demurrer is SUSTAINED WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.
