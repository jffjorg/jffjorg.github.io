DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER SUSTAINING DEFENDANT’S DEMURRER AND GRANTING DEFENDANT’S MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Dept. A

COMPLAINT FILED: 02/05/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

 

1.     Background

This is a personal injury action. Plaintiff Jailyn Robateau (“Plaintiff”) alleges in the complaint that defendants Nationwide Guard Services and Seko Logistics (collectively, “Defendants”) maintained a guard shack in an unsanitary state, resulting in an unsafe work environment. Plaintiff alleges that Nationwide Guard Services (“Nationwide”) was his employer and that defendant Seko Logistics (“Seko”) owns the premises on which the guard shack sits. The complaint alleges a cause of action for business intentional tort.

Nationwide has been dismissed from the case, leaving Seko as the remaining defendant.

2.     Demurrer with Motion to Strike

Seko demurs to the complaint on the grounds that it fails to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action and that it is uncertain. Seko contends that Seko is not alleged to have employed Plaintiff and did not owe any duty to Plaintiff to provide a safe and healthy workplace. Seko further contends that “business intentional tort” is not a recognized cause of action. Seko moves to strike the complaint’s sole cause of action for business intentional tort based on these grounds. Seko also moves to strike the complaint’s claim for punitive damages on the grounds that Plaintiff does not allege malice, oppression, or fraud; as well as moving to strike Plaintiff’s claim for $500,000 in damages on the grounds that the complaint impermissibly states an amount of damages in a personal injury case in violation of Code of Civil Procedure section 425.10, subdivision (b).

On September 10, 2024, Plaintiff filed opposition to Defendant’s motion to strike, contending that Seko’s demurrer and motion to strike are untimely. Plaintiff also contends that Seko responded to the complaint by communicating with Plaintiff with options of settlements and that nothing in the complaint is improper or uncertain. Plaintiff further contends that Code of Civil Procedure section 425.10 does not prohibit a complaint from stating punitive damages.  

3.     Demurrer and Motion to Strike Standards

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿(SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿(Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.¿(Code of Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)¿The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.¿(Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

In order to state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a complaint must set forth the elements as stated in the general punitive damage statute, Civil Code section 3294.¿(College. Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721 (College Hospital).)¿These statutory elements include allegations that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)¿¿¿

“In order to survive a motion to strike an allegation of punitive damages, the ultimate facts showing an entitlement to such relief must be pled by a plaintiff. [Citations.]¿In passing on the correctness of a ruling on a motion to strike, judges read allegations of a pleading subject to a motion to strike as a whole, all parts in their context, and assume their truth.¿[Citations.]¿In ruling on a motion to strike, courts do not read allegations in isolation.¿[Citation.]”¿(Clauson v. Superior Court (1998) 67 Cal.App.4th 1253, 1255.)¿“The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages.¿[Citation.]¿Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.¿[Citation.]”¿ (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166.)

4.     Discussion

a.     Demurrer

Plaintiff merely asserts an “intentional tort” claim against Seko.  In describing reasons for liability against Seko, Plaintiff states that the hazardous condition of the guard shack was located on Seko’s premises, and that Seko was aware of the unsanitary guard shack on their premises and failed to resolve the issue, instead exposing Nationwide’s employees to unsanitary and unsafe working conditions. However, Plaintiff does not allege that Seko and Plaintiff were in an employment relationship; rather, Plaintiff alleges that Nationwide and Plaintiff were in an employment relationship, as part of which Plaintiff worked in the guard shack on Seko’s premises. Plaintiff does not allege that Seko retained control over the work process or premises. 

Even interpreting the complaint broadly as attempting to state a cause of action for premises liability under a negligence theory, Plaintiff fails to allege sufficient facts to give rise to any duty on the part of Seko to Plaintiff in connection with insect bites Plaintiff allegedly suffered while working at her employment with Nationwide.  Duty is an essential element of the tort of negligence.  (Potter v. Firestone Tire & Rubber Co. (1993) 6 Cal.4th 965, 984.)  Everyone is responsible, not only for the result of his or her willful acts, but also for an injury occasioned to another by his or her want of ordinary care or skill in the management of his or her property or person, except so far as the latter has, willfully or by want of ordinary care, brought the injury upon himself or herself.”  (Civil Code §1714, subdiv. (a).) Generally speaking, “a landowner has a duty to act reasonably in the management of property ‘in view of the probability of injury to others.’ ”  (Garcia v. Paramount Citrus Assn., Inc. §(2008) 164 Cal.App.4thh 1448, 1453, quoting Rowland v. Christian (1968) 69 Cal.2d 108, 119.)  “In the case of a landowner's liability for injuries to persons on the property, the determination of whether a duty exists” involves balancing the Rowland factors. (Rinehart v. Boys & Girls Club of Chula Vista (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 419, 430.)  The complaint is entirely devoid of any factual allegations that would trigger an analysis under Rowland with respect to any purported duty Seko may have owed to employees of Nationwide while working on Seko’s premises.

Accordingly, Plaintiff fails to allege facts stating a valid cause of action. The court sustains Seko’s demurrer with leave to amend.

b.     Motion to Strike

As the court has sustained Seko’s demurrer to the complaint’s sole cause of action for business intentional tort, the motion to strike is denied as it is moot.

5.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court SUSTAINS Seko’s demurrer with leave to amend. As a result, the motion to strike is DENIED as it is moot.  Plaintiff shall file an amended complaint not later than twenty (20) days from the date of this order. 

Seko is ordered to give notice of this order.

1.     Background

Plaintiff Kimi Kelly (“Plaintiff”) identifies this action on the caption page of her verified complaint as a “Verified Complaint for Quiet Title.” She also lists three causes of action, for Breach of Real Estate Contract Agreement, Foreclosure Scheme and Breach of Fiduciary Agreement.  Below the enumerated causes of action, Plaintiff inserted “§519 & §523 P.C. Mortgage Fraud.”  Within the body of the complaint, Plaintiff asserts only one cause of action, for Quiet Title.  In her prayer for relief, Plaintiff prays for an order quieting title to the subject property, a judicial declaration that she is the full legal and beneficial owner of the Property, and an award of attorney’s fees if allowed by law, costs of suit, and any other relief as the court deems just and proper.

Plaintiff alleges in the complaint that defendants Saadite Green and Mia Archie Green (collectively, “Defendants”) obtained title in their name to the property located at 910 West Palmer St., Compton, CA 90220 (the “Property”) as an accommodation to Plaintiff and agreed to deed the Property to Plaintiff at a later date when Plaintiff would be eligible to hold title in her own name. Plaintiff seeks a judicial declaration that Plaintiff is the full legal and beneficial owner of the Property.  She alleges that Defendants have never lived at the Property since it was purchased on December 31, 2008.  She also alleges that she paid the down payment, all closing costs, commissions and fees for the Property, and that she has paid all expenses related to the Property since December 31, 2008, including all mortgage payments, taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance, and repair costs for the Property.  Plaintiff further alleges that she and her family have resided on the Property from 2009 to present.

2.     Demurrer

Defendants demur to the complaint on the grounds that it fails to allege sufficient facts to state a cause of action and that it is uncertain. Defendants contend Plaintiff failed to meet the pleading requirements set forth in Code of Civil Procedure § 761.020.  Defendants also contend that Plaintiff inconsistently alleges that she is a co-owner of the Property and that Defendants are the owners of record of the Property, and it is unclear whether Plaintiff has a basis to quiet title in her name. Defendants also contend that Plaintiff fails to adequately plead the existence of a contract, including its essential terms. Defendants further argue that the complaint is uncertain because, in certain parts of the complaint, Plaintiff uses nonspecific placeholder terms rather than specific information, and it is unclear which causes of action Plaintiff is asserting.  As of December 18, 2024, no opposition has been filed.

 

3.     Demurrer Standards

A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿(Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿(SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿(Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)

4.     Discussion

Code of Civil Procedure § 761.020 sets forth the pleading requirements to state a cause of action for quiet title and provides as follows:

 

 

The complaint shall be verified and shall include all of the following:

 

(a)  A description of the property that is the subject of the action. In the case of tangible personal property, the description shall include its usual location. In the case of real property, the description shall include both its legal description and its street address or common designation, if any.

 

(b)  The title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title. If the title is based upon adverse possession, the complaint shall allege the specific facts constituting the adverse possession.

 

(c)   The adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought.

 

(d)  The date as of which the determination is sought. If the determination is sought as of a date other than the date the complaint is filed, the complaint shall include a statement of the reasons why a determination as of that date is sought.

 

(e)  A prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse

                 claims.

An element of a cause of action for quiet title is the adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought.  (Orcilla v. Big Sur, Inc. (2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 982.) 

The complaint fails to comply with § 761.020, subdiv. (a) because it fails to allege the legal description of the real property that is the subject of the action.  It fails to comply with subdivision (b) because it fails to allege because it fails to allege the title of plaintiff as to which a determination is sought and the basis of the title.  It fails to comply with subdivision (c) because Plaintiff has not adequately pled facts that, if true, would establish that she holds title to the Property or otherwise has a right to ownership of the Property, or, at a minimum, the factual allegations are vague and uncertain to the point of being incomprehensible to a reasonable person.  As Defendants note, although Plaintiff makes the conclusory assertion that she is a “co-owner” of the Property, she does not allege she holds title, and in fact concedes that Defendants hold title. The complaint also fails to comply with subdivision (d) by failing to plead the date as of which the determination is sought.

Plaintiff alleges she has paid all expenses related to the Property, including the mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance, and repair costs, since the purchase of the Property in 2008. Plaintiff also states that she has resided in the Property since 2009, exclusive of Defendants.  However, she has not alleged that title is based upon adverse possession, nor does she allege the specific facts constituting any purported adverse possession as is required by § 761.020, subdiv. (b).

Plaintiff further states that her interest in the Property arises from an agreement with Defendants that Defendants would deed the Property to Plaintiff at a later date, when Plaintiff would be eligible to hold title in her own name. However, Plaintiff does not allege that this contract has come due or been breached, nor does she allege whether any such contract is written or oral (C.C.P. §430.10, subdiv. (g)), nor does she allege the terms of the purported contract or attach it to the complaint as is required.  (Otworth v. Southern Pacific Transp. Co. (1985) 166 Cal.App.3d 452.)  Moreover, assuming arguendo the agreement was oral, the express allegations of the complaint compel the conclusion that it is barred by the statute of frauds.  Contracts regarding the sale of real property or an interest therein must be in writing, signed by the party to be charged. (Civil Code § 1624(a)(3).)

Plaintiff has therefore failed to allege facts stating a cause of action for either quiet title or breach of contract.  The complaint makes no reference to any facts or allegations regarding any claims for foreclosure scheme, breach of fiduciary agreement or mortgage fraud, and therefore states no such causes of action.

 

 

5.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court SUSTAINS Defendants’ demurrer with leave to amend.  Plaintiff is ordered to file an amended pleading within twenty (20) calendar days of this order.

Defendants are ordered to serve and file notice of the court’s ruling.
