Case Number: 21STCV05451    Hearing Date: October 10, 2024    Dept: E

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

Southwest District

Torrance Dept. E

 

SONIA GRAMAJO-OLIVARRI,

 

 

 

Plaintiff,

 

Case No.:

 

 

21STCV05451

 

vs.

 

 

[Tentative] Granted

 

ALLIANTE INTERNATIONAL, NORTHGATE GONZALEZ MARKET, INC., A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, CHRISTIAN ROBLEZ, AN INDIVIDUAL, CARMEN MORENO, AN INDIVIDUAL, AND DOES 1-10, INCLUSIVE

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Defendants.

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Hearing Date:                         October 10, 2024

Moving Parties:                      Plaintiff Sonia Gramajo-Olivarri

Responding Party:                  Defendant Alliante International

HEARING:                            Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Set Two

 

The Court considered the moving papers.

RULING

            The Court grants the Motion to Compel Responses to Request for Production, Set Two.

BACKGROUND

 

On March 18, 2024, Plaintiff Sonia Gramajo-Olivarri filed the Complaint alleging causes of action for negligence, negligence per se, negligent hiring, false arrest, false imprisonment, assault, battery, and IIED against Defendants Alliante International, Northgate Gonzales Market, Robles, and Moreno resulting from Plaintiff being detained while at Defendants grocery mart.

            On July 19, 2024, Plaintiff served the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two on Defendants Alliante International via email.

            On August 22, 2024, Plaintiff sent a meet and confer letter to Defendant’s counsel.

            Defendant is yet to respond to the Production of Documents, Set Two

            On September 19, 2024, Plaintiff filed the instant motion.

            No opposition has been filed

            Trial is set for January 29, 2025.

Legal Standard

            A party must respond to interrogatories and requests for production of documents within 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a).) If a party to whom interrogatories or requests for production of documents are directed does not provide timely responses, the requesting party may move for an order compelling responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (c).) The party also waives the right to make any objections, including one based on privilege or work-product protection. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300, subd. (a).) There is no time limit for a motion to compel responses to interrogatories or production of documents other than the cut-off on hearing discovery motions 15 days before trial. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2024.020, subd. (a), 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300.) No meet and confer efforts are required before filing a motion to compel responses to the discovery. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290; Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300; Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 411.) 

 

Discussion

            Defendant has failed to respond to the Request for Production of Documents, Set Two, served on July 19, 2024. (Mergerdomian Decl. ¶ 3.) Defendant has filed no opposition to this motion.

Therefore, the Court grants the motion to compel.

Sanctions

 

Failing to respond to an authorized method of discovery is a sanctionable misuse of the discovery process. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d).) If a motion to compel a party to respond to interrogatories is successful, the court must impose a monetary sanction against that party unless the sanction would be unjust. (Code Civ. Proc § 2030.290 subd. (c), 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d); Sinaiko Healthcare Consulting, Inc. v. Pacific Healthcare Consultants (2007) 148 Cal.App.4th 390, 404.) The monetary sanction includes the moving party’s reasonable expenses, such as attorney’s fees and costs. (Code Civ. Proc § 2023.030 subd. (a).)

            Here, Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $1,460. Defendant has filed no opposition arguing why sanctions would be unjust. Plaintiffs merely assert that they have “incurred significant costs in preparing this motion to compel due to Defendants’ failure to respond to the RFPD.” (Mergerdomian Decl. ¶ 10.) However, given that sanctions are mandatory and factoring in the complexity of the instant motion, the Court grants the sanctions in the amount of 500$. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030 subd. (a), 2023.010 subd. (d).)

            Therefore, the court grants the Motion to Compel and orders sanctions in the sum of 500$.

HEARING: MOTION TO QUASH SERVICE OF SUMMONS AND TO DISMISS FOR LACK OF PERSONAL JURISDICTION

 

            The Court considered the moving, opposition, and reply papers.

 

RULING

 

            The Court grants the Motion to Quash Summons and Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction.

 

BACKGROUND

 

            On January 5, 2024, Plaintiff Devylle Campbell filed the Complaint alleging causes of action for battery, negligence, IIED, fraudulent concealment, private nuisance, public nuisance, and breach of contract against Defendants Hilton Worldwide Holdings, Inc., Park Hotels & Resorts, Inc., Seaview Investors LLC., Embassy Suites By Hilton Los Angeles International Airport North, Mauricio Joya, and Does 1 through 20, Inclusive arising from an alleged bed bug incident that occurred at the Embassy Suites By Hilton Los Angeles International Airport North on January 28, 2022.

 

            On August 30, 2024, Defendant Park Hotels & Resorts, Inc. filed the instant Motion to Quash Service of Summons and to Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction.

 

            On September 23, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On September 30, 2024, Defendant filed the Reply

           

LEGAL STANDARD

 

            “A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion for one or more of the following purposes: (1) To quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a).)  

 

In California, courts are authorized to “exercise jurisdiction over parties on any basis not inconsistent with the Constitution of [California] or the United States.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 410.10.) Personal jurisdiction may be either general or specific. (Vons Companies, Inc., supra, 14 Cal.4th at 445.) “The nature and the quality of the defendant’s contacts determine whether jurisdiction, if exercised, is general or specific. General jurisdiction exists when a defendant is domiciled in the forum state or his activities there are substantial, continuous, and systematic.” (F. Hoffman-La Roche, Ltd. v. Superior Court (2005) 130 Cal.App.4th 782, 796.) Where the contacts are sufficiently substantial, continuous, and systematic, it is not necessary that the cause of action alleged be connected with the defendant’s business relationship to the forum. (Vons, supra, 14 Cal.4th at 445.) However, “contacts that are random, fortuitous, or attenuated do not rise to the minimum level, and general jurisdiction cannot be exercised under these circumstances.” (F. Hoffman-La Roche, Ltd., supra, 130 Cal.App.4th at 795.)

 

If a defendant’s contacts with the forum state are not substantial, continuous, and systematic, the defendant may be subject to specific jurisdiction. “A court may exercise specific jurisdiction over a nonresident defendant only if: (1) ‘the defendant has purposefully availed himself or herself of forum benefits’ [citation]; (2) ‘the “controversy is related to or ‘arises out of’ [the] defendant's contacts with the forum”’ [citations]; and (3) ‘“the assertion of personal jurisdiction would comport with ‘fair play and substantial justice.’” ‘[Citations.]” (Pavlovich (2002) 29 Cal.4th 262, 269.)

 

When a defendant challenges personal jurisdiction, the plaintiff has the burden to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, the factual basis for the exercise of jurisdiction. (Vons, supra, 14 Cal.4th at 449.)

 

“[W]hen the facts giving rise to jurisdiction are conflicting, the trial court’s factual determinations are reviewed for substantial evidence . . . . Even then, we review independently the trial court's conclusions as to the legal significance of the facts . . . . When the jurisdictional facts are not in dispute, whether the defendant is subject to personal jurisdiction is purely a legal question that we review de novo.” (F. Hoffman-La Roche, Inc., supra, 130 Cal.App.4th at 794; accord Muckle v. Superior Court (2002) 102 Cal.App.4th 218, 228.)

 

Evidence consisting of “vague assertions of ultimate fact rather than specific evidentiary facts” is insufficient to meet the burden of showing minimum contacts. (Paneno v. Centres for Academic Programmes Abroad Ltd. (2004) 118 Cal.App.4th 1447, 1454.)

 

DISCUSSION

 

When a defendant challenges personal jurisdiction, Plaintiff has the burden to prove, by a preponderance of the evidence, the factual basis for the exercise of jurisdiction. (Vons, supra, 14 Cal.4th at 449.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff has provided no evidence that California has personal jurisdiction over Defendant. In the Complaint, Plaintiff alleges that “at all times mentioned herein, Plaintiff is informed, believes, and thereon alleges that Defendant, PARK HOTELS & RESORTS, INC., a Virginia corporation with its principal executive offices in McLean, Virginia, owns, operates, manages, and is doing business as EMBASSY SUITES BY HILTON LOS ANGELES INTERNATIONAL AIRPORT NORTH, a business form unknown, located at 9801 Airport Blvd, Los Angeles, California 90045.” (Complaint ¶ 5.) However, Plaintiff must “do more than merely allege jurisdictional facts.” (In re Auto. Antitrust Cases I & II (2005) 135 Cal.App.4th 100, 110.) Furthermore, Plaintiff provides no further facts or arguments in its opposition, which would lead to a finding of personal jurisdiction.

 

            Therefore, the Court grants the Motion to Quash Summons and Dismiss for Lack of Personal Jurisdiction.

HEARING: Motion to Compel Arbitration

            The Court considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

RULING

            The Court denies Defendants Motion to Compel Arbitration.

BACKGROUND

On February 29, 2024, Plaintiff Scott Carr filed the Complaint alleging causes of action for negligence and premises liability against Defendants Stadco LA, LLC, Stadco LA Manager, LLC, Hollywood Park Management Company, LLC, Hollywood Park Management Company II, LLC And Hollywood Park Land Company, LLC arising from an alleged slip and fall at SOFI Stadium.

            On May 22, 2024, Defendants filed the instant Motion to Compel Arbitration.

 

            On July 1, 2024, Plaintiff filed the Opposition.

 

            On October 2, 2024, Defendants filed the Reply.     

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

The Federal Arbitration Act (the “FAA”) applies in both federal and state courts to contracts evidencing a transaction involving interstate commerce. (9 U.S.C. §§ 1-2; Southland Corp. v. Keating (1984) 465 U.S. 1, 12.) The FAA preempts conflicting state law. (Preston v. Ferrer (2008) 552 U.S. 346, 353.) The party that contends the FAA applies bears the burden to demonstrate that the arbitration agreement is in a “‘contract evidencing a transaction involving commerce’ . . . .” (Woolls v. Super. Ct. (2005) 127 Cal.App.4th 197, 211.) 

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, “[o]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that: (a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or (b)¿Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement.”¿ 

 

 

DISCUSSION

 

On May 10, 2018, Plaintiff and Defendant Stadco LLC entered into the “Rams Stadium License Agreement (the Agreement).” The Agreement stated in pertinent part

 

“11) CHOICE OF LAW; MANDATORY AND BINDING ARBITRATION; NO CLASS OR REPRESENTATIVE ACTIONS OR ARBITRATIONS; LIMITATION ON TIME TO MAKE CLAIMS.

PLEASE READ THIS PROVISION CAREFULLY. THIS LICENSE AGREEMENT PROVIDES THAT ALL DISPUTES BETWEEN THE PARTIES WILL BE RESOLVED BY BINDING ARBITRATION. LICENSEE IS THUS GIVING UP LICENSEE’S RIGHTS TO GO TO COURT TO ASSERT OR DEFEND ITS RIGHTS UNDER THIS LICENSE AGREEMENT (EXCEPT FOR MATTERS THAT MAY BE TAKEN TO SMALL CLAIMS COURT). THE PARTIES’ RIGHTS WILL BE DETERMINED BY A NEUTRAL ARBITRATOR AND NOT A JUDGE OR JURY. THE PARTIES ARE ENTITLED TO A FAIR HEARING, BUT THE ARBITRATION PROCEDURES ARE SIMPLER AND MORE LIMITED THAN RULES APPLICABLE IN COURT. THE ARBITRATOR’S DECISIONS ARE AS ENFORCEABLE AS ANY COURT ORDER AND ARE SUBJECT TO VERY LIMITED REVIEW BY A COURT.

a) Choice of Law. THIS LICENSE AGREEMENT WILL BE CONSTRUED AND ENFORCED IN ACCORDANCE WITH ALL APPLICABLE FEDERAL LAWS AND THE LAWS OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA, EXCLUDING ITS CONFLICT OF LAW RULES. The parties waive any claims that may arise under the laws of other states, countries, territories, or jurisdictions.

b) Mandatory and Binding Arbitration. With respect to any and all disputes arising out of or relating to this License Agreement, no matter on what theory, including without limitation, contract, tort, statutory or regulatory, or common law, and including regarding the applicability or validity of this arbitration provision (collectively, “Claims”), the parties agree to negotiate in good faith to achieve a mutually satisfactory resolution. If the parties do not resolve any dispute by informal negotiation, any other effort to resolve the dispute will be conducted exclusively by binding arbitration as described in this Section 11; provided, however, that the parties have the right to litigate any Claim in small claims court, if all the requirements of the small claims court, including any limitations on jurisdiction and the amount at issue in the Claim, are satisfied. Licensee agrees to bring a Claim in small claims court only in Licensee’s county of residence or the County of Los Angeles, California. LICENSEE ACKNOWLEDGES THAT LICENSEE IS GIVING UP THE RIGHT TO LITIGATE (OR PARTICIPATE IN AS A PARTY OR CLASS MEMBER) ALL DISPUTES IN COURT BEFORE A JUDGE OR JURY, except as described in the foregoing sentence for Claims that may be litigated in small claims court. Instead, to the maximum extent permitted by applicable law, all Claims will be resolved before a neutral arbitrator, whose decision will be final except for a limited right of appeal under the Federal Arbitration Act, 9 U.S.C. sections 1-9 (or any successor thereto). Any court with jurisdiction over the parties may enforce the arbitrator's award.

Except for Claims eligible to be heard in small claims court as described above, all Claims that cannot be settled through informal negotiation will be adjudicated exclusively through confidential binding arbitration in accordance with the then current Consumer Arbitration Rules of the American Arbitration Association (the “AAA”), or, in the event the AAA declines or is unable to administer the arbitration, by a nationally recognized arbitration forum reasonably selected by Fanfaire. AAA rules are available from the AAA, which can be contacted by mail at 725 South Figueroa, Suite 400, Los Angeles, California 90017, by telephone at (800) 778-7879, or through its website at www.adr.org. The arbitrator’s award shall be binding and may be entered as a judgment in a court of competent jurisdiction. Licensee agrees that the Stadium Company, Fanfaire or the Team Party may seek any interim or preliminary relief from a court of competent jurisdiction in Los Angeles County, California necessary to protect its rights or property pending the completion of arbitration." (Motion to Compel Arbitration “MTC” p 3-4.)

 

1.     Existence of Valid Arbitration Agreement and Claims Covered by Arbitration Clause¿ 

 

“[T]he petitioner bears the burden of proving the existence of a valid arbitration agreement by the preponderance of the evidence . . . .” (Giuliano v. Inland Empire Personnel, Inc. (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 1276, 1284.) “In determining whether an arbitration agreement applies to a specific dispute, the court may examine only the agreement itself and the complaint filed by the party refusing arbitration [citation]. The court should attempt to give effect to the parties’ intentions, in light of the usual and ordinary meaning of the contractual language and the circumstances under which the agreement was made.” (Weeks v. Crow (1980) 113 Cal.App.3d 350, 353.) “To determine whether a contractual arbitration clause requires arbitration of a particular controversy, the controversy is first identified, and the issue is whether that controversy is within the scope of the contractual arbitration clause.” (Titolo v. Cano (2007) 157 Cal.App.4th 310, 316.) “Doubts as to whether an arbitration clause applies to a particular dispute are to be resolved in favor of sending the parties to arbitration. The court should order them to arbitrate unless it is clear that the arbitration clause cannot be interpreted to cover the dispute.” (California Correctional Peace Officers Ass’n v. State (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 198, 205.) 

 

[A] party opposing the petition bears the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence any fact necessary to its defense. [Citation.] In these summary proceedings, the trial court sits as a trier of fact, weighing all the affidavits, declarations, and other documentary evidence, as well as oral testimony received at the court’s discretion, to reach a final determination. (Giuliano, supra, 149 Cal.App.4th at p. 1284.) 

 

Defendants argue that general contract principles apply to determine whether an arbitration agreement exists, mutual assent to a contract can be expressed through conduct, even if the party did not read the specific terms, including the arbitration clause. They contend that Plaintiff agreed to the terms of the "Los Angeles Rams Stadium Seat License Agreement," which included an arbitration clause, by electronically signing and initialing the contract through DocuSign. (Forrester Decl. ¶ 6.)

 

Defendants argue that they have satisfied the legal requirement to demonstrate the existence of the arbitration agreement by attaching the License Agreement to their petition and quoting the arbitration clause. (Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1058, 1060.)

 

In opposition, Plaintiff argues that their claims for negligence and premises liability, which arise from the condition of the stadium's concession area, are not subject to arbitration under the "Stadium Seat License Agreement" because these claims do not stem from the contract. (Khalatian v. Prime Time Shuttle, Inc. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 651, 660.) Defendants rely on an arbitration clause within the License Agreement, which broadly covers disputes "arising out of or relating to" the Agreement. However, Plaintiff contends that this provision only applies to disputes related to the limited rights granted by the License Agreement, primarily concerning the right to purchase tickets.

 

Plaintiff emphasizes that the Agreement does not provide general admission to the stadium and that their claims, which involve personal injury, are unrelated to any specific rights or obligations created by the Agreement. Instead, Plaintiff's presence at the stadium, like that of any other ticket holder, was based on the purchase of event tickets, not the Agreement.

Therefore, Plaintiff’s claims are based on general duties owed by Defendants to all stadium attendees under California Civil Code section 1714, not duties arising from the Agreement.

 

Plaintiff points out that the Agreement was merely an initial step toward purchasing season tickets, and the Agreement is not mentioned in their complaint. Consequently, the claims do not have their "roots" in the contractual relationship created by the Agreement. The cases cited by Defendants, which apply arbitration clauses to tort claims, do not apply here because the claims are independent of the Agreement.

 

Additionally, Plaintiff argues that even broad arbitration clauses must be limited to disputes arising from the contract. (Elijahjuan v. Superior Court (2012) 210 Cal.App.4th 15, 23–24; Hoover v. American Income Life Ins. Co. (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 1193. 1208.) Since Plaintiff’s claims are based on general premises liability duties owed to all attendees, not on the Agreement, arbitration should not be compelled. Thus, Plaintiff argues that Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration should be denied.

 

In reply, Defendants argue that Plaintiff's personal injury claims are directly connected to the Agreement. They assert that the Agreement granted Plaintiff the right to purchase season tickets for games at SoFi Stadium, which opened in 2020 and where the alleged injury occurred on September 17, 2023. Defendants contend that because Plaintiff's injury occurred at SoFi Stadium while attending a game as a season ticket holder, the claims are "rooted in the relationship between the parties created by the contract." Therefore, Plaintiff's claims are linked to the Agreement, as it enabled the purchase of the season tickets that allowed Plaintiff to attend the game at which the injury occurred.

 

Broad arbitration agreements may encompass torts and contractual liabilities “as long as they have their roots in the relationship between the parties which was created by the contract.” (Berman v. Dean Witter & Co., Inc. (1975) 44 Cal.App.3d 999, 1003.) Here, the Agreement Plaintiff signed was a required step to purchase season tickets for the Los Angeles Rams, who play their home games at Sofi Stadium. (Reply, p. 3-4.) The alleged injury occurred at Sofi Stadium on September 17, 2023, the same date the Rams were playing the 49ers at Sofi Stadium. Therefore, because the Agreement was a requirement to purchase tickets for the event at which the alleged injury occurred, the Court finds that the Plaintiff’s dispute does arise out of the Agreement. Plaintiff relies on Elijahjuan to argue that Plaintiff’s claims do not arise out of the Agreement. However, Elijahjuan is distinguishable. In Elijahjuan, the Court found that an employee’s claims regarding misclassification of employees did not arise for the contract containing the arbitration agreement because “No allegation in the FAC is based on rights afforded petitioners under the terms of the Agreements.” (Elijahjuan,210 Cal.App.4th 15, 21.)  Here, however, the Agreement was required to purchase Rams season tickets, which granted Plaintiff the right to enter the stadium on game day.” Since the injury occurred on gameday, claims involving the injury arise from the right of entry granted by the Agreement.

 

            Non-Signatory Parties

 

      Defendants claim that Plaintiff’s claims against other Defendants must also go to arbitration because they are "intimately founded in and intertwined with" the License Agreement's terms. (Garcia v. Pexco, LLC (2017) 11 Cal.App.5th 782, 786.) Under the doctrine of equitable estoppel, a non-signatory to the arbitration agreement can compel arbitration when the claims against them are deeply connected to the contractual obligations. This doctrine applies to Plaintiff’s claims against other Defendants since these claims arise from the same contractual relationship under which Plaintiff purchased season tickets. (Garcia, 11 Cal.App.5th at 786) Thus, Defendants maintain that Plaintiff is bound by the arbitration clause, and all claims, including those against non-signatory Defendants, should be referred to arbitration.

 

Plaintiff argues that the seat license agreement, which contains the arbitration clause, was entered into with Defendant Stadco LA, LLC, along with LA Rams Tenant, LLC, and Fanfaire, Inc., but these entities are not defendants in this action. The named defendants in the case—Stadco LA Manager, LLC, Hollywood Park Management Company, LLC, Hollywood Park Management Company II, LLC, and Hollywood Park Land Company, LLC—were not parties to the seat license agreement and therefore cannot compel arbitration.

 

Defendants claim that they can still enforce arbitration because Plaintiff’s claims are “intimately founded in and intertwined” with the License Agreement. However, Plaintiff contends this argument lacks any evidence and that, generally, non-signatories to an arbitration agreement cannot enforce it unless they meet specific exceptions. The burden to demonstrate such an exception falls on the non-signatory, which Defendants have not done here.

 

Plaintiff argues that non-signatories can only enforce arbitration if the plaintiff’s claims are dependent on or bound up with the contractual obligations of the agreement containing the arbitration clause. (Goldman v. KPMG, LLP 173 Cal.App.4th 209.)

 

“The sine qua non for allowing a non-signatory to enforce an arbitration clause based on equitable estoppel is that the claims the plaintiff asserts against the non-signatory are dependent on or inextricably bound up with the contractual obligations of the agreement containing the arbitration clause.” (Goldman, 173 Cal.App.4th 209, 213-214.) Furthermore, the fact that the misconduct could not have happened without the signing of the agreement does not prove that the claims are inextricably bound. (See, Goldman, 173 Cal.App.4th 209, 232-233.) Here, Plaintiff argues that the inextricably bound because “Any claims he has against the other Defendants are necessarily predicated on his contractual season ticket holder agreement with StadCo.” (MTC p. 7.) While it is true that the Agreement was required for the purchasing of tickets, which allowed Plaintiff to enter the stadium where he was injured, the fact that the incident could not have occurred without the signing of the agreement does not prove that the claims are inextricably bound. (See, Goldman, 173 Cal.App.4th 209, 232-233.) Additionally, like in Goldman, Plaintiff does not reference the Agreement in their complaint. (Id. at p. 218.) Therefore, the Court finds that Plaintiff is not compelled to arbitrate its claims with non-signatory Defendants based on the Agreement.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c)

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c) provides that arbitration will be ordered when there is an agreement to arbitrate unless “[a] party to the arbitration agreement is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party, arising out of the same transaction or series of related transactions and there is a possibility of conflicting rulings on a common issue of law or fact.”

 

Plaintiff argues that under section 1281.2(c), even if the arbitration clause is broad enough to cover Plaintiff's claims against Stadco LA, LLC, arbitration cannot be compelled for claims against remaining non-signatory defendants. Therefore, the Court should deny arbitration altogether. (See Birl v. Heritage Care, LLC (2009) 172 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1319-1323.) Plaintiff argues that all three prongs of Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c) have been met. First, Plaintiff is a party to both the arbitration agreement and a pending court action involving third parties, namely the non-signatory defendants, who are not bound by or entitled to enforce the arbitration agreement. Second, Plaintiff’s claims against all defendants, including Stadco LA, LLC, arise from the same injuries caused by the same acts of negligence, making them part of the same transaction or series of related transactions. Third, if Plaintiff’s claims against Stadco LA, LLC are sent to arbitration while the claims against other defendants proceed in court, there is a significant possibility of conflicting rulings on common issues of law or fact. For these reasons, Plaintiff contends that the Court should exercise its discretion to deny arbitration altogether.

 

Defendant does not address this issue in their reply.

 

In Birl, the Court of Appeals found that the trial court did not abuse its discretion by denying a motion to compel under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c) regarding a case brought against multiple hospitals and physicians regarding the death of the Plaintiff's family member. (Birl, 172 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1322.)

 

Here, like in Birl, all three prongs of Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c) have been met. (See, Birl, 172 Cal.App.4th 1313, 1322.) First, if the arbitration agreement were to be granted as to only Stadco, Plaintiff would be a party to the arbitration with Stadco and would also be a party to the ongoing court action against the non-signatory defendants. Second, both the arbitration and the civil suit would revolve around Plaintiff's alleged injury, which occurred on September 17, 2023.  Last, since the two actions will be based on identical facts, it is likely that conflicting rulings of fact or law will be issued. 

 

Therefore, the Court uses the discretion granted to it under Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c) to Deny the Motion to Compel Arbitration in its entirety.

 

The Court’s Power to Determine Arbitrability

 

            Defendants argue that the dispute should be resolved through arbitration, as any threshold questions regarding arbitrability must be decided by the arbitrator. They assert that Plaintiff’s claims fall within the broad scope of the Arbitration Agreement, which applies to "any and all disputes arising out of or relating to this License Agreement." The claims in the complaint, involving injuries due to a dangerous condition at SoFi Stadium, are directly covered by the License Agreement and, therefore, fall within the arbitration clause.

           

            However, “The question of whether non-signatory is a party to an arbitration agreement is one for the trial court in the first instance.” (American Builder's Assn. v. Au-Yang (1990) 226 Cal.App.3d 170, 179.) Here, the Courts decision to deny the instant motion does not revolve around the arbitration agreement involving Plaintiff and Stadco, which the court found to be valid. Instead, the decision to deny the instant motion revolves around the non-signatories and Code of Civil Procedure section 1281.2, subdivision (c). Therefore, Defendants arguments regarding arbitrability are immaterial.

 

The Court denies Defendants’ motion to compel arbitration
