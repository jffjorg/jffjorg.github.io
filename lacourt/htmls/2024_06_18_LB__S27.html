DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 23LBCV00523    Hearing Date: June 18, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Claudia Lankford, individually and as successor-in-interest to Valentina Timascu, filed this action against Defendants, Long Beach Memorial Medical Center, Steven J. Appleby, M.D., and Maurice A. Bell, M.D., as well as Sorin Musat as a nominal defendant, for medical malpractice and wrongful death arising out of Defendants’ care and treatment of Decedent, Timascu. 

 

2.     Motions for Summary Judgment

a.     Law Governing Standard of Care

The standard of care against which the acts of health care providers are to be measured is a matter within the knowledge of experts.  Elcome v. Chin (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 310, 317.  Unless the conduct required by the particular circumstances is within the common knowledge of the layman, the standard of care in a malpractice action can only be proved by an expert’s testimony.  Id.  If the “common knowledge” exception does not apply to a medical malpractice action, expert evidence is conclusive and cannot be disregarded.  Id. 

 

A medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because he chooses one medically acceptable method of treatment or diagnosis and it turns out that another medically accepted method would have been a better choice.  CACI 506.  Likewise, a medical practitioner is not necessarily negligent just because his efforts are unsuccessful or he makes an error that was reasonable under the circumstances.  CACI 505.

 

Whether the standard of care in the community has been breached presents the basic issue in a malpractice action and can only be proved by opinion testimony unless the medical question is within the common knowledge of laypersons.  See Jambazian v. Borden (1994) 25 Cal.App4th 836, 844. “‘When a defendant moves for summary judgment and supports his motion with expert declarations that his conduct fell within the community standard of care, he is entitled to summary judgment unless the plaintiff comes forward with conflicting expert evidence.’“ (Munro v. Regents of University of California (1989) 215 Cal.App.3d 977, 984-985.)

 

b.     Law Governing Causation

In order to establish that defendant's negligence was a “substantial factor” in causing injury or death, the plaintiff must prove the negligence was of itself sufficient to bring about that harm.  “The law is well settled that in a personal injury action causation must be proven within a reasonable medical probability based upon competent expert testimony.  Mere possibility alone is insufficient to establish a prima facie case.  [Citations.]  That there is a distinction between a reasonable medical ‘probability’ and a medical 'possibility' needs little discussion.  There can be many possible ‘causes,’ indeed, an infinite number of circumstances which can produce an injury or disease. A possible cause only becomes ‘probable’ when, in the absence of other reasonable causal explanations, it becomes more likely than not that the injury was a result of its action.  This is the outer limit of inference upon which an issue may be submitted to the jury.” Bromme v. Pavitt (1992) 5 Cal.App.4th 1487, 1498; citing Jones v. Ortho Pharmaceutical Corp. (1985) 163 Cal.App.3d 396, 402 403.

 

c.     Analysis

Dr. Bell moves for summary judgment on the complaint, contending his care and treatment of Plaintiff complied with the standard of care and nothing he did caused or contributed to Decedent’s death.  He supports his motion with expert medical declarations.  Dr. Bell supports his motion with the Declaration of Michael Eilbert, MD.  The Court has reviewed the moving declarations and find they are sufficient to meet the burden to show that the moving defendant complied with the standard of care at all times in connection with Decedent’s care and treatment, and that nothing he did caused or contributed to Decedent’s death. 

 

The Court notes that Plaintiff indicates, with her opposition papers, that she intends to appear at the hearing and voice oral objections to Defendants’ evidence submitted with their moving papers.  CRC 3.1352 permits oral objections at the hearing, and the Court will entertain the objections at that time.

 

On 6/05/24, Plaintiff filed untimely (one day late) opposition to the motions.  Because summary judgment is an extreme remedy, the Court has read and considered the papers despite their lack of timeliness.  Plaintiff supports her oppositions with the Declaration of Dr. Jeffrey Brown, M.D.  Defendants filed objections to Brown’s declaration.  The objections are sustained.  The Court incorporates its analysis from Co-Defendant, LBMMC’s 3/05/24 summary judgment ruling by reference.  The Court therein held:

Dr. Brown’s CV indicates he is a “skilled clinical researcher with 15 years of experience in the healthcare industry at the intersection of clinical care delivery & clinical documentation.”  It states he has been retained as an EMR/EHR expert in litigation; these terms are not defined in the CV. The remainder of his CV makes it clear that he largely works for hospitals and medical groups determining whether their care is medically necessary in order to ensure insurance coverage will be provided. 

 

As Defendant correctly notes in reply, Dr. Brown does not declare that he is a licensed medical doctor or that he is familiar with the standard of care applicable to nurses or non-physician staff.  See Keene v. Prisinzano (1972) 23 Cal.App.3d 275, 279.  The objection on the ground that Dr. Brown failed to establish he is qualified to testify concerning standard of care or causation is therefore sustained.

 

Additionally, the Court notes that Dr. Brown sets forth all of the facts of the case, and then concludes, “For the reasons stated above, it is my opinion that the moving party’s medical care was below the community standard and did not meet the decedent’s needs and therefore, constituted medical negligence, and arguably constitute malpractice. More likely than not their actions (or inactions) hastened and contributed to Valentina Timascu’s unfortunate death.” 

 

This is a very vague conclusion.  The acts “arguably constitute malpractice”?  They “more likely than not” hastened death?  An expert opposing a summary judgment motion must opine that, “to a reasonable medical probability,” the defendant’s acts caused the damages.  See Saelzler v. Advanced Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 775-776.  More likely than not is not the same as to a reasonable medical probability.  These objections on the ground that Dr. Brown failed to adequately opine concerning standard of care and/or causation are therefore also sustained.

 

The Court notes that the same declaration was submitted with the opposition to these motions, and it is subject to the same objections, which are sustained.    

 

If the Court overrules Plaintiff’s objections to Defendants’ moving evidence at the time of the hearing, the motions will be granted.  If the parties submit on the tentative, the motions will be granted.

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Ralina Melinda Melgoza, et al. filed this action against Defendant, David J. Kelly, Trustee of the David J. Kelly Separate Property Trust for damages arising out of the parties’ landlord-tenant relationship. 

 

Plaintiffs’ operative complaint is their original complaint, which they filed on 7/24/23.  It includes causes of action for nuisance, negligence, breach of the implied warranty of habitability, breach of the covenant of good faith and fair dealing, breach of the implied covenant of quiet enjoyment, violation of Civil Code §1942.4, and IIED. 

 

2.     Demurrer to Complaint

a.     Legal Standard on Demurrer

A demurrer is a pleading used to test the legal sufficiency of other pleadings. It raises issues of law, not fact, regarding the form or content of the opposing party’s pleading.  It is not the function of the demurrer to challenge the truthfulness of the complaint; and for purpose of the ruling on the demurrer, all facts pleaded in the complaint are assumed to be true, however improbable they may be.

 

A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack; or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable. Blank v. Kirwan 39 Cal.3d 311 (1985). No other extrinsic evidence can be considered (i.e., no “speaking demurrers”). A demurrer is brought under CCP § 430.10 [grounds], § 430.30 [as to any matter on its face or from which judicial notice may be taken], and § 430.50(a) [can be taken to the entire complaint or any cause of action within].  Specifically, a demurrer may be brought per CCP § 430.10(e) if insufficient facts are stated to support the cause of action asserted.  Per CCP §430.10(a) a demurrer may be brought where the court has no jurisdiction of the subject of the cause of action alleged in the pleading.  Furthermore, demurrer for uncertainty will be sustained only where the complaint is so bad that the defendant cannot reasonably respond.  CCP § 430.10(f). 

 

However, in construing the allegations, the court is to give effect to specific factual allegations that may modify or limit inconsistent general or conclusory allegations. Financial Corporation of America v. Wilburn, 189 Cal.App.3rd 764, 769 (1987). And, if the facts pled in the complaint are inconsistent with facts which are incorporated by reference from exhibits attached to the complaint, the facts in the incorporated exhibits control. Further, irrespective of the name or label given to a cause of action by the plaintiff, a general demurrer must be overruled if the facts as pled in the body of the complaint state some valid claim for relief. Special demurrers are not allowed in limited jurisdiction courts. (CCP § 92(c).)

 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Goodman v. Kennedy, 18 Cal.3d 335, 348 (1976). The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.)

 

Finally, CCP section 430.41 requires that “[b]efore filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (CCP §430.41(a).) The parties are to meet and confer at least five days before the date the responsive pleading is due. (CCP §430.41(a)(2).) Thereafter, the demurring party shall file and serve a declaration detailing their meet and confer efforts. (CCP §430.41(a)(3).)

 

  1. Meet and Confer

Defense Counsel declares he and Plaintiffs’ attorney met and conferred telephonically, but were unable to resolve the issues presented by way of the demurrer.  The meet and confer requirement is therefore satisfied. 

 

  1. Seventh Cause of Action, IIED

i.              Defendant’s Contentions

Defendant demurs to the seventh cause of action, contending Plaintiffs have not pled extreme and outrageous conduct to support imposition of liability for intentional infliction of emotional distress.  Additionally, he contends Plaintiffs have not pled that they suffered severe emotional distress.

 

ii.             Extreme and Outrageous Conduct

Neither party cites any authority concerning whether and when IIED can be pled in the context of a habitability claim.  The Court is aware of Burness v. Chimney (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1062 and Stoiber v. Honeychuck (1980) 101 Cal.App.3d 903, 921, both of which considered this issue. 

 

The refusal to repair toxic mold after repeated notification, resulting in “severe physical injury and discomfort” and the inability to occupy premises for business use, was held sufficient in Burnett to state a claim for IIED. 

 

In Stoiber, the plaintiff alleged the landlord knew about “leaking sewage, deteriorated flooring, falling ceiling, leaking roof, broken windows, and other unsafe and dangerous conditions” and therefore the question of the outrageousness of landlord's conduct could go to the jury.  While conclusory allegations of a failure to repair may not be enough to show outrageous conduct, specific allegations of knowingly allowing continuing leaking sewage -- conduct which directly implicates sanitation and health – justified the cause of action as pled.

 

Plaintiffs herein allege that they complained of habitability violations, including cockroaches, plumbing issues, water leaks, flooding, water damaged ceiling, cracked ceiling, cracks and holes in walls and floors, defective windows, doors, cabinets, and frames, defective smoke detectors, defective light and ventilation, and the presence of mold.  ¶26.  They allege multiple attempts to request repairs throughout 2021 and 2022, including two complaints to the Department of Public Health, which resulted in numerous DPH violations being issued between fall of 2022 and spring of 2023.  The Court finds these allegations are sufficiently similar to those found in Burnett and Stoiber to support the allegation that Defendant engaged in sufficiently outrageous conduct to state a claim for IIED.

 

iii.            Severe Emotional Distress

Plaintiffs fail, in their opposition papers, to address Defendant’s argument that they have not pled facts showing they suffered severe emotional distress as a result of Defendant’s actions.  Plaintiffs’ allegations in this regard are found at ¶¶124-125 of their complaint.  Plaintiffs allege:

124. As a result of DEFENDANTS’ intentional conduct, PLAINTIFFS suffered from extreme emotional distress when: they were threatened with eviction, when they lost a sense of normalcy and comfort, and when they had difficulty sleeping at night because of DEFENDANTS conduct. PLAINTIFFS allege this intentional conduct by was the and actual and proximate cause of the extreme emotional distress they have suffered.

125. As a result of DEFENDANTS’ acts and omissions, PLAINTIFFS did suffer severe emotional distress, mental stress, annoyance, discomfort, aggravation, humiliation, degradation, and embarrassment, thereby entitling PLAINTIFFS to punitive damages according to proof at trial.

 

Severe emotional distress means “emotional distress of such substantial quality or enduring quality that no reasonable [person] in civilized society should be expected to endure it.”  Girard v. Ball (1981) 125 Cal.App.3d 772, 787 788.  The Court agrees with Defendant; Plaintiffs have not pled the existence of such severe emotional distress that no reasonably person should be expected to endure it.  The demurrer on this ground is sustained.  Leave to amend is granted if and only if Plaintiffs can plead facts establishing they suffered severe emotional distress as defined above. 

 

3.     Motion to Strike

Plaintiffs seek punitive damages in connection with their seventh cause of action for IIED.  Because the cause of action is not adequately pled, as detailed above, the motion to strike is moot. 

 

4.     Conclusion

Defendant’s demurrer to the IIED cause of action is sustained with leave to amend on the ground that Plaintiffs failed to plead severe emotional distress.  The motion to strike is moot.
