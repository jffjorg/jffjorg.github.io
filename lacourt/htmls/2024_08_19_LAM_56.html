DEPARTMENT 56 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

DEPARTMENT 56 JUDGE HOLLY J. FUJIE, LAW AND MOTION RULINGS. The court makes every effort to post tentative rulings by 5.00 pm of the court day before the hearing. The tentative ruling will not become the final ruling until the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(a)(2)], and are also available in the courtroom on the day of the hearing [see CRC 3.1308(b)]. If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance, all counsel must agree and choose which counsel will give notice. That counsel must 1) call Dept 56 by 8:30 a.m. on the day of the hearing (213/633-0656) and state that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling, and 2) serve notice of the ruling on all parties. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary and all parties should appear at the hearing in person or by Court Call. Court reporters are not provided, and parties who want a record of motions and other proceedings must hire a privately retained certified court reporter.

Case Number: 23STCV00036    Hearing Date: August 19, 2024    Dept: 56

 

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

FOR THE COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES - CENTRAL DISTRICT

 

TANISHA GLASPER, et al.,

                        Plaintiffs,

            vs.

 

B&B APARTMENTS, et al.,

 

                        Defendants.

 

      CASE NO.: 23STCV00036

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER RE: PETITION FOR APPROVAL OF COMPROMISE OF CLAIM OR ACTION OF DISPOSITION OF PROCEEDS OF JUDGMENT FOR MINOR

 

Date: August 19, 2024

Time: 8:30 a.m.

Dept. 56

 

MOVING PARTY:  Petitioner Tanisha Glasper (“Petitioner”)

 

The Court has considered the moving papers.  No opposition papers were filed.  Any opposition papers were required to have been filed and served at least nine court days before the hearing under California Code of Civil Procedure (“CCP”) section 1005, subdivision (b).

 

BACKGROUND

            Petitioner, individually and as guardian ad litem for minor claimant Ariana Nicholson (the “Minor”), among others, filed a complaint against Defendants.  This action arises out of a landlord/tenant relationship.  The complaint alleges: (1) breach of contract; (2) breach of implied warranty of habitability; (3) nuisance; (4) negligence; (5) intentional infliction of emotional distress; (6) wrongful eviction; (7) violation of Cal. Civ. Code Section 1950.5(1).

 

Petitioner filed the instant petition to approve the compromise of disputed claim on behalf of the Minor (the “Petition”). 

 

DISCUSSION

If an action is pending and settlement is effected prior to trial, the minor’s compromise must be approved by the court.  (CCP § 372.)  A petition to approve a minor’s compromise is governed by California Rules of Court (“CRC”), rules 7.950, et seq. and Probate Code sections 3500 and 3600 et seq.  The trial court is authorized to approve and allow payment of reasonable expenses, costs, and attorney fees in an action concerning the compromise of a minor’s claim.  (Prob. Code, § 3601, subd. (a); Curtis v. Estate of Fagan (2000) 82 Cal.App.4th 270, 277-79; see also CCP § 373.5.)

 

Attorney’s Fees

Unless the court has approved the fee agreement in advance, the court must use a reasonable fee standard when approving and allowing the amount of attorney's fees payable from money or property paid or to be paid for the benefit of a minor or a person with a disability.  (CRC, r. 7.955(a).)  The court must give consideration to the terms of the agreement between the attorney and minor’s representative and must evaluate the agreement based on the facts and circumstances existing at the time the agreement was made.  (CRC, r. 7.955(a)(2).)  CRC Rule 7.955(b)(2) sets out nonexclusive factors the court may consider in determining the reasonableness of attorney’s fees in connection with a petition for minor’s compromise.  Under CRC Rule 7.955(c), the petition must include a declaration by the attorney addressing the factors set forth in CRC Rule 7.955(b)(2) that are applicable to the matter that is before the Court.

 

Here, the Minor, by and through Petitioner, her guardian ad litem, has agreed to settle her claims against Defendants in exchange for $2,500.  Upon approval, $625 of this settlement payment will be allocated towards attorney’s fees, eaving a balance of $1,875.00 to be disbursed to Petitioner for the Minor.

 

            The Court finds that the settlement is fair and reasonable.  Further, the Court considers the requested amount in attorney’s fees, which amounts to 25% of the settlement payment, to be fair and reasonable.  For these reasons and because it is unopposed, the Court GRANTS the Petitions.  (Sexton v. Superior Court (1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

BACKGROUND

This action arises out of a dispute over the payment for medical services. After a sustained demurrer to the original complaint (“Complaint”) with leave to amend, Plaintiff filed the operative First Amended Complaint (the “FAC”) on February 26, 2024, asserting the following causes of action: (1) negligent misrepresentation; and (2) promissory estoppel; or in the alternative, (3) recovery of benefits under 29 U.S.C. section 1132, subdivision (a)(1)(B).

             

In relevant part, the FAC alleges the following: Prior to providing services to a patient, Plaintiff sought and obtained promises and representations from Defendant’s agent, Aetna Life Insurance Co. (“Aetna”), on behalf of Defendant.  Plaintiff contacted Defendant via Aetna to obtain a promise as to how Defendant presently pays for claims.  Plaintiff asked Defendant to promise and represent Defendant’s payment rate for medical services.  (FAC, ¶¶ 27-33.)  Aetna, on behalf of Defendant, stated and represented that the payment rate was usual, customary and reasonable (“UCR”).  (FAC, ¶ 31.)  Plaintiff also asked Aetna whether Defendant pays using a Medicare Fee Schedule.  (FAC, ¶ 32.)  Aetna, on behalf of Defendant, promised and represented that it does not.  (FAC ¶ 33.)  Based on these promises and representations of fact and in reliance on their veracity, Plaintiff provided medical services to the patient.  (FAC, ¶ 42.)  Neither Aetna nor Defendant, however, paid Plaintiff for the services at the UCR rate.  (FAC, ¶¶ 57-58.)  Instead, Aetna, on behalf of Defendant, paid at a Medicare rate.  (FAC, ¶¶ 39, 57.)  Based on these allegations, Plaintiff asserts the foregoing causes of action.

 

On May 31, 2024, Defendant filed the instant demurrer (“Demurrer”) as to the first and second causes of action for negligent misrepresentation and promissory estoppel, respectively.  The Demurrer is based on the grounds that the first and second causes of action are preempted by Section 514 of the Employee Retirement Income Security Act of 1974, as amended (“ERISA”), and that they fail to state a cause of action. 

 

Plaintiff filed an opposition to the Demurrer on July 31, 2024, and Defendant filed a reply on August 12, 2024.

 

JUDICIAL NOTICE

Plaintiff requests judicial notice of the August 8, 2024 Order issued by Judge Maurice A. Leiter of the Superior Court of California, County of Los Angeles, in Beach District Surgery Center v. United Airlines Inc. et al., Case No. 23STCV19725, in which Judge Leiter overruled Defendant’s demurrer on the same grounds as the present Demurrer.

 

Plaintiff’s request is DENIED.  While a court may take judicial notice of the official acts or records of any court in this state and any state of the United States under Evidence Code, § 452(d), findings in a prior judicial opinion are not a proper subject of judicial notice unless it has a res judicata or collateral estoppel effect in a subsequent action.  (Kilroy v. State of California (2004) 119 Cal.App.4th 140, 148.)  Here, there is no indication that the findings in Judge Leiter’s matter have res judicata or collateral estoppel effect in this case.   

 

MEET AND CONFER

            The meet and confer requirement has been met.

             

DISCUSSION

Legal Standard

A demurrer tests the sufficiency of a complaint as a matter of law.  (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)  The court accepts as true all material factual allegations and affords them a liberal construction, but it does not consider conclusions of fact or law, opinions, speculation, or allegations contrary to law or judicially noticed facts.  (Shea Homes Limited Partnership v. County of Alameda (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1246, 1254.)  With respect to a demurrer, the complaint must be construed liberally by drawing reasonable inferences from the facts pleaded.  (Rodas v. Spiegel (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 513, 517.)  

 

ERISA Preemption

Section 514 of ERISA provides, in relevant part, that ERISA supersedes any and all State laws insofar as they ... relate to any employee benefit plan.  (Aton Center, Inc. v. United Healthcare Ins. Co. (2023) 93 Cal.App.5th 1214, 1221 n. 2.)  A law relates to an employee benefit plan, in the normal sense of the phrase, if it has a connection with or reference to such a plan.  (Morris B. Silver M.D., Inc. v. International Longshore & Warehouse etc. (2016) 2 Cal.App.5th 793, 800 (“Morris”).)  With respect to preemption of state law claims, the Supreme Court has held common law causes of action based on alleged improper processing of a claim for benefits under an employee benefit plan, undoubtedly meet the criteria for pre-emption under section 514(a).  (Id. at 801.)

 

            Here, the Court, in its February 8, 2024 Minute Order, had previously sustained, with leave to amend, Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action in the Complaint, finding that “the first two causes of action alleging state law claims are preempted by ERISA because they arise out of and relate to the existence of an ERISA plan.”  The court's finding was based on the alleged discussion between Plaintiff and a representative of the patient's ERISA plan, acting on behalf of Defendant. The discussion revolved around alleged misrepresentations concerning the payment terms of the patient's ERISA plan, which formed the basis of Plaintiff's claims for negligent misrepresentation and promissory estoppel.  Thus, the Court concluded that Plaintiff does not allege circumstances that would extricate Plaintiff’s claims from ERISA preemption.   

 

The Court also noted in its February 8, 2024 Minute Order that the facts alleged in the Complaint differed from those of Morris.  In Morris, the plaintiff's pleading alleged that an ERISA plan had broken a promise to pay a specified amount.  In the Complaint subject of the demurrer, however, the representative's alleged promise regarding payment for services was a general statement of policy, rather than a specific amount related to specific services to be provided by Plaintiff. 

 

Plaintiff then filed the FAC, which is the subject of the current Demurrer.  The Court notes, however, that there is no substantive difference between the facts, as alleged in the FAC, and those in the Complaint.  Plaintiff merely added the qualifying phrase “[i]n connection with Provider Claims only” to certain portions of the FAC.  (FAC, ¶¶ 28-33.)  The insertion of this verbiage does not change the fact that the negligent misrepresentation and promissory estoppel claims are fundamentally grounded on the ERISA plan.  The Court thus reaches the same conclusion that Plaintiff’s negligent misrepresentation and promissory estoppel claims are inextricably intertwined with ERISA, and are thus preempted by it. 

 

A demurrer will be sustained without leave to amend if there exists no reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.  (Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)  The burden of proving such a reasonable possibility is squarely on the plaintiff.  (Cooper v. Leslie Salt Co. (1969) 70 Cal.2d 627, 636.)  Here, Plaintiff does not carry its burden of proving a reasonable possibility that the defect can be cured by amendment.

 

RULING

            Based on the foregoing, the Demurrer as to Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action in the FAC is SUSTAINED without leave to amend.
