DEPARTMENT 71 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

All parties are urged to meet and confer with all parties
concerning this tentative ruling to see if they can reach an agreed-upon
resolution of their matter.  If you are able to reach an
agreement, please notify the courtroom staff in advance of the hearing if
you wish to submit on the tentative ruling rather than argue the motion by
notifying the court by e-mailing the court at: SMCDept71@LACourt.org.  Include
the word "SUBMITS" in all caps and the Case Number in the Subject
line.  In the body of the email, please provide the date and time of the
hearing, your name, your contact information, the party you represent, and
whether that party is a plaintiff, defendant, cross-complainant,
cross-defendant, claimant, intervenor, or non-party, etc.



           
Please be advised that if you submit on the tentative and elect not to appear
at the hearing, the opposing party may still appear at the hearing and argue
the matter, and the court could change its tentative based upon the
argument.  Unless you receive a submission from all other parties
in the matter, you should assume that others might appear at the hearing to
argue.  If you submit, but still intend to appear, include the words
"SUBMITS, BUT WILL APPEAR" in the Subject line.    
If you elect to argue your matter, you are urged to do so remotely, via
Court-Connect.



If
the moving party fails to appear and/or submit to the Court’s tentative ruling,
the Court will take the  matter off calendar.

                       
  

            Note
that once the Court has issued a tentative, the Court has the inherent
authority not to allow the withdrawal of a motion and to adopt the tentative
ruling as the order of the court. 
 

 

            If you
submitted a courtesy copy of your papers containing media (such as a DVD or
thumb drive), unless you request the return of the media in your papers, the
court will destroy it following the hearing of your matter.  


Case Number: 21STCV02175    Hearing Date: January 29, 2024    Dept: 71

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 71

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

MICHAEL BARNETT, 

 

         vs.

 

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES.

 Case No.:  21STCV02175

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 29, 2024

 

Defendant County of Los Angeles’ motion for reconsideration is denied. 

 

Defendant County of Los Angeles (“County”) (“Defendant”) moves for this Court to sua sponte reconsider its ruling denying Defendant’s Motion for Summary Adjudication of the 7th cause of action for Whistleblower Retaliation pursuant to Labor Code §1102.5.  (Motion, pg. 1; Le Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094.)

 

Discussion

C.C.P. §437c(f)(2) and §1008 prohibit a party from making renewed motions, unless those motions are based on new facts or law. C.C.P. §§437c(f)(2), 1008(b).)  Here, Defendant does not add new facts, nor does it argue that there is new law.  Defendant instead argues that per Francois v. Goel (2005) 35 Cal.4th 1094, it can bring a motion for reconsideration without following the requirements of the C.C.P. 

However, the Court in Francois specifically acknowledges that a party may not bring a motion for reconsideration without meeting the requirements of §437c and §1008:

We conclude that sections 437c, subdivision (f)(2), and 1008 prohibit a party from making renewed motions not based on new facts or law, but do not limit a court's ability to reconsider its previous interim orders on its own motion, as long as it gives the parties notice that it may do so and a reasonable opportunity to litigate the question.

 

(Francois, 35 Cal.4th at pgs. 1096-1097.)

Defendant’s request is impermissible.  Defendant moves the court for reconsideration without meeting the statutory requirements for doing so. Accordingly, this motion is denied.

 

Conclusion

Defendant’s motion for reconsideration is denied.

JONATHAN POWERS, et al.,

 

         vs.

 

EQUITY WAVE LENDING, INC., A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, et al.

 Case No.:  22STCV30299

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 29, 2023

 

Moving Defendants Next Day Appraisals, LLC’s and Brett Varon’s motion for judgment on the pleadings for the second amended complaint of Plaintiffs Jonathan S. Powers, individually and as attorney-in-fact for Phyllis A. Powers and Jonathan S. Powers Roth IRA, and Henry Kreuter is granted as to the 2nd and 4th causes of action with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Defendants Next Day Appraisals, LLC (“NDA LLC”) and Brett Varon (“Varon”) (collectively, “Moving Defendants”) demur to the to the second amended complaint (“SAC”) of Plaintiffs Jonathan S. Powers (“Jon”), individually and as attorney-in-fact for Phyllis A. Powers (“Phyllis”) and Jonathan S. Powers Roth IRA, and Henry Kreuter (“Kreuter”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) on the basis Plaintiffs’ 2nd and 4th causes of action against them fail to state causes of action against them due to the holding in Tindell v. Murphy, which concluded that an appraiser owes no duty to third parties such as the Plaintiffs.  (Notice of Demurrer, pg. 2; C.C.P. §430.10(e).)

 

Request for Judicial Notice

Moving Defendants’ 10/11/23 request for judicial notice of the Varon Appraisal dated March 21, 2019 (with valuation as of January 11, 2019) is granted.  (D-RJN, Exh. A.)

 

Procedural Background

          Plaintiffs filed their complaint on September 15, 2022.  Plaintiffs filed their first amended complaint (“FAC”) on October 6, 2022.  On August 18, 2023, this Court sustained demurrers to the FAC.  On September 8, 2023, Plaintiffs filed the operative SAC against Non-moving Defendants Hightechlending, Inc. (“Hightechlending”), Equity Wave Lending, Inc. (“EWL”), Jack J. Suddarth (“Suddarth”), Edith A. Moore (“Edith”), Gregory A. Moore (“Greg”), Brian Hedger (“Hedger”), AAMC Orange County, Inc. (“AAMC”), Sandra K. Gramlich (“Gramlich”), and Moving Defendants, alleging five causes of action: (1) breach of fiduciary duty [against EWL and Suddarth]; (2) negligence [against all Defendants]; (3) constructive fraud [against EWL and Suddarth]; (4) misrepresentation [against all Defendants]; and (5) fraudulent misrepresentation and concealment [against Hedger]. 

Moving Defendants filed their Answer to Plaintiff’s initial complaint on February 16, 2023.  Moving Defendants filed their demurrer on October 11, 2023.  Plaintiffs filed their opposition on January 16, 2024.  Moving Defendants filed their reply on January 18, 2024.

Plaintiffs object to Moving Defendants’ demurrer on the basis that Moving Defendants waived their right to demur by filing their Answer in February 2023.  (C.C.P. §§430.40(a), 435(b), 472(a); Pierce v. Minturn (1851) 1 Cal. 470; McDougall v. Eaton (1912) 20 Cal.App. 164.)

C.C.P. §438 provides that a motion for judgment on the pleadings may be filed if the moving party is a defendant, and the defendant has already filed his or her answer to the complaint and the time for the defendant to demur to the complaint has expired.  (C.C.P. §438(f)(2).)  Such a motion is the functional equivalent of a general demurrer made after the time for demurrer has expired.  Accordingly, the Court will construe Moving Defendants’ demurrer as a motion for judgment on the pleadings.

 

Summary of Allegations

Plaintiffs allege in March 2018, Jon and Phyllis were presented with a secured loan investment opportunity and loan servicing arrangement by their broker Suddarth and EWL.  (SAC ¶17.)  Plaintiffs allege Suddarth and EWL were broker agents for their principals, Plaintiffs.  (SAC ¶17.)  Plaintiffs allege the real property in this dispute are two vacant lots located at 3420 Dorothy Road, City of Calabasas, County of Los Angeles, California (“Calabasas Property”) with Assessor’s Parcel Numbers 2080-015-033 and 2080-015-034 and a legal description of: Lots 61 and 62 of Tract. No. 7094, as per map recorded in book 92, pages 64 and 65 of maps, in the office of the county recorder of Los Angeles County.  (SAC ¶3.) 

Plaintiffs allege the investment opportunity presented by Suddarth and EWL to Jon and Phyllis provided that Jon and Phyllis would provide money for a secured loan to Hedger, and act as servicer of the loan secured by the Calabasas Property.  (SAC ¶19.)  Plaintiffs allege in reliance on the representations made by Suddarth, EWL, Greg, Edith, Hedger, and Hightechlending, Jon and Phyllis invested $168,000 for the lending arrangement.  (SAC ¶19.)  Plaintiffs allege Greg, Edith, and Hightechlending, as agents for Hedger, falsely misrepresented Hedger’s income and value of the real property security to Plaintiffs, by and through its agents.  (SAC ¶19.)   

          Plaintiffs allege EWL hired two licensed California real estate appraisers, Gramlich and AAMC in 2017 and Varon and NDA LLC in 2019, who failed to carefully and diligently view and investigate the Calabasas Property and provide accurate reports indicating that Calabasas Property would utilize an above ground septic system and was not connected to a sewer system.  (SAC ¶24.)  Plaintiffs allege this error by Varon, Gramlich, NDA LLC, and AAMC materially impacted the appraised value of the Calabasas Property causing an erroneous valuation of $420,000 when in fact the appraised value should have been $100,000 market value of the lots that provided security for the money loaned by Plaintiffs.  (SAC ¶24.)  Plaintiffs allege but for the failure of Suddarth, EWL, Varon, Gramlich, Nextday, AAMC, Greg, Edith, and Hightechlending to utilize due care and diligence regarding the valuation of the Calabasas Property and proper vetting and investigation of Hedger, an accurate and non-deceptive investment opportunity would have been presented to Plaintiffs.  (SAC ¶24.)  Plaintiffs allege but for Gramlich’s and Varon’s failure to perform a careful and diligent investigation and appraisal of the Calabasas Property, Plaintiffs would have been aware of the actual market value of the Calabasas Property.  (SAC ¶24.)  Plaintiffs allege they reasonably relied on the inaccurate information provided by Suddarth, EWL, Gramlich, AAMC, NDA LLC, and Varon regarding the valuation of the Calabasas Property.  (SAC ¶24.)

 

Summary of Demurrer

Moving Defendants demur to the 2nd and 4th causes of action in the SAC on the basis the pleading fails to allege facts sufficient to state causes of action against them due to the holding in Tindell v. Murphy, which dictates that an appraiser’s representation of value is made only to the party retaining him or her.  (Demurrer, pg. 3; C.C.P. §430.10(e).)

 

Meet and Confer

Before filing a demurrer pursuant to this chapter, the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a), emphasis added.)  A declaration must be filed with a demurrer regarding the results of the meet and confer process.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3).)

The same meet and confer process required for filing a demurrer must be followed before filing a motion for judgment on the pleadings under C.C.P. §438.   (C.C.P. §439.)

Moving Defendants’ counsel’s declaration states that on September 19, 2023, he emailed Plaintiff’s counsel indicating the deficiencies in the SAC.  (Decl. of Campo ¶2.)  Moving Defendants’ counsel declares the parties were unable to resolve the issues.  (Decl. of Campo ¶2.)  Moving Defendants counsel’s declaration is insufficient under C.C.P. §430.41(a)(3) because he did not meet and confer in person or by telephone, as required by the statute.  However, failure to sufficiently meet and confer is not grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer.  (C.C.P. §430.41(a)(4).)  Accordingly, the Court will consider the instant motion.

 

Legal Standard

“[A] demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.” (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  A demurrer can be used only to challenge defects that appear on the face of the pleading under attack or from matters outside the pleading that are judicially noticeable.  (See Donabedian v. Mercury Insurance Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994 [in ruling on a demurrer, a court may not consider declarations, matters not subject to judicial notice, or documents not accepted for the truth of their contents].)  For purposes of ruling on a demurrer, all facts pleaded in a complaint are assumed to be true, but the reviewing court does not assume the truth of conclusions of law.  (Aubry v. Tri-City Hospital District (1992) 2 Cal.4th 962, 967.)

In considering a motion for judgment on the pleadings, courts consider whether the factual allegations, assumed true, are sufficient to constitute a cause of action.  (Fire Insurance Exchange v. Superior Court (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 446, 452-453.)  Since the standard applicable to this motion is the same as that for a general demurrer (Burnett v. Chimney Sweep (2004) 123 Cal.App.4th 1057, 1064), the Court may grant it if, from the pleadings, together with matters that may be judicially noticed, it appears that a party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.  (C.C.P §438(d); Saltarelli & Steponovich v. Douglas (1995) 40 Cal.App.4th 1, 5.)

 

Failure to State a Claim

Negligence & Misrepresentation (2nd & 4th COAs)

A cause of action for negligence requires the following elements: (1) defendant owed the plaintiff a legal duty; (2) breached the duty; and (3) the breach caused (4) damages.  (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.)  A complaint which lacks allegations of fact to show that a legal duty of care was owed is fatally defective.  (See Jones v. Grewe (1987) 189 Cal.App.3d 950.)  The existence of a legal duty of care is properly challenged by demurrer and is a question of law for the court.  (Hegyes v. Unjian Enterprises, Inc. (1991) 234 Cal.App.3d 1103, 1111.)

The essential elements of a misrepresentation claim are (1) a representation; (2) knowledge of its falsity or disregard for its truth; (3) intent to defraud; (4) justifiable reliance; and (5) resulting damage.  (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974.)

Plaintiffs allege Defendants NDA LLC, Varon, Suddarth, AAMC, and Gramlich failed to exercise due care in the investigation and appraisal of the Calabasas Property and thereafter created and distributed an erroneous appraisal report.  (SAC ¶35.)  Plaintiffs allege it was reasonably foreseeable that the appraisal report would be reviewed and reasonably relied on by persons and/or entities such as Plaintiffs with interest in the valuation of the Calabasas Property.  (SAC ¶35.) 

Plaintiffs allege NDA LLC, Varon, AAMC, and Gramlich owed a duty as licensed real estate appraisers to Plaintiffs as it was known that they would rely on the erroneous appraisal reports.  (SAC ¶36.)  Plaintiffs allege NDA LLC, Varon, AAMC, and Gramlich were required to exercise due care and diligence in performing real property appraisal services as a similarly situated licensed real property appraiser would exercise.  (SAC ¶36.)

Plaintiffs allege NDA LLC, Varon, AAMC, and Gramlich failed to act carefully and with due diligence in the performance of their appraisal services by failing to investigate and appraise the Calabasas Property carefully and diligently and to not distribute erroneous appraisal reports.  (SAC ¶37.)  Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the negligence of NDA LLC, Varon, AAMC, and Gramlich.  (SAC ¶37.)  Plaintiffs allege the negligence of NDA LLC, Varon, AAMC, and Gramlich was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.  (SAC ¶37.)

Plaintiffs allege Defendants represented to Plaintiffs that facts were true concerning the lending transactions and/or appraisal value of the secured real property.  (SAC ¶50.)  Plaintiffs allege the representations of Defendants concerning the lending transactions and/or appraisal value of the secured real property were false.  (SAC ¶51.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants knew or should have known the representations were false.  (SAC ¶52.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants made the representations recklessly and without regard for the truth.  (SAC ¶53.)  Plaintiffs allege Defendants intended that Plaintiffs rely on the representations or should have known that Plaintiffs would have relied on the representations.  (SAC ¶54.)  Plaintiffs allege they reasonably relied on the representations of Defendants.  (SAC ¶55.)  Plaintiffs allege they were harmed by the misrepresentations of Defendants.  (SAC ¶56.)  Plaintiffs allege their reliance on Defendants’ representations was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.  (SAC ¶57.) 

An appraiser designated by a lender to value its borrower’s collateral for financing purposes generally owes its duty of care to the lender, not to the borrower.  (Greenwald & Bank, Cal. Practice Guide: Real Property Transactions, Ch. 6-L, 6:701.)  Indeed, “[w]hether the lender conducts the appraisal in house or hires an outside appraiser, the considerations are the same. The appraisal ordered by the lender is for its own protections and the borrower has [their] own means of ascertaining the desirability of the property.”  (Willemsen v. Mitrosilis (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 622, 629 [noting borrower should know lender’s appraisal is intended for lender’s benefit, not to ensure success of borrower’s investment]; see also Tindell v. Murphy (2018) 22 Cal.App.5th 1239, 1253-1254, [stating lender’s appraiser had no duty of care to borrowers for inaccurate property description as appraisal was intended to support lender’s evaluation of collateral, not borrowers’ decision whether to purchase property].)

Here, Plaintiffs allege it was reasonably foreseeable that Moving Defendants’ appraisal report would be reviewed and reasonably relied on by persons and entities such as Plaintiffs with interest in the valuation of the Calabasas Property.  (SAC ¶35.)  Moving Defendants’ appraisal report was provided for Lender/Client Atlantic Union, not for Defendant Hedger, identified as the Borrower, and most importantly, not for Plaintiffs.  (D-RJN, Exh. A.)  Here, as in Willemsen, there is no indication that Moving Defendants were aware that Plaintiffs hoped to use the appraisal report at issue as an investigational tool upon which to base their decision to approve or reject the Calabasas Property when it was prepared for Atlantic Union, whose relationship to EWL is not disclosed in the SAC.  (Willemsen, 230 Cal.App.4th at pg. 631.)  Unlike in Soderberg v. McKinney (1996) 44 Cal.App.4th 1760, where an appraiser knew that a particular group or class of persons to which plaintiffs belonged—potential investors contacted by a broker—would rely on his report in the course of a specific type of transaction he contemplated—investing in a deed of trust secured by the appraised property, Moving Defendants’ appraisal report was prepared for Atlantic Union, not EWL, and certainly not for Plaintiffs.  (Tindell, 22 Cal.App.5th at pg. 1254.)

Plaintiffs demonstrate reasonable probability of amending their claim to clarify their complaint by indicating who distributed the appraisal report to whom and who had knowledge of the distribution of the appraisal report.  Further, Plaintiffs can clarify the relationship between Atlantic Union and EWL in commissioning the appraisal report of the Calabasas Property.

Accordingly, Moving Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleadings on the 2nd and 4th causes of action is granted with 20 days leave to amend.

 

Conclusion

Moving Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleadings of the 2nd and 4th causes of action in Plaintiffs’ SAC is granted with 20 days leave to amend as to the 2nd cause of action.

1.     Motion to Compel FROG

Having reviewed Plaintiff’s unopposed Motion to Compel Responses to FROGs, the Court rules as follows.

(2 hours to prepare instant motion x $450.00) + $60.00 filing fee = $960.00

The sanctions are payable by Defendant within 20 days of this order.
