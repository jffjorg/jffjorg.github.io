DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 21STCV44398    Hearing Date: October 4, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 4, 2024                     TRIAL DATE: April 22, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         3BTech Inc. v. Great Northern Ins. Co.

 

CASE NO.:                 21STCV44398            

 

MOTION TO DISQUALIFY COUNSEL

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Great Northern Insurance Co.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiff 3BTech, Inc.

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         12/06/21: Complaint filed.

·         02/08/23: Case deemed related to 22STCV39646 3BTech Inc. v. Liberty Mutual Ins. Co., et al. and to 23PSCV00016 3BTech Inc. v. Pro-Com Products, Inc.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

This is a breach of contract action. In one of several lawsuits arising from the same incident, Plaintiff seeks coverage for the loss of inventory destroyed by a fire while in the care of the insured non-party Pro-Com Products, Inc.

 

Defendant Great Northern Ins. Co moves to disqualify Paul K. Schrieffer and the firm of P.K. Schrieffer LLP as counsel for Plaintiff 3BTech, Inc.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

Defendant’s Motion to Disqualify Counsel is DENIED.

 

DISCUSSION:

           

Defendant Great Northern Ins. Co moves to disqualify Paul K. Schrieffer and the firm of P.K. Schrieffer LLP as counsel for Plaintiff.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 128(a)(5) states that “[e]very court shall have the power...to control in furtherance of justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 128(a)(5).) “‘A trial court's authority to disqualify an attorney derives from the power inherent in every court [t]o control in furtherance of justice, the conduct of its ministerial officers, and of all other persons in any manner connected with a judicial proceeding before it, in every matter pertaining thereto. [Citations.]’ [citation] ‘. . . The paramount concern must be to preserve public trust in the scrupulous administration of justice and the integrity of the bar. The important right to counsel of one's choice must yield to ethical considerations that affect the fundamental principles of our judicial process.’ [citation]” (Kirk v. First American Title Ins. Co. (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 776, 791-792.) 

 

In ruling on a motion to disqualify, the court should weigh: (1) the party's right to counsel of choice; (2) the attorney's interest in representing a client; (3) the financial burden on a client of change of counsel; (4) any tactical abuse underlying a disqualification motion; and (5) the principal that the fair resolution of disputes requires vigorous representation of parties by independent counsel. (Mills Land & Water Co. v. Golden West Refining Co.  (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 116, 126.) Whether an attorney should be disqualified is a matter addressed to the sound discretion of the trial court. (Henriksen v. Great American Savings & Loan (1992) 11 Cal.App.4th 109, 113.) 

 
            Defendant moves to disqualify Plaintiff’s counsel on the basis that counsel’s representation of Plaintiff is directly adverse to Chubb Limited, the parent corporation of Great Northern Insurance Company. Defendant contends that Plaintiff’s counsel has previously and is currently representing Chubb and other subsidiaries in various insurance coverage disputes. The Rules of Professional Conduct categorically prohibit a lawyer from simultaneously representing clients who are directly adverse in the same tribunal (Cal. R. Prof. Conduct 1.7(d)(3)), and only permit representation which is directly adverse to a current or former client outside of that prohibition on the condition of informed written consent. (Rule 1.7; 1.9.) Although Defendant offers a raft of evidence to demonstrate the substantive similarity in the representations, Defendant pointedly does not contend that Plaintiff’s counsel has ever represented Great Northern, only other entities with which Defendant claims affiliation. Moreover, Defendant offers no authority addressing whether parent and subsidiary corporations are considered the same “client” for the purpose of this inquiry, and merely presumes the affirmative in arguing this motion. The same must be said of Defendant’s argument that acting as legal counsel for CFC Underwriting – an insurance syndicate which includes some Chubb subsidiaries among its members – equates to representation of Chubb or Great Northern Insurance Company.  Again, Defendant’s contention is unsupported by any facts establishing a “substantial relationship” between the allegedly conflicting legal engagements nor by any law justifying the link between representation of a Lloyd’s syndicate and representative of a syndicate member or the member’s parent or sister corporation.  The Court is not inclined to override Plaintiff’s right to counsel of its choice based on mere presumption. Defendant has not carried its burden to demonstrate that counsel for Plaintiff should be disqualified.

 

//

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Disqualify Counsel is DENIED.

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 4, 2024                     TRIAL DATE: March 11, 2025

                                                          

CASE:                         G&P Group, Inc, v. City National Bank

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV04378           

 

MOTION TO COMPEL FURTHER RESPONSES TO REQUESTS FOR PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS; REQUEST FOR SANCTIONS

 

MOVING PARTY:               Plaintiff G&P Group, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Defendant City National Bank

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         02/03/22: Complaint filed.

·         01/05/23: First Amended Complaint filed.

·         06/08/23: Cross-Complaint filed.

·         07/07/23: First Amended Cross-Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for breach of contract stemming from a commercial banking agreement. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant placed Plaintiff into a distressed loan program without justification and offered overdrafts to Plaintiff only to abruptly discontinue them, forcing Plaintiff out of busines.

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to requests for production propounded to Defendant.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order. 

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $15,375 against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally. Payment is to be made within 10 days of this order. 

DISCUSSION:

 

Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to requests for production propounded to Defendant.

 

Legal Standard

 

Under Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310, subdivision (a), a court may order a party to serve a further response to a demand for inspection when the court finds that: “(1) A statement of compliance with the demand is incomplete[;] (2) A representation of inability to comply is inadequate, incomplete, or evasive[; or] (3) An objection in the response is without merit or too general.”

 

The burden is on the moving party to “set forth specific facts showing good cause justifying the discovery sought by the demand.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.310, subd. (b)(1).) These facts must also be set forth in a separate statement filed by the moving party. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 3.1345(c).) This burden “is met simply by a fact-specific showing of relevance.” (TBG Ins. Servs. Corp. v. Superior Court (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 443, 448.)

 

Timeliness

 

A motion to compel further responses to requests for production must be served “within 45 days of the service of the verified response, or any supplemental verified response, or on or before any specific later date to which the propounding party and the responding party have agreed in writing.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.310(c).) The 45-day requirement is mandatory and jurisdictional. (Sexton v. Superior Court¿(1997) 58 Cal.App.4th 1403, 1410.)

 

Plaintiff served the requests for production at issue on this motion on February 22, 2023. (Declaration of Damion Robinson ISO Mot. ¶ 2.) Defendant served its initial responses on June 12, 2023. (Id. Exh. 1.) Defendant provided supplemental responses on September 29, 2023, which were verified on October 2. (Id. ¶ 11; Exh. 4.) Defendant made a rolling production of documents through December 5, 2023. (Id. ¶¶ 16-17.) Following an Informal Discovery Conference on May 30, 2024, in which Defendant agreed to permit an attorney-eyes-only review of responsive documents, the parties agreed in writing that the deadline to move to compel as to the materials at issue on this motion would be tolled until two weeks after the parties declared an impasse. (Id. ¶¶ 43-46; Exh. 14.) Although no express declaration of an impasse was ever made, Plaintiff’s counsel cancelled a planned further inspection of documents based on this dispute on June 26, 2024. (See Exh. 18.) This motion was filed and served on July 2, 2024, and thus is timely.

 

//

 

//

Meet and Confer

 

A party making a motion to compel further responses must also include a declaration stating facts showing a “reasonable and good faith attempt” to resolve informally the issues presented by the motion before filing the motion. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2016.040, 2031.310, subd. (b)(2).)

 

The Declaration of Damion Robinson attached to the moving papers describes the extensive efforts the parties undertook to informally resolve this dispute, including multiple meet-and-confer exchanges, an informal discovery conference, and in-person inspection of responsive documents as a solution to confidentiality concerns. (Robinson Decl. ¶¶ 2-53.) Plaintiff has satisfied the statutory meet-and-confer requirements.

 

Good Cause

 

            Plaintiff moves to compel further responses to Requests for Production Nos. 13, 19, and 20. Request No. 13 seeks all documents reflecting the policies, procedures, standard operating procedures and/or guidelines of Defendant’s Special Assets Division (“SAD”) program from 2020 through the present. (Robinson Decl. Exh. 1 No. 13.) Request No. 19 seeks all documents reflecting any policies, procedures, or guidelines relating to overdrafting of business accounts in effect from 2020 through the present. (Id. No. 19.) Request No. 20 seeks all documents relating to any changes to those policies, procedures, or guidelines. (Id. No. 20.) These materials are facially relevant to the core allegations asserted by Plaintiff. (FAC ¶¶ 5-15; 28-79.)

 

Defendant’s Amended Responses

 

            Defendant’s amended responses asserted a series of boilerplate objections before promising to produce certain materials. With respect to Request No. 13, Defendant asserted objections based on the attorney-client privilege, work product doctrine, privacy rights, proprietary or confidential business information, overbreadth, undue burden, irrelevance, not being reasonably calculated to lead to admissible evidence, and duplicative of other requests, before agreeing to produce “policies memorialized in CNB’s Credit Manual and SAD Manual” from the period up to February 2, 2022 “that are relevant and responsive to this request.” (Robinson Decl. Exh. 4 No. 13.) Defendant’s amended response to Request No. 19 is substantively identical. (Id. No. 19.) Defendant’s amended response to Request No. 20 is similar, except that Defendant promised to produce “policies reflecting changes to overdrafting.” (Id. No. 20.) The responses do not identify with particularity the documents which will be produced. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.280(a).) The responses also do not identify with particularity what documents are subject to the objections asserted. (See Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.240.)

 

            Plaintiff contends that, despite promising at an Informal Discovery Conference to make all responsive documents available for inspection, Defendant would not confirm that it was producing the full set of responsive SAD Manuals, would only produce one version of the Credit Manual from “late 2021,” and would not make any revisions to the Credit Manual available. (Robinson Decl. ¶ 52, Exh. 18.) Plaintiff therefore argues that Defendant did not produce all responsive documents and, further, that Defendant’s objections are too general because they do not identify with particularity the materials to which they apply.

 

            In opposition, Defendant first argues that Plaintiff’s motion was premature because, in the intervening period, the parties conducted a further viewing of additional versions of the SAD and Credit Manual on August 14, 2024, and produced versions of 42 policies referenced in the Manuals on August 16. (Declaration of Sam Bodle ISO Opp. ¶¶ 19-23.) Plaintiff concedes that further materials were made available and adds that Defendant produced additional documents on September 20, 2024, that are facially responsive. (Supplemental Declaration of Damion Robinson ISO Reply ¶ 16, Exh. 10.) Plaintiff contends, nonetheless, that the supplemental production is inadequate because Defendant did not produce general lending and underwriting guidelines, risk management policies, or forms, templates, and other similar documents which were referenced or hyperlinked in the documents which were produced. (Id. ¶¶ 6-7.)

 

            Defendant characterizes these documents as “additional requests” which are beyond the scope of the requests for production. The Court disagrees. The requests sought documents (1) reflecting policies, procedures, standard operating procedures, and guidelines relating to the SAD program; (2) reflecting policies, procedures, standard operating procedures, and guidelines relating to overdrafting; and (3) relating to changes to those materials. The requests are broadly worded but facially relevant to the claims asserted in this action, and Plaintiff is correct that such materials are potentially responsive to the requests as written. Defendant’s assertion that further responses would be unduly burdensome is unsupported by admissible evidence, as counsel for Defendant is not competent to testify as to the burden of production, and that objection is therefore meritless. Defendant’s relevance objection is similarly meritless, as the materials sought are facially relevant to the allegations in the Complaint. Defendant’s remaining objections are not even addressed in the opposition, and therefore must be deemed waived.

 

            The Court therefore finds that Plaintiff is entitled to an order compelling further, code-compliant responses without objections.

 

Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff also requests sanctions against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally, in the amount of $17,500.

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 authorizes the Court to impose monetary sanctions on any attorney engaging in the misuse of the discovery process by requiring that attorney to pay the reasonable expenses incurred by anyone as a result of that conduct. Code of Civil Procedure section 2031.310(h) requires the Court to impose sanctions against any party who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel further response, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.

 

            Here, Plaintiff requests sanctions in the amount of $17,500 against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally, which would reflect 23 hours and 20 minutes of attorney time at Attorney Robinson’s claimed rate of $750 per hour. (Robinson Decl. ¶ 59.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that he has incurred 17.5 hours of attorney time in preparation of this motion, plus 7 anticipated hours for the reply, in addition to 20 hours of meeting and conferring that were previously incurred but not sought to be reimbursed. (Id. ¶ 61.) At the outset, the Court does not have a reply declaration quantifying the actual number of hours spent on preparing the reply papers but finds that 3 hours is a reasonable amount of time for preparing the 10-page reply brief and supplemental evidence.  Thus, if justified, the Court is inclined to award attorney’s fees for a total of 20.5 hours reasonably incurred.

 

            Defendant opposes an award of sanctions because it contends that its behavior has been reasonable and accommodating, while Plaintiff’s demands have continually escalated beyond reason. The Court is not persuaded as it relates to these document requests. In response to these requests for production of facially relevant documents, Defendant asserted boilerplate objections, failed to specify responsive documents, failed to identify documents which would not be produced, and, despite agreeing to make all responsive materials available for inspection, failed to do so. Had Defendant carried out the production made on August 16, 2024 as its original production, Defendant’s claim that it acted with substantial justification might have some force, even though that production was incomplete. Instead, Defendant stalled for over eighteen months. Even if Plaintiff has engaged in similar tactics with respect to other discovery procedures, as Defendant claims, discovery is not a tit-for-tat exercise where one party’s misconduct justifies another’s. The Court therefore awards sanctions in the amount of $15,375, reflecting the 20.5 hours of attorney time reasonably incurred on this motion.  

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion to Compel Further Responses to Requests for Production is GRANTED. Defendant is ordered to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days of this order. 

 

            Plaintiff’s request for sanctions is GRANTED in the amount of $15,375 against Defendant and its counsel, jointly and severally. Payment is to be made within 10 days of this order.

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     October 4, 2024                     TRIAL DATE: October 21, 2024

           

CASE:                         Ken Houang, et al. v. Rochelle H. Sterling, et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 22STCV32478           

 

MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT OR SUMMARY ADJUDICATION

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendants Rochelle H. Sterling, individually and as trustee of the Sterling Family Trust, and Beverly Hills Properties, LLC

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Ken Houang and Shelly Houang, individually and as Guardian ad Litem for Liam Kenneth Houang

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a habitability action that was filed on October 4, 2022. Plaintiffs, who are and were tenants at a residential apartment complex, allege that Defendants permitted the premises to develop extensive uninhabitable conditions and allowed those conditions to persist in violation of statutes and municipal ordinances. Plaintiffs seek monetary and injunctive relief.

 

Defendants move for summary judgment, or, in the alternative, summary adjudication of all causes of action.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

           

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

 

//

 

//

 

//

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

            Defendants move for summary judgment. As Defendants have not demonstrated their entitlement to summary adjudication of all causes of action, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

            Defendants move for summary adjudication of each cause of action.

 

Legal Standard

 

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party can show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and, if not, to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) Code of Civil Procedure Section 437c(c) “requires the trial judge to grant summary judgment if all the evidence submitted, and ‘all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence’ and uncontradicted by other inferences or evidence, show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Adler v. Manor Healthcare Corp. (1992) 7 Cal.App.4th 1110, 1119.)  “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

As to each claim as framed by the complaint, the defendant moving for summary judgment must satisfy the initial burden of proof by presenting facts to negate an essential element, or to establish a defense. (Code Civ Proc. § 437c(p)(2); Scalf v. D. B. Log Homes, Inc. (2005) 128 Cal.App.4th 1510, 1520.) Courts “liberally construe the evidence in support of the party opposing summary judgment and resolve doubts concerning the evidence in favor of that party.” (Dore v. Arnold Worldwide, Inc. (2006) 39 Cal.4th 384, 389.) The lack of opposition by a plaintiff is not grounds to grant a motion for summary judgment if a defendant cannot meet their initial burden of proof. (See Thatcher v. Lucy Stores, Inc. (2000) 79 Cal.App.4th 1081, 1087.)

 

            Once the defendant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or a defense thereto. To establish a triable issue of material fact, the party opposing the motion must produce substantial responsive evidence. (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151, 166.)

 

//

Plaintiffs’ Evidentiary Objections

 

            Plaintiffs object to portions of the Declaration of Paul H. Burleigh in support of the motion. The Court rules on these objections as follows:

 

            Objection No. 1: SUSTAINED. Counsel for Defendants does not have personal knowledge of the authenticity of these materials. Consequently, Defendants’ Exhibit E lacks authentication and shall not be considered.

 

            Objection No. 2: SUSTAINED. Counsel for Defendants does not have personal knowledge of the authenticity of these materials. Consequently, Defendants’ Exhibit F lacks authentication and shall not be considered.

 

First Cause of Action: Breach of Implied Warranty of Habitability

 

            Defendants move for summary adjudication of the first cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability.

 

The elements of a cause of action for breach of the implied warranty of habitability “are the existence of a material defective condition affecting the premises' habitability, notice to the landlord of the condition within a reasonable time after the tenant's discovery of the condition, the landlord was given a reasonable time to correct the deficiency, and resulting damages.” (Erlach v. Sierra Asset Servicing, LLC (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 1281, 1297.)

 

The first cause of action specifically alleges that Defendants failed to provide habitable premises because the rental unit developed mold following water infiltration and was infested with cockroaches and other insects. (Complaint ¶ 24.)

 

            Defendants first argue that Plaintiffs were bound by a “Mold Addendum” and a “Pest Addendum” which imposed obligations upon Plaintiffs regarding maintenance of the rental unit, and Plaintiffs’ failure to comply with those obligations defeats the claim for breach of the warranty of habitability. Defendants’ sole authority for this position is the unpublished opinion Grabowiec v. Schopmeyer (Cal. Ct. App. Mar. 17, 2014) No. G047312, 2014 WL 1007833. As counsel for Defendants should know, unpublished authorities may only be cited when relevant for the purposes of the doctrines of law of the case, res judicata, or collateral estoppel, or when relevant to a criminal or disciplinary action because it states reasons for a decision affecting the same defendant or respondent in another such action. (Cal. Rules of Court Rule 8.1115(b).) Barring those exceptions, unpublished authorities “must not be cited or relied on.” (Id. Rule 8.1115(a).) Indeed, the first page of Grabowiec, like all unpublished opinions, expressly recites this rule above the caption of the case. As this is not a criminal matter and Defendants do not offer this opinion as the law of the case nor for res judicata or collateral estoppel purposes, the Court refuses to consider Defendants’ improper citation. Since Defendants offer no valid authority for this position, the Court finds the contentions regarding the Mold and Pest Addenda to be entirely unpersuasive.

            Defendants next argue that Plaintiffs failed to give notice within a reasonable time of the presence of mold in the rental unit, because Plaintiffs were aware of or suspected the existence of mold for four months prior to the date Defendants were notified. Defendants’ contention regarding the suspicion of mold is based entirely on improperly authenticated emails which are not admissible. (See Separate Statement of Undisputed Material Fact No. 26.) Defendants have therefore failed to demonstrate that Plaintiffs failed to give notice within a reasonable time because they have failed to offer admissible evidence supporting that position.

 

Finally, Defendants contend that Plaintiffs did not grant Defendants a reasonable time to remedy the mold infiltration because Plaintiff Shelly Houang cleaned the mold on the same day notice was given and before Defendants’ technician arrived. (SSUMF No. 31; Defendants’ Exh. C p.83:17-20.) Thus, Defendants contend they were not given a reasonable opportunity to remedy the defect. Defendants’ argument is deficient in several respects. First, cleaning mold from a wall surface is on its face not equivalent to remedying the infiltration of mold and taking precautions to prevent its recurrence. Second, Plaintiffs also allege cockroach infestations as part of the deficiencies, which Defendants do not address except in the context of the “Pest Addendum,” which is not persuasive as stated above. Thus, even if Defendants had established that they were not permitted a reasonable opportunity to cure the mold deficiency, that argument does not address all the allegations raised as to this cause of action. The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.)

 

            The Court therefore finds that Defendants have not demonstrated that Plaintiffs cannot prevail on this cause of action.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the First Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

Second Cause of Action: Negligence

 

            Defendants also contend that the second cause of action for negligence is without merit.

 

            To prevail on a claim for negligence, a plaintiff must establish that the defendant (1) owed a duty to the plaintiff; (2) that the defendant breached that duty; (3) that the defendant’s acts or omissions caused injury: (4) that resulted in damage to the plaintiff. (Ortega v. Kmart Corp. (2001) 26 Cal.4th 1200, 1205.)

 

            Defendants first assert that the Mold and Pest Addenda shifted the duty owed to Plaintiffs. That contention is entirely unsupported by legal authority and is therefore disregarded.

 

Defendants next argue that Defendants did not breach any duty for the reasons stated above with regard to mold, and because Plaintiffs did not properly cooperate with Defendants’ efforts to remove pests. These are not the only relevant allegations offered in the Complaint regarding the conditions on the premises, as Plaintiffs also allege, inter alia, water intrusion, leaks, water damage, lack of sanitary plumbing, holes in ceilings and interior walls, broken kitchen cabinets, and other building code and health and safety violations. (Complaint ¶¶ 18, 31.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.) As Defendants do not address the remaining allegations, their contentions as to a few of Plaintiffs’ charges are not sufficient to shift their burden on summary adjudication.

 

            Finally, Defendants contend that Plaintiffs have not shown that their injuries were caused by any of the conditions in the rental unit which have been alleged. Defendants’ arguments rest entirely on deposition testimony from the adult Plaintiffs wherein they admit that they did not have any formal diagnosis of any of their claimed health problems which was caused by any of the conditions alleged to exist in the unit. (See SSUMF No. 34.) This contention is insufficient. “Summary judgment law in this state, however, continues to require a defendant moving for summary judgment to present evidence, and not simply point out through argument, that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 854 [emphasis added].) Admissions that the Plaintiffs did not have formal diagnoses does not demonstrate that such diagnoses could not be reasonably obtained. Defendants have not carried their burden with respect to this argument, and, for that reason, the burden of proof does not shift to Plaintiffs.

 

            As Defendants’ challenges to the second cause of action lack merit, Defendants are not entitled to summary adjudication of this claim.

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the Second Cause of Action is DENIED.

 

Third Cause of Action: Nuisance

 

            Defendants contend that Plaintiffs cannot prevail on the third cause of action for nuisance.

 

            As stated in Civil Code section 3479:

 

Anything which is injurious to health, including, but not limited to, the illegal sale of controlled substances, or is indecent or offensive to the senses, or an obstruction to the free use of property, so as to interfere with the comfortable enjoyment of life or property, or unlawfully obstructs the free passage or use, in the customary manner, of any navigable lake, or river, bay, stream, canal, or basin, or any public park, square, street, or highway, is a nuisance.

 

(Civ. Code § 3479.) A nuisance which affects an entire community or neighborhood or which affects a “considerable number of persons” is a public nuisance. (Civ. Code § 3480.) Any other nuisance is a private nuisance. (Civ. Code § 3481.)

 

            Although the Complaint does not specify whether it alleges a public or a private nuisance, the Complaint does not allege injury to a community, neighborhood, or large number of persons. (See generally Complaint.) Further, while the opposition to this motion does not expressly concede the issue, Plaintiffs’ arguments and authorities focus exclusively on private nuisances. (See Opposition pp. 19-20.) The Court therefore construes the issue as alleged in the Complaint to be one of private nuisance only.

 

            Defendants contend that Plaintiffs cannot prevail on a claim for private nuisance because they consented to the presence of mold and cockroaches by failing to give timely notice of mold and refusing to permit Defendants to conduct pest control treatment after complaints of cockroaches. It is true, as Defendants claim, that Plaintiffs must demonstrate a lack of consent to prevail on a claim for nuisance. (See, e.g., Dept. of Fish & Game v. Sup. Ct. (2011) 197 Cal.App.4th 1323, 1352; Birke v. Oakwood Worldwide (2009) 169 Cal.App.4th 1540, 1548.) However, Defendants’ evidence regarding the failure to give timely notice of mold and refusal to permit pest control treatment are again based on inadmissible evidence. (SSUMF Nos. 17, 26.) Further, the Complaint also alleges injuries arising from water damage, separate and apart from mold infiltration. (Complaint ¶ 43.) To succeed, a motion for summary adjudication must address all allegations in support of the targeted cause of action.  (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-82.) By failing to address all allegations in the Complaint, Defendants have not carried their burden on summary adjudication.

 

            Finally, Defendants argue that Plaintiffs cannot establish negligence, which is a prerequisite to a nuisance claim, for the reasons stated with respect to the second cause of action. As the Court has rejected Defendants’ challenge to that claim, this argument fails.

 

            For the foregoing reasons, Defendants are not entitled to summary adjudication of this claim.

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication of the Third Cause of Action is DENIED.

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.

 

//

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED.

 

            Defendants’ Motion for Summary Adjudication is DENIED.
