DEPARTMENT 107 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV15796    Hearing Date: September 24, 2024    Dept: 107

 

SUPERIOR COURT OF THE STATE OF CALIFORNIA

COUNTY OF LOS ANGELES

NORTHWEST DISTRICT

 

MECHEA NELSON, AN INDIVIDUAL,

        

Plaintiff,

 

   v.

 

FOOD 4 LESS OF CALIFORNIA, INC., A CALIFORNIA CORPORATION, et al.

      

  Defendants.

   Case Number             Department

            22STCV15796     107

 

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

 

DEFENDANT FOOD 4 LESS OF CALIFORNIA, INC. MOTIONS TO COMPEL PLAINTIFF’S RESPONSES TO DEMAND FOR INSPECTION AND PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS, SET ONE, FORM INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE, AND SPECIAL INTERROGATORIES, SET ONE

 

 

 

            Background

On May 12, 2022, Plaintiff Mechea Nelson (“Plaintiff”) filed a PLD-P1-001 complaint against several defendants incLuding Ralphs Grocery Company dba Food 4 Less of California (“Defendant”), The Kroger Co., and DOES 1-10 (collectively, the “Defendants”).

This action arises out of Plaintiff allegedly slipping on water or a similar substance and falling on Defendants’ premises at 851 Sepulveda, Blvd., Torrance CA, 90502 on May 17, 2020. Plaintiff brings forth causes of action for (1) general negligence and (2) premises liability.

On November 17, 2023, Defendant served Plaintiff by electronic service with:

1.     an initial set of written discovery, which included inter alia Form Interrogatories – General (Set One)[1] (the “FROGs”); and

2.     an initial set of written discovery, which included inter alia Special Interrogatories – (Set One) [2] (the “SROGs”);

3.     an initial set of written discovery, which included inter alia Requests for Production of Documents (Set One)[3] (the “RPDs”). Having not

Plaintiff’s deadline to respond to the FROGs, SROGs, and RPDs (collectively, “Discovery Requests”) was December 19, 2023. (Declaration of Jennifer W. Naples, (“Naples Decl.”) ¶ 3.) Plaintiff’s response deadline was extended on six occasions, setting the new deadline to April 22, 2024. (Naples Decl., ¶¶ 4-10, Exhibits 2-8.) By the filing of the instant motions, June 21, 2024, Plaintiff had not yet served any response to the Discovery Requests. (Naples Decl., ¶ 11.) Defense counsel outlines Plaintiff’s several requests to extend the deadline which Defendant granted and declares that Defendant made meet and confer efforts to obtain the responses, though the efforts were not required. (FROG-Motion, pp. 5, 7.)

On June 21, 2024, Defendant filed the three pending motions seeking to compel Plaintiff’s responses to the Form Interrogatories, Special Interrogatories, and Production Requests sought from Plaintiff.  In connection with each of the motions, Plaintiff also requests an award of monetary sanctions of $1,061.65. (See FROG-Naples Decl., ¶ 12, SROG-Naples Decl., ¶ 12, and RPD-Naples Decl., ¶ 12.) 

On July 25, 2024, the case was reassigned from Judge Karen Moskowitz to Judge Eric Harmon in Department 107 at Van Nuys Courthouse. (7-25-24 Notice of Case Reassignment.)

On August 1, 2023, Defendant filed notice of an informal discovery conference to resolve the Discovery Requests on August 23, 2024. (8-1-24 Notice of Informal Discovery Conference.)

On August 14, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition to the three pending motions and request for sanctions.

On August 23, 2024, the Court corrected the Informal Discovery Conference order nun pro tunc. (8-23-24 Nun Pro Tunc Order.)

 

On August 20, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

On August 26, 2024, the consolidated motion hearing as to these Discovery Requests was scheduled for September 4, 2024, and continued, on the Court’s own motion, to September 24, 2024. (9-4-24 Minute Order.)

 

Legal Standard

Interrogatories

            If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, the propounding party may move for an order compelling responses and for a monetary sanction.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(b).)  The statute contains no time limit for a motion to compel where no responses have been served.  All that need be shown in the moving papers is that a set of interrogatories was properly served on the opposing party, that the time to respond has expired, and that no response of any kind has been served.  (See Leach v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal.App.3d 902, 905-906.)

Production of Documents

            Where there has been no timely response to a demand for the production of documents, the demanding party may seek an order compelling a response.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(b).)  Failure to timely respond waives all objections, including privilege and work product.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.300(a).)  Thus, unless the party to whom the demand was directed obtains relief from waiver, he or she cannot raise objections to the documents demanded. There is no deadline for a motion to compel responses. Likewise, for failure to respond, the moving party need not attempt to resolve the matter outside court before filing the motion.

Mandatory Sanctions – Interrogatories & Request for Production of Documents.

            Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Id.)

            Discussion

A.    Production Request and Interrogatories

            Here, Defendant moves the Court to issue orders compelling the Defendant to produce responses to Plaintiff’s Production Request and Interrogatories

            As discussed above, Plaintiff’s deadline to respond to the Production Request and Form and Special Interrogatories ultimately expired on April 22, 2024, and as of the filing of the instant motions on June 20, 2024, Plaintiff had not served any responses. (Naples Decl. ¶ 10.)

            In opposition to the instant motions, counsel for Plaintiff argues that no abuse of discovery occurred as Plaintiff was unresponsive to her attorneys’ phone calls, emails and letters. (Opposition, pp. 1-2; Declaration of Elina Shakhbazyan (“Shakhbazyan Decl.”) ¶¶ 3-6.) Plaintiff changed her telephone number and inadvertently failed to notify her own counsel. (Opposition, p. 2; Shakhbazyan Decl. ¶ 7.) Counsel for Plaintiff states that Defendant’s counsel was informed of their challenged in locating Plaintiff, and that several emails captured their efforts to find Plaintiff and finalize her discovery responses, indicating that if their efforts were not successful, they intended to file a motion to be relieved as counsel. (Opposition, p. 2:16-19.) Plaintiff’s counsel claims that despite their efforts to contact Plaintiff, Defendant proceeded with filing the instant motions. (Shakhbazyan Decl. ¶ 5.)

            According to Defendant’s reply, Plaintiff and her counsel had not communicated with Defendant since a March 29, 2024, email in which Plaintiff’s counsel explained that their office was preparing to be relieved as counsel. (Reply, p. 3.) Defendant denies Plaintiff’s counsel’s claim that they communicated about their difficulties contacting Plaintiff, thus necessitating the motion to compel. (Id.) Defendant observes the seven response deadline extensions that they granted Plaintiff’s counsel in obtaining discovery and argues for the imposition of sanctions. (Id.)

            Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s motions to compel Plaintiff to produce responses to Defendant’s Request for Production of Documents and Form and Special Interrogatories. (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2025.280(a), 2030.290(b).)  

 

B.     The Requests for Monetary Sanctions are Granted in a Reduced Amount

            Sanctions are mandatory in connection with motions to compel responses to interrogatories and requests for production of documents against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel.  (Code Civ. Proc., §§ 2030.290(c), 2031.300(c).) However, sanctions are not mandatory if the court “finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Id.)

            Here, Defendant moves the Court to impose monetary sanctions against Plaintiff or Plaintiff’s in the total amount of $1,061.65 based on the hourly rate of $200.00 being applied to: (1) two hours to prepare the motion, (2) one hour anticipated to review any opposition papers and draft a reply, (3) two hours to prepare and attend the motion hearing, and (3) the $61.65 filing fee.  (See FROG-Naples Decl., ¶ 12, SROG-Naples Decl., ¶ 12, and RPD-Naples Decl., ¶ 12.)

            In opposition, Plaintiff argues (1) that sanctions are not appropriate because they are not mandatory and (2) neither Plaintiff’s counsel nor Plaintiff have abused the discovery process. (Opposition, pp. 2-3.) Additionally, Plaintiff’s counsel declares that “if the Court decides to impose sanctions, counsel requests that they be reduced and imposed solely on the Plaintiff.” (Shakhbazyan Decl. ¶ 11.)

In reply, Defendant argues that counsel for Plaintiff has not produced a legally permissible justification for failing to respond to the several discovery deadlines. (Reply, p. 4.)

            To escape sanctions, the burden is on the attorney to prove that the attorney did not advise the client to disobey the discovery order: “An attorney's advice to a client to answer or not answer an interrogatory is a fact peculiarly within the knowledge of attorney and client. To place the burden of proof on the moving party … would effectually … frustrate the statutory purpose. ( [8:2145] Sanctions for Failure to Obey Court Order:, Cal. Prac. Guide Civ. Pro. Before Trial Ch. 8M-5.) Plaintiff’s counsel has sufficiently stated their efforts in contacting Plaintiff on several occasions ahead of the erroneously scheduled August 20, 2024 informal discovery conference. (Opposition, p. 2; Exhibit 1.)

            Defendant is entitled to monetary sanctions for reasonable attorneys’ fees incurred in connection with the motions.  Despite seven mutual extensions to respond, Plaintiff failed to serve timely responses, necessitating the filing of the motions, and Plaintiff has still not served responses. Moreover, to the extent Defendant requests time in connection with appearing at a hearing for each of the three motions, such a request is duplicative as all three motions are scheduled for the same day.  Utilizing a lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the pending motions against Plaintiff is $584.95 reflecting 2 hours incurred at $200 per hour (0.75 hour for drafting one motion and 1 hour for drafting the remaining two motions at 0.5 hour each, and 0.25 hour for attending the hearing on the three motions) plus $184.95 in filing fees. 

 

            Accordingly, the Court grants Defendant’s requests for monetary sanctions against Defendant in the reduced total amount of $584.95. 

Conclusion

Therefore, the Court GRANTS the Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Defendant to produce responses for Plaintiff’s Requests for Production of Documents (Set One), Form Interrogatories (Set One) and Special Interrogatories (Set One).

 

In addition, the Court GRANTS the Defendant’s Requests for Monetary Sanctions in the reduced total amount of $584.95 against Plaintiff Mechea Nelson.

 

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this ruling.

COURT’S [TENTATIVE] ORDER RE:

Deem RFAs Admitted; Compel Responses 

 

 

 

 

 

 

I.                BACKGROUND 

 

This is a landlord-tenant dispute.  On July 21, 2023, Plaintiff Alejandra Moreno filed a Complaint against Defendants Reseda Village Green, LLC, Alexander Alvy, FPI Management, Inc., Tegist Demissie, Ricardo Doe, Carlos Doe, and Does 1 through 40, alleging five different causes of action. 

 

On April 16, 2024, Defendants Reseda Village Green, LLC and Alvy (hereinafter, “Defendants”) served RFAs, Set One and Special Interrogatories, Set One on Plaintiff via email. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA ¶ 7; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 7; Ex. D.) Plaintiff’s responses were initially due on May 20, 2024, but Plaintiff requested three extensions, which Defendants granted. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA at ¶¶ 7-10; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶¶ 7-10.) Ultimately, Plaintiff’s responses were due on June 2, 2024, but Plaintiff failed to respond to Defendants’ RFAs or Special Interrogatories, Set One. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA at ¶¶ 10-11; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶¶ 10-11.)  Over the next ten days, Plaintiff sent multiple emails to Defendants stating she would provide the responses but failed to do so. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA at ¶ 12; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 12.)  

 

On June 14, 2024, Defendants emailed a meet-and-confer letter to Plaintiff, requesting Plaintiff serve Defendants with the responses by June 21, 2024. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA at ¶ 13; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 13) A week later, Plaintiff emailed Defendant stating, “discovery at this time is a waste of time as I am waiting on your response to my amended complaint and I am sure the matter will be consolidated under such case.”  (Lakeotes Declaration RFA at ¶ 14; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 14; Ex. J.) Defendants’ counsel responded to Plaintiff, stating Defendants’ counsel only represents Defendants in this matter. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA at ¶ 14; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 14) 

 

On June 26, 2024, Defendants filed the Motion to Deem RFAs, Set One, Admitted and requested monetary sanctions against Plaintiff. (Defendants’ Motion to Deem RFAs, Set One, Admitted at pp. 1-2, 4.) On July 2, 2024, Defendants filed the Motion for Order Compelling Responses to Plaintiff to Special Interrogatories, Set One, and requested monetary sanctions against Plaintiff.  The Court notes that in the Court Reservation System (“CRS”) Defendants improperly titled this a Motion to Compel Further Responses; however, based on the substance of the motion and the procedural posture of Plaintiff’s non-response to discovery requests at its filing, it is in fact a Motion to Compel Responses.   

 

On August 21, 2024, Defendants filed a notice of non-opposition to Defendants’ Motion to Deem RFAs, Set One, Admitted. (Notice of Non-Opposition.)  

 

On August 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed two Separate Statements. (Plaintiff Alejandra Moreno’s Declaration Separate Statement; Declaration pltff Alejandra Moreno’s Declaration.) These appear to be oppositions to the instant motions.  

 

II.             MOTION TO DEEM RFAS ADMITTED 

 

A.    Deem Admitted¿ 

 

When a party fails to timely respond to a request for admission, the propounding party may move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the requests be deemed admitted. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(b).)  The party who failed to respond waives any objections to the demand, unless the court grants them relief from the waiver, upon a showing that the party (1) has subsequently served a substantially compliant response, and (2) that the party’s failure to respond was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(a)(1)-(2).)¿  

 

The court shall grant a motion to deem admitted requests for admissions, “unless it finds that the party to whom the requests for admission have been directed has served, before the hearing on the motion, a proposed response to the requests for admission that is in substantial compliance with Section 2033.220.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)¿ “Subdivision (c) of section 2033.280 requires the court to evaluate whether the ‘proposed response to the requests for admission’ substantially complies with section 2033.220.” (St. Mary v. Superior Court (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 762, 779.)  

 

Here, Defendants served RFAs, Set One, on Plaintiff on April 16, 2024. (Lakeotes Declaration RFA ¶ 7.)  Plaintiff did not respond to Defendants’ RFAs, Set One. (Id. ¶ 11.) On August 29, 2024, Plaintiff filed two Separate Statements with the Court. (Plaintiff Alejandra Moreno’s Declaration; Declaration Pltff) But Plaintiff’s Separate Statements do not contain responses to Defendants’ RFAs, Set One. Thus, Defendants are entitled to an order deeming RFAs, Set One, admitted.

                

B.     Monetary Sanctions¿ 

 

Where a party fails to provide a timely response to requests for admission, “[i]t is mandatory that the court impose a monetary sanction under Chapter 7 (commencing with Section 2023.010) on the party or attorney, or both, whose failure to serve a timely response to requests for admission necessitated this motion.”¿ (Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.280(c).)¿ 

 

Here, Defendants seek $2,660.00 in monetary sanctions. (Lakeotes Declaration RFAs ¶ 16; Motion to Deem RFAs Admitted at p. 4.) This includes ten hours preparing the motion, three hours reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition brief and preparing Defendants’ reply brief and one hour for the hearing. (Lakeotes Declaration RFAs ¶ 16.) 

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s failure to respond to Defendants’ discovery requests a misuse of the discovery process.  The Court is also required to impose a monetary sanction on Plaintiff for failure to respond to the Requests for Admission under Code of Civil Procedure section 2033.280, subdivision (c).¿ However, the amount sought is excessive given the simplicity of this Motion and the lack of opposition and reply. Thus, the Court finds a reduction of time spent preparing the motion is warranted and reduces the ten hours requested to seven hours. The court also finds the time spent reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition brief and preparing Defendants’ reply is not warranted because Plaintiff filed no opposition and Defendants filed no reply.  

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff is granted in the reduced total amount of $1,520.00, reflecting seven hours of preparation for the original motion and one hour for the hearing at $190 per hour.  

 

III.              MOTION TO COMPEL RESPONSES

 

A.    Failure to Respond 

 

“If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response . . . [that party] waives any right to produce writings under Section 2030.230, as well as any objection to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.290.) “The court, on motion, may relieve that party from this waiver on its determination that both of the following conditions are satisfied: (1) The party has subsequently served a response that is in substantial compliance with Sections 2030.210, 2030.220, 2030.230, and 2030.240. [and] (2) The party’s failure to serve a timely response was the result of mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect.” (Id.)  

 

Defendants argue the court should grant its motion because Plaintiff failed to provide any responses to Defendants’ Special Interrogatories, Set One. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at 1.) Plaintiff argues Defendants’ discovery requests are burdensome and oppressive. (Plaintiff Declaration at p.6.)  

 

A party waives his or her objections to interrogatories if he or she fails to serve a timely response. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2031.290.) Here, Defendants served Plaintiff with Special Interrogatories, Set One, on April 16, 2024. (Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 7.) Plaintiff’s responses were originally due on May 20, 2024, but the deadline was extended multiple times. (Id. at ¶¶ 8-12.) Ultimately, Plaintiff’s responses were due on June 21, 2024. (Id. at ¶¶ 7, 13.)  But Plaintiff did not serve Defendant with responses. (Memorandum of Points and Authorities at p. 3; Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶¶ 14-15.) And Plaintiff has not subsequently served responses to Defendants’ Special Interrogatories, Set One, that substantially comply with the statutes.  Accordingly, Plaintiff has waived her objections to Defendants’ Special Interrogatories, Set One and Defendants’ motion to compel response is granted. 

 

B.     Sanctions 

 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290(c).)  

 

Defendants request attorneys’ fees totaling $2,660.00. (Lakeotes Declaration MTFC ¶ 16.) This includes ten hours for preparing the motion, three hours reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition brief and preparing Defendants’ reply, and one hour for hearing time. (Id.)  

 

The Court finds Plaintiff did not act with substantial justification and no other circumstances make the imposition of sanctions unjust.  The Court also finds Plaintiff’s failure to respond to Defendants’ discovery requests a misuse of the discovery process.  Thus, the Court finds a reduction of time spent preparing the motion is warranted and reduces the ten hours requested to seven hours. The Court also finds the time spent reviewing Plaintiff’s opposition brief and preparing Defendants’ reply is not warranted because Plaintiff filed no opposition and Defendants filed no reply.  

 

Accordingly, Defendants’ request for monetary sanctions against Plaintiff is granted in the reduced total amount of $1,520.00, reflecting seven hours of preparation for the original motion and one hour for the hearing at $190 per hour. 

 

IV.           CONCLUSION 

 

For the foregoing reasons, Defendants’ Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters in RFAs, Set One Admitted as to Plaintiff is GRANTED. 

 

Defendants’ Motion to Compel Plaintiff’s Response to Defendants’ Special Interrogatories, Set One is GRANTED 

 

Defendants’ request for sanctions as to Plaintiff is also granted in the amount of $3,040.00 to be paid by Plaintiff to Defendants’ counsel within thirty (30) days of notice of this order.¿ 

 

Moving party to give notice.¿

COURT’S [TENATIVE] ORDER RE: PETITION AND DECLARATION RE UNRESOLVED CLAIMS AND DEPOSIT OF UNDISTRIBUTED SURPLUS PROCEEDS OF TRUSTEE’S SALE

 

 

 

 

 

Dept. 107

9:00 a.m.

September 23, 2024

BACKGROUND

 

On March 6, 2024, Petitioner Western progressive, LLC (“Petitioner”) filed a Petition and Declaration Regarding Unresolved Claims and Deposit of Undistributed Surplus Proceeds of Trustee’s Sale. The Petition is unopposed.

 

Petitioner is the trustee under the Deed of Trust (“DOT”), which encumbered the real property located at 7032 Crebs Avenue, Reseda, CA 91335 (“Property”). (Pet., ¶¶1-2.) The DOT was executed by Jose Alexander-Vallejos and Julia Vallejos, husband and wife as trustors, on September 7, 2006. (Pet., ¶3.) The DOT was subsequently recorded on September 16, 2006. (Id.) The Property was sold at a trustee’s sale held on June 27, 2023 for a total of $568,500.00. (Pet., ¶¶4-6.) After paying debt to foreclosing creditor ($495,845.010), trustee’s fees and expenses ($7,685.70), and the filing fee ($435.00), the remaining surplus proceeds is $64,534.29. (Pet., ¶16.) Petitioner has received three written claims from potential claimants to the surplus proceeds and has determined there is a conflict between the potential claimants. (Pet., ¶11.)

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

1.     Petition and Declaration Regarding Unresolved Claims and Deposit of Undistributed Surplus Proceeds of Trustee’s Sale is GRANTED. The amount of $64,534.29 shall be deposited with the Court.

 

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Civil Code Section 2924k, subdivision (a) states in relevant part, “[t]he trustee, or the clerk of the court upon order to the clerk pursuant to subdivision (d) of Section 2924j, shall distribute the proceeds, or a portion of the proceeds, as the case may be, of the trustee’s sale conducted pursuant to Section 2924h in the following order of priority: (1) to the costs and expenses of exercising the power of sale and of sale, including the payment of the trustee’s fees and attorney’s fees permitted pursuant to subdivision (b) of Section 2924d and subdivision (b) of this section…(2) to the payment of the obligations secured by the deed of trust or mortgage which is the subject of the trustee’s sale…(3) to satisfy the outstanding balance of obligations secured by any junior liens or encumbrances in the order of their priority…[and] (4) to the trustor or the trustor’s successor in interest. In the event the property is sold or transferred to another, to the vested owner of record at the time of the trustee’s sale.” (Civ. Code, § 2924k, subd. (a).)

 

ANALYSIS

 

Petitioner contends the original trustors to the foreclosing DOT were Jose Alexander-Vallejos and Julia Vallejos, husband and wife as joint tenants (“Trustors”) in a Grant Deed recorded October 10, 2001. (Compendium of Exhibits (“COE”), Ex. C.) Subsequently, Petitioner asserts Trustors executed a Junior DOT, which was recorded on September 15, 2006. (COE, Ex. D.) A beneficiary of the Junior DOT foreclosed on the Property on November 15, 2022 (“Junior Foreclosure”) and the Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale (“Junior Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale”) resulting from the Junior Foreclosure was recorded on January 4, 2023. The Junior Trustee’s Deed Upon Sale vested ownership in the Property to West Coast Servicing, Inc. (“West Coast”). (COE, Ex. E.) As such, Petitioner argues the title to the Property at the time of the sale of the Foreclosing DOT on June 27, 2023, was vested in West Coast. However, Petitioner asserts the Trustors dispute the Junior DOT’s validity, thus West Coast’s eligibility to receive the excess proceeds.

 

“If, after due diligence, the trustee is unable to determine the priority of the written claims received by the trustee to the trustee's sale surplus of multiple persons or if the trustee determines there is a conflict between potential claimants, the trustee may file a declaration of the unresolved claims and deposit with the clerk of the superior court of the county in which the sale occurred, that portion of the sales proceeds that cannot be distributed, less any fees charged by the clerk pursuant to this subdivision. The declaration shall specify the date of the trustee's sale, a description of the property, the names and addresses of all persons sent notice pursuant to subdivision (a), a statement that the trustee exercised due diligence pursuant to subdivision (b), that the trustee provided written notice as required by subdivisions (a) and (d), and the amount of the sales proceeds deposited by the trustee with the court. Further, the trustee shall submit a copy of the trustee's sales guarantee and any information relevant to the identity, location, and priority of the potential claimants with the court and shall file proof of service of the notice required by subdivision (d) on all persons described in subdivision (a).” (Civ. Code, § 2924j, subd. (c) (emphasis added).)

 

Here, Petitioner has determined there is a conflict between potential claimants. Further, the declaration complies with Civil Code Section 2924j, subdivision (a). Similarly, Petitioner has provided a statement of due diligence pursuant to Civil Code Section 2924j, subdivision (b) and provided written notice required by subdivisions (a) and (d). (Pet., Attach. 11b.) Likewise, the Petition and Declaration specifies the amount of the sale proceeds deposited by Petitioner with the Court. (Pet., ¶16.) Petitioner also submits the Notice of Intent to Deposit Excess Proceeds mailed to the potential claimants. (Pet., Attach. 13.) Additionally, Petitioner submits the fees and costs associated with the trustee sale and instant Petition. (Pet., Attach. 15.) Lastly, Petitioner filed proof of service of the notice required by subdivision (d) on all potential claimants. (POS, 3/19/24.)

 

 

CONCLUSION

 

Based on the foregoing, Petitioner Western Progressive, LLC’s Petition and Declaration Regarding Unresolved Claims and Deposit of Undistributed Surplus Proceeds of Trustee’s Sale is GRANTED. The amount of $64,534.29 shall be deposited with the Court.
