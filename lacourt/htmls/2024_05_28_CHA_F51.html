DEPARTMENT F51 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

Counsel wishing to submit on a tentative ruling may inform the clerk or courtroom assisant in North Valley Department F51, 9425 Penfield Ave., Chatsworth, CA 91311, at (818) 407-2251.  Please be aware that unless all parties submit, the matter will still be called for hearing and may be argued by any appearing/non-submitting parties. If the matter is submitted on the court's tentative ruling by all parties, counsel for moving party shall give notice of ruling. This may be done by incorporating verbatim the court's tentative ruling. The tentative ruling may be extracted verbatim by copying and specially pasting, as unformatted text, from the Los Angeles Superior Court’s website, http://www.lasuperiorcourt.org. All hearings on law and motion and other calendar matters are generally NOT transcribed by a court reporter unless one is provided by the party(ies).

Case Number: 22CHCV01502    Hearing Date: May 28, 2024    Dept: F51

MAY 24, 2024

 

MOTION FOR ATTORNEY FEES

Los Angeles Superior Court Case # 22CHCV01502

 

 

Motion Filed: 1/8/24

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Jairo Hernandez (“Plaintiff”)¿ 

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Hyundai Motor America (“Defendant”)¿ 

NOTICE: OK

 

RELIEF REQUESTED: A judgment in favor of Plaintiff and against Defendant, and an order granting Plaintiff his attorney fees in the amount of $23,846.23, and costs in the amount of $2,291.14.

 

TENTATIVE RULING: The motion is granted in part. Plaintiff is awarded $12,520.07 in attorney fees and costs.

 

Plaintiff is reminded to review the 5/3/19 First Amended General Order Re Mandatory Electronic Filing for Civil.¿When e-filing documents, parties must comply with the “TECHNICAL REQUIREMENTS” which are set forth at page 4, line 4 through page 5, line 12 of the Court’s 5/3/19 First Amended General Order Re Mandatory Electronic Filing for Civil (particularly bookmarking declarations and exhibits). (CRC 3.1110(f)(4).) Failure to comply with these requirements in the future may result in papers being rejected, matters being placed off calendar, matters being continued so documents can be resubmitted in compliance with these requirements, documents not being considered and/or the imposition of sanctions.

 

BACKGROUND

 

Plaintiff brings this action against Defendant under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act (Civil Code § 1790 et seq.) for a vehicle he leased on 5/16/20, and for which Defendant issued the manufacturer’s warranty. (Compl. ¶¶ 5, 10.)

 

On 12/28/22, Plaintiff filed his complaint, alleging against Defendant the following causes of action: (1) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Express Warranty; (2) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act – Breach of Implied Warranty; and (3) Violation of the Song-Beverly Act Section 1793.2(b). On 4/5/23, Defendant filed its answer.

On 12/20/23, the parties entered into a settlement agreement. (Pl.’s Mot. 1:3–5.) On 1/8/24, Plaintiff filed the instant motion. On 1/9/24, Plaintiff filed a notice of conditional settlement of the action. On 2/20/24, Plaintiff filed his memorandum of costs. On 5/8/24, Defendant filed its opposition. On 5/14/24, Plaintiff filed his reply.

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.      Right to Recovery

 

An award of attorney fees is proper when authorized by contract, statute, or law. (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 1032, subd. (b); 1033.5, subd. (a)(10).) Here, the Song-Beverly Act authorizes a buyer “to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney’s fees based on actual time expended, determined by the court to have been reasonably incurred by the buyer in connection with the commencement and prosecution of such action.” (Civ. Code § 1794, subd. (d).)

 

Moreover, Plaintiff bases his instant motion on the settlement agreement wherein Defendant agreed to pay “Plaintiff’s attorneys’ fees, costs, and expenses in an amount determined by the Court, by way of a single noticed motion, to have been reasonably incurred by Plaintiff to date in the commencement and prosecution of this action, unless the parties agree on the amount of fees, costs and expenses to be paid absent such a motion.” (Pl.’s Mot. p. ii.) The Court notes that Plaintiff has not attached a copy of the agreement to his moving papers for the Court’s corroboration. Nevertheless, the Court finds that the Song-Beverly Act affords Plaintiff the right to recover reasonable attorney fees.

 

2.      Attorney Fees Incurred

 

A buyer prevailing under the Song-Beverly Act may recover “reasonable” attorney fees and costs as determined by the Court. (Civ. Code § 1794, subd. (d).) “The burden is on the party seeking attorney fees to prove that the fees it seeks are reasonable.” (Gonzalez v. Santa Clara County Dept. of Social Services (2017) 9 Cal.App.5th 162, 169.) In determining a reasonable fee award, the Court begins with the lodestar method of calculation, i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.¿(Karton v. Ari Design & Construction, Inc. (2021) 61 Cal.App.5th 734, 744; PLCM Group, Inc. v. Drexler (2000) 22 Cal.4th 1084, 1095–1096.)

a.       Counsel’s Hourly Rate

 

When determining a reasonable hourly rate, courts consider whether the stated rates “are within the range of reasonable rates charged by and judicially awarded [to] comparable attorneys for comparable work.” (Children's Hosp. & Med. Ctr. v. Bonta (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 740, 783.)

 

Here, Plaintiff asserts that “the rates charged by The Lemon Pros, LLP are commensurate with the experience and skill of the attorneys involved and the general rates of attorneys in Los Angeles California that handle consumer protection lawsuits on a contingency basis.” (Pl.’s Mot. 8:9–11.) Counsel declares that these rates are commensurate with the Laffey Matrix, an attorney fee billing matrix for attorneys in the Washington, D.C. metro area, which California courts have applied to attorney fee awards in the Los Angeles area. (Saeedian Decl. ¶ 12, citing Pasternack v. McCullough (2021) 65 Cal.App.5th 1050, 1057, fn. 5; Tukes v. Richard (2022) 81 Cal.App.5th 1; 569 E. Cnty. Boulevard LLC v. Backcountry Against the Dump, Inc. (2016) 248 Cal.App.4th 125; Gutierrez v. Chopard U.S. Ltd. (2022) 82 Cal.App.5th 383; Syers Properties III, Inc. v. Rankin (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 691; Nemecek v. Horn (2012) 208 Cal.App.4th 641.)

 

In opposition, Defendant presents several cases where the court elected to reduce hourly billing rates of plaintiff’s counsel in similar cases. (Defs.’ Opp. 6:1–7:8.) Defendant asserts that Plaintiff’s attorneys’ hourly rates should be reduced to an attorney rate of $350.00 per hour and paralegal rate at $250.00 per hour, because “although every lemon law case is different, like here, there were no unique issues or extraordinary motions for a lemon law case.” (Id. at 12:23–24.) Defendant further argues that “the Laffey Matrix has no bearing on appropriate fees in California state court for a lemon law matter and should be disregarded by this Court.” (Id. at 13:8–10.)

 

Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s attorney’s hourly rates exceed the range of reasonable rates charged by attorneys in these types of cases and judicially awarded in similar cases. The Court reduces the hourly rates as follows:

b.      Hours Expended

 

Plaintiff seeks to recover attorney fees for the 56.1 hours expended by his attorneys on this matter, totaling $25,699.00. Plaintiff proffers an itemized list of time entries detailing his attorneys’ work on this matter. (Ex. A to Saeedian Decl.)

 

In addition to the attorney hourly billing rate, Defendant challenges the following specific entries in Plaintiff’s attorneys’ proffered time log: (1) time incurred in drafting pleadings and discovery; (2) Mr. Acosta’s time entries; (3) Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication; (4) Plaintiff’s motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s person most knowledgeable (“PMK”); (5) time spent on internal updates and administrative tasks; and (6) time incurred in connection with the instant fee motion; and (7) time spent on clerical tasks.

 

i.                    Discovery

 

Defendant argues that the attorney fees incurred by Plaintiff in drafting pleadings and discovery to Defendant should be reduced by 3.2 hours because “pleadings and discovery are simply copy and paste” to those used in other cases involving both parties’ counsel. (Def.’s Opp. 8:21–24.) Regarding discovery requests, Defendant argues that “these demands and deposition notice are recycled from previous lemon law cases and are nearly identical in each and every case.” (Decl. Of Brian Yasuzawa ¶ 8.)

 

Plaintiff’s billing statement reflects that 2.7 hours (2.2 hours by JLA, 0.5 hours by MS) were incurred in drafting discovery requests to Defendant, and 4.2 hours were incurred (by MS) in reviewing Defendant’s discovery responses. (Ex. A to Saeedian Decl., pp. 3, 4.) The Court reduces the hours incurred for these tasks by 2.9 hours (0.2 hours by JLA, 2.7 hours by MS), a total reduction of $1,926.50.

 

ii.                  Motion for Summary Adjudication

 

Defendant further argues that the Court should strike the attorney fees incurred by Plaintiff in drafting his motion for summary adjudication against Defendant’s affirmative defenses. (Def.’s Opp. 9:22–24.) “Plaintiff made no attempt to meet and confer on any of HMA’s affirmative defenses or requested HMA to withdraw any of them. … Furthermore, Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication was premature as HMA had not yet conducted any discovery in the matter. Plaintiff’s motion is predicated on the assertion HMA had no evidence to support its defenses.” (Id. at 9:26–10:5.)

 

Plaintiff’s billing statement reflects that 6.3 hours (0.6 hours by JLA, 5.7 hours by CU) were incurred in preparing the subject motion for summary adjudication. (Ex. A to Saeedian Decl., p. 5.) The Court finds the fees associated with this motion are not reasonably recoverable because the motion was not necessary, nor was it heard by the Court. Accordingly, the Court reduces the fee demand for these associated tasks by 6.3 hours, a total reduction of $3,142.50.

 

iii.                Motion to Compel Deposition

 

Defendant further argues that the Court should strike the attorney fees incurred by Plaintiff in preparing its motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s PMK. (Def.’s Opp. 10:14–15.) Defendant maintains that the motion was improper because “at no time prior to Plaintiff filing the motion to compel did the parties meet and confer in person or via phone about this issue. … In addition, the motion was never heard and the matter settled.” (Id. at 10:14–18.) The Court agrees.

 

Plaintiff’s billing statement reflects that 1.9 hours (0.4 hours by JLA, 1.5 hours by CU) were incurred in preparing the subject motion for summary adjudication. (Ex. A to Saeedian Decl., pp. 5–6.) Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that the above fees were not reasonably incurred, and reduces the fee demand for these associated tasks by 1.9 hours, a total reduction of $887.50.

 

iv.                Administrative Tasks

 

Defendants argue generally that throughout the billing records, there entries for “purely administrative work.” (Def.’s Opp. 10:23–24.) “For example, Attorney Saeedian bills to review a notice of case assignment, review assignment of a case number, to receive and review the Notice of Case Management Conference, to receive and review defendant’s Notice of Posting Jury Fees, to receipt and review of Notice of Case Reassignment from Court, and for each and every email with Defense Counsel discussing settlement, even if the email was a simple follow-up.” (Id. at 10:26–11:3.) Defendant also argues that time incurred for internal updates are “duplicative and unnecessary” because the bill already reflects numerous entries for providing updates to the client. (Id. at 11:7–8.)

 

Plaintiff’s billing statement reflects that 1.2 hours (0.8 hours by JLA, 0.3 hours by MS, and 0.1 hours by CU) were incurred for purely administrative tasks. (Ex. A to Saeedian Decl., passim.) Based on the foregoing, the Court finds that the above fees were not reasonably incurred, and reduces the fee demand for these associated tasks by 0.6 hours, a total reduction of $230.50.

 

3.      Costs Incurred

 

Plaintiff asserts that he incurred costs and expenses totaling $2,291.14, which encompasses: (1) $1,177.75 in filing and motion fees; (2) $172.08 in jury fees; (3) $35.00 in process server fees; (4) $105.99 in electronic filing/service fees; and (4) $800.32 in other unspecified costs. (2/20/24 Mem. of Costs.) The Court notes that Defendants do not challenge the purported costs incurred by Plaintiff in this action. Nevertheless, as the Court strikes attorney fees in connection with Plaintiff’s motion for summary adjudication and motion to compel the deposition of Defendant’s PMK, the Court reduces the cost award by $651.07.
