DEPARTMENT A LAW AND MOTION RULINGS

INSTRUCTIONS: If the parties wish to submit on the tentative ruling and avoid a court appearance on the matter, the moving party must:

1. Contact the opposing party and all other parties who have appeared in the action and confirm that each will submit on the tentative ruling.

2. No later than 4:00 p.m. on the court day before the hearing, call the Courtroom (310-761-4302) advising that all parties will submit on the tentative ruling and waive hearing; and

3. Serve notice of the Court's ruling on all parties entitled to receive service.

If this procedure is followed, when the case is called the Court will enter its ruling on the motion in accordance with its tentative ruling. If any party declines to submit on the tentative ruling, then no telephone call is necessary, and all parties should appear at the hearing. If there is neither a telephone call nor an appearance, then the matter may either be taken off calendar or ruled on.

      CASE NO: 23CMCP00205

 

[TENTATIVE] ORDER GRANTING PLAINTIFF’S PETITION FOR ORDER FOR RELIEF FROM CLAIM STATUTE

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 10, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

PETITION FILED: 11/09/2023

TRIAL: None set

 

 

1.     Background

This is a personal injury case. The petition alleges that petitioner Matthew Kashinsky (Petititoner), a minor, was injured while playing soccer at Rosecrans Elementary School within the Compton Unified School District.

2.     Petition for Order For Relief from Claim Statute

          a.  Petition filed 11/09/23

Petitioner requests relief from the requirements of the Government Claims Statute, Government Code section 945.4 (the “Petition”), as Petitioner failed to file a government claim by the six-month deadline and his application for leave to file a late claim was denied.

Oppositions were filed by respondents County of Los Angeles (County) and Compton Unified School District (CUSD) (collectively, Respondents). Respondents contend that Petitioner’s failure to file a timely government claim did not arise from excusable neglect and Respondents would be prejudiced by the requested relief.

In reply, Petitioner argues that Government Code section 946.6, the code section providing for a petition for relief from Section 945.4 requirements, is a remedial statute intended to provide relief from technical rules and must be construed liberally. Petitioner contends that his claim was presented in a reasonable time, was late due to mistake or excusable neglect, and there was no showing of prejudice by Respondents.

3.  Petition for Order for Relief from Claim Statute standards

Generally, no suit for money or damages may be brought against a public entity until a written claim is presented to and rejected by that entity. (Gov. Code § 945.4; see also Munoz v. State of California (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1767, 1776.) The failure to timely file a proper government claim is fatal to the maintenance of a civil action against a public entity. (Hernandez v. City of Stockton (2023) 90 Cal.App.5th 1222, 1230–31.) Claims for personal injury must be presented “not later than six months after the accrual of the cause of action.” (Gov. Code, §911.2.)

If an application for leave to present a claim is denied, a petition may be made to the court for an order relieving the petitioner from the requirements of Government Code section 945.4. (Gov. Code § 946.6.) The court shall grant the petition if the court finds that the application for leave was made within a reasonable time and was denied or deemed denied and that any of a number of circumstances apply, including but not limited to (1) that the failure to present the claim was through mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect and the public entity would not be prejudiced in defense of the claim by granting of relief; or (2) that the person who sustained the alleged injury, loss, or damage was a minor throughout the required claims presentation period.

3.     Discussion

          a. Relief from Claim Statute

The court grants petitioner’s request for relief from the claim statute.

Under Government Code section 946.6, relief from the requirements of the Government Claims Act is mandatory if the petitioner filed an application within a reasonable time that was denied and the person who sustained the alleged injury was a minor during the claim presentation period. A reasonable time is not to exceed one year after the accrual of the cause of action. Petitioner was injured on May 14, 2022. The deadline for presenting a government claim was November 14, 2022.  Petitioner applied for leave to present a late claim on May 11, 2023. The application was made within one year of the injury. Petitioner states that application was made as soon as it was discovered that he had not filed a government claim prior to retaining counsel, and the delay occurred because Petitioner miscalendared the date for filing a government claim and because of a COVID-19 outbreak in the office of Petitioner’s counsel.

The court finds that Petitioner’s failure to file the Petition timely was the result of mistake, inadvertence and/or excusable neglect.  Petitioner filed his application for leave within a reasonable time. This application was denied.  In addition, Petitioner’s date of birth is August 1, 2014. Petitioner was therefore a minor throughout the entire claim presentation period from May 14, 2022 to November 14, 2022.

Because the requirements of Government Code section 946.6 have been fulfilled, the court will grant Petitioner’s request for an order for relief from the claim statute.

4.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS the Petition.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER DENYING PLAINTIFF’S MOTION FOR ORDER PERMITTING DISCOVERY OF FINANCIAL CONDITIONS OF DEFENDANT

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 10, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 05/18/2023

FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT FILED: 08/22/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

 

1.     Background

This is a dog bite case. The complaint alleges that a German Shepard owned by defendant Alfredo Arceo (Defendant) bit plaintiff Yesenia Flores (Plaintiff) while Plaintiff was walking on the sidewalk near Defendant’s home. The complaint alleges causes of action for negligence, premises liability, strict liability and punitive damages.

2.     Motion for Order Permitting Discovery of Financial Conditions of Defendant

Plaintiff moves for an order allowing her to conduct discovery on Defendant’s financial conditions on the grounds that there is a substantial probability that Plaintiff will prevail on her punitive damages claim, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 3295, subdivision (c).

Defendant contends that the motion should be denied because there is not a substantial probability that Plaintiff will prevail on her punitive damages claim because she cannot show malice, oppression, or fraud.  Plaintiff argues that Defendant’s conduct, before and after the dog bite incident, shows malice because Defendant did not train his dogs and videotaped Plaintiff after the incident.

3.     Discovery of Financial Conditions standards

Pretrial discovery of the financial condition of a defendant is generally not permitted unless the court finds, on the basis of supporting and opposing affidavits presented, that the plaintiff has established a substantial probability that they will prevail on their claim. (Code Civ. Proc., § 3295, subdiv. (c).)

Punitive damages are allowable upon a showing that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).)  Clear and convincing evidence of malice, oppression or fraud is required.  (Civ. Code, §§ 3294, subdiv. (a).)¿  The court may enter an order permitting discovery of a defendant’s financial condition if, after weighing the evidence submitted in favor of and in opposition to the motion for discovery, it finds that it is very likely that the plaintiff will prevail on their claim for punitive damages.  (Jabro v. Superior Court (2002) 95 Cal.App.4th 754, 758.)¿¿

“Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (College Hospital, Inc. v. Superior Court (Crowell) (1994) 8 Cal.4th 710, 725 [examining Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (c)(1)].) “A conscious disregard of the safety of others may constitute malice within the meaning of section 3294 of the Civil Code. In order to justify an award of punitive damages on this basis, the plaintiff must establish that the defendant was aware of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct, and that he willfully and deliberately failed to avoid those consequences.”¿(Taylor v. Superior Court (1979) 24 Cal.3d 890, 895-896.)¿

“As amended to include [despicable], the statute plainly indicates that absent an intent to injure the plaintiff, ‘malice’ requires more than a ‘willful and conscious’ disregard of the plaintiffs’ interests.¿The additional component of ‘despicable conduct’ must be found.”¿(College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at p. 725.)¿The statute’s reference to despicable conduct represents a “new substantive limitation on punitive damage awards.” (Ibid.)¿Despicable conduct is “conduct which is so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.¿Such conduct has been described as ‘having the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.’ ”¿(Tomaselli v. Transamerica Ins. Co. (1994) 25 Cal.App.4th 1269, 1287 (Tomaselli).) Factors in assessing the degree of reprehensibility of a defendant’s conduct are (1) whether the harm caused was physical or merely economic; (2) whether the conduct demonstrated indifference or reckless disregard for the health or safety of others; (3) the financial vulnerability of the target; (4) whether the conduct occurred repeatedly or only once; and (5) whether the harm was the result of intentional malice, trickery, or deceit, or was a mere accident. (Pfeifer v. John Crane, Inc. (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1270, 1311, fn 12.)

4.     Discussion

          a. Substantial Probability of Success

The court finds that Plaintiff has not demonstrated a substantial probability of success on her punitive damages claim, as would be required to seek discovery of Defendant’s financial condition.

Plaintiff has argued malice, but not oppression or fraud, supports an order granting the motion. Plaintiff’s argument that Defendant acted with malice rests on her contention that Defendant did not train his dogs and that Defendant disregarded an advisement from Animal Control. This conduct does not have “the character of outrage frequently associated with crime.” (Tomaselli, supra, 25 Cal.App.4th at p. 1287.) In particular, Defendant has testified in deposition that, although Marley had barked at people and animals passing by, Marley had not shown aggression towards other dogs or people in the past, indicating that Defendant may not have had knowledge of the risk of a dog bite incident; in other words, awareness of the probable dangerous consequences of his conduct. There is no indication that the dog bite incident occurred because of any intentional malice. In sum, Defendant’s conduct lacked the character of being “so vile, base, contemptible, miserable, wretched or loathsome that it would be looked down upon and despised by ordinary decent people.” (Ibid.) It does not reach the level of despicability found in cases where punitive damages were found to be proper.  Plaintiff also contends that Defendant acted with malice because his deposition testimony does not match the deposition testimony of others, and he videotaped Plaintiff at her home at the direction of his insurance company. 

Defendant contends Plaintiff cannot show malice because Defendant purchased his dogs for the protection of his home and family; that he increased the height of his fence in compliance with Animal Control’s recommendations for the safety of others; and that he testified that Marley, the dog that allegedly bit Plaintiff, has never been aggressive towards another person or dog. Defendant also argues that the lack of formal dog training does not show malice and that there is no published dog bite case in which punitive damages have been awarded. The court has also been unable to find any published dog bite case in which punitive damages were awarded.

Plaintiff has not brought forth evidence that Defendant acted with malice, whether clear and convincing evidence or otherwise.  After weighing the evidence submitted in favor of and in opposition to the motion for discovery, the court finds that plaintiff has not established a substantial probability that she will prevail on her claim for punitive damages.  Thus, she has failed to meet her burden under Code Civ. Proc., § 3295, subdiv. (c).

5.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court DENIES Plaintiff’s motion for an order permitting discovery of Defendant’s financial condition.

Moving party is ordered to give notice.

1.     Background

This is an action arising from a motor vehicle collision. The complaint alleges that a bus operated by Los Angeles County Metropolitan Transit Authority (Defendant) crossed a solid white line on the roadway and collided with plaintiff Yakelin Hinjosa Gomez’s (Plaintiff) parked car, causing property damage and personal injury. The complaint alleges a cause of action for negligence.

On June 7, 2024, the day Defendant filed its answer to Plaintiff’s complaint, it also served Plaintiff with Form Interrogatories – Set One, Request for Production of Documents, Set One (including 15 requests), Requests for Admission, Set One (including 9 requests), Special Interrogatories, Set One (including 16 interrogatories), and Form Interrogatories, Set One.  After obtaining a lengthy extension of time to respond to these reasonable discovery requests, Plaintiff failed to respond. 

On October 17, 2024, Defendant filed motions to compel responses to the foregoing special interrogatories, requests for production and form interrogatories.  It also filed a motion to deem the requests for admission admitted.  Plaintiff failed to oppose the motions.

2.     Motion to Compel Initial Responses standards

A party must respond to interrogatories, requests for production of documents, or requests for admission within 30 days of service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2031.260, subd. (a); Code Civ. Proc., § 2033.250, subd. (a).) However, these time limits are extended if served by mail, overnight delivery, fax, or electronically. (See Code Civ. Proc., §1010.6, subd. (a)(4); see also Code Civ. Proc., §1013.) “If the last day for the performance of any act provided or required by law to be performed within a specified period of time is a holiday, then that period is hereby extended to and including the next day that is not a holiday.” (Code Civ. Proc., §12a, subd. (a).) Failure to timely respond waives all objections including privilege or on the protection of work product. (See Code Civ. Proc., §2031.300, subd. (a); see also Code Civ. Proc., §2030.290, subd. (a).) 

Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.030 provides that, “[t]o the extent authorized by the chapter governing any particular discovery method . . . , the court, after notice to any affected party, person, or attorney, and after opportunity for hearing, may impose . . . [monetary, issue, evidence, or terminating] sanctions against anyone engaging in conduct that is a misuse of the discovery process . . . .” Code of Civil Procedure section 2023.010 provides that misuse of the discovery process includes, but is not limited to, failing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.

3.     Discussion

          a. Motion to Compel Initial Responses

Defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set One), Special Interrogatories (Set One), Requests for Production (Set One), and Requests for Admission (Set One) were served on Plaintiff on June 7, 2024. Plaintiff requested an extension to provide responses, which was granted for August 8, 2024. (Preciado Decl., ¶ 7.)

Defendant’s motions were filed on October 17, 2024, substantially past the response deadline. Plaintiff had not provided discovery responses as of the date of filing of the motions, and has apparently failed to serve responses even to Defendants’ Requests for Admission, Set One, even after being served with the motion.

          b. Sanctions

Defendant requests $1,125 in attorney’s fees per motion against Plaintiff and her counsel of record, TRIMAX LAW FIRM, jointly and severally. This amount is based on counsel Arthur C. Preciado’s hourly rate of $250 and a time breakdown of 2 hours spent researching and drafting each motion, 1.5 hours spent drafting each reply, and one hour attending the hearing on this matter. This sums to 18 hours at $250 per hour, for a total of $4,500 in sanctions. The court finds Counsel Preciado’s hourly rate to be reasonable.  However, the amount sought is unreasonable in light of the duplicative nature of each motion, coupled with Plaintiff’s failure to oppose the motion, rendering reply briefs unnecessary.  Additionally, because these motions will be heard together, one hour for the hearing is reasonable.

In sum, the court will grant sanctions for $750 per motion (3 hours at $250 per hour), for a total of $3,000 in sanctions.

4.     Conclusion

Based on the foregoing, the court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to deem Defendant’s Requests for Admissions, Set One, admitted. The court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to respond to Requests for Production (Set One). The court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to respond to Form Interrogatories (Set One). The court GRANTS Defendant’s motion to compel Plaintiff to respond to Special Interrogatories (Set One).

Based on the foregoing, the court also GRANTS Defendant’s request for sanctions in the amount of $3,000 against Plaintiff and TRIMAX LAW FIRM, jointly and severally.

Plaintiff is ordered to serve code-compliant responses, without objection, to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories (Set One), Request for Production of Documents (Set One) and Special Interrogatories (Set One) within fourteen (14) days from service of this order.

Defendant is ordered to give notice.

[TENTATIVE] ORDER OVERRULING DEFENDANTS’ DEMURRER TO THE FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT

 

Dept. A

 

DATE: December 10, 2024

TIME: 8:30 A.M.

 

COMPLAINT FILED: 03/25/2024

FIRST AMENDED COMPLAINT FILED: 07/19/2024

TRIAL: None set

 

 

This is an unlawful detainer case. Plaintiff Veronica Childs (Plaintiff) filed her First Amended Complaint (FAC) on July 9, 2024.  A proof of service of summons and complaint was filed on July 9, 2024, indicating the FAC was personally served on Defendants Erica Ortiz and Mario Acosta (Defendants) that same day.  The FAC alleges Defendants owe Plaintiff over $38,000 in unpaid back rent and refuse to vacate Plaintiff’s property.  Defendants filed their demurrer to the FAC on July 31, 2024.

On July 31, 2024, Defendants Erica Ortiz and Mario Acosta filed a demurrer to the first cause of action of the FAC for unlawful detainer on the grounds that the three-day notice to pay or quit alleged in the FAC to have been served on Defendants was not served on Defendants on March 12, 2024, and the complaint therefore fails to state a cause of action.  Plaintiff did not file opposition to the demurrer.  No reply was filed.

A tenant may demur to a UD complaint.  (Code of Civ. Proc. §1170.) A demurrer must be filed within 5 days after service of the summons. See Code of Civ. Proc. §§1167, 1167.3, 1170.  The demurrer was filed on July 31, 2024, 22 days after service of the summons on July 9, 2024.  Therefore, it was untimely and is overruled on that ground.

The demurrer is also overruled on substantive grounds.  A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747 (Hahn).)¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context. (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power (2006) 144 Cal.App.4th 1216, 1228.)¿In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or via proper judicial notice.¿ (Donabedian v. Mercury Ins. Co. (2004) 116 Cal.App.4th 968, 994.)¿“A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.”¿(SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.”¿(Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.)¿

The ultimate facts alleged in the complaint must be deemed true, as well as all facts that may be implied or inferred from those expressly alleged.¿(Marshall v. Gibson, Dunn & Crutcher (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1397, 1403; see also Shields v. County of San Diego (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 103, 133 [stating, “[o]n demurrer, pleadings are read liberally and allegations contained therein are assumed to be true.”]) “The court does not, however, assume the truth of contentions, deductions or conclusions of law. [Citation.]” (Durell v. Sharp Healthcare (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 1350, 1358.)

The FAC alleges at paragraphs 9-10 that the 3-day notice to pay rent or quit was personally served on Defendants Erica Ortiz and Mario Acosta on March 12, 2024, and that the period stated in that notice expired at the end of the day on March 15, 2024.  Defendants demur to the complaint on the grounds that Plaintiff fails to state a cause of action because Plaintiff allegedly did not serve Defendants with the required three-day notice to pay or quit on March 12, 2024. Because all factual allegations in the complaint must be deemed true at the demurrer stage, Defendants have not presented a valid argument as to why the complaint fails to state a cause of action.

Based on the foregoing, the court OVERRULES Defendants’ demurrer to the unlawful detainer cause of action in the FAC.  Defendants are ordered to give notice.
