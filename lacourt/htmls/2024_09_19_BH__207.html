DEPARTMENT 207 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22SMCV01230    Hearing Date: September 19, 2024    Dept: 207

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

207

HEARING DATE

September 10, 2024 -  continued to September 19, 2024

CASE NUMBER

22SMCV01230

MOTION

Motion to be Relieved as Counsel

MOVING PARTY

Mark W. Huston, Esq.

OPPOSING PARTY

(none)

 

MOTION

 

Mark W. Huston, Esq., counsel for Cross-Defendant Sara Soleimany (“Counsel”) moves to be relieved as counsel, citing a breakdown of the attorney-client relationship.  The motion is unopposed.

 

LEGAL STANDARD

 

Code of Civil Procedure section 284 provides “[t]he attorney in an action or special proceeding may be changed at any time before or after judgment or final determination as follows: 1. Upon the consent of both client and attorney, filed with the clerk, or entered in the minutes; 2. Upon the order of the court, upon the application of either client or attorney, after notice from one to the other.”

 

Procedural Requirements

 

California Rules of Court, rule 3.1362, requires:

 

(1) the motion must be made on form MC-051; (subd. (a));

 

(2) it must be accompanied by a declaration on form MC-052 stating why the motion is brought under Code of Civil Procedure section 284(2) instead of a consent brought under section 284(1); (subd. (c));

 

(3) a proposed order on form MC-053 must be lodged with the court, specifying all hearing dates scheduled in the action or proceeding, including the date of trial, if known; (subd. (e)); and

 

(4) The documents must be served on the client and on all parties that have appeared in the case. (subd. (d).)

 

If the notice is served by mail or electronic service, it must be accompanied by a declaration indicating that the address served is the current address, or in the case of service by mail, that it was served on the last known address and a more current address could not be located after reasonable efforts within 30 days before filing the motion.  (Ibid.)  The court may delay the effective date of the order relieving counsel until proof of service of a copy of the signed order on the client has been filed with the court.”  (Ibid.) 

 

Substantive Requirements

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(a) outlines the reasons a lawyer must withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client is bringing an action, conducting a defense, asserting a position in litigation, or taking an appeal, without probable cause and for the purpose of harassing or maliciously injuring any person;

 

(2)   the representation will result in violation of the Rules of Professional Conduct or the State Bar Act;

 

(3)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it unreasonably difficult to carry out the representation effectively; or

 

(4)   the client discharges the lawyer.

 

Rules of Professional Conduct, rule 1.16(b) outlines the reasons a lawyer may withdraw from representation of a client:

 

(1)   the client insists upon presenting a claim or defense in litigation, or asserting a position or making a demand in a non-litigation matter, that is not warranted under existing law and cannot be supported by good faith argument for an extension, modification, or reversal of existing law;

 

(2)   the client either seeks to pursue a criminal or fraudulent course of conduct or has used the lawyer’s services to advance a course of conduct that the lawyer reasonably believes was a crime or fraud;

 

(3)   the client insists that the lawyer pursue a course of conduct that is criminal or fraudulent;

 

(4)   the client by other conduct renders it unreasonably difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(5)   the client breaches a material term of an agreement with, or obligation, to the lawyer relating to the representation, and the lawyer has given the client a reasonable warning after the breach that the lawyer will withdraw unless the client fulfills the agreement or performs the obligation;

 

(6)   the client knowingly and freely assents to termination of the representation;

 

(7)   the inability to work with co-counsel indicates that the best interests of the client likely will be served by withdrawal;

 

(8)   the lawyer’s mental or physical condition renders it difficult for the lawyer to carry out the representation effectively;

 

(9)   a continuation of the representation is likely to result in a violation of these rules or the State Bar Act; or

 

(10)         the lawyer believes in good faith in a proceeding pending before a tribunal that the tribunal will find the existence of other good cause for withdrawal.

 

DISCUSSION

 

            Counsel has filed forms MC-051, MC-052, and MC-053.  The attorney declaration (MC-052) indicates that the motions were filed instead of filing a consent because “There has been a breakdown of the attorney/client relationship, including the lack of communication” that makes it “impossible for our law firm to continue representing her interests and fulfilling our ethical obligations to diligently defend her in this action.”  (MC-052 at ¶ 2.)  As such, the Court finds that the motion complies with the Rules of Profession Conduct, rule 1.16(b)(4).) 

 

            The proof of service indicates each of the required forms were electronically served on the client and on counsel for Plaintiff and on counsel for Defendants and Cross-Complainants ABS Shoring, Inc. and George Soleimany.  The motion has not been served on Defendant and Cross-Complainant AIM Development, Inc. (“AIM”). 

 

The original Declaration also indicates Counsel has been unable to confirm the client’s address is current within the past 30 days, but has mailed the motion to the client’s last known address where Counsel has also mailed the client’s last several bills, none of which have been returned as undeliverable.  Therefore, the Court finds service on the client was proper.

 

With regard to service on AIM, prior counsel for AIM was relieved effective March 19, 2024, when counsel filed a notice of ruling, demonstrating service of the Court’s order on AIM. 

 

Moving Counsel has filed a supplemental declaration and a request for judicial notice, which demonstrates AIM dissolved as of August 7, 2023.  (Huston Decl. ¶¶ 2-3 and Ex. A.)  However,

 

(a) A corporation which is dissolved nevertheless continues to exist for the purpose of winding up its affairs, prosecuting and defending actions by or against it and enabling it to collect and discharge obligations, dispose of and convey its property and collect and divide its assets, but not for the purpose of continuing business except so far as necessary for the winding up thereof.

 

(b) No action or proceeding to which a corporation is a party abates by the dissolution of the corporation or by reason of proceedings for winding up and dissolution thereof.

 

(Corp. Code, § 2010.) 

 

Therefore, because the motion was not properly served on AIM, the motion is procedurally defective.   

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

Therefore, the Court denies the Motion to be Relieved as Counsel without prejudice on procedural grounds. 

 

            Counsel shall provide notice of the Court’s ruling and file the notice with a proof of service forthwith.

MOTION

 

This case arises from a dispute between former business partners.  Plaintiffs Stephen R. Hofer (“Hofer”); Stephen R. Hofer Law Corporation d/b/a Aerlex Law Group (“ALG”); Aerlex Tax Services, LLC (“ATS LLC”); and Aerlex Tax Services, LLP (“ATS LLP”) (ATS LLC and ATS LLP will be referenced as “ATS”) (collectively, “Plaintiffs”) brought suit against Defendants Vicky Boladian (“Boladian”) and Boladian Aviation Law Group, APC (“BALG”) (collectively, “Defendants”) alleging thirteen causes of action: (1) breach of contract – settlement agreement; (2) breach of contract – operating agreement and buy-sell agreement; (3) breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing; (4) breach of fiduciary duty; (5) intentional misrepresentation; (6) fraud in the inducement; (7) wrongful dissociation; (8) conversion; (9) intentional interference with contractual relations; (10) computer data access and fraud act; (11) receiving stolen property; (12) unlawful business practices; and (13) declaratory relief.

 

Defendants now move for judgment on the pleadings as to Plaintiffs’ first, second, fifth, sixth, seventh, eighth, ninth, tenth, and eleventh causes of action on the grounds that they fail to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action, pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure section 438, subdivision (c)(1)(B)(ii).  Plaintiffs oppose the motion and Defendants reply.

 

REQUEST FOR JUDICIAL NOTICE

 

            Defendants request judicial notice of the September 22, 2020 Settlement Agreement entered into among Boladian, Hofer, and ALG, as alleged in paragraphs 13 and 23-27 of the Complaint.

 

“Judicial notice may not be taken of any matter unless authorized or required by law.”  (Evid. Code, § 450.)  Evidence Code section 452 outlines the matters of which a Court may take judicial notice as follows:

 

(a)   The decisional, constitutional, and statutory law of any state of the United States and the resolutions and private acts of the Congress of the United States and of the Legislature of this state.

 

(b)   Regulations and legislative enactments issued by or under the authority of the United States or any public entity in the United States.

 

(c)   Official acts of the legislative, executive, and judicial departments of the United States and of any state of the United States.

 

(d)   Records of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States.

 

(e)   Rules of court of (1) any court of this state or (2) any court of record of the United States or of any state of the United States.

 

(f)    The law of an organization of nations and of foreign nations and public entities in foreign nations.

 

(g)   Facts and propositions that are of such common knowledge within the territorial jurisdiction of the court that they cannot reasonably be the subject of dispute.

 

(h)   Facts and propositions that are not reasonably subject to dispute and are capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy.

 

            In support of the request, Defendants cite to Ingram v. Flippo (1999) 74 Cal.App.4th 1280 (hereafter Ingram), where the trial court took judicial notice of a letter and media release made by a District Attorney.  Specifically, the appellate court found judicial notice proper under Evidence Code section 452, subdivision (h) for three reasons: (1) “the complaint excerpted quotes from the letter and summarized parts of it in some detail[,] (2) “both sides referred to the letter and quoted from it[,]” and (3) the appellant did not oppose the request.  (Id. at p. 1285, fn. 3.) 

 

However, unlike a letter and media release made by a District Attorney, which appear to be public records and therefore capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy (public government records), private contracts and agreements between parties are not capable of immediate and accurate determination by resort to sources of reasonably indisputable accuracy. 

 

Therefore, the Court denies the request for judicial notice.

 

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR JUDGMENT ON THE PLEADINGS

 

A motion for judgment on the pleadings has the same function as a general demurrer, but may be made after the time to demur has expired. (Code Civ. Proc., § 438, subd. (f).)  “Like a demurrer, the grounds for the motion [for judgment on the pleadings] must appear on the face of the challenged pleading or from any matter of which the court is required to take judicial notice.” (Civic Partners Stockton, LLC v. Youssefi (2013) 218 Cal.App.4th 1005, 1013.)  In ruling on a motion for judgment on the pleadings, “[a]ll allegations in the complaint and matters upon which judicial notice may be taken are assumed to be true.” (Rippon v. Bowen (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1308, 1313.) 

 

ANALYSIS

 

1.     First Cause of Action – Breach of Settlement Agreement

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for breach of contract, the plaintiff must prove (1) the contract, (2) the plaintiff's performance of the contract or excuse for nonperformance, (3) the defendant's breach, and (4) the resulting damage to the plaintiff.”  (Richman v. Hartley (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 1182, 1186.)  Here, the Complaint alleges:

 

13. The Settlement Agreement entered into among Boladian, Hofer, and ALG on or about September 22, 2020 provides, among other things, that:

 

a) “Boladian shall promptly inform Hofer in advance of any major decisions that affect ATS, and any decisions that impact Hofer personally or ALG. . . . Neither Boladian nor ATS shall take any action that harms the Aerlex brand, ALG, Hofer, or Hofer’s interest in ATS.”

 

b) “ATS and ALG shall jointly . . . ensure that all ATS and ALG marketing decisions are made in an acceptable way that is consistent with the Aerlex brand and messaging and does not harm ATS, Hofer, ALG, or Boladian.”

 

c) “ALG and ATS will continue in the future to maintain cordial relations as sister companies and where and when appropriate will recommend that clients utilize each other’s services. ALG shall identify and recommend ATS as ALG’s preferred provider of post-acquisition aircraft related tax services … . ATS shall identify and recommend ALG as ATS’s preferred provider of legal services for aircraft transactional work and such other matters that ALG is qualified to perform.”

 

d) “This Agreement, including the releases herein, shall be binding upon and inure to the benefit of each of the Parties hereto and their respective successors and assigns.”[1]

 

[…]

 

24. Hofer and ALG, and ATS as a third-party beneficiary, on the one hand, and Boladian on the other hand, entered into the Settlement Agreement.

 

25. Plaintiffs did all, or substantially all, of the significant things that the Settlement Agreement required them to do.

 

26. Boladian breached the Settlement Agreement by, inter alia:

 

a) Failing to promptly inform Hofer in advance of her decision to withdraw from ATS to start her own competing law firm;

 

b) Failing to promptly inform Hofer in advance of the decisions she took which impacted Hofer and ALG, including her decision to convert Hofer’s 55% ownership interest in ATS; to open a law firm to directly compete with ALG; and to induce ATS and ALG clients to join her new firm;

 

c) Failing to work jointly with ALG to make decisions that were consistent with the Aerlex brand and not harmful to ATS, Hofer or ALG; and,

 

d) Failing to work cordially with ALG as a “sister company” and refusing to identify and recommend ALG as a preferred provider of legal services for aircraft transactional work.

 

27. Plaintiffs were harmed and continue to be harmed by the aforementioned breaches in an amount to be determined. Boladian’s breaches were and are a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.

 

(Complaint, ¶¶ 13, 24-27.)

 

Defendant argues this fails to state a cause of action on behalf of ATS, as an alleged third-party beneficiary of the agreement, because it does not state facts to support this third party beneficiary theory.

 

The Court disagrees.  The Complaint alleges Hofer and Boladian created ATS LLC in 2014, with Hofer owning a 55% membership interest, and Boladian owning a 45% interest, and that Hofer and Boladian also jointly formed the successor entity, ATS LLP in 2023.  (Complaint ¶ 4.)  Further, the essential terms of the settlement agreement, as alleged in the Complaint, specifically reference mutual protections for ATS and ALG.  (See Complaint ¶ 13.)  Whether the ATS entities are in fact an intended third party beneficiary entitled to enforce the settlement agreement, or simply incidental beneficiaries not entitled to enforce the agreement, is a factual determination that cannot be decided at the pleadings stage.

 

Defendant next argues the complaint fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action because it does not identify any specific terms Boladian allegedly breached.  The Court disagrees.

 

The Complaint alleges the Settlement Agreement requires “Boladian shall promptly inform Hofer in advance of any major decisions that affect ATS” (Complaint ¶ 13(a)), which Boladian allegedly breached by “Failing to promptly inform Hofer in advance of her decision to withdraw from ATS to start her own competing law firm”  (Complaint ¶ 26(a).)

 

Similarly, the Complaint alleges the Settlement Agreement requires Boladian to promptly inform Hofer in advance of “any decisions that impact Hofer personally or ALG” (Complaint ¶ 13(a)) which Boladian allegedly breached by “Failing to promptly inform Hofer in advance of the decisions she took which impacted Hofer and ALG, including her decision to convert Hofer’s 55% ownership interest in ATS; to open a law firm to directly compete with ALG; and to induce ATS and ALG clients to join her new firm”  (Complaint ¶ 26(b).)

 

The Complaint further alleges the Settlement Agreement requires “Neither Boladian nor ATS shall take any action that harms the Aerlex brand, ALG, Hofer, or Hofer’s interest in ATS.” (Complaint ¶ 13(a) , which Boladian allegedly breached by “Failing to work jointly with ALG to make decisions that were consistent with the Aerlex brand and not harmful to ATS, Hofer or ALG.” (Complaint ¶ 26(c).)

 

The Complaint also alleges the Settlement Agreement requires:   “ALG and ATS will continue in the future to maintain cordial relations as sister companies and where and when appropriate will recommend that clients utilize each other’s services. ALG shall identify and recommend ATS as ALG’s preferred provider of post-acquisition aircraft related tax services . . . .   ATS shall identify and recommend ALG as ATS’s preferred provider of legal services for aircraft transactional work and such other matters that ALG is qualified to perform.” (Complaint ¶ 13(c).)  Boladian allegedly breached these terms by “Failing to work cordially with ALG as a “sister company” and refusing to identify and recommend ALG as a preferred provider of legal services for aircraft transactional work.”  (Complaint ¶ 26(d).)

 

Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the first cause of action.

 

2.     Second Cause of Action – Breach of Operating Agreement and Buy-Sell Agreement

 

Plaintiff first argues that the Second Cause of Action for Breach of the Operating Agreement and Buy-Sell Agreement is brought on behalf of ALG, which is neither alleged to be a party to those agreements, nor a third-party beneficiary of that agreement. 

 

To the contrary, the Complaint alleges, “Hofer and ATS, and ALG as a third-party beneficiary, on the one hand, and Boladian on the other hand, entered into the [Operating Agreement] and the Buy-Sell.”  (Complaint ¶ 29.)  Further, the allegations regarding the Operating Agreement specifically reference ALG: “ATS and ALG shall jointly … ensure that all ATS and ALG marketing decisions are made in an acceptable way that is consistent with the Aerlex brand and messaging and does not harm ATS, Hofer, ALG, or Boladian.” (Complaint at ¶ 14(f); Ex. A at ¶ 5.8.)  Whether ALG is in fact a third-party beneficiary entitled to enforce the Operating Agreement and/or the Buy-Sell Agreement are factual determinations to be made at later stages of the litigation. 

 

Plaintiff next argues that the non-compete clause of the Buy-Sell Agreement was not triggered, because the Complaint does not allege Boladian sold her interests in ATS LLC or ATS LLP; rather, the Complaint alleges ATS LLC was dissolved in or around March 2023 (Complaint ¶¶ 4, 17), and ATS LLP was dissolved by operation of law in or around September 15, 2023, when Boladian withdrew from the partnership (Complaint ¶ 20.) 

 

The Buy-Sell Agreement pertains to the “transfer” of a party’s membership interest in ATS.  “Transfer” is defined as “sell, assign, convey, donate, gift, bequeath, transfer or otherwise dispose of (other than by an Involuntary Transfer) or contract to transfer any of a Member's Interest.”  (Ex. B to Complaint at p.3.)  “Involuntary Transfer” is defined as “any transaction, proceeding or action by or in which a Member shall be deprived or divested of any right, title or interest in or to any of his or her Interest, including, without limiting the generality of the foregoing, seizure under levy of attachment or execution, transfer in connection with bankruptcy or other court proceeding to a trustee in bankruptcy or receiver or other officer or agent.”  (Ibid.) A Member’s “Interest” in ATS is defined as “(i) that Member's share of the profits and losses of ATS and the right to receive distributions of ATS's assets (as defined above as the Distribution Percentage) and (ii) that Member's right to participate in the management and operation of the business and affairs of ATS, including but not limited to the right to vote on, consent to, or otherwise participate in any decision or action of or by the Members granted pursuant to this Buy-Sell Agreement, the Operating Agreement, or the Act.”  (Ibid.)

 

Because the contractual terms are not immediately clear, ultimately whether dissolving ATS LLC or Boladian’s voluntary withdrawal from ATS LLP constitutes a voluntary “Transfer” triggering the noncompete clause or an “Involuntary Transfer” that does not, is a factual question to be resolved at later stages of the litigation.

 

Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the second cause of action.

 

3.     Fifth and Sixth Causes of Action – Intentional Misrepresentation

 

“To establish a claim for fraudulent misrepresentation, the plaintiff must prove: ‘(1) the defendant represented to the plaintiff that an important fact was true; (2) that representation was false; (3) the defendant knew that the representation was false when the defendant made it, or the defendant made the representation recklessly and without regard for its truth; (4) the defendant intended that the plaintiff rely on the representation; (5) the plaintiff reasonably relied on the representation; (6) the plaintiff was harmed; and (7) the plaintiff's reliance on the defendant's representation was a substantial factor in causing that harm to the plaintiff.”  (Graham v. Bank of America, N.A. (2014) 226 Cal.App.4th 594, 605–606.)  To plead with adequate particularity, the complainant must “show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Charpentier v. Los Angeles Rams Football Co., Inc. (1999) 75 Cal.App.4th 301, 312.)

 

“In California, fraud must be pled specifically; general and conclusory allegations do not suffice.”  (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996) 12 Cal.4th 631, 645.)  “This particularity requirement necessitates pleading facts which show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered.”  (Ibid.) 

 

“One of the purposes of the specificity requirement is notice to the defendant, to furnish the defendant with certain definite charges which can be intelligently met.”  (Alfaro v. Community Housing Improvement System & Planning Assn., Inc. (2009) 171 Cal.App.4th 1356, 1384.)  As such, less specificity is required “when it appears from the nature of the allegations that the defendant must necessarily possess full information concerning the facts of the controversy[.]”  (Ibid.)  “Even under the strict rules of common law pleading, one of the canons was that less particularity is required when the facts lie more in the knowledge of the opposite party.”  (Ibid.)

 

The Complaint alleges:

 

16. The parties continued their partnership in ATS for nearly three years following the settlement, during which time they generally adhered to the terms of the 2020 Agreements. However, at some point during this time and unbeknownst to Hofer, Boladian concocted a plan to extricate herself both from the 2020 Agreements and from the partnership with Hofer – not by having an honest and forthright conversation with Hofer about her desire to end or modify the partnership, but by engaging in an underhanded and deceitful scheme to steal 100% of the company and its assets for herself.

 

17. The first step of Boladian’s plan was to convert ATS from an LLC to an LLP. In or around March 2023, Boladian approached Hofer and convinced him that she was illegally practicing law as an LLC, so she needed to change the company’s corporate form and convert it from an LLC to an LLP.[2] Boladian represented to Hofer that the dissolution of the LLC and the formation of the LLP would affect neither the parties’ ownership interests in ATS, nor the terms and conditions of the ATS Operating Agreement or Buy-Sell Agreement. Hofer confirmed that understanding in writing, and when he co-signed the Plan of Dissolution, he wrote that he was doing so “on the understanding that our intent is to retain the existing structure and intent of our various prior agreements.”

 

18. The second step of Boladian’s plan was to secretly form her own law firm, Boladian Aviation, which she did in August 2023, and she immediately began surreptitiously transferring information, property, and manpower from ATS to her new firm.

 

19. For the third step of her plan, Boladian drained ATS’s bank accounts by making unplanned member distributions to herself and Hofer on September 6, 2023. This was inconsistent with the parties’ rule of always maintaining a minimum of $200,000-$250,000 in the ATS operating account. In fact, Boladian left just enough in the operating account to cover one month’s rent and the ATS employees’ September 15 payroll before making them employees of Boladian Aviation.

 

20. When Hofer caught wind of what was going on, Boladian immediately executed on the final stage of her plan and informed him on September 12th that she was withdrawing from ATS LLP effective September 15, 2023, hence dissolving the partnership by operation of law. She quickly siphoned off the remainder of ATS’s personnel, clients, information, property and assets to her new firm. In fact, the only thing of substance she left in ATS was Hofer’s personal guaranty of ATS’s two-year commercial lease, which Boladian abandoned without informing him or even the landlord. In fact, despite representing to Hofer that she was leaving “55% of the furniture and equipment” in ATS’s office, she took everything but a table, a few chairs, some computer room equipment, and miscellaneous litter scattered throughout the space. She even took the physical files owned by ATS and boxes of files belonging to ALG.

 

21. Because the dissolution of ATS LLC was fraudulently induced, the dissolution is void and all agreements to which ATS LLC was a party and/or beneficiary remain in effect.

 

22. Even if ATS LLC were legally dissolved, the parties intended that the LLP would serve as a successor-in-interest to the LLC and that all agreements to which the LLC was a party would apply equally to the LLP as though the latter was expressly named therein. Indeed, the agreements each specify that they are binding upon the signatories’ successors and assigns. For this reason, Plaintiffs will hereafter refer to ATS LLC and ATS LLP collectively as “ATS.”

 

[…]

 

44. Boladian represented to Plaintiffs (and others) in writings sent from March to August 2023, that she was forming ATS LLP as a successor to ATS LLC, to continue the business of ATS, and that she would continue to abide by the 2020 Agreements.

 

45. Boladian’s representation were false. She had no intention of continuing the business of ATS, nor any intention of abiding by the 2020 Agreements. In fact, she fraudulently orchestrated the conversion of the LLC to an LLP, knowing that her planned withdrawal from the LLP would have the effect of dissolving that entity by operation of law.

 

46. Boladian knew that the aforementioned representations were false when she made them, and she intended that Plaintiffs rely on the representations.

 

47. Plaintiffs reasonably relied on Boladian’s representations and, believing them to be true, agreed to dissolve ATS LLC and form ATS LLP.

 

48. Plaintiffs were harmed as a result of Boladian’s fraud, and their reliance on her false representations was a substantial factor in causing their harm.

 

49. In doing the things herein alleged, Boladian acted with oppression, fraud, and malice. Boladian’s conduct was intended to cause injury to Plaintiffs and was despicable conduct carried out with a willful and conscious disregard of their rights. Boladian’s conduct was also deceitful, and Boladian concealed material facts with an intention to deprive Plaintiffs of their property, legal rights, and otherwise cause injury, such that an award of punitive damages is appropriate.

 

(Complaint ¶¶ 19-22, 44-49.)

 

            Defendants argue this fails to allege with requisite specificity any false statements Boladian made to Hofer.  Specifically, Defendants point out that Boladian’s alleged statements that ATS LLC was illegally practicing law as an LLC was true when made, as Corporations Code sections 13401 et seq. specifically prohibits the provision of professional legal services as an LLC.  The Court notes that a limited liability company may not render “professional services,” which including legal services, in California.  (Corp. Code, §§ 17701.04, subd. (e); 13401; 13401.3.)  As such, that statement appears true when made.

 

However, Boladian also allegedly told Hofer “that the dissolution of the LLC and the formation of the LLP would affect neither the parties’ ownership interests in ATS, nor the terms and conditions of the ATS Operating Agreement or Buy-Sell Agreement” and “Hofer confirmed that understanding in writing, and when he co-signed the Plan of Dissolution, he wrote that he was doing so “on the understanding that our intent is to retain the existing structure and intent of our various prior agreements.” (Complaint ¶ 17.)  These statements appear to have been false when made, as Boladian, within six months of making that statement to Hofer, utilized ATS’s new partnership structure to avoid the non-compete provision of the Buy-Sell Agreement. 

 

Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the fifth cause of action.

 

4.     Sixth Cause of Action – Fraudulent Inducement

 

Similarly, with regard to the sixth cause of action, the Complaint alleges, “Based upon Boladian’s representations, supposedly based upon legitimate legal advice from her counsel, that the only possible solution was a change in structure from an LLC to an LLP, and based upon her representations that the change would not substantively affect any of the rights, duties and obligations of the parties as set forth in the 2020 Agreements, Hofer agreed to execute a Plan of Dissolution for ATS LLC.”  (Complaint ¶ 52.) 

 

Thus, while the statement that it was illegal to provide legal services as an LLC appears true when made, Boladian’s statements that “the only possible solution was a change in structure from an LLC to an LLP” (as opposed to a professional corporation or other possible corporate restructuring) and that “the change would not substantively affect any of the rights, duties, and obligations of the parties as set forth in the 2020 Agreements” appear knowingly false when made, as Boladian utilized the new partnership structure to attempt to avoid the noncompete provisions of the Buy-Sell Agreement within six months of making those statements.

 

Therefore, the Court similarly denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the sixth cause of action. 

 

5.     Eighth Cause of Action – Conversion

 

“Conversion is the wrongful exercise of dominion over the property of another.  The elements of a conversion claim are: (1) the plaintiff's ownership or right to possession of the property; (2) the defendant's conversion by a wrongful act or disposition of property rights; and (3) damages.”  (Welco Electronics, Inc. v. Mora (2014) 223 Cal.App.4th 202, 208.) 

 

The Complaint alleges:

 

62. Plaintiffs owned, possessed, and had a right to possess certain property. Hofer owned, possessed and had a right to 55 % of ATS. ATS owned, possessed and/or had a right to its personal property, client relationships, employee relationships, data, information, digital and physical files, funds and assets.

 

63. Defendants substantially interfered with Plaintiffs’ property by knowingly or intentionally taking control and/or possession of the property belonging to Plaintiffs.

 

64. Plaintiffs did not consent to Defendants’ conduct and were and continue to be damaged thereby in an amount to be determined.

 

(Complaint ¶¶ 62-64.) 

 

            Defendants contend these allegations are insufficient because during the wind-up process, Boladian distributed Hofer’s 55% interest in ATS to him.  The Court disagrees.  Plaintiffs have alleged Defendants wrongfully took clients, employees, computers, files, etc. from ATS, and continue to provide legal services to those clients while retaining 100% of the profits. 

 

            Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the eighth cause of action.

 

6.     Ninth Cause of Action – Intentional Interference with Contractual Relations

 

“To prevail on a cause of action for intentional interference with contractual relations, a plaintiff must plead and prove (1) the existence of a valid contract between the plaintiff and a third party; (2) the defendant's knowledge of that contract; (3) the defendant's intentional acts designed to induce a breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; (4) actual breach or disruption of the contractual relationship; and (5) resulting damage.”  (Reeves v. Hanlon (2004) 33 Cal.4th 1140, 1148.)

 

The Complaint alleges:

 

67. Until September 2023, ALG and ATS acted as “sister companies” and shared many of the same clients, with whom each had contractual relationships.

 

68. Boladian and Boladian Aviation knew of the aforementioned contractual relationships. Boladian and Boladian Aviation wished to interfere with the contracts by inducing the clients to terminate their relationships with ALG and/or ATS and enter into new contracts with Boladian Aviation.

 

69. While still a managing member of ATS and a contract employee for ALG, and while bound by the 2020 Agreements, Boladian actively solicited ATS and ALG clients to her new law firm. Boladian informed ATS and ALG clients that she was leaving ATS and starting her own firm, Boladian Aviation. She stated: “All of the employees of the Aerlex Tax Team have decided to join me at Boladian Law. It would be a pleasure to keep servicing your aviation tax needs and there will be a smooth transition in your current projects should you decide to join me as a client of Boladian Law. If you do decide to continue to retain me in your representation, please include in your email response that any existing retainer (if any) be transferred from the Aerlex Tax Services trust account into the Boladian Aviation Law Group trust account. We will be sending new engagement agreements in the upcoming week. Please advise of your decision at your earliest convenience, so that I can ensure your matters are properly handled.”

 

70. When a client failed to respond, Boladian sent a follow up email marked “URGENT” and stating, “Tomorrow is our last day at the firm. Please advise if you would like us to continue representing your aviation tax matters.” 71. Plaintiffs were harmed as a result of Defendants’ interference with their contractual relationships in an amount to be determined. Boladian and Boladian Aviation’s conduct was a substantial factor in causing Plaintiffs’ harm.

 

(Complaint at ¶¶ 67-70.)

 

Defendants contend these allegations fail because Plaintiffs fail to identify the specific contracts and the third parties with whom they contracted.  The Court disagrees.  The Complaint alleges that Defendants interfered with Plaintiffs’ contractual relationships with their clients by soliciting those clients to leave ATS and/or ALG and become clients of Boladian Law Firm instead.  Under these circumstances, Plaintiffs are not required to plead the specific name and date of each client, especially where, as here, such client information is equally known to Defendants.

 

Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the ninth cause of action.

 

7.     Seventh, Tenth, and Eleventh Causes of Action – Wrongful Dissociation (Corp., § 16602), Computer Data Access and Fraud Act (Cal. Penal, § 502), and Receiving Stolen Property (Cal. Penal, § 496)

 

Corporations Code section 16602, subdivision (b) provides, “A partner’s dissociation is wrongful only if […] It is in breach of an express provision of the partnership agreement.”

 

Defendants first argue that the seventh cause of action for wrongful dissociation, pursuant to Corporations Code section 16602 fails because the Complaint does not identify any provisions of the partnership agreement or operating agreement that Boladian breached.  Here, the Complaint alleges:

 

14. The ATS Amended and Restated Operating Agreement (the “OA”) entered into among Boladian, Hofer, and ATS on or about September 22, 2020 (Exhibit A) provides, among other things, that:

 

a) “Neither the Managers nor any Member shall take any action inconsistent with the express intent of the parties to this Agreement.” (Exh. A, ¶ 2.6)

 

b) “Although both Initial Members are Managers, Boladian shall have sole decision-making power with regard to the management of the day-to-day operations of the Company … with such decisions to be made in good faith, with reasonable judgment, and in the best interests of the Company and its Members.” (Id., ¶ 5.1)

 

c) “Boladian shall promptly inform Hofer in advance of any major decisions that affect the Company, or any decisions that affect Hofer personally or his law practice.” (Id., ¶ 5.1)

 

d) “The Members may engage in any lawful business activities, provided that neither the Members nor their affiliates shall engage in such activities which are in competition with those of the Company. A business activity shall be deemed to be in competition with the Company if such activity is of the same type and in the same geographic area as the business of the Company as it may exist from time to time.” (Id., ¶ 5.4)

 

e) “The Managers shall cause all assets of the Company, whether real or personal, to be held in the name of the Company.” (Id., ¶ 5.6)

 

f) “ATS and ALG shall jointly … ensure that all ATS and ALG marketing decisions are made in an acceptable way that is consistent with the Aerlex brand and messaging and does not harm ATS, Hofer, ALG, or Boladian.” (Id., ¶ 5.8)

 

g) “…[P]roposed actions concerning the Company’s business shall be made by a Vote of the Managers. An action shall be approved if both Managers Vote in favor of such action. Votes of the Managers shall be taken at meetings or as otherwise provided herein.” (Id., ¶ 5.1)

i) “A Member’s withdrawal from the Company shall not release that Member from any obligations and liabilities under this Agreement accrued or incurred before the effective date of withdrawal. A withdrawing Member shall divest the Member’s entire Membership Interest before the effective date of withdrawal in accordance and subject to the provisions of that certain Amended and Restated Buy-Sell Agreement of Aerlex Tax Services, LLC … the terms of which are incorporated herein by this reference.” (Id., ¶ 8.1)

31. Boladian breached the OA and the Buy-Sell by failing to do the things required thereunder, and also by doing things prohibited by the OA and Buy-Sell, including, inter alia:

 

a) Failing to make management decisions of ATS in good faith, with reasonable judgment, and in the best interests of ATS and Hofer;

 

b) Failing to promptly inform Hofer in advance of major decisions affecting ATS, Hofer and ALG, including her decision to withdraw from ATS; to convert Hofer’s ownership interest in ATS; and to open a law firm to directly compete with ALG, including by stealing ATS and ALG clients;

 

c) Competing with ATS by engaging in activity of the same type and in the same geographic area;

 

d) Converting ATS assets to Boladian Aviation;

 

e) Failing to make marketing decisions that were consistent with the Aerlex brand and that avoided harm to ATS, Hofer and ALG.

 

f) Failing to hold a vote of the managers before taking major actions concerning ATS’s business and unilaterally taking actions that required a unanimous vote of the members, including actions that would make it impossible to carry on the ordinary business of the company and the disposition of all or substantially all of the company’s assets;

 

g) Withdrawing from ATS without complying with the terms of the Buy-Sell or divesting her membership interest prior to the effective date of the withdrawal;

 

h) Refusing to notify Hofer in advance of her plan to divest of her membership interest in ATS;

 

i) Refusing to offer Hofer the opportunity to acquire her membership interest in ATS and refusing to sell it to him in accordance with the terms of the Buy-Sell and the 2023 Certificate of Agreed Value; and,

 

j) Refusing to abide by the terms of the non-compete and non-solicitation provisions of the Buy-Sell.

 

Therefore, the Court denies Defendant’s motion for judgment as to the seventh cause of action.

 

Defendants also argue that the Tenth and Eleventh causes of action for violations of Penal Code sections 496 and 502 fail because as a part owner of ATS with control over the day-to-day operations, Boladian was authorized to access ATS’s servers and computers and did not steal any property.

 

Penal Code section 502, subdivision (c) provides:

 

(c) Except as provided in subdivision (h), any person who commits any of the following acts is guilty of a public offense:

 

(1) Knowingly accesses and without permission alters, damages, deletes, destroys, or otherwise uses any data, computer, computer system, or computer network in order to either (A) devise or execute any scheme or artifice to defraud, deceive, or extort, or (B) wrongfully control or obtain money, property, or data.

 

(2) Knowingly accesses and without permission takes, copies, or makes use of any data from a computer, computer system, or computer network, or takes or copies any supporting documentation, whether existing or residing internal or external to a computer, computer system, or computer network.

 

(3) Knowingly and without permission uses or causes to be used computer services.

 

Subdivision (h) of Section 502 exempts acts “committed by a person within the scope of lawful employment. For purposes of this section, a person acts within the scope of employment when the person performs acts which are reasonably necessary to the performance of their work assignment.”  Similarly, Penal Code section 496 provides, “A principal in the actual theft of the property may be convicted pursuant to this section.” 

 

As discussed above, although Boladian had permission to access the ATS servers, Plaintiffs allege that Defendants knowingly accessed and without permission used the data to devise and execute a scheme to defraud Plaintiffs and wrongfully control or obtain their money, property, and data, including client information. 

 

Therefore, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment as to the tenth and eleventh causes of action.

 

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

 

For the foregoing reasons, the Court denies Defendants’ motion for judgment on the pleadings in its entirety.
