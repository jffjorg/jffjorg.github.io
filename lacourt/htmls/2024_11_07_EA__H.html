DEPARTMENT H LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23PSCP00030    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: H

Castro v. City of Pomona, et al., Case No. 23PSCP00030


ORDER ON PETITION FOR WRIT OF MANDATE

Plaintiff Andrew Castro’s Complaint for Declaratory and Injunctive Relief and Verified Petition for Writ of Mandate is DENIED.

 

I.        Background

Plaintiff Andrew Castro (“Castro”) is Arthur Martinez’s (“Martinez”) nephew. Martinez was murdered in 1973. On November 15, 2021, Castro made a California Public Records Act request to the City of Pomona (the “City”) and the Pomona Police Department (the “Department”) seeking the disclosure of “all investigative reports including supplemental reports, all audio recordings, pictures, and physical evidence” pertaining to Martinez’s murder. The City denied Castro’s request on November 16, 2022.

On January 19, 2023, Castro filed a “Complaint for Declaratory and Injunctive Relief and Verified Petition for Writ of Mandate” (“Complaint/Petition”) against the City, the Department, and Does 1-10. For the reasons stated herein, Castor’s petition is denied.
 

II.      Legal Standard 

Individual citizens have a right to access government records pursuant to the California Public Records Act (the “Act”) (Gov. Code § 7921.000, et seq.).[FN1] In enacting the Act, the California Legislature determined that “access to information concerning the conduct of the people’s business is a fundamental and necessary right of every person in this state.” (Gov. Code, § 7921.000.)

Further, Article 1, Section 3(b) of the California Constitution affirms that “[t]he people have the right of access to information concerning the conduct of the people’s business” and that “[a] statute . . . shall be broadly construed if it furthers the people’s right of access, and narrowly construed if it limits the right of access.”

The Act “does not allow limitations on access to a public record based upon the purpose for which the record is being requested, if the record is otherwise subject to disclosure.” (Gov. Code § 7921.300.) “Any reasonably segregable portion of a record shall be available for inspection by any person requesting the record after deletion of the portions that are exempted by law.” (Gov. Code § 7922.525(b).) 

“To establish an agency has a duty to disclose under [the Act], the petitioner must show that: (1) the record qualif[ies] as [a] public record[ ] …; and (2) the record is in the possession of the agency.”  (Anderson-Barker v Sup.Ct. (2019) 31 Cal.App.5th 528, 538 [quotations and citation omitted].) Once that initial burden is met, the agency has the burden to prove “that a particular record is exempt from disclosure.”  (Sacramento County Employees’ Retirement System v. Superior Court (2013) 195 Cal.App.4th 440, 453.)  

 

III.     Discussion

Castro seeks a writ of mandate directing the City and the Department to comply with the Act and produce all public documents, including, but not limited to “all investigative reports including supplemental reports, all audio recordings, pictures, and physical evidence.” (Complaint/Petition, 5:3-6.) In the alternative, Castro seeks the issuance of an order to show cause why the Court should not issue a writ. Castro also seeks a judicial declaration that the City and the Department violated the Act by failing to produce the requested documents.[FN2] Castro further seeks nominal damages and an award of costs.

Castro’s uncle, Martinez, was murdered in 1973. (Complaint/Petition, 1:21-25.) In the verified Complaint/Petition, Castro alleges that the Department investigated the case for years and knew who murdered Martinez but was unable to gather sufficient evidence for the District Attorney’s office to be able to file the case. (Id., 1:25-27.) Castro alleges that he contacted the Department in 2004 “to request that the case be reopened” and that he worked closely with Department detectives “[f]or the past 18 years” investigating the case. (Id., 2:4-5.) Castro alleges that the suspect, Michael Andrade (“Andrade”), passed away before murder charges could be filed against him. (Id., 2:10-12.) Castro alleges that after learning Andrade had passed away, he asked Department Detective Rodriguez (“Rodriguez”) if he could have the whole case file and evidence pertaining to the case and was subsequently told by “Matt,” a supervisor from the records department at the Department, that while the Department was willing to release everything in Castro’s request it “had to be done legally through the courts.” (Id., 2:13-17.) Castro alleges that on November 15, 2021, he made request under the Act for “all investigative reports including supplemental reports, all audio recordings, pictures and physical evidence, and that the City denied his request on November 16, 2022. (Id., 3:21-27.) 

In opposition to the Complaint/Petition, the City and the Department submit a declaration from Rodriguez, a Department police officer who has been serving as a homicide detective for the past four years and who was involved in the investigation of Martinez’s murder between approximately 2020 and 2022. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶¶ 2 and 3.) Rodriguez represents that, over the years, the Department named Andrade as a suspect and gathered circumstantial evidence that led it to believe that Andrade committed the murder, that the Department brought its evidence to the District Attorney’s office in different counties on multiple occasions, and that each time the District Attorney’s office declined to file the case on the grounds of insufficient evidence, including in 2022, when he personally requested that the case be filed. (Id., ¶ 3.) Rodriguez confirms that the investigation of Martinez’s murder has not been closed and that a homicide case may remain open indefinitely. (Id., ¶ 4.) 

Castro argues that disclosure of the records is governed by Government Code section 7923.605. The City and the Department, in turn, contend that the records sought by Castro are exempt from disclosure pursuant to Government Code sections 7923.600, 7923.605, 7923.610, and 7922.000. 

Government Code section 7923.605 provides, in relevant part, that notwithstanding Section 7923.600,[FN3] “a state or local law enforcement agency shall disclose the names and addresses of persons involved in, or witnesses other than confidential informants to, the incident, the description of any property involved, the date, time, and location of the incident, all diagrams, statements of the parties involved in the incident, the statements of all witnesses, other than confidential informants, to the victims of an incident, or an authorized representative thereof, . . . unless the disclosure would endanger” (1) the “safety of a witness or other person involved in the investigation” or (2) the “successful completion of the investigation or a related investigation.”

The City and the Department contend that the records Castro requests are “per se investigatory records” exempt under Government Code section 7923.600 (Opp., 4:6-7 and 16-17) and that this exemption “extends indefinitely, even after an investigation is closed.” (Rivero v. Superior Court (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 1048, 1052).[FN4] They further posit that disclosure of the records is prohibited by Government Code section 7923.605, because it would “endanger both the safety of witnesses and other individuals involved, as well as hinder successful completion of the investigation.” (Id., 5:3-5.)

Rodriguez represents that, while the Department “sometimes allows a family member to review certain aspects of [a] [homicide] case file at the station,” it “does not have a practice of turning over an entire homicide file case file to any individual.” (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 5.) He attests that “turning over an entire homicide case file may endanger the victim’s family and witnesses or subject the [suspect’s] family to retaliation,” or “may affect the integrity of the investigation and the case itself, or may hinder successful resolution of the case.” (Id.) He notes that the disclosure of the names of potential suspects, or information known about them potentially linking them to the crime could be used to alert a suspect or result in evidence being hidden or destroyed,” and could “dissuade individuals with information from coming forward.” (Id.) He represents that such “concerns exist even if there has only ever been one suspect in a case,” as “[t]here is always a chance that the police department is incorrect or that new evidence may emerge.” (Id.) 

Neither Uribe v. Howie (1971) 19 Cal.App.3d 194 nor Black Panther Party v. Kehoe (1974) 42 Cal.App.3d 645 supports Castro’s position that he is entitled to the Department’s investigatory files of Martinez’s murder. In Uribe, the petitioner was refused permission to inspect monthly pesticide spray reports submitted to the county agricultural commissioner to determine whether she had been exposed to pesticides. The trial court held that these records were exempt from disclosure because they were reports of a local agency compiled for correctional, law enforcement, or licensing purposes within the meaning of former Government Code section 6254, subdivision (f). The appellate court, however, determined that only reports that “were currently being used to investigate” a pesticide applicator would be exempt. In Black Panther Party, the plaintiffs sought disclosure of letters of complaint alleging unethical or abusive practices by licensed collection agencies, which the trial court deemed were exempt under former Government Code section 6254, subdivision (f). The appellate court rejected the plaintiffs’ argument (based on Uribe) that the complaints were not the subject of current investigations and were thus subject to disclosure; in doing so, the appellate court noted that “[i]n Uribe, the record in question was not a complaint but a routine report in a public file. It could gain exemption not because of its content but because of the use to which it was put, that is, when and if it became part of an investigatory file. Here, by their very content, the documents are independently entitled to exemption as ‘records of complaints;’ their exemption is not dependent upon the creation of an investigatory file.” (Id. at 654.)

Here, Castro seeks the Department’s investigation files, not just complaints or reports that were not originally created for investigatory purposes or that might lead to an investigation at some later date. Martinez’s murder case file, moreover, is not merely labeled “investigatory,” but is being used in the Department’s investigation.

The City and the Department further argue that the records are exempt under Government Code section 7923.610, to the extent Castro seeks any arrest information related to Martinez’s murder. Section 7923.610 requires a local law enforcement agency to disclose certain arrest information, such as the name and occupation of every individual arrested by the agency, the individual’s physical description, the time, date and location of arrest, the time and date of booking, the factual circumstances surrounding the arrest, and such other information, unless “disclosure of a particular item of information would endanger the safety of a person involved in an investigation or would endanger the successful completion of the investigation or a related investigation.” As noted in Rodriguez’s declaration, the disclosing investigatory records in an open case poses a danger to the safety of witnesses, the integrity of the investigation, and the successful completion of the investigation. (Rodriguez Decl., ¶ 5.) 

Finally, the City and the Department contend that the records are exempt from disclosure pursuant to Government Code section 7922.000, which permits a public agency to justify withholding any record by demonstrating “that on the facts of the particular case the public interest served by not disclosing the record clearly outweighs the public interest served by disclosure of the record.” “[A] public interest is not the same as a private interest. . . just because a member of the public has an interest in something that does not necessarily make that interest one of public concern.” (Los Angeles Unified School Dist. v. Superior Court (2014) 228 Cal.App.4th 222, 240.) Castro’s interest in disclosure here is private and personal in nature based on his familial relationship to Martinez; in contrast, there is a strong public interest against disclosing the investigatory records, as noted above.

Under the circumstances of this case, Castro has not established that the City and the Department failed to disclose any non-exempt, responsive public records or information. The petition for writ of mandate is thus denied. The Court further declines to issue an order to show cause or to issue declaratory and/or injunctive relief because it finds that the City and the Department have complied with the Act. The Court denies Castro’s request for nominal damages for the same reason. Finally, the Court denies Castro’s request for costs because he did not prevail in this action. (Gov. Code § 7923.115, subd. (a).)

 

[FN1] The Act was previously codified as Government Code section 6250 et seq. and was recently recodified and reorganized without substantive change effective January 1, 2023.

[FN2] The Court is not in possession of Castro’s mother’s notarized affidavit, nor of Castro’s November 15, 2021 public records request or the City’s November 16, 2022 denial, which are referenced in the Complaint/Petition as attached as Exhibits 1 through 3, respectively. The Court’s analysis is premised on the assumption that Castro’s public records request sought “all investigative reports including supplemental reports, all audio recordings, pictures, and physical evidence,” as represented.

[FN3] 7923.600, subdivision (a) provides, in relevant part, as follows: “[T]his division does not require the disclosure of records of complaints to, or investigations conducted by, . . . any state or local police agency, . . . for . . . law enforcement . . . purposes.”

[FN4] Rodriguez confirms that the investigation has not closed. (Rodriguez Decl., 2:22-23.)

Case Number: 24PSCV00312    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: H

Pusey, et al. v. Emanate Health Foothill Presbyterian Hospital,
Case No., 24PSCV00312

ORDER ON MOTION TO COMPEL

Defendant Emanate Health Foothill Presbyterian Hospital’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs to Respond to Form Interrogatories, Set No. One is GRANTED. Plaintiffs are ordered to provide responses, without objections, to Emanate Health Foothill Presbyterian Hospital’s Form Interrogatories, Set No. One, within 30 days from the date of the notice of ruling. Sanctions are awarded in the reduced amount of $412.50 and are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.[FN]

 

I.        Background 

Plaintiffs Sandra Miranda Pusey, Erika Melissa Pusey, and Cassandra Marie Morales (together, “Plaintiffs”) allege that after being admitted to Defendant Emanate Health Foothill Presbyterian (“Defendant”) for treatment for adverse reactions to a medication, on November 9, 2022, Elwood Thomas Pusey was moved while he was undergoing dialysis and died the same day. 

On January 31, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a complaint, asserting a medical malpractice cause of action against Defendant and Does 1-50. 

Pending before the Court is Defendant’s motion to compel responses to form interrogatories. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted.

II.      Legal Standard

A response to interrogatories is due 30 days after service. (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.260, subd. (a).) “If a party to whom interrogatories are directed fails to serve a timely response, . . . [t]he party propounding the interrogatories may move for an order compelling response to the interrogatories.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (b).) 

“The court shall impose a monetary sanction . . . against any party, person, or attorney who unsuccessfully makes or opposes a motion to compel a response to interrogatories, unless it finds that the one subject to the sanction acted with substantial justification or that other circumstances make the imposition of the sanction unjust . . .” (Code Civ. Proc., § 2030.290, subd. (c).)


III.     Discussion

Defendant moves the Court for an order compelling Plaintiffs to provide verified responses, without objections, to Defendant’s form and special interrogatories. Defendant also seeks sanctions in the amount of $1,470.00. 

At the outset, the motion has been partially withdrawn (i.e., as it pertains to Defendant’s Special Interrogatories, Set No. One), pursuant to a notice filed by Defendant on October 29, 2024. The following analysis thus pertains only to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories, Set No. One. 

Defendant’s counsel Evan R. Garcia (“Garcia”) represents as follows:

On May 15, 2024, Defendant served Plaintiffs with the subject discovery. (Garcia Decl., ¶ 2, Exh. A.) Responses were due on June 14, 2024. (Id., ¶ 2.) On June 11, 2024, Plaintiffs’ counsel requested, and received, a 30-day extension, such that responses were due on July 17, 2024. (Id., ¶ 3, Exh. B.) On July 18, 2024, Defendant’s counsel reached out to Plaintiffs’ counsel to ask about the status of the now-overdue responses and agreed to give Plaintiffs’ counsel another extension to August 16, 2024. (Id., ¶ 4, Exh. C.) No responses were received. (Id., ¶ 4.) On September 6, 2024, Defendant’s counsel wrote a letter to Plaintiffs’ counsel in an attempt to obtain the overdue responses, without objections, extending the deadline to September 16, 2024. (Id., ¶ 5, Exh. D.) On September 23, 2024, Defendant’s counsel emailed Plaintiffs’ counsel to remind him that Defendant had not received responses. (Id., ¶ 6, Exh. E.) Defendant’s counsel received no response; as such, the instant motion was filed. (Id., ¶ 6.) 

Plaintiffs, in opposition, represent that they “will serve their responses to form interrogatories prior to the hearing on the instant motion and will file with the Court a declaration commensurate with the service of the responses to the form interrogatories.” (Opp., 1:26-28; see Leech Decl., ¶ 5.) 

There is no indication that Plaintiffs have provided verified responses to the subject discovery, without objections and/or that Defendant has withdrawn the motion; as such, the motion is granted. Plaintiffs are ordered to provide responses, without objections, to Defendant’s Form Interrogatories Sets No. One, within 30 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

 

Sanctions 

Defendant seeks sanctions against Plaintiffs in the amount of $1,470.00 [calculated as follows: 2 hours preparing motion, plus 1 hour preparing reply, plus 3 hours traveling to/attending hearing at $235.00/hour, plus $60.00 filing fee]. 

Utilizing a Lodestar approach, and in view of the totality of the circumstances, the Court finds that the total and reasonable amount of attorney’s fees and costs incurred for the work performed in connection with the pending motion is $412.50 (i.e., 1 hour attorney time at $235.00/hour, plus 0.5 hours appearance time (split with Motion #2), plus $60.00 filing fee). Sanctions are payable within 20 days from the date of the notice of ruling.

   

[FN] Defendant’s Motion to Compel Plaintiffs to Respond to Special Interrogatories, Set No. One has been withdrawn, pursuant to an October 29, 2024 notice to the Court.  

Case Number: 24PSCV01437    Hearing Date: November 7, 2024    Dept: H

Pimentel v. Rentokil North America, Inc., et al., Case No. 24PSCV01437

ORDER ON PRO HAC VICE APPLICATION

Defendants Rentokil North America, Inc. and Richard Thongsavanh’s Application for Russell W. Jackson to Appear Pro Hac Vice is GRANTED.

Background

In this wrongful termination case, plaintiff Jose Antonio Pimentel (“Plaintiff”) asserts several causes of action against his former employer, Rentokil North America, Inc. (“Rentokil”), and his former manager, Richard Thongsavanh (“Thongsavanh”), in connection with Plaintiff’s brain cancer diagnosis.  Specifically, Plaintiff alleges that before being diagnosed with cancer, he worked as a pest control technician for Rentokil but never received the proper training for the handling of hazardous chemicals, even though his manager falsely certified that Plaintiff had received the requisite training.  Plaintiff further alleges that after being diagnosed with cancer and while he was undergoing chemotherapy, Rentokil unlawfully discriminated against him and terminated him based on his medical condition. 

The First Amendment Complaint asserts several causes of action against Rentokil, Thongsavanh, and Does 1 through 10, including, among others, discrimination, retaliation, wrongful termination, failure to provide overtime wages, unlawful and unfair business practices, and negligent hiring, supervising, and retention.

On August 5, 2024, Guadalupe Pimentel (“G. Pimentel”) was substituted in as Plaintiff pursuant to a motion granted by the Court.

A Case Management Conference is set for January 9, 2025.

Pending before the Court is Rentokil and Thongsavanh’s unopposed application for Russell W. Jackson to appear pro hac vice. For the reasons stated herein, the motion is granted.

 

Legal Standard 

“A person who is not a licensee of the State Bar of California but who is an attorney in good standing of and eligible to practice before the bar of any United States court or the highest court in any state, territory, or insular possession of the United States, and who has been retained to appear in a particular cause pending in a court of this state, may in the discretion of such court be permitted upon written application to appear as counsel pro hac vice, provided that an active licensee of the State Bar of California is associated as attorney of record. No person is eligible to appear as counsel pro hac vice under this rule if the person is: (1) A resident of the State of California; (2) Regularly employed in the State of California; or (3) Regularly engaged in substantial business, professional, or other activities in the State of California.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(a).)

“A person desiring to appear as counsel pro hac vice in a superior court must file with the court a verified application together with proof of service by mail in accordance with Code of Civil Procedure section 1013a of a copy of the application and of the notice of hearing of the application on all parties who have appeared in the cause and on the State Bar of California at its San Francisco office. The notice of hearing must be given at the time prescribed in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005 unless the court has prescribed a shorter period.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(c)(1).)

“The application must state: (1) The applicant’s residence and office address;

(2) The courts to which the applicant has been admitted to practice and the dates of admission; (3) That the applicant is a licensee in good standing in those courts; (4) That the applicant is not currently suspended or disbarred in any court; (5) The title of each court and cause in which the applicant has filed an application to appear as counsel pro hac vice in this state in the preceding two years, the date of each application, and whether or not it was granted; and (6) The name, address, and telephone number of the active licensee of the State Bar of California who is attorney of record.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(d).)

“The State Bar of California may set an appropriate fee to be paid by counsel pro hac vice.” (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 9.40(e)[FN1].) 

Discussion 

Rentokil and Thongsavanh (“together, “Defendants”) move, pursuant to California Rules of Court Rule 9.40, for an order allowing the pro hac vice admission of Russell W. Jackson.

The application, coupled with Defendants’ “Notice of Filing of Proof of Service and Proof of Payment to the State Bar for Pro Hac Vice Application of Russell Jackson” filed October 1, 2024, reflect compliance with the requirements of California Rules of Court Rule 9.40. Accordingly, the application is granted.                                                              

[FN1] The fee is currently $500.00. (See https://www.calbar.ca.gov/Portals.0/documents/rules/Rules_Appendix_ASched-Chgs-Deadlines.pdf.)
