DEPARTMENT W LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20VECV01429    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: W

RICHARD JACOBSON, et al. v. VALLEY OAKS III CONDOMINIUM ASSOCIATION INC., et al.

 

plaintiffs richard jacobson and jennifer jacobson’s motion to set aside dismissal

 

Date of Hearing:        December 13, 2024                          Trial Date:       None set.  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        20VECV01429

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiffs Richard Jacobson and Jennifer Jacobson

Responding Party:     No opposition.    

 

BACKGROUND

 

This lawsuit arises out of Defendants Valley Oaks III Condominium Association, Hilda Viscarra, Wendy Duran, and Humberto Granados alleged breach of contractual obligations concerning the subject property. Plaintiffs claim Valley Oaks failed to uphold the CC&Rs of the subject property and defendant Hilda negligently allowed defendants Wendy and Humberto to violate the CC&Rs. Plaintiffs claim defendant Wendy violated the CC&Rs by allowing defendant Robert, a guest to the subject property, harass other members of the Valley Oaks III.

 

On December 7, 2020, plaintiffs filed a complaint asserting causes of action for (1) Breach of Contract; (2) Breach of Invasion of Privacy; (3) Breach of Implied Covenant of Quiet Enjoyment; (4) Violation of Davis-Sterling Act; (5) Assault; (6) Negligence; (7) Intentional/Negligent Infliction of Emotional Distress; (8) Private Nuisance; and (9) Violation of the Business and Professions Code.

 

On January 22, 2021, the instant case was related to Case No. 20VERO01790. Plaintiffs dismissed Valley Oaks III Condominium Association on November 7, 2023. Valley Oaks dismissed their cross-complaint on November 9, 2023.  

 

[Tentative] Ruling

 

Plaintiffs Richard Jacobson and Jennifer Jacobson’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is GRANTED.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Plaintiffs move the court for an order granting relief from dismissal, without prejudice, entered on May 23, 2024 on the grounds that the dismissal was taken against Plaintiffs due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise or excusable neglect by Plaintiff’s counsel. Plaintiff intends to request a default judgment as against Defendants Hilda Viscarra, Wendy Duran, and Humberto Granados.

 

“Section 473(b) provides for both discretionary and mandatory relief [from dismissal]. [Citation.]” (Pagnini v. Union Bank, N.A. (2018) 28 Cal.App.5th 298, 302.) An application for relief under this section must be made no more than six months after entry of the judgment, dismissal, order, or other proceeding from which relief is sought and must be accompanied by an affidavit of fault attesting to the mistake, inadvertence, surprise or neglect of the moving party or its attorney. (Cal. Code of Civ. Proc. § 473(b); English v. IKON Business Solutions (2001) 94 Cal.App.4th 130, 143.) Additionally, an application for relief under this section “shall be accompanied by a copy of the answer or other pleading proposed to be filed herein, otherwise the application shall not be granted.” (CCP § 473(b).) Relief under this section is mandatory when based on an attorney affidavit of fault; otherwise, it is discretionary. (Ibid.) When dismissal is the attorney’s fault, the six-month period starts to run from the date of the entry of dismissal. (CCP § 473(b); Sugasawara v. Newland (1994) 27 Cal.App.4th 294, 295.) 

 

Plaintiff first notes the request is timely. Plaintiff is making the request for relief less than six months after the dismissal was entered by the court on May 23, 2024. The court agrees. Moreover, Plaintiff argues there was attorney excusable neglect, mistake, inadvertence, and/or surprise. Plaintiff presents counsel’s declaration whereby the hearing for this matter was not calendared or communicated to Plaintiff’s counsel upon joining the firm and when Plaintiff’s counsel began working on the default judgment package, she experienced a personal family matter as well as her own personal health matter and is currently in the hospital. (Monon Decl. ¶¶4-6.) As a result, Plaintiff’s failure to appear for the hearing was a result of excusable neglect, inadvertence, surprise, and/or mistake.

 

Under the circumstances, the court finds that Plaintiff’s argument has merit.  Plaintiff has also attached their default judgment package. Accordingly, Plaintiffs Richard Jacobson and Jennifer Jacobson’s Motion to Set Aside Dismissal is GRANTED.

 

 


Case Number: 22VECV00728    Hearing Date: December 13, 2024    Dept: W

ALBERT BOYAJIAN V. SURGE GLOBAL BAKERIES PARTNERS GP, ET AL.

 

CROSS-DEFENDANTS ABLERT BOYAJIAN AND GLOBAL CHIPS, INC.’S DEMURRER TO CROSS-COMPLAINTS SURGE PRIVATE EQUITY LLC, THOMAS BEAUCHAMP, LEWIS SHARP AND SURGE GLOBAL BAKERIES PARTNERS LP’S CROSS-COMPLAINT WITH MOTION TO STRIKE

 

Date of Hearing:        December 13, 2024                          Trial Date:       January 26, 2026  

Department:              W                                                        Case No.:        22VECV00728

 

Moving Party:            Plaintiff/Cross-Defendant Albert Boyajian and Global Chips, Inc.

Responding Party:     Defendant/Cross-Complainant Surge Private Equity LLC, Thomas Beauchamp, Lewis Sharp and Surge Global Bakeries Partners LP

Meet and Confer:      Yes. (Brelje Decl. ¶¶4-9.)

 

BACKGROUND

 

On May 31, 2022, Plaintiff Albert Boyajian filed a complaint against Defendants Surge Global Bakeries Holdings LLC, Surge Global Bakeries Partners LP, Surge Global Bakeries Partners GP, LLC, Surge Private Equity LLC, Surge Private Equity, McLarty Capital Partners SBIC II, LP, The Firmament Group, Thomas Beauchamp, Lewis Sharp, Lauren Steel, Parris Boyd, Tobias Keller, Andy Burchfield, and Ashur Aghasi for (1) Limited Partner Derivative Suit; (2) Limited Partner Derivative Suit; (3) Fraudulent Concealment; (4) Breach of Contract; and (5) Breach of Contract. On August 30, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint.

 

This action involves shareholder derivative and direct claims by Plaintiff. Plaintiff alleges he sold his profitable bakery for an equity position in a parent entity, a place on the board of directors of his former bakery (now a wholly-owned subsidiary), and a Consulting Agreement. However, Plaintiff alleges he did not know, when he sold his company, that the purchase was merely the first step of a lender’s scheme to exploit the company for the lender’s benefit, entirely contrary to the best interests of the equity holders. Plaintiff contends after the purchase, the company unwarrantedly acquired an additional two companies and took on additional dramatic debt, during periods of mismanagement by inept managers and the lender who controlled the board. Moreover, since the takeover, Plaintiff alleges the lender has not given plaintiff any information on the companies; and has not called a meeting of the board.

 

On October 1, 2024, Surge Private Equity LLC, Thomas Beauchamp, Lewis Sharp and Surge Global Bakeries Partners LP filed a Cross-Complaint against Albert Boyajian and Global Chips for breach of contract, breach of implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing, fraud and deceit, and negligence.

 

[TENTATIVE] RULING:

 

Cross-Defendants Albert Boyajian and Global Chips, Inc.’s Demurrer to the Cross-Complaint is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND. Cross-Defendants Albert Boyajian and Global Chips, Inc.’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

DISCUSSION

 

Cross-Defendants Albert Boyajian and Global Chips, Inc. demur to the entirety of Cross-Complainants Surge Global Bakeries Partners, LP, Surge Private Equity, LLC, Lewis Sharp and Thomas Beauchamp’s Cross-Complaint on the grounds the Cross-Complaint fails to state facts constituting a cause of action against Cross-Defendants and is uncertain, ambiguous and unintelligible. Cross-Defendants also move to strike Cross-Complainants’ punitive damage allegations.

 

Breach of Contract

 

“The standard elements of a claim for breach of contract are: ‘(1) the contract, (2) plaintiff’s performance or excuse for nonperformance, (3) defendant’s breach, and (4) damage to plaintiff therefrom.’” (Wall Street Network, Ltd. v. New York Times Co. (2008) 164 Cal.App.4th 1171, 1178.) 

 

Cross-Defendants demur to the breach of contract cause of action on the grounds the Cross-Complaint fails to allege what specific terms were allegedly breached and Cross-Complainants failed to attach copies of the written agreement.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue the demurrer is moot to the breach of contract claim because Cross-Complainants have agreed to attach the agreements and to amend the Cross-Complaint accordingly.

 

The court finds the cause of action fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a claim for breach of contract. Although Cross-Complainants agreed to attach the agreements, the Cross-Complaint still fails to allege what specific terms of the agreements were allegedly breached. It is unclear whether the conduct in paragraph 10 were terms of the agreement.

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the first cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Breach of Covenant of Good Faith and Fair Dealing

 

The elements for breach of the implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing are: (1) existence of a contract between plaintiff and defendant; (2) plaintiff performed his contractual obligations or was excused from performing them; (3) the conditions requiring defendant’s performance had occurred; (4) the defendant unfairly interfered with the plaintiff’s right to receive the benefits of the contract; and (5) the plaintiff was harmed by the defendant’s conduct. (Merced Irr. Dist. V. County of Mariposa (E.D. Cal. 2013) 941 F.Supp.2d 1237, 1280 (discussing California law).) Allegations must demonstrate defendant’s conduct for failure or refusal to discharge contractual responsibilities was a conscious and deliberate act, not an honest mistake, bad judgment or negligence. (Id.) “‘[T]he implied covenant of good faith and fair dealing is limited to assuring compliance with the express terms of the contract, and cannot be extended to create obligations not contemplated by the contract.’” (Ragland v. U.S. Bank Nat. Assn. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 182, 206 (quoting Pasadena Live v. City of Pasadena (2004) 114 Cal.App.4th 1089, 1094).) 

 

Cross-Defendants demur to the breach of covenant of good faith and fair dealing on the grounds the allegations are conclusory and again, has not alleged terms of the contract.

 

Cross-Complainant does not address this cause of action. Because the breach of contract action fails, the court finds the breach of the covenant of good faith also fails. Although a breach of the covenant of good faith must go beyond the statement of a mere breach of contract, the breach of convent depends on the breach of contract. (Green Valley Landowners Assn. v. City of Vallejo (2015) 241 Cal.App.4th 425, 433.)

 

Accordingly, the demurrer to the second cause of action is SUSTAINED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.

 

Fraud and Deceit

 

Elements of action for fraud and deceit based on concealment are: (1) defendant concealed or suppressed a material fact; (2) defendant was under duty to disclose the fact to plaintiff; (3) defendant intentionally concealed or suppressed the fact with intent to defraud plaintiff; (4) plaintiff was unaware of the fact and would not have acted as he did if he had known of the concealed or suppressed fact; and (5) as result of concealment or suppression of the fact, plaintiff sustained damage.  (Blickman Turkus, LP v. MF Downtown Sunnyvale, LLC (2008) 162 Cal.App.4th 858, 877-84.)   Fraud must be pled with particularity, including facts that show how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the representations were tendered. (Lazar v. Superior Court (1996)12 Cal.4th 631, 645.) 

 

Cross-Defendants demur to the third cause of action for fraud and deceit on the grounds Cross-Complainants do not allege how, when, where, to whom, and by what means the alleged false representations by the Cross-Defendants were tendered to the Cross-Complainants and Cross-Complainants do not allege anywhere on the face of the Cross-Complaint when the Cross-Defendants allegedly had knowledge of these purported items. Additionally, Cross-Defendants argue the statute of limitations has run.

 

In opposition, Cross-Complainants argue that this court has already ruled the fraudulent concealment cause of action sufficient for the purposes of a demurrer and because the allegations in the Cross-Complaint are similar to the allegations in the complaint, this argument doesn’t apply. Cross-Complainant also argues the action is not time barred because Cross-Complainants were not necessarily damaged until recently but even if the claims were time barred, the doctrine of set-off revives cross-claims up to the millions of dollars Cross-Defendants are claiming as Plaintiffs.

 

The court disagrees. The allegations in the Complaint have no bearing on allegations in the Cross-Complaint. While the court does agree that the particularity in pleading fraud is relaxed when it is apparent from the allegations that the defendant necessarily possesses knowledge of the facts (Tarmann v. State Farm (1991) 2 Cal.App.4th 153, 158), Cross-Complainants’ fraud cause of action is still conclusory. Whether or not Cross-Complainants may be able to allege specific time, place or content of what was concealed, the allegations are too conclusory. There are no allegations how Cross-Complainants relied on the misrepresentation, how the fiduciary duty arose between the parties, or that Cross-Defendants intentionally concealed the information.

 

As for the statute of limitations contention, the court agrees Code of Civil Procedure section 431.70 is meant to be for affirmative relief. “[W]hen the Legislature limited the relief available under section 431.70, but only where ‘the cross-demand would otherwise be barred by the statute of limitations,’ it was not eliminating the requirement of filing a cross-complaint to obtain affirmative relief; rather, it was merely confirming the possibility of full relief where the statute of limitations imposes no bar.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 198.) The Supreme Court of California further stated they believe “the best reading of section 431.70 is that a setoff claim may only be used defensively, being in nature a defensive pleading asserting that the claim constituted prior payment for the amount sought in the plaintiff's complaint.” (Construction Protective Services, Inc. v. TIG Specialty Ins. Co. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 189, 197–198.)

 

However, because the Cross-Complaint on its face is not clear as to when these events occurred (the court cannot assume it is the November 28, 2018 agreement), the court SUSTAINS the cause of action with leave to amend. 

 

Negligence

 

To plead a cause of action for negligence, one must allege (1) a legal duty owed to plaintiffs to use due care; (2) breach of duty; (3) causation; and (4) damage to plaintiff. (County of Santa Clara v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2006) 137 Cal. App. 4th 292, 318.) “In order to state a cause of action for negligence, the complaint must allege facts sufficient to show a legal duty on the part of the defendant to use due care, a breach of such legal duty, and the breach as the proximate or legal cause of the resulting injury.” (Bellah v. Greenson (1978) 81 Cal.App.3d 614, 619.)  In California, negligence may be pleaded in general terms. (Landeros v. Flood (1976) 17 Cal.3d 399, 407-408.) 

 

Cross-Defendants demur to the negligence cause of action on the grounds while negligence may be pleaded in general terms, Cross-Complainants do not allege what duty Cross-Defendants purportedly owed them, do not detail the breach of the alleged duty nor allege any proximate cause in the negligence cause of action. Cros-Defendant also demurs on the grounds the claim is barred by the statute of limitations.

 

The court agrees the fourth cause of action fails to plead facts sufficient to support a claim for negligence. There are no allegations regarding what duty was owed, how it was breached and so on. Moreover, because it is unclear from the Cross-Complaint when these events occurred, the court SUSTAINS the cause of action with leave to amend.

 

Uncertainty

 

The court OVERRULES the demurrer on uncertainty as the court has sustained the demurrer on the general demurrer.

 

Motion to Strike

 

Cross-Defendants move to strike Cross-Complainants allegations and prayer for relief regarding punitive damages on the grounds the Cross-Complaint is devoid of any specific facts that would support the finding of malice, oppression, or fraud.

 

The court agrees Cross-Complainants have failed alleged facts sufficient to support a claim for punitive damages. Accordingly, the motion to strike is GRANTED WITH LEAVE TO AMEND.
