Case Number: 23AVCV00234    Hearing Date: January 14, 2025    Dept: A14

Background

 

This is a quiet title action. Plaintiffs The Juan Hinojosa Family Trust (the “Trust”) and Juan Hinojosa (“Juan” and collectively “Plaintiffs”)[1] allege that (1) Juan and his late wife purchased 37058 Calle Grande, Palmdale, CA 93550 (the “Property”) as joint tenants; (2) the Property was transferred to Juan upon his wife’s death by Affidavit Death of Joint tenant as Juan is the surviving joint tenant; (3) Defendant Angela Hinojosa (“Angela”) prepared or had someone prepare the Affidavit Death of Joint Tenant; (4) Angela and Defendant Joey Martinez (“Martinez” and collectively “Defendants”) informed Juan that if he allowed them to live at the Property, they would help Juan with his taxes, insurance, and household bills; (5) Defendants moved in around April or May 2018; (6) upon moving in, Angela urged Juan to put her on deed, claiming that the Property would remain Juan’s, that she would pay the property taxes, utilities, and all other related costs; (7) Juan signed a grant deed on May 02, 2018 and did not know that the language in the grant deed stated that the Property was a “bona fide gift” due to his limited English skills; (8) upon being placed on the grant deed, Angela demanded that Juan pay half of the property taxes and insurance, and all of the utilities; (9)  the relationship between Juan and Angela dissolved further when Juan intended to marry a second wife and have her live at the Property; and (1) on July 02, 2021, Angela filed a temporary restraining order against Juan which was unsuccessful.

 

On March 07, 2023, Plaintiffs filed their Complaint.

 

A First Amended Complaint (“FAC”) was filed on May 28, 2024.

 

The operative pleading is the Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) filed August 08, 2024 which alleges seven[2] causes of action: (1) Imposition of Constructive Trust; (2) Quiet Title to Real Property; (3) Fraud; (4) Elder Abuse; (5) Abuse of the Judicial Process; (6) Unjust Enrichment; and (7) Declaratory Relief.

 

On October 23, 2024, Defendants filed a Demurrer with Motion to Strike and a Special Motion to Strike based on anti-SLAPP statutes.

 

On November\ 18, 2024, Defendants filed their Reply which discussed Plaintiffs’ failure to oppose the filings.

 

On November 20, 2024, Plaintiff filed an Ex Parte Application for an Order Shortening Time or for A Continuance of a Demurrer, Motion to Strike, and Motion to Strike (Anti-SLAPP) and their Oppositions.

 

On November 21, 2024, the Court held a hearing on the Ex Parte Application. The Court struck Plaintiffs’ Oppositions and continued the motions to January 14, 2025 to allow Plaintiff to file timely Oppositions.

 

On January 06, 2025, Plaintiff filed his Oppositions. “All papers opposing a motion so noticed shall be filed with the court and a copy served on each party at least nine court days. . . before the hearing.” (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 1005(b).) “Section 1013, which extends the time within which a right may be exercised or an act may be done, does not apply to a notice of motion, papers opposing a motion, or reply papers governed by this section.” (Id.) The hearing is set for January 14, 2025. Nine court days before the hearing is December 31, 2024. Plaintiffs’ Oppositions are not timely.

 

On January 07, 2025, Defendants filed Replies to the Demurrer and the Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike. Chief among the arguments is that Plaintiffs’ Oppositions are untimely and discusses Plaintiffs’ attempt to reinstate his Oppositions as improper and against the Court’s November 21, 2024 Minute Order.


“No paper may be rejected for filing on the ground that it was untimely submitted for filing. If the court, in its discretion, refuses to consider a late filed paper, the minutes or order must so indicate.” (Cal. Rules of Court, Rule 3.1300(d).) The Court does not consider Plaintiffs’ untimely Oppositions.

Legal Standard

 

Standard for Demurrer – A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn v.¿Mirda¿(2007) 147 Cal. App. 4th 740, 747.) ¿When considering demurrers, courts read the allegations liberally and in context.¿ (Taylor v. City of Los Angeles Dept. of Water and Power¿(2006) 144 Cal. App. 4th 1216, 1228.)¿ In a demurrer proceeding, the defects must be apparent on the face of the pleading or by proper judicial notice.¿ (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.30(a).)¿A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters.¿ (SKF Farms v. Superior Court¿(1984) 153 Cal. App. 3d 902, 905.)¿ Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.¿¿(Ibid.)¿¿The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.¿ (Hahn,¿supra,¿147 Cal.App.4th at 747.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

Pursuant to¿Code Civ. Proc.¿§430.10(e), the party against whom a complaint has been filed may object by demurrer to the pleading on the grounds that the pleading does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.¿ It is an abuse of discretion to sustain a demurrer without leave to amend if there is a reasonable probability that the defect can be cured by amendment.¿¿(Schifando¿v. City of Los Angeles¿(2003) 31 Cal.4th 1074, 1082,¿as modified (Dec. 23, 2003).)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

Standard for Motion to Strike – The Court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a).) The Court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Id., § 436(b).) The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. (Id. § 436.) The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. (Id. § 437.)¿¿ 

 

Standard for Special Motion to Strike under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 – Under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16, a cause of action asserted against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of his/her right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution, in connection with a public issue, is subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(b)(1); See also Wilbanks v. Wolk (2004) 121 Cal.App.4th 883, 892.)¿ 

 

In determining whether to grant or deny a special motion to strike, the Court engages in a two-step process.¿ (See, e.g.,¿Shekhter¿v. Fin.¿Indemn. Co.¿(2001), 89 Cal.App.4th 141, 150.)¿ This process is essentially a “summary-judgment-like procedure at any early stage of the litigation.” (Varian Med. Sys., Inc. v.¿Delfino¿(2005), 35 Cal.4th 180, 192.)¿ “First, the court must determine whether defendant has made a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action ‘arises from’ a protected activity.¿ Second, if the defendant makes this showing, the trial court must determine whether the plaintiff has established a probability of prevailing on the claim.”¿ (Optional Capital, Inc. v. Das Corp.¿(2014), 222 Cal.App.4th 1388, 1398.)¿¿¿¿ 

¿ 

The motion requires the moving party to bear the initial burden of establishing a prima facie showing that the plaintiff’s cause of action arises from the defendant’s free speech or petition activity. (Wilbanks, supra, 121 Cal.App.4th at 894.) The defendant may meet this burden by showing the act which forms the basis for the plaintiff's cause of action was a written or oral statement made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding; or such a statement in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body; or such a statement was made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest.¿ (Wilcox v. Superior Court (1994) 27 Cal. App. 4th 809, 820 [overruled in part as to showing of intent of party that filed the complaint].)¿ 

¿ 

Once a defendant meets their burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to establish a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim, i.e., make a prima facie showing of facts which would, if proved at trial, support a judgment in plaintiff’s favor. (Feldman v. 1100 Park Lane Associates (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1467, 1497-78.) Specifically, the plaintiff carries their shifting burden by making an evidentiary showing like that required under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 437c. (Id.; See also Ludwig v. Superior Court (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 8, 15.)¿ 

 

If the special motion to strike is granted, the court must enter judgment for the defendant.¿ (DuPont Merck Pharmaceutical Co. v. Superior Court¿(2000), 78 Cal.App.4th 562, 564.)¿ No leave to amend may be given.¿ (Schaffer v. City and County of San Francisco¿(2008), 168 Cal.App.4th 992, 1005.)¿ 

 

¿¿¿¿¿ 

-----¿¿¿¿ 

¿¿¿¿ 

Meet and Confer Requirement– Before filing a demurrer or a motion to strike, the demurring or moving party is required to meet and confer with the party who filed the pleading demurred to or the pleading that is subject to the motion to strike for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.) If the parties are unable to meet and confer, a declaration stating under penalty of perjury that a good faith attempt to meet and confer was made and explaining the reasons why the parties could not meet and confer. (Ibid.) It appears from Patricia Rodriguez’s Declaration that a meet and confer did not occur. (See Decl. of Demurring or Moving Party RE: Meet and Confer.) A determination by the court that the meet and confer process was insufficient shall not be grounds to overrule or sustain a demurrer, or to grant or deny a motion to strike. (See Cal. Code Civ. Proc. §§ 430.41 and 435.5.)

Discussion

 

Application –

 

        i.            Demurrer

 

a.      First Cause of Action (Imposition of Constructive Trust)

 

Defendants argue that the SAC fails to allege any wrongful act that resulted in unjust enrichment. That is, Defendants argue that the deed transfer was conducted with the full awareness and voluntary participation of Juan and that Angela has been contributing to the maintenance and expenses of the property as agreed upon by both parties.

 

Defendants’ arguments fail as they attempt to contradict the allegations within the pleadings. The SAC states, in relevant part:

 

Plaintiff alleges that he did not prepare the Affidavit of Death of Joint Tenant (Exhibit 2) nor the deed in Exhibit 3. Plaintiff has very limited English ability and does not read English. Although the Grant Deed has typed in the form ““xx_Or transfer is EXEMPT from tax for the following reason: “This is a bona fide gift and the grantor received nothing in return, R & T 11911.” The plaintiff did not know that the words “bona fide gift” was written into the deed.

 

[. . .]

 

However, when the second tax bill came due by April 2019, Defendant demanded that he pay half of the property taxes and insurance, and she and Joey would continue paying all the utilities. She was no longer friendly and loving as she had been before the Deed (Exhibit 3) was recorded, and defendant Angela forced him to pay half of his taxes and insurance before April 10, 2019. She thereby breached her agreement with Plaintiff that she would pay all the property tax bills in exchange for living in the home and placing her on the title to the subject property.

 

(SAC ¶¶ 20 and 25.)

 

A general demurrer admits the truth of all factual, material allegations properly pled in the challenged pleading, regardless of possible difficulties of proof.¿¿(Blank v. Kirwan (1985) 39 Cal.3d 311, 318.)¿ Thus, no matter how unlikely or improbable, plaintiff’s allegations must be accepted as true for the purpose of ruling on the demurrer.¿¿(Del E. Webb Corp. v. Structural Materials Co.¿(1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 593, 604.)¿ Nevertheless, this rule does not apply to allegations expressing mere conclusions of law, or allegations contradicted by the exhibits to the complaint or by matters of which judicial notice may be taken.¿¿(Vance v. Villa Park¿Mobilehome¿Estates¿(1995) 36 Cal.App.4th 698, 709.)¿A general demurrer does not admit contentions, deductions, or conclusions of fact or law alleged in the complaint; facts impossible in law; or allegations contrary to facts of which a court may take judicial notice.¿¿(Blank,¿supra, 39 Cal.3d at p. 318.)¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿¿ 

 

Defendants do not argue that the allegations are conclusory, only that the alleged facts are untrue. This is not appropriate for a demurrer.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the First Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

 

b.      Second Cause of Action (Quiet Title to Real Property)

 

Defendants argue that the allegations show that Juan voluntarily added Angela to the deed, thereby conceding her co-ownership in the Property and, as such, Juan has negated any basis for his Quiet Title claim.

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 761.020 provides that a complaint for quiet title shall be verified and include: (1) a description of the property that is the subject of the action, including both the legal description and its street address or common designation if the subject property is real property; (2) the plaintiffs title as to which determination is sought and basis of the title; (3) the adverse claims to the plaintiffs title against which determination is sought; (4) the date of which the determination is sought; and (5) a prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims. (Code Civ. Proc, § 761.020, subds. (a)-(e).)

 

A review of the SAC reveals this cause of action relates to the Property and is based on Angela’s adverse claim to title based upon a fraudulently obtained grant deed due to Juan’s inability to speak English and understand what he was signing and a breach of verbal agreement. (See SAC ¶¶ 20, 25.)

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Demurrer to the Second Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

 

c.       Third Cause of Action (Fraud)

 

Defendants argue that the allegations of fraud in the SAC fail to meet the heighted pleading requirements as the SAC does not demonstrate how Juan justifiably relied on any alleged misrepresentation by Angela, that Juan added Angela voluntarily to the deed, that there are no claims of justifiable reliance on any purported misrepresentation due to the voluntary nature of Angela’s addition to the deed, and that the allegations show that Angela’s addition was made by Juan voluntarily and with full awareness.

 

“The elements of fraud that will give rise to a tort action for deceit are: “ ‘(a) misrepresentation (false representation, concealment, or nondisclosure); (b) knowledge of falsity (or ‘scienter’); (c) intent to defraud, i.e., to induce reliance; (d) justifiable reliance; and (e) resulting damage.’ ” (Engalla v. Permanente Medical Group, Inc. (1997) 15 Cal.4th 951, 974 [internal quotation marks omitted].)

 

Here, the fraud is based on misrepresentation. There is an allegation regarding what would occur after Angela was placed on the deed (i.e., full payment of certain items) as well as an English contract that was signed by Juan, who had limited English. Defendants do not address how Juan could have signed the document with full awareness as it is alleged that his English is limited. While Rosenthal v. Great Western Fin. Securities Corp. (1996) 14 Cal.4th 394 (“Rosenthal”) held that misrepresentations by themselves would not deprive the other party of a reasonable opportunity to discover the essential terms of an agreement, it also held that misrepresentations made to a party who had a limited understanding of English might warrant a different result. (Rosenthal, supra, 14 Cal.4th at p. 428 [“In light of plaintiffs' prior relationship with [the corporation], which they were led to believe was also the employer of Divine and Daikovich, their limited ability to understand English, and Divine and Daikovich's representations that their oral recitals accurately reflected the terms of the agreements, plaintiffs would not have been negligent in relying on the [corporation's] representatives instead of reading the agreements themselves”].) As such, unlike Defendants’ representations, there is a basis for misrepresentation and reliance on that misrepresentation.

 

The Court notes that there are no allegations related to Martinez that form the underlying basis for this action.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Third Cause of Action is SUSTAINED in part as to Martinez and OVERRULED as to Angela.

 

d.      Fourth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse)

 

Defendants argue that Juan voluntarily added Angela to the Deed, that it was motivated by other reasons, there was no undue influence or manipulation, that Juan’s lifestyle contradicts that he is vulnerable or incapable of making an informed decision, the allegations related to emotional and psychological abuse are not sufficient, and Juan did not adequately connect the alleged conduct to his status as an elder.

 

The allegations in the SAC for this cause of action are based on the following, including:

 

·         Angela’s petition for temporary restraining order against Juan;

·         Preventing Juan from parking in his garage;

·         Taking documents personal documents related to ownership of the Property, insurance, and medical records;

·         Installed locks to bedroom doors in the Property;

·         Changed the mailbox locks, preventing Juan from accessing his mail;

·         Removed Juan’s refrigerator and clothes dryer from the house without approval;

·         Defendants installed cameras in and around the home with large signs that state “you are being watched;”

·         Nonstop insults, slurs, and threats from June 12, 2021 to the present;

·         One defendant took Juan’s keys on or about July 26, 2022 and threw the keys into Plaintiff’s bedroom;

·         Spraying noxious odors into Juan’s bedrooms and hallway bathroom;

·         Playing radio or other device for hours; and

·         Installing an alarm system that is triggered when Juan leaves his bedroom.

 

(See SAC ¶¶ 52-57.)

 

Defendants’ legal standard for this cause of action focuses on financial elder abuse. This does not appear to be what Plaintiffs are alleging.

 

 “Abuse of an elder or a dependent adult” is defined as “[p]hysical abuse, neglect, financial abuse, abandonment, abduction, or other treatment with resulting physical harm or pain or mental suffering[,]” “[t]he deprivation by a care custodian of goods or services that are necessary to avoid physical harm or mental suffering[,]” or “[f]inancial abuse as defined in Section 15610.30.” (Cal. Wel. & Inst. Code, §15610.07.) Each phrase is further defined by various statutes. Plaintiffs have not identified what statute they are moving under, making this cause of action uncertain.

 

Accordingly, the Court SUSTAINS the Demurrer to the Fourth Cause of Action.

 

e.       Fifth Cause of Action (Abuse of Judicial Process)

 

Defendants argue that Juan has not alleged facts that meet the elements of malicious prosecution or abuse of process and the SAC does not specify any prior action that Defendants have pursued against Juan without probable cause or malice. Defendants argue that the allegations are vague and do not provide detail to support the claim of abuse of the judicial process.

 

While Defendants argue that this cause of action is a claim for malicious prosecution, it is not sufficiently clear to the Court that the malicious prosecution standard is the correct legal standard for the Fifth Cause of Action. Specifically, the Fifth Cause of Action focuses on the temporary restraining order only and states that it was “merely another in a series of attempts to force plaintiff from his home.” (SAC ¶ 64.)

 

The Court seeks clarification at the hearing.

 

At this time, the Court is inclined to SUSTAIN the Demurrer to the Fifth Cause of Action due to uncertainty.

 

f.        Sixth Cause of Action (Unjust Enrichment)

 

Defendants argue that the SAC fails to demonstrate how Defendants have been unjustly enriched as the property transfer was agreed upon and Defendants have shared in the property expenses as agreed upon.

 

Defendants’ arguments fail for the same reasons outlined in the Court’s analysis for the First Cause of Action. As such, the Court incorporates its analysis, ante, for the Sixth Cause of Action.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Sixth Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

 

g.      Seventh Cause of Action (Declaratory Relief)

 

Defendants argue that the SAC does not present a valid controversy regarding declaratory relief as the property transfer was willingly executed and all other causes of actions have been properly demurred to.

 

Defendants’ argument regarding a willingly executed transfer fails for the same reasons outlined in the Court’s analysis for the First Cause of Action. As such, the Court incorporates its analysis, ante, for the Seventh Cause of Action.

 

As to the argument that all other claims have been properly demurred to, based on the Court’s analysis, various claims remain.

 

Accordingly, the Demurrer to the Seventh Cause of Action is OVERRULED.

 

      ii.            Motion to Strike

 

Defendants seek to strike the following:

 

Paragraph 47:

 

The acts, statements, and conducts of Defendant Angela Hinojosa as aforesaid were done intentionally, maliciously, fraudulently, oppressively, despicably, and in conscious disregard for the rights and wellbeing of Plaintiff, thereby justifying the award of punitive damages and exemplary damages in an amount to be determined at trial.

 

Paragraph 64:

 

Defendants who used abusive language and threats of physical violence in attempts to eject him from the Subject Property, and Plaintiff therefore is entitled to punitive damages.

 

Paragraph 67:

 

The Plaintiff alleges that the aforementioned acts of the Defendant to attempt to eject him from the Subject Property was willful and malicious in that the Defendant without any rights; to do so used abusive language and threats of physical violence in attempts to eject him from the Subject Property, and Plaintiff therefore is entitled to punitive damages. Plaintiff is also entitled to reasonable attorney’s fees.

 

PRAYER FOR RELIEF REQUEST:

 

1) Third Cause of Action - For punitive damages

 

2) Fourth Cause of Action – For punitive damages

 

3) Fifth Cause of Action – For punitive damages

 

4) Sixth Cause of Action – For Punitive damages

 

(Motion to Strike 2:9-28.)

 

The gravamen of Defendants’ argument is that the claims for punitive damages are not properly pled as the fraud claim fails to be pled with particularity.

 

The Court notes that the Demurrer to the Fourth and Fifth Causes of Action has been sustained.

 

To state a prima facie claim for punitive damages, a plaintiff must allege the elements set forth in the punitive damages statute, Civil Code section 3294. (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal.4th 704, 721.) A plaintiff must allege that the defendant has been guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. (Civ. Code, § 3294, subd. (a).) “Malice is defined in the statute as conduct intended by the defendant to cause injury to the plaintiff or despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” (Coll. Hosp., Inc. v. Superior Court, supra, 8 Cal.4th 704, 725.) “The mere allegation an intentional tort was committed is not sufficient to warrant an award of punitive damages. Not only must there be circumstances of oppression, fraud or malice, but facts must be alleged in the pleading to support such a claim.” (Grieves v. Superior Ct. (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 166 [internal citations and footnotes omitted].)

 

The remaining Third Cause of Action focuses on fraud. As the fraud claim has survived demurrer, the Court believes that punitive damages have been pled with enough specificity to move past the pleading stages.

 

Accordingly, the Motion to Strike is DENIED in part as to punitive damages related to the Third Cause of Action (Fraud), and GRANTED in part as to the Fourth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse) and Fifth Cause of Action (Abuse of Judicial Process).

    iii.            Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike

 

Defendants bring this Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike based on Plaintiffs’ Fourth Cause of Action (Abuse of Process). Defendants present that the Fourth Cause of Action stems from a Domestic Violence Restraining Order (Case # 21STR0002978) and that such an action is protected under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(e)(1) and (e)(2). Defendants argue that Plaintiffs have not demonstrated that the temporary restraining order was filed for any purpose other than addressing alleged harassment or that the process itself was improper, and that the allegations of malice or wrongful intent are conclusory and lack factual support.

 

In the first step, the trial court determines whether the claim “‘arises from’ an ‘“‘act in furtherance of a person's right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue.’”’” (Laker v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2019) 32 Cal.App.5th 745, 759–760 [quoting City of Montebello v. Vasquez (2016) 1 Cal.5th 409, 422].) The court must evaluate whether the moving defendant has shown the speech or act underlying the plaintiff's claim falls within one or more of the four categories of protected acts set out in Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(e). (Wilson, supra, 7 Cal.5th at p. 884.)

 

Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16(e) provides:

 

As used in this section, “act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue” includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.

 

The filing of a temporary restraining order is protected petitioning activity under Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 425.16 as well as Kenne v. Stennis (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 953. (See Kenne v. Stennis (2014) 230 Cal.App.4th 953, 970 [“We agree with the holding in Siam, supra, 130 Cal.App.4th 1563, as well as the reasoning and policies upon which that holding is based, and conclude that plaintiff cannot prevail on her malicious prosecution claim against Hatter because that claim is based on the filing and prosecution of the two civil harassment petitions under section 527.6, which petitions, for public policy reasons, cannot form the basis of a malicious prosecution action. The trial court therefore erred when it denied the special motion to strike the malicious prosecution claim against Hatter.

 

Our conclusion in this regard is supported by the recent decision in S.A. v. Maiden (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 27 [176 Cal. Rptr. 3d 567]. In that case, a wife requested and obtained a TRO against her husband under the Domestic Violence Prevention Act, Family Code section 6200 et seq. (S.A., at p. 33.) When the wife subsequently voluntarily withdrew her request for a restraining order, the husband sued her for malicious prosecution, as well as for other torts. (Id. at p. 34.) Relying on, inter alia, Siam, supra, 130 Cal.App.4th 1563, the court held that a request for and the issuance of a restraining order under the Domestic Violence Protection Act, like one requested and issued under section 527.6, cannot as a matter of law be the basis of a claim for malicious prosecution. (S.A., at pp. 36–38.)”].)

 

Accordingly, Defendants have met their burden to establish a prima facie showing that the Plaintiffs’ Fourth Cause of Action arises from the defendant’s free speech or petition activity. The burden shifts to Plaintiffs.

 

The Court does not consider Plaintiffs’ untimely Opposition. As such, Plaintiffs do not meet their burden to establish a probability that they will prevail on the claim, i.e., make a prima facie showing of facts which would, if proved at trial, support a judgment in plaintiff’s favor.

 

Accordingly, the Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

Leave to Amend

 

Regarding leave to amend, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy¿(1976), 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) While under California law leave to amend is liberally granted, “leave to amend should not be granted where, in all probability, amendment would be futile.” (Vaillette v. Fireman's Fund Ins. Co. (1993), 18 Cal. App. 4th 680, 685).¿ “A trial court does not abuse its discretion when it sustains a demurrer without¿leave to amend¿if either (a) the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists, or (b) it is probable from the nature of the defects and previous unsuccessful attempts to plead that the plaintiff cannot state a claim.” (Cantu v. Resolution Trust Corp.¿(1992)¿4 Cal.App.4th 857, 889.) 

 

The Demurrer to the following causes of action have been sustained: (1) Third Cause of Action (Fraud) as to Martinez only, (2) Fourth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse), (3) Fifth Cause of Action (Abuse of Judicial Process).

 

As to the Third Cause of Action, the facts and the nature of the claims are clear and no liability exists as to Martinez. The Court does not allow leave to amend for the Third Cause of Action.

 

As to the Fourth Cause of Action, the Court allows leave to amend as the claims are uncertain.

 

As to the Fifth Cause of Action, the Court does not allow leave to amend due to its analysis in the Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike.

 

Accordingly, leave to amend is allowed as to the Fourth Cause of Action only.

Conclusion

 

Defendants Angela Hinojosa and Joey Martinez’s Demurrer is OVERRULED in part as to the First Cause of Action (Imposition of Constructive Trust), Second Cause of Action (Quiet Title to Real Property), Third Cause of Action (Fraud) as to Defendant Angela Hinojosa only, Sixth Cause of Action (Unjust Enrichment), and Seventh Cause of Action (Declaratory Relief); and SUSTAINED as to the Third Cause of Action (Fraud) as to Defendant Joey Martinez only, Fourth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse), and Fifth Cause of Action (Abuse of Judicial Process).

 

Defendants Angela Hinojosa and Joey Martinez’s Motion to Strike is DENIED in part as to punitive damages related to the Third Cause of Action (Fraud), and GRANTED in part as to the Fourth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse) and Fifth Cause of Action (Abuse of Judicial Process).

 

Defendants Angela Hinojosa and Joey Martinez’s Anti-SLAPP Motion to Strike is GRANTED.

 

Leave to amend is allowed as to the Fourth Cause of Action (Elder Abuse) only. The Juan Hinojosa Family Trust and Juan Hinojosa are to file an amended pleading within 30 days of this Court Order.

“Although a judge hearing a section 664.6 motion may receive evidence, determine disputed facts, and enter the terms of a settlement agreement as a judgment [citations], nothing in section 664.6 authorizes a judge to create the material terms of a settlement, as opposed to deciding what terms the parties themselves have previously agreed upon.” (Weddington Productions, Inc. v.
