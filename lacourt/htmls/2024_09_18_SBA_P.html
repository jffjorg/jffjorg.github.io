DEPARTMENT P LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23TRCV02565    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: P

Motion to Strike

             The court considered the moving, opposition and reply papers.

RULING

            Defendant’s motion to strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.

 BACKGROUND

            This personal injury action involves an alleged incident that occurred on July 13, 2023, while Plaintiff Daimel Garcia (“Plaintiff”) was shopping at a Walmart location. He alleges that after any perceived threat was neutralized, loss prevention and security employees of Walmart stopped and severely assaulted him by punching him in the head repeatedly even after he had been knocked down to the floor, which resulted in concussion syndrome.

On August 9, 2023, Plaintiff filed a Complaint against Defendant Walmart, Inc (“Defendant”).

            On October 27, 2023, Plaintiff filed a First Amended Complaint (“FAC”).

            On June 24, 2024, the court granted Defendant’s motion to strike punitive damages and allowed 20 days leave to amend.

            On July 12, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (“SAC”) alleging causes of action for: (1) Negligence; (2) Assault and Battery; and (3) Premises Liability.

            On August 13, 2024, Defendant filed a motion to strike. On September 5, 2024, Plaintiff filed an opposition. On September 10, 2024, Defendant filed a reply.

LEGAL AUTHORITY

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435(b)(1).) The court may, upon a motion or at any time in its discretion and upon terms it deems proper: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court. (Code Civ. Proc., § 436; Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.) 

Leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 348.) The burden is on the complainant to show the Court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Id.) 

DISCUSSION

i.                    Meet and Confer Requirement 

Defendant’s counsel’s declaration satisfies the meet and confer requirements. (Code Civ. Proc. § 435.5; Wise Decl., ¶ 5.)  

ii.                  Merits 

Defendant moves to strike portions of the complaint relating to punitive damages. 

To state a claim for punitive damages under Civil Code section 3294, a plaintiff must allege specific facts showing that the defendant has been guilty of malice, oppression or fraud. (Smith v. Superior Court (1992) 10 Cal. App. 4th 1033, 1042.) The basis for punitive damages must be pled with specificity; conclusory allegations devoid of any factual assertions are insufficient. (Id.) A motion to strike may lie where the facts alleged, if proven, would not support a finding that the defendant acted with malice, fraud or oppression. (Turman v. Turning Point of Central California (2010) 191 Cal. App. 4th 53, 63.)  

 

“Malice” is defined in section 3294(c)(1) as “conduct which is intended by the defendant to cause injury” or “despicable conduct which is carried on by the defendant with a willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others.” “Oppression” is defined in section 3294(c)(2) as “despicable conduct subjecting a person to cruel and unjust hardship in conscious disregard of that person’s rights.” The term “despicable” has been defined in the case law as actions that are “base,” “vile,” or “contemptible.” (See, e.g., Shade Foods, Inc. v. Innovative Products Sales & Marketing, Inc. (2000) 78 Cal. App. 4th 847, 891.)  

 

To prove that a defendant acted with “willful and conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others,” it is not enough to prove negligence, gross negligence or even recklessness. (Dawes v. Superior Court (1980) 111 Cal. App. 3d 82, 87.) Rather, a plaintiff must allege facts demonstrating that “the defendant acted in such an outrageous and reprehensible manner that the jury could infer that he [or she] knowingly disregarded the substantial certainty of injury to others.” (Id. at 90). Further, the allegations must be sufficient for a reasonable jury to conclude that Defendant’s conduct was “despicable” defined as “base, vile or contemptible.” (College Hospital Inc. v. Superior Court (1994) 8 Cal. 4th 704, 725.) 

 

            “An employer shall not be liable for [punitive] damages ..., based upon acts of an employee of the employer, unless the employer had advance knowledge of the unfitness of the employee and employed him or her with a conscious disregard of the rights or safety of others or authorized or ratified the wrongful conduct or was personally guilty of oppression, fraud or malice. With respect to a corporate employer, the advance knowledge and conscious disregard, authorization, ratification, or act of oppression, fraud, or malice must be on the part of an officer, director, or managing agent of the corporation.” (Civil Code § 3294.) The California Supreme Court interpreted the “latter statement as requiring the officer, director, or managing agent to be someone who ‘exercise[s] substantial discretionary authority over decisions that ultimately determine corporate policy.’” (White v. Ultramar, Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 563, 577.) Furthermore, there must be allegations that the managing agent was acting as a corporate representative or in a professional capacity. (College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal.4th at 724.)

As to punitive damages, the SAC alleges that Defendants were on notice of the violent and dangerous nature of its security and loss prevention employees, and failed to implement safety measures to address the threat to patrons. (SAC, ¶ 14.) The SAC also alleges that Defendants and its managing agents ratified the conduct of its loss prevention employees, including but not limited to Robert Ewing and Cayota Batts, by failing to terminate or in any other way discipline any of their employees involved in the incident, and by failing to provide any additional training with respect to the use of physical force and violence. (Id., ¶ 25.)

The court finds there are insufficient facts pled in the SAC to support a claim for punitive damages as to a corporate defendant, such as Walmart. As the court previously ruled, the allegations regarding advance knowledge are entirely conclusory. The FAC contains no facts regarding prior incidents or advance knowledge of any corporate managing agents to support these conclusory allegations.

As to ratification, “[a] corporation cannot confirm and accept that which it does not actually know about.’” (Cruz v. Home Base (2000) 83 Cal.App.4th 160, 167, citing College Hospital, supra, 8 Cal. 4th at 726 [for ratification sufficient to justify punitive damages against corporation, there must be proof that officers, directors, or managing agents had actual knowledge of the malicious conduct and its outrageous character.].) As noted by this court in the prior motion to strike, there are no facts pled to show whether Walmart’s managing agent knew of the incident in order to take disciplinary action or provide training to prevent violence.

Based on the foregoing, the motion to strike punitive damages is granted. As Plaintiff was given an opportunity to cure the defects in the pleading, and failed to do so, it appears there is no reasonable possibility of successful amendment. Thus, leave to amend is denied.

            Defendant is ordered to give notice of ruling.


Case Number: 23TRCV04104    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: P

Having considered plaintiff’s demurrer and motion to strike, defendants’ opposition, and plaintiff’s reply, the court rules as follows: the demurrer to first and second causes of action in the cross-complaint is overruled; the demurrer to the third cause of action in the cross-complaint is sustained without leave to amend; the motion to strike is granted in part without leave to amend. 

I.                   PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On December 11, 2023, Plaintiff Rolling Hills Community Association of Rancho Palos Verdes (Plaintiff) filed a Complaint alleging three causes of action against Defendants George Cashman Jr. (Cashman), Cynthia C. Makin (Makin), and the GFC Administrative Trust. The Complaint arises from an alleged breach of the “Declaration No. 150 of Establishment of Basic Protective Restrictions, Conditions, Covenants, Reservations, Liens, Charges, and Certain Local Restrictions” (CCRs), when Plaintiff discovered that a fence extends beyond Plaintiff’s permitted easement in violation of the CCRs. The Complaint alleges causes of action for: (1) trespass; (2) breach of CC&Rs; and (3) declaratory and injunctive relief.

 Plaintiff filed a Motion for Preliminary Injunction, which was fully briefed, and Defendants Makin and Cashman (Defendants) opposed the motion.  

On June 4, 2024, Defendants filed a Cross-Complaint against Plaintiff alleging causes of action for: (1) quiet title; (2) declaratory relief; and (3) injunctive relief. 

On June 18, 2024, after hearing oral argument and taking the matter under submission, the Court granted the motion for preliminary injunction in part. (06/18/24 Minute Order.) The Court indicated that “Plaintiff may remove the fence from their property identified as ‘Lot 127.’ Such removal will be at no cost to Defendants. No undertaking is required.” (06/18/24 Minute Order at p. 1.) The Court found that Plaintiff was likely to prevail on the merits as to Lot 127. (06/18/24 Minute Order at p. 3.) The Court noted that “Defendants provide[d] no evidence that they own Lot 127, [and] Defendants merely attempt to put the title in question” and that such “attempt fail[ed] because Plaintiff . . . produced evidence that they in fact own Lot 127.” (06/18/24 Minute Order at p. 3.) As to the property adjacent to Portuguese Bend Road, the Court found that Plaintiff had not carried its burden. (06/18/24 Minute Order at pp. 4-5.)   

On September 11, 2024, Defendants filed and served an Errata the Cross-Complaint and have provided a verification for the Cross-Complaint attached thereto. 

Now, Plaintiff demurs to each cause of action in the Cross-Complaint, and also moves to strike portions of the Cross-Complaint. Defendants filed oppositions to the demurrer and motion to strike, to which Plaintiff filed replies. 

II.                FACTUAL BACKGROUND 

The Court finds it necessary to set forth the allegations of the Complaint and the Cross-Complaint. The Complaint alleges the following: Plaintiff is the owner of Lot 127 in Rolling Hills and is the holder of road easements in a 698-home planned development (the “Development”) in the City of Rolling Hills. (Complaint, ¶¶ 3-4.) Defendants are the owners of a property at 2 Crest Road East, Rolling Hills, CA 90274 (the “Cashman Property”), which is managed by Defendant GFC Administrative Trust. (Complaint, ¶ 5.)

 The Cashman Property is bound by the CCRs, which are enforceable equitable servitudes that govern all owners. (Complaint, ¶ 9.) Plaintiff is a gated community with private roads, all owned and controlled by Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) The roads were created by recorded easements dedicated to Plaintiff and the Cashman Property is bordered on one side by Portuguese Bend Road, which has a 40-foot road easement on the Cashman Property (the “Road Easement”). (Complaint, ¶ 11.) The Road Easement is recorded in Plaintiff’s CCRs and parcel maps recorded against the Cashman Property with the Office of the County Recorder. (Complaint, ¶ 11.) 

Defendants own and control a three-rail white wooden fence that extends up to 15 feet into the Road Easement. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Plaintiff has requested that Defendants remove their fence so that Plaintiff can widen Portuguese Road’s shoulder to allow fire trucks to use the shoulder in the event of a community evacuation. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) Defendants refuse to remove the fence and blocked Plaintiff from removing the fence. (Complaint, ¶ 12.) 

On the Crest Road East side of the Cashman Property, their property abuts Lot 127, which is owned by Plaintiff. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) There, Defendants own and control a three-rail white wooden fence that extends approximately 24-feet onto Plaintiff’s Lot 127. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) Defendants have refused to remove their fence and have blocked Plaintiff from removing the fence. (Complaint, ¶ 13.) 

In the Cross-Complaint, Defendants allege the following: Plaintiff’s purported ownership of Lot 127 stems from a 1989 quitclaim deed that post-dates the creation and use of the subdivision by 50 years and the use of Lot 127 by Defendants and/or their predecessors by approximately 25 years. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 7.) Defendants’ right to use a portion of Lot 127 vested years before Plaintiff acquired the 1989 quitclaim deed, and Plaintiff took title to Lot 127 with notice and knowledge of the Defendants’ predecessor’s use of that real property. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 7.) The Road Easement has been extinguished and/or terminated under the doctrine of adverse possession. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 10.) Defendants allege that Plaintiff does not own fee title to Lot 127 and the quitclaim deed that purported to convey an interest in Lot 127 to Plaintiff was defective. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 14.) Defendants allege that the owners of the Cashman Property, over the decades, have established a prescriptive easement, an equitable easement, an irrevocable license, an implied easement, and maintain the right to use Lot 127 that includes the fencing and the mature trees. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 15-19.) 

III.             LEGAL STANDARD

A party may demur to a complaint on the grounds that it “does not state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (e).) A party may also demur to a complaint on the grounds that the complaint is uncertain. (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10, subd. (f).) A demurrer tests whether the complaint states a cause of action. (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.) A special demurrer for uncertainty under Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10, subdivision (f) is disfavored and will only be sustained where the pleading is so bad that defendant or plaintiff cannot reasonably respond—i.e., cannot reasonably determine what issues must be admitted or denied, or what counts or claims are directed against him or her. (Khoury v. Maly’s of Calif., Inc. (1993) 14 Cal.App.4th 612, 616.)

When considering demurrers, courts accept all well pleaded facts as true. (Fox v. JAMDAT Mobile, Inc. (2010) 185 Cal.App.4th 1068, 1078.) “A demurrer tests the pleadings alone and not the evidence or other extrinsic matters. Therefore, it lies only where the defects appear on the face of the pleading or are judicially noticed.” (SKF Farms v. Superior Court (1984) 153 Cal.App.3d 902, 905.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn, supra, 147 Cal.App.4th at p. 747.) On demurrer, a court does “not accept contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Simonyan v. Nationwide Insurance Company of America (2022) 78 Cal.App.5th 889, 895.)

Although courts construe pleadings liberally, sufficient facts must be alleged to support the allegations pled to survive a demurrer. (Rakestraw v. California Physicians' Serv. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 39, 43.) Where a demurrer is sustained, leave to amend must be allowed where there is a reasonable possibility of successful amendment. (Goodman v. Kennedy (1976) 18 Cal.3d 335, 349.) The burden is on the party who filed the pleading subject to demurrer to show the court that a pleading can be amended successfully. (Ibid.)

“Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading may serve and file a notice of motion to strike the whole or any part thereof.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1).) A court may “[s]trike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 436, subd. (a).) A court may “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (b).) 

IV.             DISCUSSION 

Moving Party’s Arguments 

Plaintiff contends that each cause of action in the Cross-Complaint fails because, in its ruling on the motion for preliminary injunction, the Court made clear that ownership of Lot 127 was settled. Plaintiff further argues that the “law of the case” doctrine holds that the Court’s decision as to Lot 127 is settled. As to the third cause of action, Plaintiff asserts that injunctive relief is a remedy and not a cause of action. 

As to the motion to strike, Plaintiff argues that: (1) Defendants have failed to verify their Cross-Complaint for quiet title; and (2) the cause of action for injunctive relief must be stricken as it emanates from the unverified cause of action for quiet title.   

As to both the demurrer and motion to strike, Plaintiff requests that leave to amend not be allowed. 

Responding Party’s Opposition 

In opposition to the demurrer, Defendants assert the following: (1) the granting or denial of a preliminary injunction does not amount to an adjudication of the ultimate rights in controversy; (2) a demurrer must dispose of an entire cause of action to be sustained; (3) ownership is a question of fact when conflicting facts exist; (4) the “law of the case” doctrine only applies to determinations of an appellate court and not to interim rulings such as a preliminary injunction; and (5) the demurrer challenges the third cause of action for injunctive relief solely on the grounds as a standalone cause of action.   

In opposing the motion to strike, Defendants argue that: (1) they have provided a verification of the Cross-Complaint; and (2) similar to the demurrer, the motion to strike challenges the third cause of action for injunctive relief solely on the grounds as a standalone cause of action.   

 Defendants request leave to amend if the demurrer is sustained. 

Moving Party’s Reply 

On reply, Plaintiff argues that: (1) Defendants are mistaken that the demurrer did not address the elements of a quiet title cause of action; (2) the Cross-Complaint is still unverified; (3) Defendants’ argument as to Lot 127 fails as to the judicially noticeable documents submitted in support of the motion for preliminary injunction settled the issue; (4) the Court has authority to bifurcate issues, so Defendants’ argument regarding a demurrer disposing of an entire cause of action fails; (5) Defendants do not address the flaws in their injunctive relief cause of action; (6) the “law of the case” doctrine holds that the Court’s decision with respect to Lot 127 is settled; and (7) the third cause of action for injunctive relief is a remedy and not a cause of action.   

As to the motion to strike, Plaintiff argues that: (1) Defendants have still failed to verify their Cross-Complaint; (2) the injunctive relief cause of action fails because it emanates from the unverified Cross-Complaint; and (3) the cause of action for injunctive relief must be stricken as it is not a standalone cause of action.    

Merits of the Demurrer and Motion to Strike 

A.    Meet and Confer 

Before filing a demurrer, the demurring party is required to meet and confer “in person, by telephone, or by video conference with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to the demurrer for the purposes of determining whether an agreement can be reached through a filing of an amended pleading that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41, subd. (a).) A motion to strike has a similar meet and confer requirement. (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5.) 

The Court finds that the meet and confer requirement has not been met as the parties only exchanged email and letter correspondence as to the issues in the Cross-Complaint. (Meerson Decls.) The Court will still assess the demurrer and motion to strike on the merits; however, the Court reminds the parties of the need to comply with the requirements of the Code of Civil Procedure.

 B.     The Law of the Case Doctrine is Inapplicable, and the Issue of Ownership of Lot 127 is not Settled

“[A] preliminary injunction is an order that is sought by a plaintiff prior to a full adjudication of the merits of its claim.” (Costa Mesa City  Employees’ Assn. v. City of Costa Mesa (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 298, 305, citation omitted.) “The purpose of such an order is to preserve the status quo until a final determination following trial.” (Ibid.) A preliminary injunction “does not constitute a final adjudication of the controversy.” (Ibid., emphasis added.)

“The law of the case doctrine holds that when an appellate opinion states a principle or rule of law necessary to the decision, that principle or rule becomes the law of the case and must be adhered to through its subsequent progress in the lower court and upon subsequent appeal.” (People v. Cooper (2007) 149 Cal.App.4th 500, 524, emphasis added, citations omitted.) “For the doctrine to apply, the point of law involved must be necessary to the prior decision . . . [and] the matter must have been actually presented and determined by the court, and . . . the application of the doctrine will not result in an unjust decision.” (Ibid., citations omitted.)  

The Court finds that the law of the case doctrine is inapplicable to the Court’s ruling on Plaintiff’s motion for a preliminary injunction. First, the Court finds that Plaintiff’s reliance on Nelson v. Tucker Ellis LLP (2020) 48 Cal.App.5th 827 is inapposite because such case involved whether a principle of law articulated by the First District Court of Appeal constituted the law of the case in a subsequent appeal. (Nelson v. Tucker Ellis LLP, supra, 48 Cal.App.5th 827, 837-38.) Plaintiff’s citation to In re Marriage of Nicholas (2010) 186 Cal.App.4th 1566 on reply is inapposite because In re Marriage of Nicholas did not even address the applicability of the law of the case doctrine.

Here, there has been no appellate opinion issued in this case because no party has appealed a ruling of this Court. Moreover, the Court did not state a principle of law as to the ownership of Lot 127 in its order on the motion for preliminary injunction. To the contrary, the Court did not definitively find that Plaintiff owned Lot 127. (06/18/24 Minute Order.)

In ruling on the motion for preliminary injunction, the Court found that Plaintiff was likely to prevail on the merits and that Defendants presented no evidence that they possessed title to Lot 127. (06/18/24 Minute Order at p. 3.) However, there was no final order as to the ownership of Lot 127. (06/18/24 Minute Order.) The a preliminary injunction is not a final adjudication; rather it is an interim order to preserve the status quo.

Based on the allegations of the Complaint and Cross-Complaint, the issue of ownership of Lot 127 is in conflict. “A question as to ownership of property may be one of fact or law, depending upon whether the determination of the issue involves a decision upon conflicting facts or upon the application of the law to a stated set of facts.” (Lieb v. Superior Court In and For Orange County (1962) 199 Cal.App.2d 364, 368.) Here, despite the ruling on the request for preliminary injunction, there is clearly a dispute as to who owns Lot 127.

Given that the Court has deemed ownership of Lot 127 to still be at issue, and that the law of the case doctrine is inapplicable, the basis for the demurrer fails.

In addition, Plaintiff’s demurrer is premised on the ownership of Lot 127 but does not address the Road Easement while the first and second causes of action in the Cross-Complaint make allegations concerning both parcels. By only seeking a demurrer only as to Lot 127, Plaintiff’s demurrer has only targeted a portion of the first and second causes of action which is improper. (PH II, Inc. v. Superior Court (1995) 33 Cal.App.4th 1680, 1682 (“[a] demurrer does not lie to a portion of a cause of action.”).)  

C.     First Cause of Action for Quiet Title

An action for quiet title seeks “to establish title against adverse claims to real or personal property or any interest therein.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 760.020, subd. (a).) In an action for quiet title, a plaintiff must plead (1) “[a] description of the property that is the subject of the action,” specifically the location of tangible personal property and the legal description and street address or common designation of real property, (2) “[t]he title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title,” (3) “[t]he adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought,” (4) “[t]he date as of which the determination is sought,” and (5) “[a] prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 761.020.)

Here, the quiet title cause of action seeks to quiet title to Lot 127 and the Road Easement. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 22-24.) Defendants have stated sufficient facts to state a cause of action for quiet title as the requirements of CCP § 761.020 have been met. (Cross-Complaint, ¶¶ 25-29; Cross-Complaint at p. 10:12-24.) Moreover, by filing the Notice of Errata with the attached verification, the Cross-Complaint is now verified.

The Court therefore OVERRULES Plaintiff’s demurrer to the first cause of action in the Cross-Complaint.

D.    Second Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

Initially, the Court notes that while the demurrer challenges the sufficiency of the second cause of action for declaratory relief in the notice of motion, Plaintiff makes no argument in the memorandum of points and authorities concerning the sufficiency of the cause of action for declaratory relief.  The reply brief also fails to make an argument as to the sufficiency of the second cause of action for declaratory relief. Plaintiff has therefore conceded to the sufficiency of the second cause of action because “[c]ontentions are waived when a party fails to support them with reasoned argument and citations to authority.” (Moulton Niguel Water Dist. v. Colombo (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 1210, 1215.)

“To qualify for declaratory relief, a party would have to demonstrate its action presented two essential elements: (1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the party’s rights or obligations.” (Jolley v. Chase Home Finance, LLC (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 872, 909, quotation marks and brackets omitted.)

Here, Defendants have alleged that a dispute exists regarding the parties’ rights, obligations, and duties to use the Cashman Property and Lot 127, which includes the Road Easement. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 31.) Defendants seek a judicial declaration of the parties’ rights with respect to the use of the Cashman Property and Lot 127. (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 32.)

The Court therefore finds that the second cause of action for declaratory relief is sufficiently alleged, and the Court OVERRULES Plaintiff’s demurrer to the second cause of action in the Cross-Complaint.

 E.     Third Cause of Action for Injunctive Relief 

“The elements of a cause of action for injunctive relief are (1) a tort or other wrongful act constituting a cause of action; and (2) irreparable injury, i.e., a factual showing that the wrongful act constitutes an actual or threatened injury to property or personal rights which cannot be compensated by an ordinary damage award.” (Brownfield v. Daniel Freeman Marina Hospital (1989) 208 Cal.App.3d 405, 410, citation omitted.) Notably, “injunctive relief is a remedy and not, in itself, a cause of action, and a cause of action must exist before injunctive relief can be granted.” (Camp v. Board of Supervisors (1981) 123 Cal.App.3d 334, 356.) 

Here, Defendants have stated valid causes of action for quiet title and declaratory relief. Defendants allege that they have no adequate remedy at law for the threatened injuries “including . . . the potential loss of their fences and the removal of mature trees that have grown on their [p]roperty for decades.” (Cross-Complaint, ¶ 35.) 

Injunctive relief, however, is not a cause of action and is merely a remedy. Under Camp v. Board of Supervisors, supra, 123 Cal.App.3d 334, 356, the Court SUSTAINS the demurrer to the third cause of action in the Cross-Complaint WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.  

F.      Motion to Strike 

Plaintiff seeks to strike the causes of action for quiet title and injunctive relief from the Cross-Complaint, and their associated prayers for relief. 

As to the quiet title cause of action, Plaintiff contends that the Cross-Complaint is not verified and cites to Ridec LLC v. Hinkle (2023) 92 Cal.App.5th 1182 (Ridec) for the proposition that a verification must filed alongside the Cross-Complaint. Ridec, however, does not articulate a rule of law that a verification must be filed at the same time as a cross-complaint. (Ridec LLC v. Hinkle, supra, 92 Cal.App.5th 1182, 1196.) 

The Court therefore DENIES Plaintiff’s request to strike the quiet title cause of action and the associated prayer for relief.

 As to the injunctive relief cause of action, the Court agrees with Plaintiff that injunctive relief is not a standalone cause of action. The Court therefore GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to strike such cause of action from the Cross-Complaint and its associated prayer for relief WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND. 

The motion to strike is therefore GRANTED IN PART.

     V. CONCLUSION 

Based on the foregoing, the Court OVERRULES the demurrer of Plaintiff to the first and second causes of action in the Cross-Complaint. The Court SUSTAINS the demurrer of Plaintiff to the third cause of action in the Cross-Complaint WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND.    

The Court GRANTS IN PART the motion to strike filed by Plaintiff. The Court DENIES Plaintiff’s request to strike the quiet title cause of action from the Cross-Complaint. The Court GRANTS Plaintiff’s request to strike the third cause of action for injunctive relief and its associated prayer for relief from the Cross-Complaint WITHOUT LEAVE TO AMEND. 

Plaintiff Rolling Hills Community Association of Rancho Palos Verdes to give notice. 

The demurrer filed by Plaintiff Rolling Hills Community Association of Rancho Palso Verdes on 8/7/2024 is overruled as to the first and second causes of action in the cross-complaint and sustained without leave to amend as to the third cause of action therein. 

The motion to strike filed by Plaintiff Rolling Hills Community Association of Rancho Palos Verdes on 8/7/2024 is granted in part. The Court denies Plaintiff’s request to strike the quiet title cause of action from the Cross-Complaint and its associated prayer for relief. The Court grants Plaintiff’s request to strike the third cause of action for injunctive relief and its associated prayer for relief from the Cross-Complaint without leave to amend. 


Case Number: 24TRCV00666    Hearing Date: September 18, 2024    Dept: P

Having considered non-party County of Los Angeles’s motion and exhibits attached thereto, the court rules as follows: the motion to quash purported service of complaint is granted.

I. PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND

On February 27, 2024, Plaintiff Chaka Theus (“Plaintiff”) filed a Complaint against Defendants Lilianna Navarro (“Navarro”), Raquel Rivas (“Rivas”), Ariel Pounds (“Pounds”), Brandon Nichols (“Nichols”), Cynthia Miller (“Miller”), and Children and Family Services County of LA (“CFS”), alleging causes of action for: (1) general negligence; and (2) intentional tort. The Complaint is set forth on a Form PLD-PI-001.

On June 6, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Proof of Service of Civil Subpoena, which indicates that a civil subpoena was served on Deputy Clerk Kelly Walker on June 5, 2024. (See 06/06/24 Proof of Service.)

On July 17, 2024, Plaintiff filed a Proof of Service. (07/17/24 Proof of Service.) The July 17, 2024 proof of service indicates that a JUD-100 form and CIV-100 form was served on Christina Talamontes by personal service on July 17, 2024. (07/17/24 Minute Order.)

On August 6, 2024, non-party County of Los Angeles (“County”), specially appearing, filed and served the instant Motion to Quash Purported Service of Complaint. County moves “to quash the purported service of the Complaint by Plaintiff . . . on County’s employees, directors, and Departments in this case.” (Not. of Mot. at p. 1:5-7.)

On August 12, 2024, Defendants Pounds and Rivas filed and served a Notice of Joinder indicating that they join the motion filed by non-party County.

On August 13, 2024, Defendant Nichols filed a Notice of Joinder indicating that he joins the motion filed by non-party County. The notice of joinder filed by Defendant Nichols was served on Plaintiff on August 8, 2024.

As of September 13, 2024, no opposition has been filed to the motion to quash. Any opposition to the motion was required to have been filed and served at least nine court days prior to the hearing. (Code Civ. Proc., § 1005, subd. (b).)

An OSC re: Failure to File Proof of Service, Failure to Prosecute, and Failure to File Case Management Conference Statement is set for hearing on September 18, 2024.

II. FACTUAL BACKGROUND

Plaintiff alleges that due to the professional negligence of Defendants Navarro, Rivas, Miller, Pounds, and Nichols, she was placed in harms way by providing a known gang member her statement and name. (Complaint at p. 4.) Plaintiff alleges that she is “being harassed, [her] apartment is being broken into, car vandalize[d] and [her] life [is] being threaten[ed].” (Complanit at p. 4.)

III. LEGAL STANDARD

“A defendant who takes the position that the service of summons as made upon him did not bring him within the jurisdiction of the court, may serve and file a notice of motion to quash the service.” (Coulston v. Cooper (1966) 245 Cal.App.2d 866, 868.) “The effect of such notice is to place upon the plaintiff the burden of proving the facts that did give the court jurisdiction, that is the facts requisite to an effective service.” (Ibid.) “A defendant, on or before the last day of his or her time to plead or within any further time that the court may for good cause allow, may serve and file a notice of motion . . . [t]o quash service of summons on the ground of lack of jurisdiction of the court over him or her.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10, subd. (a)(1).) Personal service is accomplished when a copy of the summons and complaint is delivered to the person to be served. (Code Civ. Proc., § 415.10.)

IV. DISCUSSION

Moving Party’s Arguments

Non-party County alleges that the “Children and Family Services County of LA” is not a suable entity, and that the purported service efforts are not effective as to any defendant. The joining defendants argue in their notices of joinder that the purported service efforts are not effective as to any defendant.

Responding Party’s Opposition

No opposition filed as of September 13, 2024.

Moving Party’s Reply

No reply brief filed as of September 13, 2024.

Merits of the Motion

Initially, the Court notes that none of the proofs of service filed by Plaintiff in this action show service of the summons and complaint on any defendant. Thus, based on the record before the Court, no defendant has been served in this action with the summons and complaint. While the Court recognizes that Plaintiff is self-represented, a self-represented litigant is “held to the same standards as attorneys.” (Kobayashi v. Superior Court (2009) 175 Cal.App.4th 536, 543.)

Here, non-party County argues that “Children and Family Services County of LA” is not a suable entity for purposes of a money damages lawsuit as it is not a public entity. A public entity “includes the state, the Regents of the University of California, the Trustees of the California State University and the California University, a county, city, district, public authority, and any other political subdivision or public corporation in the State.” (Gov. Code § 811.2.) A county may sue and be sued. (Gov. Code, § 23004, subd. (a).)

Although non-party County is not named in the Complaint, County argues that Plaintiff is suing affiliated entitles and that non-party County is a public entity.

While the Court finds that Plaintiff has not served the summons and complaint on any party to an action, a motion to quash service of summons can only be made by a party to an action. (Code Civ. Proc., § 418.10.) Based on non-party County’s motion, it appears that County concedes and recognizes that it is a public entity and that it should be named in the Complaint instead of Defendant Children and Family Services County of LA.

Thus, taken together, non-party County and—by joining in the motion—Defendants Pounds, Rivas, and Nichols all argue that no defendant has been properly served in this action. The Court agrees. Plaintiff has failed to oppose the motion and “[a] failure to oppose a motion may be deemed consent to the granting of the motion.” (Cal. R. Ct., Rule 8.54(c).) The Court therefore finds that Plaintiff has conceded to the arguments raised in the motion. By failing to oppose the motion, Plaintiff has not met her burden of showing that service was proper.

V. CONCLUSION

Based on the foregoing, the motion to quash filed by non-party County of Los Angeles is GRANTED.

Non-party County of Los Angeles to give notice.

The Motion to Quash Purported Service of Complaint filed by non-party County of Los Angeles on 08/06/2024 is granted.
