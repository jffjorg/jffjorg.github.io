Case Number: 19LBCV00255    Hearing Date: February 22, 2024    Dept: S27

Plaintiff Jeron Green, Sr. moves for reconsideration of the Court’s October 3, 2023 order pursuant to California Code of Procedure Section 1008. Plaintiff has failed to file proof of service of his motion for reconsideration. Accordingly, Plaintiff’s motion is DENIED. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice. 

 

Parties who intend to submit on this tentative must send an email to the court at gdcdepts27@lacourt.org indicating intention to submit on the tentative as directed by the instructions provided on the court website at www.lacourt.org.  If the department does not receive an email indicating the parties are submitting on the tentative and there are no appearances at the hearing, the motion may be placed off calendar.  If a party submits on the tentative, the party’s email must include the case number and must identify the party submitting on the tentative. If any party does not submit on the tentative, the party should make arrangements to appear remotely at the hearing on this matter.  


Case Number: 20LBCV00129    Hearing Date: February 22, 2024    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiffs, Michael, Injin and Nicholas Park filed this action against Defendants, Foasberg Laundry and Cleaners, Inc., Richard D. Foasberg, the Estate of Richard D. Foasberg, Marisa Foasberg, Lynette Jean Vavrek, and JRJ Limited Partnership for damages arising out of contamination on Defendants’ property for which Plaintiffs have incurred removal and/or remedial action costs in connection with the Hazardous Substance Account Act. 

 

Plaintiffs filed their complaint on March 3, 2020, and Defendants filed an answer on October 7, 2020.  Plaintiffs’ complaint includes causes of action for California Superfund Act, Negligence, Trespass, Nuisance, Breach of Contract, and Declaratory Relief.  The trial is currently set for April 8, 2024.

 

Defendants have cross-complained against, among others, R.R. Street & Co., Inc., contending it is responsible in whole or in part for the subject contamination.  There are multiple cross-complaints between the parties. 

 

On January 24, 2024, Foasberg, Estate of Richard D. Foasberg, and JRJ Limited Partnership (“moving parties”) filed the instant motion to continue trial and all related discovery and motion deadlines. On February 7, 2024, R.R. filed an opposition. On February 8, 2024, Plaintiffs filed a notice of non-opposition. On February 14, 2024, moving parties filed a reply.

 

On February 5, 2024, Intervenors Twin City Fire Insurance Company and Hartford Fire Insurance Company filed a joinder to moving parties’ motion to continue trial.

 

2.     Motion to Continue Trial

a.     Legal Standard to Continue Trial

Trial dates are firm to ensure prompt disposition of civil cases. (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (a).)¿ Continuances are thus generally disfavored.¿ (See id., rule 3.1332, subd. (b).)¿ Nevertheless, the trial court has discretion to continue trial dates.¿ (Hernandez v. Superior Court (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 1242, 1246.)¿ Each request for continuance must be considered on its own merits and is granted upon an affirmative showing of good cause.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (c); Hernandez, supra, 115 Cal.App.4th at p. 1246.)¿¿¿ 

 

Circumstances that may indicate good cause include: (1) the unavailability of an essential lay or expert witness due to death, illness, or other excusable circumstances; (2) the unavailability of a party due to death, illness, or other excusable circumstances; (3) the unavailability of trial counsel due to death, illness, or other excusable circumstances; (4) the substitution of trial counsel where there is an affirmative showing that the substitution is required in the interests of justice; (5) the addition of a new party if (A) the new party has not had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial, or (B) the other parties have not had a reasonable opportunity to conduct discovery and prepare for trial in regard to the new party’s involvement in the case; (6) a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts; or (7) a significant, unanticipated change in the status of the case as a result of which the case is not ready for trial.¿ (Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (c).)¿¿ 

 

The court must also consider such relevant factors as: (1) the proximity of the trial date; (2) whether there was any previous continuance, extension of time, or delay of trial caused by any party; (3) the length of the continuance requested; (4) the availability of alternative means to address the problem that gave rise to the motion or application for a continuance; (5) the prejudice that parties or witnesses will suffer as a result of the continuance; (6) if the case is entitled to a preferential trial setting, the reasons for that status and whether the need for a continuance outweighs the need to avoid delay; (7) the court’s calendar and the impact of granting a continuance on other pending trials; (8) whether trial counsel is engaged in another trial; (9) whether all parties have stipulated to a continuance; (10) whether the interests of justice are best served by a continuance, by the trial of the matter, or by imposing conditions on the continuance; and (11) any other fact or circumstance relevant to the fair determination of the motion or application.¿ (Id., at rule 3.1332, subd. (d).)

 

b.     Parties’ Positions

Moving parties move the Court for an Order continuing the trial and all related dates by 12 to eighteen 18 months from the current trial date of April 8, 2024, as the Court’s calendar allows. In the alternative, they seek an Order vacating the current April 8, 2024 trial date and setting a status conference for the third quarter of 2024 to check in with the parties on the status of the environmental investigation and the litigation as a whole.

 

Moving parties contend that they have been working diligently since 2021 to complete the environmental investigation at the site where the contamination at issue in the lawsuit is taking place but have faced numerous delays caused by obtaining regulatory oversight by the Los Angeles Regional Water Quality Control Board and issues with the Long Beach Department of Public Works providing permits for the investigative work. As the result of these delays, moving parties contend that the case is not ready for trial. They contend that critical elements of the claims could not be determined at an overly premature trial, damages would not be fully ascertained, and the result would almost certainly be a mistrial.

 

In their notice of non-opposition, Plaintiffs state that they cannot agree to moving parties’ requested time frame for continuance. Instead, they request the trial be moved to a time between November, 2024 and February, 2025. Additionally, concurrently with their notice of non-opposition, Plaintiffs are filing a request to remove the litigation to the complex department based on the “highly technical issues present in this matter.” 

 

In opposition, R.R. contends moving parties have not been diligent in pursuing this litigation. R.R. contends moving parties did not start the environmental investigation of the site as early as they should have when, as early as April 3, 2018, nearly six years ago, Western Environmental Engineers Company submitted a detailed environmental site assessment of the subject property to Plaintiffs, showing that the levels of PCE, TCE, and chloroform contained in the soil vapor at the site are above acceptable levels. According to R.R., moving parties should have aware of this report when it was delivered to Plaintiffs or at the very latest when this complaint was filed.  R.R. contends that given that the current trial date is more than four years after this case was initiated, it is too late to continue trial. Furthermore, R.R. contends that any prerequisite requirements to trials concerning claims under the Hazardous Substance Account Act, including the completion of a Remedial Investigation and Feasibility Study and the selection of a remedy, do not apply to R.R. in this case as the HSAA cross-claims against R.R. were disposed of on summary judgment and the only remaining claims are common-law causes of action.

 

Moving parties contend R.R. has failed to adequately participate in the pre-trial discovery process. Moving parties’ damages for its products liability claim are dependent on the costs to remediate the site, and those costs cannot be adequately determined at this stage without a full delineation of the contamination at the site. Moving parties also dispute that they delayed in beginning the environmental investigation process.

 

c.     Good Cause

To grant a request to continue trial, the court must find good cause. For the reasons described below, the Court finds that good cause exists to continue trial by 12 months.

 

            i. Moving Parties’ Diligence

 

Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (c) lists seven factors that must be evaluated in order to determine whether good cause exists. The only factor relevant here is the sixth factor: a party’s excused inability to obtain essential testimony, documents, or other material evidence despite diligent efforts. The parties dispute whether moving parties were diligent in conducting an environmental inspection upon becoming aware of the environmental problems at the subject property.

 

Moving parties hired environmental consultant Genesis Engineering & Redevelopment, Inc., to prepare and conduct an environmental investigation of the accident site for the purpose of delineating the extent of contamination, conducting a Remedial Investigation and Feasibility Study (“RI/FS”), and eventually developing, selecting, and implementing an appropriate and cost-effective remedy. (Van der Hoven Decl., ¶ 2.) Genesis created a Soil Vapor and Groundwater Assessment Work Plan (“SVGAWP”) on February 22, 2021 to assess the extent of the problems and how to address them. (Van der Hoven Decl., ¶ 5c.) In August 2021, moving parties began implementing the plan by conducting indoor air vapor intrusion sampling at the site, which showed no significant indoor vapor intrusion concern. (Van der Hoven Decl. ¶ 5c.) That same month, they installed four soil vapor borings to assess the lateral and vertical extent of PCE impacts in soil vapor and determined that PCE was above concentration levels but TCE detected in one sample was not above the commercial screening level. (Van der Hoven Decl. ¶ 5d.) Moving parties contend they subsequently ran into various delays caused by regulatory oversight that they could not avoid.

 

First, they went through significant delays in carrying out aspects of the SVGAWP that required them to obtain permits from a regulatory agency, such as for the installation of groundwater monitoring wells. After attempts to use various other agencies, moving parties eventually obtained the Los Angeles Regional Water Quality Control Board to assume jurisdiction over the environmental investigation of the site. (Radford Decl. ¶ 6.) The RWQCB did not assign a case manager until November 18, 2022, after which Genesis promptly submitted the SVGAWP to the RWQCB on January 31, 2023. (Radford Decl. ¶ 7.) Subsequently, the RWQCB issued a comment letter on May 5, 2023, requesting a revised version of the SVGAWP by June, 2023 with an expanded scope of work. (Radford Decl. ¶ 9.) Moving parties sent a revised plan on June 9, 2023 and on October 26, 2023, the RWQCB finally approved the Revised Work Plan. (Radford Decl. ¶ 11.) However, this approval included recommendations for a second expansion of the SVGAWP, including conducting public outreach and conducting public crawl space and garage air sampling at neighboring properties six months apart, in the wet and dry seasons. (Radford Decl. ¶ 11.) Furthermore, even currently, moving parties are facing hurdles in implementing the revisions. On November 16, 2023, the City of Long Beach Department of Public Works denied moving parties’ renewed permit for the installation of a groundwater monitoring well on a public right-of-way adjacent to the site, citing for the first time problems with their insurance information. (Radford Decl. ¶ 12.)

 

The second category of delay moving parties faced was that they were not able to sit down with the adjacent property owner, Bob Blair of Blair Properties, until late 2022. (Radford Decl. ¶ 8.) Once they did, Blair required that Genesis and Foasberg’s counsel meet with him in September, 2022 to provide a presentation regarding the scope of work called for in the SVGAWP. (Radford Decl. ¶ 8.) When moving parties and Genesis met with Blair, Blair brought his own environmental consultant, Boyer Consultants. (Radford Decl. ¶ 8.) After the presentation, Blair demanded that moving parties significantly expand the scope of the investigation to include indoor air sampling for all residential units at the neighboring properties. (Radford Decl. ¶ 8.) Blair took the position that access would not be granted until such a time as moving parties agreed to the expanded, and costly, additional scope of work. (Radford Decl. ¶ 8.)

 

Despite these delays, moving parties contend they are still diligently attempting to carry out the remainder of the SVGAWP. Genesis anticipates that it will commence field work in February of 2024. (Van der Hoven Decl. ¶ 5e.) Genesis estimates that, as of the filing of this Motion, that the environmental investigation at the site is approximately 35% completed. (Van der Hoven Decl. ¶ 5e.) As such, Genesis estimates that the delineation of the site will be completed in late 2024 or early 2025; however, that timeframe could increase if the revised plan indicates that additional investigation is needed to fully delineate the site. (Van der Hoven Decl. ¶ 5e.) A study to determine the proper remedy will begin after that.

 

R.R.’s contention in opposition that moving parties contributed to the delay is unsupported by the evidence described above. Moving parties’ failure to fully complete the inspection by this time does not evidence a lack of diligence in attempting to complete the inspection.

 

Based on moving parties’ arguments, it appears that they have been diligent in attempting to carry out the environmental inspection of the subject site. This factor thus weighs in favor of granting a continuance.

d.     Additional Factors

Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1332, subd. (d) lists additional factors to consider in determining whether good cause exists to continue trial.

           

1.     Proximity of the Trial Date

Here, the current trial date is April 8, 2024, less than two months away. This is not sufficient time for moving parties to complete the environmental inspection in order for the jury to assess the extent of the damages. This factor weighs in favor of granting a continuance.

           

2.     Evidence of Prior Continuances Granted on the Same Grounds

This Court has previously granted a trial continuance on virtually the same grounds, on April 8, 2021, when it granted moving parties’ initial motion to continue trial, vacating the trial date of August 9, 2021. (Radford Decl., ¶ 4f.)

           

3.     Length of Continuance Requested is Reasonable and Best Serves the Interests of Justice

Here, moving parties request a continuance of 12-18 months to allow them to finish conducting the environmental inspection. The length of the continuance requested is directly tied to the issue that led moving parties to file the request for continuance and thus is a reasonable request.

 

4.     The Issues Necessitating Moving Parties’ Continuance Request Cannot Be Addressed by Alternative Means

Moving parties contend there is currently no other alternative, quicker means to investigate the contamination of the site and to prepare this matter for trial. (Radford Decl., ¶ 18.) This factor supports granting a continuance.

 

5.     Prejudice to The Parties Will Occur if a Trial Date Continuance is Denied

Moving parties contend that all parties will be prejudiced to some degree if the continuance is denied and the trial proceeds in April, 2024 because they may be forced to participate in two trials, one based on liability and one based on the costs of the inspection and remedy. (Radford Decl., ¶¶ 19, 21.) Furthermore, they argue that no parties will be significantly prejudiced as Plaintiffs have not opposed the motion and R.R. does not point to any significant prejudice.

 

In opposition, R.R. fails to demonstrate that it will be prejudiced if a continuance is granted, other than that it will incur further legal expenses. This prejudice is not significant enough to justify the prejudice that would result to moving parties were the case to proceed to trial before they could complete an environmental inspection. This factor supports granting a continuance.

 

e.     Ripeness of Environmental Claims

Moving parties contend that because this case is based on environmental claims, it is not ripe for trial until such a time as an RI/FS has been completed and a remedy has been selected. Moving parties contend that the HSAA expressly incorporates the liability standards set forth in the Comprehensive Environmental Response, Compensation, and Liability Act and CERCLA requires the completion of an RI/FS and the formulation of a remedy.

 

In opposition, R.R. contends that this argument does not apply to R.R. because the only remaining causes of action against R.R. are common law causes of action, not claims arising out of environmental statutes.

           

As the Court has determined there are other grounds for granting a continuance, the Court will not consider the merits of moving parties’ arguments based on ripeness.

 

f.      Conclusion

The motion to continue the trial is granted. The trial is continued to February 24, 2025 and FSC to February 21, 2025 at 8:30 AM.

Case Number: 22STCV11375    Hearing Date: February 22, 2024    Dept: S27

1.         Background Facts 

 

On April 4, 2022, Plaintiff, ZRP Group, LLC filed this action against Defendants, City of Long Beach, Sami Kherimi, and Does 1-20 for damages arising out of a flood that occurred when the City energized the fire suppression system at Plaintiff’s business location. Kherimi is the City employee who energized the system.   

 

After Plaintiff’s complaint was filed, Falls Lake National Insurance Company filed a complaint in subrogation. On December 12, 2023, the Court dismissed Falls Lake’s complaint with prejudice. Additionally, the City filed a cross-complaint against Long Beach Acquisitions, LLC and Shirley Vancamp, Plaintiff’s landlord, for indemnification and related claims.  Long Beach Acquisitions filed a cross-complaint against the City. On January 30, 2024, the Court dismissed both cross-complaints without prejudice.  

 

 

Plaintiff's complaint alleges the following causes of action:  

 

1.   Breach of Mandatory Duty (Government Code Section 815.6 and California Plumbing Code, Chapter I Inspections and Testing, Sections 105.3 and 105.3.1) (against the City and Does 1-20) 

 

2.   Negligence (Government Code Section 820.4) (against Kherimi, employees of the City, and Does 1-20) 

 

2.         Motion for Summary Judgment 

 

a.             Moving Parties' Positions 

 

On September 20, 2023, the City and Kherimi (“Moving parties”) filed the instant motion for summary judgment, or in the alternative, summary adjudication.  

 

i. Breach of Mandatory Duty 

Moving parties contend the claim for breach of mandatory duty fails as a matter of law for the following reasons:  

o  California Code of Regulations (“CCR”) Title 19, § 904.2(h) does not impose a duty, mandatory or otherwise, on the City,  

o a City water valve is not a “fire extinguishing system” and, thus, § 904.2(h) is inapplicable,  

o Khemiri’s act of exercising a City water valve does not amount to a fire sprinkler system test and, thus, there were not duties imposed on Khemiri under § 904.2(h),  

o CCR Title 19, § 903.2.1.2 does not impose a duty, mandatory or otherwise, on the City,  

o Under § 903.2.1.2, Plaintiff was required by law to maintain any existing fire sprinkler system on its property in operable condition, at all times,  

o CCR Title 24, § 105.3 does not create any affirmative duty,  

o § 105.3 is inapplicable because Plaintiff’s fire sprinkler system was not set up for “exhibition purposes”,  

o §§ 105.3 and 105.3.1 impose no mandatory duty on the City,  

o the City’s water valve and Plaintiff’s “fire extinguishing systems” do not amount to plumbing systems under §§ 105.3 and 105.3.1.  

 

ii. Negligence 

Moving parties contend the claim for negligence fails as a matter of law for the following reasons:  

o  As to Kherimi, the complaint fail to state sufficient facts, 

o Assuming arguendo that Plaintiff has pled a viable negligence claim, Khemiri is nevertheless immune from liability under Government Code §§ 850.2 and 850.4, 

o Assuming arguendo that Plaintiff has pled a viable negligence claim, Khemiri is entitled to judgment as a matter of law because Plaintiff’s negligence was the cause of its own alleged damages,  

o As to the City, there is no evidence that City owed a duty to Plaintiffs and because City has no vicarious liability.   

 

b.             Plaintiff’s Positions in Opposition 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion for summary judgment on the following grounds.  

 

i. Breach of Mandatory Duty 

As to the claim for breach of mandatory duty, Plaintiff contends that a triable issue of fact as to whether there was a breach of CCR Title 19, § 904.2(h) exists because § 904.2(h) applies to City Water Department employees, the subject water valve is part of the automatic fire extinguishing system, and thus Khemiri was performing a fire sprinkler system test at the time of the accident.   

 

As to liability based on CCR Title 19, § 903.2.1.2, Plaintiff does not dispute that this does not give rise to a cause of action.  

 

As to liability based on plumbing regulations, Plaintiff agrees that § 105.3 does not apply because this was not an exhibition but contends that CCR §105.3.1 applies because it requires an air test for the sanitary condition of drainage or plumbing where there is reason to believe that it has become defective and here, moving parties had reason to believe the plumbing system was defective. 

 

ii. Negligence  

As to negligence, Plaintiff contends that Khemiri failed to adhere to the Long Beach Water Department Standard Operating Procedure by failing to call his supervisor for permission to rotate or operate the water valve on the day of the subject accident and failing to investigate or inquire further about the valve before operating on it. Plaintiff puts forth the testimony of Dwayne McGee, a water utility mechanic who has worked with the City of Long Beach Water Department for 19 years and has supervised Khemiri, to support its position that Khemiri breached his duty of care.  

 

Plaintiff also contends that immunity under Government Code Sections 850.2 and 850.4 does not apply because negligence here is based on Khemiri’s failures, not defective equipment.  

 

c.             Moving Parties’ Positions in Reply 

 

In reply, moving parties contend that Plaintiff’s mandatory duty claim fails because the enactments upon which it is premised do not impose any duty on moving parties.  

 

Moving parties contend the negligence claim against Khemiri fails because Government Code §§ 850.2 and 850.4 provide immunity not only to defective fire protection equipment and facilities, but also extend to acts of negligence by nonfirefighting employees. Thus, regardless of whether Plaintiff’s injuries are viewed as resulting from the condition of the subject valve or from the alleged negligent maintenance of the valve, Khemiri is immune from liability under Government Code §§ 850.2 and 850.4. 

 

d.             Law Governing Motions for Summary Judgment 

 

Summary judgment is proper “if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.”  (Code Civ. Proc. §437c(c).)  Where a defendant seeks summary judgment or adjudication, he must show that either “one or more elements of the cause of action, even if not separately pleaded, cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.”  (Id. at §437c(o)(2).)  A defendant may satisfy this burden by showing that the claim “cannot be established” because of the lack of evidence on some essential element of the claim.  (Union Bank v. Superior Court (1995) 31 Cal.App.4th 574, 590.)  Once the defendant meets this burden, the burden shifts to the plaintiff to show that a “triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action or defense thereto.”  (Ibid.)   

 

The moving party bears the initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing that there are no triable issues of material fact.  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  A defendant moving for summary judgment must show either (1) that one or more elements of the cause of action cannot be established or (2) that there is a complete defense to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p).)  A defendant may discharge this burden by furnishing either (1) affirmative evidence of the required facts or (2) discovery responses conceding that the plaintiff lacks evidence to establish an essential element of the plaintiff's case. If a defendant chooses the latter option he or she must present evidence “and not simply point out that plaintiff does not possess and cannot reasonably obtain needed evidence….” Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 865-66, 

 

[A] defendant may simply show the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action “by showing that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence.” (Id. at p. 854.)  Thus, rather than affirmatively disproving or negating an element (e.g., causation), a defendant moving for summary judgment has the option of presenting evidence reflecting the plaintiff does not possess evidence to prove that element. “The defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an element of the plaintiff's cause of action. The defendant may also present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing” to support an essential element of his case. (Aguilar, supra, at p. 855.) Under the latter approach, a defendant's initial evidentiary showing may “consist of the deposition testimony of the plaintiff's witnesses, the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses, or admissions by the plaintiff in deposition or in response to requests for admission that he or she has not discovered anything that supports an essential element of the cause of action.” (Lona v. Citibank, N.A., supra, 202 Cal.App.4th at p. 110.)  In other words, a defendant may show the plaintiff does not possess evidence to support an element of the cause of action by means of presenting the plaintiff's factually devoid discovery responses from which an absence of evidence may be reasonably inferred. (Scheiding v. Dinwiddie Construction Co. (1999) 69 Cal.App.4th 64, 83.) 

 

Thus, a moving defendant has two means by which to shift the burden of proof under the summary judgment statute: “The defendant may rely upon factually insufficient discovery responses by the plaintiff to show that the plaintiff cannot establish an essential element of the cause of action sued upon. [Citation.] [Or a]lternatively, the defendant may utilize the tried and true technique of negating (‘disproving’) an essential element of the plaintiff's cause of action.” (Brantly v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1598.)  Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1103. 

 

Until the moving defendant has discharged its burden of proof, the opposing plaintiff has no burden to come forward with any evidence. Once the moving defendant has discharged its burden as to a particular cause of action, however, the plaintiff may defeat the motion by producing evidence showing that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to that cause of action.  (Id. at §437c(p)(2).)  On a motion for summary judgment, the moving party's supporting documents are strictly construed and those of his opponent liberally construed, and doubts as to the propriety of summary judgment should be resolved against granting the motion.  (D’Amico v. Board of Medical Examiners (1974) 11 Cal.3d 1, 21.) 

 

e.             Objections 

 

The objections are moot as they are not required to reach the conclusion here.  

 

f.              Analysis 

 

The motion is granted.   

 

1. Breach of Mandatory Duty 

Plaintiff’s first cause of action for breach of mandatory duty is based on the theory that moving parties’ violations of various municipal code sections subjects it to liability under Government Code Section 815.6.   

 

Government Code section 815.6 states: “Where a public entity is under a mandatory duty imposed by an enactment that is designed to protect against the risk of a particular kind of injury, the public entity is liable for an injury of that kind proximately caused by its failure to discharge the duty unless the public entity establishes that it exercised reasonable diligence to discharge the duty.” (Haggis v. City of Los Angeles, 22 Cal. 4th 490, 498, 993 P.2d 983, 987 (2000).) To be liable under section 815.6 requires that the enactment at issue be obligatory, rather than merely discretionary or permissive, in its directions to the public entity; it must require, rather than merely authorize or permit, that a particular action be taken or not taken. (Id. at 993.) It is not enough, moreover, that the public entity or officer have been under an obligation to perform a function if the function itself involves the exercise of discretion. (Ibid.)  

 

Plaintiff claims that one way the City breached a mandatory duty was by failing to comply with C.C.R. § 904.2(h). Section 904.2(h) provides that “[a]t the time of testing and maintenance [of an automatic fire extinguishing systems], building management shall be consulted to avoid unnecessary disturbance of normal business operation, and to contact the local fire authority prior to testing.” (Cal. Code Regs., tit. 19, § 904.2(a), (h)). Section 904.2(h) places a duty on persons a plaintiff has, or should have, retained to inspect and test its fire suppression system.  

 

Moving parties contend that CCR § 904.2(h) does not impose any duty on the City, a public entity, because the statute is not directed at public entities. They argue that Khemiri is a Water Utility Mechanic, not a specialty contractor or other licensee authorized to perform inspection, testing, and maintenance of fire extinguishing systems within the meaning of Section 901. (19 C.C.R. 5, §§ 901, 905.) They contend that Plaintiff, not the City or Khemiri, had the duty to have Plaintiff’s sprinklers routinely inspected, tested, and maintained.  

 

Plaintiff argues that CCR § 904.2(h) does not limit who has to give notice of testing. However, Plaintiff offers no support for this contention. Accordingly, moving parties successfully meet their burden to show there is no triable issue as to whether CCR § 904.2(h) imposes a mandatory duty on moving parties. Here, it does not.  

 

CCR § 105.3.1 requires an air test for the sanitary condition of drainage or plumbing where there is reason to believe that it has become defective. Moving parties also contend that CCR § 105.3.1 does not impose a mandatory duty on moving parties. Plaintiff contends that the City breached a mandatory duty by failing to comply with CCR § 105.3.1 and that §105.3.1 does apply to moving parties. However, Plaintiff again supplies no caselaw for the contention that it CCR § 105.3.1 applies. Accordingly, moving parties meet their burden of demonstrating there is no triable issue of fact as to whether CCR § 105.3.1 creates a mandatory duty for moving parties.  

 

Moving parties successfully demonstrate that they had no mandatory duty regarding the water valve and fire extinguishing system. Thus, summary judgment is granted as to the first cause of action for breach of mandatory duty as to the City and summary adjudication is granted as to the first cause of action against Khemiri for breach of mandatory duty. 

 

2. Negligence 

Plaintiff’s second cause of action for negligence is based on the theory that Khemiri breached his duty of care by failing to provide notice to Plaintiff or the building’s owner prior turning on the fire suppression system.  

¿ 

“The elements of negligence are (1) a legal duty to use due care, (2) the breach of such legal duty, and (3) the breach was the proximate or legal cause of injury.”¿ (Orey v. Superior Court (2013) 213 Cal.App.4th 1241, 1255.)¿ “In California, the causation element of negligence is satisfied when the plaintiff establishes (1) that the defendant's breach of duty (his negligent act or omission) was a substantial factor in bringing about the plaintiff's harm and (2) that there is no rule of law relieving the defendant of liability.”¿ (Leslie G. v. Perry & Associates (1996) 43 Cal.App.4th 472, 481.)¿¿“Except as otherwise provided by statute (including Section 820.2), a public employee is liable for injury caused by his act or omission to the same extent as a private person.”¿ (Gov. Code § 820(a).)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

“While proximate cause ordinarily is a question of fact, it may be decided as a question of law if ' “under the undisputed facts, there is no room for a reasonable difference of opinion.” ' [Citation.]”¿ (Grotheer v. Escape Adventures, Inc. (2017) 14 Cal.App.5th 1283, 1303 [affirming grant of summary judgment on causation].)¿ “To establish the element of actual causation, it must be shown that the defendant's act or omission was a substantial factor in bringing about the injury. [Citation.]”¿ (Padilla v. Rodas (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 742, 752 [affirming grant of summary judgment for plaintiff's failure to establish causation]; see also Leyva v. Garcia (2018) 20 Cal.App.5th 1095, 1104 [“In order for a plaintiff to satisfy the causation element of a negligence cause of action, he or she must show the defendant's act or omission was a substantial factor in bringing about the plaintiff's harm.”].)¿¿¿ 

¿ 

“The law does not require that negligence of the defendant must be the sole cause of the injury complained of in order to entitle the plaintiff to damages therefor. All that is required in either respect is that the negligence in question shall be a proximate cause of the injury complained of.”¿ (Modica v. Crist (1954) 129 Cal.App.2d 144, 148 [internal quotations and citations omitted]; see also Condon v. Ansaldi, 203 Cal. 180, 182-83 [although driver’s negligence was not sole cause of pedestrian’s injury, the pedestrian could hold the driver liable if driver’s negligence was proximate cause of injury].)¿ In certain circumstances, a plaintiff's conduct can be the “sole” proximate cause of his or her own harm.¿ (See, e.g., Fredette v. City of Long Beach (1986) 187 Cal.App.3d 122, 133 [“the situation ... involved the negligence of plaintiff as the sole and proximate cause of the accident”]; Bell v. Seatrain Lines, Inc. (1974) 40 Cal.App.3d 16, 29 [jury was correctly instructed there should be no recovery “if plaintiff's own negligence was the sole proximate cause of his injury”].)¿ 

 

Moving parties contend that Plaintiff fails to demonstrate Khemiri owed a duty to Plaintiff. In opposition, Plaintiff contends Khemiri failed to discharge his duty to act like a reasonable water utility mechanic by investigating or inquiring into the water valve. The allegations regarding Khemiri sufficiently allege that Khemiri failed to act with the requisite level of care after discovering that the water valve had not been turned on for three years. Thus, the negligence claim properly alleges that Khemiri owed a duty to Plaintiff and breached that duty by failing to follow the proper protocols.  

 

Moving parties further contend that Khemiri is immune from liability under Government Code §§ 850.2 and 850.4.  

 

 “Neither a public entity nor a public employee is liable for failure to establish a fire department or otherwise to provide fire protection service.”  (Gov. Code § 850.) “Neither a public entity that has undertaken to provide fire protection service, nor an employee of such a public entity, is liable for any injury resulting from the failure to provide or maintain sufficient personnel, equipment or other fire protection facilities.” (Gov. Code § 850.2.) “Neither a public entity, nor a public employee acting in the scope of his employment, is liable for any injury resulting from the condition of fire protection or firefighting equipment or facilities or . . . for any injury caused in fighting fires.”  (Gov. Code § 850.4.)   

 

The fire protection service statutes are construed to “‘provide for a broad immunity from liability for injuries resulting in connection with fire protection service.’” Cairns v. County of Los Angeles (1997) 62 Cal.App.4th 330, 335.  In Varshock v. Department of Forestry & Fire Protection (2011) 194 Cal.App.4th 635, the court examined the language of Government Code section 850.4 and concluded “that by relieving public entities and employees of liability ‘for any injury caused in fighting fires’ under section 850.4, the Legislature intended immunity to apply to any claim based on death, personal injury, or property damage that results from an act or omission of a public entity or employee while responding to or combating an actual fire.”  Varshock, supra, at 642.  Courts have applied section 850.4 broadly to a wide range of government activities.  See, e.g., Cairns, supra, 62 Cal.App.4th at 336 (statutory immunity applied to bar nuisance claim stemming from city’s and county’s continued road closure that made firefighter ingress and egress virtually impossible); People ex rel. Grijalva v. Superior Court (2008) 159 Cal.App.4th 1072, 1078 (statutory immunity applied to bar affirmative defenses including comparative negligence against complaint filed by California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection to recoup firefighting costs, even though the department’s premature demobilization of firefighting resources caused the fire’s severity); Bettencourt v. State of California (1975) 51 Cal.App.3d 892, 894 (government immunity for loss of cattle that escaped through wire fence cut by firefighters to fight grass fire). 

 

Here, the fire protection statutes provide immunity to Khemiri. The subject valve fed the fire line that serviced both Plaintiff’s property and the property adjacent to it. (UMF 63.) Khemiri feared that these buildings may have been without adequate fire protection for nearly three years. (UMF 65, 67.) His decision to exercise the valve, ascertain its position, and ensure that it still worked brings his actions within the type contemplated by the immunity statutes. Thus, immunity applies and Khemiri is entitled to summary adjudication on the second cause of action for negligence.  

 

Conclusion 

 

The City of Long Beach’s motion for summary judgment as to the first cause of action for breach of mandatory duty is GRANTED. Sami Khemiri’s motion for summary judgment is GRANTED as to the first and second causes of action for breach of mandatory duty and negligence.
