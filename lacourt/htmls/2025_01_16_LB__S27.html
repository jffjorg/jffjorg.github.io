DEPARTMENT S27 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS



Case Number: 22LBCV0731    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: S27

1.     Complaint

Plaintiffs, James and Cynthia Setka filed this action against Defendants, Ford Motor Company and Southbay Ford, Inc. for damages under the Song-Beverly Act and for negligent repairs. 

 

2.     Motion for Attorneys’ Fees

a.     Parties’ Positions

Plaintiffs seek to recover attorneys’ fees and costs incurred in this litigation in the total amount of $128,902.62, which includes a 1.35 multiplier.  Plaintiffs contend they are entitled to an award of fees and costs per Civil Code §1794(d). 

 

Defendant, in opposition to the motion, argues Plaintiffs’ attorneys’ hourly rates are inflated, the request includes unreasonable and unnecessary tasks, there is no need for a lodestar enhancement, and costs should not be awarded.    

 

Plaintiffs, in reply, contend Defendant is referencing the rates of other lemon law lawyers who are not Plaintiffs’ attorney in this case, all fees are justified, and Defendant has not met its burden to show the rates should be reduced.  Plaintiffs contend they were not required to file a memorandum of costs. 

 

b.     Hourly Rate 

Plaintiffs support their motion with the declaration of their attorneys, Payam Shahian and Angel M. Baker.  Counsel concedes the action was contingency, as are all actions taken by their office.  Shahian declares the attorneys working on the case bill their time at rates between $395/hour and $595/hour.  Counsel supports the rates with the Laffey Matrix, which is a summary of rates charged by contingency fee attorneys. 

 

Defendant contends the rates are excessive and should not be more than $350/hour for partners and $275/hour for non-partners.  Both parties cite other trial court decisions in this regard.  There is no known appellate authority in this regard, and of course the decision of an appropriate hourly rate is left to the sound discretion of the trial court. 

 

The Court finds the rates are extremely high, especially because this was a garden variety lemon law case.  The Court is reducing the rates to a blended rate of $400/hour.

 

c.     “Canned” Work

The majority of Defendant’s argument is that Plaintiff is billing an excessive number of hours for what is essentially “canned” work – work that is repeated in connection with every lemon law case litigated.  The Court tends to agree.  The Court has many of these cases before it, and is aware that the work done in the cases is redundant and should not be billed anew in every case.  The Court is inclined to reduce the billing by 1/3 in light of its finding that the billing is padded and redundant with other cases. 

 

d.     Lodestar

The Court agrees with Defendant and finds a multiplier is not appropriate in this case.  Pursuant to Ketchem v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1139, the Court should only award a multiplier based on exceptional representation if it finds the quality of representation far exceeds the quality of representation that would have been provided by an attorney of comparable skill and experience billing at the hourly rate used in the lodestar calculation.  The Court noted that, otherwise, the fee award would result in unfair double counting and would be unreasonable.  The Court finds an hourly rate of $400/hour is sufficient to compensate for the work done.

 

e.     Conclusion

The total hours billed in this case was 179.  At the blended rate of $400/hour, that would be a fee of $71,600.  When reduced by 1/3, the total fee awarded becomes $47,972. 

 

f.      Costs

The Court notes that the motion also seeks recovery of costs and expenses.  Defendant contends the request is improper because Plaintiffs failed to file a memorandum of costs.  Plaintiffs, in reply, contend a memorandum was not necessary because they are seeking costs and expenses per CCP §1794(d), rather than costs per CCP §1033.5. 

 

Plaintiffs cite nothing for the position that, when costs and expenses are sought under §1794(d), a motion is the proper mechanism to recover the costs and expenses and a memorandum of costs is not. 

 

The Court has researched the issue, and finds a memorandum of costs was required.  A plaintiff in a lemon law case seeking costs and expenses under California Civil Code Section 1794(d) is required to file a memorandum of costs to obtain an award of costs and expenses after prevailing in the action.

 

California Code of Civil Procedure Section 1032(b) states that, except as otherwise expressly provided by statute, a prevailing party is entitled as a matter of right to recover costs in any action or proceeding (West's Ann.Cal.C.C.P. § 1032). Additionally, California Rules of Court, Rule 3.1700(a)(1) specifies that a prevailing party who claims costs must serve and file a memorandum of costs within 15 days after the date of service of written notice of entry of judgment or dismissal (Kahn v. The Dewey Group, 240 Cal.App.4th 227 (2015)).

 

In the context of lemon law cases under the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act, Civil Code Section 1794(d) provides that if the buyer prevails, the court shall allow the buyer to recover as part of the judgment a sum equal to the aggregate amount of costs and expenses, including attorney's fees, reasonably incurred in connection with the commencement and prosecution of the action (West's Ann.Cal.Civ.Code § 1794). This provision does not exempt the prevailing party from the general requirement to file a memorandum of costs.

 

Case law supports this requirement. In Murillo v. Fleetwood Enterprises, Inc., the court affirmed the denial of the plaintiff's motion to strike the defendants' memorandum of costs, indicating that Civil Code Section 1794(d) does not bar the entitlement to costs under the general statutes allowing costs to the prevailing party (Murillo v. Fleetwood Enterprises, Inc., 17 Cal.4th 985 (1998)).

Because Plaintiff did not file a memorandum of costs, the request for costs and expenses is denied. 

 

Plaintiff is ordered to give notice.

Case Number: 24LBCV01451    Hearing Date: January 16, 2025    Dept: S27

1.     Background Facts

Plaintiff, Jose Luis Farfan filed this action against Defendants, Duluth Services, Inc. and TABC, Inc. for FEHA discrimination and related claims. 

 

2.     Motion to Compel Arbitration

a.     Parties’ Positions

Duluth moves to compel Plaintiff to submit this dispute to binding arbitration.  TABC joins the motion.  Defendants contend Plaintiff agreed, upon accepting employment with Defendants, to submit all claims to binding arbitration.  They contend the FAA governs the parties’ agreement, and all requirements to submit the claims to arbitration are satisfied.  They seek an order staying this action pending the outcome of binding arbitration. 

 

Plaintiff opposes the motion.  Notably, opposition was due on or before 1/03/25, but was not filed until 1/06/25.  The Court has considered the opposition, but advises Plaintiff that it may not do so in the future.  Plaintiff argues the motion should be denied because (a) he never agreed to arbitrate his claims with Moving Defendants, (b) the agreement is unconscionable, and (c) Defendants waived their right to move to compel arbitration/the agreement is contrary to public policy. 

 

b.     General Law Governing Motions to Compel Arbitration

Pursuant to CCP §1281, “A written agreement to submit to arbitration an existing controversy or a controversy thereafter arising is valid, enforceable and irrevocable, save upon such grounds as exist for the revocation of any contract.”  Pursuant to §1281.2, ”[O]n petition of a party to an arbitration agreement alleging the existence of a written agreement to arbitrate a controversy and that a party thereto refuses to arbitrate such controversy, the court shall order the petitioner and the respondent to arbitrate the controversy if it determines that an agreement to arbitrate the controversy exists, unless it determines that:

(a) The right to compel arbitration has been waived by the petitioner; or

(b) Grounds exist for the revocation of the agreement. 

(c) A party to the arbitration is also a party to a pending court action or special proceeding with a third party . . ..  This subdivision shall not be applicable to an agreement to arbitrate disputes as to the professional negligence of a health care provider made pursuant to Section 1295.”

 

c.     Electronic Agreements

There is a three-step process used to determine whether a defendant has adequately established the plaintiff’s electronic signature on an employment agreement.  Fabian v. Renovate America, Inc. (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 1062; Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047; Ruiz v. Moss Bros. Auto Group, Inc. (2014) 232 Cal.App.4th 836

 

STEP 1 – Defendant meets its initial burden to show an agreement to arbitrate by attaching a copy of the contract to its petition, which purportedly bears the plaintiff's electronic initials and signature. Espejo v. Southern California Permanente Medical Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1057-1058 ["as a preliminary matter the [trial] court is only required to make a finding of the agreement's existence, not an evidentiary determination of its validity.”]). To meet its burden, the moving party need only attach a copy of the agreement to the petition and incorporate it by reference. (Id. at 1058; Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1330 [“The provisions must be stated verbatim or a copy must be physically or electronically attached to the petition and incorporated by reference."]).

 

STEP 2 – Plaintiff shifts the burden by declaring that she/he did not sign the contract

 

STEP 3 – Defendant then has the burden of proving by a preponderance of the evidence that the electronic signature was authentic. Defendant’s burden of authenticating an electronic signature is not great. (Ruiz at 844) The party seeking authentication may carry its burden in any manner, including by presenting evidence of the contents of the contract in question and the circumstances surrounding the contract's execution. (ibid).  

 

Defendant can attribute the signature to Plaintiff by showing the "efficacy of any security procedure applied to determine the person to which the electronic record or electronic signature was attributable.” (Civ. Code, § 1633.9, (a)). The effect of an electronic signature attributed to a person "is determined from the context and surrounding circumstances at the time of its creation, execution, or adoption, including the parties' agreement, if any, and otherwise as provided by law." (Civ. Code, § 1633.9, (b)).

 

A party can establish that the electronic signature was the act of Plaintiff “by presenting evidence that a unique login and password known only to that person was required to affix the electronic signature, along with evidence detailing the procedures the person had to follow to electronically sign the document and the accompanying security precautions.” (Bannister v. Marinidence Opco, LLC (2021) 64 Cal.App.5th 541, 545).

 

In Espejo v. Southern Cal. Permanente Med. Group (2016) 246 Cal.App.4th 1047, 1053-54, the employer’s systems consultant submitted a declaration detailing how the username and password were generated, how the system worked, and how the employer was able to conclude that the employee had signed the subject arbitration agreement.  The Court of Appeals found the evidence sufficient to establish the employee had signed the agreement. 

 

In this case, Defendant provided evidence with its moving papers sufficient to meet the requirements above.  Specifically, Defendant supported its motion with the Declaration of Lindsey Collum, who works with new hire onboarding, and who explains the process pursuant to which she obtained Plaintiff’s agreement to arbitrate all claims.  Plaintiff does not contest the showing concerning electronic signature in his opposition papers. 

 

d.     Does Agreement Cover Moving Defendants?

Plaintiff contends the subject agreement was between him and his employer, Aerotek, Inc., which is not a named party to the action.  He contends the agreement does not cover his claims against Defendants, who are not parties to the agreement. 

 

Plaintiff, in his declaration in opposition to the motion, states, in pertinent part:

3. I was employed by Duluth Services, INC. (“Defendant”), as a press operator from in or around November 2021 to July 12 2022. 

4. At the time I signed the document in question, I was not told of any connection or relationship between Aerotek, Inc., Duluth Services, Inc. and TABC, INC.. 

 

Plaintiff also supports his contention with his attorney’s declaration.  Plaintiff’s attorney declares that, in correspondence with Defense Counsel, Defense Counsel provided him a copy of the applicable arbitration agreement, which is with Aerotek.  Indeed, Plaintiff’s attorney attaches the correspondence and agreement to his declaration, and the agreement is between Plaintiff and Aerotek.  It does appear that Defense Counsel provided the wrong agreement in that correspondence. 

 

Plaintiff fails, however, to address the agreement that was attached to the moving papers, which clearly is between Plaintiff and Duluth, not between Plaintiff and Aerotek.  Exhibit 4 to the moving papers is an agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant, Duluth Services, Inc.  The agreement is the one discussed above (in connection with the portion of this ruling detailing how the agreement was obtained), and it is signed by both Plaintiff and Duluth. 

 

Notably, the agreement expressly applies to any claims Plaintiff may make against Duluth’s “clients or customers,” which can only be reasonably construed to mean the employers at which Duluth places employees.  This would include TABC.  The Court therefore finds Plaintiff signed an agreement with Duluth, and it covers his claims against both Duluth and TABC.

 

e.     Unconscionability

Plaintiff next argues the agreement should not be enforced because it is unconscionable. 

 

i.              Law Governing Unconscionability

A contract will be found to be unconscionable where: (1) it is adhesive, in that all or part of the contract falls outside the reasonable expectations of the weaker party; and (2) equitably, the terms unreasonably favor the other party.  (Stirlen v. Supercuts (1997) 51 Cal.App.4th 1519, 1524.)  Unconscionability has both a “procedural” and a “substantive” element. 

 

The procedural element focuses on two factors: oppression and surprise.  “Oppression” arises from an inequality of bargaining power which results in no real negotiation and an absence of meaningful choice. “Surprise” involves the extent to which the supposedly agreed upon terms of the bargain are hidden in the prolix printed form drafted by the party seeking to enforce the disputed terms.  A contractual term is substantively suspect if it reallocates the risks of the bargain in an objectively unreasonable or unexpected manner.  Procedural unconscionability has to do with matters relating to freedom of assent. 

 

Substantive unconscionability involves the imposition of harsh or oppressive terms on one who has assented freely to them.  The prevailing view is that these two elements must both be present in order for a court to exercise its discretion to refuse to enforce a contract or clause under the doctrine of unconscionability.  (Id., at 1532-1533.)  In Stirlen, the court found that an arbitration provision was unconscionable in that included a forced waiver of punitive damages by the plaintiff employee, in violation of Civil Code §1668, and accorded the defendant employer greater rights in pursuing a court action for injunctive or equitable relief. 

 

ii.             Procedural Unconscionability

Plaintiff contends the contract was presented to him on a take it or leave it basis, and it is therefore procedurally unconscionable.  These types of contracts are referred to as contracts of adhesion.  Pursuant to Ramirez v. Charter Communications, Inc. (2024) 16 Cal.5th 478, contracts of adhesion are typically found to be procedurally unconscionable under California law.

 

iii.            Substantive Unconscionability 

As noted above, even if a contract is procedurally unconscionable, it will still be enforced unless it is also substantively unconscionable.  Plaintiff does not meaningfully articulate his argument regarding substantive unconscionability in his opposition papers.  He states only that the agreement imposes limitations that undermine the rights of employees to pursue work-related claims, akin to those identified in Little v. Auto Stiegler, Inc. (2003) 29 Cal.4th 1064. 

 

Pursuant to Sanchez v. Valencia Holding Co., LLC (2015) 61 Cal.4th 899, the burden to establish unconscionability is on the party asserting the defense.  Because Plaintiff did not state which terms of the contract are substantively unconscionable or how they are similar to those in Little, Plaintiff failed to meet the burden.

 

Plaintiff also argues he was not informed of the legal implications of the agreement, including that he was waiving his right to a jury trial and foregoing his ability to be heard in a public forum, at the time he signed the agreement.  The agreement, on its face, so states.  Plaintiff does not cite any authority for the position that an employer must sit down and explain the terms of the contract to an employee if the terms are clear on their face.  This argument is therefore also rejected.

 

f.      Waiver

Plaintiff states in his introduction that he also argues Defendants have waived their right to arbitrate.  He does not further discuss this argument in the body of his points and authorities, and therefore fails to show there has been a waiver. 

 

Instead, he briefly argues there are public policy reasons not to enforce the agreement, relying on Gentry v. Superior Court (2007) 42 Cal.4th 443 to support his position.  Gentry is inapposite for two reasons.  First, it dealt with class arbitration waivers in the employment context.  Second, it has been abrogated by OTO, LLC v. Kho (2019) 8 Cal.5th 111. 

 

Plaintiff failed to show there is any public policy reason to decline to enforce the parties’ arbitration agreement. 

 

g.     Conclusion

Defendant met its moving burden to show there is a binding arbitration agreement, signed by Plaintiff, that requires Plaintiff to submit his claims to arbitration.  Plaintiff failed to establish any grounds for refusal to enforce the agreement. 

 

Per CCP §1281.2, the motion to compel arbitration is granted. 

 

The litigation against Defendants is stayed pending the outcome of arbitration.  CCP §1281.4.

 

3.     Joinder

As noted above, the parties’ agreement expressly applies to clients and customers of Duluth, and the moving papers establish that TABC is such an entity.  Plaintiff does not make any meaningful argument to the contrary.  The joinder is therefore also granted.

The Court asks Counsel to make arrangements to appear remotely at the CMC and hearing on the motion.
