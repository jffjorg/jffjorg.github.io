DEPARTMENT 55 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV23653    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Hearing on Demurrer – without Motion to Strike

BACKGROUND

LEO GONZALEZ et al. (“Plaintiffs”) brought this lawsuit based on allegations that Plaintiffs are tenants at 625 S. Burlington Ave., Los Angeles, which has uninhabitable conditions, including cockroach infestations, widespread mold contamination, open exposure to raw sewage, plumbing issues, faulty and insufficient utilities, structural defects, and deficient security. 

The FAC asserts twelve (12) causes of action arising from Plaintiffs’ residential tenancies in the property located at 625 S. Burlington Ave., Los Angeles (“the Property”). Plaintiffs allege Defendants, owners and/or managers of the Property, permitted grossly uninhabitable conditions to persist at the Property throughout their tenancies.

Cross-Defendant/Cross-Complainant INGENIOUS ASSET GROUP, INC. (“Ingenious”), the property management company that formerly managed the Property at issue, filed a Second Amended Cross-Complaint (“SAXC”), alleging causes of action for (1) negligence, (2) express indemnity, (3) equitable/implied indemnity, (4) apportionment and contribution, and (5) declaratory relief.

On May 14, 2024, all the plaintiffs/cross-defendants except Gloria Escobar demurred to Ingenious’s SAXC. (The Court refers to the demurring parties as “Plaintiffs” collectively, notwithstanding Escobar’s absence from the demurrer.)

Ingenious opposes the demurrer.

LEGAL STANDARD

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10 enumerates eight (8) statutory grounds for demurrer, among them a pleading’s failure to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (See Code Civ. Proc., § 430.10(d); Young v. Gannon (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 209, 220.)

When considering a demurrer, a court reads the allegations stated in the challenged pleading liberally and in context, and “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

Put differently: for purposes of demurrer, the court treats all facts alleged – but only the facts alleged – in the complaint as true. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School District (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 732.)

MEETING AND CONFERENCE

“Before filing a demurrer ... , the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).)

Counsel attests that section 430.41 is satisfied. (Barajas Decl., ¶ 2.) Counsel is not strictly correct; he states the parties met and conferred “by letter”, rather than by telephone or in person. (Ibid.) Nonetheless, the Code is satisfied in spirit, and the Court turns to the merits of the demurrer.

ANALYSIS

Plaintiffs demur to Ingenious’s first cause of action for negligence, its third cause of action for equitable/implied indemnity, its fourth cause of action for apportionment and contribution, and its fifth cause of action for declaratory relief, all for failure to state a claim as described in Code of Civil Procedure section 430.10(e).

            1. First Cause of Action for Negligence

“To succeed in a negligence action, the plaintiff must show that (1) the defendant owed the plaintiff a legal duty, (2) the defendant breached the duty, and (3) the breach proximately or legally caused (4) the plaintiff's damages or injuries. (Thomas v. Stenberg (2012) 206 Cal.App.4th 654, 662.) Plaintiffs argue Ingenious has not pled that Plaintiffs owe it any duty. The Court agrees.

Ingenious argues Plaintiffs owe their landlords a duty not to damage their rental property, and Ingenious, as their landlord’s agent, is owed the same duty. Ingenious relies on the definition of “landlord” that appears in section 151.02 of the Los Angeles Municipal Code. Ingenious cites no other precedent for its proposition that a “landlord”, as defined in a local ordinance, should be equated with the term owner/lessor, as defined at common law. The Court fails to see how Plaintiffs owe Ingenious a duty not to damage property Ingenious does not own. And a definition of “landlord” lifted from a municipal rent stabilization ordinance does not explain why Plaintiffs would owe a management company such a duty.

Ingenious contends “agents of lessors are included with the definition of ‘lessor’ by the jurisdiction in which the subject property and tenants are located.” (Opp., 6:4-5.) Not so. An agent of the lessor is included in the definition of the term landlord, not the term lessor. The LARSO sets out many rights and obligations as between landlords and tenants. It does not adjust the common-law duties a lessee owes to a lessor – or at least, Ingenious has not shown that it does simply by quoting the LARSO.

The demurrer to Ingenious’s first cause of action is sustained. Ingenious concedes it is only the agent for the owner of the Property. It has not alleged Plaintiffs owe it the duty it claims. This is the second time Plaintiffs have demurred to this claim asserted by Ingenious, and the second time Ingenious has failed to establish any legal basis for asserting a negligence claim against Plaintiffs. The demurrer therefore is sustained without leave to amend.

2. Third and Fourth Causes of Action for Equitable/Implied Indemnity and Apportionment/Contribution

“ ‘The right to indemnity flows from payment of a joint legal obligation on another's behalf. [Citations.] The elements of a cause of action for indemnity are (1) a showing of fault on the part of the indemnitor and (2) resulting damages to the indemnitee for which the indemnitor is contractually or equitably responsible.’ (Expressions at Rancho Niguel Ass'n v. Ahmanson Developments, Inc. (2001) 86 Cal.App.4th 1135, 1139 … .)

Particularly given that Ingenious has not stated a claim for negligence, it also has not alleged facts showing Plaintiffs, its purported indemnitors, are jointly at fault for any damages to Ingenious.

As for contribution: the proper mechanism to allocate liability between alleged joint tortfeasors in an underlying suit is through a claim for equitable indemnity. Contribution contemplates liability as between multiple tortfeasors liable on the same judgment. Matters of fault as between plaintiffs and defendant presumptively will be settled through findings of comparative negligence and/or indemnity in the main suit. (See generally American Motorcycle Assn. v. Superior Court (1978) 20 Cal.3d 578 [discussing the purpose and distinctions between contribution and indemnity theories].)

Because the facts underlying Ingenious’s indemnity claim are unclear, it is also unclear what distinguishes its claims for indemnity and contribution. Both fail.

The Court sustains Plaintiffs’ demurrer to IAG’s third and fourth causes of action without leave to amend.

            3. Fifth Cause of Action for Declaratory Relief

“To qualify for declaratory relief under section 1060, [a party must show] their action … present[s] two essential elements: ‘(1) a proper subject of declaratory relief, and (2) an actual controversy involving justiciable questions relating to the rights or obligations of a party.’ [Citation.] ‘The “actual controversy” language in ... section 1060 encompasses a probable future controversy relating to the legal rights and duties of the parties.’ [Citation.] It does not embrace controversies that are ‘conjectural, anticipated to occur in the future, or an attempt to obtain an advisory opinion from the court.’ [Citation.]” (Lee v. Silveira (2016) 6 Cal.App.5th 527, 546.)

Ingenious concedes it no longer manages the Property; it has no relationship with Plaintiffs, contractual or otherwise. There is no current or probable future controversy. Ingenious contends there is a “need for a determination as to the rights and duties of the parties ... during the time that Ingenious was acting as the lessor’s agent”. (Opp., 7:1-2.) The other causes of action will dispose of those questions. There are no ongoing rights and duties to clarify by declaration.

The demurrer to the fifth cause of action for declaratory relief is sustained without leave to amend.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer is sustained in its entirety, without leave to amend.


Case Number: 22STCV37218    Hearing Date: July 31, 2024    Dept: 55

NATURE OF PROCEEDINGS: Hearing on Demurrer – with Motion to Strike (CCP 430.10) and Motion to Strike Motion to strike portions of cross-complaint (CCP 435, 436).

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff Towns End Development, Inc. (“TED”) sued defendant Virginia F. Valdez, individually and as trustee of the Valdez Family Trust dated December 23, 1998 alleging causes of action for (1) specific performance of a real estate purchase agreement, (2) breach of contract, (3) declaratory relief, (4) an accounting, and (5) arbitration.

Defendant Valdez, individually and as trustee, cross-complained against Plaintiff TED and against Clinton Virgil, LLC, Arixa Secured Income Fund, LLC, and Score Portfolio I, LLC. Valdez’s cross-complaint asserts causes of action for (1) forgery, (2) cancellation of instrument, (3) quiet title, (4) slander of title, (5) financial elder abuse, and (6) declaratory and injunctive relief.

TED demurred to and moved to strike portions of Defendant’s cross-complaint. Defendant opposes the demurrer and motion to strike.

LEGAL STANDARD

            Demurrer

Where pleadings are defective, a party may raise the defect by way of a demurrer. (Coyne v. Krempels (1950) 36 Cal.2d 257, 262.) A demurrer for sufficiency tests whether the complaint alleges facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action. (Cal. Code Civ. Proc. § 430.10; Young v. Gannon (2002) 97 Cal.App.4th 209, 220.)

When considering a demurrer, a court reads the allegations stated in the challenged pleading liberally and in context, and “treat[s] the demurrer as admitting all material facts properly pleaded, but not contentions, deductions or conclusions of fact or law.” (Serrano v. Priest (1971) 5 Cal.3d 584, 591.) Put differently: for purposes of demurrer, the court treats all facts alleged – but only the facts alleged – in the complaint as true. (Picton v. Anderson Union High School District (1996) 50 Cal.App.4th 726, 732.) “The only issue involved in a demurrer hearing is whether the complaint, as it stands, unconnected with extraneous matters, states a cause of action.” (Hahn v. Mirda (2007) 147 Cal.App.4th 740, 747.)

            Motion to Strike

“The court may, upon a motion made pursuant to Section 435 [notice of motion to strike whole or part of complaint], or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper: (a) Strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading.” (Id. § 436 (a).) Irrelevant matters include immaterial allegations that are not essential to the claim or those not pertinent to or supported by an otherwise sufficient claim. (Id., § 431.10.) The court may also “[s]trike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court.” (Id. § 436 (b).)

Motions to strike are used to reach defects or objections to pleadings that are not challengeable by demurrer, such as words, phrases, and prayers for damages. (See Code Civ. Proc. §§ 435, 436, and 437.) The proper procedure to attack false allegations in a pleading is a motion to strike. (Id. § 436 (a).) A motion to strike is also the proper procedure to attack a purportedly improper remedy such as unjustified punitive damages. (Venice Town Council, Inc. v. City of Los Angeles (1996) 47 Cal.App.4th 1547, 1561-1562.)

 

EVIDENTIARY RULINGS

TED requests judicial notice of four records already on file in this action. The Court grants the requests, although they do not change the analysis.

MEETING AND CONFERENCE

“Before filing a demurrer ... , the demurring party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who filed the pleading that is subject to demurrer for the purpose of determining whether an agreement can be reached that would resolve the objections to be raised in the demurrer.” (Code Civ. Proc., § 430.41(a).) An equivalent requirement applies to motions to strike. (Id., § 435.5.)

Counsel attests that sections 430.41 and 435.5 are satisfied. (Mink Decl., ¶ 2.)

ANALYSIS - DEMURRER

TED demurs to all causes of action in Defendant’s cross-complaint, arguing each fails to state facts sufficient to constitute a cause of action and/or is fatally uncertain.

/ / /

/ / /

            1. Judicial Estoppel

TED argues Defendant has made admissions to the Court in sworn declarations that should supersede contradictory allegations in her cross-complaint, based on the doctrine of judicial estoppel. The Court disagrees.

“ ‘Judicial estoppel is an equitable doctrine aimed at preventing fraud on the courts.’ [Citation.] It prohibits a party from taking inconsistent positions in the same or different judicial proceedings. [Citation.]” (M. Perez Co., Inc. v. Base Camp Condominiums Assn. No. One (2003) 111 Cal.App.4th 456, 463 (M. Perez).)

TED implicitly appeals to a particular species of judicial estoppel, the sham pleading doctrine. The sham pleading doctrine permits the court, upon demurrer, to disregard allegations in a pleading that a party has otherwise contradicted via judicial admissions. (See Nguyen v. Western Digital Corp. (2014) 229 Cal.App.4th 1522 [definition].)

To estop a plaintiff from pleading her claim under the sham pleading doctrine, she must have made a “judicial admission”, that is, an “unequivocal concession of the truth” of the relevant matter. (Humane Society of U.S. v. Superior Court (2013) 214 Cal.App.4th 1233, 1249.)

The Court does not find the statements in Defendant’s declaration to be unequivocal concessions contradicting her cross-complaint, such that they may be regarded as judicial admissions that implicate the sham pleading doctrine.

If TED means to argue the Court should apply some broader equitable concept of judicial estoppel to strike Plaintiff’s pleading, the Court declines to do that as well. Even if Plaintiff’s position in her cross-complaint is inconsistent with her declaration, the inconsistency does not “ ‘ “ ‘have an adverse impact on the judicial process’ ” ’ ”, such that the Court could properly cut her case off at the pleading stage based on purely equitable concerns.

The Court rejects the argument that Defendant is judicially estopped from stating any of her claims.

            2. First Cause of Action for Forgery

TED argues there is no civil cause of action for forgery. Defendant does not oppose this contention but argues the demurrer to this cause of action should be overruled because the allegations can be “treat[ed] ... as any of the other included causes of action in the Cross-Complaint.” (Opp., 3:23-25.)

A cause of action may proceed if it states any cognizable claim, even on a different theory than the plaintiff proposes. (See Lee Newman, M.D., Inc. v. Wells Fargo Bank (2001) 87 Cal.App.4th 73, 78-79.) Here, Defendant concedes her legal theory is unclear, but she fails to identify any that should be substituted for it. Her opposition suggests she might have meant to plead fraud. If so, she may attempt to do so by amendment; but as things stand, the cause of action is fatally uncertain.

The demurrer is sustained with twenty days’ leave to amend.

3. Second Cause of Action for Cancellation of Instrument

TED argues “[a] cause of action for cancellation may be based on forgery”; that forgery is the same as fraud; and that Defendant has not stated a claim for fraud, so her claim for cancellation of instrument fails. (Dem., 6:9-19.)

A cause of action for cancellation of instrument does not require a plaintiff to plead fraud. “ ‘ “ To prevail on a claim to cancel an instrument, a plaintiff must prove (1) the instrument is void or voidable due to, for example, fraud, and (2) there is a reasonable apprehension of serious injury including pecuniary loss or the prejudicial alternation of one's position.” ’ ” (Weeden v. Hoffman (2021) 70 Cal.App.5th 269, 294, emphasis added.) Fraud is only one basis on which an instrument might be void or voidable. Forgery is a different basis with different ramifications for the enforceability of a deed. (See People v. Schmidt (2019) 41 Cal.App.5th 1042, 1058-1059 [distinguishing fraudulently induced deed, voidable, from forged deed, void ab initio].)

Here, Defendant has pled the deed to the Property is void due to Plaintiff’s forgery of the deed. “[F]orgery includes ‘ “a false making of a writing” ’ that ‘ “falsely purports to be the writing of another.” ’ ” (La Jolla Group II v. Bruce (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 461, 478.) Defendant has pled these elements, so she has stated a claim for cancellation.

The demurrer to the second cause of action is overruled.

4. Third, Fourth, and Sixth Causes of Action for Cancellation, Quiet Title, and Declaratory Relief

TED argues Defendant’s third, fourth, and sixth causes of action are subject to demurrer for the exact same reasons as the second. (See Dem., 12:1-6.) In fact, the demurrer to the second cause of action failed, and the demurrers to the third, fourth and sixth, fail for the same reasons. Defendant does not need to plead fraud to state any of these claims, nor is she subject to heightened pleading requirements.

The demurrers to the third, fourth, and sixth causes of action are overruled.

            5. Fifth Cause of Action for Financial Elder Abuse

TED’s demurrer to the fifth cause of action relies on the same erroneous premise: that in order to plead financial elder abuse, Defendant needs to plead a fraud claim. Not so.

A plaintiff claiming elder abuse must allege (1) she is an elder adult and (2) a defendant took, secreted, appropriated, or retained her real or personal property, or assisted another to do so, (3) to a wrongful use, with an intent to defraud, or by undue influence. (Wel. & Inst. Code § 15610.30, subd. (a); Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 174.)

For the purposes of the abuse statute, “ ‘[e]lder’ means any person residing in this state, 65 years of age or older.” (Wel. & Inst. Code § 15610.27.)

As to the second element, “a person or entity takes, secretes, appropriates, obtains, or retains real or personal property when an [elder] is deprived of any property right, including by means of an agreement, or testamentary bequest … .” (Id. ¶ 15610.30, subd. (c).)

Defendant has pled she is an elder adult and TED took her real property. She can satisfy the third element of her claim by pleading the property was taken to a wrongful use, without pleading fraud. Taking property “[to] a wrongful use is defined as taking, secreting, appropriating, or retaining property in bad faith. Bad faith occurs where the person or entity knew or should have known that the elder had the right to have the property transferred or made readily available to the elder or to his or her representative. [Citation.]” (Teselle v. McLoughlin (2009) 173 Cal.App.4th 156, 174.)

Defendant has pled that TED intentionally took her property by forging her signature and/or defrauding her into signing a document under false pretenses. She alleges facts that amount to taking for a wrongful use. She has stated a claim for financial elder abuse.

The demurrer to the fifth cause of action is overruled.

ANALYSIS - MOTION TO STRIKE

The demurrer having been sustained with leave to amend, the motion to strike is denied as moot.

CONCLUSION

The demurrer filed by Towns End Development, Inc. on May 28, 2024 is sustained as to the first cause of action with twenty days’ leave to amend, and otherwise overruled.

The motion to strike filed by Towns End Development, Inc. on May 28, 2024 is denied.
