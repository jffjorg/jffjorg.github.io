DEPARTMENT 17 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 23STCV03252    Hearing Date: January 3, 2025    Dept: 17

Superior Court of California

County of Los Angeles

 

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

LYNN STEPANIAN

 

         vs.

 

RCM ENTERTAINMENT SERVICES, INC.

 

 Case No.:  23STCV03252

 

 

 

 Hearing Date: January 3, 2025

 

 

Raven Defendants’ demurrer is OVERRULED.

 

On 2/14/2023, Lynn Stepanian (Plaintiff) initiated this action. On 7/15/2024, Plaintiff filed a Second Amended Complaint (SAC) against 10 different Defendants, alleging: (1) breach of contract; (2) quantum meruit; (3) breach of good faith and fair dealing; (4) wrongful termination; (5) unjust enrichment; (6) fraudulent inducement; (7) fraudulent transfer; (8) wrongful termination; (9) discrimination; (10) retaliation; (11) violation of Labor Code sections 201 and 203; (12) declaratory relief; (13) promissory estoppel; (14) intentional infliction of emotional distress; and (15) violation of Business and Professions Code section 17200.

 

            On 8/14/2023, Raven Capital Management, LLC, (Raven Capital Management), Rive Releasing, LLC, (Rive Releasing), OR Acquisition Co, LLC, (OR Acquisition), Rive TM, LLC, (Rive TM), Rive CS, LLC, (Rive CS), RCM AWC, LLC, (RCM AWC), RCM Film Services LLC (RCM Film Services), James Masciello, (Masciello), and Matthew Sidari, (Sidari, and collectively the Defendants) demurred to the SAC.

 

            On 11/22/2024, Defendants Raven Capital Management (Raven), Rive Releasing LLC (Rive), OR Acquisition Co., LLC (OR Acquisition), Rive TM, LLC (Rive TM), Rive CS, LLC (Rive CS), RCM AWC, LLC (RCM AWC), RCM Film Services LLC (RCM Film), James Masciello, and Matthew Sidari (collectively, the Raven Defendants) demurred to the SAC.

 

Discussion

 

            Raven Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s SAC is defective because it was filed without leave to amend, and that the Raven Defendants are not proper parties to this case.

 

            As to the first contention that Plaintiff filed an SAC without leave to amend, this is incorrect. The Court granted Plaintiff’s oral motion for leave to amend on 6/7/2024, as reflected in the Court’s 6/7/2024 Minute Order: “Plaintiff's oral motion to file a Second Amended Complaint as to RCM Entertainment Services only is granted.” (P’s RJN, Exh. B.)

 

            As to the second contention, Raven Defendants argue that they are not proper parties here because Plaintiff knew their identity and the facts giving rise to her cause of action, and that alter-ego is not properly pled.

 

            However, as to knowledge, Plaintiff’s SAC alleges that the fraudulent transfer occurred after she filed her Complaint as part of an effort to avoid paying Plaintiff the wages sought in her Complaint. As such, the fact that Plaintiff was aware of the identities of the Raven Defendants is not determinative here, given that she has alleged facts which could show she did not yet know the facts to support causes of action against them. While Raven Defendants may dispute this, this is a factual determination not properly made at this pleading stage.

 

            As to alter-ego, to sufficiently allege alter ego liability, the plaintiff must allege facts which could show: (1) a unity of interest and ownership such that their separate personalities no longer exist, and (2) failure to disregard the corporate entity would sanction a fraud or bring about injustice. (Mesler v. Bragg Management Co. (1985) 39 Cal.3d 290, 300.)

 

            Here, Plaintiff alleges that:

 

-         RCM and each of the Raven Defendants were wholly owned and controlled by Raven. (SAC ¿¿ 2-9.)

 

-         RCM and the Raven Defendants were all controlled and managed by Raven’s Chief Executive Officer and principle, Masciello, as well as Raven’s Director, Sidari. (SAC ¿¿ 10-11.)

 

-         RCM Film was formed on November 13, 2023, after plaintiff filed this lawsuit in February 2023, and has the same employees, office, telephone number, and management as RCM. (SAC ¿ 13.)

 

Accepted as true, these allegations are sufficient to show a unity of interest at the pleading stage. More specific facts which can establish unity of interest are contemplated by the discovery process.

 

 Plaintiff also alleges that serious injustice and fraud would result if there was a failure to pierce RCM’s corporate veil. More specifically, Plaintiff alleges that Raven Defendants were created to allow RCM to avoid any liability for its alleged wrongful actions. (SAC ¿ 74.) As such, a failure to pierce the corporate veil would enable this alleged fraudulent conduct.

 

As such, the Court finds alter-ego liability to be sufficiently pled.

 

Finally, Raven Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s individual causes of action are insufficiently pled. However, all of Raven Defendants’ arguments are either unpersuasive or turn on factual determinations not properly made here.

 

For example, Raven Defendants argue that her wrongful termination claim is insufficiently pled because “Plaintiff utterly fails to articulate what public policy is implicated by her seeking payment for work she allegedly completed outside the scope of her employment.” (Motion, 23: 4-6.) However, Plaintiff clearly alleges that he was denied wages owed, retaliated against, and discriminated against. As such, Plaintiff has alleged facts which could show wrongful conduct which violates California public policy interests. Similarly, Raven Defendants argue that Plaintiff hasn’t alleged any facts which could show severe emotional distress. However, allegations that she was wrongfully terminated and retaliated/discriminated against clearly can support this cause of action at the pleadings stage.  

 

By way of another example, Raven Defendants argue that her employment contract plainly provides that she was employed only by RCM, not Raven. However, Plaintiff alleges alter-ego liability. As such, whether or not she was, in fact, an employee of Raven will require factual determinations related to alter-ego.  

 

At the pleading stage, the Court finds each cause of action is sufficiently alleged.

 

Based on the foregoing, Raven Defendants’ demurrer is overruled.

 

It is so ordered.

DEPARTMENT 17

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

FN-OSM INC.

                          

         vs.

 

LABY PROPERTY GROUP, LLC, et al.

 

 Case No.:  24STCV23093

 

 

 

 Hearing Date:  January 3, 2025

 

Defendant’s motion to quash is GRANTED. 

 

            On 9/9/2024, Plaintiff FN-OSM Inc. (Plaintiff) filed suit against Laby Property Group, LLC (Defendant), alleging breach of contract.

 

            On 12/6/2024, Defendant moved to quash service of summons.

 

Discussion

 

            Defendant argues that service in this case was defective, as the proof of service indicates that service was made at an unassociated address purportedly on its old registered agent (Mike Matzner), despite that the description in the proof of service does not match Matzner. 

 

            Plaintiff’s proof of service states that on 10/30/2024, service was made personally on Laby Property Group, LLC, at 28625 Forest Meadow Pl, Castaic, CA 91384, on 10/23/2024, via its old registered agent Mike Matzner.

 

            However, Defendant submitted evidence that the Secretary of State website lists Laby’s property management group’s address of 17547 Ventura Blvd. #304, Encino, CA 91316, for the entity (which is where Mr. Matzner works), and it also lists 5945 Canoga Ave. Woodland Hills, CA 91367 as a mailing address.

 

The address listed on the Proof of Service of “28625 Forest Meadow Pl, Castaic, CA 91384” is not associated with Defendant, and Defendant’s counsel indicated that he was not able to find the address listed on any document associated with Defendant.

 

Defendant’s counsel also submitted evidence that he has met Mr. Matzner personally, and he does not match the description of the person served in the proof of service. Mr. Matzner is a much larger individual, both in height and in weight. Defendant’s counsel does not know Mr. Matzner’s age, but would estimate he is significantly younger than 60.

 

Once service is challenged by way of a motion to quash, plaintiffs carry the burden of proving the validity of service. (Dill v. Berquist Construction Co. (1994) 24 Cal.App.4th 1426, 1439- 40.) Here, Plaintiff has not submitted evidence to rebut Defendant’s substantial evidence indicating invalid service. Rather, Plaintiff indicates that counsel employed skip tracing to locate Mr. Matzner’s home address. However, Plaintiff does not set forth any legal authority to show that service of a corporation by service on the agent at their personal residence is code-compliant. (“This failure to cite pertinent legal authority is enough reason to reject the argument”); Akins v. State of California (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 1, 50, 71 (contention waived by failure to cite legal authority). Plaintiff also does not address the contention that the individual described in the proof of service does not match Mr. Matzner’s description.

 

Plaintiff also argues that this motion is untimely citing CCP section 418.10. However, Plaintiff still opposed the motion on the merits, and there is no indication of prejudice if the motion is heard.

 

As a final note, the Court wishes to address the procedural history of this action, as relayed by Defendant’s counsel in the moving papers. Specifically, Defendant’s counsel wrote:

 

Plaintiff’s counsel Nava initially reached out to the Defendant’s property manager, indicating his client held some claim, but failed to divulge the essence of the claim. Defendant’s counsel reached out to inquire, and after several follow up emails, still nothing of substance was communicated.

 

Eventually a phone call was had, and Defendant’s counsel recalls that the claim explained by Plaintiff’s counsel as one which was precluded by the limitations of the lease, e.g. damages limited by insurance recovery under an AIR lease, when no such recovery was had.

 

Plaintiff’s counsel then indicated suit had been filed. Defendant’s counsel inquired with his client as to whether it had been received, and followed up, no suit was ever served or received in any manner by the Defendant or its property management group.

 

Defendant’s counsel requested a copy from Plaintiff’s counsel, who, over the course of ten emails indicated only that a case had been filed, but continuously failed to provide a case number, or a conformed complaint. Eventually a file was sent, which was corrupted, and default was threatened. Defendant’s counsel responded that the file was corrupted, and that he could not file a response without even a case number, and demanded that a case number be pasted into the email if Plaintiff’s counsel was not playing games.

 

Defendant’s counsel, only this day, December 6, 2024, at approximately 8:00pm, finally received the case number in an email from Plaintiff’s counsel

           

            (Motion, 3: 8-19.)

 

            The Court is concerned by this conduct and advises Plaintiff’s counsel to avoid future gamesmanship and unnecessary motion practice where possible.

 

            Based on the foregoing, Defendant’s motion to quash is granted. 

 

It is so ordered.
