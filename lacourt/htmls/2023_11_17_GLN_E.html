Case Names:

First Amended Cross-Complaint (FACC): ROBERT FRIEDLAND, an individual, and CAREEN A. FRIEDLAND, an individual, v. MARK GERAGOS, an individual; PAULETTE GERAGOS, an individual, ALL PERSONS UNKNOWN, CLAIMING ANY LEGAL OR EQUITABLE RIGHT, TITLE, ESTATE, LIEN OR INTERST IN THE PROPERTY DESCRIBED IN THE CROSS-COMPLAINT ADVERSE TO CROSS-COMPLAINANTS’ TITLE, OR ANY CLOUD UPON CROSS-COMPLAINAINTS’ TITLE THERETO, AND ROES 1-20 inclusive 

 

[TENTATIVE RULING ON SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE, ANTI-SLAPP] 

RELIEF REQUESTED 
Cross-Defendants, Mark Geragos and Paulette Geragos, move the Court for an order striking the following causes of action from Cross-Complainants’ First Amended Cross-Complaint (“FACC” or “Cross-Complaint”) pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, Section 425.16 (the “anti-SLAPP” statute):  

 

1. Second Cause of Action entitled, “Nuisance”; and  

2. Third Cause of Action entitled, “Trespass.” 

 

In addition, as expressly permitted by the California Supreme Court in Baral v. Schnitt (2016) 1 Cal. 5th 376, 376, Cross-Defendants separately will, and hereby do, move the Court for an order striking the following specific allegations from the FACC pursuant to Code of Civil Procedure, Section 425.16 (the “anti-SLAPP” statute): 

 

• ¶12: Pg. 4, Ln. 1-9: “On or about the years 2020 and 2021, the Geragoses substantially interfered with the private use and enjoyment by the Friedlands of the Friedland Property.”  

 

• ¶ 19: Pg. 5, Ln. 7-9: “In acting as alleged herein, Cross-defendants have acted with conscious or reckless disregard for the rights of the Friedlands, and with oppression and malice, entitling the Friedlands to an award of exemplary damages.”  

 

• ¶ 23: Pg. 5, Ln. 26 – Pg. 6, Ln. 2: “In the First Amended Complaint at paragraph 12, Plaintiffs allege that “with respect to the Disputed Area” Geragos invested in landscape and improving the creek bed located between the properties. Plaintiff and their two dogs enjoy the creek on a daily basis.” The Friedlands allege that the “Disputed Area” is within the boundaries of the Friedland property, and its use as described is a trespass on the Friedland property.” 

 

• ¶ 24: Pg. 6, Ln. 3-12: “In responses to Interrogatories verified on February 4, 2022, the response to Interrogatory 11 states that:  

“Plaintiffs spent approximately $500 on the plants and flowers Mrs. Geragos gardens in the Disputed Area each year. Responding Party spent approximately in excess of $100,000 to acquire and install the permanent landscaping and design of the Commonwealth Property, which includes portions of the Disputed Area. The wireless perimeter fence cost approximately thousands of dollars to install and requires regular maintenance to ensure it is operating correctly.”  

The Friedlands allege that the “Disputed Area” is within the boundaries of the Friedland property and its use as described is a trespass on the Friedland property.”

LEGAL STANDARD – SPECIAL MOTION TO STRIKE 

“A SLAPP suit – a strategic lawsuit against public participation – seeks to chill or punish a party's exercise of constitutional rights to free speech and to petition the government for redress of grievances. [Citation.] The Legislature enacted¿Code of Civil Procedure section 425.16—known as the anti-SLAPP statute—to provide a procedural remedy to dispose of lawsuits that are brought to chill the valid exercise of constitutional rights. [Citation.]” (Neville v. Chudacoff (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1255, 1261 citing Rusheen v. Cohen (2006) 37 Cal.4th 1048, 1055-1056.) 

 

“A cause of action against a person arising from any act of that person in furtherance of the person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States Constitution or the California Constitution in connection with a public issue shall be subject to a special motion to strike, unless the court determines that the plaintiff has established that there is a probability that the plaintiff will prevail on the claim.” (CCP §425.16(b)(1).) 

“As used in this section, “act in furtherance of a person’s right of petition or free speech under the United States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue” includes: (1) any written or oral statement or writing made before a legislative, executive, or judicial proceeding, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (2) any written or oral statement or writing made in connection with an issue under consideration or review by a legislative, executive, or judicial body, or any other official proceeding authorized by law, (3) any written or oral statement or writing made in a place open to the public or a public forum in connection with an issue of public interest, or (4) any other conduct in furtherance of the exercise of the constitutional right of petition or the constitutional right of free speech in connection with a public issue or an issue of public interest.” (CCP §425.16(e).) 
 
“Subdivision (e) elaborates the four types of acts within the ambit of a SLAPP…” (Hecimovich v. Encinal School Parent Teacher Organization (2012) 203 Cal.App.4th 450, 463.) “Both the third and fourth categories of conduct that fall within section 425.16 are subject to the limitation that the conduct must be in connection with an issue of public interest. The Legislature intended this requirement to have a limiting effect on the types of conduct that come within the third and fourth categories of the statute.” (Kieu Hoang v. Phong Minh Tran (2021) 60 Cal.App.5th 513, 524.) “Thus, statements, writings and pleadings in connection with civil litigation are covered by the anti-SLAPP statute, and that statute does not require any showing that the litigated matter concerns a matter of public interest. [Citations.]” (Neville v. Chudacoff (2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 1255, 1261.) 

 

Consideration of a section 425.16 motion to strike involves a two-step process. “First, the court decides whether the defendant has made a threshold showing that the challenged cause of action is one arising from protected activity. The moving defendant’s burden is to demonstrate that the act or acts of which the plaintiff complains were taken ‘in furtherance of the [defendant]’s right of petition or free speech under the Unites States or California Constitution in connection with a public issue,’ as defined in the statutes. (§425.16 subd. (b)(1). If the court finds such a showing has been made, it then determines whether the plaintiff has demonstrated a probability of prevailing on the claim.” (Equilon Enterprises, LLC v. Consumer Cause, Inc. (2002) 29 Cal.4th 53, 67.) 

 

“In making its determination, the court shall consider the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (CCP §425.16(b)(2).) 

Although the defendant bears the initial burden, a cause of action does not “arise from” protected activity simply because it is filed after protected activity took place. (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 76-77.) Nor does the fact “[t]hat a cause of action arguably may have been triggered by protected activity” necessarily entail that it arises from such activity. (Id. at 78.) The trial court must instead focus on the substance of plaintiff’s lawsuit in analyzing the first prong of a special motion to strike. (Scott v. Metabolife Internat, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 404, 413-414.) “The defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.” (Peregrine Funding, Inc. v. Sheppard Mullin Richter & Hampton LLP (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 658, 670.) “[I]t is the principal thrust or gravamen of the plaintiff’s cause of action that determines whether the anti-SLAPP statute applies (citation), and when the allegations referring to an arguably protected activity are only incidental to a cause of action based essentially on non-protected activity, collateral allusions to protected activity should not subject the cause of action to the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Martinez v. Metabolife Internat, Inc., (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 188.) 

 

“In deciding whether the ‘arising from’ requirement is met, a court considers ‘the pleadings, and supporting and opposing affidavits stating the facts upon which the liability or defense is based.” (§425.16 (b); City of Cotati, supra at 79.) 

 

TENTATIVE RULING  
 
As a preliminary matter, the briefs on this motion—on both sides—are not exactly models of clarity.  

 

The Geragoses argue that the FACC implicates the anti-SLAPP statue because it arises out of the Geragoses’ constitutionally protected activities – filing pleadings with a court and communications in anticipation of court proceedings.

 

The Friedlands argue that the  gravamen of the contested causes of action is that Geragos’ filed a complaint for quiet title and a statutorily mandated lis pendens.  

 

The Geragoses argue: 

 

There can be no dispute the first prong of the anti-SLAPP statute is met. The allegations on the face of the FACC confirm claims are being advanced against Cross-Defendants due to their participation in the litigation process. More specifically, Cross-Complainants seek not only to quiet title to the “Disputed Area” (a superfluous claim given that is exactly what Cross-Defendants seek in their Complaint), but also trespass and nuisance given the Disputed Area. Furthermore, Cross-Complainants’ discovery responses, an email communication relating to this litigation, and the allegations in the underlying lawsuit filed by Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants make clear the second and third causes of action all arise out of protected activity. (FACC, ¶¶ 23-26).  

 

The express allegations in the FACC alone would suffice in meeting the first prong. However, there is much more. Defendants/Cross-Complainants’ verified discovery responses confirm, without equivocation, that the only “harm” (i.e., damages) suffered was due to the filing of the lis pendens. (Babaian Decl., ¶¶ 4-5; Ex. A). Their retained expert witness has opined, and intends to offer such opinion in this case, stating the value of the property was adversely affected due to the filing of the lawsuit and lis pendens by Plaintiffs/Cross-Defendants, nothing more. (Babaian Decl., ¶¶ 9-10; Ex. B). 

 

(Movants’ Mot. p. 17.) 

 

While neither the anti-SLAPP statutory framework nor the case law interpreting that framework are models of legislative precision, it appears that, under the case law, there is some support for the Geragoses’ argument that the first prong has been met. (See Manhattan Loft, LLC v. Mercury Liquors, Inc. (2009) 173. Cal.App.4th 1040, 1050 [finding that landlord’s filing of slander of title cause of action in response to tenants’ filing of lis pendens fell “squarely within” the anti-SLAPP’s protection].)

 

However, other cases hold that a cause of action does not “arise from” protected activity simply because it is filed after protected activity took place. (City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 76-77.)

 

Nor does the fact “[t]hat a cause of action arguably may have been triggered by protected activity” necessarily entail that it arises from such activity. (Id. at 78.)

 

The trial court should, other cases make clear, instead focus on the substance of plaintiff’s lawsuit in analyzing the first prong of a special motion to strike. (Scott v. Metabolife Internat, Inc. (2004) 115 Cal.App.4th 404, 413-414.)

 

“The defendant’s act underlying the plaintiff’s cause of action must itself have been an act in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.” (Peregrine Funding, Inc. v. Sheppard Mullin Richter & Hampton LLP (2005) 133 Cal.App.4th 658, 670.)

 

“[I]t is the principal thrust or gravamen of the plaintiff’s cause of action that determines whether the anti-SLAPP statute applies (citation), and when the allegations referring to an arguably protected activity are only incidental to a cause of action based essentially on non-protected activity, collateral allusions to protected activity should not subject the cause of action to the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Martinez v. Metabolife Internat, Inc., (2003) 113 Cal.App.4th 181, 188.) 

 

Here, the Geragoses’ acts underlying the Friedlands’ allegations are those pertaining to nuisance and trespass, not acts in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.

 

Although the FACC mentions the FAC, the lis pendens, and interrogatories, these appear to be incidental to the causes of action. 

 

As further explained in City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 77: 

 

To construe “arising from” in¿section 425.16, subdivision (b)(1)¿as meaning “in response to,” as Owners have urged, would in effect render all cross-actions potential SLAPP's. We presume the Legislature did not intend such an absurd result. (See generally¿People v. Mendoza¿(2000) 23 Cal.4th 896, 912, fn. 7¿[98 Cal.Rptr.2d 431, 4 P.3d 265].)¿Absurdity aside, to suggest that all cross-actions arise from the causes of action in response to which they are pled would contravene the statutory scheme governing cross-complaints. 

 

(City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 77.)

As to the other arguments asserted by the Geragoses, the Court has its doubts about Cross-Defendants’ arguments.

 

As stated in City of Cotati: 

 

Owners also have complained that City filed its lawsuit tactically, so that they would be “forced ... to bear the expense and burden of simultaneously litigating two different legal actions in two different jurisdictions.”¿But City's subjective intent, as discussed, is not relevant under the anti-SLAPP statute. As a corollary, a claim filed in response to, or in retaliation for, threatened or actual litigation is not subject to the anti-SLAPP statute simply because it may be viewed as an oppressive litigation tactic.  

 

(City of Cotati v. Cashman (2002) 29 Cal.4th 69, 78.) 

    

The Court notes that the arguments that the Geragoses make on regarding the litigation privilege and Civil Code §47(b) are, frankly, unclear.

 

The Court also notes that despite the Geragoses requesting to strike 12 portions of the Friedlands’ FACC, in addition to the second and third causes of action, Cross-Defendants provide no argument as to the legal basis as to why those portions should be stricken.  

 

The Court further notes that the Friedlands’ Opposition is similarly unclear.

 

Notably, the Opposition brief doesn’t even attempt to establish a probability of prevailing on the second and third causes of action. 

 

Finally, in reply, the Geragoses reassert that the entire basis for the Cross-Complaint implicate the anti-SLAPP statute and the opposition failed to address this evidence.

 

The Reply argues that the Cross-Complaint and the evidence in the moving papers establish that the only reason for the lawsuit was because of the recorded lis pendens. (Reply 3: 9-28.)

 

Moreover, the Geragoses’ claim that there is another basis for advancing these allegations or that the allegations are ancillary fail as the gravamen of the FACC is based on the filing of a lawsuit and the lis pendens. (Id. at 5: 14-17; 7: 6-11.) Lastly, Cross-Defendants argue that the Cross-Complainants have failed to present any evidence in their opposition.

 

The Court finds that the first anti-SLAPP prong has not been satisfied here.

 

“A claim arises from protected activity when that activity underlies or forms the basis for the claim.” (Park v. Board of Trustees of California State University (2017) 2 Cal.5th 1057, 1062.)

Additionally, “the mere fact that an action was filed after protected activity took place does not mean the action arose from that activity for the purposes of the anti-SLAPP statute.” (Id. at 162.) As the Supreme Court continued, “in ruling on an anti-SLAPP motion, courts should consider the elements of the challenged claim and what actions by the defendant supply those elements and consequently form the basis for liability.” (Id.)

 

Again, to accept the Geragoses’ argument here would appear to open the door, as noted in Cotati, to the argument that any cross-complaint is subject to anti-SLAPP.  That wouldn’t, in the Court’s view, make much sense.

 

This matter involves a property dispute. The issues raised in both the FAC and FACC concern property lines and whether the other party trespassed or interfered with the use of private property. Cross-Defendants have failed to establish that action of filing a cross-complaint based on the lis pendens is subject to the anti-SLAPP statute; the action does not arise out of acts in furtherance of the right of petition or free speech.

 

For the reasons set out above, the Geragoses’ motion to strike the second and third causes of action pursuant to the anti-SLAPP statute is DENIED. Their motion to strike the enumerated portions of the FACC mentioned in Cross-Defendants’ notice is also DENIED.

LEGAL STANDARD

The function of a motion for summary judgment or adjudication is to allow a determination as to whether an opposing party cannot show evidentiary support for a pleading or claim and to enable an order of summary dismissal without the need for trial. (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 843.) “The function of the pleadings in a motion for summary judgment is to delimit the scope of the issues; the function of the affidavits or declarations is to disclose whether there is any triable issue of fact within the issues delimited by the pleadings.” (Juge v. County of Sacramento (1993) 12 Cal.App.4th 59, 67, citing FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal. App. 3d 367, 381-382.)

Code of Civil Procedure section 437c, subdivision (a) provides that “a party may move for summary judgment in an action or proceeding if it is contended that the action has no merit or that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.” The motion shall be granted if all the papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact and that the moving party is entitled to judgment as a matter of law. (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c(c).) In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact. (Id.)

Plaintiffs state that they filed their quiet title actions against Defendant to obtain a declaratory judgment so stating. Therefore, Plaintiffs argue the MSJ should be denied because although Hratchia purports to disclaim any interest in the Properties in his MSJ, his words and actions say otherwise.

 

(04/07/2023 Min. Order, p. 6.)

 

What the Court particularly pointed out in the 04/07/2023 Minute Order was as follows:

 

While it is clear that Hratchia is stating he does not have any interest in either of the Properties, nor that he claims an interest in the Properties, the Court is not certain why Hratchia stated, “I say direct interest, because I do maintain an economic interest in 10415 Commerce, LLC, the entity, which is not at issue in this case and is undisputed.” (Decl. Bardakjian ¶14.) The Court is confused why Defendant is stating this if Defendant is admitting this is not at issue, and the Court is confused as to why this is relevant.

 

Nonetheless, it appears Defendant successfully met its burden to show there is no triable issue of material fact and that Defendant shifted its burden to Plaintiff to show there is a triable issue of material fact.

 

However, in Opposition, Plaintiffs successfully met their burden by submitting evidence to establish that there is a triable issue of material fact: whether Movant claims title to the properties, adversely to Plaintiffs.

 

(04/07/2023 Min. Order, p. 7.)

 

In Reply, Plaintiffs argue that Defendant does not have an interest in the properties. Plaintiffs assert that Defendant’s request to continue the hearing to obtain the depositions of Mr. David and Mr. Yegiyants will not establish a dispute as to Defendant’s interest in the properties. Plaintiffs request the Court to deny Defendant’s request for a continuance and grant summary adjudication on their first and third causes of action against Defendant. In the instant motion, Plaintiffs seek to address that Hratchia has since waived his “indirect” interest in the Properties – his economic interest in Plaintiff 10415 Commerce LLC, and that since Hratchia previously submitted a declaration (in Hratchia’s prior MSJ heard on 4/7/2023) confirming that he does not have or claim a “direct” interest in either of the Properties, judgment should be granted in Plaintiffs’ favor.

 

To address the “indirect” interest issue, Plaintiffs submitted the declaration of Plaintiff, Yegiyants, which stated in relevant part:

(See concurrently filed Request for Judicial Notice, Ex. 9 [New York Judgment, ¶¶ 12-13.)

 

Hratchia and the other defendants have since caused payment of the $1.5 million contemplated in that judgment. I attach as Exhibit “1” to this Declaration a true (but redacted) copy of the check for that payment, dated March 31, 2023.

 

(Yegiyants Decl. ¶2-3.)

 

Further, as stated in Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement, “In the declaration in support of the Motion for Summary Judgment, Hratchia stated that he had no direct interest, adverse or otherwise, in the Properties” (Pl. Further, the Court notes that UMF 1-12 in Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement tend to lean in favor of granting Plaintiffs’ motion.

 

However, the Court plans to hear argument from both parties’ as to the issues listed below.

 

Elements of the Quiet Title Causes of Action
“A plaintiff or cross-complainant has met his or her burden of showing that there is no defense to a cause of action if that party has proved each element of the cause of action entitling the party to judgment on the cause of action. Once the plaintiff or cross-complainant has met that burden, the burden shifts to the defendant or cross-defendant to show that a triable issue of one or more material facts exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto. The defendant or cross-defendant shall not rely upon the allegations or denials of its pleadings to show that a triable issue of material fact exists but, instead, shall set forth the specific facts showing that a triable issue of material fact exists as to the cause of action or a defense thereto.” (CCP §437c(p)(1).)

 

Here, an issue with Plaintiffs’ motion is that Plaintiffs don’t clearly explain how they have proved each element of the causes of action for quiet title.

 

CCP §761.020 states as follows:

 

The complaint shall be verified and shall include all of the following:

(a) A description of the property that is the subject of the action. In the case of tangible personal property, the description shall include its usual location. In the case of real property, the description shall include both its legal description and its street address or common designation, if any.

(b) The title of the plaintiff as to which a determination under this chapter is sought and the basis of the title. If the title is based upon adverse possession, the complaint shall allege the specific facts constituting the adverse possession.

(c) The adverse claims to the title of the plaintiff against which a determination is sought.

(d) The date as of which the determination is sought. If the determination is sought as of a date other than the date the complaint is filed, the complaint shall include a statement of the reasons why a determination as of that date is sought.

(e) A prayer for the determination of the title of the plaintiff against the adverse claims.

(CCP §761.020(a)-(e).)

The Court would like Plaintiffs to address how it has proven each element of a quiet title cause of action. If Plaintiffs can do so, the Court would likely grant Plaintiffs’ motion.

 

Judicial Estoppel
Defendant argues that Plaintiffs’ motion is barred by judicial estoppel.

 

Defendant argues that “In April of this year, Plaintiffs Arman Yegiyants and 10415 Commerce, LLC (collectively “Plaintiffs”) successfully opposed Defendant Hratchia Bardakjian’s Motion for Summary Judgment on the very same issue presented here, convincing this Court that there were triable issues of fact with respect to whether Mr. Bardakjian had an ownership interest in the 703 and 707 East Angeleno properties (collectively, the “Angeleno Properties”). As such, Plaintiffs are judicially estopped from taking the exact opposition here—to wit that there is no triable issue of fact with respect to whether Mr. Bardakjian has an ownership interest in the Angeleno Properties.” (Oppo. p. 1.)

 

The Court does not find this argument convincing because Defendant seems to incorrectly frame the issue. In Defendant’s MSJ, Defendant argued it disclaimed an interest and therefore there were no causes of action for quiet title. In Plaintiffs’ Opposition to Defendant’s MSJ, Plaintiffs argued how that despite Defendant alleges he is disclaiming an interest, his actions and his words raise a triable issue of fact that he in fact is claiming an interest.

 

Plaintiffs, in opposing Defendant’s MSJ, argued that granting Defendant’s MSJ would have a different result than if there was a trial wherein Plaintiffs could get an order declaring their interest in the subject properties.

 

Here, in Plaintiffs’ instant MSJ, Plaintiffs come forward with proof from the New York Judgment pertaining to the “indirect” interest issue.

 

Continuance
Defendant argues as follows:

 

Further, in opposing Mr. Bardakjian’s prior motion for summary judgment, Plaintiffs submitted the Declaration of Arman Yegiyants, which directly contradicted the prior testimony of John David in this case. Mr. Yegiyants’ declaration raises new issues with respect to the ownership of the Angeleno Properties, that requires additional discovery to sort through. John David testified earlier in this case that he had no documents pertaining to the Angeleno LLCs, that he knew nothing about them, and that he was never a “manager” of either of them. While John David has refused to provide a declaration concerning Mr. Yegiyants’ latest false assertions, his counsel has confirmed that Mr. David would testify that the assertions in Mr. Yegiyants’ declaration were false. Such testimony raises innumerable additional factual questions as to the ownership of the Angeleno Properties.

 

While counsel for Mr. David promised to cooperate in presenting his client for deposition, his trial schedule has apparently prevented him from doing so. Meanwhile, Mr. Yegiyants has failed to appear twice and present counsel has indicated that he would oppose any effort to depose Mr. Yegiyants in advance of the hearing on this motion.

 

Accordingly, Defendant seeks a continuance of this hearing in order to conduct the depositions of Messrs. David and Yegiyants on these factual issues—which are crucial to determining the ownership of the properties in question.

 

(Oppo. p.1-2.)

 

“If it appears from the affidavits submitted in opposition to a motion for summary judgment or summary adjudication, or both, that facts essential to justify opposition may exist but cannot, for reasons stated, be presented, the court shall deny the motion, order a continuance to permit affidavits to be obtained or discovery to be had, or make any other order as may be just. The application to continue the motion to obtain necessary discovery may also be made by ex parte motion at any time on or before the date the opposition response to the motion is due.” (CCP §437c(h).)

 

Here, the Court cannot understand Defendant’s argument as to what facts are essential to justify opposition that need to be obtained still in discovery because Defendant does a poor job at explaining why any of its arguments on the depositions of Yegiyants and John David are legally relevant. The Court has no idea as to what Defendant’s alleged discovery he seeks will help him establish.

 

Further, the Court notes that Defendant’s arguments in Opposition are difficult to understand because Defendant did not file a Separate Statement. Therefore, Plaintiffs’ Separate Statement seemingly appears uncontested, and it is hard to decipher what issues Defendant thinks are still at issue.

 

“The opposition papers shall include a separate statement that responds to each of the material facts contended by the moving party to be undisputed, indicating if the opposing party agrees or disagrees that those facts are undisputed. The statement also shall set forth plainly and concisely any other material facts the opposing party contends are disputed. Each material fact contended by the opposing party to be disputed shall be followed by a reference to the supporting evidence. Failure to comply with this requirement of a separate statement may constitute a sufficient ground, in the court’s discretion, for granting the motion.” (CCP §437c(b)(3) [emphasis added].)

 

One Final Judgment
Plaintiffs also argued, “Clearly, Hratchia does not dispute that he does not have an interest in the Properties. Indeed, he stated as much under oath in support of his Motion for Summary Judgment. (SS # 5.) Plaintiffs therefore are entitled to judgment as a matter of law on their quiet title causes of actions against Hratchia, as the Court contemplated when it ordered the parties to meet and confer regarding a stipulated judgment. Alternatively, to the extent that the Court declines to enter a final judgment against Hratchia due to the one final judgment rule (Code Civ. Proc., § 904.1(a)), Plaintiffs respectfully request that the Court summarily adjudicate the First and Third Causes of Action for Quiet Title, confirming that Hratchia does not have an interest in the Properties.” (Pl. MSJ p. 8.)

 

Here, the Court is not clear as to the thrust of this argument.  This issue will be taken up at the hearing.

TENTATIVE RULING
The Court tentatively plans to GRANT Movant’s request for attorney fees and costs.

As noted by Plaintiffs’ Opposition, “There is no authority offered to support the continued payment of attorneys’ fees and costs following the former receiver’s conclusion of his court appointed duties and his discharge from acting as the receiver.” (Pl. Oppo. p. 1.)

ECJ’s citations to People v. Riverside University (1973) 35 Cal.App.3d 572 and Southern California Sunbelt Developers, Inc. v. Banyan Limited Partnership (2017) 8 Cal.App.5th 910 in the reply brief appear to support the proposition that a receiver can continue, in some circumstances, to be entitled to payment of fees after discharge.

In the 04/17/2020 Joint Stipulation for Order Appointing Eric Beatty as Replacement Receiver Order, this Joint Stipulation noted in relevant part:

5. Receiver Adams is directed to immediately file with the Court and serve on the parties his final accounting on a noticed motion basis in accordance with the requirements in Code of Civil Procedure section 1005.

 

6. Any fees owed to Receiver Adams that have been approved by the Court shall be paid from the receivership estate. Receiver Adams may not incur any additional fees from those necessary to transition the receivership to Replacement Receiver and file his final accounting.

 

7. All of the parties in this Receivership Action agree to this Stipulation. There are no other parties in the Receivership Action.

 

(04/17/2020 Joint Stipulation)

 

Further, in the 10/23/2020 Minute Order, the Court noted in relevant part:

 

Motion for Order of Discharge and Exoneration of Surety is GRANTED. Pursuant to CRC Rule 3.1184(a), the Court grants the Receiver’s request for discharge of the Receiver and for exoneration of the Receiver’s surety.

 

Motion of Former Receiver for Payment of Fees, Costs and Advances is GRANTED, as modified. The Court finds pursuant to CRC Rule 3.1184 (d) that the Final Account and Report supports with sufficient detail an allowance of compensation for the Former Receiver and for advances in the sum requested of = $387,090.89, less deductions established by the City of: $100,163.88 and less deductions established by defendant Chase of: $19,323.00, for a total award of $267,604.01.

 

Motion of Ervin Cohen & Jessup LLP, Attorney for Receiver, to Approve Award of Attorneys’ Fees and Costs is GRANTED in part. The Court finds that the fees and costs appropriately supported by the motion are $24,564.50 in fees and $912 in costs, for a total award of $25,476.50. This sum will be approved and awarded by the Court.

 

Motion to Surcharge Former Receiver for Unauthorized Attorney’s Fees and Any Improper, Excessive and Unaccounted Expenses is DENIED WITHOUT PREJUDICE to defendants seeking such relief in a separate civil action, pursuant to an appropriate request to for leave to sue the Receiver, which the Court would likely grant. If leave is granted to sue the Receiver and defendants opt to file a separate civil action, the Court will also consider staying the payment of the fees and advances awarded to the Former Receiver this date. The Court will not in any case delay the payment of attorney’s fees ordered to be paid to Ervin Cohen & Jessup.

 

(Min. Order 10/23/2020, p.9-10.)

 

The Former Receiver notes that his continued involvement in this case is as a result of the Bardakjians suing him based on his actions taken as a court-appointed receiver.  While the Bardakjians argue that “[a] receiver has no official dues and is not a proper party to any action after being discharged by the court” they also are currently maintaining a lawsuit against that same Former Receiver.  The Bardakjians’ argument appears to be a heads-I-win, tails-you-lose one.

 

The Court will hear further argument at the hearing on these issues, but, as noted, the tentative is to GRANT the motion.  Neither the Bardakjians nor the City of Duarte raised any issues with the amount sought or the billing breakdowns in their short briefs in opposition to this motion.
