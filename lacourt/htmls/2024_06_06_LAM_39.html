DEPARTMENT 39 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 21STCV18885    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: 39

 
TENTATIVE RULING



DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 6, 2024

CASE NUMBER

21STCV18885

MOTION

Motion for Summary Judgment

MOVING PARTIES

Defendant Joseph Daneshrad

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff D and M General Constructor, Inc.

 

 

BACKGROUND

Plaintiff D and M General Constructor, Inc. (“Plaintiff”) alleges Defendant Joseph Daneshrad (“Defendant”) represented Plaintiff in an underlying case: D and M General Constructor, Inc. v. Eshaghian, et al. (Super. Ct. L.A. County, 2016, No. BC609762). Plaintiff alleges Defendant withdrew from his representation of Plaintiff shortly before trial and thereby disadvantaged Plaintiff.  (Complaint, ¶¶ 8(h), 10.)  Defendant moves for summary judgment on Plaintiff’s complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the motion.

 

LEGAL STANDARD – MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

“[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears the burden of persuasion that there is no triable issue of material fact and that he is entitled to judgment as a matter of law[.] There is a triable issue of material fact if, and only if, the evidence would allow a reasonable trier of fact to find the underlying fact in favor of the party opposing the motion in accordance with the applicable standard of proof.”  (Aguilar v. Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 850.)  “[T]he party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material fact.”  (Ibid.)  

 

DISCUSSION

Defendant moves for summary judgment, not summary adjudication.  However, Defendant fails to address Plaintiff’s claim for breach of contract.  In support of the claim for breach of contract, Plaintiff alleges, in pertinent part, Defendant breached the agreement between Plaintiff and Defendant by “failing to charge his client only for services that were in fact rendered . . . .”  (Complaint, ¶ 8, subd. (o).)  The pleadings set the outer limits of materiality in a summary judgment proceeding.  (FPI Development, Inc. v. Nakashima (1991) 231 Cal.App.3d 367, 382.)  “There is little flexibility in the procedural imperatives of [Code of Civil Procedure section 437c], and the issues raised by a motion for summary judgment (or summary adjudication) are pure questions of law.  As a result, section 437c is unforgiving; a failure to comply with any one of its myriad requirements is likely to be fatal to the offending party.”  (Brantley v. Pisaro (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 1591, 1607.)  The Court, therefore, denies the motion as procedurally defective.  The Court provides the following analysis to assist the parties.

To prevail on the claim for attorney malpractice, Plaintiff will have to show Defendant had a duty to use the skill, prudence, and diligence that other attorneys commonly possess and exercise; Defendant breached that duty; and Defendant’s breach of the duty caused damages to Plaintiff.  (Landmark Screens, LLC v. Morgan, Lewis & Bockius, LLP (2010) 183 Cal.App.4th 238, 247-248.)  Defendant contends Plaintiff cannot show Defendant’s breach of any duty to Plaintiff caused Plaintiff to sustain damages.

Plaintiff settled the claims in the underlying action.  As such, to prevail, Plaintiff must advance evidence to show “that a greater settlement was lost.”  (Filbin v. Fitzgerald (2012) 211 Cal.App.4th 154, 172.)  Defendant presents evidence Plaintiff agreed to a settlement one week after substituting in new counsel to replace Defendant in the underlying action.  (See Request for Judicial Notice, Exh. 7, p. 8.)[1]  As such, Defendant shifts the burden to Plaintiff to provide  evidence demonstrating Defendant’s conduct caused Plaintiff to lose the opportunity to settle for a greater sum. 

In opposition, Plaintiff submits the declaration of its current counsel, Steven A. Morris (“Morris”), who also represented Plaintiff in the underlying action after Defendant withdrew from it. Morris avers that, at the time of the settlement, Morris was concerned Plaintiff would lose a pending summary judgment motion because Defendant had failed to adequately oppose it.  As such, Morris adds he was required to accept less in settlement on Plaintiff’s behalf than Plaintiff should have obtained.  (Declaration of Steven A. Morris in Opposition to Motion for Summary Judgment, ¶¶ 7, 11-12.)  This evidence suffices to raise triable issues of material fact in opposition to the motion.

Defendant is ordered to give notice of the court’s order and to file proof of service of same.


[1] Defendant’s request for judicial notice, filed concurrently in support of the motion for summary judgment, is granted.


Case Number: 23STCV06287    Hearing Date: June 6, 2024    Dept: 39

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

DEPARTMENT

39

HEARING DATE

June 6, 2024

CASE NUMBER

23STCV06287

MOTIONS

Demurrer to Second Amended Complaint;

Motion to Strike Prayer for Punitive Damages in Second Amended Complaint

MOVING PARTY

Defendant General Motors, Inc.

OPPOSING PARTY

Plaintiff Tanisha Johnson

 

MOTION

Plaintiff Tanisha Johnson (“Plaintiff”) alleges she purchased a defective vehicle from Defendant General Motors, Inc. (“Defendant”).  Defendant demurs to the fifth cause of action in Plaintiff’s second amended complaint.  Defendant also moves to strike Plaintiff’s prayer for punitive damages in the second amended complaint.  Plaintiff opposes the motions. 

 

ANALYSIS

“It is black letter law that a demurrer tests the legal sufficiency of the allegations in a complaint.”  (Lewis v. Safeway, Inc. (2015) 235 Cal.App.4th 385, 388.)  In ruling on a demurrer, the court must “liberally construe[]” the allegations in the complaint.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 452.)  “This rule of liberal construction means that the reviewing court draws inferences favorable to the plaintiff, not the defendant.”  (Perez v. Golden Empire Transit Dist. (2012) 209 Cal.App.4th 1228, 1238.)

Any party, within the time allowed to respond to a pleading, may serve and file a motion to strike the whole pleading or any part thereof.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435, subd. (b)(1); Cal. Rules of Court, rule 3.1322, subd. (b).)  On a motion to strike, the court may: (1) strike out any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading; or (2) strike out all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of California, a court rule, or an order of the court.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 436, subd. (a)-(b); Stafford v. Shultz (1954) 42 Cal.2d 767, 782.)

Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is for fraud by concealment.  Plaintiff must allege fraud with particularity.  “This means: (1) general pleading of the legal conclusion of fraud is insufficient; and (2) every element of the cause of action for fraud must be alleged in full, factually and specifically, and the policy of liberal construction of pleading will not usually be invoked to sustain a pleading that is defective in any material respect.”  (Wilhelm v. Pray, Price, Williams & Russell (1986) 186 Cal.App.3d 1324, 1331.) 

“There are four circumstances in which nondisclosure or concealment may constitute actionable fraud: (1) when the defendant is in a fiduciary relationship with the plaintiff; (2) when the defendant had exclusive knowledge of material facts not known to the plaintiff; (3) when the defendant actively conceals a material fact from the plaintiff; and (4) when the defendant makes partial representations but also suppresses some material facts.”  (LiMandri v. Judkins (1997) 52 Cal.App.4th 326, 336, internal quotations and citations omitted.) 

Plaintiff alleges Defendant “knew (or should have known) that the cooling system had one or more defects that can result in various problems . . . .”  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶ 62); “while Defendant . . . knew about the cooling system defect, Defendant . . . nevertheless concealed and failed to disclose the defective nature of the Vehicle cooling system to Plaintiff prior to and at the time of sale and thereafter”  (id., ¶ 64); and “Defendant . . . acquired its knowledge of the cooling system defect and its potential consequences prior to Plaintiff acquiring the subject vehicle, though [sic] sources not available to consumers such as Plaintiff, including but not limited to pre-production testing data, early consumer complaints about the Cooling System Defect made directly to [Defendant] and its network of dealers, aggregate warranty data compiled from [Defendant’s] network of dealers, testing conducted by [Defendant] in response to these complaints, as well as warranty repair and part replacements data received by [Defendant] from [Defendant’s] network of dealers, amongst other sources of internal information . . . .”  (id., ¶ 66). 

Plaintiff does not allege specific facts to support the generalities noted above.  Therefore, the court sustains the demurrer to the fifth cause of action. 

The court notes Defendant also argues the statute of limitations bars Plaintiff’s claim for fraud.  Plaintiff alleges she did not suspect her vehicle was defective beyond repair until shortly before filing this action.  (Second Amended Complaint, ¶¶ 33-34.)  Under the delayed discovery rule, a claim does not accrue until “a plaintiff knew or should have known of the wrongful conduct at issue.”  (April Enterprises, Inc. v. KTTV (1983) 147 Cal.App.3d 805, 832.)  Further, the statute of limitations did not run while Defendant represented it could repair any defects the  vehicle may have had.  (Cardinal Health 301, Inc, v. Tyco Electronics, Corp. (2008) 169 Cal.App.4th 116, 133–134.)  The Court cannot resolve on demurrer the evidentiary issue of when Plaintiff knew or should have known of her claims against Defendant. 

Plaintiff may recover civil penalties, not punitive damages, for violations of the Song-Beverly Consumer Warranty Act.  (See Civ. Code, § 1794, subd. (c).)  Accordingly, as the Court sustains the demurrer to the fifth cause of action for fraud, the motion to strike the prayer for punitive damages is moot. 

Plaintiff requests leave to amend.  As the court has already provided Plaintiff with two opportunities to allege a cause of action for fraud, and Plaintiff has failed to do so, leave to amend would be futile, and is unwarranted.  (Grieves v. Superior Court (1984) 157 Cal.App.3d 159, 168.)  However, this ruling is without prejudice to Plaintiff’s ability to seek leave to amend should Plaintiff learn, through discovery for example, of specific facts supporting a claim for fraud.

CONCLUSION AND ORDER

Defendant’s demurrer to Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action is sustained without leave to amend.  To the extent it is not moot, Defendant’s motion to strike is granted.

Defendant is ordered to provide notice of this order and file proof of service of same.
