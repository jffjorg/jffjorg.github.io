DEPARTMENT F43 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22CHCV00236    Hearing Date: April 29, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 4-29-24

Case #22CHCV00236, Monocent Inc., vs. Mina Kateb

Trial Date: 6-17-24

 

MOTION TO COMPEL COMPLIANCE WITH DEPOSITION SUBPOENA OF THIRD PARTY

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Mina Kateb

RESPONDING PARTY: No response has been filed.

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

Kateb has requested an order compelling compliance with a deposition subpoena to third party Doug Bend, Esq., for production of business records.

 

RULING: Motion is granted.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

Plaintiff and Cross-Defendant Mina Kateb (Kateb) filed this motion to compel compliance with deposition subpoena of business records after serving the subpoena on Doug Bend, Esq., (Bend) on December 5, 2023. Bend was involved in transactions relating to Monocent and drafting documents for Monocent, the company that Kateb claims to be a shareholder for.

 

Kateb’s subpoena sought non-privileged documents and communications related to Monocent and Kateb; documents relating to Kateb’s corporate shares; documents relating to the corporate status of Monocent; and documents relating to the creation and/or preparation of Monocent’s governing documents and Secretary of State filings.

 

In a letter dated December 28, 2023, Bend refused to produce any documents responsive to the subpoena and claimed that the attorney-client privilege applied to all of the communications and documents. He also stated that he could provide responsive information if there were a court order requiring him to do so. Kateb’s counsel attempted to meet and confer with Bend regarding the documents and communications at issue, but the efforts were fruitless. Kateb filed this motion to compel compliance with deposition subpoena on February 26, 2024.

 

No opposition has been filed to Kateb’s motion. Kateb filed a reply on April 22, 2024, in which she represents that counsel for Monocent stated in a letter that “my client has approved Mr. Doug Bend, Monocent Corporate attorney, to provide you with all non-privileged material you have requested through your subpoena.” (Reply at p. 2.)

 

ANALYSIS

A party “may obtain discovery regarding any matter, not privileged, that is relevant to the subject matter involved . . . if the matter either is itself admissible in evidence or appears reasonably calculated to lead to the discovery of admissible evidence.” (CCP § 2017.010.) A party may obtain discovery regarding relevant material. (CCP § 2017.010.)

 

Evidence is relevant “if it might reasonably assist a party in evaluating its case, preparing for trial, or facilitating a settlement.” (Glenfed Development Corp. v. Sup. Ct. (1997) 53 Cal.App.4th 1113, 1117.) Any doubt on relevance is resolved generally in favor of production. (Mercury Interactive Corp. v. Klein (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 60, 98.)

 

California law permits litigants to subpoena records from nonparties. (CCP § 2020.010.) Subpoenas solely seeking the production of business records, rather than appearance at deposition, are specifically authorized. (CCP § 2020.410.) Such business records subpoenas must command compliance “on a date that is no earlier than 20 days after the issuance, or 15 days after the service, of the deposition subpoena, whichever date is later.” (CCP § 2020.410(c).)

 

Service of a deposition subpoena obligates the production of whatever documents or things are specified in the subpoena, and to appear in any proceeding to enforce discovery. (CCP § 2020.220(c).) A deposition subpoena is enforceable through a motion to compel compliance with the subpoena. (CCP § 1987.1.) If a deponent fails to produce any document under the deponent’s control that is specified in a deposition subpoena, the party seeking discovery may move for an order compelling that production. (CCP § 2025.480.)

 

In this case, the disputed facts of consequence include (1) whether Kateb is a shareholder of Monocent; (2) the percentage of Monocent that Kateb owns; (3) whether Defendants have taken actions to defraud Kateb out of her interest in Monocent; and (4) the current value of Monocent. The documents and communications that Kateb seeks from Bend are related to whether Kateb is shareholder of Monocent, and if so, what percentage she owns. Kateb also seeks information related to the current corporate status of Monocent. Therefore, the documents and communications that Kateb wants to obtain are relevant to this action and likely to lead to discoverable information.

 

Furthermore, Kateb argues that the subpoena on its face does not call for the production of privileged documents because the requests are for “non-privileged” documents and communications. If Bend does claim that the documents that Kateb seeks are privileged, then he must list then on a privilege log so that Kateb may determine the basis for the objection. (Bank Of America, N.A. v. Superior Court (2013) 212 Cal.App.4th 1076, 1099-1100 (“The party claiming the privilege has the burden of establishing the preliminary facts necessary to support its exercise”); Wellpoint Health Networks v. Superior Court (1997) 59 Cal.App.4th 110, 129-143 (ordering that party withholding documents responsive to subpoena on the ground of attorney-client privilege should prepare a privilege log for all such documents and “[t]he information in the privilege log must be sufficiently specific to allow a determination of whether each withheld document is or is not [in] fact privileged”).)

 

Currently, Bend has failed to demonstrate that the documents sought are subject to the attorney-client privilege. Kateb also argues that he has failed to even demonstrate that Monocent is his client. Additionally, Kateb argues that the documents responsive to the subpoena would fall within exceptions to the attorney-client privilege. Any communications shared with third parties would not be privileged unless (1) the disclosure was confidential and (2) the confidential disclosure was reasonably necessary for the accomplishment of the purpose for which the lawyer was consulted. (Seahaus La Jolla Owners Assn. v. Superior Court (2014) 224 Cal.App.4th 754, 768.) Kateb claims that the documents she seeks are communications with third parties.

 

Kateb also argues that documents transmitted to or from an attorney are not automatically privileged and that for communications to privileged, the dominant purpose of the communications must have been for obtaining legal advice. Kateb argues that business advice would not be privileged.

 

Finally, Kateb argues that communications between Taheri and Bend are subject to the crime fraud exception to the attorney-client privilege. Under Section 956(a) of the Evidence Code “[t]here is privilege under this article if the services of the lawyer were sought or obtained to enable or aid anyone to commit a crime or fraud.” (See B.P. Alaska Exploration, Inc. v. Superior Ct. (1988) 199 Cal.App.3d 1240, 1262 (the proponent of the crime fraud exception must make a showing of “evidence from which reasonable inferences can be drawn to establish the . . . fraud”).) Kateb claims that she has made a prima facie showing of fraud by alleging that Taheri sought the services of Bend to assist him in concealing material information about Monocent from Kateb, despite his express admission to Bend that Kateb was a shareholder of Monocent. She argues that this would strip the attorney-client privilege from any communications between Taheri and Bend. (BP Alaska, 199 Cal.App.3d at 1262 (holding that the crime fraud exception applies to “communications sought to enable the client to plan to commit a fraud, whether the fraud is successful or not).)

 

Given the foregoing arguments and the lack of opposition from Bend and Monocent, the Court grants Plaintiff’s motion to compel compliance with the deposition subpoena of Doug Bend. Bend must produce any non-privileged documents and communications in his possession that are responsive to Kateb’s requests. To the extent that he claims privilege, he must produce a privilege log.

 

Moving party to give notice.


Case Number: 23CHCV02008    Hearing Date: April 29, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 4-29-24

Case #23CHCV02008 , Bank of American N.A. vs. Alonzo Salmon

Trial Date: N/A

 

MOTION TO DEEM REQUESTS FOR ADMISSIONS ADMITTED

 

MOVING PARTY: Plaintiff Bank of America

RESPONDING PARTY: Defendant Alonzo Salmon

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

For Plaintiff’s Requests for Admissions to be deemed admitted

 

RULING: Motion granted

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On August 16, 2023, Plaintiff Bank of America N.A. (Plaintiff) served Defendant Alonzo Salmon (Defendant) with Requests for Admissions, Set One, by mail. Plaintiff has not received a response to these requests for admissions. On October 23, 2023, Plaintiff’s counsel attempted to resolve this discovery problem informally. They requested that Defendant’s responses be received no later than 5 p.m. on November 2, 2023. Defendant did not respond to that letter. Plaintiff filed this motion on December 1, 2023. Plaintiff seeks an order deeming the Requests for Admissions admitted. No opposition has been filed.

 

ANALYSIS

“Any party may obtain discovery . . . by a written request that any other party to the action admit the genuineness of specified documents, or the truth of specified matters of fact, opinion relating to fact, or application of law to fact. A request for admission may relate to a matter that is in controversy between the parties.” (CCP § 2033.010.)  “Within 30 days after service of requests for admission, the party to whom the requests are directed shall serve the original of the response to them on the requesting party, and a copy of the response on all other parties who have appeared…” (CCP § 2033.250(a).) 

 

If a party to whom request for admissions are served fails to provide a timely response, the party to whom the request was directed waives any objections, including based on privilege or the work product doctrine. (CCP § 2033.280(a).) The requesting party can move for an order that the genuineness of any documents and the truth of any matters specified in the request be deemed admitted. (CCP § 2033.280(b).)

 

Defendant has not served any responses to Plaintiff’s requests for admission. Plaintiff moved for an order deeming the requests for admission admitted. The Court grants Plaintiff’s motion.

 

ORDER

1.      Plaintiff’s motion to deem requests for admissions admitted is granted.


Case Number: 23CHCV02662    Hearing Date: April 29, 2024    Dept: F43

Dept. F43

Date: 4-29-24

Case #23CHCV02662, Golden Hammer Ops, LLC vs. JETNET, LLC, et al.

Trial Date: N/A

 

APPLICATION TO SEAL

 

MOVING PARTY: Defendant and Cross-Complaint JETNET, LLC

RESPONDING PARTY: Plaintiff Golden Hammer Ops, LLC

 

RELIEF REQUESTED

JETNET is requesting that the Court seal certain documents that were filed with the court because they are protected by a contractual confidentiality and non-disclosure clause.

 

RULING: Application to seal is denied without prejudice.

 

SUMMARY OF ACTION

On September 5, 2023, Golden Hammer Ops, LLC (Golden Hammer) sued JETNET, LLC (JETNET), Daniel Streufert, and ADSBEXCHANGE.com (ADSB) for misappropriation of trade secrets, intentional interference with contractual relations, and violations of the California Computer Fraud and Abuse Act.

 

On October 30, 2023, JETNET cross-complained against Golden Hammer and its owner, Gregory Rowlee (Rowlee), alleging causes of action for breach of contract, intentional interference with contractual relations, and declaratory relief. JETNET alleges that Golden Hammer and Rowlee breached an agreement between the two parties and interfered with JETNET’s contract with a third party, AWS.

 

ADSB demurred to the complaint, relying in part on the asset purchase agreement (APA) by which JETNET acquired ADSB.  Pursuant to the parties’ stipulated protective order, redacted versions of the APA were attached to the parties’ points and authorities, and selected portions of the APA were quoted in their papers. An unredacted version was lodged with the court by Golden Hammer conditionally under seal.  The court overruled the demurrer, and did so without reliance on the APA, although it had been raised as an issue.

 

While the demurrer was pending, JETNET filed a motion to seal the following: (1) “Portions of Golden Hammer Ops Opposition to the Demurrer of Defendant [ADSB]…; (2) “Portions of the [APA] attached as Exhibit A to the Declaration of Nicholas Sethi in Support of the Opposition.”; and (3) “Portions of [ADSB’s] Reply in support of its Demurrer…”

 

JETNET contends that sealing those materials is proper because the APA states that its contents are confidential and are not to be disclosed or otherwise communicated to anyone other than the parties to that contract.  JETNET argues that it is part of a small industry with few other players and that the confidentiality of “transactional documents” is needed to maintain one’s competitive standing and to foster economic activity in the field.  On the other hand, JETNET contends, there is limited public interest involved, particularly where the terms of the APA played no role in the court’s order overruling the demurrer.  Finally, JETNET contends that the sealing order is necessary to further the goals of the parties’ stipulated protective order, and that failing to do so would render that order meaningless.  The motion is supported by the declaration of JETNET lawyer Andrew Winetroub, who addressed only the circumstances surrounding the demurrer, references to the APA, and the existence of the APA’s confidentiality provision. Golden Hammer opposes the motion, primarily on the basis that the public has an interest in the facts surrounding the ADSB acquisition and because JETNET’s application fails to meet the standards required for an order to seal court documents.

 

ANALYSIS

The First Amendment to the United States Constitution provides for a qualified right of public access to court proceedings, both civil and criminal.  So too does the California Constitution, which states in Article I, § 3(b)(1), that “the people have the right of access to information concerning the conduct of the people’s business, and therefore the meetings of public bodies and the writings of public officials and agencies shall be open to public scrutiny.”  Section 3(b)(2) provides that a statute or court rule that limits the right of access shall be narrowly construed, while one that furthers the right of access shall be broadly construed.

 

Likewise, Code of Civil Procedure section 124 states that “[e]xcept as provided in Family Code § 214 or any other provision of law, the sittings of every court shall be public.”

 

“Unless confidentiality is required by law, court records are presumed to be open.” (California Rules of Court, rule 2.550(c) (Rule 2.550).) Rule 2.550(d) states that: “The court may order that a record be filed under seal only if it expressly finds facts that establish: (1) There exists an overriding interest that overcomes the right of public access to the record; (2) The overriding interest supports sealing the record; (3) A substantial probability exists that the overriding interest will be prejudiced if the record is not sealed; (4) The proposed sealing is narrowly tailored; and (5) No less restrictive means exist to achieve the overriding interest.” If a trial court fails to make the required findings, “the order is deficient and cannot support sealing.” (Overstock.com, Inc. v. Goldman Sachs Group, Inc. (2014) 231 Cal.App.4th 471, 487.)

 

The party seeking to seal the documents must establish with “specificity” that a serious injury is likely to occur if the materials are not sealed and that protecting this interest overcomes the public’s right of access to court records and items of public interest. (Universal City Studios, Inc., v. Superior Court (2003) 110 Cal.App.4th 1273, 1282 (Universal City Studios).)  Accordingly, any order sealing a record must support its finding of the need to seal the record with facts, and “[d]irect the sealing of only those documents and pages, or, if reasonably practicable, portions of those documents and pages, that contained the material that needs to be placed under seal. All other portions of each document or page must be included in the public file.” (Rule 2.550(e)(1)(B).)

 

Relying on Universal Studios, supra, 110 Cal.App.4th at page 1273, JETNET contends that an overriding interest exists based on the APA’s contractual nondisclosure provision.  However, the existence of such a provision by itself is not enough.  Instead, the party seeking to seal a record on the basis of such a contractual provision must also submit facts establishing both likely injury from the record’s disclosure and that the means of redaction or exclusion are limited to preventing such injury. (Ibid.)

 

Golden Hammer opposes the motion, contending that the APA plays an important role in this litigation, that there is a strong public interest in the operations of flight tracking companies such as JETNET, that JETNET has failed to establish prejudice from leaving the documents unsealed, and that the request is not narrowly tailored.  JETNET argues that it only seeks to seal those portions of the APA that are not already unredacted in the public record following ADSBs demurrer. It is only the unredacted portions of the APA in the public record, JETNET argues, that are relevant to determining the outcome of the demurrer and any further disclosure would be prejudicial and unnecessary.

 

Finally, JETNET argues in its motion that the proposed sealing is narrowly tailored and that no less restrictive means exists to achieve the overriding interest.

 

The Court concludes that JETNET has failed to meet the standards for a sealing order.  First, it has not provided the Court with the actual language of the APA’s confidentiality provision.  Second, apart from argument, it has made no showing of why a sealing order is required in this context.  Instead, the factual assertions concerning the effect of disclosure in the industry is unsupported by any factual showing.  Next, the request is not narrowly tailored.  As to the APA itself, JETNET has not addressed whether any portions of the APA might be exempt from a sealing order.  As to the papers filed in connection with ADSB’s demurrer, its motion refers to only unspecified portions of those documents.  Finally, as Golden Hammer’s opposition points out, the APA includes multiple provisions that are either prefatory, definitional, or that otherwise need not necessarily be sealed, and JETNET has failed to explain why any such provisions must be sealed. In short, JETNET has failed to establish a likelihood of serious injury apart from the contractual provision itself, or that its proposed redactions are limited to preventing such injury.  (Universal City Studios, supra, 110 Cal.App.4th at p. 1281; accord In re Providian Credit Card Cases (2002) 96 Cal.App.4th 292, 309 [courts should view with skepticism overbroad sealing requests].)

 

Accordingly, the Court finds that JETNET has failed to override the public’s interest in access to court proceedings.  In recognition of the possibility that JETNET may yet be able to do so, however, the court denies the motion without prejudice.
