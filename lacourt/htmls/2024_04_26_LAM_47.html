DEPARTMENT 47 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 20STCV15786    Hearing Date: April 26, 2024    Dept: 47

Tentative Ruling

 

Judge Theresa M. Traber, Department 47

 

 

HEARING DATE:     April 26, 2024                        TRIAL DATE: May 21, 2024

                                                          

CASE:                         James Weldon Schott v. Miguel A. Melgoza

 

CASE NO.:                 20STCV15786           

 

(1) MOTION FOR TERMINATING SANCTIONS

(2) MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT

 

MOVING PARTY:               (1)(2) Plaintiff James Weldon Schott

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): (1) Defendant Miguel A. Melgoza in propria persona

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         04/24/20: Complaint filed.

·         09/27/22: First Amended Complaint filed.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is an action for partition of a parcel of real property and for fraud, conversion, and breach of fiduciary duty. Plaintiff alleges that Defendant induced him to loan money for the purchase of the property to operate it as a dispensary.

 

Plaintiff moves for terminating sanctions. Plaintiff also moves for summary judgment.

           

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant Miguel Melgoza’s Answer to the First Amended Complaint is hereby stricken in its entirety and default entered this date against Defendant.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is DENIED as moot.

 

            All future hearings are advanced to this date and vacated.

 

            The Court will set a default prove-up hearing in this partition action at the hearing on the motions.  

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Motion for Terminating Sanctions

 

            Plaintiff moves for terminating sanctions for Defendant’s failure to respond to discovery despite Court orders.

 

Request for Judicial Notice

 

            Plaintiff requests that the Court take judicial notice of its October 31, 2023 Minute Order in this action granting Plaintiff’s Motions to Compel Responses to Form Interrogatories and Requests for Production and his Motion to Deem the Truth of Matters Specified in Requests for Admissions, and imposing monetary sanctions on Defendant. Plaintiff’s request is GRANTED pursuant to Evidence Code section 452(d) (court records).

 

Legal Standard for Terminating Sanctions

 

The Court has the authority to impose sanctions against a party that engages in any misuse of the discovery process (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030), including “[f]ailing to respond or to submit to an authorized method of discovery.” (Code Civ. Proc. §§ 2023.010(d).) A party engaging in this conduct may be subject to sanctions including monetary sanctions (Code Civ Proc. § 2023.030(a)), evidence sanctions (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(c)), or terminating sanctions. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2023.030(d).)  “[T]rial courts should select sanctions tailored to the harm caused by the misuse of the discovery process and should not exceed what is required to protect the party harmed by the misuse of the discovery process.” (Dept. of Forestry & Fire Protection v. Howell (2017) 18 Cal.App.5th 154, 191, disapproved of on other grounds in Presbyterian Camp & Conference Centers, Inc. v. Superior Court (2021) 12 Cal.5th 493.) Sanctions are generally imposed in an incremental approach. (Id.) Generally, the appropriate sanctions when a party repeatedly and willfully fails to provide evidence to the opposing party as required by the discovery rules is preclusion of that evidence from the trial. (Juarez v. Boy Scouts of America, Inc. (2000) 81 Cal.App.4th 377, 390, disapproved of on other grounds by Brown v. USA Taekwondo (2021) 11 Cal.5th 204.)

 

In considering a motion for terminating sanctions, the Court is to attempt to “tailor the sanction to the harm caused by the withheld discovery.” (Collisson & Kaplan v. Hartunian (1994) 21 Cal.App.4th 1611, 1618-1619.) 

 

[T]he question before this court is not whether the trial court should have imposed a lesser sanction; rather, the question is whether the trial court abused its discretion by imposing the sanction it chose. [Citation.] Moreover, imposition of a lesser sanction would have permitted [defendants] to benefit from their stalling tactics. [Citation.] The trial court did not abuse its discretion by tailoring the sanction to the particular abuse.

 

(Id. at 1620.), and

 

            Moreover, in deciding whether to impose a terminating sanction, the trial court is to consider the totality of the circumstances: the “conduct of the party to determine if the actions were willful; the detriment to the propounding party; and the number of formal and informal attempts to obtain the discovery.” (Lang v. Hochman (2000) 77 Cal.App.4th 1225, 1246.)

 

Defendant’s Failure to Respond to Discovery

 

            Plaintiff argues that terminating sanctions should be granted because Defendant has failed to respond to basic discovery, has failed to comply with Court orders to produce responses to that discovery, and has failed to pay sanctions for misuse of the discovery process.

 

            On April 10, 2023, Plaintiff served Defendant with form interrogatories and requests for production via overnight service. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 1.A [Form interrogatories]; 2.A [Requests for Production].) After Defendant failed to provide responses within the deadlines provided, Plaintiff moved to compel responses on July 31, 2023. (Plaintiff’s Exh. 1-2.) The Court granted the motions on October 31, 2023, ordering Defendant to provide verified, code-compliant responses without objections within 30 days, and ordering sanctions against Defendant only in the amount of $2,180, to be paid in the same timeframe. (RJN Exh. A.) On December 7, 2023, the Court granted a motion by Defendant’s former counsel of record to withdraw from representation in this action on the basis that Defendant had ceased responding to his counsel. (See December 6, 2023 Minute Order.) Plaintiff’s counsel states that he attempted to contact Defendant Melgoza via letter on December 29, 2023 outlining the procedural status of the case, informing Defendant of his noncompliance, and advising Defendant of Plaintiff’s intent of filing this motion. (Declaration of Matthew T. Ward ISO Mot. Exhs. 4-5.) As of the date this motion was filed, Defendant had not responded, nor has he paid the sanctions owed. (Ward Decl. ¶¶ 8-9.)

 

            Plaintiff contends that terminating sanctions are warranted because Defendant has failed to comply with Court orders, ignored monetary sanctions, and appears to have abandoned the case altogether. However, when this matter initially came for hearing on March 14, 2024, Defendant appeared pro se and orally requested a continuance to respond to this motion. The Court granted this request and ordered Defendant to serve and file any opposition to this motion and a declaration accounting for his lack of responsiveness and failure to participate in the case. (March 14, 2024 Minute Order.)

 

            Defendant did not file any opposition to this motion, and while he has filed a declaration which purports to explain his conduct, no proof of service was attached to the declaration, and Plaintiff represents in its reply brief that the declaration was not served. Nevertheless, as Plaintiff was able to retrieve the declaration and prepare a response, the Court will address the declaration.

 

            At the outset, the Court observes that the declaration is not made under penalty of perjury, as pointed out by Plaintiff. For any matter which is to be evidenced or supported by an unsworn declaration, that declaration must instead contain a certification or declaration of its truth under penalty of perjury under the laws of the state of California which is dated and signed by the declarant. (Code Civ. Proc. § 2015.5.) Accepting, for the moment, that the declaration is true, Defendant contends that he was absent from the case because of a family crisis between November 2023 and January 2024 because his family was a victim of an unspecified crime in Mexico. Defendant claims that he had limited communication while abroad, and that this was the cause of his absence and the loss of his attorney. Defendant contends that he wishes to continue the case, will retain new counsel, and will fully cooperate with the court. The declaration does not clearly state that Defendant will pay the sanctions previously ordered, nor that he will provide responses to outstanding discovery, and provides no timetable for when the Court can expect him to effect compliance. Moreover, as Plaintiff argues in reply, the declaration does not account for the complete lack of responses or appearance from Defendant between May and October 2023, nor does it account for the period after January 2024.

 

            The Court is persuaded by Plaintiff’s arguments. Defendant has failed to comply with basic discovery requests for almost a full year and is four months overdue on paying the monetary sanctions imposed by this Court. Defendant’s declaration is not competent evidence because it is not made under penalty of perjury, and, even if it were, Defendant has not accounted for his failure to respond for mor than five months or to rectify his noncompliance since January 2024. The Court is therefore forced to conclude that Defendant’s failure to comply with his discovery obligations and with this Court’s orders is willful, and that no lighter measure will serve to remedy the prejudice to Plaintiff or deter future violations by Defendant. Plaintiff is therefore entitled to an order granting terminating sanctions.

 

Conclusion

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

Motion for Summary Judgment

 

            Plaintiff also moves for summary judgment against Defendant. However, as the Court has granted terminating sanctions against Defendant, Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is MOOT.

 

CONCLUSION:

 

            Accordingly, Plaintiff’s Motion for Terminating Sanctions is GRANTED.

 

            Defendant Miguel Melgoza’s Answer to the First Amended Complaint is hereby stricken in its entirety and default entered this date against Defendant.

 

            Plaintiff’s Motion for Summary Judgment is MOOT.

 

            All future hearings are advanced to this date and vacated.

 

            The Court will set a default prove-up hearing in this partition action at the hearing on the motions.

CASE:                         Arturo Montoya-Bautista et al. v. Architectural Surfaces, Inc., et al.

 

CASE NO.:                 23STCV11727           

 

MOTION TO STRIKE PORTIONS OF COMPLAINT

 

MOVING PARTY:               Defendant Dal-Tile Distribution, Inc.

 

RESPONDING PARTY(S): Plaintiffs Arturo Montoya-Bautista and Magdalena Balderas-Molina

 

CASE HISTORY:

·         05/24/23: Complaint filed.

·         12/28/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Lowe’s Home Centers, LLC

·         12/29/23: Cross-Complaint filed by Jacobe Enterprises, Inc.

 

STATEMENT OF MATERIAL FACTS AND/OR PROCEEDINGS:

           

            This is a toxic tort action alleging that Plaintiff Montoya-Bautista developed silicosis and consequential injuries by breathing in dust from Defendants’ stone products generated by his work as a fabricator and polisher of stone countertops.

 

Defendant Dal-Tile Distribution, Inc. moves to strike portions of the Complaint.

 

TENTATIVE RULING:

 

            Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.

 

DISCUSSION:

 

Defendant Dal-Tile Distribution, Inc. moves to strike select portions of the Complaint pertaining to “[o]ther products to be identified during discovery (Complaint p.11:27) and pertaining to fines imposed on Defendant and convictions of its officers for hazardous waste dumping. (Complaint ¶¶ 274-275.)

 

Legal Standard

 

The court may, upon a motion, or at any time in its discretion, and upon terms it deems proper, strike any irrelevant, false, or improper matter inserted in any pleading. Code Civ. Proc., § 436(a). The court may also strike all or any part of any pleading not drawn or filed in conformity with the laws of this state, a court rule, or an order of the court. Id., § 436(b). The grounds for a motion to strike are that the pleading has irrelevant, false or improper matter, or has not been drawn or filed in conformity with laws. Id.§ 436. The grounds for moving to strike must appear on the face of the pleading or by way of judicial notice. Id.§ 437. “When the defect which justifies striking a complaint is capable of cure, the court should allow leave to amend.” Vaccaro v. Kaiman (1998) 63 Cal.App.4th 761, 768. A motion to strike can be used where the complaint or other pleading has not been drawn or filed in conformity with applicable rules or court orders.  Code Civ. Proc., § 436(b). This provision is for "the striking of a pleading due to improprieties in its form or in the procedures pursuant to which it was filed."  Ferraro v. Camarlinghi (2008) 161 Cal.App.4th 509, 528 (emphasis in original).

 

Meet and Confer

 

 Before filing a motion to strike, the moving party shall meet and confer in person or by telephone with the party who has filed the pleading subject to the motion to strike and file a declaration detailing their meet-and-confer efforts.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5(a).) However, an insufficient meet-and-confer process is not grounds to grant or deny a motion to strike.  (Code Civ. Proc., § 435.5(a)(4).)

 

The Declaration of Na Hyun Seo states that Defendant sent a meet-and-confer letter to Plaintiffs on November 21, 2023, but Plaintiffs never responded to efforts to contact them by phone or email. (Declaration of Na Hyun Seo ISO Mot. ¶¶ 2-3; Exh. A.) Defendant has thus satisfied its statutory meet-and-confer obligation.

 

Plaintiffs’ Filings

 

            On April 8, 2024, the Court admonished Plaintiffs’ counsel for their failure to properly label their motions and papers properly in this case:

 

As a preliminary matter, each of Plaintiffs’ filings in this matter is identified as Memorandum of Points and Authorities without reference to the specific motion or the Defendant to which the motion is directed. Plaintiffs’ repeated failure to label their motions correctly has wasted extensive judicial resources because of the sheer volume of filings in the instant docket. Counsel for the various Defendants in this action have had no difficulty in properly labeling their filings in this case, and most parties in other proceedings before this Court are similarly untroubled by this requirement. Plaintiffs’ counsel is admonished for this failure and is expressly ordered to properly label each filing going forward, so the Court can identify the opposition that responds to each motion filed by one of the many defendants in this action.

 

(April 8, 2024 Minute Order [emphasis added].) Despite the Court’s explicit instruction, Plaintiffs’ counsel filed its response to this motion on April 15, 2024 which is labeled in the Court’s records only as “Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Opposition.” The Court ordered Plaintiffs’ counsel to label the filing in such a manner to permit the Court to match opposition and motion. Simply labeling the filing as “Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Opposition” does not serve that purpose and does not comply with the Court’s order. A compliant designation would be, as an example, “Memorandum of Points and Authorities in Opposition to Dal-Tile Distribution’s Motion to Strike.” Plaintiffs’ counsel is again ordered to properly label each filing going forward, and, to eliminate any possibility of confusion, is expressly instructed follow the above format for each of its filings in the instant matter from now on. Further failure to comply with this instruction will not be tolerated.

 

“Other Stone Products”

 

Defendant moves to strike an allegation relating to “[o]ther stone products to be identified during the course of discovery.” (Complaint p.11:27.)

 

Defendant is correct that this allegation is inconsistent with the requirements of Bockrath v. Aldrich Chemical Co., Inc. (1999) 21 Cal.4th 71, the leading case on pleading requirements in toxic tort cases. That case requires Plaintiffs to “identify each product that allegedly caused the injury.” (Id. at 80.) If Plaintiffs had alleged only that Defendant had manufactured something that caused the injury and that they would identify that “something” during discovery, that would be inadequate. It is only the naming of a specific product that saves Plaintiff’s allegations against this Defendant.  The further reference to other products “to be identified during discovery” is improper. 

 

By analogy, when seeking to amend a complaint, “a vague suggestion that additional facts might be uncovered through discovery is insufficient to justify allowing plaintiffs further leave to amend their complaint.” (AREI II Cases (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 1004, 1020.) If that type of suggestion is inadequate to justify leave to amend, it must be inadequate at the outset.  

 

In so ruling, the Court emphasizes this does not mean that Plaintiffs shall be forever barred from identifying additional injury-causing products during the course of further discovery which were not listed in his Complaint. “If discovery and investigation develop factual grounds justifying a timely amendment to a pleading, leave to amend must be liberally granted.” (Mabie v. Hyatt (1998) 61 Cal.App.4th 581, 596.) Plaintiffs are also not categorically precluded from pursuing evidence of other injury-causing products. The Discovery Act specifically permits discovery “reasonably calculated to lead to discovery of admissible evidence.” (Code Civ. Proc. § 2017.010.)  Moreover, any “doubts as to relevance should generally be resolved in favor of permitting discovery.” (Colonial Life & Accident Ins. Co. v. Superior Court (1982) 31 Cal.3d 785, 790.)

Hazardous Waste Dumping

 

            Defendant also moves to strike paragraphs 274 and 275 of the Complaint as irrelevant. These paragraphs allege:

 

274. In 1991 the Texas Water Commission imposed a record $1 million fine against Dal-Tile for 12 years of illegally dumping hazardous waste into two gravel pits in southeastern Dallas County. It was the largest fine ever levied by a state agency for environmental violations. The company was required by law to send contaminated wastes from the manufacturing process to special landfills but, according to state investigators, dumped the material from 1975 to 1987 in pits that were not specially licensed. The commission also found that Dal-Tile used waste oils on farmland for dust control, failed to notify the state it had discharged industrial solid wastes into water, and failed to keep records of the waste and file annual reports.

 

275. In 1993, a federal grand jury indicted Robert Brittingham, Jr., and company president John Lomonaco on 17 criminal counts, including conspiracy to dump hazardous waste. Brittingham was found guilty in 1993, fined $4 million, and sentenced to five years' probation, which he began fulfilling with 15 hours a week in community service by financing and operating a $6 million lead-abatement program for Dallas. The total cost of the fines and dumpsite cleanup came to $16.5 million.

 

(Complaint ¶¶ 274-75.) Defendant argues with considerable force that these allegations have no bearing on the instant case because hazardous waste dumping between 1975 and 1987 is totally irrelevant to the subject of this action. Plaintiffs, in opposition, argue that these allegations are proper to show conscious disregard of health and safety to support the prayer for punitive damages against Defendant. The Court is not persuaded. Hazardous waste dumping in the 1970s and 1980s is not relevant to whether Defendant consciously disregarded the risk to health and safety created by dust from stone countertops.

 

Conclusion

 

Accordingly, the motion to strike is GRANTED without leave to amend. Leave to amend to identify new products may be granted in the future if discovery reveals that an amendment to list another product or products is justified. 

 

CONCLUSION:

 

Accordingly, Defendant’s Motion to Strike is GRANTED without leave to amend.
