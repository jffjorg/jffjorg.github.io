DEPARTMENT 73 LAW AND MOTION RULINGS


Case Number: 22STCV06871    Hearing Date: June 7, 2024    Dept: 73

06/07/2024

Dept. 73

Hon. Rolf Treu, Judge presiding

 

ANIMAL PROTECTION AND RESCUE LEAGUE, INC. v. DAVID CHANG, et al. (22STCV06871)

 

Counsel for Plaintiff/moving party:  Bryan Pease (Pease Law, APC); David Tenenbaum (Law Offices of G. David Tenenbaum); David Simon (Simon Law Group)

 

Counsel for Defendants/opposing party:  Scott Burroughs (Doniger Burroughs)

 

PLAINTIFF’s Motion FOR ATTORNEYS’ FEES (filed 02/16/2024)

 

TENTATIVE RULING

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

 

I. BACKGROUND

 

On February 24, 2020, Plaintiff Animal Protection and Rescue League, Inc. (“Plaintiff” or “APRL”) filed this action against Defendants David Chang, Majordomo, MG JV LLC, Air Food L’Appart, Marc Moubarak and Therry Babet.

 

Plaintiff alleges two causes of action for: (1) unfair business practices and (2) declaratory relief. Plaintiff filed the instant lawsuit to enjoin the sale of foie gras, i.e., fattened duck liver, which is obtained from forced fed duck and geese, and which violates Health & Safety Code section 25982. 

 

On November 20, 2023, this Court issued a final judgment on Plaintiff’s claims stating it failed to carry its burden at trial and ruled in favor of Defendants.

 

On February 16, 2024, Plaintiff APRL filed a Motion for Attorneys’ Fees, arguing:

·       Plaintiff moves for attorneys fees asserting that although it didn’t meet its burden at trial, Plaintiff obtained the relief it was seeking through Defendants complying with the law voluntarily as a result of the lawsuit.

·       Under CCP §1021.5, a plaintiff is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees if four criteria are met: (1) it is the “successful party”; (2) its lawsuit has enforced an important right affecting the public interest; (3) a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and (4) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make an award of fees appropriate. (CCP § 1021.5.)

·       Plaintiff asserts it has met these criteria and the fees sought are reasonable.

 

In opposition, Defendants argue that:

·       Plaintiff’s motion is not timely. Plaintiff filed its motion more than 60 days after notice of entry of judgment was served.

·       Plaintiff does not satisfy the criteria of CCP §1021.5 because it was not a successful party. The Court did not find that Plaintiff had standing. The Court also stated that “[t]he individual Defendants are not proper parties” and that “Air Food L’appart is not an entity or a person.” Final Judgment pg. 1. Thus, “there is no defendant against whom a judgment can be properly entered.” Id. As such, it would be inappropriate to issue an award of attorney’s fees against Defendants against whom there could be no judgment.

o   Defendants conduct did not change after litigation. Defendants claim they never served foie grase. Plaintiff cites to evidence like Yelp comments, but the Court stated in its Final Judgment that the Yelp reference is not admissible.

o   Plaintiff’s lawsuit lacked merit

o   Settlement efforts were not reasonable

·       Plaintiff’s lawsuit did not enforce an important right affecting the public interest or confer a significant benefit on the general public

·       The lawsuit was not necessary, and the burden of private enforcement did not outweigh Plaintiff’s stake in the lawsuit

·       Should the Court exercise discretion to award any fees, they should be reduced

·       Plaintiff’s conduct is sanctionable

In reply, Plaintiff argues that:

·       The Court should rule on Plaintiff’s motion because it was filed due to mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect pursuant to CCP §473.

·       The Court stated in its Judgment that Plaintiff lacked standing, however, the Court did not rule that as a matter of law. Plaintiff asserts that evidence shows, including Yelp comments, that Defendants used to serve fois grase but no longer does.

 

II. ANALYSIS

 

A.     Legal Standard

 

CCP §§ 1032 and 1033.5 provide that a prevailing party may recover its costs as a matter of right, and that those costs may include attorneys’ fees when authorized by statute or contract. 

 

Section 1021.5 provides the “explicit statutory authority for court-awarded attorney fees under a private attorney general theory” where the lawsuit “has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest regardless of its source – constitutional, statutory or other.” (Woodland Hills Residents Assn., Inc. v. City Council, (1979) 23 Cal.3d 917, 925, quoting Cal. Code Civ. P. 1021.5 (internal quotations and emphasis omitted). Under CCP §1021.5, a plaintiff is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees if four criteria are met: (1) it is the “successful party”; (2) its lawsuit has enforced an important right affecting the public interest; (3) a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and (4) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make an award of fees appropriate. (CCP § 1021.5.)

 

“[A]bsent circumstances rendering the award unjust, an attorney fee award should ordinarily include compensation for all the hours reasonably spent, including those relating solely to the fee.  We explained that the purpose behind statutory fee authorizations—i.e., encouraging attorneys to act as private attorneys general and to vindicate important rights affecting the public interest—will often be frustrated, sometimes nullified, if awards are diluted or dissipated by lengthy, uncompensated proceedings to fix or defend a rightful fee claim.” (Ketchum v. Moses (2001) 24 Cal.4th 1122, 1133-34 (emphasis in original).)  “The fee setting inquiry in California ordinarily begins with the ‘lodestar,’ i.e., the number of hours reasonably expended multiplied by the reasonable hourly rate.... The lodestar figure may then be adjusted, based on consideration of factors specific to the case, in order to fix the fee at the fair market value for the legal services provided.” (Id. at 1134.) Other factors to consider in fixing the reasonableness of fees to be considered beyond the fee agreement alone include, but are not limited to “(1) the time and labor required, the novelty and difficulty of the questions involved, and the skill requisite to perform the legal service properly; (2) the likelihood that the acceptance of the particular employment will preclude other employment by the lawyer; (3) the fee customarily charged in this locality for similar legal services; (4) the amount involved and the results obtained; (5) the time limitations imposed by the circumstances; and (6) the experience, reputation, and ability of the lawyer or lawyers performing the services.” (Glendora Community Redevelopment Agency v. Demeter (1984) 155 Cal.App.3d 465, 470.)

 

B.     Timeliness

 

First, Defendants argue the motion must be denied because Plaintiff failed to timely file its motion.

 

California Rules of Court Rule 3.1702(b)(1) provides the motion for a claim of attorneys’ fees for services up to and including the rendition of judgment in the trial court must be served and filed within the time for filing a notice of appeal under California Rules of Court Rules 8.104 and 8.108.  The deadline is mandatory. (Community Youth Athletic Center v. City of National City (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1385, 1444.) California Rules of Court Rule 8.104(a)(1) provides that unless provided otherwise, a notice of appeal must be filed on or before the earliest of “(A) 60 days after the superior court clerk serves on the party filing the notice of appeal a document entitled “Notice of Entry” of judgment or a filed-endorsed copy of the judgment, showing the date either was served; (B) 60 days after the party filing the notice of appeal serves or is served by a party with a document entitled “Notice of Entry” of judgment or a filed-endorsed copy of the judgment, showing the date either was served; or (C) 180 days after entry of judgment.”  California Rules of Court Rule 8.108 provides situations in which the time to appeal prescribed in Rule 8.104(a) is extended, such as when a motion for a new trial, to vacate judgment, for judgment notwithstanding the verdict, a cross-appeal, or a motion to reconsider an appealable order is filed. 

 

Here, the Clerk of the Court served by mail on Defendants the Notice of Entry of Judgment on November 20, 2023. On November 28, 2023, Defendants filed and served Notice of Entry of Judgment.

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees must have been served and filed by January 29, 2024. Plaintiff served the instant motion on Defendants on January 29, 2024, but filed its motion on February 16, 2024.

 

Defendants argue that since Plaintiff did not file its motion until after the deadline, the motion should be denied. None of the situations described in California Rules of Court Rule 8.108 apply to extend the time to file the motion and there is also no stipulation is on record by the parties extending the time to file the motion. 

 

However, Plaintiff argues that the Court has discretion to consider the late filed motion under Code of Civil Procedure section 473.

 

“While an attorney fee motion should be timely filed, the rule provisions are mandatory but not jurisdictional, and on a proper showing under section 473, the trial court may grant relief for such mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect.” (Community Youth Athletic Center v. City of National City (2013) 220 Cal.App.4th 1385, 1444.) “[T]he trial court has considerably more latitude in ruling on an extension of time to file an attorney’s fees motion under the good cause standard of Rule 3.1702(d) than it does granting relief from a judgment, dismissal [or] order under Code of Civil Procedure, section 473, subdivision (b).” (Robinson v. U-Haul Co. of California (2016) 4 Cal.App.5th 304, 327.) “Even a claim of inadvertence, if it is not prejudicial, may constitute good cause for a late filing.” (Id. at p. 326.) “Rule 3.1702(d) is ‘remedial’ and is to be given a liberal, rather than strict interpretation.” (Ibid., citation omitted.) Good cause means “a good reason for a party’s failure to perform that specific requirement [of the statute] from which he seeks to be excused.” (Ibid.)  

 

The party seeking relief from an untimely filed motion for attorneys’ fees “bears the burden of proof in establishing a right to relief.” (Hopkins & Carley v. Gens (200 Cal.App.4th 1401, 1410.) “It is simply not enough to assert a general state of misapprehension or ignorance on some subject bearing on a possible defense.” (Ibid.) Although “courts are liberal in relieving parties of defaults caused by inadvertence or excusable neglect, they do not act as guardians for incompetent parties or parties who are grossly careless as to their own affairs. There must be rules and regulations by which rights are determined and judgments become final.” (Id. at p. 1415, internal quotations omitted.)   

 

“A party seeking . . . relief on the ground of attorney error must demonstrate that the error was excusable, since the attorney’s negligence is imputed to the client.” (Huh v. Wang (2007) 158 Cal.App.4th 1406, 1419.) “The inexcusable negligence of an attorney is usually not a proper basis for granting the client’s motion under section 473.” (Ibid.) “Excusable neglect is that neglect which might have been the act of a reasonably prudent person under the same circumstances.” (Ibid.) The “press of business alone generally does not constitute grounds for relief.” (Id. at p. 1423.) “To constitute grounds for relief, an exceptional workload generally must be accompanied by some factor outside the attorney’s control that makes the situation unmanageable, such as a mistake caused by a glitch in office machinery or an error by clerical staff.” (Id. at p. 1424.) A court is not required to grant discretionary relief where counsel failed to calendar a matter. (Ibid.) 

 

A party must show that their inadvertence was excusable. (Zamora v. Clayborn Contracting Group, Inc. (2002) 28 Cal.4th 249, 258.) “In determining whether the attorney’s . . . inadvertence was excusable, the court inquires whether a reasonably prudent person under the same or similar circumstances might have made the same error.” (Ibid.) “Conduct falling below the professional standard of care . . . is not therefore excusable.” (Ibid.)  

 

Here, Plaintiff submitted the declaration of its counsel, Bryan Pease, stating that the motion was filed late due to mistake, inadvertence, or excusable neglect. Pease states that he had attempted to file the motion on January 29, 2024, but the filing was rejected by the Court. (Pease Suppl. Decl. ¶ 6, Ex. 1.) Plaintiff did not realize the error until checking the docket on February 16, 2024, as Defendants were supposed to have filed their opposition by the day before, i.e. February 15, 2024, for the February 29, 2024 hearing date. (Pease Suppl. Decl. ¶¶ 7-8.) When Plaintiff did not see the motion on the docket and then went into the efile system to see the rejection, Plaintiff immediately re-submitted the motion for filing that same morning of February 16, 2024. (Reply p. 2, Pease Suppl. Decl. ¶¶ 7-8.)

 

The Court finds Plaintiff’s inadvertence and neglect is excusable. Plaintiff provided a copy of the rejection notice indicating its counsel attempted to file the motion on January 29, 2024. Plaintiff inadvertently attempted to file the document under a different document name and the efiling was rejected by the Court, but was still served upon Defendants’ counsel. The Court exercises its discretion to consider the motion and continues on the merits.

D.     Discussion

 

APRL moves for attorneys’ fees under CCP §1021.5.

 

Section 1021.5 provides the “explicit statutory authority for court-awarded attorney fees under a private attorney general theory” where the lawsuit “has resulted in the enforcement of an important right affecting the public interest regardless of its source – constitutional, statutory or other.” (Woodland Hills Residents Assn., Inc. v. City Council, (1979) 23 Cal.3d 917, 925, quoting Cal. Code Civ. P. 1021.5 (internal quotations and emphasis omitted).) Under CCP §1021.5, a plaintiff is entitled to an award of reasonable attorney fees if four criteria are met: (1) it is the “successful party”; (2) its lawsuit has enforced an important right affecting the public interest; (3) a significant benefit has been conferred on the general public or a large class of persons; and (4) the necessity and financial burden of private enforcement are such as to make an award of fees appropriate. (CCP § 1021.5.)

 

The moving party bears the burden of establishing each prerequisite to an award of attorney’s fees under section 1021.5. (Ebbetts Pass Forest Watch v. California Dept. of Forestry and Fire Protection (2010) 187 Cal.App.4th 376, 381.) If any one of the elements required for an award under section 1021.5 is absent, that alone will suffice to deny a fee request. (Satrap v. Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 72, 81.) The decision to award fees lies within the trial court's discretion and will not be disturbed on appeal absent a prejudicial abuse of discretion. (Baggett v. Gates (1982) 32 Cal.3d 128, 142-43.)

 

The Court does not find that Plaintiff is entitled to attorneys’ fees because it has not established that it was the “successful party.”

 

A plaintiff is a “successful party” entitled to a catalyst fee award if “(1) the lawsuit was a catalyst motivating the defendants to provide the primary relief sought; (2) that the lawsuit had merit and achieved its catalytic effect by threat of victory, not by dint of nuisance and threat of expense . . . and, (3) that the plaintiffs reasonably attempted to settle the litigation prior to filing the lawsuit.” (Tipton Whittingham v. City of Los Angeles (2004) 34 Cal. 4th 604, 608.)

 

Here, Plaintiff has not shown that its “the lawsuit had merit and achieved its catalytic effect by threat of victory, not by dint of nuisance and threat of expense.” This Court issued a final judgment ruling in favor of Defendants. (Final Judgment, p. 1.) The Court stated that Plaintiff failed to carry its burden at trial and that Plaintiff lacks standing. (Final Judgment, p. 1.) The Court further stated that the “individual Defendants are not proper parties” and “there is no defendant against whom a judgment can properly be entered.” (Final Judgment, p. 1.) Therefore, Plaintiff fails to show its lawsuit had merit or that it achieved any “catalytic effect by threat of victory.” Plaintiff’s cited authority is unpersuasive. In Bjornestad v. Hulse (1991) 229 Cal. App. 3d 1568 and Sagaser v. McCarthy (1986) 176 Cal.App.3d 288, 315, the plaintiff’s actions were rendered moot by legislative action changing the state of law. Here, Plaintiff’s lawsuit was not rendered moot but was dismissed because of its failure to carry its burden on the merits, lack of standing and proper defendants. Therefore, Plaintiff has not established it was the “successful party.” Because the Court does not find that Plaintiff is the “successful party,” that alone will suffice to deny a fee request and the Court will not discuss the other elements of section 1021.5. (Satrap v. Pacific Gas & Elec. Co. (1996) 42 Cal.App.4th 72, 81.)

 

While Defendants’ opposition asserts that sanctions should be imposed against Plaintiff for bringing its frivolous motion, the Court declines to exercise its discretion to issue sanctions. In all civil cases, the judge may order a party or counsel, or both, “to pay the reasonable expenses, including attorney's fees, incurred by another party as a result of actions or tactics, made in bad faith, that are frivolous or solely intended to cause unnecessary delay.” (CCP § 128.5(a).) Whether an action is frivolous is governed by an objective standard: any reasonable attorney would agree it is totally and completely without merit. [Citations.] There must also be a showing of an improper purpose, i.e., subjective bad faith on the part of the attorney or party to be sanctioned.” (In re Marriage of Sahafzadeh-Taeb & Taeb (2019) 39 Cal. App. 5th 124, 135.) Defendants have not shown the motion was filed with an improper purpose.

 

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

 

III. DISPOSITION

 

Plaintiff’s Motion for Attorneys’ Fees is DENIED.

II. ANALYSIS



 



A.      Legal
Standard for Motion for Summary Judgment



 



A party may
move for summary judgment “if it is contended that the action has no merit or
that there is no defense to the action or proceeding.”  (Cal. Civ. Proc.
Code § 437c(a).)   “Summary judgment shall be granted if all the
papers submitted show that there is no triable issue as to any material fact
and that the moving party is entitled to a judgment as a matter of law. 
In determining if the papers show that there is no triable issue as to any
material fact, the court shall consider all of the evidence set forth in the
papers, except the evidence to which objections have been made and sustained by
the court, and all inferences reasonably deducible from the evidence, except
summary judgment shall not be granted by the court based on inferences
reasonably deducible from the evidence if contradicted by other inferences or
evidence that raise a triable issue as to any material fact.”  (Id.,
§ 437c, subd. (c).) 



 



A defendant
moving for summary judgment must show “that one or more elements of the cause
of action . . . cannot be established, or that there is a complete defense to
the cause of action.”  (Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (p)(2); see also,
Code Civ. Proc., § 437c, subd. (o).) 



 



“The
defendant may, but need not, present evidence that conclusively negates an
element of the plaintiff's cause of action.  The defendant may also
present evidence that the plaintiff does not possess, and cannot reasonably
obtain, needed evidence—as through admissions by the plaintiff following
extensive discovery to the effect that he has discovered nothing.  But…
the defendant must indeed present evidence."  (Aguilar v.
Atlantic Richfield Co. (2001) 25 Cal.4th 826, 855 [italics in
original].)   “In other words, all that the defendant need do is to
show that the plaintiff cannot establish at least one element of the cause of
action--for example, that the plaintiff cannot prove element¿X.”  (Id.,
at 853.) The court in Aguilar distilled summary judgment to “a
single proposition:  If a party moving for summary judgment in any action
. . . would prevail at trial without submission of any issue of material fact
to a trier of fact for determination, then he should prevail on summary
judgment.  In such a case . . . the ‘court should grant’ the motion ‘and
“avoid a . . . trial’ rendered ‘useless’ by nonsuit or directed verdict or
similar device.  (Id. at 855.) 



 



As noted in Aguilar,
“the party moving for summary judgment bears an initial burden of production to
make a prima facie showing of the nonexistence of any triable issue of material
fact; if he carries his burden of production, he causes a shift, and the
opposing party is then subjected to a burden of production of his own to make a
prima facie showing of the existence of a triable issue of material
fact.”  (Aguilar, supra, 25 Cal.4th at 850.)  Thus, courts
usually follow a three-step analysis: “First, we identify the issues framed by
the pleadings . . . . [¶] Secondly, we determine whether the moving party’s
showing has established facts which negate the opponent’s claim and justify a
judgment in movant’s favor. . . . [¶] When a . . . motion prima facie justifies
a judgment, the third and final step is to determine whether the opposition
demonstrates the existence of a triable, material factual issue.”  (Ojavan
Investors, Inc. v. Cal. Coastal Comm. (1997) 54 Cal.App.4th 373, 385
[citation and footnote omitted].) 



  



Opposing
parties must present substantial evidence in order to avoid summary
judgment.  (Sangster v. Paetkau (1998) 68 Cal.App.4th 151,
163.)  “In some instances…, ‘evidence may be so lacking in probative value
that it fails to raise any triable issue.’”  (Whitmire v.
Ingersoll-Rand Co. (2010) 184 Cal.App.4th 1078, 1083-1084.)  “A
complete failure of proof concerning an essential element of the nonmoving
party's case necessarily renders all other facts immaterial.”  (Hunter
v. Pacific Mechanical Corp (1995) 37 Cal.App.4th 1282, 1286, disapproved on
other grounds by Aguilar, supra, at 865; accord Saelzler v. Advanced
Group 400 (2001) 25 Cal.4th 763, 780 [“‘If the plaintiff is unable to meet
her burden of proof regarding an essential element of her case, all other facts
are rendered immaterial.’”].) 



 



Courts
“construe the moving party's affidavits strictly, construe the opponent's
affidavits liberally, and resolve doubts about the¿propriety¿of granting the
motion in favor of the party opposing it.’” (Unilab Corp.v. Angeles-IPA
(2016) 244 Cal.App.4th 622, 636; internal citation omitted.) 
“The court focuses on issue finding; it does not resolve issues of fact. The
court seeks to find contradictions in the evidence, or inferences reasonably
deducible from the evidence, which raise a triable issue of material
fact.”  (Johnson v. United Cerebral Palsy, etc. (2009) 173
Cal.App.4th 740, 754; internal citation omitted.)   
"[S]ummary judgment cannot be granted when the facts are susceptible [of]
more than one reasonable inference...” (Rosas v. BASF Corp.¿(2015) 236
Cal.App.4th 1378, 1392.)

frightened Plaintiff, who felt threatened. (Compl., ¶ 23.).



 



In the Regent’s motion, it argues that even if Plaintiff’s allegations
were true, Bush’s conduct does not rise to the level of actionable harassment
because (1) Plaintiff has not sufficiently shown Bush’s alleged harassment was
based on Plaintiff’s gender and (2) the alleged conduct is not sufficiently
severe or pervasive. The Court agrees. While Plaintiff alleges she considered
Bush’s actions “sexist”, Plaintiff does not specify how Bush’s actions were
based on Plaintiff being a woman and that Bush would not have taken such
actions if Plaintiff was not a woman. While Plaintiff alleges Bush regularly made
disparaging comments about female coworkers, it does not appear that the
comments he made were sufficiently severe or pervasive. The “law does not exhibit ‘zero tolerance’ for offensive words and
conduct. Rather, the law requires the plaintiff to meet a threshold standard of
severity or pervasiveness.” (Etter v. Veriflo Corp. (1998) 67
Cal.App.4th 457, 467.) “FEHA’s prohibitions are not a ‘civility code’ and are
not designed to rid the workplace of vulgarity.” (Sheffield v. Los Angeles
County Dept. of Social Services (2003) 109 Cal.App.4th 153, 162–162.) Bush’s comments about Noela Hueso and Deb
Widener appear to be offensive utterances but do not appear to be “severe” such
as by being physically threatening, epithets or slurs. Further, the few
incidents that Plaintiff also alleged was harassing conduct, such as the
comment Bush made about mansplaining, yelling at Plaintiff for contradicting
him in a Zoom meeting, and inviting her to an inappropriate performance, do not
appear to be based on Plaintiff’s gender and appear to be isolated incidents. Plaintiff
must show harassment of a repeated, routine or generalized nature, especially
when harassing conduct is not severe. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th
1035, 1043.) The Regents
has shown that the alleged conduct does not constitute actionable harassment. The
Regents thus carries its initial burden.



 



In opposition, Plaintiff argues
Bush’s conduct was sufficiently pervasive and severe to create a hostile work
environment. First, Plaintiff asserts Bush’s alleged harassment was based on
her gender and Plaintiff submits as evidence the declarations of past female
employees who assert that Bush also harassed them based on their gender. Courts
have allowed “me too” evidence to “prove a defendant's motive or intent even
where the conduct occurred outside the plaintiff's presence and at times other
than when the plaintiff was employed.” (Meeks v. Autozone, Inc.
(2018) 24 Cal. App. 5th 855, 871.) Plaintiff provides the declarations as
circumstantial evidence as to Bush’s motive. However, the declarations provide
conclusory testimony. For example, in the declaration of Kristen Stankowski,
she merely states she believes “that Mr. Bush targeted me because I am a woman”
and that she filed complaints of harassment and discrimination based on her
gender. (Stankowski Decl., ¶¶ 5, 14.) The other employees do not allege
specific facts or incidents. Thus, the Court does not find Plaintiff’s
circumstantial evidence very persuasive.



 



Second, Plaintiff asserts that the February 3, 2022 incident was sufficiently
severe. The Complaint alleges that on or around February 3, 2022, Plaintiff, Bush,
and other coworkers had a Zoom meeting, during which Plaintiff expressed
disagreement with Bush. “Immediately after the Zoom meeting ended, BUSH barged
into Plaintiff’s office and pushed open her door so forcefully that Plaintiff
stumbled back. BUSH, who is at least six inches taller and weighs approximately
100 pounds more than Plaintiff, closed the door to Plaintiff’s office, blocked
Plaintiff from leaving the office, and positioned himself only inches away from
Plaintiff’s face. Then with his fists clenched at his side, BUSH screamed,
“Don’t you ever confront or contradict me like that again!” BUSH continued to
scream at Plaintiff for being confrontational because she simply disagreed with
him about his comments regarding Ms. Hobson. BUSH then sat in Plaintiff’s
office and aggressively stared at her for several minutes before leaving.
During this encounter, Plaintiff feared that BUSH was going to physically
attack her.” (Compl., ¶ 23.) Plaintiff
cites to Sheffield v. Los Angeles County Dept. of Social Services (2003)
109 Cal.App.4th 153, 163 where the court held there was genuine issue of
material fact regarding the severity of the harassing conduct where the harassing
defendant, who was much bigger than plaintiff, made a threat of violence by
making a fist and slamming it into her other palm while looking at the
plaintiff. However, the Court notes that in Sheffield, the defendant’s slamming
of her fist into her palm was done after repeated, harassing phone calls to the
plaintiff after the plaintiff rebuffed defendant’s request for a date. (Ibid.)
Here, the incident with Bush doesn’t seem to rise to the level of severity to
constitute harassment as it only occurred once, Bush did not yell at her
regularly, and during this incident his fists were clenched at his side as
opposed to slamming it into his fist. Based on the foregoing, Plaintiff has not
shown the alleged conduct is severe and pervasive to constitute harassment. Plaintiff
fails to create a triable issue of material fact regarding her third cause of
action.



 



In sum, the Court finds that the Regents have met their burden to show Bush’s
conduct did not constitute harassment. Plaintiff failed to meet her burden of raising
triable issues of material fact. As such, summary adjudication for the third
cause of action in the Complaint for harassment based on gender is GRANTED.



 



D.     Fourth Cause
of Action for Harassment based on Race



 



The Regent’s
also move for summary judgment as to the fourth cause of action on the ground
that



Plaintiff
is unable to establish a prima facie claim for harassment based on her race.



 



The Complaint alleges the following
conduct constituted harassment based on Plaintiff’s race:



(1) “In or around March 2021, BUSH informed Plaintiff that he
would be recommending that she serve as the Development Liaison to the
Chancellor's Council of the Arts because the council was too white, Plaintiff
was Latina, and had a background fundraising in the arts. Plaintiff felt
tokenized due to her Hispanic race.” (Compl.,
¶ 14.);



(2) “In or around August 2021, BUSH asked Plaintiff to attend a
Latino donor event at the donor’s house. BUSH insisted on driving and taking
Plaintiff home. Although Plaintiff declined BUSH’s offer to drive, he
relentlessly insisted that he drive. BUSH made Plaintiff feel pressured to
agree as if she had no choice. While driving to the event, BUSH became
extremely aggressive and violent in a terrifying display of road rage. BUSH was
screaming and cursing at another driver and almost caused the car to crash. Plaintiff
felt terrified and was worried that she would be the target of his anger if she
did not empathize with his feelings.”
(Compl., ¶ 15.);



(3) “In response to Plaintiff stating that she drove to Boyle
Heights to get tamales, BUSH stated, "I don't go to the East Side of LA,
it's too Latino,'' or words similar to that effect.” (Compl., ¶ 16.);



(4) BUSH made derogatory remarks about black donors.
“Specifically, when speaking about Felicia Henderson, an African-American
female donor, BUSH told Plaintiff, ''You'll never see a dime from her," or
words similar to that effect.” (Compl.,
¶ 20.);



(5) “BUSH complained to Plaintiff about having to research
potential Mexican donor leads” …and “handed Plaintiff the list to review and
asked if she knew any of the donors. Plaintiff was offended because she felt
like she was being tokenized due to her Hispanic race.” (Compl., ¶ 19.);



(6) “On or around February 3, 2022, Plaintiff, BUSH, and other
coworkers had a Zoom meeting to discuss a proposal to a private foundation for
the cinema collection of the first African American female filmmaker. During
the meeting, BUSH stated that the proposal would be a good project for Mellody
Hobson and stated, “it’s so great that Mellody has her little pet project,” or
words similar to that effect. Plaintiff immediately stated that she did not
agree with BUSH and she did not feel it was appropriate to tokenize Ms. Hobson
based on her race or to diminish her role in the Hobson-Luca Foundation based
on her sex.” (Compl., ¶ 22.)



 



The Regents argue these occasional comments were not severe or
pervasive to constitute actionable harassment. The Court agrees. The incident that
occurred in or around August 2021 where Bush allegedly experience road rage does
not appear to be related to race. Although it appears that the other comments made by Bush are
based on race and Plaintiff alleges she found them offensive, the Court finds
these statements do not rise to the level of severe or pervasive. Harassment
that is occasional, isolated, or sporadic is insufficient. Plaintiff must show
harassment of a repeated, routine or generalized nature, especially when
harassing conduct is not severe. (Hughes v. Pair (2009) 46 Cal.4th 1035,
1043; see McCoy v. Pacific Maritime Assn. (2013) 216 Cal.App.4th 283,
294 [finding that, over the four months plaintiff worked in the office, at the
most nine comments were made to plaintiff, and this was insufficient to show
that the workplace was permeated with harassment].) Here, Plaintiff only points
to five incidents where she felt that Bush made racial comments. The Regents has shown that the comments he made were not severe or
pervasive. The Regents thus carries its initial burden.



 



In opposition, Plaintiff fails
to carry her burden. Plaintiff submits as evidence the declaration of another
female employee who had worked with Bush who stated that Bush made disparaging
comments about her boyfriend’s Mexican race. She states “at one point Jonathan
was purchasing a new vehicle, which Mr. Bush learned about. In response, Mr.
Bush stated that he imagined that Jonathan would drive a lowrider, which is a
stereotype of the type of vehicle that Mexican individuals drive. Mr. Bush also
made comments saying, “I feel like Jonathan would have his hair slicked back”
which is another stereotype relating to Mexicans. Bush also said, “I don’t go
east of La Brea Avenue,” and he would make negative comments about the fact
that Jonathan’s family lived in Monterey Park. Mr. Bush would tell me “why are
you going over there?” as if it was a bad thing.” (Tsalik Decl., ¶ 8.) While
these comments provide circumstantial evidence of Bush’s motive and intent,
they do not prove Bush had a propensity to harass or the pervasiveness of
harassment in Plaintiff’s work environment. (Brennan v. Townsend &
O'Leary Enterprises, Inc. (2011) 199 Cal. App.
4th 1336, 1359.) Next, Plaintiff cites to Dee v.
Vintage Petroleum, Inc. (2003) 106 Cal.App.4th 30, 36, in which the court
found a single discriminatory comment, combined with other evidence of abuse,
made by a supervisor about the plaintiff’s ethnicity, “it is your Filipino
understanding versus mine,” as sufficient to raise a triable issue of fact. The
Court of Appeal concluded the “Filipino understanding” comment was “an ethnic
slur, both abusive and hostile.” (Ibid.) On the other hand here, while
Bush’s comments are offensive to Plaintiff, they do not consist of ethnic slurs
or other abusive language. Plaintiff fails to show that Bush’s conduct was
severe or pervasive, thus, Plaintiff fails to carry her burden.



 



Therefore, the court GRANTS summary adjudication as to Plaintiff’s
fourth cause of action for harassment based on her race.



 



A.      First and
Second Causes of Action for Discrimination based on Gender and Race



 



The Regent’s argue that Plaintiff cannot prevail on this cause of action
because Plaintiff did not suffer an adverse employment action.¿¿ 



 



The
California Supreme Court has adopted the federal burden-shifting test for
assessing employment discrimination claims. (Guz v. Bechtel National, Inc. (2000)
24 Cal.4th 317, 354.) To establish a prima facie case of discrimination, the
Plaintiff must show that (1) the employee is a member of a protected class; (2)
the employee was qualified for the position sought or performing competently in
the position held; (3) the employee suffered an adverse employment action; and
(4) some other circumstance suggests a discriminatory motive. (See McDonnell
Douglas Corp. v. Green (1973) 411 U.S. 792, 802.) To challenge an
employment discrimination claim on summary judgment, an employer must either
show that one or more of the prima facie elements is lacking, or must offer a
legitimate, nonretaliatory reason for the adverse employment action. (See Caldwell
v. Paramount Unified Sch. Dist. (1995) 41 Cal.App.4th 189.)¿ If the
employer produces a legitimate reason for the adverse employment action, the
presumption of discrimination “drops out of the picture,” and the burden shifts
back to the employee to prove that the claimed legitimate reason is merely a
pretext. (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th
1028, 1042.)¿ 



¿ 



"In
responding to the employer's showing of a legitimate reason for the
complained-of action, . . . '" . . . the employee' "must demonstrate
such weaknesses, implausibilities, inconsistencies, incoherencies, or
contradictions in the employer's proffered legitimate reasons for its action
that a reasonable factfinder¿could¿rationally find them 'unworthy of
credence,' [citation], and hence infer 'that the employer did not act for the [
. . . asserted] non-discriminatory reasons.' [Citations.]" '" '"
(McRae v. Department of Corrections & Rehabilitation¿(2006)¿142
Cal.App.4th 377, 388-389.) "The plaintiff must do more than raise the
inference that the employer's asserted reason is false. '[A] reason cannot be
proved to be "a pretext¿for discrimination" unless it is
shown¿both¿that the reason was false,¿and¿that discrimination was
the real reason.' [Citation.] If the plaintiff produces¿no¿evidence from
which a reasonable fact finder could infer that the employer's true reason was
discriminatory, the employer is entitled to summary judgment. [Citation.]"
(Hicks v. KNTV Television, Inc.¿(2008) 160 Cal.App.4th 994, 1003.)¿ 



¿ 



"Although
an employee's evidence submitted in opposition to an employer's motion for
summary judgment is construed liberally, it 'remains subject to careful
scrutiny.' [Citation.] The employee's 'subjective beliefs in an employment
discrimination case do not create a genuine issue of fact; nor do
uncorroborated and self-serving declarations.' [Citation.] The employee's
evidence must relate to the motivation of the decision makers and prove, by
nonspeculative evidence, 'an actual causal link between prohibited motivation
and [discrimination].'" (Featherstone v. Southern California Permanente
Medical Group¿(2017)¿10 Cal.App.5th 1150, 1159.)¿



 



An “adverse employment action” is an action that has a substantial
and material adverse effect on the terms, conditions, or privileges of
employment. (Yanowitz v. L'Oreal USA, Inc. (2005) 36 Cal.4th 1028,
1051-1052.) “A change that is merely contrary to the employees interests or not
to the employee's liking is insufficient.” (McRae v. Department of
Corrections & Rehabilitation (2006) 142 Cal.App.4th 377, 386-387.)
“[S]imple teasing . . . offhand comments, and isolated incidents (unless
extremely serious) will not amount to discriminatory changes in the ‘terms and
conditions of employment.’” (Etter v. Veriflo Corp. (1998) 67
Cal.App.4th 457, 463.) A mere offensive utterance or even a pattern of social
slights cannot properly be viewed as materially affecting the terms,
conditions, or privileges of employment. (Yanowitz, supra, 36 Cal. 4th
at 1054.)



 



The Complaint alleges that “[a]s a result of the discriminatory treatment, Plaintiff suffered
an adverse employment action when she was constructively terminated on or
around August 30, 2022. (Compl., ¶
131.) The Complaint further alleges “Plaintiff was constructively
terminated due to Defendant’s failure to enforce its zero-tolerance policies
regarding anti-discrimination, anti-harassment, anti-racism, workplace
violence, and abuse against BUSH. Defendant’s failure to enforce the above
policies; its refusal to conduct a bonafide investigation into the numerous
complaints against BUSH; its refusal to terminate and/or discipline BUSH; its
refusal grant Plaintiff’s accommodation request to be precluded from working
with BUSH in any capacity; and its failure to guarantee Plaintiff’s protection
from BUSH subjected Plaintiff to a hostile and unsafe work environment that
resulted in her constructive termination.” (Compl., ¶ 74.)



 



The Regents argue that Plaintiff was not
constructively terminated.  “In order to amount to constructive discharge, adverse working
conditions must be unusually ‘aggravated’ or amount to a ‘continuous pattern’
before the situation will be deemed intolerable…” (Turner v. Anheuser-Busch,
Inc. (1994) 7 Cal.4th 1238, 1247.) “[T]he standard by which a constructive
discharge is determined is an objective one—the question is “whether a
reasonable person faced with the allegedly intolerable employer actions or conditions
[…] would have no reasonable alternative except to quit.” (Ibid.) A
constructive discharge occurs when the employer coerces the employee’s
resignation, either by creating working conditions that are intolerable under
an objective standard, or by failing to remedy objectively intolerable working conditions
that actually are known to the employer.” (Id. at 1244.)



 



The Regents assert that Plaintiff was offered several reasonable
alternatives instead of resigning. The Regents contend it offered: “(1)
reassignment to a comparable position in another department, (2) limiting her
communications with Bush to email only, (3) a change to her work location, (4)
including a third person in meetings with Bush on a temporary basis, and (5) a
leave of absence.” (UMF No. 59.) Moreover, UCLA investigated Gonzalez’s
complaints about Bush. (UMF Nos. 39, 69.) Plaintiff’s claim of constructive
termination is further attenuated as Plaintiff has also not shown a pattern of
harassment towards her, as discussed above. Therefore, it is not evident that
Bush’s conduct toward Plaintiff created objectively intolerable working
conditions. The Regents carry its burden.



 



Plaintiff argues that Bush subjected
Plaintiff to harassment that led to her constructive termination, and the
Regents denied Plaintiff’s requests to work remotely an additional four months
and preclude contact between Plaintiff and Bush. However, as discussed above,
the Court does not find Bush’s conduct constituted harassment. In addition,
although the Regents did not grant Plaintiff’s specific work requests, it
provided five different reasonable alternatives. Plaintiff has not carried her
burden here. Since Plaintiff cannot show she suffered constructive termination
or an adverse action, Plaintiff’s discrimination claims fail.



 



Accordingly, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication as to
Plaintiff’s first and second causes of action for discrimination based on her gender
and race.



 



B.      Fifth Cause
of Action for Discrimination Based on Disability



 



The Regents move for summary judgment as to the
cause of action for disability discrimination on the grounds that (1) Plaintiff
did not have a qualifying disability under the FEHA, and (2) Plaintiff did not
suffer an adverse employment action because of any disability.



To successfully assert a claim for discrimination, Plaintiff must
satisfy the requirements of the three-step McDonnell Douglas test. (See Guz
v. Bechtel Nat. Inc. (2000) 24 Cal.4th 317, 354- 55.) Generally, a prima
facie case requires showing that (1) plaintiff was a member of a protected
class; (2) plaintiff was qualified for the position plaintiff sought or was
performing competently in the position plaintiff held; (3) plaintiff suffered
an adverse employment action, such as termination, demotion, or denial of an
available job; and (4) some other circumstance suggests discriminatory motive.
(Id. at 355.)  



In the context of disability discrimination claims, the
Courts of Appeal boil these prima facie elements down from four to three: “that
the plaintiff (1) suffered from a disability or was regarded as suffering from
a disability, (2) could perform the essential duties of a job with or without
reasonable accommodations, and (3) was subjected to an adverse employment
action because of the disability or perceived disability.” (Glynn v.
Superior Court (2019) 42 Cal.App.5th 47, 53 fn.1.)¿¿ 



 



A
physical or medical disability constitutes a disability under FEHA when that
physical or mental disability requires “a limitation upon a major life
activity.” (Gov. Code. § 129261.1(c).) The limitation need not be a substantial
one as required under the Federal Americans with Disabilities act of 1990. (Ibid.) 



 



The Complaint alleges “[o]n or around March 9, 2022, Plaintiff saw her primary care
physician, Dr. Jacob M. Gold. Dr. Gold stated that it was his medical opinion
that Plaintiff was suffering from significant mental and physical distress as a
result of continuing to work with BUSH, who Plaintiff accused of assaulting
her. Dr. Gold further ordered that Plaintiff was able to perform her job and as
long as she no longer was required to work with BUSH.” (Compl., ¶ 122.) “On or around May 13, 2022, Plaintiff was
diagnosed with anxiety and acute stress disorder as a result of BUSH’s February
3, 2022 attack and resulting retaliatory treatment.” (Compl., ¶ 126.) “On or around July 5, 2022, Plaintiff submitted
an updated doctor’s note that stated, due to her medical condition, she was
permanently precluded her from having any contact with BUSH.” (Compl., ¶ 127.)



 



The
Regents argue that the only disability Plaintiff alleges is stress due to Bush’s
supervision. However, as the Regents note, “an employee’s inability to work
under a particular supervisor because of anxiety and stress related to the
supervisor’s standard oversight of the employee’s job performance does not
constitute a mental disability under FEHA.” (Higgins-Williams
v. Sutter Med. Found. (2015) 237 Cal.App.4th 78, 84.) In Higgins-Williams, the plaintiff alleged that actions by
her supervisors caused her to have a panic attack and leave work. (Id.
at 81-82.) The court found that, because the plaintiff's condition only
prevented her from working with two particular supervisors, not from working in
general, her anxiety disorder and stress did not constitute a FEHA-recognized
mental disability, and summary judgment was properly granted in favor of her
employer on all of her disability-related claims. (Id. at 84-86.). Similarly
here, Plaintiff specifically alleges
she suffered “anxiety and acute stress disorder as a result of
Bush’s February 3, 2022 attack and resulting retaliatory treatment.” (Compl., ¶ 126.) Plaintiff does not dispute
that she could otherwise perform her job, except working with Bush. Plaintiff
has not established that her anxiety and stress constituted a FEHA-recognized
mental disability. The Regents have carried their burden.



 



The burden shifts to Plaintiff. Plaintiff argues in her opposition that Plaintiff’s diagnosis of anxiety and acute stress disorder were
qualifying disabilities because they limited her ability to work. She argues her
anxiety was often triggered when working in her office and Plaintiff was able
to effectively perform her job with the accommodation of being allowed to work
from home. Plaintiff states she received regularly recurring psychiatry
sessions from Dr. Kusens from May 2022 through April 2023. (Gonzalez Depo., at
232:11-21.) In Plaintiff’s opposition she states “[t]hus, Plaintiff’s diagnosed
anxiety disability was a FEHA recognized disability and was not merely related
to normal work stress.” (Oppos., p. 19.) However, Plaintiff does not provide
any other reason for her anxiety and stress not related to Bush or work. Plaintiff’s
contention that her anxiety and stress were not only caused by Bush and work is
unsupported. Plaintiff has not carried her burden. Plaintiff thus fails to show that a triable issue of
material fact remains over whether her stress was caused by something other
than her ability to work under a particular supervisor. Since Plaintiff
has not shown that she suffered a disability under FEHA, the Court does not discuss
the Regents argument that Plaintiff also did not suffer an adverse
employment action because of any disability.



 



Accordingly, the Court GRANTS summary adjudication as to
Plaintiff’s fifth cause of action.

as to the cause of action for failure to prevent discrimination, harassment, or

D.     Seventh Cause
of Action for Failure to Provide Reasonable Accommodations



 



The Regents also move for summary adjudication as to the
cause of action for failure to accommodate.  



 



“The essential elements of a failure
to accommodate claim are: 1) the plaintiff has a disability covered by the
FEHA; 2) the plaintiff is a qualified individual (i.e., he or she can perform
the essential functions of the position); and 3) the employer failed to
reasonably accommodate the plaintiff’s disability. (Jensen v. Wells Fargo
Bank (2000) 85 Cal.App.4th 245, 256.)¿ “[T]he employer cannot prevail on
summary judgment on a claim of failure to reasonably accommodate unless it
establishes through undisputed facts that ‘1) reasonable accommodation was
offered and refused, 2) there simply was no vacant position within the employer’s
organization for which the disabled employee was qualified and which disabled
employee was capable of performing with or without accommodation, or (3) the
employer did everything in its power to find a reasonable accommodation, but
the informal interactive process broke down because the employee failed to
engage in discussions in good faith.’ [Citation.]” (King v. United Parcel
Service, Inc. (2007) 152 Cal.App.4th 426, 442.)¿  



 



Here, as discussed above, the Regents met its burden of
proving no triable issue of material fact remains over whether Plaintiff had a
disability covered by the FEHA. Plaintiff failed to prove a triable issue of
material fact remained over whether her disability was covered by the FEHA.
Summary judgment is GRANTED as to this cause of action for the same reasons as
the cause of action for disability discrimination.

(Ibid.) Thus, in addition to the $560.00 costs of suit, Plaintiff seeks judgment in the total amount of $33,248.35. (Buller Decl., ¶ 13.)

 

Since Plaintiff has complied with the requirements, Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is GRANTED.

 

III. CONCLUSION 

 

Plaintiff’s request for default judgment is GRANTED.
